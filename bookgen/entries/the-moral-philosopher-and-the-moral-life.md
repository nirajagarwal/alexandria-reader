# The Moral Philosopher and the Moral Life

## The Jamesian Insight

> "There is no such thing possible as an ethical philosophy dogmatically made up in advance. We all help to determine the content of ethical philosophy so far as we contribute to the race's moral life. In other words, there can be no final truth in ethics any more than in physics, until the last man has had his experience and said his say."

*— "The Moral Philosopher and the Moral Life" (1891)*

> "The essence of good is simply to satisfy demand. The demand may be for anything under the sun. There is really no more ground for supposing that all our demands can be accounted for by one universal underlying kind of motive than there is ground for supposing that all physical phenomena are cases of a single law."

*— "The Moral Philosopher and the Moral Life" (1891)*

> "Since everything which is demanded is by that fact a good, must not the guiding principle for ethical philosophy (since all demands conjointly cannot be satisfied in this poor world) be simply to satisfy at all times as many demands as we can? That act must be the best act, accordingly, which makes for the best whole, in the sense of awakening the least sum of dissatisfactions."

*— "The Moral Philosopher and the Moral Life" (1891)*

> "The course of history is nothing but the story of men's struggles from generation to generation to find the more and more inclusive order. Invent some manner of realizing your own ideals which will also satisfy the alien demands—that and that only is the path of peace! Following this path, society has shaken itself into one sort of relative equilibrium after another by a series of social discoveries quite analogous to those of science."

*— "The Moral Philosopher and the Moral Life" (1891)*

## The Idea Unpacked

James's essay on moral philosophy is one of the most radical statements in the history of ethics, and its radicalism is easy to miss because the prose is so reasonable. He proposes something simple and devastating: there is no moral truth waiting to be discovered, no ethical system that can be deduced from first principles, no moral law written into the fabric of the universe. Every moral claim arises from a sentient being making a demand on the world. Your hunger is a demand. A child's need for safety is a demand. The artist's need to create is a demand. The tiger's need to hunt is a demand. Each of these, by virtue of being demanded, has moral standing.

This is not relativism. James is not saying all demands are equal or that satisfying your craving for ice cream has the same moral weight as preventing a child's suffering. He is saying that all demands are real, all create genuine moral claims, and the work of ethics is to navigate among them. The problem is that demands conflict. We cannot satisfy them all. You cannot feed the gazelle and the lion. You cannot protect individual liberty and guarantee collective welfare in every instance. You cannot honor every cultural tradition and also advance universal human rights without remainder.

The ethical task, then, is not to discover which demands are truly valid and which are illusory. It is to satisfy as many demands as possible with the least sacrifice. This is a maximization problem, not a deduction problem. Ethics becomes experimental: we try arrangements, we see what works, we discover through living which configurations of human life manage to honor more claims with less violence to competing claims. The "more inclusive order" that James describes is not a final destination but an ongoing achievement, a social discovery process analogous to science.

What makes this radical is that it denies the possibility of any completed ethical system. Kant cannot be right, because no categorical imperative can anticipate every possible demand. Mill cannot be right, because no calculus of pleasure and pain captures the full range of what sentient beings value. Natural law theorists cannot be right, because nature is silent on which demands should take precedence. James sweeps away the entire project of foundational ethics. There are no foundations. There is only the messy, ongoing work of attending to the demands that arise, weighing them against each other, and building provisional arrangements that satisfy as many as we can manage.

This is ethics for a pluralistic universe. It has no room for moral certainty, no tolerance for dogma, and no patience with systems that claim to have solved the ethical problem once and for all. But it also has no room for cynicism. Every demand matters. The work of inclusion is real work. History does move, fitfully and imperfectly, toward orders that take more demands into account. James offers neither the comfort of moral law nor the nihilism of moral chaos. He offers responsibility without guarantees.

## The 130-Year Conversation

### Moral Pluralism and the Tragedy of Values

Isaiah Berlin developed what he called value pluralism over half a century after James's essay, but the structural insight is the same. Berlin argued that genuine values can conflict irreducibly. Liberty and equality are both real values, but they pull in different directions. Justice and mercy are both real values, but in any particular case, you may have to choose. There is no master value that harmonizes all others, no Hegelian synthesis that resolves the tension. The conflicts are not puzzles to be solved but permanent features of moral life.

James saw this clearly in 1891. His claim that "all demands conjointly cannot be satisfied in this poor world" is the foundation of Berlin's argument. Where many ethical theories assume that a sufficiently sophisticated system could eliminate moral dilemmas, James and Berlin insist that the dilemmas are built into the multiplicity of legitimate goods. A world with only one value would have no conflicts, but it would also have no richness. The plurality of demands is what makes moral life tragic, but it is also what makes it meaningful.

Bernard Williams extended this insight into a critique of moral theory itself. He argued that the quest for a systematic ethical theory distorts moral experience by forcing it into a single framework. Thick ethical concepts like courage, loyalty, and compassion cannot be reduced to thin concepts like duty or utility without losing something essential. Williams would have recognized James's essay as an ally: both refuse the totalizing ambitions of moral philosophy. Both insist that moral wisdom consists in navigating irreducible conflicts, not in pretending they can be dissolved.

What James adds to Berlin and Williams is the temporal dimension. The more inclusive order is not a static arrangement but a historical process. Each generation inherits a provisional settlement and must renegotiate it in light of new demands, new voices, new forms of life that were excluded from the previous order. The tragedy is permanent, but the configurations shift. This is ethics as an ongoing experiment, not a once-and-for-all solution.

### The Mathematics of Moral Attention

Peter Singer and the effective altruism movement have operationalized something very close to James's principle: satisfy as many demands as possible with the least sacrifice. EA asks which interventions reduce the most suffering or create the most well-being per dollar, per hour of effort, per unit of attention. It treats resource allocation as a moral optimization problem. This is Jamesian in structure: given that we cannot satisfy all demands, how do we satisfy the most?

But James's formulation is broader than EA's utilitarianism in two important ways. First, he includes the full range of human demands, not just material welfare. The scientist's demand to pursue truth, the artist's demand to create beauty, the contemplative's demand for solitude—these are legitimate claims on the moral universe even if they do not maximize pleasure or minimize pain in any straightforward sense. James's ethics has room for projects that EA's calculus struggles to accommodate.

Second, James recognized that the optimization problem is never solved. The more inclusive order is not a final state but a moving target. William MacAskill's work on longtermism captures something of this: the demands of future generations must also be weighed, and our uncertainty about what they will value complicates the calculation. But even MacAskill's framework assumes we can eventually get the calculation right. James is more skeptical. Every solution creates new demands, new conflicts, new voices that were not audible in the previous arrangement.

Where EA and James converge most powerfully is in the refusal of moral paralysis. The fact that we cannot satisfy all demands is not a reason to throw up our hands. It is a reason to think carefully about which demands we can satisfy and to take responsibility for the trade-offs we make. Derek Parfit's work in *Reasons and Persons* explored the logic of these trade-offs with relentless precision, but he also acknowledged that moral reasoning faces irreducible conflicts. James would approve of the precision and the acknowledgment. What he would add is that the conflicts are not defects to be engineered away but features of a world with real, plural, competing goods.

### The Empirical Turn in Ethics

Jonathan Haidt's moral foundations theory is a naturalized version of James's pluralism. Haidt argues that moral intuitions arise from at least six foundations: care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity, and liberty. These are not derivable from a single principle. They evolved to solve different adaptive problems, and they often conflict. A moral system built on care and fairness will clash with one built on loyalty and authority. There is no algorithm for resolving the clash, only the messy negotiation that James described.

Haidt's work treats ethics as something to be investigated rather than deduced. Joshua Greene's dual-process model of moral judgment does the same. Greene distinguishes between fast, automatic moral intuitions and slow, deliberative moral reasoning, arguing that the trolley problem debates reveal the tension between these two systems. This is experimental ethics in James's sense: we learn what we value by observing how we actually make moral judgments, not by deducing what we should value from first principles.

Paul Bloom's critique of empathy as a moral guide is also Jamesian. Bloom argues that empathy is biased, parochial, and often counterproductive as a basis for ethical action. We empathize more with people who look like us, with individual victims rather than statistical suffering, with present rather than future generations. A Jamesian ethic asks us to attend to demands whether or not they trigger our empathic response. The child in front of you and the child on the other side of the world both have legitimate claims, even if only one activates your emotional circuitry.

Kwame Anthony Appiah's *Experiments in Ethics* explicitly frames moral philosophy as an empirical discipline. Appiah draws on psychology, anthropology, and history to explore how moral practices actually function, rather than prescribing how they should function from philosophical first principles. This is the experimental method James proposed: try arrangements, observe the results, revise in light of experience. The contemporary field of moral psychology is doing what James called for in 1891: treating ethics as an open question to be investigated through living.

### Expanding the Circle of Moral Concern

James's principle that "everything which is demanded is by that fact a good" has radical implications for who counts morally. If demand itself creates moral standing, then the circle of moral concern must expand to include every being capable of making a demand: not just adult humans, but children, future generations, non-human animals, potentially ecosystems and the biosphere itself.

Peter Singer's *The Expanding Circle* traces this logic historically. The moral circle has grown from family to tribe to nation to humanity, and Singer argues it must now extend to all sentient beings. This is a direct application of James's principle. The suffering of a pig is a demand. The fear of a chicken in a factory farm is a demand. These demands do not disappear because we prefer not to hear them.

Martha Nussbaum's capabilities approach to animal justice operationalizes this expansion. She argues that each species has characteristic capabilities, and justice requires enabling those capabilities to flourish. This is more Jamesian than Singer's utilitarianism because it respects the plurality of goods. The flourishing of a whale is not the same as the flourishing of a human, but both are legitimate demands on a just world.

The legal personhood now granted to rivers in New Zealand and Ecuador, to forests in Colombia, represents another expansion. Christopher Stone's 1972 essay "Should Trees Have Standing?" asked whether natural entities could be legal persons with rights. The question seemed absurd at the time. Now it is settled law in multiple jurisdictions. The river's demand to flow, the forest's demand to regenerate—these are being recognized as claims that must be weighed in moral and legal deliberation.

Will MacAskill's *What We Owe the Future* extends the circle forward in time. Future generations cannot speak, but their demands are real. They will need a habitable planet, stable institutions, and the resources to flourish. James's more inclusive order must include them. The difficulty is that they cannot participate in the negotiation, and we cannot know exactly what they will value. But their status as moral claimants is clear on Jamesian grounds.

This expansion is difficult, uncomfortable, and incomplete. Including animal welfare in the moral calculus means confronting the violence embedded in most human diets. Including future generations means accepting constraints on present consumption. Including ecosystems means acknowledging that the river's claim to flow might outweigh the farmer's claim to irrigate. James offered no algorithm for resolving these conflicts. He offered only the principle: take the demand seriously, weigh it against competing demands, seek the arrangement that satisfies the most with the least sacrifice. The experiment continues.

## The Living Question

James proposed that ethics is an ongoing experiment with no fixed endpoint, and 130 years of subsequent thought have confirmed both the necessity and the difficulty of this vision. We live in a world where moral certainty is weaponized by fundamentalists who claim direct access to divine command, and moral relativism is exploited by cynics who claim that all values are arbitrary constructions. James's experimental ethics offers a third path: take every demand seriously, satisfy as many as possible, accept that trade-offs are unavoidable, and keep working toward the more inclusive order without pretending you have arrived.

This is ethics for adults. It offers no guarantees, no final answers, only the ongoing discipline of moral attention. The difficulty is that attention is costly. Hearing a new demand means acknowledging a new claim on your resources, your time, your commitment. The animal rights activist demanding you change your diet, the climate scientist demanding you reduce your consumption, the philosopher demanding you consider the welfare of beings not yet born—these voices are inconvenient. It would be easier to invoke a moral system that tells you definitively which demands count and which do not.

But James insists there is no such system. The demands are real, and the only honest response is to weigh them. Some will be satisfied, some will not, and you will be responsible for the choice. This is why his ethics is both liberating and demanding. No moral authority can claim your obedience on the grounds that they possess the final truth. But no moral laziness can excuse you from the work of deciding which demands to honor.

The more inclusive order that history struggles toward is not a utopia where all conflicts vanish. It is an arrangement that manages to hear more voices, honor more claims, and inflict less violence on the demands that must be sacrificed. We have made progress. Slavery is abolished, women's suffrage is secured, LGBTQ rights are expanding, animal welfare is entering moral consciousness. But each inclusion reveals new exclusions. Future generations, wild animals, ecosystems, artificial intelligences—the list of claimants grows.

James would say this is as it should be. The experiment is never finished. The philosopher's task is not to discover the final ethical truth but to attend carefully to the demands that arise, to weigh them honestly against each other, and to advocate for arrangements that satisfy as many as possible. The living question is not whether we have reached the more inclusive order. It is whether we are still moving toward it, whether we are still listening for the demands we have not yet learned to hear, and whether we have the courage to revise our settlements when new voices demand to be included. The experiment requires something like faith, but it is not faith in a final answer. It is faith that the work of inclusion matters, that attention is a moral discipline, and that the struggle to satisfy more demands with less sacrifice is worth the effort, even though it will never be complete.
