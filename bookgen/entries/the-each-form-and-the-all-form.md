# The Each-Form and the All-Form

> "Pragmatically interpreted, pluralism or the doctrine that it is many means only that the sundry parts of reality may be externally related. Everything you can think of, however vast or inclusive, has on the pluralistic view a genuinely 'external' environment of some sort or amount. Things are 'with' one another in many ways, but nothing includes everything, or dominates over everything. The word 'and' trails along after every sentence. Something always escapes."

*— A Pluralistic Universe (1909), Lecture IV*

> "The each-form is logically as acceptable and empirically as probable as the all-form. The only clear thing about it is that both are useful in varying degrees for different purposes."

*— A Pluralistic Universe (1909), Lecture VIII*

> "The all-form allows of no growth, for growth is a transition from a less to a more complete state. If the all be perfect, how can the parts grow better? If the all be imperfect, how can it be the all?"

*— A Pluralistic Universe (1909), Lecture VII*

## The Idea Unpacked

James was picking a fight with the dominant philosophical vision of his time. British and American idealism, drawing from Hegel, held that reality is fundamentally one unified whole. This Absolute, or World-Soul, contains everything. Individual things are not genuinely separate; they are aspects or appearances of the One. From this "all-form" perspective, the universe is already complete, a finished totality, and particular experiences are fragments of a perfect whole that we cannot yet see in its entirety.

James found this vision intellectually elegant but humanly intolerable. If the universe is already complete in the Absolute, then nothing is genuinely at stake. Suffering is only apparent, evil is already overcome, and human effort adds nothing to a reality that is already whole. The all-form offers cosmic comfort at the price of making finite life unreal.

Against this, James proposed the "each-form." Reality is fundamentally plural: a collection of particular experiences, partially connected, with genuine independence between parts. The universe is not a totality but a distribution. Things are "with" one another in many ways, but no single perspective gathers them all. The word "and" keeps trailing along. Something always escapes.

This was not mere philosophical preference. The each-form makes room for what mattered most to James: the reality of individual experience, the possibility of genuine novelty, and the unfinished character of existence. If reality is composed of particular experiences that are externally related, then new connections can form, genuine losses can occur, and the future is not already written into the structure of the whole. The each-form allows for growth, because growth requires incompleteness. It allows for risk, because risk requires genuine alternatives. It allows for human effort to matter, because effort contributes to a reality still in the making.

James knew this was a harder vision to hold. The all-form offers the security of completeness; the each-form offers only the adventure of contribution. But he believed the each-form was truer to experience and more honest about the stakes of living. If we take seriously the felt reality of choice, the genuine sting of loss, and the open texture of time, we are living in the each-form, whether we admit it or not.

### Reductionism and Emergence

The each-form and all-form distinction has reappeared in contemporary science as the debate between reductionism and emergence. Reductionism is the all-form in scientific clothing: the view that everything about a system is fully determined by its smallest parts. Given the positions and momenta of all the atoms in your brain, a sufficiently powerful calculator could in principle predict everything about your experience. The higher levels—consciousness, meaning, agency—are not genuinely new; they are just complex configurations of the fundamental.

Strong emergence is the scientific ally of the each-form. It holds that genuinely new properties arise at higher levels that are not predictable from, or reducible to, the behavior of the parts. Philip Anderson's famous 1972 essay "More Is Different" made the case that each level of organization in nature obeys its own laws. The symmetry-breaking that produces a solid from liquid water is not simply complexity; it is a qualitatively new state. Psychology is not just complicated neuroscience, and neuroscience is not just complicated chemistry.

Stuart Kauffman has spent decades arguing that biological organization involves genuinely emergent order that no reductionist framework can fully capture. The biosphere, he argues, is in the "adjacent possible"—constantly creating configurations that could not have been predicted from initial conditions. George Ellis has developed a framework of top-down causation in which higher-level constraints genuinely shape lower-level processes. The whole is not just the sum of the parts; the whole constrains what the parts can do.

The stakes are exactly what James identified: whether novelty is real. If reductionism is true, then everything that will ever happen is implicit in the initial state of the universe plus the laws of physics. The appearance of life, consciousness, and civilization were already latent in the Big Bang. If strong emergence is true, then genuinely new forms arise that could not have been predicted, and the universe is creative, not just complicated. James would have sided with emergence without hesitation. The each-form allows for genuine novelty; the all-form forecloses it.

### The Architecture of Coordination

The each-form versus all-form distinction is not merely metaphysical. It has shaped how we design institutions and technologies. Centralized, top-down systems embody the all-form: one plan, one authority, total coordination from a single point. Distributed, bottom-up systems embody the each-form: local autonomy, emergent coordination through partial connections, no commanding overview.

The internet is an each-form system. No central authority routes all traffic; packets find their way through a network of independent nodes following simple local rules. Wikipedia is an each-form encyclopedia: no editorial board coordinates all contributions; the coherence emerges from millions of partial connections and local improvements. Open-source software develops through distributed collaboration without centralized control, as Eric Raymond chronicled in *The Cathedral and the Bazaar*. Blockchain technologies attempt to coordinate trust without a central ledger. These systems produce large-scale order without a central planner, and they are resilient precisely because no single point of failure can bring them down.

Elinor Ostrom's work on polycentric governance showed that common-pool resources—forests, fisheries, irrigation systems—are often managed most sustainably not by a single central authority but by multiple overlapping local authorities. Polycentricity is the institutional embodiment of the each-form: many centers, partially coordinated, with no commanding unity. Friedrich Hayek argued that market economies work precisely because they aggregate dispersed local knowledge that no central planner could possess. Spontaneous order, he called it. Jamesian pluralism, we might say.

The all-form is not without virtues. Centralized coordination can act decisively, allocate resources efficiently, and prevent duplication. But it cannot accommodate genuine novelty or local knowledge, and it fails catastrophically when the center is wrong. The each-form is messier, slower to reach consensus, and tolerates inefficiency. But it is adaptive, resilient, and capable of surprising innovation. James's metaphysics has institutional implications: how we imagine the universe shapes how we organize ourselves.

### The Unfinished Universe

James insisted that the universe is not complete. "The all-form allows of no growth," he wrote, "for growth is a transition from a less to a more complete state." If reality is already whole in the Absolute, then history is just the unfolding of what was already implicit. Nothing is genuinely at risk because the outcome is guaranteed. The each-form, by contrast, allows for a universe that is still being made, where the future is genuinely open and where outcomes are not yet settled.

Contemporary cosmology is ambiguous on this question. The laws of physics may be fixed, but their historical expression is radically contingent. Lee Smolin has argued for cosmological natural selection: the idea that universes with different physical constants are born from black holes, and those that produce more black holes are more likely to reproduce. On this view, the laws of physics themselves evolve, and the universe is genuinely creative at the deepest level. Reality is not a finished product but an ongoing process.

Stuart Kauffman has developed a parallel vision in biology. Life, he argues, is not just complicated chemistry; it is a fundamentally creative process that continually explores the adjacent possible, producing forms that could not have been predicted from initial conditions. In *Reinventing the Sacred*, Kauffman proposes that nature's creativity—the emergence of genuinely novel forms—is itself worthy of reverence. We do not need a transcendent God to find the sacred; we need only recognize the creativity inherent in becoming.

Roberto Mangabeira Unger and Lee Smolin's *The Singular Universe and the Reality of Time* goes further: they argue that the very laws of nature may evolve over cosmological time. Time is not an illusion, and the universe is not a block. It is a singular, historical process in which genuinely new forms emerge. This is James's vision at cosmological scale: the each-form written into the structure of physics itself.

## The Living Question

The choice between the each-form and the all-form is not merely academic. It shapes how we live. The all-form offers the comfort of completeness: everything is already contained in the whole, nothing is truly lost, nothing is truly at risk, and the cosmic outcome is guaranteed. It is the metaphysical equivalent of insurance. The each-form offers the terror and the dignity of incompleteness: things can genuinely be won or lost, the future is genuinely open, our actions contribute to a reality that is still in the making, and there is no guarantee that it will turn out well.

James chose the each-form because he believed it was truer to experience and because it made human effort significant. But the choice is difficult to sustain. We are drawn to the security of the all-form, to the promise that everything will be all right in the end, that our losses are only apparent, that the Absolute contains and redeems all suffering. The each-form offers no such comfort. It says only: this matters, it is not yet decided, and what you do now will be woven into a reality that is still being made.

Can we live with that openness? Can we find meaning in contribution rather than completion? The all-form says the universe is already finished and we are glimpsing fragments of a perfect whole. The each-form says the universe is unfinished and we are among the makers. One offers the peace of acceptance, the other the dignity of participation. James believed we could choose the each-form without despair, that the unfinished universe was not a tragedy but an invitation. Whether he was right is a question each of us must answer by living.
