# Meliorism

> "Meliorism treats salvation as neither necessary nor impossible. It treats it as a possibility, which becomes more and more of a probability the more numerous the actual conditions of salvation become."
> 
> *— Pragmatism (1907), Lecture VIII: Pragmatism and Religion*

> "The world is all the richer for having a devil in it, so long as we keep our foot upon his neck."
> 
> *— Pragmatism (1907), Lecture VIII: Pragmatism and Religion*

> "It is clear that pragmatism must incline towards meliorism. Some conditions of the world's salvation are actually extant, and she cannot possibly close her eyes to this fact: and should the residual conditions come, salvation would become an accomplished reality. Naturally the terms I use here are exceedingly summary. You may interpret the word 'salvation' in any way you like, and make it as diffuse and distributive, or as climacteric and integral a phenomenon as you please."
> 
> *— Pragmatism (1907), Lecture VIII: Pragmatism and Religion*

## The Idea Unpacked

Meliorism is James's name for the position between optimism and pessimism, but it is not a compromise. It is the most demanding stance a person can take.

The optimist believes the world will be saved no matter what we do. Leibniz's "best of all possible worlds," Hegel's march of Spirit, the technological utopianism that assumes innovation will solve every problem: these are variations on optimism. The optimist can be lazy. If the outcome is guaranteed, effort becomes optional, a way to pass time while history unfolds toward its inevitable triumph.

The pessimist believes the world cannot be saved no matter what we do. Schopenhauer's will-to-suffering, the ecological doomers who say we have already crossed every tipping point, the political fatalists who see power as always self-perpetuating: these are variations on pessimism. The pessimist can be lazy too. If the outcome is foreclosed, effort becomes pointless, a waste of energy better conserved for private consolations.

Only the meliorist must work. The meliorist believes the world can be improved and that the improvement depends partly on our effort. Salvation—however you define it: less suffering, more justice, greater flourishing—is genuinely possible but not guaranteed. The outcome is in doubt, and what we do matters.

This is not a moderate position. It is the most psychologically strenuous position available. It requires action without certainty of success, hope without guarantees, and the willingness to struggle for an outcome that may not arrive. James called this the "strenuous mood," and he believed it was the most authentic and energizing stance a person could take. Meliorism refuses both the comfort of inevitability and the comfort of impossibility. It insists on genuine contingency: the world might get better, and whether it does depends partly on you.

James saw meliorism as the natural ethical stance of pragmatism. Pragmatism makes the meaning of ideas depend on their consequences, and the consequences of meliorism are clear: it motivates action in a way that neither optimism nor pessimism can. The optimist does not need to act. The pessimist cannot afford to. Only the meliorist must choose, again and again, to keep their foot on the devil's neck.

## The 130-Year Conversation

### Effective Altruism and Rational Hope

The effective altruism movement is meliorism in contemporary form. EA begins with the claim that the world's suffering can be substantially reduced through intelligent, sustained effort. Not eliminated—EA is not utopian—but reduced. A child who dies of malaria is a tragedy that could have been prevented for less than the cost of a cup of coffee. Factory farming causes immense suffering that could be diminished through better policies or meat alternatives. Existential risks like engineered pandemics or misaligned artificial intelligence could destroy humanity but might be mitigated through careful research and coordination.

The outcome is not guaranteed. Peter Singer's drowning child thought experiment crystallizes the meliorist dilemma: you can save the child, but you might fail, and even if you succeed, there will be another child drowning tomorrow. William MacAskill's "longtermism" extends the horizon: our actions today shape the far future, but the causal chains are uncertain and the time scales dizzying. Toby Ord's assessment in *The Precipice* is that humanity has a one-in-six chance of extinction this century. One in six. Not inevitable, not impossible. Genuinely in doubt.

EA's focus on measurable impact, cost-effectiveness, and cause prioritization is pragmatic meliorism: which interventions actually work? Where does an additional dollar do the most good? The movement's internal tensions are the tensions James identified. How much should an individual sacrifice? Can systemic problems be solved through individual donations? What counts as effective when the consequences stretch across centuries? These questions have no tidy answers, and meliorism does not promise tidiness. It promises only that the effort is meaningful because the outcome is genuinely open.

The psychological challenge EA faces is James's challenge: can people sustain intense effort toward uncertain goals without burning out or collapsing into either complacency or despair? The evidence is mixed. Some EA practitioners report finding the framework energizing, a way to channel moral concern into concrete action. Others report exhaustion, guilt spirals, or a creeping sense that nothing they do is ever enough. Meliorism is motivating, but it is never comfortable.

### Climate Activism and the Possibility of Catastrophe

Climate change is the defining meliorist test of the twenty-first century. The optimist says technology will solve it: carbon capture, fusion energy, geoengineering. The pessimist says it is already too late: the ice sheets are collapsing, the feedbacks are accelerating, we have squandered our chance. The meliorist says: the outcome is genuinely in doubt, and our actions genuinely matter.

Bill McKibben has been making the meliorist case for decades. The situation is dire, but it is not hopeless. Every tenth of a degree matters. Every coal plant that does not get built, every forest that does not get cleared, every policy that accelerates the transition to renewables: these make a difference. The difference between two degrees of warming and three degrees is not academic. It is the difference between manageable disruption and civilizational collapse, between millions displaced and billions.

Kate Marvel, a climate scientist, writes about living with uncertainty. The models give us probabilities, not certainties. The future depends on what we do, but also on tipping points we may not fully understand, feedback loops that may be stronger or weaker than we think, political and technological developments we cannot predict. This is meliorism's terrain: acting under conditions of irreducible uncertainty, where inaction guarantees disaster but action does not guarantee success.

Rebecca Solnit's *Hope in the Dark* is a meliorist manifesto. Hope is not the belief that everything will be all right. It is the belief that what we do matters even when the outcome is uncertain. Solnit catalogs the unpredicted victories: apartheid fell, the Berlin Wall came down, marriage equality spread faster than anyone expected. These were not inevitable. They happened because people acted as though their actions mattered, even when success seemed unlikely.

Jonathan Safran Foer's *We Are the Weather* makes the intimate case. Individual choices—what we eat, how we travel, what we buy—matter, not because they alone will solve the problem but because they are part of the solution. The meliorist does not get to say, "My contribution is too small to matter." Every contribution either helps or does not help. The cumulative effect of millions of small choices is not small.

The question meliorism poses to climate activism is whether hope can be sustained over the decades-long effort required. Optimism burns out when predictions do not materialize. Pessimism burns out because it sees no point. Meliorism offers no emotional relief, only the steady, effortful engagement with a problem whose outcome depends partly on what we do. Whether this is psychologically sustainable is not yet clear.

### Progress Studies and the Evidence for Improvement

Steven Pinker's *Enlightenment Now* and Hans Rosling's *Factfulness* present meliorism's empirical case. The world has improved dramatically over the past two centuries. Extreme poverty has plummeted. Child mortality has dropped. Violence has declined. Literacy has spread. Life expectancy has soared. These are not opinions; they are measurements.

Pinker argues that this progress is the result of Enlightenment values: reason, science, humanism. The world got better because people worked to make it better, and the methods that worked—evidence-based policy, technological innovation, institutions that protect rights—can continue to work. This is meliorism: progress is possible, and it happens through effort. But progress is not automatic. It requires defending and extending the conditions that made it possible.

Hans Rosling emphasized the cognitive bias toward negativity. People systematically underestimate progress because bad news is dramatic and good news is incremental. A plane crash makes headlines; the steady decline in aviation fatalities does not. Rosling's factfulness is meliorism's optimism: the evidence shows we can make things better, and recognizing that we have made things better can sustain the motivation to continue.

But the meliorist case is contested. Jason Hickel argues in *The Divide* that global poverty metrics are misleading, that aggregate progress masks deepening inequality and ongoing exploitation. Amia Srinivasan and other critics note that metrics reflect values: what counts as progress depends on what you measure, and the choice of metrics can obscure injustice. If GDP grows while ecosystems collapse, is that progress? If life expectancy increases while meaning and connection erode, have we improved the world?

James would have welcomed this debate. Meliorism is not naive boosterism. It does not claim that everything is getting better or that all change is progress. It claims only that improvement is possible and that the improvement depends on what we do. Past progress does not guarantee future progress; it demonstrates that progress is achievable. The question of what constitutes progress—which values should guide our efforts—is itself part of the meliorist project. We must decide not only how to improve the world but what improvement means.

Rutger Bregman's *Humankind* adds a psychological dimension. If humans are fundamentally decent, as Bregman argues, then the meliorist project is plausible: we are capable of building better systems, solving coordination problems, extending moral concern. If humans are fundamentally selfish or tribal, the meliorist must work against the grain of human nature, and the effort becomes harder but not impossible. James believed that our beliefs about human nature become self-fulfilling. The meliorist who acts as though people can cooperate makes cooperation more likely. The pessimist who acts as though people cannot cooperate makes cooperation less likely. The world we get depends partly on the world we expect.

## The Living Question

Meliorism asks the hardest question: can you sustain effort toward a goal whose achievement is uncertain?

This is not a rhetorical question. It is the question your life must answer. The optimist does not need effort because the outcome is guaranteed. The pessimist does not need effort because the outcome is foreclosed. Only the meliorist must work without knowing whether the work will succeed.

James called this the strenuous mood, and he believed it was the most authentic and energizing stance a person could take. But authenticity is not the same as sustainability. Can a person live for decades in the strenuous mood without collapsing into either complacency or despair? Can a movement sustain itself on the fuel of uncertain hope?

The evidence from activism, from social change, from long-term efforts to reduce suffering or expand justice, is that meliorism is both possible and difficult. Some people sustain it. They keep their foot on the devil's neck for a lifetime, knowing the devil might slip free, knowing the struggle might fail, and they act anyway. Others burn out, or retreat into private life, or harden into cynicism as a defense against caring too much.

The climate crisis will test whether meliorism can scale. It is one thing to sustain effort toward a goal that might be achieved in your lifetime. It is another to sustain effort toward a goal whose partial success will still mean loss, displacement, extinction. The meliorist stance toward climate change is: we do not know how much we can save, but we know that what we save depends on what we do. Is that enough? Can people organize their lives around that? Can societies?

James thought meliorism was psychologically energizing because it made action meaningful. When the outcome depends on you, every choice matters. But mattering is not always comfortable. It means you cannot excuse yourself. You cannot say the problem is too big or the solution too distant. You must choose, again and again, whether to act or to retreat.

The deepest question meliorism raises may be about hope itself. Is hope sustainable when it is not coupled to certainty? Can you hope for what might not happen? James believed you could, that the very uncertainty made hope more real, because it meant your hope was not a prediction but a commitment. You hope by acting as though the outcome you hope for is possible, and your action makes it more possible. The world might get better. Whether it does depends partly on whether you believe it can and act accordingly.
