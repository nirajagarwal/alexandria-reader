# Tearing

***Salt Water Rising to the Surface***

> **Category:** Response | Expression

The lacrimal glands produce tears constantly, a thin film that bathes the surface of the eye. Three layers: lipid on top to prevent evaporation, aqueous in the middle for oxygen and nutrients, mucin at the base to help it spread. The eye blinks, the eyelid sweeps across, the tear film renews itself. This happens every few seconds, all day, without thought. But sometimes the system floods. The glands receive a signal—irritation, emotion, injury—and production surges. Tears overwhelm the drainage ducts at the inner corner of each eye. They spill over the lower lid and run down the face. The body washing something away, or marking something within. Salt water rising to the surface, visible to others, felt by the one who cries.

## Basal, Reflex, Emotional

Three kinds of tears, three triggers, one gland. Basal tears maintain the cornea, produced at a steady rate of about one microliter per minute. Reflex tears answer irritation: smoke, wind, onion vapor, a speck of dust. The trigeminal nerve detects the threat and signals the lacrimal gland to flood the eye, washing away the irritant. These tears arrive within seconds, volume ten times normal, clearing the foreign matter through sheer flow. Emotional tears follow a different pathway. Stress, grief, joy, pain, or empathy activate the limbic system, which communicates with the autonomic nervous system, which triggers the lacrimal gland. The chemical composition differs. Emotional tears contain higher concentrations of protein, hormones, and endorphins. The body releasing something internal along with the salt water.

## The Gland and Drainage

The lacrimal gland sits in the upper outer corner of each eye socket, beneath the brow. It produces the aqueous layer, the bulk of the tear. Smaller glands in the eyelid contribute mucin and lipid. Tears spread across the cornea with each blink, drawn by surface tension and the sweeping motion of the lid. They collect at the inner corner, draining through two small openings called puncta, one in each lid. From there, tears flow through the lacrimal sac and down the nasolacrimal duct into the nasal cavity. This is why crying produces a runny nose: tears exit through the same passage. When production exceeds drainage capacity, tears spill over the lid margin and run down the cheek. The system designed for a trickle, overwhelmed by a flood.

## Newborn to Elder

Infants cry without tears for the first few weeks of life. The lacrimal glands function, producing basal tears, but the volume remains low and emotional crying does not yet trigger the reflex. True tearing begins around two to three months. By childhood, the full range appears: basal, reflex, emotional. Adolescence brings hormonal changes that affect tear composition and frequency. Women report crying more often than men, a difference linked to testosterone, which may suppress the lacrimal gland, and prolactin, which may enhance it. Older adults experience dry eye as gland function declines and tear production drops. The film thins, evaporates faster, leaves the surface exposed. The paradox: elderly eyes water more, but from inadequate drainage rather than excess production. The tears that remain cannot do their work.

## When the Film Fails

Dry eye disease: the lacrimal gland produces too little, or the tears evaporate too quickly, or the film lacks sufficient lipid to seal the surface. The cornea dries, becomes inflamed. Vision blurs. The eye feels gritty, burns. Reflex tearing may kick in, flooding the eye, but these tears lack the proper composition and do not restore the film. Sjögren's syndrome attacks the lacrimal gland directly, an autoimmune condition that destroys moisture-producing tissue. The eyes dry completely, the surface ulcerates. Excessive tearing, epiphora, occurs when ducts block or narrow. Tears pool and spill, not from emotion but from plumbing failure. Infection, tumor, or scar tissue obstructing the nasolacrimal duct. Chronic tearing inflames the skin of the cheek. The face wet, the eye irritated, the drainage system collapsed.

## The Chemical Release

Emotional tears contain leucine enkephalin, an endogenous opioid peptide, a natural painkiller. They carry away cortisol and other stress hormones, draining them from the bloodstream through the lacrimal gland. Crying may function as a biological reset, lowering stress markers, calming the nervous system. Studies show mood improvement after crying, though the mechanism remains unclear. The tears themselves, or the social response they provoke, or the release of pressure in the act. Reflex tears wash out irritants but also deliver antibodies, lysozyme, lactoferrin—proteins that kill bacteria and protect the corneal surface. Emotional and reflex tears overlap in function but diverge in composition. The body deploying the same fluid for different purposes, the eye weeping to cleanse or to signal.

## Ritual and Signal

Tears mark grief across every culture, though the expression varies. Public crying acceptable in some contexts, suppressed in others. Funerary rites incorporate weeping, keening, controlled displays of sorrow. Tears signal vulnerability, authenticity, emotional overwhelm. They elicit empathy, caretaking, or discomfort depending on context. Infants cry to summon help, the sound and sight triggering adult response. Adults cry for similar reasons, though the need is less concrete. Tears of joy appear at weddings, births, reunions. The body responding to positive emotional intensity with the same reflex as sorrow. Some cultures distinguish: crocodile tears, false crying, manipulation. Others ritualize: professional mourners hired to weep at funerals, their tears commodified. The act universal, the meaning constructed.

## The Visible Overflow

Tears break containment. They appear on the face where internal states usually remain hidden. The body betraying emotion, or announcing it. Children learn to suppress crying, or to cry strategically. Adults cry alone, or in front of intimates, or at films where the darkness hides the face. Tears blur vision, distort the world. They leave salt tracks on the cheeks, redness around the eyes, swelling of the lids. The face after crying is unmistakable. The tears themselves transparent, but the evidence lingers. Tissue pressed to the eyes, the nose blown, the breathing unsteady. Something rose to the surface and spilled over. The lacrimal gland flooded the system, and the body showed what it held.
