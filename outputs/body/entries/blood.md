# Blood

**1. The River of Cells:** Five liters flow through an adult body — not a liquid but a suspension, cells carried in plasma like boats on a river. Red cells outnumber white 700 to 1. Each red cell lives 120 days, travels the circuit once per minute, and dies in the spleen where macrophages tear it apart for parts. Your bone marrow makes two million new red cells every second. The cells themselves contain no nucleus, no DNA, no machinery for self-repair. They are disposable ferries, built to carry oxygen and nothing else.

**2. The Incan Transfusion:** In 1492, Pope Innocent VIII lay dying. His physicians drained blood from three ten-year-old boys and fed it to him by mouth. The boys died. The pope died. No one understood that blood drunk is merely protein digested. The first successful transfusion came in 1665: Richard Lower kept a dog alive by connecting its veins to another dog's arteries. Human-to-human transfusion followed in 1818. Most recipients died from immune reactions until Karl Landsteiner discovered blood types in 1901, work that earned him the Nobel Prize twenty-nine years later.

**3. The Alphabet of Antigens:** Type O can give to anyone. Type AB can receive from anyone. These labels describe antigens — protein markers on red cell surfaces. Your immune system memorizes your markers at birth and attacks any blood cell wearing the wrong ones. Beyond A, B, and O lie thirty-five other blood group systems with names like Kidd, Duffy, and Diego. Bombay blood, rarest of all, lacks the H antigen that builds A and B. Bombay donors can give only to other Bombay recipients. Fewer than 20,000 people worldwide carry it.

**4. The Horseshoe Crab Bargain:** Human blood clots when exposed to bacterial toxins. Horseshoe crab blood does something stranger: it turns to gel around bacteria, walling them off. In 1956, Frederick Bang discovered this. Within two decades, pharmaceutical companies were harvesting crab blood to test every injectable drug for contamination. A quart of horseshoe crab blood sells for $15,000. Each year, 500,000 crabs are caught, bled — losing thirty percent of their blood — and returned to the sea. Thirty percent die afterward. The rest survive with blue blood half-gone.

**5. The Doping Arms Race:** Blood carries oxygen. More red cells mean more oxygen means more endurance. Athletes train at altitude to force their marrow to overproduce cells. Some inject erythropoietin, the hormone that triggers red cell production. Some store their own blood months before competition, then transfuse it back before the event. Testing agencies measure red cell count and hemoglobin levels, but the margins are narrow. Normal human variation overlaps with doping levels. Innocent athletes test positive. Guilty ones engineer their timing to stay beneath thresholds.

**6. The Taster's Trade:** Blood tastes of iron because hemoglobin holds iron at its core. Each molecule grips four iron atoms. Four oxygen molecules bind to the iron, ride through arteries, release in capillaries. The iron itself never touches oxygen — it hides in a pocket of protein, prevented from rusting. But break a cell, expose hemoglobin to air, and the iron oxidizes. Blood in the mouth tastes metallic because saliva breaks cells. Blood in the body has no taste. It moves in darkness, iron locked away, oxygen handed off in the space between heartbeats.
