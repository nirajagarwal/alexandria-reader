# Seizing

***When the Brain Loses Control***

> **Category:** Extreme

A seizure is the brain's electrical system misfiring in synchrony. Neurons that normally fire in coordinated patterns discharge together in overwhelming waves. The body responds to this neurological storm with involuntary movements, altered consciousness, or both. What the person experiences depends on where in the brain the abnormal activity begins and how far it spreads. Some seizures announce themselves with warning sensations—an aura of strange smell, rising dread, or déjà vu. Others strike without prelude. The episode may last seconds or minutes. Afterward, the brain requires time to restore order. Confusion, exhaustion, or sleep follows. The person often retains no memory of the event itself, only the void where time should be and the witnesses' accounts of what their body did without them.

## The Electrical Cascade

The brain operates through precise electrical signaling. Neurons maintain a careful balance of excitation and inhibition, firing individually or in small groups to create thought, movement, sensation. A seizure breaks this balance. Excessive excitation or insufficient inhibition causes nearby neurons to activate together. The synchronized discharge spreads like a wave across cortical tissue. In focal seizures, this storm remains localized—one hand jerks, or awareness narrows to a single overwhelming sensation. In generalized seizures, both hemispheres participate from the start. The entire brain loses its normal rhythm.

## Tonic-Clonic Convulsion

The grand mal seizure, now called generalized tonic-clonic, follows a recognizable sequence. The tonic phase begins with sudden muscle rigidity throughout the body. Arms flex or extend. Legs stiffen. The person falls if standing. Air forced from lungs through a rigid larynx produces a cry or groan. Breathing stops during this phase. The jaw clamps shut. After ten to twenty seconds, the clonic phase begins. Muscles contract and relax in violent alternation. Limbs jerk symmetrically. The head may strike the ground. Saliva pools in the mouth, sometimes mixed with blood from a bitten tongue or cheek. The bladder may release. The person remains completely unconscious throughout. When the electrical storm subsides, muscles relax. Breathing resumes, often labored. The postictal state begins: profound confusion, deep sleep, or gradual return to awareness over minutes or hours.

## Absence and Awareness

Not all seizures involve convulsion. Absence seizures suspend consciousness without warning. The person stops mid-sentence, mid-step, mid-gesture. Eyes may flutter or stare. Ten to twenty seconds pass. Awareness returns as suddenly as it left, the person resuming activity with no memory of the pause. These seizures occur primarily in children, sometimes dozens per day. Teachers and parents mistake them for daydreaming until the pattern reveals itself. Focal aware seizures preserve consciousness while producing strange experiences. The temporal lobe's involvement generates intense emotions without cause—terror, ecstasy, profound familiarity. Visual cortex seizures create geometric hallucinations. Motor cortex involvement causes repetitive movements: lip smacking, hand rubbing, walking in circles. The person remains aware but cannot stop the behavior.

## Status Epilepticus

A seizure that will not end is a medical emergency. Status epilepticus occurs when the electrical storm persists beyond five minutes or when seizures occur in rapid succession without recovery between. The brain cannot restore normal function. Neurons fire continuously, depleting energy reserves. Body temperature rises. Blood chemistry shifts toward acidosis. Muscle breakdown releases proteins that can damage kidneys. Without intervention, permanent brain damage or death follows. Emergency protocols prioritize stopping the seizure with benzodiazepines, then identifying the cause. Fever, infection, stroke, poisoning, or medication withdrawal can trigger status epilepticus in people with no history of epilepsy. In established epilepsy, missed medications or alcohol use often precipitate the crisis.

## From Infancy to Age

Febrile seizures affect three percent of children, typically between six months and five years. High fever triggers the event; the developing brain's threshold for seizure is lower than an adult's. The child's body stiffens or jerks for one to two minutes. Parents rush to emergency rooms. The seizure is usually benign, causing no lasting harm, though it terrifies witnesses. Most children outgrow this vulnerability. Epilepsy can begin at any age but shows peaks in early childhood and after sixty. In the elderly, stroke becomes the leading cause. Scar tissue from dead brain cells creates electrical abnormalities. The first seizure may occur months or years after the stroke itself. Brain tumors, dementia, and head trauma also increase seizure risk with age. The postictal confusion that follows a seizure in an elderly person may be mistaken for worsening dementia.

## When Control Fails

Epilepsy is not a single disease but a condition defined by recurrent unprovoked seizures. Causes include genetic mutations affecting ion channels, structural brain abnormalities from development or injury, infections like meningitis, or scarring from previous trauma. In half of cases, no cause is identified. Antiseizure medications work by enhancing inhibition or reducing excitation in neural circuits. They control seizures in seventy percent of people with epilepsy. For the remainder, seizures continue despite multiple medication trials. Some undergo surgery to remove the seizure focus if it can be localized and is not in critical brain regions. Others receive vagus nerve stimulators or responsive neurostimulation devices that detect abnormal electrical activity and deliver pulses to abort seizures before they generalize. The unpredictability of seizures—not knowing when the next will strike—shapes daily life more than the seizures themselves.

## The Cultural Mark

Seizures have been interpreted as divine possession, demonic invasion, sacred disease, and moral failing. Ancient texts describe epilepsy with accuracy but misunderstand cause. Hippocrates argued against supernatural explanations, locating the problem in the brain. Medieval Europe associated seizures with witchcraft or sin. The term "epilepsy" comes from Greek, meaning "to seize upon"—as if by an external force. People with epilepsy faced institutionalization, forced sterilization, and legal barriers to marriage into the twentieth century. The diagnosis carried such stigma that many concealed it. Modern understanding of epilepsy as an electrical disorder of the brain has reduced but not eliminated this burden. Workplace discrimination persists. Driving restrictions isolate. The visible loss of control during a seizure marks a person as different, as unreliable, as potentially dangerous despite evidence to the contrary.

## Witnessing Protocol

The bystander can do little to stop a seizure but much to prevent injury. Do not restrain the person. Do not force anything into the mouth—the tongue cannot be swallowed, and teeth can break objects inserted to prevent biting. Move sharp or hard objects away from the seizing person. Turn them onto their side if possible to keep the airway clear. Note the time the seizure begins. Most resolve in one to two minutes. If the seizure continues beyond five minutes, or if a second seizure begins before the person regains consciousness, call emergency services. If the person is pregnant, injured, or diabetic, or if this is their first known seizure, call immediately. When the seizure ends, stay with the person as they recover. Speak calmly. Do not offer food or drink until they are fully alert. Expect confusion, fatigue, or embarrassment. The person may not remember falling or losing control. What remains is the knowledge that it happened and the uncertainty of when it will happen again.
