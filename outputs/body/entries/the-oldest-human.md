# The Oldest Human

**1. The Claimants:** Jeanne Calment of France died in 1997 at 122 years and 164 days, the longest verified human lifespan. She met Vincent van Gogh as a teenager, witnessed the Eiffel Tower's construction, and outlived her daughter and grandson. Japan's Kane Tanaka reached 119 in 2022. But verification grows uncertain before birth certificates became standard — many "supercentenarians" from remote regions lack documentation solid enough to satisfy gerontologists. The oldest verified man, Jiroemon Kimura, died at 116, highlighting a pattern: nearly all extreme-age survivors are women.

**2. The Survival Arithmetic:** Past 110, you enter a statistical wilderness. Roughly one in 1,000 centenarians becomes a supercentenarian. Past 115, the numbers thin to dozens worldwide. Death rates don't accelerate past 105 — they plateau at about 50% annually, as if mortality reaches a ceiling. This "late-life mortality plateau" suggests human lifespan may lack a hard limit, though no one has breached 130. The body becomes a coin flip: survive this year, flip again. Each year narrows the field until one person remains, temporarily, the oldest.

**3. The Cellular Countdown:** Human cells divide roughly 50 times before stopping, a limit called the Hayflick number. Telomeres — protective caps on chromosome ends — shorten with each division until cells enter senescence or die. Supercentenarians show no special telomere length, but their bodies manage cellular damage with unusual efficiency. Their immune systems clear senescent cells better. Their tissues resist inflammation longer. They don't age slowly so much as they age cleanly, accumulating less biological debris as decades pass. The oldest human isn't necessarily the strongest, but the tidiest at the molecular level.

**4. The Genetic Lottery:** Studies of Ashkenazi Jewish centenarians revealed overrepresented variants in genes regulating insulin and growth hormone signaling. Lower IGF-1 levels correlate with longer life across species. But genetics explains perhaps 25% of longevity variation — the rest is chance and circumstance. Calment smoked until 117, ate two pounds of chocolate weekly. Kimura ate small portions and worked until 90. No single behavior predicts extreme age. The centenarian's secret, frustratingly, is mostly having centenarian parents, then living somewhere without famine, war, or endemic disease during childhood.

**5. The Verification Crisis:** Birth certificates became common in Europe around 1870, in many countries much later. Before that, baptismal records, census documents, and family bibles serve as proof. Researchers have debunked numerous age claims: a "132-year-old" Indonesian man whose documents actually described his father; a Soviet woman whose daughter may have assumed her identity after her death. The oldest reliably documented person ever born is probably Calment, but the true record-holder could have lived in an era before records, their age unknowable, their name forgotten.

**6. The Final Days:** Calment went blind, nearly deaf, needed a wheelchair after breaking her femur at 114. But she kept her mind — gave interviews, made jokes, released a rap record at 120. The body fails in pieces: hearing goes, then vision, then mobility, while consciousness often persists. Some supercentenarians die of specific causes — pneumonia, falls — but many simply stop, organs shutting down in sequence with no clear trigger. Medical records use terms like "senescence" or "multiple organ failure." The oldest human dies because everything, finally, is old.
