# Milk

**1. The First Food:** Milk is blood, filtered. Mammary glands pull water, proteins, fats, and sugars from the bloodstream and recombine them into a liquid designed for infant survival. The composition changes constantly — colostrum in the first days is thick with antibodies, later milk thins and sweetens. Human milk contains over 200 different oligosaccharides, complex sugars the infant cannot even digest. They exist solely to feed beneficial bacteria in the baby's gut, building an immune system from the inside out.

**2. The Mammalian Invention:** Milk likely began as sweat. Early proto-mammals secreted nutrient-rich moisture through skin glands to keep eggs moist. Offspring that licked these secretions survived better. Over millions of years, dedicated glands evolved, eggs became internalized, and lactation became the defining feature of an entire class of animals. Monotremes — platypuses and echidnas — still have no nipples. They sweat milk through patches of skin, and their young lap it from the fur.

**3. The Tolerance Mutation:** Most humans stop producing lactase — the enzyme that digests milk sugar — after weaning. They become lactose intolerant, as all mammals are meant to be. But around 7,500 years ago in Central Europe, a mutation appeared that kept the lactase gene switched on into adulthood. It spread explosively. Populations that herded cattle and could drink milk year-round had a caloric advantage that shaped empires. Today the mutation exists in roughly 35% of humans, concentrated in Northern Europe and parts of Africa and the Middle East.

**4. The Wet Nurse Economy:** For millennia, wealthy women outsourced lactation. Wet nurses were hired, traded, and regulated by law. The Roman Empire required contracts specifying diet and behavior. Medieval guilds set prices by breast size. In 18th century France, police bureaus matched nurses to families, keeping registries of breast health and moral character. The practice created a shadow economy — rural women nursed urban infants while their own children were fed pap, a paste of bread and water. Infant mortality among nursed-out children was catastrophically high.

**5. The Witch's Mark:** In early modern Europe, any unusual nipple could condemn a woman. Inquisitors searched accused witches for "supernumerary teats" — extra nipples used to suckle demons and familiars. Moles, skin tags, hemorrhoids, and natural anatomical variations were recorded as evidence. Approximately 1-2% of humans are born with polymastia (extra breast tissue) or polythelia (extra nipples), vestigial remnants of the milk lines that run from armpit to groin in mammalian embryos. Witch-hunters saw Satan's signature.

**6. The Kinship Fluid:** Milk creates family across species. The Mongols drank mare's milk fermented into kumis, considering it a gift from the sky. Islamic law recognizes milk kinship — a child nursed by a woman becomes her child in law, forbidden from marrying her biological children. The Toda people of India practiced wet-nursing by buffalo, believing the animal's milk made children strong and bound them to the herd. Milk banks now store human milk for premature infants, creating anonymous bonds between strangers, one body feeding another's child across the distance of freezers and hospitals.
