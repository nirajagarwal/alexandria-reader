# The Bruise

**1. The Bloom:** A bruise is blood outside its channels. Capillaries rupture, and red cells flood the spaces between tissue fibers. The body reads this as damage and sends platelets to seal the breach, white cells to clear debris. What you see through skin is pooled hemoglobin changing color as it breaks down — red to purple to green to yellow — a chemical cascade visible through translucent layers. The spectrum follows iron's transformation: fresh and oxygenated, then deoxygenated, then metabolized into biliverdin and bilirubin. A bruise is a wound you can watch heal in real time.

**2. The Mongolian Blue:** Eighty percent of East Asian infants are born with slate-gray patches across their lower backs and buttocks — melanocytes trapped in deep dermal layers during fetal development. Western doctors once mistook these for bruises, evidence of abuse. The spots fade by school age as melanin migrates upward, but the historical confusion led to children removed from homes, parents investigated. In 1883, German anthropologist Erwin Bälz named them after Mongolian populations, cementing a racial category onto a trait found across ancestry groups. The medical term is now dermal melanocytosis.

**3. The Color Wheel:** Forensic pathologists date bruises by hue — red means hours old, yellow means healing. But the method is unreliable. A 2016 review of bruise-aging studies found no consistent color timeline. Depth matters more than time: surface bruises yellow faster than deep ones. Skin tone obscures the spectrum. Blood thinners alter the pattern. The belief that you can timestamp violence by its colors has helped convict the innocent and free the guilty. Courts still admit bruise-dating as evidence despite decades of contradictory data.

**4. The易经 Mark:** In traditional Chinese medicine, gua sha practitioners scrape skin with smooth stones or coins until petechiae bloom — thousands of pinpoint hemorrhages that look like road rash. The redness, they say, draws sha (stagnant blood) to the surface, releasing toxins and restoring qi flow. Modern analysis shows the technique does increase microcirculation and activates anti-inflammatory pathways, though not through mystical channels. The bruises last three to five days. Olympic athletes appeared at Beijing 2008 and Rio 2016 with gua sha marks across their shoulders, turning ancient therapy into sports-medicine curiosity.

**5. The Spontaneous:** Some people bruise from nothing — a brush against a doorframe, turning in sleep, the pressure of tight socks. Von Willebrand disease affects one in a thousand, making blood slower to clot. Hemophilia runs deeper and rarer. Vitamin C deficiency weakens capillary walls; scurvy sailors bruised at the touch. Long-term corticosteroid use thins skin until subcutaneous blood shows through like ink through tissue paper. The medical term is ecchymosis when it's large, purpura when it's widespread, petechiae when it's pinpoint. Three words for blood where it shouldn't be.

**6. The Cadaver Stain:** After death, red cells settle with gravity. Blood pools in whatever part of the body lies lowest, creating lividity — purple-red patches that look like bruises but aren't. They appear within two hours and fix permanently after eight as cells hemolyze and stain tissue. Forensic investigators use lividity patterns to determine if a body was moved after death. A corpse found face-down with lividity on its back was repositioned. But lividity can lie too: tight clothing creates pale pressure points that mimic ligature marks, turning elastic waistbands into false evidence of binding.
