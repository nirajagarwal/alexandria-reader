# Laughing

***The Body's Involuntary Release***

> **Category:** Expression

Laughter begins as a series of short, spasmodic exhalations, each lasting about seventy-five milliseconds. The diaphragm and intercostal muscles contract in rapid succession, forcing air across the vocal cords in rhythmic bursts. The larynx opens wide between contractions, creating the characteristic staccato sound. Meanwhile, facial muscles activate in a precise pattern: the zygomatic major pulls the mouth corners upward, the orbicularis oculi crinkles the eyes. This cascade spreads through the torso, shaking the shoulders, sometimes doubling the body forward. The entire event occurs outside voluntary control. Once triggered, laughter runs its course. The brain's motor cortex does not command these contractions. They originate in the brainstem, in ancient circuits shared with all social mammals. For fifteen to twenty seconds, the body performs a response older than speech.

## The Brainstem's Command

Laughter emerges from the periaqueductal gray, a region surrounding the cerebral aqueduct deep in the midbrain. This structure coordinates involuntary vocalizations: crying, gasping, moaning. When activated—by humor, surprise, social cues, or sometimes nothing identifiable—it sends signals down to the respiratory centers in the medulla. These centers override normal breathing rhythm. The expiratory muscles receive rapid-fire commands. Inhalation becomes shallow and irregular. Exhalation dominates, each burst shorter and more forceful than ordinary breath. The vocal cords resist the airflow just enough to vibrate, producing sound. This is not learned behavior. Infants born deaf laugh on the same schedule as hearing children, with the same acoustic structure. The circuitry is hardwired.

## Contagion Without Thought

Human laughter spreads through groups automatically. The sound enters the ear, travels to the auditory cortex, then activates the premotor cortex and the supplementary motor area. These regions prepare the body to laugh in response, often before conscious awareness registers what is funny. Brain imaging shows that hearing laughter activates the listener's facial muscles within milliseconds. Groups synchronize their laughter, each person's rhythm influencing the others. This occurs even when the content is not understood, even when nothing is objectively humorous. Laughter in one body triggers the cascade in nearby bodies. The mechanism predates language. Primates show the same pattern: one individual's pant-hoot vocalization spreads through the troop. Social cohesion built on shared muscle contractions.

## Oxygen Debt and Tears

Sustained laughter creates physiological strain. The rapid exhalations expel carbon dioxide faster than inhalations replace oxygen. Blood oxygen saturation drops slightly. The heart rate increases, sometimes to one hundred twenty beats per minute. Blood pressure rises. The abdominal muscles ache from repeated contraction. If laughter continues beyond a minute, the diaphragm may spasm, causing hiccups or a sharp pain below the ribs. The lacrimal glands activate, producing tears that spill from the eyes. The exact trigger for these tears remains unclear. One hypothesis suggests overflow from facial nerve stimulation. Another points to pressure changes in the skull affecting the glands. The body blurs the line between joy and distress. The same tears flow.

## Newborn to Elder

Infants produce their first genuine laughter around three and a half months, usually in response to physical stimulation: gentle tickling, blown raspberries, sudden movements. By six months, social laughter emerges. The baby laughs when the caregiver laughs, establishing reciprocal exchange. Toddlers laugh more than any other age group, averaging three hundred times per day. Adolescents laugh about half as often. Adults average fifteen to twenty instances daily. The frequency declines with age, though the capacity remains. Elderly individuals laugh as readily as young adults when the context supports it. The vocal quality changes. The larynx stiffens with age, pitching the sound higher. The diaphragm weakens, reducing volume and duration. But the brainstem circuits persist, unchanged.

## When It Fails

Gelastic seizures produce uncontrollable laughter without mirth. A lesion or electrical disturbance in the hypothalamus triggers the brainstem directly, bypassing all emotional input. The person laughs for minutes, unable to stop, often distressed by the involuntary response. Pseudobulbar affect, caused by stroke or neurodegenerative disease, creates sudden outbursts of laughter or crying disconnected from emotion. The descending pathways that inhibit brainstem vocalization centers are damaged. The raw reflexes surface without regulation. In severe depression, laughter disappears entirely. The periaqueductal gray remains intact, but the prefrontal cortex no longer activates it. The capacity exists; the trigger is absent. Reemergence of spontaneous laughter marks recovery more reliably than self-reported mood.

## Ritual and Power

Humans have always separated permitted from forbidden laughter. Medieval European courts designated official fools, licensed to provoke laughter in controlled settings. Religious orders imposed silence, treating unsolicited laughter as spiritual failure. Military training suppresses laughter during formation, conditioning recruits to override the brainstem response. Dictatorships monitor public laughter. The sound carries information about group sentiment that words can obscure. Laughter at the wrong moment, at the wrong target, becomes evidence of dissent. Neuroscience confirms this intuition: laughter cannot be fully faked. Voluntary laughter activates different brain regions than spontaneous laughter, produces different acoustic signatures. The body betrays what the mind attempts to conceal. Listeners detect the difference without conscious analysis.

## The Shared Spasm

Laughter occurs most often in the presence of others. Solitary laughter is rare, usually prompted by media designed to simulate social interaction: recorded voices, written jokes, screened performances. The brainstem requires social context. Studies using laugh tracks confirm this: audiences laugh more readily, more often, and longer when they hear others laughing, even when they know the laughter is artificial. The body does not evaluate authenticity before responding. It registers the sound and initiates the cascade. Human conversation contains far more laughter than humor. People laugh to signal agreement, to soften criticism, to acknowledge hierarchy, to bridge awkward silence. Most laughter follows statements that are not jokes. The sound functions as social lubrication, coordinating bodies in real time. Shared rhythm, shared breath, the brief collapse of separateness.
