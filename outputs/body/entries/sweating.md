# Sweating

***Water streaming from two million pores***

> **Category:** Response

The body makes water and pushes it through the skin. Two to four million sweat glands cover the human surface, more densely on palms, soles, forehead, and armpits. The eccrine glands respond to heat, producing clear, odorless fluid that spreads across the skin and evaporates, drawing warmth away from the blood beneath. The apocrine glands, concentrated in specific zones, activate with emotion and exertion, releasing a thicker secretion that bacteria transform into scent. This is the body's primary cooling system, refined over millions of years. Humans sweat more profusely than any other primate. A person can lose two liters per hour in extreme heat or effort, the skin becoming a river. Without this system, core temperature would climb toward lethal thresholds within minutes of hard work or high heat. The body makes its own rain, or dies.

## The Two Systems

Eccrine glands develop in the womb and cover nearly the entire body surface. Each consists of a coiled tube deep in the dermis, connected to a duct that opens at a pore. When the hypothalamus detects rising core temperature, it signals these glands to secrete. The fluid they produce is mostly water with small amounts of salt, urea, and other metabolic byproducts. Apocrine glands emerge at puberty, clustering in armpits, groin, and around the nipples. They connect to hair follicles rather than opening directly onto skin. Their secretion contains proteins and lipids. Fresh apocrine sweat has no smell. Only when skin bacteria metabolize these compounds does the characteristic odor develop. Both systems operate continuously at low levels, even in rest.

## The Cooling Mechanism

Water on skin evaporates, phase-changing from liquid to vapor. This transformation requires energy, which it draws from the warmth of the skin itself. Each gram of evaporated sweat removes about 0.58 kilocalories of heat. Blood vessels near the surface dilate during heat stress, bringing more warm blood close to where sweat will cool it. The cooled blood returns to the core, lowering internal temperature. This system works efficiently only in low humidity. When air is already saturated with water vapor, sweat cannot evaporate. It accumulates on skin, drips, and fails to cool. The body continues producing it anyway, losing water without thermal benefit. In dry heat, a person might sweat heavily without noticing, the fluid vanishing as soon as it emerges.

## The Sensation

Sweat begins as a prickling awareness, often at the hairline or upper lip. The skin becomes slick. Droplets form and roll. Heavy sweating creates a feeling of being drenched, clothes clinging and heavy. Cold sweat, produced during shock or fear, feels clammy rather than warm, the skin simultaneously wet and chilled. Palmar sweating makes the hands slippery, sometimes interfering with grip. The scalp sweats profusely, and the liquid runs into eyes, stinging with salt. After intense sweating, when the body rehydrates, there is sometimes a feeling of internal relief, as if tissues were gasping and can now breathe. The smell of one's own sweat is usually mild, familiar. The sweat of others, depending on individual chemistry and bacterial flora, ranges from barely noticeable to overwhelming.

## Across the Lifespan

Newborns have functional sweat glands but produce little sweat in the first weeks. The system matures gradually through infancy. Young children sweat less efficiently than adults, with lower output per gland, making them more vulnerable to heat stress. Adolescence brings the activation of apocrine glands and the emergence of body odor. Peak sweating capacity occurs in early adulthood. As the body ages, sweat glands become less responsive. Older adults produce less sweat under heat stress, recovering more slowly from exertion. The skin itself thins, and blood flow to the surface diminishes. These changes increase the risk of hyperthermia in the elderly. Some individuals are born with hypohidrotic ectodermal dysplasia, lacking most sweat glands. They must avoid heat and exertion, cooling themselves artificially.

## When It Fails

Hyperhidrosis causes excessive sweating unrelated to temperature or exertion. Hands, feet, underarms, or face drip constantly. The condition is often genetic, beginning in childhood or adolescence. It can make handshakes awkward, damage paper, interfere with holding tools. Treatments include aluminum chloride solutions, anticholinergic drugs, botulinum toxin injections that temporarily disable glands, or surgical sympathectomy severing the nerves that trigger sweating. Anhidrosis is the opposite, the inability to sweat. It can result from nerve damage, skin injury, or genetic conditions. Without sweating, core temperature rises dangerously during exertion or heat exposure. Bromhidrosis refers to excessive body odor, usually from bacterial overgrowth in apocrine zones. Heat stroke occurs when sweating cannot match heat production, and core temperature exceeds 40 degrees Celsius, disrupting cellular function.

## Evolutionary Heritage

Most mammals have sparse sweat glands and cool themselves by panting or seeking shade. Horses and humans are the exceptions, both capable of sustained exertion in heat. This adaptation emerged in human ancestors on the African savanna, where the ability to pursue prey over long distances in midday heat provided a hunting advantage. Persistence hunting relied on sweating. A human could run an antelope to exhaustion, the animal overheating while the human cooled. This required hairlessness as well. Fur traps sweat against skin, preventing evaporation. Human ancestors lost their body hair, maximizing cooling surface. The density of eccrine glands increased. This system made it possible to travel long distances, to work through the heat of the day, to inhabit climates that would otherwise be lethal.

## Ritual and Meaning

Sweat lodges and saunas appear in cultures worldwide, spaces designed to provoke intense sweating for purification or healing. Indigenous American sweat lodge ceremonies combine heat, humidity, and prayer. Finnish saunas, heated to extreme temperatures, induce profound sweating followed by cold plunges. These practices are believed to cleanse toxins, strengthen the body, or alter consciousness. Athletes speak of "sweating it out," linking perspiration to effort and achievement. The phrase "breaking a sweat" means beginning real work. To "sweat blood" is to suffer or struggle. Visible sweat on a laborer's brow signifies honest toil. In some contexts, sweating signals weakness, nervousness, or guilt. Antiperspirants and deodorants are a major industry, billions spent annually to suppress or mask this natural function.
