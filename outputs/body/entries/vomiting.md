# Vomiting

***The Body's Violent Expulsion***

> **Category:** Response

The stomach contracts in reverse. What entered through the mouth exits the same way, forced upward by muscular spasm. The process begins in the brain, in response to toxins, pathogens, motion sickness, or signals from the gut. Nausea precedes it, a warning. Saliva floods the mouth. The diaphragm and abdominal muscles tighten. The lower esophageal sphincter relaxes. The stomach compresses. What follows is ejection, rapid and forceful, through the esophagus and out the mouth. Vomiting clears the stomach of dangerous substances, but it also occurs when nothing dangerous is present, triggered by pain, disgust, pregnancy, or disease. It is an ancient protective reflex, shared across vertebrates. The body prioritizing survival over comfort, purging what it cannot safely hold.

## The Command Center

The vomiting center resides in the medulla oblongata, the brainstem's base. It receives signals from multiple sources: the chemoreceptor trigger zone detecting toxins in the blood, the vestibular system sensing motion, the gut itself reporting distension or chemical irritation, and higher brain regions processing disgust or fear. When enough signals converge, the medulla initiates the sequence. It coordinates the muscles involved: diaphragm, abdomen, esophagus, throat. The autonomic nervous system shifts into crisis mode. Heart rate increases. Blood pressure rises. Sweating begins. The body mobilizes for emergency evacuation before conscious awareness fully registers what is happening.

## The Preparation

Nausea arrives first, an aversive sensation without precise location. The mouth waters, saliva production spiking to protect teeth from stomach acid. The upper lip may curl in the grimace that signals disgust across cultures. Breathing changes, becoming shallow and rapid. The person seeks stillness, an instinct to minimize triggers. Swallowing becomes difficult, the throat anticipating upward flow. This prodrome can last seconds or minutes. In some cases, nausea resolves without vomiting. In others, the progression is inevitable. The body gathering force, preparing the pathway, giving final warning before the reflex becomes unavoidable.

## The Mechanism

The lower esophageal sphincter opens. The pyloric sphincter at the stomach's base closes, preventing contents from moving into the intestines. The diaphragm contracts sharply downward. The abdominal muscles contract inward. Between these opposing forces, the stomach compresses like a squeezed bag. Pressure builds. The upper esophageal sphincter relaxes. The soft palate rises, blocking the nasal passage. The glottis closes, sealing the airway. Breathing stops. The contents surge upward through the esophagus at speed, propelled by muscular pressure rather than peristalsis. The mouth opens. Ejection occurs in one to several waves, each preceded by the same compression cycle.

## Infancy to Age

Newborns spit up frequently, a normal overflow from an immature digestive system. True vomiting in infants signals illness or obstruction. Children vomit more readily than adults, their trigger threshold lower. By adolescence, the pattern resembles adult response. In pregnancy, morning sickness affects most women in the first trimester, triggered by hormonal changes, though the evolutionary purpose remains debated. The elderly vomit less frequently but with more dangerous consequences: aspiration risk increases with weakened reflexes, dehydration occurs more rapidly. The reflex persists across the lifespan, but vulnerability to its effects grows with age.

## When Control Fails

Hyperemesis gravidarum traps pregnant women in relentless vomiting, leading to dehydration and weight loss requiring hospitalization. Cyclic vomiting syndrome produces episodes lasting hours or days, recurring without clear cause. Bulimia nervosa weaponizes the reflex, self-induced vomiting causing esophageal tears, dental erosion, electrolyte imbalance. Gastroparesis paralyzes the stomach, causing vomiting of undigested food hours after eating. Chemotherapy triggers severe nausea through direct effects on the chemoreceptor zone. Brain tumors or increased intracranial pressure produce projectile vomiting without preceding nausea. The reflex meant to protect becomes, in dysfunction, a source of harm.

## Aftertaste and Recovery

After vomiting, the mouth tastes of acid and bile. The throat burns. The abdominal muscles ache from violent contraction. Weakness follows, partly from the physical exertion, partly from fluid loss. Thirst arrives. The person often feels immediate relief if vomiting was triggered by ingested toxin or acute nausea, the removal of the cause ending the distress. But repeated vomiting brings dehydration, electrolyte depletion, and exhaustion. The body must rebuild what was lost. In the aftermath, sensitivity to smell and taste remains heightened. Many foods become temporarily aversive. The system recalibrating, cautious about what it will accept.

## Cultural Revulsion

Humans hide vomiting when possible, retreating to privacy. The act is stigmatized, associated with loss of control, excess, or contamination. Public vomiting brings social judgment. Children learn this early, apologizing even when illness is involuntary. Yet in some contexts, vomiting is normalized: morning sickness in pregnancy, hangovers after drinking, purging in certain eating disorders. Ancient medical practice included therapeutic emetics, intentional vomiting to balance humors or expel sickness. Modern medicine suppresses vomiting with antiemetics except when purging is necessary, as in poisoning. The reflex is universal, but shame around it is culturally constructed, varying by context and cause.

## The Contagion Effect

Watching someone vomit can trigger the same response in observers. Hearing the sound, smelling the expelled contents, even reading a detailed description can produce nausea. This empathetic response may serve group survival: if one member of a band ingests poison, others who ate the same food benefit from purging before symptoms worsen. Mirror neurons activate. The observer's brain simulates the experience. Disgust evolved to protect against pathogens, and vomit signals contamination. The sight of another's distress becomes the body's own warning. What began in one stomach spreads through the group, an involuntary chain reaction of protective expulsion.
