# The Skin

**1. The Largest Organ:** An average adult carries roughly twenty square feet of skin — about the area of a twin bedsheet — weighing eight pounds. It replaces itself entirely every twenty-eight days, shedding fifty million cells per minute. Most household dust is dead skin. The body manufactures this barrier continuously: new cells push upward from the basal layer, flattening and dying as they rise, becoming the tough, waterproof surface that keeps the world out and the body in. A single square inch contains nineteen million cells, sixty thousand melanocytes, and up to three hundred sweat glands.

**2. The Fingerprint Accident:** Fingerprints form in the womb between weeks ten and seventeen, when the fetus touches the amniotic sac. The friction creates ridges in the basal layer that never change, even as the skin regenerates. No two prints match, not even between identical twins, because no two fetuses make exactly the same movements in exactly the same fluid at exactly the same developmental moment. The uniqueness is coincidental — an artifact of random fetal movement preserved forever. Koalas have fingerprints too, evolved independently, confusing crime scenes in Australia.

**3. The Tattoo Prisoners:** Tattoo ink sits trapped in the dermis, the skin's second layer. When the needle punctures the epidermis, immune cells rush to consume the foreign particles — but the ink molecules are too large to break down. The immune cells hold the ink inside themselves until they die, at which point nearby cells consume them, inheriting the ink. A tattoo is a colony of stained immune cells passing pigment from generation to generation. Laser removal works by shattering the ink into smaller pieces that the immune system can finally digest. The body never wanted it there.

**4. The Grafting Gardens:** Severe burn victims need new skin fast — their own can't regenerate quickly enough. Surgeons use dermal regeneration templates: scaffolds of collagen and shark cartilage that the body colonizes with its own cells. Before these existed, they used cadaver skin, pigskin, even fish skin. In Brazil, researchers graft tilapia skin onto burns. The fish collagen is similar enough to human collagen that it works. Burn wards once resembled strange gardens, bodies wrapped in foreign skins, waiting to become themselves again.

**5. The Color Spectrum:** Melanin comes in two forms: eumelanin (brown-black) and pheomelanin (red-yellow). Everyone has the same number of melanocytes; skin color depends on how much melanin they produce and which type dominates. Melanin absorbs ultraviolet radiation, protecting DNA from mutation. Lighter skin evolved in northern latitudes where less sunlight meant less vitamin D synthesis. Darker skin evolved near the equator as protection. The spectrum between is recent evolutionary history, written in melanin. Albinism disables melanin production entirely. The skin becomes a map of where ancestors lived and what light they faced.

**6. The Corpse Boundary:** Skin dies from the outside in. After death, bacteria begin digesting it within hours, starting with the gut and working outward. The skin holds longer than most organs, maintaining the body's shape even as everything inside liquefies. Morticians prioritize skin — embalming slows bacterial growth, makeup masks discoloration. The funeral industry exists largely to preserve this boundary, to maintain the illusion that the person is still contained. When the skin finally fails, the body stops being a body. It becomes something else.
