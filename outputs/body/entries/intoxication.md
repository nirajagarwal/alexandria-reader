# Intoxication

**1. The Slowing:** Ethanol molecules pass through the stomach lining into blood, cross the blood-brain barrier, and bind to GABA receptors — the brain's primary inhibitory system. This binding makes neurons less likely to fire. The frontal cortex, newest in evolutionary terms, succumbs first. Judgment, planning, and social restraint fade while older structures — emotion, memory formation, motor control — persist temporarily. The classic progression of drunkenness follows the brain's architecture backward through time. What feels like freedom is actually systematic shutdown, newest systems first, descending toward the brainstem that keeps you breathing.

**2. The Akkadian Warning:** The oldest known prohibition against intoxication appears in Mesopotamian law around 1750 BCE. The Code of Hammurabi prescribes death by drowning for priestesses who open a wine shop. Earlier texts describe beer as a gift from the goddess Ninkasi, taught to humans after the flood. The Sumerians had at least eight words for various states of drunkenness. Their medical texts list beer-based remedies for dozens of ailments. The same civilization that invented writing also systematized both the celebration and control of altered consciousness. The tension is five thousand years old.

**3. The Enzyme Lottery:** Human populations show dramatic variation in alcohol metabolism, determined largely by two genes. ALDH2, common in East Asian populations, produces an enzyme variant that processes acetaldehyde — ethanol's toxic metabolite — much more slowly. The result: facial flushing, nausea, rapid heartbeat from even small amounts of alcohol. This genetic variant protects against alcoholism through misery. It arose once, perhaps ten thousand years ago, and spread through China and Japan. Today roughly 560 million people carry it. Natural selection has given different populations different relationships with the same molecule.

**4. The Surgeon's Problem:** Ether, chloroform, and alcohol all produce unconsciousness through similar mechanisms — disrupting neural membranes, enhancing inhibitory signals. Before 1846, surgeons relied on alcohol for anesthesia, dosing patients into stupor before cutting. The line between surgical unconsciousness and fatal respiratory depression was narrow and invisible. Patients thrashed, screamed through the haze, or simply stopped breathing. The introduction of ether and chloroform represented not a new principle but better control — more reliable dosing, faster onset, clearer endpoints. Modern anesthesia still uses the same neural targets. We've refined the sledgehammer into a scalpel.

**5. The Ballantine Measure:** In 1950s Scotland, forensic scientist James Ballantine developed urine alcohol testing after a bus crash killed nineteen people. The driver's blood alcohol was never measured. Ballantine's method became standard in British courts, establishing legal thresholds for impairment. But those thresholds — 80mg per 100ml in the UK, 0.08% in the US — represent political compromise more than physiological truth. Impairment begins at any measurable level. Reaction time, judgment, and risk perception all decline linearly with dose. The legal limit acknowledges that societies require some margin of intoxication to function.

**6. The Tolerance Paradox:** Chronic alcohol use forces neurons to compensate for constant inhibition by reducing GABA receptors and increasing excitatory glutamate signaling. The brain rebalances itself around the presence of the drug. In this adapted state, normal function requires alcohol. Sudden withdrawal removes the brake while the accelerator remains floored. Neurons fire chaotically. Hallucinations, seizures, and delirium tremens follow. The body's attempt to maintain equilibrium becomes the mechanism of dependence. Tolerance is not adaptation to alcohol but adaptation to its absence being unbearable.
