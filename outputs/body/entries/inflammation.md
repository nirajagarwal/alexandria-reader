# Inflammation

***The Body's Alarm and Repair***

> **Category:** Response

Inflammation is the body's immediate response to damage or invasion. When tissue is injured, cells rupture and spill their contents. Chemical signals flood the area within seconds. Blood vessels widen. Their walls become porous. Fluid seeps into the surrounding tissue, carrying white blood cells and proteins that fight infection and begin repair. The site swells, reddens, warms, and aches. This is not malfunction. This is the immune system marshaling resources to contain a threat and rebuild what was broken. The process is ancient, shared with creatures that possessed neither brain nor bone. It saves lives daily. It also, when misdirected or prolonged, destroys the very tissue it evolved to protect.

## The Chemical Cascade

Damaged cells release histamine, bradykinin, and prostaglandins into their surroundings. These molecules act as emergency broadcasts. Histamine forces blood vessels to dilate and leak. Bradykinin triggers pain receptors, creating the sensation that warns the organism to protect the injured area. Prostaglandins amplify both effects and raise local temperature. The heat accelerates chemical reactions, making immune responses more efficient. Within minutes, the tissue environment transforms from normal to hostile for invading bacteria. The same heat that helps kill microbes also causes the characteristic warmth felt through the skin.

## The Swelling

Blood plasma escapes through the loosened vessel walls, accumulating in the spaces between cells. This fluid carries antibodies, clotting factors, and complement proteins that tag foreign particles for destruction. The swelling physically dilutes toxins and provides more space for immune cells to maneuver. Pressure builds as fluid volume increases. This pressure, combined with bradykinin, creates pain that intensifies with movement. The body is signaling: keep still, let repair happen. The swollen area becomes a battlefield and construction site simultaneously. When inflammation resolves, lymphatic vessels drain the excess fluid, carrying away debris and dead cells.

## White Cells Arrive

Neutrophils, the most abundant white blood cells, reach the site first. They squeeze through vessel walls and follow chemical gradients toward the damage. Each neutrophil can engulf and digest dozens of bacteria before dying. Their corpses, mixed with tissue debris and fluid, form pus. Monocytes arrive hours later and transform into macrophages, larger cells that consume neutrophil remains and coordinate the next phase of healing. Macrophages also secrete growth factors that stimulate tissue regeneration. The redness visible on the skin surface reflects this massive increase in blood flow and cellular activity beneath.

## Acute to Chronic

Acute inflammation lasts days to weeks and ends when the threat is eliminated. The swelling recedes. New tissue fills the wound. But inflammation can persist when the trigger cannot be cleared: a splinter embedded too deep, an autoimmune attack on the body's own cells, excess fat tissue secreting inflammatory signals. Chronic inflammation smolders for months or years. The prolonged presence of immune cells and their caustic chemicals damages healthy tissue. Organs scar. Blood vessels stiffen. The process meant to protect becomes a source of disease. Arthritis, atherosclerosis, and certain cancers grow from inflammation that cannot resolve.

## Fever's Role

When inflammation spreads beyond the local site, or when bacteria enter the bloodstream, the hypothalamus raises the body's temperature set point. Prostaglandins reach the brain and reset its thermostat. The person shivers to generate heat, seeking blankets while their skin burns to the touch. Fever accelerates immune cell activity and inhibits bacterial reproduction. Most human pathogens thrive at normal body temperature but struggle above it. The discomfort of fever is the cost of a faster immune response. At 104 degrees Fahrenheit, the benefit reverses. Proteins begin to denature. The body must cool or risk damage to its own tissues.

## Evolutionary Origins

Single-celled organisms already possessed rudimentary inflammatory responses 600 million years ago. They could detect membrane damage and release signals that attracted other cells. As organisms grew more complex, this system elaborated. Insects have hemolymph that clots around wounds and cells that engulf invaders. Fish possess all the major components of mammalian inflammation. The response is so ancient because the problem it solves is universal: how to survive injury and infection. Bodies that could not mount inflammation did not survive to reproduce. Every human inherits this system, refined over geological time, still using some of the same molecules as their invertebrate ancestors.

## When It Misfires

In allergies, the inflammatory cascade activates against harmless substances: pollen, peanuts, pet dander. Histamine floods tissue in response to molecules that pose no threat. The throat swells, airways constrict, and in severe cases, blood pressure drops precipitously. Anaphylaxis is inflammation attempting to expel an invader that does not exist, with potentially fatal results. Autoimmune diseases reflect a different failure: the immune system inflaming the body's own tissue. In rheumatoid arthritis, joint linings become permanent sites of inflammation. In inflammatory bowel disease, the intestinal wall attacks itself. The system designed to distinguish self from other loses that ability.
