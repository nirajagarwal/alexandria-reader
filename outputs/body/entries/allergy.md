# Allergy

**1. The Misfire:** Your immune system exists to distinguish self from invader — to attack the genuinely dangerous and ignore the harmless. Allergy is when this recognition fails. A protein in peanuts, a fleck of pollen, a trace of shellfish triggers the same cascade meant for parasites and bacteria. Mast cells degranulate. Histamine floods tissue. Blood vessels dilate, airways constrict, skin erupts. The body mobilizes its defenses against nothing, or against something that poses no actual threat. The reaction can range from a rash to anaphylaxis: the immune system at war with a phantom enemy, sometimes violently enough to kill its own host.

**2. The Hygiene Hypothesis:** Allergies were rare a century ago. Now they affect over 30% of people in developed nations. One explanation: we are too clean. The immune system evolved in a world dense with parasites, bacteria, and dirt. Children who grow up on farms, who play in soil, who live with animals, develop fewer allergies. The theory suggests that without genuine threats to calibrate against, the immune system becomes hypersensitive, attacking harmless proteins for lack of real work. Modern sanitation may have traded infectious disease for immune dysfunction. The body, understimulated, invents enemies.

**3. The Escalation:** First exposure does nothing. The immune system meets the allergen — bee venom, latex, cashew protein — and quietly prepares. It creates antibodies, specifically immunoglobulin E, and primes mast cells throughout the body. This is sensitization. The person feels nothing and doesn't know it has happened. Second exposure triggers the prepared response: mast cells recognize the allergen, bind it, and explode their chemical payload. Each subsequent exposure can intensify the reaction. The body remembers and overreacts more severely each time. What began as a mild itch can progress to throat closure and cardiovascular collapse. The immune system learns the wrong lesson.

**4. The Oral Tolerance Paradox:** Your gut encounters foreign proteins constantly — food, bacteria, the cellular debris of digestion. The intestinal immune system has learned careful tolerance, distinguishing nourishment from threat. But this tolerance can break. Eczema in infancy, especially around the mouth, may allow food proteins to contact immune cells through damaged skin rather than through the gut. The body learns to attack the protein instead of tolerate it. New protocols introduce allergens early, through eating, to train tolerance before skin exposure can train attack. Timing matters. The route matters. The same substance can be friend or enemy depending on which tissue meets it first.

**5. The Unexpected Targets:** People develop allergies to their own tears, their own sweat, their own semen. Cold urticaria means hives from temperature drop — touching ice cream, swimming in cool water, breathing winter air. Aquagenic urticaria means water itself causes welts, even the water in one's own skin. Some people react to sunlight, to pressure, to vibration. Exercise-induced anaphylaxis strikes mid-workout, sometimes only when certain foods were eaten beforehand. The immune system can learn to attack nearly anything, even stimuli that seem too fundamental, too constant, too much a part of simply existing to possibly be threats. The body becomes allergic to the world, or to itself.

**6. The Ancient Adaptation:** The same antibody that causes allergies — IgE — evolved to fight parasitic worms. In regions where intestinal parasites are common, IgE levels run high and allergies stay rare. Remove the parasites and allergy rates climb. Some researchers have deliberately infected allergy patients with hookworms; symptoms improved. The therapy remains experimental and unsettling, but the logic is elegant: give the immune system its evolutionary target and it stops attacking pollen and peanuts. The body was designed for a world we no longer inhabit. Allergy may be the price of eliminating the enemies we evolved to fight.
