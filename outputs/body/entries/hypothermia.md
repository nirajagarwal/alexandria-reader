# Hypothermia

***When the Core Temperature Falls***

> **Category:** Extreme

The body maintains its core at 37 degrees Celsius. A narrow band, fiercely defended. When the environment pulls heat away faster than metabolism can replace it, the core temperature drops. Below 35 degrees, hypothermia begins. The body responds with every tool it possesses: shivering, vasoconstriction, metabolic acceleration. These defenses burn through reserves. When they fail, the systems that depend on warmth begin to falter. The heart slows. The brain dulls. Consciousness fragments. Below 28 degrees, the body can no longer generate heat. Death becomes likely unless warming is imposed from outside. Hypothermia kills slowly, then suddenly. It is cold converting order into entropy, life into stillness.

## The Threshold

Hypothermia is not simply being cold. It is the state where core body temperature drops below the point where normal cellular function can continue. The skin may freeze, but if the core remains at 37 degrees, this is frostbite, not hypothermia. The defining measure is internal: the temperature of blood flowing through the heart, the organs deep in the trunk, the brain inside the skull. At 35 degrees, mild hypothermia begins. At 32, moderate. At 28, severe. Each degree lost represents not just cooling but the progressive shutdown of systems that require warmth to operate. Enzymes slow. Electrical signals weaken. The body is a chemical engine, and the temperature is dropping below operating range.

## The First Defense

Shivering begins before core temperature drops. When skin receptors detect cold and surface temperature falls, the hypothalamus triggers rapid, involuntary muscle contractions. Small muscle groups throughout the body fire in rapid succession, generating heat through friction and metabolic activity. This can increase heat production fivefold. The chest, back, and limbs shake. The jaw clenches and trembles. It is uncomfortable, exhausting, and effective. Shivering continues as long as the core temperature remains above approximately 32 degrees and the body has glycogen to burn. Below that threshold, muscle coordination fails and shivering ceases. The body has spent its most powerful tool.

## Shutting the Periphery

As heat drains away, blood vessels in the skin and extremities constrict. The body prioritizes the core, the brain, the heart, the vital organs housed in the trunk. Blood retreats from fingers, toes, nose, ears. These peripheral tissues cool rapidly, their metabolism slows, their function degrades. Fingers become clumsy, unable to perform fine movements. This is not numbness from cold alone but actual loss of function as tissue temperature drops. The hands cannot grip. The feet stumble. Meanwhile, the blood that would have warmed those parts remains circulating through the chest and abdomen, keeping core temperature elevated as long as possible. The body sacrifices the edges to protect the center.

## The Slowing Mind

The brain is extraordinarily temperature-sensitive. As core temperature drops through the mild hypothermia range, cognitive function deteriorates. Judgment falters. Memory fails. Speech slurs. The person becomes confused, apathetic, unable to recognize their own danger. This is not a psychological response to cold but a direct neurological effect. The electrical activity of neurons depends on temperature-dependent ion channels and enzyme function. When the brain cools, signals propagate more slowly, synapses fire irregularly, executive function crumbles. By 32 degrees, decision-making is severely impaired. The victim may remove clothing, convinced they are warm. They may stop seeking shelter. They may lie down in the snow. The organ meant to save them is failing.

## The Failing Heart

The heart muscle depends on coordinated electrical signals to contract in sequence. As core temperature drops, the cardiac conduction system becomes unstable. Below 32 degrees, the heart rate slows dramatically, sometimes to thirty beats per minute or less. The rhythm becomes irregular. Below 28 degrees, the heart is prone to ventricular fibrillation, a chaotic quivering that pumps no blood. The cold heart is also a stiff heart. The muscle itself loses flexibility, contracts weakly, fills inefficiently. Blood pressure drops. Circulation falters. Oxygen delivery to tissues declines even as those tissues, now cold themselves, require less oxygen. The heart continues beating far below temperatures that would kill through other means, but each beat is weaker, slower, less effective. Eventually it stops.

## The Oldest Death

Humans evolved in Africa, in warmth. The ability to maintain constant body temperature allowed our ancestors to remain active when reptiles could not, to hunt and forage regardless of season. But this mechanism is expensive, requiring constant fuel and working only within certain environmental limits. Hypothermia has killed humans since they first migrated from warm climates into cold ones. Mountaineers, sailors, soldiers, the lost and injured, the homeless in winter. It is an ancient death, older than civilization. Every culture that experiences winter has words for this way of dying, rituals for the bodies found frozen. The body can endure much, but it cannot endure the loss of warmth indefinitely. We are tropical animals, and the cold remains our enemy.

## Rewarming

A person with mild hypothermia can rewarm themselves: dry clothing, warm shelter, hot drinks, shivering doing its work. Below 32 degrees, rewarming must be external and controlled. Rapid surface warming can kill. When cold blood from the periphery returns to the core too quickly, core temperature drops further, a phenomenon called afterdrop. The cold heart cannot tolerate sudden warming, which can trigger fatal arrhythmias. Severe hypothermia requires hospital rewarming: warmed intravenous fluids, heated oxygen, sometimes cardiopulmonary bypass to warm the blood directly. There is a saying in emergency medicine: no one is dead until they are warm and dead. People have survived core temperatures below 14 degrees. The cold preserves as it kills. The brain, cooled, requires less oxygen. Warming must be slow, monitored, patient. The body is coaxed back to life.
