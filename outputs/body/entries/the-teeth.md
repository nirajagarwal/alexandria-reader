# The Teeth

**1. The Hardest Surface:** Enamel covers the crown of each tooth in a layer thinner than a credit card yet harder than steel. It is 96% mineral — mostly hydroxyapatite crystals arranged in rods that run from the surface to the dentin beneath. This makes it the hardest substance the human body produces, capable of withstanding decades of grinding forces that exceed 200 pounds per square inch. Yet it cannot repair itself. Once damaged, it remains damaged. The body that can heal bones and skin and organs cannot regenerate this crystalline armor. We get two sets in life, and the second must last.

**2. The Fossil Record:** Teeth survive when everything else decays. Long after bone crumbles and cloth dissolves, teeth remain in the earth, their enamel resisting water and acid and time. This is why we know more about ancient diets than ancient thoughts — teeth record what was eaten, how hard it was, whether it wore from left or right. The 400,000-year-old teeth from Qesem Cave in Israel show wear patterns from holding tools. The Neanderthal teeth from El Sidrón Cave in Spain contained bits of chamomile and yarrow, suggesting either medicine or seasoning. We are the only mammal that cooks our food, and our teeth show it: smaller, weaker, more prone to cavities than our ancestors'.

**3. The Medieval Extraction:** Barbers pulled teeth until the 19th century. The patient sat on the floor between the barber's knees while he gripped the tooth with iron forceps and pulled, sometimes for minutes, the tooth splintering and tearing from the jaw. No anesthesia. The barber pole's red stripe represented blood. Guy de Chauliac, the 14th-century French surgeon, recommended loosening teeth for days before extraction by touching them with the blood of a frog or the fat of a green lizard. In 1846, William Morton demonstrated ether anesthesia at Massachusetts General Hospital by extracting a tooth painlessly. The patient woke and said, "This is no humbug."

**4. The Third Set:** Sharks replace their teeth constantly — up to 50,000 in a lifetime, arranged in rows like a conveyor belt. Humans lost this ability somewhere in our mammalian ancestry, keeping only two sets: the 20 milk teeth that fall out and the 32 adult teeth that do not. But the genetic machinery remains dormant. In 2021, Japanese researchers regenerated teeth in mice by blocking a single protein, USAG-1. Human trials are planned. The prospect raises questions dentistry has never faced: if we could grow new teeth at 40, at 70, would we still fear the drill? Would we treat our second set differently if we knew a third was possible?

**5. The Status Symbol:** In 18th-century England, sugar was so expensive that blackened, rotting teeth signaled wealth — only the rich could afford enough sugar to destroy their teeth. Queen Elizabeth I's teeth were famously black. Poor people who wanted to appear wealthy would blacken their teeth with soot. By the Victorian era, the fashion reversed: white teeth became the goal, and the wealthy bought them from the poor. Desperate people sold their teeth to dentists, who pulled them and implanted them in rich mouths. Battlefield teeth were particularly prized — young soldiers with good dental health. After Waterloo, so many teeth were scavenged from dead soldiers that dentures became known as "Waterloo teeth."

**6. The Nerve Map:** A tooth has no pain receptors in its enamel. The agony comes from dentin, the layer beneath, riddled with microscopic tubes that connect to a nerve-filled pulp chamber at the tooth's core. When decay or trauma breaches the enamel, bacteria enter these tubes, and the nerve responds. But the nerve cannot distinguish between hot and cold, pressure and acid — it has only one signal: pain. This is why a cavity feels exactly like ice water feels like a crack. The nerve knows something is wrong but cannot tell you what. Dentists drill not just to remove decay but to silence the nerve's alarm before it becomes unbearable.
