# The Nose

**1. The Cartilage Tower:** The visible nose is mostly cartilage, not bone. Only the bridge is skeletal — two small nasal bones wedged between the eye sockets. Below that: flexible plates of hyaline cartilage that bend without breaking when you're punched or fall face-first. The tip contains two alar cartilages shaped like tiny wings. This architecture explains why broken noses heal crooked — cartilage remembers its trauma. Surgeons rebuilding noses after accidents sometimes harvest cartilage from ribs or ears, shaping it like sculptors working with living clay.

**2. The Neanderthal Question:** Neanderthals had enormous noses — broad, projecting, with huge internal chambers. For decades, scientists assumed this was adaptation to Ice Age cold, warming frigid air before it reached the lungs. Recent analysis suggests otherwise: their noses were big because their faces were big, pulled forward by massive teeth and jaw muscles. Modern humans have small, retracted faces, which leaves our noses protruding by default. We look like we have noses. Neanderthals looked like they had faces. Both are right.

**3. The Tycho Problem:** In 1566, Danish astronomer Tycho Brahe lost most of his nose in a duel over mathematics. For the rest of his life, he wore a prosthetic nose made of brass and silver, glued on daily. When his body was exhumed in 1901, the nasal opening in his skull showed green staining from copper alloy. A 2012 analysis found additional evidence of a separate gold nose, perhaps for special occasions. He died of a burst bladder after refusing to leave a banquet to urinate, which seemed ruder than enduring agony.

**4. The Columella's Revenge:** Syphilis, in its tertiary stage, destroys the nasal septum. The cartilage wall between nostrils collapses, and the bridge caves inward, creating a "saddle nose." Before antibiotics, this was common enough that surgeons developed techniques to rebuild noses using forehead flaps — a procedure originally pioneered in ancient India for rhinotomy, the judicial amputation of noses from adulterers and thieves. The punishment became the blueprint for the cure. The forehead still remembers how to be a nose if you fold it correctly.

**5. The Turbinate Labyrinth:** Inside your nose: three shelves of bone called turbinates, covered in mucous membrane full of blood vessels. They're not just dividers — they're radiators and humidifiers, warming and moistening 20,000 breaths per day. The inferior turbinate can swell to block airflow almost completely, which is why one nostril always feels more open than the other. This switches every few hours in a process called the nasal cycle, controlled by the autonomic nervous system for reasons no one fully understands. Your nose has a rhythm you've never noticed.

**6. The Vomeronasal Ghost:** Humans have a vomeronasal organ — a tiny pit in the septum that in other mammals detects pheromones. Ours doesn't connect to the brain. It sits there vestigial, a sensory antenna with its wire cut. In embryos, it forms and then stops developing. Some researchers claim it might still function, triggering responses we can't consciously perceive. Others say it's evolutionary debris, like the appendix. Either way, there's a sense we used to have, and the hardware remains in every nose, waiting for signals that will never come.
