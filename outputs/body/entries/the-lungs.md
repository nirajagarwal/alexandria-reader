# The Lungs

**1. The Surface Area:** Unfold a pair of human lungs and they cover roughly 70 square meters — half a tennis court. This impossible geometry exists because the airways branch 23 times before terminating in 300 million alveoli, grape-like sacs wrapped in capillaries where oxygen crosses into blood. The membrane separating air from bloodstream is one cell thick. Every minute, your entire blood supply passes through this vast, hidden prairie of gas exchange, refreshed and returned.

**2. The Asymmetry:** Your right lung has three lobes; your left has two. The missing lobe leaves room for your heart, which tilts leftward and rests in a depression called the cardiac notch. This asymmetry means your left lung holds about 10% less air. During cardiac surgery, doctors sometimes collapse the left lung entirely — its absence tolerated because the architecture already acknowledges the heart's precedence. The body negotiates with itself.

**3. The Drowning Child:** In 1667, Robert Hooke kept a dog alive by cutting a hole in its windpipe and pumping air through bellows. The heart kept beating with the chest motionless — proving that breathing's purpose was not mere motion but gas exchange. The experiment horrified him. He wrote that he would "hardly be induced to make any further trials of this kind." Yet his reluctant demonstration became the foundation of mechanical ventilation, saving millions who cannot breathe for themselves.

**4. The Black Lung:** Autopsies of urban dwellers reveal lungs mottled gray and black — microscopic carbon particles trapped in tissue. The lungs have no mechanism to expel fine particulates; macrophages engulf them but cannot digest them, becoming permanent residents. Coal miners, before regulations, inhaled enough dust that their lungs turned solid black, stiff as leather. The medical term is anthracosis. The body keeps a record of every breath, written in soot.

**5. The Competitive Breath-Hold:** Free divers descend hundreds of feet on a single breath, remaining underwater for ten minutes or more. The mammalian dive reflex kicks in — heart rate plummets, blood retreats from extremities to preserve the brain. Lungs compress to the size of fists. At depth, they take on water to prevent collapse. The current record is 11 minutes, 35 seconds. Humans, it turns out, are more aquatic than we remember.

**6. The Iron Lung:** For most of human history, if your breathing muscles failed, you died. Then in 1928, the iron lung — a steel cylinder enclosing the body, neck protruding. A pump cycled air pressure, forcing the chest to expand and contract. Thousands of polio patients lived inside these machines for months, years, a lifetime. The last surviving iron lung patient died in 2024. She had been inside for 70 years, breathing by proxy, tethered to a machine that remembered how.
