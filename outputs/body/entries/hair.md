# Hair

**1. The Factory Floor:** A single scalp produces approximately 100,000 follicles, each cycling through growth, rest, and death on its own schedule. The anagen phase lasts two to seven years, building the shaft upward at half a millimeter per day. Then catagen — three weeks of shutdown as the root detaches. Finally telogen, a three-month sleep before the strand falls and the cycle begins again. You lose fifty to one hundred hairs daily. The shower drain is not evidence of disease but of continuous renewal. What looks like catastrophe is the body running exactly as designed.

**2. The Dead Weight:** Hair is corpse tissue. The visible shaft contains no nerves, no blood supply, no living cells. Only the root, buried in the dermis, remains alive. The strand itself is pure keratin — the same protein that forms fingernails, bird feathers, rhinoceros horn. Its strength comes from disulfide bonds between sulfur atoms, cross-linking the protein chains into cables. This is why permanent waves work: break the bonds with chemicals, reshape the hair, reform the bridges. The dead can be reshaped, but they cannot heal. Cut hair never repairs itself.

**3. The Evidence:** In 1977, researchers discovered that hair absorbs and retains toxins, drugs, and environmental markers for months or years. A single strand becomes a timeline. Forensic analysts can determine cocaine use from six months prior. Archaeologists extract diet information from mummies. Hair reveals arsenic poisoning in Napoleon's exile, mercury treatment for syphilis in Victorian sailors, radiation exposure in Hiroshima survivors. The shaft records what the body encounters, writing history in keratin. Hair remembers what skin forgets.

**4. The Ancestral Coat:** Humans have the same number of hair follicles as chimpanzees. We are not hairless — we are covered in vellus, fine near-invisible strands that replaced the thick pelt somewhere between three and four million years ago. The leading theory: our ancestors became persistence hunters on the African savanna, chasing prey for hours until it collapsed from heat exhaustion. Fur became fatal. We traded thermal insulation for the ability to sweat efficiently, to run at midday, to outlast faster animals by simply refusing to stop. The hair thinned. The prey died tired.

**5. The Victorian Jewelry:** Mourning hair art flourished in the nineteenth century. Jewelers wove the hair of the dead into bracelets, watch chains, rings, and elaborate framed wreaths. Queen Victoria wore a locket containing Prince Albert's hair until her own death. The craft required astonishing skill — braiding, weaving, even knitting with human strands. The jewelry served as tangible connection to the deceased, more intimate than photographs because the hair had actually grown from their body. Some pieces combined hair from multiple generations, creating family trees you could wear.

**6. The Phantom Sensation:** Cutting hair produces no pain because the shaft is already dead, but many people report feeling the cut anyway. The sensation is proprioceptive memory — the brain tracking the body's boundaries. When those boundaries suddenly change, the nervous system briefly insists something is wrong. Soldiers often describe feeling their beards for days after shaving. Women report phantom ponytails, reaching back to adjust weight that's gone. The body maintains a map of itself, and hair, though dead, is drawn on that map. Remove it and the map takes time to update.
