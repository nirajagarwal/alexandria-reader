# Menstruating

***The Monthly Unmaking***

> **Category:** Rhythm

The uterus builds a lining each month, then tears it down. This cycle begins in adolescence and continues for decades, marking time in the body with blood. Approximately every twenty-eight days, the endometrium—the inner tissue of the uterus—thickens with blood vessels and glands, preparing to receive a fertilized egg. When no pregnancy occurs, hormone levels drop sharply. The thickened lining detaches from the uterine wall and exits through the vagina over three to seven days. This is menstruation. The body bleeds not from injury but from orchestrated demolition. Between menarche and menopause, most women menstruate roughly four hundred times. The cycle repeats with such regularity that deviation signals either pregnancy or disorder. In this rhythm, the body continuously prepares for one possibility while defaulting to another.

## The Hormone Cascade

Four hormones govern the cycle. Follicle-stimulating hormone from the pituitary triggers the ovaries to mature an egg and produce estrogen. Rising estrogen thickens the uterine lining. Mid-cycle, luteinizing hormone surges, triggering ovulation. The emptied follicle becomes the corpus luteum, secreting progesterone to maintain the endometrium. If no embryo implants, the corpus luteum dies after fourteen days. Progesterone and estrogen plummet. Without hormonal support, the spiral arteries feeding the endometrium constrict, starving the tissue. Cells die. The lining fragments. Prostaglandins trigger uterine contractions to expel the debris. This is why menstruation hurts: the uterus is cramping to shed what it built.

## The Material Itself

Menstrual flow contains more than blood. Tissue from the endometrial lining, mucus from cervical glands, vaginal secretions, and occasionally small clots all exit together. The volume ranges from thirty to eighty milliliters across the full period—roughly two to five tablespoons. The blood does not clot immediately because the endometrium releases anticoagulants. When flow is heavy, clots may form anyway, appearing as darker, jelly-like masses. The color changes throughout: bright red at peak flow, darker brown at the beginning and end as older blood oxidizes. The cervix dilates slightly to allow passage. The body reabsorbs iron from the lost blood, but chronic menstruation still depletes iron stores, particularly when flow is heavy.

## From Menarche to Menopause

Menstruation begins during puberty, typically between ages ten and fifteen, when rising estrogen first triggers endometrial growth. Early cycles are often irregular, sometimes anovulatory—the body practicing before ovulation begins reliably. For the first year or two, the pattern stabilizes. Menstruation then continues for three to four decades. Pregnancy halts it; breastfeeding may suppress it. Perimenopause, beginning in the forties, brings irregular cycles as ovarian function declines. Periods may come closer together or skip months. Eventually, menstruation ceases entirely. Menopause is diagnosed after twelve consecutive months without a period. The uterus stops building what it will not use.

## The Pain Continuum

Pain ranges from negligible to incapacitating. Prostaglandins cause the uterine muscle to contract; higher levels mean stronger cramps. The pain concentrates in the lower abdomen but may radiate to the lower back and thighs. Some women experience nausea, diarrhea, or headache from prostaglandin effects elsewhere. For most, over-the-counter anti-inflammatory drugs that block prostaglandin synthesis provide relief. For some, pain exceeds normal physiology. Endometriosis occurs when endometrial tissue grows outside the uterus, bleeding each month into the abdominal cavity, causing severe pain and adhesions. Adenomyosis occurs when endometrium invades the uterine muscle, creating painful, heavy periods. Fibroids, benign tumors in the uterine wall, also worsen bleeding and cramping. Severe pain is not normal, though it is common.

## When the Cycle Breaks

Amenorrhea is the absence of menstruation. Primary amenorrhea means periods never started by age sixteen; secondary means they stopped for three or more months after previously occurring. Pregnancy is the most common cause of secondary amenorrhea. Others include excessive exercise, low body weight, stress, polycystic ovary syndrome, thyroid disorders, and pituitary tumors. The body will sacrifice reproduction to preserve life; when resources are scarce, menstruation ceases. Menorrhagia is excessively heavy bleeding, soaking through protection hourly or lasting more than seven days. Causes include fibroids, polyps, bleeding disorders, and hormonal imbalances. Untreated, it causes anemia. Oligomenorrhea is infrequent menstruation, cycles longer than thirty-five days. The body signals through menstruation that something upstream has changed.

## Blood Taboo and Seclusion

Nearly every culture has ritualized menstruation, most through restriction. Ancient Jewish law required seven days of separation and ritual bathing before resuming marital relations. Hindu tradition barred menstruating women from kitchens and temples. Some Indigenous cultures isolated women in menstrual huts. The explanations varied—spiritual pollution, physical danger, sacred power too potent for daily life—but the pattern held: menstruating women were set apart. Modern euphemisms persist: "on the rag," "Aunt Flo," "that time of month." Advertising for menstrual products showed blue liquid until 2020. The bleeding that half of humanity experiences monthly remained unspeakable. Even now, menstruation is managed in private, evidence disposed of, the cycle acknowledged only when it disrupts.

## The Synchrony Question

The widespread belief that women living together synchronize menstrual cycles originated from a 1971 study of college dormitory residents. Later research failed to replicate the findings. Mathematical analysis shows that cycles of varying lengths will randomly overlap and diverge; perceived synchrony is likely coincidence noticed and remembered while divergence is ignored. No mechanism has been identified by which human pheromones could alter cycle timing. Yet the belief persists, perhaps because it suggests connection, a shared rhythm among women. The body does not synchronize, but the desire to find pattern in the bleeding remains.
