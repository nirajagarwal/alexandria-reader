# The Tumor

**1. The Unwelcome Growth:** A tumor is flesh that forgets how to stop. Cells divide according to instructions written in DNA — genes that say "grow" and genes that say "halt." A tumor begins when these instructions fail. The brakes give out. The cells multiply without purpose, piling into masses that serve no function. Benign tumors grow but stay contained, pressing against surrounding tissue like an elbow in crowded space. Malignant tumors invade, sending fingers through healthy tissue, breaking off fragments that travel through blood to seed distant organs. Cancer is the name we give malignant tumors. The word comes from the Greek *karkinos* — crab — because to ancient physicians, the swollen veins radiating from breast tumors resembled legs.

**2. The Ancient Presence:** Tumors are older than humans. Paleontologists have found osteosarcoma — bone cancer — in a 77-million-year-old dinosaur femur. A 240-million-year-old turtle carries evidence of bone tumors in its fossilized shell. Even older: tumors in 405-million-year-old fish jaws. The capacity for cells to rebel against their programming arrived early in multicellular life. Egyptian mummies show bone cancer, nasal tumors, bladder stones that turned malignant. The oldest written description comes from Egypt around 1600 BCE: breast tumors "bulging" from the chest, hard and cool to touch. The papyrus records the physician's conclusion: "There is no treatment."

**3. The First Surgeries:** For millennia, the knife was the only option. Remove the visible mass, hope the roots didn't reach too deep. Scottish surgeon John Hunter, in the 1760s, distinguished between tumors that could be cut away cleanly and those with "tentacula" that infiltrated surrounding flesh. His advice: operate only if you can remove everything. Nineteenth-century surgeon William Halsted believed in radical surgery — take the tumor, take the surrounding tissue, take the lymph nodes, take the chest muscle. His mastectomy removed everything from collarbone to ribs. Women survived the cancer but lived with hollowed chests. Later surgeons learned that less cutting often worked as well. The body keeps what it can.

**4. The Radiation Answer:** In 1896, a German physicist named Wilhelm Röntgen discovered X-rays by accident while experimenting with cathode tubes. Within months, doctors were using the rays to peer inside living bodies. Within a few years, they noticed something else: skin exposed to X-rays developed burns, then healed. Could the rays destroy abnormal tissue? By the 1920s, radiation therapy was treating tumors, burning away malignant cells faster than healthy ones. Modern radiation uses precisely aimed beams, multiple angles converging on the tumor, sparing surrounding tissue. Proton beams stop at exact depths. But radiation is fire. It saves lives and leaves scars — heart damage, lung fibrosis, bones that never quite recover.

**5. The Immortal Cells:** In 1951, a cervical tumor killed a woman named Henrietta Lacks. Before she died, doctors took a sample of the tumor without her knowledge. The cells, labeled HeLa, did what no human cells had done in a laboratory: they survived. They thrived. They doubled every twenty-four hours. They still do. HeLa cells have been shipped to research labs worldwide, used in experiments that led to the polio vaccine, cancer treatments, gene mapping, HIV research. They've traveled to space. They've been cloned and frozen and thawed and multiplied into tonnage that exceeds the weight of Henrietta Lacks's body many thousands of times over. The tumor that killed her became biologically immortal. Her family wasn't told for decades.

**6. The Body's Surveillance:** Every day, cells in your body mutate. Cosmic rays pass through you. Copying errors occur during division. Chemicals damage DNA. Most mutations do nothing. Some are corrected by molecular proofreaders that patrol the genome. Some trigger the cell's self-destruct sequence — apoptosis, programmed death. The immune system hunts abnormal cells, marks them for destruction. A tumor is what happens when all these defenses fail simultaneously. Certain viruses suppress immunity and cause cancer — HPV, hepatitis, Epstein-Barr. Some people inherit broken DNA repair genes and grow tumors young. But most tumors are chance: the wrong mutations at the wrong moment in the wrong cell. Your body is already fighting tumors you'll never know existed.
