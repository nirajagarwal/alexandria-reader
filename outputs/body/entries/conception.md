# Conception

**1. The Meeting Place:** Fertilization occurs in the ampulla, the widest section of the fallopian tube, about a third of the way from the ovary to the uterus. Of the roughly 200 million sperm that begin the journey, fewer than 200 reach this site. The egg, released during ovulation, survives only 12-24 hours. Sperm can live up to five days in the female reproductive tract, waiting. When one penetrates the zona pellucida—the egg's protein shell—the membrane instantly hardens, becoming impenetrable to all others. The genetic material merges over the next 12 hours, creating a single cell with 46 chromosomes.

**2. The Capacitation Dance:** Sperm cannot fertilize immediately after ejaculation. They must undergo capacitation—a series of biochemical changes that occur in the female reproductive tract over 6-8 hours. The cholesterol in their cell membranes is stripped away. Their tails begin to beat differently, faster and more forcefully, in a pattern called hyperactivation. Only capacitated sperm can penetrate the cumulus cells surrounding the egg and bind to its surface. The female body transforms sperm into something capable of conception—they arrive incomplete, and are finished there.

**3. The Twin Question:** Identical twins occur when a single fertilized egg splits, usually within the first two weeks. No one knows why this happens. It's not genetic, not predictable, not influenced by age or fertility treatments. The rate holds steady across all populations: about 3-4 per 1,000 births. Fraternal twins result from two eggs fertilized by two sperm—essentially siblings who shared a womb. Their rate varies dramatically by genetics, maternal age, and geography. Nigeria has the highest rate of fraternal twinning in the world; Japan has the lowest.

**4. The Chemical Conversation:** Within hours of fertilization, the embryo begins secreting human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG)—the hormone pregnancy tests detect. But hCG is not just a signal; it's a survival mechanism. It prevents the corpus luteum (the follicle that released the egg) from degenerating, forcing it to continue producing progesterone. Without progesterone, the uterine lining sheds and menstruation begins. The embryo essentially hijacks the mother's hormonal cycle before it has even reached the uterus, before it has any organs, before it has begun to look like anything we would recognize.

**5. The Ancient Observation:** Aristotle believed conception required the male's "generative substance" and the female's menstrual blood. The man provided form and motion; the woman provided matter. This theory dominated European medicine for nearly 2,000 years. Antonie van Leeuwenhoek, examining his own semen under a microscope in 1677, saw sperm and initially believed each one contained a tiny, fully-formed human—a homunculus—that simply grew larger in the womb. The egg's equal contribution wasn't proven until 1875, when German biologist Oskar Hertwig watched a sea urchin egg and sperm fuse under magnification.

**6. The Window That Closes:** Human fertility is remarkably inefficient. Even in ideal conditions—healthy partners, perfect timing—conception occurs in only about 25% of cycles. After fertilization, 30-50% of embryos fail to implant. Of those that do, 15-20% miscarry, mostly in the first trimester. Only about 30% of fertilized eggs become live births. The female body is intensely selective, rejecting embryos with chromosomal abnormalities through mechanisms we still don't fully understand. Most women who have tried to conceive have been pregnant without knowing it—a biochemical pregnancy, ended before a missed period.
