# Digesting

***The Body Breaking Down the World***

> **Category:** Sustenance

Food enters the mouth and becomes the body. Between those states lies digestion: a thirty-foot journey through muscular tubes, chemical baths, and cellular gates. The process begins with teeth tearing and grinding, saliva dissolving. It continues in the stomach's acid churn, the intestines' patient extraction. Nutrients pass through membranes into blood. What remains moves on, waste destined for expulsion. The body cannot build itself from whole food. It must dismantle every meal into molecules small enough to cross from gut to self. This transformation happens without permission or awareness, orchestrated by organs that began forming in the womb and will work until death. Thirty tons of food pass through the average human lifetime. The gut processes all of it, sorting the useful from the disposable, feeding the trillion cells that depend on this continuous harvest.

## The Mouth's Preparation

Digestion begins before swallowing. Teeth fracture food into fragments. The incisors shear, the molars crush. Chewing increases surface area, exposing more material to chemical breakdown. Saliva floods the mouth from three pairs of glands, releasing enzymes that begin splitting starches into sugars. The tongue shapes each bite into a bolus, a soft mass ready for transit. Taste receptors signal sweetness, bitterness, salt, sour, umami—data the brain uses to predict what follows. The act of chewing stimulates the stomach to prepare acid, the pancreas to ready enzymes. The body anticipates its work before the first swallow sends food down the esophagus.

## The Stomach's Violence

The stomach is a muscular sac that churns food into liquid. Its lining secretes hydrochloric acid strong enough to dissolve metal, held back from eating the stomach itself by a layer of protective mucus. This acid bath kills most bacteria, sterilizing the meal. Pepsin, an enzyme, begins breaking proteins into smaller chains. The stomach's contractions occur every twenty seconds, grinding and mixing for two to four hours. The result is chyme, a thick, acidic soup. A muscular valve called the pyloric sphincter guards the exit to the small intestine, releasing chyme in controlled pulses. Too fast and the intestines cannot absorb nutrients. Too slow and nausea builds. The stomach balances emptying with readiness downstream.

## The Intestines' Extraction

The small intestine is twenty feet of coiled tube lined with millions of finger-like projections called villi. Each villus branches into microvilli, creating a surface area the size of a tennis court. Here, pancreatic enzymes break down proteins, fats, and carbohydrates into amino acids, fatty acids, and simple sugars. Bile from the liver emulsifies fats, suspending them in water so enzymes can reach them. The intestinal wall absorbs these molecules into capillaries and lymph vessels. Glucose enters the bloodstream. Amino acids follow. Fats travel through lymph before joining circulation. This transfer happens molecule by molecule, cell by cell, across the barrier that separates gut contents from body interior. What crosses becomes part of the self. What does not moves into the large intestine.

## The Colon's Salvage

The large intestine receives a watery slurry of indigestible fiber, dead cells, mucus, and unabsorbed nutrients. Its primary work is water reclamation. The colon absorbs ninety percent of the fluid that enters it, concentrating waste into solid form. Trillions of bacteria—the gut microbiome—ferment fiber and produce vitamins the body cannot make: vitamin K, several B vitamins. These bacteria are not invaders but residents, established in infancy, maintained by diet. They break down substances human enzymes cannot touch. Gas forms as a byproduct: hydrogen, methane, carbon dioxide. The colon moves waste forward through rhythmic contractions called peristalsis, slower than in the small intestine. Stool accumulates in the rectum. When stretch receptors signal fullness, the urge to defecate arrives.

## Newborn to Elder

A newborn's gut is sterile at birth, colonized within hours by bacteria from skin, milk, and environment. Early feeding establishes a microbiome that influences immune development and metabolism. Infants digest milk exclusively, their intestines not yet ready for solid food. Weaning introduces the enzymes and bacteria needed for complex carbohydrates and proteins. Through childhood and adulthood, digestion remains efficient. Stomach acid production peaks in youth. With age, acid secretion declines. The gut lining regenerates more slowly. Transit time increases, constipation becomes common. Enzyme production drops. The microbiome shifts, losing diversity. Some elderly lose the ability to digest lactose or absorb vitamin B12. The gut ages like all organs, its work continuing but slowed.

## When It Fails

Ulcers form when stomach acid erodes the protective mucus lining, exposing tissue to its own corrosive secretions. Inflammatory bowel diseases—Crohn's, ulcerative colitis—attack the intestinal wall, causing bleeding, pain, malabsorption. Celiac disease triggers immune destruction of villi when gluten is present, flattening the absorptive surface. Irritable bowel syndrome disrupts motility without visible damage, causing cramps, diarrhea, constipation. Pancreatitis halts enzyme production, leaving food undigested. Gallstones block bile ducts, preventing fat digestion. Intestinal obstruction stops transit entirely, a surgical emergency. Gastroesophageal reflux sends acid upward into the esophagus, which lacks the stomach's protection. Each failure disrupts the cascade. When digestion stops, the body starves despite eating.

## The Evolutionary Inheritance

Digestion predates humanity. Single-celled organisms engulf food particles, breaking them down in internal vacuoles. Early animals developed through-guts: a tube with mouth and anus, allowing continuous feeding. Fish, amphibians, reptiles, mammals all share this basic plan. Herbivores evolved longer intestines to extract nutrients from plant matter. Carnivores evolved shorter guts for rapidly absorbed meat. Humans sit between, omnivorous, with a gut length suited to mixed diet. The appendix, once thought vestigial, may serve as a reservoir for beneficial bacteria, reseeding the gut after infection. Cooking, developed two million years ago, externalized some digestion, allowing smaller teeth, shorter intestines, and the caloric surplus that fed larger brains. The gut shaped the body. The body shaped the mind.
