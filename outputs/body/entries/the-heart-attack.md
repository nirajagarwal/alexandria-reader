# The Heart Attack

**1. The Electrical Storm:** A heart attack begins not with stopping but with chaos. The coronary artery clogs — plaque ruptures, clot forms, blood flow ceases. Muscle cells downstream starve for oxygen. Within minutes they begin to die, and as they die they leak potassium. This shifts the electrical charge of surrounding tissue. The heart's rhythm, normally a coordinated wave of contraction, fractures into disorganized firing. Ventricles quiver uselessly instead of pumping. This is ventricular fibrillation: the muscle spasming four hundred times per minute, accomplishing nothing. Without intervention, consciousness fades in seconds. The brain, getting no blood, shuts down room by room.

**2. The Herrick Description:** Before 1912, doctors didn't know heart attacks existed as a distinct entity. They saw chest pain, sudden death, but couldn't connect them. Then James Herrick, a Chicago physician, described the complete picture: thrombosis in the coronary artery, death of heart muscle, the clinical syndrome that followed. He presented his findings to colleagues who found them mildly interesting. It took a decade for the medical world to realize he'd identified the leading cause of death in the modern world. Herrick himself lived to ninety-one, his own coronary arteries apparently cooperative. His paper changed nothing immediately. Medicine needed time to catch up to what he'd seen.

**3. The Medieval Substitute:** Before anyone understood coronary occlusion, people still grabbed their chests and fell. Medieval physicians called it syncope, suffocation of the heart, or angina pectoris — the strangling of the chest. They blamed vapors, an excess of black bile, or divine punishment. Treatment involved bloodletting, which lowered blood pressure and sometimes accidentally helped. Or they prescribed foxglove, the flower that contains digitalis, which slows and strengthens the heartbeat. Some patients survived. The doctors assumed their theories were correct. The heart kept its secrets. Bodies were buried, autopsies rare. No one cut open the coronary arteries to see the clots waiting inside.

**4. The Presidential Death:** When President Lyndon Johnson clutched his chest in 1955 — he was Senate Majority Leader then — doctors put him on strict bed rest for six weeks. This was standard care: don't move, let the heart heal. Johnson, bored and furious, made calls from his hospital bed, conducting Senate business while horizontal. Later research would show the bed rest was wrong. Immobility increases clot risk, weakens the body. Modern protocol gets patients upright and walking within a day. Johnson survived his attack, became president, signed Medicare into law. He died of another heart attack in 1973, four years after leaving office. His autopsy showed coronary arteries almost entirely blocked. He'd been running the country on backup circulation.

**5. The Warning Pain:** The classic symptom is crushing chest pain radiating down the left arm. But only if you're male. Women often get different signals: nausea, jaw pain, overwhelming fatigue, a sense of indigestion. This difference went unrecognized for decades because early heart research studied men almost exclusively. Researchers assumed hearts worked the same regardless of sex. They didn't. Women having heart attacks were sent home with antacids. The mortality gap persisted because the warning signs were written for the wrong population. Even now, women wait longer for treatment, are less likely to receive aggressive intervention. The symptom lists have been rewritten, but the original assumption — that the male experience is the default — carved its groove deep.

**6. The Survival Window:** From first symptom to permanent heart damage: ninety minutes. This is the golden window when intervention saves muscle. Paramedics now carry clot-busting drugs. Emergency rooms have catheterization labs running twenty-four hours. A thin tube threads up through the femoral artery, finds the blockage, deploys a stent to prop the vessel open. Blood flows again. Muscle survives. The patient, who might have died in 1950, walks out days later with prescriptions and instructions. But only if they call for help. Many don't. They wait, hoping the pain will pass, not wanting to bother anyone. The heart muscle doesn't wait. It dies quietly, cell by cell, while its owner debates whether this is serious enough to warrant concern.
