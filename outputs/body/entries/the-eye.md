# The Eye

**1. The Upside-Down World:** Light enters through the cornea, passes the iris, strikes the lens. The lens bends everything backward and flips it upside down. What hits your retina is an inverted image — the floor is ceiling, left is right. Your brain has been correcting this since infancy, running the film in reverse so seamlessly you never noticed. In the 1890s, psychologist George Stratton wore inverting goggles for eight days. By day five, his world had righted itself. His brain had learned to flip the flip. When he removed them, normal vision looked upside down for hours.

**2. The Replaceable Window:** The cornea has no blood vessels. It is clear because it is bloodless, fed entirely by oxygen absorbed directly from air and nutrients diffusing from the fluid behind it. This makes it uniquely willing to accept transplants — no blood type matching required. The first successful corneal transplant happened in 1905, decades before kidney or heart. More than 185,000 corneas are transplanted yearly. A donated cornea can restore sight up to two weeks after the donor's death. The eye's window outlasts the house.

**3. The Fossil Record:** Trilobites had compound eyes 500 million years ago — thousands of tiny lenses packed into mineral calcite, now fossilized and still translucent. You can hold a trilobite and see through the same lenses that watched Cambrian seas. Eyes evolved independently at least fifty times. Octopus eyes look nearly identical to ours but developed from completely different tissue. Evolution keeps arriving at the same solution: a dark chamber, a lens, light-sensitive cells. The physics of seeing allows only so many designs.

**4. The Blind Spot:** Your optic nerve exits through the back of your eyeball, punching a hole in your retina where no photoreceptors can sit. You have a blind spot in each eye the size of twelve full moons. You never see it because your brain invents what should be there, painting in wallpaper, completing faces, guessing at text. It doesn't leave a void; it manufactures continuity. The world you see is partially fiction, written by the neural tissue behind your eyes, filling gaps so smoothly you believed you saw everything.

**5. The Egyptian Weighing:** Ancient Egyptians believed the heart held thought and personality. The brain was scooped out and discarded during mummification. But they kept the eyes. Eyes were painted on coffins, carved into amulets, wrapped in linen and replaced in the skull. The Eye of Horus became medicine, protection, mathematics — its parts representing fractions used by pharmacists for three thousand years. In death, you could lose your brain. You could not lose the part that witnessed life and would witness eternity.

**6. The Impossible Blue:** No vertebrate eye contains blue pigment. Blue eyes have no blue in them — only clear tissue over a dark layer. Short wavelengths of light scatter back out before reaching the darkness beneath. It is the same physics that makes the sky blue. Brown eyes have melanin in the front layers that absorbs the light before it can scatter. Blue is not a color in the eye but an absence structured to create color, architecture mistaken for paint, emptiness that looks like something.
