# Sweat

**1. The Two Kinds:** Humans have two types of sweat glands. Eccrine glands cover most of the body, opening directly onto skin, producing the watery fluid that cools through evaporation. Apocrine glands cluster in armpits, groin, and around nipples, opening into hair follicles, secreting a thicker substance that bacteria metabolize into odor. We're born with all our eccrine glands — two to four million of them — but apocrine glands don't activate until puberty. This is why children sweat but don't smell. The distribution isn't even: palms and soles have the highest concentration, over six hundred glands per square centimeter, while the small of the back has barely one hundred.

**2. The Cooling Paradox:** Sweat only works because humans are mostly hairless. Our closest relatives, chimpanzees, have the same number of sweat glands but dense fur, rendering the mechanism useless — the moisture can't evaporate. This nakedness made persistence hunting possible: early humans could chase prey for hours under the African sun, letting animals faster in short bursts overheat and collapse while we kept running, sweating, cooling. A human can outrun a horse in a marathon on a hot day. We traded claws and fangs for the ability to regulate our temperature better than almost any other mammal. We are apex predators because we learned to stay cool.

**3. The Fingerprint Reader:** Sweat contains everything: water, salt, urea, ammonia, sugars, lactic acid, trace metals, and amino acids unique to each person. Crime labs can extract DNA from the sweat in a fingerprint days after it was left. During World War II, the Office of Strategic Services tested agents' stress levels by analyzing sweat composition — certain compounds spike under fear. Today's wearable biosensors read sweat in real time, detecting glucose for diabetics, cortisol for stress, even early markers of disease. The fluid is so information-dense that researchers call it "a liquid biopsy." Everything your blood knows, your sweat eventually tells.

**4. The Gustatory Response:** Some people sweat from eating. Gustatory sweating — beads forming on the forehead or upper lip triggered by spicy foods, hot drinks, or even strong flavors — affects up to thirty percent of the population. It's caused by cross-wiring in facial nerves: signals meant for salivary glands accidentally activate sweat glands nearby. The condition becomes more pronounced after damage to the parotid gland or facial surgery. In rare cases, people sweat from one side of the face only, a stark border running down the midline. The phenomenon has no name in English but is called *auriculotemporal syndrome* in medical literature, after the nerve involved.

**5. The Color Spectrum:** Normal sweat is clear, but chromhidrosis makes it run yellow, green, blue, or black. The condition, caused by lipofuscin pigment in apocrine glands, is benign but socially devastating — colored stains appear on clothing, bedsheets, skin. Blue sweat is the most dramatic form. Treatment usually involves injecting botulinum toxin to paralyze the glands. Before antibiotics, workers in copper mines developed green sweat from metal exposure, their bodies secreting what they absorbed. The medical literature includes a case of red sweat from a teenage girl eating excessive amounts of tomato-based foods, the lycopene pigment excreted through her pores.

**6. The Absence:** Anhidrosis — the inability to sweat — is discovered only when the body overheats. People born without functional sweat glands learn to avoid heat, take cold baths, wear ice vests. The condition can be genetic or acquired through nerve damage, burns, or certain medications. Without the ability to cool, core body temperature rises unchecked during exertion. Cases exist of people with anhidrosis dying from heatstroke while doing ordinary activities on summer days. The opposite condition, hyperhidrosis, drenches the body constantly, hands dripping, feet sliding in shoes. Both extremes make sufferers hyper-aware of what most people never notice: the quiet, constant work of staying precisely cool enough.
