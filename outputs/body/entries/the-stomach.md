# The Stomach

**1. The Churning Chamber:** The stomach is a J-shaped muscular bag that can stretch to hold two liters of food — about eight cups. Its walls contain three layers of smooth muscle running in different directions: longitudinal, circular, and oblique. This triple weave allows the stomach to churn food into chyme, a acidic paste with the consistency of oatmeal. The process takes two to six hours depending on what you've eaten. Fat slows everything down. Water passes through in minutes. The stomach is not where most digestion happens — that's the small intestine — but it's where food gets broken down enough to proceed.

**2. The Acid Bath:** Gastric acid has a pH of 1.5 to 3.5, strong enough to dissolve metal. The stomach produces about 1.5 liters daily. Parietal cells pump hydrogen ions across a million-to-one concentration gradient, one of the steepest in biology. The stomach protects itself with a mucus barrier that regenerates completely every few days. When this barrier fails, the acid eats into the stomach wall itself — an ulcer. For centuries ulcers were blamed on stress or spicy food. In 1982, Barry Marshall proved they were caused by *Helicobacter pylori* bacteria. He drank a beaker of the culture to infect himself, developed gastritis, then cured it with antibiotics.

**3. The Pavlovian Bell:** Ivan Pavlov won the 1904 Nobel Prize for work on digestion, not conditioning. He surgically created "Pavlov pouches" — isolated sections of dog stomach with their nerve supply intact — to study gastric secretion. The pouches showed that the stomach begins producing acid before food arrives, triggered by smell and anticipation. The famous bell-and-drool experiments were a side observation. Pavlov's real contribution was proving the "cephalic phase" of digestion: your brain can make your stomach ready to receive. The stomach is always listening to the head. Anxiety suppresses acid production. Hunger amplifies it.

**4. The Empty Warning:** Hunger pangs are the stomach contracting on itself. These "migrating motor complexes" sweep through every ninety minutes when the stomach is empty, clearing out lingering debris. They're loudest at night. The growl — borborygmus — comes from air and fluid being squeezed through the tight passages. But the stomach has no receptor for fullness. That signal comes from stretch receptors in the stomach wall and hormones released when food hits the small intestine. This is why you can still feel hungry with a full stomach, and why gastric bypass works: less stomach wall to stretch, earlier satiety signals.

**5. The Second Brain:** The stomach is wrapped in five hundred million neurons — more than in the spinal cord. This "enteric nervous system" can operate independently of the brain. It generates its own rhythms, releases neurotransmitters, and communicates via the vagus nerve. Ninety percent of the body's serotonin is made in the gut. When people say they have a "gut feeling," they're describing real neural activity. The stomach-brain axis goes both ways: anxiety causes nausea, but gut inflammation may contribute to depression. The stomach is not just digesting food. It's processing information.

**6. The Resurrection Myth:** In medieval Europe, people believed the stomach was the seat of the soul. The "caul" — the greater omentum, a fatty apron draped over the intestines — was thought to carry special power. Knights wore dried caul into battle. Some cultures believed you could transplant courage by eating an enemy's stomach. The Egyptians removed most organs during mummification but sometimes left the stomach, believing the deceased would need to eat in the afterlife. The stomach as metaphor persists: we stomach insults, have no stomach for violence, feel gut-wrenched. The organ that accepts what we give it becomes the symbol for what we can bear.
