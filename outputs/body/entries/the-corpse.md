# The Corpse

**1. The Cooling:** Within minutes of death, the body begins losing heat at roughly 1.5 degrees Fahrenheit per hour, faster in cold rooms, slower when fever preceded death. This is algor mortis — the cooling. Forensic investigators touch the liver through an incision to gauge core temperature, calculating time of death through thermal loss. But the equation shifts with body mass, clothing, air movement, the silent variables of dissolution. In winter morgues, bodies arrive already cold. In summer cars, they cook. The corpse is a cooling stone, and temperature is the first language of the dead.

**2. The Stiffening:** Two to six hours after death, calcium floods muscle cells that can no longer pump it out. Proteins lock together. The jaw tightens first, then the face, then the body proceeds downward — shoulders, arms, trunk, legs — until the whole form is rigid. This is rigor mortis, and it peaks around twelve hours, then releases as proteins decompose. Morticians must work fast or wait, because a stiffened body resists positioning. Ancient Greeks bound jaws with cloth to keep mouths shut during rigor. The dead briefly become statues of themselves before softening back into matter.

**3. The Pooling:** Blood obeys gravity after the heart stops. It settles to the lowest parts — back, buttocks, legs if upright — turning the skin purple-red where vessels leak and pool. This is livor mortis, visible within two hours, fixed by eight. It maps position: a body found face-up with lividity on the front was moved. Coroners photograph the patterns. Embalmers drain the blood before it settles, replacing it with formaldehyde dyed pink to mimic life. The corpse remembers which way was down.

**4. The Tibetan Sky:** In Tibet, where frozen ground prevents burial and wood is scarce for cremation, the dead are given to vultures. This is jhator — the sky burial. The body is carried to high rocks, cut into pieces by rogyapas who learned the practice from their fathers. Vultures descend, stripping flesh in hours, leaving only bone. The skeleton is ground with barley flour and fed to crows. Nothing remains. The practice sees the body as an empty vessel, a final act of generosity. The corpse becomes sky, becomes bird, becomes air.

**5. The Soap Lady:** In 1875, workers digging in Philadelphia unearthed a woman dead nearly a century. Her body had transformed into adipocere — corpse wax — a grey-white substance formed when body fat saponifies in wet, airless soil. She was intact, features preserved, skin waxy and hard. The process requires specific chemistry: alkaline soil, moisture, exclusion of air. Bacteria break down fat into fatty acids that combine with minerals, turning flesh into something like soap. She rests now in a museum case, the woman who became a candle that never burned.

**6. The Plastination:** Gunther von Hagens invented a process in 1977 that turns corpses into dry, odorless, permanent sculptures. Plastination replaces water and fat with polymers — silicone, epoxy, polyester. The body is frozen, sliced thin or kept whole, submerged in acetone, then vacuum-sealed in liquid plastic that hardens in every cell. Muscles stay red. Organs keep their shape. Whole humans are posed playing chess, riding horses, holding their own skins. Museums display them. Medical schools study them. The corpse crosses into a third kingdom — no longer alive, no longer decomposing, but preserved in a synthetic forever.
