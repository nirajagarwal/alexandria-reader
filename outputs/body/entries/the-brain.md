# The Brain

**1. The Folded Universe:** Three pounds of fat and protein, folded like a crumpled letter to fit inside the skull. If spread flat, the cerebral cortex would cover a small tablecloth — two and a half square feet of gray matter, wrinkled to increase surface area without expanding the head beyond what a birth canal permits. Eighty-six billion neurons, each connecting to thousands of others. More synapses than stars in the Milky Way. It consumes twenty percent of the body's energy while comprising two percent of its weight. In sleep, it merely dims. It never stops.

**2. The Phineas Gage Problem:** In 1848, a tamping iron blasted through railroad foreman Phineas Gage's frontal lobe. He survived. He walked. He spoke. But friends said he was "no longer Gage" — impulsive, profane, unable to plan. The case revealed that personality lives in tissue, that character occupies physical space behind the forehead. Before Gage, the brain was considered mostly interchangeable matter. After, scientists began mapping which regions held memory, language, restraint. Damage a specific fold and you lose the ability to recognize faces. Another fold: you forget how to be afraid.

**3. The Plasticity Window:** A woman in her fifties loses her left hemisphere to surgery — the half containing her language centers. Within months, she relearns speech. Children who lose entire hemispheres can grow nearly normal. The remaining half doubles its workload, rewires itself, claims abandoned territory. But this adaptability closes with age. Adult brains still learn, still form new connections, but the wholesale reorganization of childhood becomes impossible. The window doesn't slam shut — it slowly frosts over. Stroke victims recover more in the first six months than in the following six years.

**4. The Preserved Specimens:** Einstein's brain was stolen at autopsy, photographed, sliced into 240 blocks, and scattered across America in jars and slides. It looked unremarkable except for an unusually large inferior parietal lobe — the region associated with mathematical reasoning. Lenin's brain sits in a Moscow institute, sectioned into 30,000 slides. The Mütter Museum displays dozens in formaldehyde, gray and smooth as river stones. All equally wrinkled. All once containing someone's first memory, last thought, the face of a mother, the lyrics to a song they couldn't forget.

**5. The Phantom Persistence:** After amputation, the brain's map doesn't update. The hand area keeps firing, expecting signals that never arrive, interpreting silence as pain or presence. Patients feel phantom fingers curl, phantom ankles itch. The map is so persistent that stimulating the face — which neighbors the hand area — can create sensations in the missing limb. Touch the cheek and feel the ghost thumb. The brain builds its map of the body in childhood and defends it against all evidence. You can lose the flesh, but the territory remains claimed.

**6. The Trepanation Reflex:** Humans have drilled holes in each other's skulls for at least 7,000 years. Stone Age skulls show healed edges — the patients survived the procedure. Medieval doctors prescribed it for headaches, possession, madness. Some modern enthusiasts drill their own skulls, claiming increased consciousness, seeking the pressure release of an open fontanel. No evidence supports this. But the urge persists: the belief that something trapped inside needs release, that the bone is a prison, that the self might escape through a hole.
