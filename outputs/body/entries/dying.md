# Dying

**1. The Cascade:** The body knows before the mind. Hours or days before clinical death, the peripheral blood vessels constrict, drawing warmth inward to protect the core. Hands and feet grow cold, then mottled — a pattern called livedo reticularis, purple lace beneath the skin. The kidneys slow, darkening urine to deep amber. Breathing changes rhythm, becoming irregular, sometimes pausing for long seconds. This is Cheyne-Stokes respiration, named for the doctors who catalogued it in 1818 and 1854. The body is conducting an orderly retreat, abandoning the distant territories to preserve what remains of the center.

**2. The Terminal Lucidity:** In perhaps one in ten deaths, particularly among dementia patients, something inexplicable occurs. Hours or minutes before death, clarity returns. A woman who hasn't recognized her children in years calls them by name. A man mute for months speaks in complete sentences. The phenomenon was documented in 1887 but largely ignored until recent studies confirmed its existence. No one knows why. Some speculate that the dying brain, released from whatever process was damaging it, briefly resurges. Others suggest the healthy tissue, no longer suppressed, emerges. The lucidity rarely lasts more than an hour. Then it's gone.

**3. The Weighing:** In 1907, Duncan MacDougall of Massachusetts placed dying tuberculosis patients on an industrial scale, seeking to measure the soul's weight. Six patients. One scale. At the moment of death — which MacDougall timed with a stopwatch — he claimed the body lost three-quarters of an ounce. Twenty-one grams. He published in American Medicine and the Journal of the American Society for Psychical Research. His methodology was terrible: small sample, uncontrolled conditions, confirmation bias. The dogs he tested showed no weight change, which he took as proof they lacked souls. The number persists anyway, appearing in films and novels, more durable than the science that debunked it.

**4. The Rattle:** As the throat muscles relax and secretions pool in the upper airways, breathing produces sound. The death rattle, properly called terminal respiratory secretions. It happens in perhaps half of all deaths. The dying person is typically unconscious and not distressed by it, but the sound disturbs those present — a wet, rattling noise with each breath. Medical staff sometimes administer anticholinergic drugs to reduce secretions, not for the patient's comfort but for the family's. The rattle means death is close, usually within hours. It is one of the most reliable signs that the cascade has reached its final stage.

**5. The Moment:** There is no single moment. Breathing stops, but cells continue dying at different rates. The brain's cerebral cortex — consciousness, personality — survives perhaps six minutes without oxygen. The brainstem lasts longer. Heart muscle cells can be revived after an hour. Bone and skin cells persist for days. Gut bacteria thrive and spread, no longer confined. Some hair follicles and nail cells continue dividing for weeks. Death is not an instant but a wave moving through the body at varying speeds, each tissue falling silent on its own schedule. Legal death is a useful fiction, a line drawn across a spectrum.

**6. The Staging:** Elisabeth Kübler-Ross proposed five stages of dying in 1969: denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance. Her book made her famous and created a framework that medicine, hospice, and therapy adopted wholesale. It was based on interviews with two hundred terminal patients in Chicago. The problem: most people don't experience the stages in order. Many skip stages entirely. Some cycle through them repeatedly or feel several simultaneously. Kübler-Ross later said she regretted how rigidly her model was applied, that she'd meant it descriptively, not prescriptively. But the five stages had become doctrine, a map that often didn't match the territory.
