# Cardiac Arrest

***When the Pump Stops***

> **Category:** Threshold

The heart stops. Not gradually, not with warning, but in an instant. The coordinated electrical rhythm that has sustained every moment of conscious life ceases. The chambers no longer contract. Blood no longer moves. Within seconds, the brain begins to starve. Within minutes, cells start dying in cascades that spread through tissue. The body has four to six minutes before the damage becomes irreversible. Everything that makes a person present—thought, sensation, awareness—depends on that continuous rhythm. When it stops, the threshold between life and death becomes immediate and absolute.

## The Electrical Failure

The heart beats because of electricity. A cluster of cells in the right atrium, the sinoatrial node, generates rhythmic electrical impulses. These spread through specialized conducting pathways, causing the chambers to contract in sequence. Cardiac arrest occurs when this system fails. The most common cause is ventricular fibrillation: the ventricles quiver chaotically instead of pumping. Other arrests occur when the electrical activity simply stops, a condition called asystole. The heart may also continue generating electrical signals while the muscle fails to respond, a state called pulseless electrical activity. In all cases, the outcome is identical. No blood moves. The brain receives no oxygen.

## The Brain's Countdown

Consciousness ends within ten seconds. The brain requires twenty percent of the body's oxygen despite comprising two percent of its mass. When blood flow stops, the glucose and oxygen already present in neural tissue sustain function briefly. Vision narrows. Hearing fades. Thought fragments. The person may not realize what is happening before awareness disappears. Brain cells begin dying after four minutes without oxygen. The damage starts in the hippocampus and cerebral cortex, regions responsible for memory and higher cognition. After six minutes, the injury becomes widespread. After ten minutes, recovery without severe neurological damage is rare. The window is narrow and unforgiving.

## The Cascade Through Tissue

Other organs die more slowly. The kidneys can survive thirty minutes without blood flow. The liver tolerates similar periods. Even skeletal muscle remains viable for hours if cooled. But these organs depend on the brain's survival. A person can live without a kidney. No one survives without brain function. The heart itself, paradoxically, can often be restarted even after twenty minutes of arrest. It is the brain that sets the real deadline. During arrest, cells shift to anaerobic metabolism, producing lactic acid. Toxins accumulate. When circulation resumes, the sudden return of oxygen triggers inflammatory cascades that can cause additional damage, a phenomenon called reperfusion injury.

## The Causes

Cardiac arrest has many triggers. Most occur in people with underlying heart disease. Coronary artery blockages can trigger ventricular fibrillation. Inherited conditions affecting the heart's electrical system can cause sudden arrest in otherwise healthy people, including children and athletes. Severe trauma, massive bleeding, drug overdoses, drowning, and electrocution can all stop the heart. So can extreme imbalances in blood chemistry—too much or too little potassium, severe acidosis, profound hypothermia. The heart's electrical system is sensitive. Many insults can disrupt it. The final pathway is always the same: the organized rhythm becomes chaos or silence.

## Resuscitation's Mechanics

Cardiopulmonary resuscitation replaces the heart's function manually. Chest compressions squeeze the heart between sternum and spine, forcing blood through the body. The compressions must be hard, fast, and continuous—at least two inches deep, at least one hundred per minute. Ribs often crack. Rescue breathing or mechanical ventilation provides oxygen. An automated external defibrillator can analyze the heart's rhythm and deliver electrical shocks to restore organized beating, but only if the arrest is caused by ventricular fibrillation. For other types of arrest, only drugs and continued compressions offer hope. Even with perfect CPR, blood flow reaches only twenty-five percent of normal. It buys time, nothing more.

## Survival's Mathematics

Most cardiac arrests are fatal. In the United States, less than ten percent of people who arrest outside a hospital survive to discharge. The survival rate depends entirely on the response time. For every minute without CPR, survival decreases by seven to ten percent. After ten minutes without intervention, survival approaches zero. The best outcomes occur when bystanders start compressions immediately and defibrillation happens within three to five minutes. In hospitals, where help is immediate, survival rates reach twenty-five percent. But survival is not recovery. Many who live suffer neurological damage ranging from subtle cognitive deficits to persistent vegetative states. The brain's narrow window does not forgive delay.

## The Return

When resuscitation succeeds, the heart restarts. The rhythm returns. Blood flows. But the person does not immediately wake. The brain has been injured. Most remain unconscious for hours or days. Doctors induce hypothermia, cooling the body to slow metabolism and reduce further injury. Gradually, neural function may return. Some people wake with full cognition intact. Others wake altered—memories lost, personality changed, motor control damaged. Some never wake at all. The body can return from the threshold. The self may not. What comes back is not always what was lost. The heart beats again, but the question of who survived remains open until the brain declares itself.
