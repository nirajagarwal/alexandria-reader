# Singing

***The Voice Made Music***

> **Category:** Expression

Singing is the controlled shaping of breath and vibration into pitched sound. The lungs push air through the larynx, where vocal folds stretch and oscillate at precise frequencies. The throat, mouth, and nasal cavities amplify and color these vibrations. Unlike speech, singing sustains pitches, extends vowels, and moves through organized patterns of tone. It is voluntary but builds on involuntary reflexes. The same structures that evolved for breathing and swallowing now produce melody. Every culture has developed it. Infants hum before they speak. The elderly sing hymns when other memories fade. Singing occurs alone in showers and together in stadiums. It is among the oldest human technologies, requiring nothing but the body itself.

## The Larynx's Transformation

The larynx sits in the throat, a box of cartilage holding two muscular folds. In speech, these vocal folds vibrate irregularly, producing the rough texture of consonants and quick vowels. In singing, they stretch taut and vibrate at stable frequencies. The cricothyroid muscles tilt the thyroid cartilage forward, lengthening the folds for higher pitches. The thyroarytenoid muscles thicken them for lower tones. This is not crude opening and closing. The folds oscillate hundreds of times per second, their edges rippling in waves. The frequency determines the pitch. The tension determines the timbre. A soprano's high C vibrates at 1,046 cycles per second. A bass's low E at 82.

## Resonance Chambers

The sound produced at the vocal folds is thin, barely audible. The throat, mouth, and sinuses transform it. These cavities act as resonators, amplifying certain frequencies while dampening others. The shape of the vocal tract determines which frequencies emerge. A tenor rounds his lips and lowers his soft palate to darken a vowel. A soprano raises her soft palate and widens her pharynx to brighten one. The tongue moves forward or back, up or down, sculpting the space through which sound travels. The nasal passages open for certain tones, adding a buzzing overtone. Every millimeter of adjustment changes the sound. Trained singers learn to manipulate these spaces without thinking, the way a hand learns to grip a pen.

## Breath Control

Singing demands more air than speaking and releases it more slowly. The diaphragm contracts, pulling the lungs downward and drawing air in. The intercostal muscles between the ribs hold the chest expanded. The abdominal muscles press gently upward, creating steady pressure that pushes air through the larynx at a controlled rate. A sustained note requires this balance: enough pressure to keep the vocal folds vibrating, but not so much that they slam together. Singers train for years to maintain this balance unconsciously. The breath must support long phrases without gasping interruptions. A trained singer can hold a note for thirty seconds or more, the air released in a thin, steady stream.

## Evolutionary Foundations

The larynx evolved as a valve to protect the airway during swallowing. In mammals, it sits high in the throat, allowing simultaneous breathing and drinking. In human infants, it sits high as well, descending around age two. This descent creates a longer vocal tract, enabling a wider range of sounds. It also makes humans uniquely vulnerable to choking. The descended larynx is not an adaptation for speech or singing. It is a byproduct of other changes in the skull and throat. But once present, it was exploited. The same neural circuits that control breathing and swallowing were repurposed for vocalizing. Singing likely predates language. Melodic contours carry emotion more directly than words.

## Infancy to Age

Newborns cry but do not sing. By six months, they coo in response to music, matching pitch imperfectly. Toddlers sing spontaneously, inventing melodies as they play. Their vocal folds are short and thin, producing high, reedy tones. At puberty, hormones thicken the folds. In boys, the larynx grows dramatically, dropping the voice an octave. In girls, the change is subtler. Both sexes lose the effortless highs of childhood. Adult voices settle into their range by the early twenties. With age, the folds stiffen and thin. The voice becomes breathy, losing its clear core. Range narrows. High notes vanish first. Yet elderly singers often retain the neural pathways for melody long after speech deteriorates. Dementia patients who cannot remember their children's names will sing entire verses of songs learned in youth.

## When It Fails

Nodules form on vocal folds from overuse or poor technique, thickening the edges and creating a hoarse rasp. Polyps grow from acute strain, dangling into the airway and disrupting vibration. Paralysis of the recurrent laryngeal nerve leaves one fold immobile, producing a weak, breathy voice. Spasmodic dysphonia causes involuntary spasms, the voice breaking into strangled fragments. Presbyphonia is the aging voice, the folds bowed and atrophied. Laryngitis inflames the folds, making phonation painful or impossible. Professional singers develop these conditions at higher rates. The voice is fragile. It is also resilient. Rest and therapy restore function in most cases. Complete loss is rare but devastating. The inability to sing erases a mode of expression older than words.

## Ritual and Connection

Humans sing together at births, weddings, funerals. Religious ceremonies across all traditions include sung prayers and chants. National anthems bind strangers into collective identity. Lullabies soothe infants in every language. Work songs coordinate labor and ease monotony. Protest songs galvanize movements. The act of singing in unison synchronizes heartbeats and breathing. Choral singers report a dissolution of self into the group. This is not metaphor. Neurological studies show that synchronized singing activates the same brain regions as social bonding and physical touch. The voice is both intimate and public. A whispered song in a lover's ear. A stadium of thousands roaring a chorus. The same mechanism, the same folds, the same breath.
