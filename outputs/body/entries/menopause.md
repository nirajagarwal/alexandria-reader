# Menopause

**1. The Last Egg:** A woman is born with every egg she will ever have — roughly one million at birth, dwindling to four hundred thousand by puberty. Each month from menarche forward, a cohort of eggs begins to mature; usually one completes the journey to ovulation, the rest dissolve back into the ovary. By the mid-forties, the reserve runs critically low. Follicles stop responding to hormonal signals. Estrogen and progesterone production drops sharply. The menstrual cycle becomes erratic, then ceases. The average age is fifty-one, but the process — perimenopause — can begin a decade earlier, a slow dimming before the lights go out.

**2. The Evolutionary Puzzle:** Menopause is rare. Most female mammals remain fertile until death or near it. Only five species experience prolonged post-reproductive life: humans, short-finned pilot whales, false killer whales, narwhals, and belugas. The leading theory is the grandmother hypothesis — older women who no longer bear children can invest in their grandchildren's survival, improving the fitness of their genetic line. In traditional societies, the presence of a maternal grandmother significantly increases child survival rates. Post-reproductive orcas lead their pods to salmon runs, sharing knowledge accumulated over decades. Fertility ends; usefulness does not.

**3. The Missing Histories:** For most of recorded history, menopause barely existed in medical literature. Ancient Greek physicians attributed mid-life changes to the retention of excess blood. Medieval doctors blamed the womb itself, that wandering troublemaker. The word "menopause" was coined in 1821 by French physician Charles de Gardanne, who called it "la ménespausie" — a pause in the monthly courses. But most women didn't live long enough to experience it. Life expectancy for women in 1900 was forty-eight. Only in the mid-twentieth century, as longevity increased, did menopause become a common, shared experience requiring medical attention and cultural reckoning.

**4. The Hormone Gold Rush:** In the 1960s, gynecologist Robert Wilson published "Feminine Forever," promoting estrogen replacement as a way to prevent the "tragedy" of aging in women. He called untreated menopause a "living decay." Pharmaceutical companies, including Wyeth-Ayerst with its Premarin (pregnant mare's urine estrogen), funded his research. Millions of women began hormone therapy. Then, in 2002, the Women's Health Initiative study was halted early: hormone replacement increased the risk of breast cancer, heart disease, and stroke. Prescriptions dropped by half within two years. The pendulum has since settled toward middle ground: short-term hormones for severe symptoms, case by case.

**5. The Heat:** Hot flashes — the body's thermostat suddenly misfiring — affect three-quarters of menopausal women. They last an average of seven years but can persist for over a decade. The mechanism involves the hypothalamus, the brain's temperature control center, which becomes hypersensitive as estrogen declines. Blood vessels dilate, the heart races, sweat erupts, the face flushes. They strike most often at night, disrupting sleep, which disrupts mood, cognition, daily function. Some cultures report them rarely; Japanese women, for instance, historically reported fewer hot flashes, though this gap has narrowed as diets and lifestyles have Westernized.

**6. The Bone Hollowing:** Estrogen protects bone density. When it plummets, osteoclasts — cells that break down bone — outpace osteoblasts, which build it. The skeleton becomes porous, fragile. Women can lose up to twenty percent of bone density in the five to seven years following menopause. One in two women over fifty will break a bone due to osteoporosis. Hip fractures carry a mortality rate of twenty percent within a year. Weight-bearing exercise, calcium, vitamin D, and sometimes bisphosphonates slow the loss. The body, which once built a skeleton strong enough to carry a child, now dismantles it in silence.
