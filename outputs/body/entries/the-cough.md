# The Cough

**1. The Blast:** A cough accelerates air to 50 miles per hour in less than a second. The vocal cords slam shut, pressure builds below them like water behind a dam, then they snap open and 2.5 liters of air explodes outward. The force is enough to rupture blood vessels in the eyes, crack ribs, or herniate abdominal muscles. In one documented case, a man coughing during a flu bout tore his carotid artery. The body's most violent reflex doesn't care what breaks in the process. It exists to expel — mucus, dust, blood, food gone wrong — at any cost.

**2. The Persian Warning:** In medieval Persia, physicians recognized different coughs as omens. A dry, barking cough meant heat in the lungs. A wet, rattling one suggested cold and phlegm. But the cough they feared most was the one that tasted of copper — blood mixed with sputum, the signature of consumption. Al-Razi, the 9th-century physician, wrote that a cough lasting forty days without fever meant the lungs were "eating themselves." He was describing tuberculosis, which would kill more humans than any other infectious disease in recorded history. For a thousand years, the cough was diagnosis and death sentence both.

**3. The Wrong Pipe:** When food or liquid enters the larynx, mechanoreceptors trigger the cough in 0.2 seconds — faster than conscious thought. But some people have no cough reflex at all. Stroke victims with damaged brain stems, patients with certain neurological diseases, chronic alcoholics. They aspirate silently, food particles colonizing their lungs, breeding pneumonia. Speech therapists teach them to hold their breath while swallowing, to tuck their chin, to thicken liquids to syrup. Without the cough, eating becomes a calculated risk. The esophagus and trachea share the same intersection, separated only by a flap of cartilage and a reflex that most people never think about until it fails.

**4. The Hundred-Day Cough:** Whooping cough got its name from the desperate gasp children make after coughing until their lungs are empty. The *Bordetella pertussis* bacterium paralyzes the cilia in the airways, the tiny hairs that normally sweep out mucus. Thick ropes of phlegm accumulate. The child coughs, coughs again, coughs twenty times in succession, face turning purple, until they finally inhale with a high-pitched "whoop." Before vaccines, it killed 8,000 American children each year. Even now, unvaccinated infants can cough so hard they fracture their own ribs or stop breathing entirely. The cough lasts three months — one hundred days of paroxysms that leave children exhausted and terrified of the next fit.

**5. The Polite Weapon:** In the 1918 flu pandemic, a single cough expelled 200 million virus particles in a cloud that hung in the air for hours. Masks became mandatory in San Francisco; those caught without one faced arrest. Yet the cough remains our most socially acceptable form of expulsion — we cough into our hands, into the crook of our elbow, into nothing at all. The Japanese invented the "cough button" for public toilets, a button that plays flushing sounds to mask bodily noises. But for coughs themselves, no button exists. We suppress them in theaters, delay them in meetings, muffle them in libraries. The reflex that exists to violently expel pathogens is also the one we're trained to swallow back down.

**6. The Chronic Mystery:** Some people cough for years without clear cause. The lungs are clear, the sinuses clean, the stomach acid controlled. Yet they cough dozens of times daily, a dry hack that disrupts sleep, strains relationships, makes strangers recoil. It's called chronic refractory cough, and for decades doctors blamed anxiety or attention-seeking. Recent research suggests hypersensitive vagus nerve endings in the throat, nerves that misfire at the smallest irritation — a breath of cold air, a slight tickle. Gabapentin, a nerve pain medication, sometimes helps. Sometimes nothing does. They cough, and cough, and the body offers no explanation for its own violence.
