# The Orgasm

**1. The Cascade:** An orgasm is a sequence of involuntary muscle contractions — typically 3 to 15, occurring at 0.8-second intervals — centered in the pelvic floor but radiating outward. Blood pressure spikes. Heart rate doubles. The brain's lateral orbitofrontal cortex, which governs reason and self-control, goes dark. Endorphins flood the system. Then: resolution, a refractory period that varies wildly by sex and individual. In males, prolactin levels surge, creating a recovery window that can last minutes or days. Females often have no such pause. The entire event, from arousal to resolution, rewrites the body's chemistry for hours afterward.

**2. The Evolutionary Wager:** Why pleasure at all? Natural selection cares about reproduction, not sensation. Yet orgasm exists in species far beyond those that need it for conception — bonobos, dolphins, even some birds. One theory: it's a reward system, ensuring creatures repeat behaviors that propagate genes. Another: in humans, female orgasm may increase conception through uterine contractions that help transport sperm, though evidence remains mixed. The pleasure might be incidental, a byproduct of shared developmental pathways between sexes. Or perhaps bonding and social cohesion matter as much as conception itself.

**3. The Discovery of the Clitoris:** Gabriele Falloppio described it in 1561, but credit often goes to Realdo Colombo, who claimed discovery two years earlier and called it "the seat of woman's delight." Both men were wrong — it was never lost. Midwives and women knew. But medical illustration largely ignored it for centuries. Gray's Anatomy omitted it until 1948. The full structure — most of it internal, with roots extending 9 centimeters into the body — wasn't completely mapped until 1998, by Australian urologist Helen O'Connell. An organ with 8,000 nerve endings, twice that of a penis, barely acknowledged in medical textbooks for 400 years.

**4. The Absent Climax:** Anorgasmia affects roughly 10-15% of women and 5% of men. Causes vary: medication side effects, particularly SSRIs, which can delay or eliminate orgasm entirely; nerve damage from surgery or diabetes; psychological blocks from trauma or anxiety; simple anatomical variation. Some people never experience orgasm and never miss it. Others lose the capacity after years of having it. Treatment ranges from physical therapy to medication adjustment to therapy. But for some, no intervention works. The body sometimes withholds what it has already given.

**5. The Little Death:** The French call it *la petite mort* — the little death. Sixteenth-century writers used the term for fainting, for epileptic seizures, for any moment when consciousness briefly fled the body. By the nineteenth century, it meant orgasm specifically. The language captures something real: brain scans during climax resemble certain altered states — meditation, seizure, even near-death experience. The self dissolves. Time distorts. Mystics across traditions have noted the overlap between spiritual ecstasy and sexual release. Saint Teresa of Ávila's famous vision, depicted in Bernini's sculpture, describes an angel piercing her heart with a golden spear: agony, rapture, indistinguishable.

**6. The Ghost Release:** Some spinal cord injury patients, paralyzed below the waist with no genital sensation, still report orgasms. Brain imaging confirms it: their sensory cortex lights up during climax, though no signals travel from below. The brain, it seems, can generate the experience without the body's participation. Even stranger: reports exist of orgasms triggered by stimulation of eyebrows, feet, phantom limbs that no longer exist. The subjective sensation lives in the brain's map of the body, not the body itself. Pleasure, perhaps, is a place we visit, not a place we are.
