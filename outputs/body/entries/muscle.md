# Muscle

**1. The Perpetual Tremor:** Your muscles are never still. Even at rest, they fire in microscopic contractions called fasciculations — tiny stutters of electrical activity that keep the tissue ready. This baseline tension, called muscle tone, prevents joints from dislocating under their own weight. Without it, you would collapse like an empty suit. The tremor is so constant that complete paralysis feels profoundly wrong to the body, a silence where there should be hum. Anesthesiologists must sometimes use neuromuscular blockers to stop this firing during surgery, rendering patients more motionless than sleep ever could.

**2. The Speed Spectrum:** Muscle fibers come in two types, distributed differently in every body. Type I fibers — slow-twitch, red with oxygen-rich myoglobin — contract steadily for hours. They fill the postural muscles of marathon runners and migrating birds. Type II fibers are white, explosive, built for the pounce and the sprint. Olympic weightlifters have up to 65% fast-twitch in their thighs. The ratio is largely genetic. You can train what you have, but you cannot change what you were given. Elite athletes are often people who discovered, accidentally, that their bodies were already built for their sport.

**3. The Oxygen Debt:** Muscles can outrun their blood supply. During intense exertion, they switch to anaerobic metabolism, splitting glucose without oxygen and producing lactate as waste. The burn you feel is not lactate itself but the hydrogen ions it releases, temporarily acidifying the tissue. This system buys you seconds to minutes of power before exhaustion forces you to stop and breathe. Recovery erases the debt — lactate is cleared within an hour. The soreness that arrives a day later, delayed-onset muscle soreness, comes from microscopic tears in the fibers themselves, the necessary damage of getting stronger.

**4. The Freezing:** Rigor mortis begins two to six hours after death. Without ATP to fuel the release cycle, myosin heads lock onto actin filaments and cannot let go. The muscles stiffen in whatever position the body held at death. The effect peaks at twelve hours, then fades over the next day as enzymes begin breaking down the proteins. Coroners use rigor's progression to estimate time of death. In rare cases — drowning in cold water, certain poisonings — rigor can set in instantly, freezing a final gesture. The body's last motion becomes its temporary monument.

**5. The False Strength:** Hysterical strength — a mother lifting a car off her child — may not be myth. Normally, the central nervous system limits muscle recruitment to about 80%, protecting tendons and bones from the force fibers can generate. Extreme stress can override this governor. The adrenaline surge redistributes blood, sharpens focus, and permits fuller activation of motor units. The strength was always there; the body simply kept it locked away. Afterward, people often injure themselves: torn muscles, ruptured tendons, stress fractures. The safety limit exists for a reason. The lock is not weakness. It is wisdom.

**6. The Memory:** Muscle memory is not stored in the muscle. It lives in the cerebellum and motor cortex, a learned pattern of neural firing that bypasses conscious thought. But muscles themselves do remember, in a sense. Myonuclei — the cell nuclei that build new protein — accumulate during strength training and persist even through months of atrophy. When you rebuild lost muscle, you rebuild it faster. The body keeps a blueprint. This is why childhood athletes can return to form in adulthood, and why steroid users show lasting advantages years after they stop. The tissue forgets nothing. The history is written in the cells.
