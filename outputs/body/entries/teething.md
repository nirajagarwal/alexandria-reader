# Teething

***The First Violence of Growth***

> **Category:** Growth

The infant's body begins to reshape itself at around six months, pushing hard structures through soft tissue. The jaw swells. The gums ache and split. Twenty teeth emerge over two years, each one forcing its way from bone through flesh to break the surface. The baby cannot explain what is happening. The body simply executes its program: teeth arrive, the mouth bleeds, the child becomes equipped to bite. This is growth as invasion, the skeleton announcing itself through pain. Every human who has ever chewed food survived this first wounding. The baby drools, cries, gnaws on anything within reach, and waits for the hurt to pass.

## The Buried Teeth

Long before birth, tooth buds form in the jawbone. Ten in the upper jaw, ten in the lower. Each bud contains the blueprint for a tooth: enamel, dentin, pulp, root. They sit waiting in the bone, calcifying slowly. The baby is born with a complete set of primary teeth already formed, hidden beneath the gums. Around six months, hormonal signals trigger eruption. The tooth begins to move upward, its crown pushing toward the gum surface. The process is called osteoclastic resorption—cells called osteoclasts dissolve the bone ahead of the tooth, clearing a path. The tooth rises millimeter by millimeter. There is no anesthetic for this. The body does not pause for comfort.

## Pressure and Inflammation

As the tooth moves through bone, it presses against the gum tissue from below. The gums swell, turning red and tender. Blood flow increases to the area. The tissue stretches. Nerve endings register the distortion as pain. Some infants tolerate this well. Others scream for hours. The pain is cyclical—worse when the tooth is actively moving, better during pauses. The tooth does not emerge smoothly. It stalls, pushes, stalls again. The gum may develop a bluish cyst where the tooth is about to break through, a pocket of fluid and blood marking the impact site. When the tooth finally pierces the surface, the gum splits. A sharp white edge appears. The baby tastes blood for the first time.

## The Gnawing Response

Infants with erupting teeth become fixated on biting. They chew on fingers, toys, fabric, the edges of furniture. This is not random behavior. Pressure on the gums provides counter-stimulation, a sensory distraction from the deep ache. The act of biting also massages the gums, increasing blood flow and potentially speeding eruption. Babies will gnaw until their gums bleed, then continue gnawing. They cannot articulate the discomfort, so they apply the only remedy available: sustained pressure against the hurt. Parents offer teething rings, cold washcloths, anything firm and safe. The baby bites down, jaw muscles working, seeking relief that never fully comes until the tooth completes its journey.

## Sequence and Timing

The teeth arrive in a predictable order. The two bottom central incisors first, usually between six and ten months. Then the four upper incisors. Then the lower lateral incisors, the first molars, the canines, the second molars. The entire set of twenty primary teeth completes by age three. Each tooth takes days to weeks to fully emerge. The baby endures waves of discomfort as one tooth breaks through, enjoys a brief respite, then begins the process again with the next. Occasionally two teeth erupt simultaneously. The pain doubles. Some children teeth early, some late. Genetics determines the schedule. The body follows its own timeline, indifferent to readiness or preference.

## Disrupted Sleep and Feeding

Teething infants wake throughout the night. The pain intensifies when lying down—increased blood flow to the head makes the gums throb harder. The baby wakes crying, cannot be easily soothed, falls back asleep only to wake again an hour later. This continues for weeks. Feeding becomes difficult. Nursing or bottle-feeding requires sucking, which increases pressure on already tender gums. Some babies refuse to eat. Others want to nurse constantly, seeking comfort rather than nutrition. The saliva production increases dramatically—drool soaks the baby's clothes, causes rashes on the chin and chest. The infant's misery is obvious but cannot be fully relieved. Parents try cold teething toys, pain medication, gentle gum massage. Nothing stops the teeth from coming.

## When It Goes Wrong

Sometimes teeth erupt in the wrong sequence. Sometimes they emerge crooked or crowded. Sometimes they fail to erupt at all, remaining impacted in the jaw. Natal teeth—present at birth—occur in about one in two thousand babies. These must often be removed if loose, to prevent choking. Delayed teething, no teeth by thirteen months, can signal developmental issues or nutritional deficiencies. Teething does not cause fever, despite popular belief. Fevers during teething are coincidental infections, common because teething age overlaps with declining maternal antibodies. True complications are rare but real: gum infections, enamel defects, cysts that require surgical intervention. Mostly, teething is just pain that must be endured until it ends.

## The Permanent Replacement

Around age six, the process repeats. The primary teeth loosen and fall out. The permanent teeth, which have been growing in the jaw beneath them, push upward. This time the child is old enough to understand what is happening, but the discomfort persists. Thirty-two permanent teeth will emerge over the next twelve years. The last are the wisdom teeth, erupting in late adolescence—or not erupting at all, impacting painfully in jaws too small to accommodate them. For some people, teething never fully ends. Wisdom teeth can erupt in the twenties or thirties, requiring extraction. The body continues to push hard structures through soft tissue, even when there is no room left. Growth does not ask permission. It simply happens, and the flesh must yield.
