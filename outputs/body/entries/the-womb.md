# The Womb

**1. The Inverted Shape:** The uterus is an upside-down pear, thick-walled and hollow, about the size of a fist when empty. Its walls are three layers deep — the perimetrium wrapping the outside, the myometrium in the middle (smooth muscle that can contract with the force of 400 pounds per square inch during labor), and the endometrium lining the inside, a tissue that builds and sheds in monthly cycles. The organ itself is almost entirely muscle, designed to expand fifty times its resting volume. At birth, a female infant's uterus weighs about four grams. By adulthood, it weighs seventy. After pregnancy, it never returns completely to its original size.

**2. The Wandering Womb:** Ancient Greeks believed the uterus was an animal inside the body, capable of moving freely through the torso. Plato called it "an animal within an animal," frustrated when it couldn't conceive. Hippocrates thought it could migrate upward toward the lungs, causing suffocation and madness — hysteria, from *hystera*, Greek for womb. Treatments involved coaxing it back down with sweet-smelling substances near the vagina and foul odors near the nose. This theory persisted for two thousand years. As late as the 1800s, physicians blamed the mobile uterus for symptoms ranging from anxiety to paralysis, prescribing pelvic massage as cure.

**3. The Transplant:** In 2014, a woman in Sweden gave birth after receiving a uterus transplant from a living donor. The donor was her sixty-one-year-old mother. Surgeons connected the organ's major blood vessels to the recipient's, attached ligaments, sutured the vaginal canal. The surgery took ten hours. The uterus came alive. Menstruation resumed. One year later, after IVF, pregnancy. The baby was delivered by cesarean section at thirty-one weeks, healthy. Since then, over one hundred children have been born from transplanted uteruses. Some donors are deceased. Some recipients were born without a uterus at all.

**4. The Tipping Point:** Twenty percent of women have a retroverted uterus — tipped backward toward the spine rather than forward over the bladder. This was once considered pathological. Victorian doctors blamed it for infertility, back pain, painful intercourse, miscarriage. They invented pessaries — devices inserted into the vagina to prop the uterus into the "correct" position. Some performed surgery, shortening ligaments or suspending the uterus from abdominal wall. None of it worked because nothing was wrong. A retroverted uterus is a variation, not a defect. It usually causes no symptoms. During pregnancy, it typically rotates forward on its own.

**5. The Empty Tomb:** Asherman's syndrome occurs when scar tissue forms inside the uterine cavity, usually after a D&C procedure or infection. The walls stick together. The endometrium cannot grow. Menstruation stops. The space that should be there — the potential space — fills with adhesions. Women describe feeling their body has sealed itself shut. The condition was first identified in 1948 by Joseph Asherman, who documented seven cases of women whose periods vanished after uterine surgery. Treatment involves hysteroscopy: a camera enters through the cervix, tiny scissors cut the scar tissue apart, sometimes a balloon is placed inside temporarily to keep the walls separate while healing.

**6. The Stone Child:** In rare cases, an ectopic pregnancy in the abdominal cavity can calcify if the fetus dies and the body cannot expel it. The immune system encases the tissue in calcium deposits, forming a lithopedion — literally, "stone child." The woman may carry it for decades, unaware. The oldest known case was discovered during an autopsy in France in 1582: a fossilized fetus the woman had carried for twenty-eight years. Another was removed from a ninety-two-year-old Colombian woman in 2013; she'd carried it for fifty years, dismissing the abdominal mass as a tumor. Fewer than three hundred cases have been documented in medical literature worldwide.
