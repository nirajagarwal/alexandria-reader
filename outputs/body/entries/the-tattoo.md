# The Tattoo

**1. The Scar Made on Purpose:** A tattoo is controlled wounding. The needle punctures skin 50-3,000 times per minute, driving pigment into the dermis at depths between 1-2 millimeters. Too shallow and the body sheds it with dead skin. Too deep and the ink blurs into surrounding tissue. The dermis is permanent territory—cells there don't turn over like surface skin. The body reads the pigment as foreign but can't expel it. Macrophages arrive to consume the invader, fail, and remain at the site, swollen with ink, for decades. You wear the corpses of your immune response.

**2. The Frozen Man's Marks:** Ötzi the Iceman, dead 5,300 years in an Alpine glacier, carried 61 tattoos. They weren't decorative. Lines and crosses clustered over his lower spine, knees, and ankles—joints ravaged by arthritis. The marks were made by cutting skin and rubbing in charcoal. Modern acupuncture maps place several directly over traditional pain points. His tattoos were medicine, applied to a body that hurt. When he died—an arrow through the shoulder—his therapist's marks went with him into the ice.

**3. The Sailor's Record:** For three centuries, Western tattoos belonged to sailors, prisoners, and the dispossessed. A British sailor's body was a logbook: an anchor for Atlantic crossings, a dragon for Asian waters, a crossed cannon for military service, a pig and rooster on the feet to prevent drowning (livestock crates floated when ships sank). A rope around the wrist meant he'd worked as a deckhand. A turtle meant he'd crossed the equator. James Cook's crew brought the Tahitian word *tatau* back to England in 1769. The practice followed.

**4. The Visible Immune System:** Fresh tattoos leak—plasma, ink, blood—for days. The wound is real. Within hours, neutrophils flood the site. Within weeks, fibroblasts lay down collagen around the pigment, walling it off. The ink never leaves the dermis, but it does migrate: macrophages carrying pigment particles drift to nearby lymph nodes. Dissections of tattooed cadavers reveal colored lymph nodes, like internal tattoos the body gave itself. The stain spreads inward, invisible, mapping the same design through the lymphatic system.

**5. The Renaissance Ban:** The Council of Calcuth in 787 AD prohibited tattoos for Christians: "Let no one cause scars or marks on his body." For a thousand years, European tattoos vanished from the historical record—or went underground. Pilgrims returning from Jerusalem had crosses inked on their arms as proof of the journey, a practice the Church tolerated but didn't celebrate. Tattoos marked the边缘, the exiled, the outside. Then colonialism reversed the flow: British sailors returned from Polynesia wearing their travels. By the 1890s, London society women visited tattoo parlors in secret.

**6. The Laser's Violence:** Removal is worse than application. Q-switched lasers fire nanosecond pulses that shatter ink particles into fragments small enough for macrophages to carry away. Each session is more painful than the original tattoo—superheated ink explodes microscopically inside the skin. Black ink absorbs all wavelengths and disappears easily. Green and yellow reflect most light and persist. White ink sometimes darkens instead of fading, its titanium dioxide oxidizing under the beam. Full removal takes years. Ghost outlines often remain, the body's scar tissue remembering the shape of what was there.
