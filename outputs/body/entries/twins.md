# Twins

**1. The Split:** Around day four or five after conception, a fertilized egg sometimes divides completely into two embryos. No one knows why. The split creates identical twins — two people from one original cell, carrying the same DNA like a blueprint photocopied. If the division happens late, after day nine, the twins may share more than genetics. They might share an amniotic sac, a placenta, or in rare cases, fail to separate fully. Timing is everything. The earlier the split, the more separate the bodies.

**2. The Vanishing:** In early ultrasounds of twin pregnancies, both embryos appear. Weeks later, one is gone — absorbed by the other twin, the mother, or the placenta. This happens in up to one in eight twin conceptions. The surviving twin carries no memory, no scar. Sometimes, though, traces remain: a patch of different-colored skin, cells with foreign DNA threaded through their organs. They live as one person who began as two, carrying their twin in scattered fragments.

**3. The Minneapolis Study:** From 1979 to 1999, Thomas Bouchard studied twins separated at birth and raised apart. Jim Lewis and Jim Springer, reunited at 39, had both married women named Linda, divorced, remarried women named Betty. Both drove Chevrolets, smoked Salems, bit their nails. Both named their sons James Alan — one spelled Allan. The studies suggested genes shape more than bone structure: they bend personality, choice, taste. Critics pointed to coincidence and confirmation bias, but the Jims remained unsettling.

**4. The Language:** Twins sometimes develop idioglossia — a private language. Phonemes and syntax known only to them, used until outside language drowns it out. Grace and Virginia Kennedy spoke "Poto and Cabengo" until age eight: "Pulana Cabengo" meant "Potato and Cabbage," their nicknames. Linguists studied them in 1977, found their language mixed English, German, invented words. As they learned standard speech, their private tongue faded. Some twin languages leave no trace. Others, like the Kennedys', survive on old tapes.

**5. The Parasitic:** A fetus in fetu — one twin absorbed but not destroyed, growing inside the other's body as a mass of tissue, bone, hair, sometimes teeth. Discovered in childhood or adulthood as a mysterious tumor. Doctors have removed these remnants from abdomens, skulls, mouths. Not a twin exactly, but twin tissue that never developed, never separated, never died. It has no brain, no chance, just cells following their instructions inside the wrong body.

**6. The Mirror:** Identical twins face each other like reflections. One right-handed, one left. Hair whorls spinning opposite directions. A mole on the right cheek, then the left. About 25% of identical twins are mirror images — reversed asymmetries from a late embryonic split. In extreme cases, one twin's organs sit on the wrong side: heart on the right, liver on the left, a body flipped. They are the same person, almost, reading each other backward.
