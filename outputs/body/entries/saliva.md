# Saliva

**1. The Daily River:** An adult human makes between half a liter and one and a half liters of saliva each day — enough to fill a wine bottle. Production never stops. Even during sleep, the salivary glands produce about a tenth of their waking output, just enough to keep the mouth from turning into parchment. Three pairs of major glands — parotid, submandibular, sublingual — do most of the work, but hundreds of minor glands pepper the inner cheeks and lips. The flow increases at the sight or smell of food. Sometimes at the thought of it.

**2. Older Than Teeth:** Saliva predates chewing. Early fish had mucus-secreting cells in their mouths to ease the passage of food. As vertebrates moved onto land, these cells evolved into proper glands, adding enzymes to begin digestion before swallowing. Amylase, the starch-breaking enzyme in human saliva, appears across mammals. Dogs have almost none — they gulp rather than chew. Herbivores produce gallons of it daily. The exact composition shifts with diet. We carry an ancient river, customized by what we eat.

**3. Pavlov's Question:** In the 1890s, Ivan Pavlov cut holes in the cheeks of dogs and inserted tubes to collect their saliva. He measured output when meat appeared. Then he added a metronome. After repetition, the metronome alone made the dogs drool. The experiment demonstrated conditioned reflex, but Pavlov spent decades investigating saliva itself — its rate, its triggers, its chemistry. He won the Nobel Prize in 1904 for work on digestion, not behavior. The dogs were the method. The saliva was the subject.

**4. The Healing Mouth:** Human saliva contains histatins — proteins that kill bacteria and fungi, promoting wound healing. This is why mouth wounds close faster than skin cuts. Why animals lick their injuries. The practice is ancient and partially effective. Saliva also contains opiorphin, a painkiller six times stronger than morphine, though in concentrations too low to numb dental work. And epidermal growth factor, which speeds tissue repair. The mouth heals itself with every swallow. Kissing exchanges bacteria, but also protective proteins. The mouth is its own pharmacy.

**5. The Silk Road Spit:** For centuries, Chinese silk merchants checked pearls' authenticity with saliva. Real pearls feel gritty against the teeth because their surface is composed of microscopic calcium carbonate plates. Fake pearls, smooth glass or plastic, slide slickly across enamel. The test required no tools, just a mouth. Saliva provided the moisture needed to detect texture. Japanese pearl cultivators in the early 1900s had to perfect surface texture to pass the spit test. Auctioneers still sometimes use it. The mouth as instrument of appraisal, the tongue as laboratory.

**6. When the River Dries:** Xerostomia — chronic dry mouth — affects millions. Radiation therapy for head and neck cancers often destroys salivary glands permanently. Over four hundred medications list dry mouth as a side effect. Without saliva, teeth decay rapidly. Speech becomes difficult. Food tastes like cardboard. Swallowing requires water. Artificial saliva exists but works poorly. Some patients suck on sugar-free candy constantly. Others carry water bottles everywhere. The mouth becomes a desert. You notice saliva only when it's gone, like noticing air only when you can't breathe.
