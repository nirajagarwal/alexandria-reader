# The Stroke

**1. The Sudden Country:** A stroke is the moment blood stops reaching part of the brain — either blocked by a clot or spilled from a ruptured vessel. Brain cells begin dying within minutes, at a rate of 1.9 million per minute during a major stroke. The damage maps onto the body in precise, terrible ways: the left hemisphere controls the right side of the body, so a stroke on the left often paralyzes the right arm, the right leg, half the face. Speech may vanish entirely or become a flood of nonsense words. Time is tissue. The window for intervention is roughly four and a half hours.

**2. The Asymmetry Test:** Emergency responders use FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call. Ask someone to smile — one side of the face may not move. Ask them to raise both arms — one drifts downward. Ask them to repeat a simple sentence — words slur or disappear. The test exists because strokes announce themselves through sudden asymmetry, the body dividing against itself. But posterior strokes affecting the back of the brain skip these signs entirely. They bring vertigo, double vision, difficulty swallowing. No face droop. No arm drift. Harder to catch.

**3. The Clot Retriever:** In 2015, five trials changed stroke treatment overnight. Doctors began threading catheters up through the femoral artery to the brain, deploying devices called stent retrievers — wire mesh tubes that grab the clot and pull it out whole. Before this, only clot-dissolving drugs existed. Now patients arrive paralyzed and walk out hours later. But it requires the stroke to happen near a comprehensive stroke center, which most don't. Geography is destiny. Rural stroke patients are twice as likely to die. The device works miracles, but only if you can reach the machine.

**4. The Recovered Map:** After a stroke, the brain can rewire itself, recruiting nearby regions to take over lost functions. A violinist who lost her left hand's dexterity after a stroke spent two years retraining, and brain scans showed her right hemisphere had built new pathways to control fine motor movement. The younger the patient, the more plastic the brain. Children can lose entire hemispheres and recover language and movement that would be permanently lost in adults. But recovery is exhausting labor. Neurons that fire together wire together, so therapy means thousands of repetitions, teaching the brain new routes to old destinations.

**5. The Warning Stroke:** A transient ischemic attack is a stroke that resolves itself within minutes or hours, leaving no permanent damage. Blood flow returns. Symptoms vanish. People call them mini-strokes, but the name is misleading — they're warning shots. One in three people who have a TIA will have a full stroke within a year if untreated. The body is rehearsing its failure. Yet half of people who experience TIA symptoms don't seek medical care. The arm that went numb came back. The speech that slurred cleared up. So they wait, and the warning goes unheeded.

**6. The Locked-In Body:** A stroke in the brainstem can sever all voluntary muscle control while leaving consciousness intact. The patient is awake, aware, able to think and feel, but cannot move or speak. Most can still move their eyes vertically. This is locked-in syndrome — the self trapped inside a paralyzed body, communicating by blinking. Jean-Dominique Bauby, editor of French Elle, dictated an entire memoir this way after a stroke at forty-three, blinking to select letters as an assistant recited the alphabet. He died two days after publication. The body became a prison, but the mind kept speaking.
