# The Sneeze

**1. The Velocity:** A sneeze launches droplets at speeds up to 100 miles per hour. The spray reaches six feet in a tenth of a second, carrying particles that remain airborne for hours in still air. High-speed cameras reveal that the mouth opens milliseconds before the nose, creating a two-stage expulsion. The tongue presses against the palate, directing the force forward rather than upward. An average sneeze releases 40,000 droplets; some estimates reach 100,000. The largest particles fall quickly. The smallest — less than five microns across — drift like fog through rooms, hallways, ventilation systems.

**2. The Impossibility of Eyes-Open:** You cannot sneeze with your eyes open. The reflex is involuntary, coordinated by the brainstem's sneezing center. Signals cascade through multiple cranial nerves simultaneously: closing the eyes, opening the mouth, contracting the diaphragm and chest muscles. The eyelid closure protects the eyes during the violent pressure spike, though the popular myth that your eyeballs would pop out is false. Some people can suppress the sneeze itself through willpower or pressing their upper lip, but the eye-closing happens before conscious control can intervene. It's one of the body's few truly autonomous acts.

**3. The Photic Response:** Twenty to thirty-five percent of humans sneeze when they look at bright light. This is the photic sneeze reflex, inherited as an autosomal dominant trait. The exact mechanism remains debated. The leading theory: crossed wires. The optic nerve's signal to constrict pupils travels near the trigeminal nerve, which triggers sneezing. When the optic nerve fires strongly — bright sun after darkness — the trigeminal nerve reads it as nasal irritation. Aristotle noticed it. Francis Bacon tested it. The condition has an acronym: ACHOO (Autosomal Dominant Compelling Helio-Ophthalmic Outburst). Fighter pilots are screened for it.

**4. The Missing Sensation:** People in persistent vegetative states can sneeze. So can anencephalic infants born without most of their brain. The sneeze reflex lives in the brainstem and spinal cord, not the cortex. It doesn't require consciousness or awareness of irritation. Laboratory studies trigger sneezes in decerebrate animals by stimulating the nasal mucosa directly. The body can expel intruders without the mind knowing anything is wrong. This is why you sometimes sneeze without knowing why — the trigeminal nerve detected something too small for your conscious perception to register. The reflex runs deeper than thought.

**5. The Superstition's Geography:** "Bless you" originated in multiple cultures independently, suggesting something universal in our discomfort with sneezing. Romans said *absit omen* — "may the omen be absent." Ancient Hindus: *jivah* — "may you live." The blessing isn't about the soul escaping through the nose, as popular myth claims. That explanation appears only in the 19th century. Earlier sources suggest fear of disease — the sneeze as symptom — or the vulnerable moment when breath stops and the body convulses. In some Bantu languages, you bless yourself after sneezing. In Japan, saying nothing is polite. The sneeze demands acknowledgment, or deliberate silence.

**6. The Gustatory Trigger:** Some people sneeze after eating, especially large meals or specific foods. The mechanism involves the parasympathetic nervous system becoming overactive during digestion. Dark chocolate, spicy food, and peppermint are common triggers. The most dramatic version is the "snatiation reflex" — sneezing triggered specifically by stomach fullness. The portmanteau combines "sneeze" and "satiation." Like the photic reflex, it's genetic, inherited, harmless, and embarrassing at restaurants. Your great-grandmother probably had it too. The body confuses satisfaction with invasion, celebration with threat, and responds to a good meal as if something were attacking your nose.
