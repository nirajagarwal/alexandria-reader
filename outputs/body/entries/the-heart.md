# The Heart

**1. The Unceasing Muscle:** Your heart began beating before you had a brain to think about it — twenty-two days after conception, a cluster of cells started pulsing in rhythm. It will beat three billion times in an average lifetime, pumping enough blood to fill a hundred Olympic swimming pools. Unlike skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle never tires, never rests. Each cell connects to its neighbors through gap junctions that pass electrical signals in waves, coordinating the squeeze. Stop one part and the others keep trying. The heart doesn't wait for instructions from the nervous system — it generates its own beat from within.

**2. The Egyptian Weighing:** Ancient Egyptians believed the heart was the seat of intelligence, memory, and emotion. During mummification, they removed and preserved nearly every organ except the heart — the brain was discarded as useless. In the afterlife, the god Anubis would weigh the heart against the feather of Ma'at, goddess of truth. A heart heavy with sin would tip the scales and be devoured by Ammit, the soul-eater. For this reason, the Book of the Dead included a spell — Chapter 30B — to prevent the heart from testifying against its owner during judgment.

**3. The Transplant Line:** In 1967, Christiaan Barnard performed the first human heart transplant in Cape Town. The donor was a young woman killed in a car accident; the recipient, a fifty-four-year-old grocer named Louis Washkansky, lived eighteen days before dying of pneumonia. The second recipient lived nineteen months. Today, over five thousand hearts are transplanted annually. A donor heart can survive only four to six hours outside the body, packed in ice, racing against time. The longest-surviving heart transplant recipient lived thirty-three years with someone else's heart beating in his chest. His original heart sits preserved in a medical museum.

**4. The Broken Heart:** Takotsubo cardiomyopathy — broken heart syndrome — is real. Sudden emotional stress floods the body with catecholamines, stunning the heart muscle. The left ventricle balloons into a shape resembling a Japanese octopus trap, the takotsubo. Blood flow drops. Symptoms mirror a heart attack — chest pain, shortness of breath, irregular rhythm — but the coronary arteries are clear. It strikes mostly women over fifty and is triggered by grief, shock, or fear. Most recover completely within weeks. The heart learns to beat normally again, though the shape it held in that moment of breaking shows up on scans months later.

**5. The Heart That Thunders:** Blue whales have hearts the size of small cars, weighing four hundred pounds. A human could crawl through the aorta. It beats five times per minute during a deep dive, slowing to conserve oxygen. The heartbeat is so powerful it can be detected from two miles away with the right hydrophone. At the other extreme, the Etruscan shrew — smallest mammal by weight — has a heart that beats 1,500 times per minute at rest, twenty-five beats per second. Its entire body is a furnace, burning energy at a rate that would kill a human in hours.

**6. The Cell Memory Myth:** Heart transplant recipients sometimes report new cravings, memories that aren't theirs, personality changes. They dream of their donor's life, taste foods they never liked. Science finds no mechanism for this — neurons don't exist in heart muscle to store memory. Yet the accounts persist: a man who receives a teenager's heart suddenly craves chicken nuggets; a woman begins painting landscapes she's never seen. The heart does contain forty thousand neurons, a "little brain" that processes information independent of the cranium. Whether memory lives there remains unknown, filed under mysteries the body keeps.
