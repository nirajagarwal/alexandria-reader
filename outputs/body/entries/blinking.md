# Blinking

***The Eyes Closing Themselves***

> **Category:** Rhythm

The eyelids sweep downward across the cornea every few seconds. This happens without thought, faster than conscious control. The upper lid descends, the lower rises slightly, and for a fraction of a second the eye is sealed. Then the lids part again. This occurs roughly fifteen times per minute in a rested person, more when tired or stressed, less when focused. Each blink lasts between 100 and 400 milliseconds. In that brief darkness, a film of tears spreads across the surface of the eye. The mechanism is so reliable that most people remain unaware of it until the rhythm is disrupted. The eyes blink whether anything threatens them or not. They blink in sleep, during dreams. They blink alone in dark rooms. The body maintains the cornea constantly, compulsively, through this repeated closure that has occurred billions of times since birth.

## The Lid's Architecture

The eyelid is skin stretched over muscle and cartilage. The levator palpebrae superioris muscle raises the upper lid. The orbicularis oculi, a ring of muscle around the eye socket, closes both lids. The tarsal plates, thin sheets of connective tissue, give the lids their curved shape. Along the rim of each lid, the meibomian glands secrete oil. The lacrimal gland above the outer corner of the eye produces tears. When the lids close, they press against each other, pushing tears and oil across the cornea. The surface of the eye has no blood vessels to supply oxygen or nutrients. It depends entirely on this mechanical spreading of fluid.

## The Neural Loop

Blinking occurs in two modes. Spontaneous blinks arise from the basal ganglia, deep structures that govern automatic movements. These blinks happen without sensory trigger or conscious decision. The brain simply issues the command at intervals, maintaining a baseline rhythm. Reflex blinks respond to stimuli: a sudden light, a rush of air, an object approaching the face. Sensors in the cornea detect these threats. The signal travels to the brainstem, which sends the command to close the lids. This happens in 30 to 50 milliseconds, too fast for thought. The third type is voluntary. A person can choose to blink, though sustaining this conscious control for long reveals how exhausting attention becomes when applied to functions the body performs freely.

## The Tear Film

Each blink spreads three layers across the eye. The innermost layer, mucin, clings to the corneal cells, allowing water to adhere to the hydrophobic surface. The middle layer, aqueous, makes up most of the tear volume. It contains electrolytes, proteins, and oxygen dissolved from the air. The outermost layer, lipid, floats on top and slows evaporation. Between blinks, the tear film thins. Dry spots begin to form. The cornea starts to lose moisture. Before damage occurs, the next blink arrives, restoring the film. Without this rhythm, the cornea dries out in seconds. Vision blurs. Pain follows. The tissue begins to die.

## From Birth to Age

Newborns blink rarely, once or twice per minute. Their corneas require less maintenance, or perhaps the neural circuits governing spontaneous blinks have not yet matured. By age five, children blink at adult rates. The rhythm remains stable for decades. In old age, tear production declines. The meibomian glands atrophy. The oil layer thins, the aqueous evaporates faster, and the eyes dry out between blinks. Some elderly people blink more frequently to compensate. Others blink at the same rate and endure chronic dryness. The cornea becomes more vulnerable. The transparent window that allowed clear vision for seventy years begins to cloud.

## When It Fails

Bell's palsy paralyzes one side of the face. The eyelid on that side will not close. The cornea dries, ulcerates, and scars without the blink reflex. Patients must tape the eye shut at night and apply artificial tears during the day. Parkinson's disease slows the blink rate. The eyes dry out. Botulinum toxin injected around the eyes for cosmetic purposes sometimes weakens the orbicularis oculi. The blink becomes incomplete. The cornea suffers. Sjögren's syndrome destroys the lacrimal glands. Tears cease. Blinking spreads nothing. The eyes burn constantly. Damage accumulates despite the lids' faithful motion.

## The Vulnerable Moment

Each blink creates a blind interval. The visual cortex fills in the gap so seamlessly that most people never notice the darkness. Experiments show that during a blink, the brain suppresses awareness of motion. This prevents the disorienting sensation of seeing the world jerk downward as the eyelids fall. But in that suppressed moment, an object moving through the visual field can vanish. Magicians exploit blink suppression. So do predators. The lioness charges during the gazelle's blink. The boxer throws the punch as his opponent's eyes close. Evolution built in a trade-off: protect the cornea, but lose the world for a tenth of a second, fifteen times per minute, for an entire lifetime.

## Ritual and Symbol

Cultures recognize the blink as a marker of consciousness. The unblinking stare signals aggression, madness, or the inhuman. To outstare someone is to dominate them. Statues of gods and demons often have wide, unblinking eyes. The Egyptians painted eyes on tomb walls so the dead could see without blinking. Zen meditation teaches awareness of the blink, an attempt to notice what the body does in secret. Lovers learn each other's blink rates. Liars are said to blink more, though studies find no consistent pattern. The blink is so ordinary it becomes invisible except when absent. Then its absence terrifies.
