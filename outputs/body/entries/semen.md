# Semen

**1. The Recipe:** Each ejaculation contains 200 to 500 million sperm cells swimming in a tablespoon of fluid. But sperm makes up only 2-5% of semen's volume. The rest is a cocktail brewed by three glands: the seminal vesicles contribute fructose sugar for energy and prostaglandins to help sperm motility; the prostate adds a milky, alkaline fluid containing enzymes and zinc; the bulbourethral glands provide mucus for lubrication. The mixture's pH hovers around 7.2-8.0, slightly alkaline, to protect sperm from the vagina's acidic environment. Composition varies with diet, hydration, and time since last ejaculation.

**2. The Dutch Draper's Secret:** Anton van Leeuwenhoek discovered spermatozoa in 1677, peering through his handcrafted microscope at his own semen. The Dutch cloth merchant kept the source material vague in his letter to the Royal Society, insisting the sample was "the excess of conjugal relations" and not obtained through "sinful" means. His sketches showed "animalcules" with tails, swimming vigorously. The Church condemned the research as obscene. Van Leeuwenhoek believed each sperm contained a tiny, fully-formed human. Two hundred years passed before science understood that sperm and egg contribute equally to a new life.

**3. The Seminal Vesicle War:** During World War II, researchers discovered that seminal plasma contains extraordinarily high concentrations of prostaglandins—hormones first isolated from prostate gland secretions. These compounds cause uterine contractions, helping transport sperm. But prostaglandins affect far more: they induce labor, regulate inflammation, control blood pressure, influence mood. The pharmaceutical race to synthesize them led to medications for glaucoma, erectile dysfunction, and abortion. One class of prostaglandin, found abundantly in semen, is now used to ripen the cervix before childbirth—medical science returning the molecule to its original context.

**4. The Allergic Response:** Some people develop an immune reaction to human semen, experiencing hives, swelling, difficulty breathing, or anaphylaxis within minutes of contact. The allergy typically targets proteins in the seminal plasma, not sperm cells themselves. First documented in 1958, seminal plasma hypersensitivity affects primarily women but can occur in anyone exposed. Severity ranges from local itching to systemic shock. Treatment includes antihistamines, gradual desensitization therapy, or condom use. In rare cases, the allergy targets one partner's semen specifically while tolerating others—the immune system recognizing individual protein signatures as surely as a fingerprint.

**5. The Coagulation Puzzle:** Semen liquefies in two stages. Upon ejaculation, proteins cause it to coagulate into a gel within minutes—possibly an evolutionary adaptation to prevent leakage and keep sperm near the cervix. Then proteolytic enzymes from the prostate dissolve the coagulum over 20-30 minutes, releasing sperm to swim forward. Failure to liquefy suggests prostate dysfunction. The mechanism mirrors blood clotting, using similar protein cascades, though serving opposite purposes: blood clots to stop loss, semen clots to prevent loss, then liquefies to enable journey. The body using one chemical language for contradictory goals.

**6. The Forensic Signature:** Forensic scientists detect semen through acid phosphatase, an enzyme present in concentrations 400 times higher than in other body fluids. Under UV light, semen fluoresces—though so do other substances, making visual detection unreliable. DNA analysis revolutionized rape investigations, but the presence of semen only confirms sexual contact, not consent. Vasectomized men still produce semen, minus sperm. Post-vasectomy samples look identical to casual observation—the ghost of fertility lingering in familiar form, chemical proof that reproduction and pleasure, once joined, can be separated by a surgeon's knife and still leave most of the equation intact.
