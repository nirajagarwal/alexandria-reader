# Falling Asleep

***The Crossing Into Absence***

> **Category:** Threshold

The body does not switch off. It transitions. Falling asleep is a controlled descent through stages, each marked by specific changes in brain activity, muscle tone, and sensory processing. The cortex quiets. The thalamus reduces its filtering. Neurons that kept consciousness alert begin firing in synchronized waves. Within ten to twenty minutes, if undisturbed, the waking mind dissolves and something else takes over. This happens to every human, every night, whether they notice the passage or not. The experience is universal but remains strange: a voluntary surrender of awareness, trusted so completely that most people lie down in darkness and expect nothing bad to happen. The body knows how to do this. The conscious mind only has to stop interfering.

## The Dimming Signal

Two systems govern wakefulness. One tracks sunlight through cells in the retina that send signals to the suprachiasmatic nucleus, a cluster of neurons above the optic nerve crossing. The other tracks hours awake through adenosine, a byproduct of neural activity that accumulates in the brain throughout the day. As adenosine levels rise, they bind to receptors that suppress the arousal centers in the hypothalamus and brainstem. The urge to sleep builds as a chemical pressure. When darkness falls, the pineal gland releases melatonin, which reinforces the circadian signal. These two streams converge: the body is tired, and the world is dark. The threshold nears.

## The Letting Go

Muscles receive permission to release. The motor cortex stops sending constant correction signals to maintain posture. Limbs grow heavy. The jaw slackens. Eyelids close not from effort but from the absence of effort to keep them open. Respiration slows and deepens as control shifts from voluntary to automatic centers in the medulla. Heart rate drops. Blood pressure falls. The body begins conserving energy before sleep fully arrives. Some people experience hypnic jerks in this transition: sudden muscle contractions as the motor system misfires during the handoff between waking and sleeping control. The sensation of falling, the limbs flailing, then stillness again. A glitch in the crossing.

## Stage One

The brain enters light sleep. Alpha waves, the electrical signature of relaxed wakefulness, give way to theta waves, slower oscillations between four and eight hertz. The sleeper remains partially aware of the environment. A door closing in another room registers. Thoughts drift but remain semi-logical, blending memory with loose association. The eyes may move slowly beneath the lids. Muscle tone decreases but does not vanish. This stage lasts only a few minutes in healthy adults. It is easily interrupted. A touch, a sound, a shift in temperature can pull consciousness back to full waking. The sleeper often denies having been asleep at all.

## Stage Two

True sleep begins. Brain activity shows distinctive bursts called sleep spindles: rapid oscillations of twelve to sixteen hertz that last a second or two. These appear to play a role in memory consolidation, replaying neural patterns from the day in compressed form. K-complexes emerge as well, single high-amplitude waves that may suppress cortical arousal in response to external stimuli. The sleeper becomes less responsive. Breathing becomes more regular. Body temperature drops. Muscle tone continues to decrease. This stage occupies about half of total sleep time in adults. It is maintenance sleep, the baseline state the brain returns to between deeper phases.

## The Descent Into Slow-Wave

Delta waves appear, oscillations below four hertz that signify the deepest non-dreaming sleep. The cortex has synchronized into a slow rhythm, neurons firing together in waves that sweep across the brain. Blood flow to the cortex decreases. Growth hormone releases from the pituitary, prompting tissue repair. The glymphatic system becomes active: cerebrospinal fluid flushes through the brain's interstitial space, clearing metabolic waste that accumulated during waking hours, including beta-amyloid proteins that may contribute to neurodegeneration. The sleeper is difficult to wake. If roused, they appear confused, disoriented, slow to respond. Children spend more time in slow-wave sleep than adults. The elderly may lose it almost entirely.

## When the Gate Stays Closed

Some people cannot cross the threshold. Insomnia takes many forms: difficulty initiating sleep, difficulty maintaining it, or waking too early without return. The causes range from psychiatric disorders to chronic pain to conditioned arousal, where the bed itself becomes associated with wakefulness through repeated failed attempts. The body lies down, but the arousal systems refuse to quiet. Cortisol remains elevated. Heart rate stays high. The mind churns through worries or simply remains alert for no identifiable reason. Sleep restriction therapy exploits the homeostatic drive: limit time in bed to match actual sleep duration, then gradually extend it as efficiency improves. The body learns again that bed means sleep.

## Across the Lifespan

Newborns do not fall asleep the way adults do. They enter REM sleep directly, bypassing the staged descent. Their sleep is polyphasic, scattered across the day and night in bursts. The circadian rhythm establishes itself over the first months as light exposure entrains the suprachiasmatic nucleus. By age two, most children have consolidated sleep into a single nighttime block. Adolescents experience a circadian shift: melatonin release delays by one to two hours, pushing natural sleep onset toward midnight. School start times conflict with biology. The elderly lose sleep depth and continuity. They wake more frequently, return to sleep less easily, and spend more time in lighter stages.

## The Cultural Envelope

Humans have ritualized the threshold. Bedtime routines, lullabies, prayers, the arrangement of pillows and blankets. The !Kung San of the Kalahari sleep in short bursts around a central fire, waking to tend it through the night. Medieval Europeans practiced segmented sleep: first sleep from dusk until midnight, an hour or two of waking activity, then second sleep until dawn. Electric lighting compressed and delayed sleep across industrialized societies. The eight-hour consolidated block is a modern invention, not a biological imperative. The body adapts, but the circadian and homeostatic drives remain. The threshold will come.
