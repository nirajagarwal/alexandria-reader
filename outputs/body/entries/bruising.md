# Bruising

***Blood Trapped Between Layers***

> **Category:** Healing

A blow lands. Capillaries rupture beneath the skin. Blood spills into surrounding tissue. The body has no immediate way to drain it, so it pools. The skin above changes color as red cells break down. What begins as red darkens to purple, then green, then yellow, each hue marking a stage of chemical transformation. The bruise is a visible clock, counting days as the body reclaims what leaked. It is trauma made legible, injury without breach. The skin remains intact while everything beneath reorganizes. In two weeks, the evidence fades. In some bodies, it appears with barely a touch. In others, it takes force.

## The Impact

Something strikes the body with sufficient force to compress tissue. The skin itself is elastic, designed to stretch and return. But beneath it lie networks of capillaries, vessels so small their walls are a single cell thick. When pressure exceeds what these walls can withstand, they tear. Blood escapes. In healthy tissue, this requires meaningful impact. A fall, a collision, a blow. The force must be enough to crush cells that spend every moment resisting the pressure of blood flow from within. When it happens, there is often pain, the immediate signal of tissue damage. But the bruise itself forms slowly, blood seeping into spaces where it does not belong.

## The Spectrum

The color progression is chemistry made visible. Fresh blood is red, oxygenated and iron-rich. As it sits in tissue, red blood cells rupture. Hemoglobin breaks down. The body sends enzymes to process it. First comes hemoglobin without oxygen: a dark red-purple. Then biliverdin, a green pigment as heme degrades. Then bilirubin, yellow, as breakdown continues. The bruise is a gradient in time, older at the edges where cleanup began first, newer at the center. On pale skin, every stage shows clearly. On darker skin, the same chemistry occurs but visibility differs. Purple may appear nearly black. Yellow may be invisible. The process runs identically beneath.

## The Reabsorption

White blood cells arrive: macrophages that engulf and digest. They consume the spilled blood, process the hemoglobin, transport the iron back into circulation. Lymphatic vessels drain fluid. The pooled blood shrinks day by day. What cannot be salvaged is broken into components the body can use elsewhere. Iron returns to bone marrow. Proteins are recycled. The bruise fades from its edges inward, the perimeter cleared first. In two weeks, most bruises vanish. Large ones take longer, the volume too great to process quickly. The body has no mechanism to hurry this. It proceeds at the pace of cellular cleanup, steady and thorough.

## The Variables

Some bodies bruise more readily than others. Thin skin offers less cushion over capillaries. Age thins skin further; elderly bruise from bumps that would leave the young unmarked. Certain medications interfere with clotting. Aspirin, warfarin, steroids. Blood stays liquid longer, pools more easily. Vitamin C deficiency weakens capillary walls; scurvy produces bruises from nothing. Hemophilia prevents normal clotting; minor trauma becomes major hemorrhage. Von Willebrand disease produces the same. Women bruise more easily than men, hormones affecting vessel fragility. Athletes in contact sports bruise constantly, tissue hammered repeatedly. The same impact produces different results depending on what lies beneath.

## Newborn to Elder

Infants bruise rarely. Their activity is limited, their falls cushioned. When bruises appear without explanation, physicians look for abuse or bleeding disorders. Toddlers learning to walk bruise constantly, shins and foreheads marking every stumble. Children at play accumulate them without concern. Adolescents in sports wear them as evidence. Adults bruise less, having learned to avoid impact. Pregnancy increases bruising, blood volume up and vessels more fragile. Middle age brings the same. The elderly bruise from contact that would not register in the young. Skin thins. Vessels grow brittle. A blood pressure cuff leaves marks. Bumping a doorframe produces a hand-sized purple bloom that lingers for weeks.

## When It Speaks

Bruises should match injury. When they do not, the body is speaking. Easy bruising without trauma suggests bleeding disorders, liver disease, or leukemia. Bruises in unusual patterns raise suspicion. The elderly bruised on the back or neck. Children bruised on soft tissue. Bruises shaped like hands, belts, cords. Forensic pathologists read them like text. Age, force, origin. A single bruise is an event. Multiple bruises at different stages tell a story. The body cannot lie about when it was struck. Chemistry proceeds on its own schedule. What looks like a single incident reveals itself as repeated harm.

## The Anomalies

Some bruises occur without any impact. Spontaneous purpura: small red or purple spots from capillaries bursting under normal blood pressure. Henoch-Schönlein purpura in children, capillaries inflamed and leaking. Thrombocytopenic purpura, too few platelets to seal breaks. These appear on legs and arms, sometimes torso, tiny hemorrhages the body cannot prevent. They do not hurt. They simply arrive. At the other extreme: hematomas, bruises so large they swell the tissue, blood pooling in quantities that form palpable lumps. The body struggles to process these. They may calcify, turning to bone in muscle tissue, permanent reminders of single terrible impacts.
