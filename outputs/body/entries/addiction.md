# Addiction

**1. The Dopamine Lie:** Addiction is not simply "too much dopamine." The brain's reward system involves dozens of neurotransmitters and pathways. What changes in addiction is prediction — the dopamine neurons that normally fire when reward arrives start firing earlier and earlier, at the sight of the drug, the place, the ritual. Eventually they fire in anticipation and plummet when the substance arrives. The brain learns to expect a reward larger than anything can deliver. This is why wanting intensifies while liking fades. The addict needs more to feel less.

**2. The Rat Park Experiment:** In the 1970s, psychologist Bruce Alexander built Rat Park — a spacious colony with wheels, balls, and companions. Rats living there largely ignored morphine-laced water that isolated rats compulsively drank. The finding challenged decades of research using caged, solitary animals. Alexander argued that addiction arises not from the drug alone but from the cage. Human studies of Vietnam veterans supported this: most soldiers who used heroin in-country stopped without treatment after returning home. Context shapes chemistry. Environment is pharmacology.

**3. The Kindling Effect:** Repeated withdrawal from alcohol or benzodiazepines doesn't just reset the brain — it primes it. Each withdrawal episode makes the next one worse, lowering the threshold for seizures and intensifying symptoms. This "kindling" persists even after years of sobriety. A person who quit drinking a decade ago may experience worse withdrawal than they did the first time. The brain remembers its past addictions in ways we're still mapping. Some neural changes appear permanent. The body keeps the score in synapses.

**4. The Naloxone Reversal:** Naloxone blocks opioid receptors within seconds, yanking a person from overdose back to consciousness. Paramedics call the moment of revival "getting Narcanned" — users wake thrashing, vomiting, furious at having their high ripped away. The drug was synthesized in 1961, sat largely unused for decades, then became standard issue as overdose deaths climbed. Many users now carry it themselves. The autoinjector fits in a pocket. Some states grant legal immunity to people who call 911 for an overdose, recognizing that fear of arrest kills as surely as fentanyl.

**5. The Ibogaine Underground:** Ibogaine, from the African iboga shrub, produces a 24-hour hallucinogenic experience that some describe as reliving their life in fast-forward. Studies suggest it can interrupt addiction, resetting the brain's tolerance and eliminating withdrawal symptoms — sometimes after a single dose. But it's illegal in the United States and can stop the heart. Underground clinics operate in Mexico and Canada. Users describe emerging with clarity they hadn't felt in years. Mechanism unknown. Reproducibility uncertain. Desperation doesn't wait for clinical trials.

**6. The Chronic Relapse Rate:** Addiction has roughly the same relapse rate as diabetes, asthma, and hypertension — 40 to 60 percent of people return to use after treatment. Yet we call those other conditions medical and addiction moral. The brain changes are visible on scans: shrunken frontal cortex, hyperactive amygdala, altered dopamine receptors. These changes persist long after the substance leaves the body. Recovery isn't abstinence. It's rebuilding a brain that has learned, at the synaptic level, that nothing matters more than the next dose.
