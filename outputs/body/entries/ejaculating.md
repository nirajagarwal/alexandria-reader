# Ejaculating

***The Body's Explosive Release***

> **Category:** Reproduction

Ejaculation is the sudden expulsion of semen from the penis, the culmination of sexual arousal. In the seconds before, smooth muscles in the reproductive tract contract in sequence, drawing fluid from the seminal vesicles, the prostate, and the vas deferens into the urethra. Pressure builds. Then rhythmic contractions of the pelvic floor muscles—the bulbospongiosus, the ischiocavernosus—force the semen outward in pulses, typically three to seven spurts over three to ten seconds. The heart races. Blood pressure spikes. Muscle tension throughout the body peaks and releases. The brain floods with oxytocin and prolactin. For most men, this is followed by a refractory period during which another ejaculation cannot occur, a span that lengthens with age. This is the body's method of delivering sperm, but it is also one of the most intense physical experiences a human body produces.

## The Nervous System's Orchestration

Ejaculation requires precise coordination between the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems. Arousal—the filling of erectile tissue with blood, the erection itself—is primarily parasympathetic. But ejaculation is sympathetic. As stimulation intensifies, nerve signals from the spinal cord trigger the emission phase: smooth muscle contractions push semen into the posterior urethra. The internal urethral sphincter closes, preventing backward flow into the bladder. Then the expulsion phase: somatic nerves activate the pelvic floor muscles, creating the rhythmic contractions that propel semen out. This handoff between neural systems is why ejaculation feels like a threshold crossing, a point of no return. Once emission begins, expulsion follows automatically. The body has committed.

## The Components

Semen is not a single fluid but a mixture assembled in stages. The seminal vesicles contribute about sixty percent of the volume: a thick, yellowish fluid rich in fructose, which fuels sperm, and prostaglandins, which may help sperm motility. The prostate adds a milky, slightly acidic secretion containing enzymes that liquefy the semen after ejaculation, plus zinc and citric acid. The vas deferens delivers sperm from the testes, mixed with fluid from the epididymis. The bulbourethral glands release a small amount of clear fluid before ejaculation, sometimes containing sperm, which is why withdrawal is unreliable as contraception. The typical volume is two to five milliliters, containing forty million to three hundred million sperm. The liquid coagulates immediately after ejaculation, then liquefies within twenty minutes, a process thought to help sperm reach the cervix.

## Across the Lifespan

Boys can have orgasms from infancy, but ejaculation does not begin until puberty, when the seminal vesicles and prostate mature. The first ejaculation, semenarche, typically occurs between ages eleven and fifteen, often during sleep. Volume and sperm count peak in the late teens and twenties. With age, testosterone declines, and ejaculation changes. The volume decreases. The force of expulsion weakens. The refractory period lengthens—minutes in youth, hours or days in old age. Some men in their seventies and eighties ejaculate rarely or not at all, though they may still experience orgasm. The contractions that powered ejaculation in youth become less vigorous, the pelvic floor muscles less responsive. But the neurological circuitry remains. The body still knows the pattern.

## The Refractory Period

After ejaculation, most men enter a refractory period during which another erection and ejaculation are physiologically impossible. This is unique to males. The mechanism involves prolactin, which surges after orgasm and suppresses dopamine, the neurotransmitter driving sexual arousal. Oxytocin also rises, promoting relaxation and bonding. The sympathetic nervous system, dominant during ejaculation, gives way to parasympathetic recovery. The penis becomes hypersensitive, sometimes painfully so. The refractory period varies widely: minutes for young men, hours for older men, sometimes a full day. A few men report no refractory period and can ejaculate multiple times in quick succession, though this is rare. The evolutionary purpose is unclear. It may prevent overexertion. It may space reproductive attempts. The body enforces rest.

## When It Fails

Premature ejaculation, ejaculation within one to two minutes of penetration, is the most common sexual dysfunction in men, affecting roughly one in three at some point. The cause is often psychological—anxiety, stress—but may involve serotonin signaling in the brain or heightened sensitivity in the penis. Delayed ejaculation, the inability to ejaculate despite stimulation, is less common but distressing. Causes include medication side effects, nerve damage, hormonal imbalances, or psychological factors. Retrograde ejaculation occurs when semen enters the bladder instead of exiting the urethra, caused by nerve damage, diabetes, or surgery affecting the bladder neck. The orgasm occurs, but no semen appears. Anejaculation, the complete absence of ejaculation, can result from spinal cord injury, multiple sclerosis, or certain medications. The wiring breaks. The body cannot complete the sequence.

## The Evolutionary Imperative

Ejaculation is the delivery mechanism for sperm, but the intensity of the experience suggests more. The pleasure may function as a motivator, ensuring that men pursue sexual activity despite effort, risk, or social constraint. The refractory period may prevent sperm depletion, spacing reproductive attempts. The volume and force of ejaculation may increase the likelihood of conception, propelling sperm deeper into the vagina. Some researchers propose that the pulsatile contractions help sperm navigate the cervix. The prostate's secretions may protect sperm from the acidic environment of the vagina. The coagulation and liquefaction of semen may serve to keep semen in place initially, then release it for transit. Every aspect has been shaped by millions of years of selection. The body has optimized for one outcome: the sperm reaching the egg.

## The Cultural Weight

Few bodily functions carry as much symbolic, religious, and social meaning. Many religions have regulated ejaculation: prohibitions against masturbation, rules about nocturnal emissions, requirements for ritual cleansing. The ancient Greeks considered semen a vital essence, its loss debilitating. Victorian medicine warned that ejaculation depleted the body, causing illness and insanity. This anxiety persists in modern "semen retention" movements, which claim abstaining increases energy, focus, or masculinity, despite no physiological basis. Some athletes avoid ejaculation before competition, believing it weakens performance, though studies find no such effect. Pornography has shaped expectations, depicting ejaculation as a visual spectacle, distorting perceptions of volume, force, and control. The body does what it does, but culture insists on making it mean something beyond itself.
