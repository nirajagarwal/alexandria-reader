# The Seizure

**1. The Storm:** A seizure is an electrical storm in the brain. Neurons that normally fire in coordinated patterns suddenly synchronize, discharging together in waves that spread across cortical tissue. The experience depends on where the storm begins. In the motor cortex, muscles convulse. In the temporal lobe, smell and memory collide — burnt toast, childhood kitchens, déjà vu so intense it feels like prophecy. The visual cortex produces geometric hallucinations, zigzag fortifications that medieval mystics called the architecture of heaven. Most seizures last under two minutes. The brain exhausts itself, neurotransmitters depleted, and silence returns.

**2. The Sacred Disease:** Hippocrates rejected the term. Writing in 400 BCE, he argued that epilepsy was no more divine than any other illness, caused not by gods but by an excess of phlegm in the brain. The name stuck anyway. Romans called epileptics *lunatici*, believing seizures followed the moon. Saints who experienced visions during seizures — Joan of Arc, Paul on the Damascus road, Teresa of Ávila — were either prophets or patients, depending on who was judging. Dostoevsky, who had temporal lobe epilepsy, wrote that the aura before his seizures brought "a feeling of happiness such as it is quite impossible to imagine in a normal state."

**3. The Patterns:** Electroencephalography revealed what the storm looks like: spike-and-wave patterns on paper, rhythms so distinctive that neurologists can diagnose seizure type from the trace alone. Absence seizures produce three-per-second spikes; the patient stares blankly for ten seconds, consciousness suspended, then returns with no memory of leaving. Generalized tonic-clonic seizures — what used to be called grand mal — show fast spikes followed by slower waves as muscles contract and release. The recording looks like mountain ranges. Between seizures, in some patients, the EEG shows nothing abnormal. The brain keeps its secret.

**4. The Bite:** During a tonic-clonic seizure, jaw muscles clamp with enough force to crack teeth. The tongue, thick and muscular, often catches between molars. The resulting laceration is typically on the side, not the tip — a detail that helps distinguish real seizures from psychogenic ones. Blood fills the mouth. Foam appears at the lips, pink-tinged, frightening to witnesses. The old advice was to put something between the teeth, a wallet or spoon, to prevent the bite. This causes more harm than it prevents. The tongue heals. Broken teeth and jaws do not.

**5. The Word:** *Epilepsy* comes from the Greek *epilambanein*, "to seize upon" — something taking hold of you from outside. Old English had *fallende evel*, the falling evil. French called it *le haut mal*, the high sickness, as opposed to *le petit mal*, the small one. Each name tried to capture the sensation of being overtaken. Seizure itself is from Latin *capere*, to take or grasp, the same root as *capture*. The vocabulary of possession and theft. Something reaching into your skull and grabbing hold. Language has never quite known whether to blame the brain or something beyond it.

**6. The Surgery:** When medication fails, surgeons can remove the seizure focus — the cluster of neurons where the storm begins. The operation requires the patient to be awake. Cortical tissue has no pain receptors, but surgeons need to map language and motor function before cutting. They touch electrodes to the surface of the brain while the patient speaks or moves fingers. Stay away from this area. This part is safe. Sometimes they remove entire lobes. Henry Molaison lost both hippocampi to seizure surgery in 1953 and could no longer form new memories. His seizures stopped. He lived in an eternal present until he died in 2008.
