# Mucus

**1. The Barrier:** Your body produces about a liter of mucus each day. Most of it you swallow without noticing. It lines your respiratory tract, digestive system, and other openings — a thin, sticky film that traps dust, bacteria, viruses, and anything else trying to get inside. The mucus escalator in your lungs moves particles upward at roughly one centimeter per minute, eventually delivering them to your throat where they're swallowed and destroyed by stomach acid. Without this constant production and transport, your lungs would fill with debris within hours. You are always drowning, always being saved.

**2. The Architecture:** Mucus is 95% water, but that remaining 5% makes it something stranger than spit. Mucin proteins — long, bottle-brush molecules studded with sugar chains — form a mesh that can stretch and contract. The structure changes with hydration: too little water and it becomes a plug; too much and it flows like soup. Healthy mucus has the consistency of raw egg white. Cystic fibrosis patients produce mucus with defective salt channels, making it thick enough to clog and kill. The difference between breathing and suffocating can be measured in water molecules.

**3. The Ancient Defense:** Hagfish produce mucus as a weapon. When threatened, they release proteins that, upon contact with seawater, expand into a liter of slime in less than a second. The gel clogs the gills of attacking fish. Sharks have suffocated on it. The hagfish ties itself into a knot and scrapes the slime off its own body, then swims away. Their mucus genes are 500 million years old — older than bones, older than jaws. We share the chemistry. Your nose runs for the same reason a hagfish survives: mucus has always been about keeping the outside out.

**4. The Color Code:** Clear mucus means your defenses are working normally. White or cloudy means your immune system has mobilized — increased blood flow, more white cells. Yellow or green appears when those white blood cells die and release an enzyme called myeloperoxidase, which contains iron. You are blowing out the corpses of your own defenders mixed with the corpses of invaders. Brown or black mucus can mean inhaled smoke, dirt, or old blood. Pink or red means fresh blood, a breach in the barrier. Mucus is a battlefield report written in color.

**5. The Victorian Obsession:** Phlegm was once considered one of the four humors governing health and temperament. To be "phlegmatic" meant having an excess of it — supposedly making you calm, rational, and cold. Spitting was a medical act. Spittoons were furniture. The 19th century "phthisis" epidemic — tuberculosis — made blood-streaked sputum a symbol of romantic tragedy and slow death. Physicians examined mucus like fortune-tellers reading tea leaves, diagnosing disease by texture and hue. We stopped looking so closely only when we found better tests. The mucus still knows.

**6. The Outliers:** Some people produce almost no mucus — a rare condition called primary ciliary dyskinesia. Their cilia, the tiny hairs that move mucus, don't beat properly. They suffer chronic infections, infertility, and often have their organs reversed left-to-right, because the same cilia that sweep mucus also guide embryonic development. Meanwhile, goblet cells — the mucus-producing cells lining your airways — can multiply wildly in chronic bronchitis, transforming lungs into slime factories. The body knows only feast or famine. There is no moderate mucus disorder.
