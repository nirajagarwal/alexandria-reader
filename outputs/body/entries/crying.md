# Crying

***Salt Water from the Soul***

> **Category:** Expression

Tears form in the lacrimal glands, small almond-shaped organs tucked into the upper outer corners of each eye socket. When grief, pain, or overwhelming emotion arrives, the limbic system sends signals through the autonomic nervous system to these glands. They flood the eye with fluid. The tears overflow the lower lid, spill down the cheeks. The body shakes with sobs—spasms of the diaphragm and intercostal muscles, the same ones used in breathing but now jerking in irregular bursts. The face contorts. The throat tightens. The chest heaves. This is not voluntary. The body does this whether the mind consents or not. Every human cries. Newborns do it without tears for the first weeks, then with tears for years. Adults do it less, or hide it, but the mechanism remains. The body insists on this release.

## The Machinery of Tears

Three types of tears exist. Basal tears coat the eye constantly, keeping the cornea moist and clear. Reflex tears respond to irritants—onion fumes, dust, wind—and flush the eye clean. Emotional tears arrive unbidden when the limbic system, particularly the hypothalamus, detects psychological distress. These tears carry different chemistry: higher concentrations of stress hormones, endorphins, and protein. The lacrimal glands, fed by branches of the facial nerve, can produce them in astonishing volume. During heavy crying, tears stream faster than the nasolacrimal ducts can drain them into the nose. They spill over. The nose runs because tears flow backward into the nasal cavity. Crying drenches the face because the drainage system cannot keep pace with production.

## The Breath That Breaks

Sobbing is a respiratory convulsion. The diaphragm contracts in sharp, involuntary spasms. The glottis—the opening between the vocal cords—closes intermittently, creating the characteristic catch in the breath. Air rushes in through a narrowed airway, producing the gasping inhale. The chest and shoulders heave. The abdominal muscles tighten and release in waves. This disrupts normal breathing rhythm completely. Some sobs are silent, just the shuddering torso. Others produce sound: wails, keening, the raw noise of air forced through a constricted throat. The autonomic nervous system controls this. The crying person cannot simply stop, cannot breathe normally by willing it. The body must exhaust the pattern before control returns.

## Infant to Elder

Newborns cry without tears for the first two to three weeks. The lacrimal glands exist but do not yet produce emotional tears. They cry to signal need—hunger, pain, cold, fear. The tears arrive later, once the glands mature. Children cry frequently and openly. Toddlers may cry for hours, their small bodies wracked with sobs over what seems trivial to adults. The capacity is immense. Adolescence brings cultural pressure to suppress it, particularly for boys. Adult crying frequency varies enormously: some weep weekly, others go years without tears. The elderly often cry more easily again. The threshold lowers. Tears come at memories, at beauty, at news both good and terrible. The lacrimal glands still function, though they produce less basal fluid, leading to dry eyes. Emotional tears, however, remain.

## When Grief Becomes Pathology

Some people cannot cry. Hypohidrotic ectodermal dysplasia damages lacrimal glands from birth. Sjögren's syndrome destroys them in adulthood. These individuals experience emotion fully but produce no tears. The absence feels wrong, a release valve sealed shut. Others cry too easily. Pseudobulbar affect, often following stroke or neurological disease, causes uncontrollable crying or laughing unconnected to emotion. The person sobs without sadness, embarrassed by their own face. Depression can either suppress crying entirely—the person feels they should weep but cannot—or make it constant, tears arriving at the smallest prompt. Prolonged crying, lasting hours daily for weeks, signals clinical depression or complicated grief. The body has shifted into a state it cannot exit without intervention.

## The Social Crucible

Most cultures have rules about crying. Who may cry, when, in front of whom. Women are permitted tears more readily than men in most societies, though the biology does not differ. Men cry less in public, more in private, or not at all. The suppression begins in childhood. Boys learn early that tears mean weakness. The phrase "boys don't cry" translates across languages. Grief rituals prescribe crying: wakes, funerals, mourning periods when tears are not just allowed but expected. Professional mourners existed in many cultures, hired to wail at funerals, to cry for the family. Crying in front of strangers violates norms in some contexts, builds intimacy in others. To cry in front of someone is to be vulnerable. The person who comforts a crying individual enters a role of trust. The tears themselves communicate more than words.

## The Chemistry of Release

Emotional tears contain leucine enkephalin, a natural painkiller, and adrenocorticotropic hormone, released during stress. Crying may literally drain stress hormones from the body through the tears themselves. Many people report feeling better after crying—lighter, calmer, exhausted but relieved. This is not universal. Some feel worse: headaches, swollen eyes, raw throat, the shame of having broken down. But the pattern suggests crying serves a regulatory function. The body accumulates tension and discharges it through tears. The autonomic nervous system, locked in sympathetic overdrive during emotional crisis, shifts toward parasympathetic afterward. Heart rate slows. Breathing deepens. The person may sleep. Suppressing tears prevents this release. The stress remains, the tension unresolved. Those who cannot cry or will not cry carry what might have been wept away.

## Thresholds and Triggers

Some events reliably trigger tears: death of a loved one, physical agony, betrayal, overwhelming beauty. Others are idiosyncratic. A particular song. The kindness of a stranger. Seeing someone else cry. Onions aside, the triggers are emotional, not mechanical. But what constitutes "overwhelming emotion" varies. Some people cry when angry, the tears arriving against their will, muddying the expression of rage. Others cry from frustration, from helplessness, from being deeply moved by art or nature. Athletes cry after victory and defeat. The tears mean triumph, or devastation, or both. Actors cry on command, triggering the mechanism by summoning memory or imagination. The lacrimal glands cannot distinguish between genuine grief and performed grief. The body responds to the signal from the limbic system. Once that signal arrives, the tears follow. The face crumples. The breath breaks. The salt water flows.
