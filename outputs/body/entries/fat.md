# Fat

**1. The Insulation Economy:** Fat stores energy at nine calories per gram, more than double what protein or carbohydrate can manage. A man of average weight carries enough stored fat to walk from New York to Boston without eating. The body hoards it in adipocytes — cells that can swell to fifty times their empty size, balloons of triglyceride wrapped in membrane. When food grows scarce, hormones unlock these stores. The fat breaks down, floods the bloodstream, feeds the brain and muscles. This is why humans survived ice ages. This is why dieting feels like fighting your own survival instinct.

**2. The Thermal Layer:** Subcutaneous fat sits just beneath the skin, a living insulation that kept our ancestors warm before we invented clothing. Whales and seals layer it thick — blubber that lets them swim arctic waters without freezing. Humans lost most of their body hair but kept the fat, distributed unevenly: more on torsos, less on extremities. Women carry more than men, especially on hips and thighs, a reserve for pregnancy and nursing. In extreme cold, this fat can mean the difference between hypothermia and survival. In extreme heat, it becomes a liability — too much insulation when you need to shed warmth.

**3. The Hormone Factory:** For centuries, anatomists saw fat as inert packing material. They were wrong. Fat tissue secretes leptin, which tells the brain when you've eaten enough. It produces adiponectin, which regulates blood sugar. It converts testosterone to estrogen, makes inflammatory signals, talks to the immune system. Liposuction patients sometimes develop metabolic problems afterward — you've removed an endocrine organ. The body needs fat to function. Too little and fertility drops, bones weaken, hormones crash. The fashion industry's ideal is often the medical definition of malnourishment.

**4. The Yellow and the Brown:** White fat stores energy. Brown fat burns it, generating heat through packed mitochondria that give it a darker color. Babies have substantial brown fat, concentrated between the shoulder blades — they can't shiver yet, so they burn calories for warmth instead. Adults were thought to have none until PET scans revealed deposits in the neck and upper back, active during cold exposure. Some researchers now study brown fat activation as an obesity treatment: what if you could train the body to burn its own white fat for heat?

**5. The Marble and the Grain:** Intramuscular fat — the white streaks in a ribeye, the marbling that makes Wagyu beef expensive — makes meat tender and flavorful. It melts during cooking, bastes the muscle fibers from inside. In humans, athletes have almost none; sedentary people accumulate it slowly. Visceral fat is worse: it wraps around organs in the abdomen, invisible from outside, secreting inflammatory compounds that promote heart disease and diabetes. You can be thin and still carry dangerous amounts of visceral fat. You can be heavy and metabolically healthy. The mirror lies more than people think.

**6. The Preserved Bodies:** Bog bodies from Iron Age Europe emerged from peat after two thousand years with skin and fat intact — the acidic, oxygen-poor water prevented decay. Soap-like adipocere forms when fat tissue interacts with water and bacteria, transforming into a waxy substance that mummifies corpses in shipwrecks and wet graves. Victorian anatomists called it "grave wax." It feels like cold cheese. Bodies recovered from the Titanic wreck site had faces and hands reduced to adipocere, features smoothed but recognizable. Fat outlasts flesh, sometimes by centuries.
