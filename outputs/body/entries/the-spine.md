# The Spine

**1. The Column of Thirty-Three:** Twenty-four moveable bones stacked atop nine fused ones. The vertebrae increase in size from neck to pelvis — the cervicals thin and hollow, the lumbars thick as fists. Between each pair sits a disc of cartilage with a jelly center, compressible as a shock absorber. The whole assembly curves in four waves: neck forward, chest back, lower back forward, sacrum back. Stand up straight and you're actually standing in S-curves. The spinal cord threads through holes in each vertebra, passing signals between brain and body at 268 miles per hour. Damage one vertebra and everything below it goes dark.

**2. The Fish Inheritance:** Your spine remembers being horizontal. Early vertebrates swam with side-to-side flexion — the same motion a running dog still uses. When our ancestors stood upright, the same column that worked beautifully parallel to the ground began bearing weight it never evolved for. The lower back curves inward to balance the head's forward position, creating a perpetual compression point. This is why back pain is nearly universal in humans and rare in quadrupeds. We traded stability for height. The spine pays the debt daily.

**3. The Bone Screw Revolution:** In 1891, a German surgeon named Berthold Hadra wired together broken vertebrae with steel piano wire. It failed. For decades, spinal fusion meant bed rest and plaster casts. Then Roy Camille invented the pedicle screw in 1963 — a threaded bolt that could anchor into the thick stem of each vertebra without touching the spinal cord. Surgeons could suddenly stabilize the spine from within. Now over 400,000 Americans get spinal fusion surgery yearly, their vertebrae joined with screws and rods, trading flexibility for relief from pain that makes standing unbearable.

**4. The Emperor's Hunch:** Ankylosing spondylitis turns the spine to bone. The vertebrae fuse together, the discs calcify, the whole column becomes a single rigid structure. Left untreated, the spine curves forward into a permanent stoop. This afflicted the Roman Emperor Caracalla, whose coins show him in profile with head thrust forward. Contemporary accounts describe his "bent posture" and chronic rage — possibly from unrelenting pain. The disease was called "bamboo spine" before anyone understood it was the immune system attacking the joints. Now biologics can stop the fusion, but cannot reverse it. What turns to bone stays bone.

**5. The Serpent Symbol:** Every medical symbol features a snake wrapped around a staff — the Rod of Asclepius. One theory traces this to ancient treatment for Guinea worm, a parasite extracted by slowly winding it around a stick. But the snake-spine connection runs deeper. In kundalini yoga, spiritual energy coils at the spine's base like a serpent, rising through chakras aligned with the vertebrae. Chinese medicine maps the Du meridian up the spine's center. The caduceus shows two snakes intertwined like the sympathetic nerve chains running parallel to the cord. The spine as pathway for forces beyond nerve signals — a symbolism that predates understanding of the nervous system itself.

**6. The Tail We Keep:** The coccyx is four fused vertebrae — all that remains of our tail. Most people never notice it until they fall on ice and land hard on their tailbone. Then it makes itself known: a deep, unreachable ache that hurts to sit, to stand, to exist. Unlike other bones, the coccyx rarely breaks clean. It dislocates, bends, fractures internally. Occasionally humans are born with vestigial tails, complete with muscle and nerves but no bone — surgical removal is routine. The coccyx serves as an anchor point for pelvic floor muscles, but it's largely decorative. A monument to the thirty-million-year-old body plan we left behind when we came down from the trees.
