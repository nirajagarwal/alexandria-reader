# Tears

**1. Three Kinds:** The eye makes three chemically distinct fluids. Basal tears coat the cornea constantly, a thin film refreshed every few seconds. Reflex tears flood in response to irritation — onion vapors, eyelashes, wind. Emotional tears arrive only in humans, triggered by grief, rage, joy, or beauty. Each type has a different protein signature. Under a microscope, emotional tears crystallize into patterns more complex than the others, salt forming shapes like frost on glass. The body tags its sorrows at the molecular level.

**2. The Egyptian Weighing:** Ancient Egyptians believed tears held weight in the afterlife. They collected mourners' tears in small bottles called lacrimatories, sealed them, and buried them with the dead. The more tears, the more beloved the deceased. Romans adopted the practice. Archaeologists find these vessels in tombs across the Mediterranean — thumb-sized vials of evaporated grief. Whether the bottles actually held tears or perfumed oil remains debated. Either way, people needed to believe sorrow could be measured, held, kept.

**3. The Punctum:** Tears drain through two tiny holes in the inner corners of your eyelids, one in the upper lid, one in the lower. These are the puncta lacrimalia. They lead to narrow ducts that funnel tears down into your nose — which is why crying makes you sniffle. When people "cry their eyes out," they're flooding this drainage system. Blocked puncta cause chronic watering. Ophthalmologists can plug them deliberately with microscopic silicone stoppers to keep dry eyes moist. Your sadness drips into the same cavity that smells the world.

**4. Crocodile Syndrome:** Some stroke patients develop a condition called gustatory lacrimation. They cry when they eat. The neural wiring between salivary glands and tear ducts gets crossed during healing. Bite into a sandwich and tears roll down. The syndrome is also called "crocodile tears" — not because crocodiles fake emotion, but because crocodiles actually weep while eating. Their tears aren't sympathetic. The act of swallowing stimulates glands near their eyes. Ancient observers thought crocodiles cried from remorse while devouring prey. They were watching simple hydraulics and seeing guilt.

**5. The Withholding:** Nineteenth-century physicians warned that suppressing tears caused madness, hysteria, and "congestions of the brain." They were wrong about the mechanism but not entirely wrong. Modern studies find that chronic tear suppression correlates with higher stress hormones and increased physical pain sensitivity. Emotional tears contain stress chemicals the body is expelling. People who can't cry after trauma — alexithymia, emotional numbing — report feeling physically trapped. The unshed tears don't go nowhere. They're reabsorbed into tissue, recycled into blood. You carry your crying inside you.

**6. The Oldest Story:** Scientists debate why humans evolved emotional crying. Other primates don't do it. The leading theory: tears are a signal that can't be faked. Dilated pupils, flushed cheeks — those can be controlled. But lacrimal glands flooding your face? That's involuntary testimony. Tears announce vulnerability so clearly that even enemies across a battlefield can read them. They disarm. They demand response. Somewhere in our deep past, the humans who could signal their breaking point survived better than the ones who couldn't. We are descended from the ones who knew when to weep.
