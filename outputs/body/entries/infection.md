# Infection

**1. The Invasion:** An infection begins when a pathogen breaches the body's barriers — skin, mucous membranes, the acid moat of the stomach. Bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites exploit wounds, inhalation, ingestion, or sexual contact. Once inside, they replicate. A single bacterium can become a billion in twenty-four hours. The body detects molecular signatures it doesn't recognize — foreign proteins, unusual sugars, viral RNA. White blood cells swarm. Temperature rises. The battlefield is your flesh, and inflammation is the smoke from the fighting. You feel this as heat, swelling, redness, pain. The infection itself is invisible. What you experience is the war.

**2. Childbed Fever:** In the 1840s, Vienna General Hospital had two maternity wards. One was staffed by doctors, the other by midwives. Mothers in the doctors' ward died at five times the rate of those attended by midwives. Ignaz Semmelweis noticed that doctors came from autopsies to deliveries without washing their hands. He mandated chlorine hand-washing. Death rates plummeted. His colleagues rejected the theory — gentlemen's hands could not carry disease. Semmelweis died in an asylum at forty-seven, possibly from an infected wound. Fifteen years later, Pasteur proved germ theory. By then, Semmelweis had been forgotten.

**3. The Fever Threshold:** Human body temperature averages 98.6°F, but this is not fixed. Fever begins around 100.4°F. Above 104°F, proteins begin to denature. Above 106°F, brain damage becomes likely. Yet fever is not malfunction — it's strategy. Many pathogens reproduce optimally at normal body temperature. A few degrees higher slows them while accelerating immune cell activity. The body trades comfort for advantage. Persistent fever above 103°F in adults warrants concern not because the temperature itself is inherently dangerous, but because it signals an infection the immune system cannot quickly suppress. The height of the fever tells you how hard the body is fighting.

**4. Sepsis:** Sometimes the immune response becomes more dangerous than the infection. In sepsis, inflammatory signals cascade out of control. Blood vessels dilate. Blood pressure drops. Organs begin to fail from lack of oxygen. Sepsis kills more people in U.S. hospitals than breast cancer, prostate cancer, and AIDS combined — over 270,000 deaths annually. It can begin from something as minor as a urinary tract infection or an untreated tooth abscess. The progression can be swift: confusion, rapid breathing, clammy skin. Survival depends on early recognition and aggressive treatment. The body, trying to save itself, risks destroying itself in the attempt.

**5. Antibiotics and Silence:** Alexander Fleming returned from vacation in 1928 to find mold growing on a forgotten petri dish. Around the mold, bacterial colonies had died. He isolated the substance — penicillin — but couldn't purify it for medical use. That took another decade and a team of chemists. The first patient treated with penicillin, in 1941, was a police officer dying of a facial infection. He improved dramatically until the supply ran out. He relapsed and died. Mass production began during World War II. Infections that had been death sentences — pneumonia, syphilis, septicemia — became curable. Then bacteria began evolving resistance. Now some infections resist all known antibiotics. The silence after penicillin may not last forever.

**6. The Latent:** Some infections never leave. Herpes viruses hide in nerve cells, erupting periodically. Tuberculosis bacteria wall themselves in lung tissue, dormant for decades before reactivating. HIV integrates into the host's DNA. The immune system cannot eradicate what it cannot find. This is different from chronic infection, where pathogens actively replicate at low levels. Latency is waiting. The virus sleeps. It may never wake. But stress, immunosuppression, or age can trigger reactivation. You carry old infections like scars inside you, invisible until suddenly they aren't. The body remembers every invasion, and some invaders remember how to return.
