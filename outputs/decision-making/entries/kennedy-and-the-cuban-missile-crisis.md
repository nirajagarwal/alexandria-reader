# Kennedy and the Cuban Missile Crisis
***How deliberate process prevented nuclear war***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** October 16, 1962. A U-2 spy plane photographed Soviet nuclear missile sites under construction in Cuba, ninety miles from Florida. The missiles could reach Washington in thirteen minutes. President Kennedy had publicly warned the Soviets months earlier that offensive weapons in Cuba would be intolerable. Now they were there, and he'd been caught off-guard.

The Joint Chiefs of Staff recommended immediate air strikes followed by invasion. Most of Kennedy's advisors agreed. The military presented it as clean and simple: destroy the missiles before they're operational. Kennedy had forty-eight hours, maybe less, before the sites became functional. His brother Robert later wrote that if six of them had been making the decision alone that first day, they would have gone to war.

**The decision:** Kennedy did something unusual — he slowed down. He formed an executive committee (ExComm) that met continuously for thirteen days. He sometimes left the room so advisors would speak more freely. He demanded written alternatives. He brought in outside voices. He split the group into teams arguing different positions.

The military kept pushing for strikes. But as ExComm worked through scenarios, problems emerged. Air strikes couldn't guarantee destroying all missiles. Invasion would kill Soviet troops, forcing escalation. The Soviets had submarines with nuclear torpedoes nearby. CIA director John McCone asked a crucial question: "How would we feel if the Soviets did this to our missiles in Turkey?"

Kennedy chose a naval blockade — which he called a "quarantine" to avoid the legal implications of "blockade." He coupled it with secret back-channel diplomacy: the US would publicly promise not to invade Cuba and privately agree to remove missiles from Turkey later. He gave Khrushchev room to back down without public humiliation.

**The outcome:** The Soviets removed their missiles. Nuclear war was avoided. But it was closer than most people knew — Soviet submarine commanders had authorization to use nuclear torpedoes if under sustained attack. At one confrontation, two of three officers aboard a submarine voted to fire. The third, Vasili Arkhipov, refused. He saved the world, and Kennedy's process gave him the chance to.

**The lesson:** When stakes are highest, resist the pressure to decide immediately. The first option presented is rarely the best option. Build a process that generates alternatives and surfaces hidden assumptions. Give yourself permission to ask "then what happens?" multiple times. Create space for dissent — the person arguing against consensus might be right.

Kennedy's strength wasn't knowing the answer on day one. It was building a system that found better answers by day thirteen.
