# The Outsider Test
***What advice would you give someone else in your situation?***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** When facing a decision, imagine a friend or colleague came to you with the exact same situation. What would you tell them to do? The advice you'd give someone else is often clearer and wiser than what you'd tell yourself.

**When to use it:** When you feel stuck or confused. When your gut and your head disagree. When you suspect emotions or ego are clouding your judgment. When you keep flip-flopping. The test works especially well for decisions where you already know too much about your own rationalizations.

**How to do it:** 

1. Write down your situation as if describing it to someone else. Use their name, not "I." Be specific about the facts.

2. Read it back as if a friend sent it to you. What jumps out? What would you tell them?

3. Notice the gap between that advice and what you're actually considering. That gap is where your biases live.

4. Optional but powerful: Actually tell someone else. Frame it hypothetically if needed. "A friend is deciding whether to..." Listen to what comes out of your mouth.

**Example:** Sarah has spent three years building a feature at her company. Usage is declining. Her manager hints it might get cut. Sarah keeps proposing revisions, convinced the next iteration will work. 

Then she imagines her colleague Mark facing this: "Mark spent three years on a feature. The data shows declining usage. His manager is sending signals. Mark wants to propose another revision." 

Suddenly clear: "I'd tell Mark to let it go. Document the learnings. Move to something with momentum. Stop defending a sunk cost."

**Watch out for:** The test fails if you're too generous or too harsh with the hypothetical friend. Some people give others permission they won't give themselves ("It's okay to quit"). Others hold others to impossible standards ("They should make it work"). The goal isn't to be nice or tough — it's to be clear. Also, if you already know what you "should" do but don't want to, the outsider test just becomes another voice you'll ignore.
