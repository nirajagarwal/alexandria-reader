# Analysis Paralysis
***When gathering more information becomes a way to avoid deciding***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You keep researching. One more article. One more spreadsheet. One more conversation with someone who might have insight. The decision stays unmade while you accumulate information you'll never use. You tell yourself you're being thorough, but really you're stalling.

**Why it happens:** Making a choice means accepting risk and giving up alternatives. More information feels like it should reduce risk and clarify which alternative is best. Sometimes it does. But past a certain point, you're not reducing uncertainty — you're delaying commitment. The research becomes a refuge. As long as you're still gathering data, you haven't failed yet. The decision can't turn out wrong if you haven't made it.

This gets worse when stakes are high and options are similar. Choosing between jobs that both seem good? Between apartments that both work? The closer the options, the more frantically you search for the deciding factor. You want the choice to be obvious. You want to feel certain. More research promises that certainty, but rarely delivers it.

**How to spot it:** Ask yourself: "What would I need to know to decide?" Write it down. Then ask: "Am I actually gathering that information, or am I just gathering more information?" If you can't articulate what specific uncertainty you're trying to resolve, you're probably stalling.

Another sign: you're re-researching things you already know. Reading the fourth apartment review. Revisiting salary comparisons you've already made. When the information starts feeling circular, you've left research territory and entered avoidance territory.

**How to escape:** Set a deadline and stick to it. "I'll decide by Friday." Not "I'll decide by Friday unless I find something important" — that escape hatch will swallow you. Just Friday.

Before the deadline, list what you genuinely don't know and would change your decision. If you can get that information before Friday, get it. If you can't, or if the list is empty, you're done researching.

Accept that good decisions don't require certainty. They require enough information plus the courage to commit. Most decisions can be made well with 70% of the information. The last 30% costs disproportionately much and helps disproportionately little.

**Example:** Sarah had job offers from two tech companies. She'd researched culture, compensation, growth trajectory, manager reputation. They were both good. She kept digging — reading every Glassdoor review, messaging people on LinkedIn, building spreadsheets comparing minor benefits differences. Three weeks passed. One company's patience wore thin and they moved to their second choice.

The research hadn't revealed anything new after the first week. It had just postponed the moment where Sarah had to choose one path and abandon the other. The paralysis cost her both options.
