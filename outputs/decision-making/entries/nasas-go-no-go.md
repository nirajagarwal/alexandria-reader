# NASA's Go/No-Go
***The structured countdown that forces clear decisions under pressure***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A systematic polling method where each responsible party explicitly declares whether their system is ready to proceed. Used by NASA for rocket launches, it transforms a complex group decision into a series of clear individual commitments. The flight director calls each position: "CAPCOM?" "Go." "FIDO?" "Go." "Surgeon?" "No-go." One "no-go" stops everything.

**When to use it:** When multiple people or systems must be ready simultaneously. When the stakes are high and there's no turning back once you commit. When you need clear accountability — not consensus, not discussion, but individual judgment on the record. Product launches. Major deployments. High-risk operations. Any moment where "we should be fine" isn't good enough.

**How to do it:** First, identify every critical dependency — every system, function, or person whose failure would doom the whole effort. Assign one person to each. Second, establish clear criteria: what "go" actually means for each position. Third, create the polling order — usually most detailed systems first, strategic overview last. Fourth, at the decision point, poll each position individually. They answer only "go" or "no-go." If anyone says "no-go," they must briefly state why. Fifth — and this is crucial — "go" is binding. You're putting your judgment on record.

**Example:** Apollo 11, July 16, 1969. Flight Director Gene Kranz polls the room before launch: "Booster?" "Go." "Retro?" "Go." "FIDO?" "Go." Down the list. Twenty positions. Every "go" is someone saying "my system is ready, and I'll be responsible if I'm wrong." When Neil Armstrong's heart rate spikes during landing approach and the computer throws alarms, the same process runs again. "Guidance?" "Go." "CAPCOM?" "Go." The room decides in seconds. They land with seventeen seconds of fuel remaining. The process didn't make the decision easy, but it made it possible — everyone knew their responsibility.

**Watch out for:** The pressure to say "go" can become overwhelming. If the boss is pushing, if everyone else has said "go," if delay is expensive — the pull toward conformity is strong. The process only works if "no-go" is genuinely acceptable. Also, you need real criteria. If people are guessing what "go" means, you're just performing certainty. Finally, this isn't consensus-building. It's not a discussion. That can feel cold, but speed and clarity are the point. Run your debates before the poll, not during.
