# Narrow Framing
***When you see only the choice in front of you, missing better options that exist just outside your field of vision***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You frame a decision as binary — yes or no, this job or that job, accept the offer or walk away — when other possibilities exist. The question itself limits your thinking. Should I fire this underperforming employee? Should I move to Austin? Should I accept this project? Each question seems to demand a single yes-or-no answer, and you find yourself stuck choosing between two unsatisfying options.

**Why it happens:** The human mind craves simplicity. Binary choices feel clean, decisive. They're also how most decisions present themselves initially — someone makes you an offer, a problem demands a response, a deadline approaches. The frame arrives pre-built, and you slip into it without noticing. Time pressure makes this worse. When you need to decide quickly, you grab the obvious options rather than searching for alternatives. The mental effort of expanding your choices feels like a luxury you can't afford.

**How to spot it:** Notice the word "or" in your internal monologue. "Should I do X or Y?" is the signature of narrow framing. Notice when a decision feels forced, like you're being pushed down a path. Notice when both options feel wrong but you're still trying to choose between them. Ask yourself: who set up these options? Often it wasn't you.

**How to escape:** Add a third option. Then a fourth. Make it a rule: never choose between two alternatives until you've generated at least three. Ask "What else?" Ask "What would this look like if it were easy?" Ask "How would someone unconstrained solve this?" Sometimes the best option is to reject the entire frame — not this job or that job, but staying put and negotiating for what you actually want. Not firing the employee or keeping them in their current role, but restructuring the role itself. The goal isn't to analyze infinite possibilities. It's to break the artificial constraint of the binary.

**Example:** Netflix in 2011 faced what seemed like a binary choice: raise prices on their combined DVD-mail and streaming service, or keep prices low and sacrifice margins. They chose to raise prices and split the services — Qwikster for DVDs, Netflix for streaming. Customers revolted. 800,000 cancelled. Stock dropped 77%. The narrow frame was "raise prices or don't raise prices." The unconsidered alternative: raise prices gradually, grandfather existing customers, invest the profits in content to make the value obvious before changing the price. They were so locked into their either-or that they missed the spectrum between. Reed Hastings later called it his biggest mistake. The lesson wasn't about pricing. It was about noticing when you're only seeing two doors in a hallway that has many.
