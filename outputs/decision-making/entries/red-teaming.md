# Red Teaming
***Hire people to break your plan***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** Red teaming means deliberately assigning someone to attack your decision. Not to play devil's advocate in a meeting, but to actively try to prove you wrong. The red team's job is to find flaws, expose assumptions, and show you how your plan fails. They're adversaries by design.

**When to use it:** Before committing to high-stakes decisions where confirmation bias runs strong. When you've fallen in love with a strategy. When everyone agrees too quickly. When failure means catastrophic loss — launching a product, entering a market, making an acquisition, implementing security protocols.

**How to do it:** First, give the red team actual authority and time. "Point out problems" isn't enough. Their mandate is to make your plan fail on paper so it doesn't fail in reality. Second, choose people who can think adversarially — not just critics, but people who can inhabit an opponent's mindset or imagine cascading failures. Third, protect them. If attacking the plan hurts their career, they'll pull punches. Fourth, take their work seriously. The point isn't to hear them out and then proceed unchanged. It's to genuinely revise based on what they find.

**Example:** Before the Bay of Pigs invasion, the CIA was confident. Castro's forces would crumble, popular uprising would follow. But no one ran a real red team. When Kennedy established one before the Cuban Missile Crisis a year later, the dynamic changed. The ExComm included people specifically tasked with challenging assumptions about Soviet intentions and the consequences of airstrikes. That red team helped avoid nuclear war. The difference wasn't intelligence or resources — it was institutionalized skepticism.

**Watch out for:** Fake red teaming is worse than none. If the exercise is theater — if the red team knows their job is to raise concerns that will be noted and ignored — you've just inoculated yourself against real criticism. Also, red teams can be wrong. They might find flaws in a sound plan or miss the actual vulnerabilities. The goal isn't to follow their recommendations blindly, but to stress-test your thinking against an intelligent adversary. Finally, don't red team trivial decisions. The process has costs — time, energy, morale. Save it for when the stakes justify the scrutiny.
