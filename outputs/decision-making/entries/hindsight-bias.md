# Hindsight Bias
***After the outcome, everything seems inevitable***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** Once you know how something turned out, you convince yourself you "knew it all along." The decision that seemed risky becomes obvious in retrospect. The surprise outcome becomes predictable. You rewrite your memory of what you thought before, replacing uncertainty with false clarity.

**Why it happens:** Your brain automatically integrates new information into existing knowledge. When you learn an outcome, you can't "unsee" it — the result colors everything that came before. This happens instantly and unconsciously. Your memory doesn't store a video of your past thinking; it reconstructs it, and that reconstruction is contaminated by what you now know.

This affects how you judge your own past decisions and other people's choices. The CEO who rejected Netflix's acquisition offer seems foolish now. The friend who took the job that didn't work out seems reckless. You forget the information they lacked, the alternatives that seemed viable, the uncertainty that was genuine.

**How to spot it:** Notice when you're saying "I knew that would happen" or "It was obvious they should have..." Ask yourself: would you have bet money on this outcome beforehand? Did you actually predict it, or does it just feel predictable now?

Watch for asymmetric judgment — being harsh on failed decisions while being generous about successful ones. If you're criticizing someone's choice because it turned out badly, ask whether you'd praise the same reasoning if it had succeeded.

**How to escape:** Before outcomes are known, write down your predictions and reasoning. Date them. This creates evidence of your actual uncertainty. When evaluating decisions, reconstruct what was knowable at the time — what information existed, what alternatives were visible, what risks were apparent then, not now.

Judge decisions by their process, not just their results. A good decision with a bad outcome is still a good decision. A terrible decision that happened to work is still terrible. Ask: "Given what they knew then, was this reasonable?" not "Given what we know now, was this correct?"

**Example:** In 2006, Yahoo turned down Microsoft's offer to buy them for $44.6 billion. By 2008, Yahoo's value had collapsed. In hindsight, this looks catastrophically stupid. But at the time of the decision, Yahoo was a major internet player, Google was their real competition, and many analysts thought they could recover. The decision was debatable then — we've just rewritten it as obvious because we know how the story ends.
