# Regret Minimization
***Imagine yourself at 80, looking back — which choice would you regret not making?***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A framework that shifts your perspective from present concerns to future hindsight. Instead of asking "What should I do now?" you ask "When I'm old, which decision will I wish I'd made?" It helps cut through immediate fears and social pressure by anchoring to your longer-term self.

**When to use it:** When facing big, irreversible decisions — career changes, relationship commitments, geographic moves. When short-term costs (money, status, comfort) make you hesitant about something you deeply want to try. When you're stuck between what's safe and what's meaningful.

**How to do it:**

1. Picture yourself at 80, looking back at this moment in your life
2. Imagine you chose Option A — what do you feel? Relief? Pride? Wistfulness about the road not taken?
3. Now imagine you chose Option B — same question
4. Notice which choice creates more regret in that future version of you
5. Weight that future regret against your present fear or discomfort
6. Decide accordingly

**Example:** Jeff Bezos used this framework in 1994 when deciding whether to leave his hedge fund job to start an internet bookstore. The safe choice was obvious — good salary, clear career path, no risk. But he imagined himself at 80: "When I'm in the rocking chair, will I regret not trying this internet thing? I knew that would haunt me. But I wouldn't regret trying and failing." He quit his job. Amazon's first office was his garage.

The framework didn't guarantee success — most startups fail. But it clarified that his real risk was inaction, not failure. The regret of wondering "what if" outweighed the regret of a failed attempt.

**Watch out for:** This tool assumes your 80-year-old self values what your current self values. Sometimes that's wrong — you might think you'll regret not being more ambitious, when you'll actually regret not spending time with people you love. It also doesn't help with decisions where both paths create different types of regret (staying vs. leaving a difficult relationship, for instance). And it can make present costs seem smaller than they are — sometimes the pain of the risky path isn't just fear, it's real harm you'll inflict on yourself or others.

Use this when you suspect you're letting immediate discomfort override deeper priorities. Don't use it to justify recklessness or ignore the needs of people depending on you.
