# Satisficing
***Choose the first option that meets your needs, not the best possible option***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** Satisficing means setting a clear threshold for "good enough" and taking the first option that crosses it, rather than exhaustively comparing all possibilities to find the optimal choice. The term combines "satisfy" and "suffice" — you're looking for something that satisfies your requirements sufficiently, not perfectly.

**When to use it:** Use satisficing when the cost of continued searching outweighs the benefit of a marginally better option. It's especially valuable for decisions you'll make repeatedly, where the stakes are modest, or where options above your threshold are genuinely comparable. Examples: choosing a restaurant for lunch, hiring for a role when you have qualified candidates, picking a hotel for a business trip, selecting software tools when several meet your requirements.

**How to do it:**

1. **Define your criteria clearly** before you start looking. What actually matters? Write down 3-5 non-negotiables and 2-3 nice-to-haves.

2. **Set your threshold** for each criterion. What's good enough? Be specific: "within 15 minutes of the office" not "close by."

3. **Search sequentially**, evaluating each option against your threshold as you encounter it.

4. **Stop and choose** the first option that meets all non-negotiables and enough nice-to-haves. Don't look back.

5. **Commit** without second-guessing. Remind yourself: you defined good enough when you were thinking clearly, not when you were tired of looking.

**Example:** In 2004, Netflix needed to hire engineers rapidly to scale their streaming infrastructure. Their VP of Engineering, Patty McCord, implemented a satisficing approach: clear criteria (strong coding skills in specific languages, experience with distributed systems, cultural fit with their values), then hired the first candidates who met the bar. No endless comparison of equally qualified people. No "let's see a few more candidates." This let them build their team fast enough to stay ahead of demand. They weren't trying to hire the single best engineer in Silicon Valley — they were building a team of people who could all do the job well.

**Watch out for:** Satisficing fails when your threshold is wrong. Set it too low and you'll regret taking the first mediocre option. Set it too high and you'll never find anything that qualifies — you've turned satisficing back into maximizing. Also, beware using satisficing for rare, high-stakes decisions (choosing a spouse, picking a business partner, buying a house). When you'll live with the choice for years and there's genuine variance in outcomes, the exhaustive search might be worth it.
