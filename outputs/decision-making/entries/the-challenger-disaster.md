# The Challenger Disaster
***When engineers knew the answer but couldn't make it heard***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** January 27, 1986, the night before the Space Shuttle Challenger's launch. NASA gathered engineers on a conference call to discuss the weather — temperatures would drop to 26°F at Cape Canaveral, far colder than any previous launch.

Bob Ebeling and Roger Boisjoly, engineers at Morton Thiokol (the company that built the rocket boosters), were alarmed. They'd been warning about O-rings for months. These rubber seals kept hot gases from escaping the boosters. In cold temperatures, the rubber stiffened. When rubber stiffens, it doesn't seal properly. Previous launches showed evidence of O-ring erosion — close calls that hadn't yet become catastrophes.

The engineers recommended postponing. Their managers at Thiokol initially agreed and told NASA no-go. Then NASA pushed back. The launch had already been delayed multiple times. Teachers across America had lesson plans ready — Christa McAuliffe, the first teacher in space, was on board. What was the evidence, really? Could they *prove* failure would occur?

Thiokol management asked for a five-minute off-line caucus. One manager said they needed to "take off their engineering hat and put on their management hat." The engineers protested. Boisjoly laid out his data again, showing charts of O-ring damage at various temperatures. When he finished, the room was silent. The managers didn't refute him. They simply stopped looking at his charts and talked among themselves.

Thiokol reversed their recommendation. Launch approved.

**The decision:** NASA proceeded. The next morning, 73 seconds after liftoff, Challenger exploded. All seven crew members died. The cause: O-ring failure. Exactly as predicted.

**The outcome:** The Rogers Commission investigated. Richard Feynman famously dropped an O-ring into ice water during a televised hearing, demonstrating how cold made it rigid. The report was damning — not just about the technical failure, but about the decision-making process.

Bob Ebeling carried guilt for decades. "I should have done more," he said before his death in 2016. But he'd done everything an engineer could do. He'd presented data. He'd argued. He'd refused to change his assessment. The failure wasn't in his analysis. It was in a system where the people with the data didn't have the authority, and the people with the authority didn't want to hear the data.

**The lesson:** Sometimes the right answer is already in the room. The question is whether anyone with power to act is listening.

Watch for decisions where people with the most relevant knowledge aren't the ones making the call. Watch for moments when disagreement gets treated as disloyalty rather than data. Watch for pressure to be a "team player" when being a team player means ignoring what you know.

And if you're the engineer in the room — the one with the charts everyone's stopped looking at — document it. Say it clearly. Know that even if you're overruled, speaking up matters. Ebeling and Boisjoly were right. They said so. The record shows it. That's not nothing.
