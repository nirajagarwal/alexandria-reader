# The Sunk Cost Trap
***You keep pouring resources into something because of what you've already spent***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You stay in a failing relationship because you've already invested five years. You keep funding a project that's clearly doomed because you've already spent $200,000. You finish a book you hate because you're 400 pages in. The past spending — time, money, effort, emotion — becomes the reason to keep spending, even when the future return doesn't justify it.

**Why it happens:** Loss aversion is stronger than gain attraction. Our brains treat "wasted" resources as a defeat to be avoided rather than a fact to be accepted. We confuse two different questions: "Was the original decision good?" and "Is continuing the right choice now?" We feel the weight of past investment as obligation. Admitting we were wrong feels like compounding the loss rather than cutting it short. There's also social pressure — we imagine having to explain to others (or ourselves) why we "gave up."

**How to spot it:** Ask yourself: "If I were starting fresh today, with no history, would I choose this?" If the answer is no, you're likely caught in the trap. Another signal: you find yourself defending a choice by listing everything you've already put into it rather than what you expect to get out of it. Or you notice you're using the word "waste" — "It would waste everything I've invested." The investment is already gone; that's not the question anymore.

**How to escape:** Make the past irrelevant to your decision. Imagine you just inherited this situation from someone else. What would you do? Calculate based only on future costs and benefits. The $200,000 is spent whether you quit now or in six months. The five years are gone. The 400 pages are read. The only question is: what happens next?

Sometimes it helps to flip the frame: every day you continue is a new investment. You're not just maintaining what you spent; you're spending fresh resources right now. Is *that* worth it?

**Example:** In 1982, Washington State was building five nuclear power plants. Costs exploded from $4 billion to over $24 billion. Two plants were clearly doomed — wrong locations, impossible construction, massive cost overruns. But the Washington Public Power Supply System kept pouring money in for years, arguing they couldn't "waste" what they'd already spent. They eventually defaulted on $2.25 billion in bonds, the largest municipal bond default in U.S. history. If they'd cut their losses earlier, they would have saved billions. The sunk costs were gone either way. The question was always: should we spend *more*? The trap made them blind to it.
