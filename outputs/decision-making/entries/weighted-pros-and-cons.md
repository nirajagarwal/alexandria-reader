# Weighted Pros and Cons
***Making factors comparable by assigning them numbers***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A method for comparing options by listing their advantages and disadvantages, then assigning numerical weights to reflect each factor's relative importance. Instead of treating "closer to family" and "30% salary increase" as incomparable, you make them comparable by deciding, for instance, that the salary matters twice as much to you right now.

**When to use it:** When you're stuck between options that involve different types of value — money versus time, security versus opportunity, comfort versus growth. When you have multiple factors pulling in different directions and need to see which option actually serves your priorities. Not for simple choices where the answer is obvious, but for real trade-offs where listing pros and cons leaves you still uncertain.

**How to do it:**

1. List your options across the top of a page
2. Down the left side, write every factor that matters to this decision
3. For each factor, assign a weight from 1-10 based on its importance to you right now
4. For each option, score how well it performs on that factor (use -5 to +5, or 1-10, whatever feels natural)
5. Multiply each score by its weight
6. Add up the weighted scores for each option
7. Look at the totals, then look at what surprises you about them

**Example:** Maria is choosing between two job offers. She lists factors: salary (weight: 8), commute time (7), learning opportunities (9), team culture (8), job security (6). The startup scores higher on learning and culture but lower on security and commute. The corporate job pays more but seems less exciting. When she multiplies it out, the startup wins 285 to 267. But she notices she's disappointed — she wanted the corporate job to win. That disappointment is information. She realizes she underweighted financial stability given her student loans. She adjusts that weight to 9, recalculates, and the corporate job edges ahead. More importantly, she now knows what she's trading off and why.

**Watch out for:** The numbers feel objective but they're not — you chose them. If you find yourself fiddling with weights until you get the answer you wanted, that's useful information about what you actually want, but don't pretend the math made the decision. Also, some factors resist quantification — "this person makes me feel alive" doesn't convert cleanly to a 7. Use this tool to clarify your thinking, not to outsource your judgment.
