# Decision Fatigue
***Your mental energy for decisions depletes through the day***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You make worse decisions as the day goes on. Not because you lose intelligence, but because making decisions — even small ones — drains a specific resource. By evening, you're more likely to take the default option, make impulsive purchases, pick fights, or avoid deciding altogether. The quality of your judgment degrades predictably.

**Why it happens:** Every decision requires mental effort, even trivial ones. What to wear. Which email to answer first. Whether to have the salad. These micro-decisions accumulate. Your brain, conserving energy, starts taking shortcuts: defaulting to the easiest option, saying yes when it should say no, or postponing choices that require real thought. This isn't weakness — it's biology. The mental resources for effortful choice are genuinely limited.

**How to spot it:** You're snapping at people for minor annoyances. You're buying things you don't need. You can't decide where to eat dinner even though you're hungry. Important emails sit unanswered because opening them feels overwhelming. You're choosing based on what's easiest, not what's best. The decisions themselves aren't necessarily wrong — but the process feels different. There's less deliberation, more surrender.

**How to escape:** First, recognize which decisions actually matter. Most don't. Automate the trivial ones. Mark Zuckerberg's identical gray t-shirts aren't about fashion — they're about preserving decision energy for Facebook. Create systems: meal prep Sunday, same breakfast daily, standing Friday lunch spot. These aren't failures of imagination; they're strategic conservation.

Second, schedule important decisions early. Interview candidates in the morning. Review job offers before noon. Have difficult conversations when you're fresh. Judges grant parole more often at the start of sessions than at the end — not because criminals are different, but because the judges are depleted.

Third, take real breaks. Decision fatigue isn't permanent. Food helps — Israeli judges were more lenient after lunch breaks. So does sleep. But the break needs to actually rest your decision-making capacity, which means not scrolling through thirty restaurant options or shopping websites.

**Example:** A hospital emergency room at 2 AM. The doctors have been on shift for 11 hours. A patient arrives with ambiguous symptoms. The tired doctor, facing her 40th decision of the shift, orders the standard battery of tests instead of thinking through what's actually needed. Not because she's incompetent — she's exhausted. The morning version of herself would have made a different choice. Now she defaults to the protocol, which is usually fine but occasionally misses something important. Decision fatigue doesn't announce itself. It just quietly degrades your judgment until someone else points out the pattern.
