# The 5 Whys
***Ask "why" five times to find the real problem***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** The 5 Whys is a technique for getting past surface symptoms to root causes. You start with a problem, ask why it happened, then ask why that happened, repeating until you reach something actionable. Five iterations usually gets you there, though sometimes you need fewer or more.

**When to use it:** When you're facing a recurring problem, when a quick fix didn't work, or when you suspect the obvious solution won't actually solve anything. It's particularly useful in operations, but works anywhere you need to understand cause and effect — failed projects, broken processes, relationship conflicts.

**How to do it:**

1. State the problem clearly. "Our product launch was three weeks late."
2. Ask why it happened. "Because testing found critical bugs two days before launch."
3. Ask why that happened. "Because we didn't start testing until the last week."
4. Ask why that happened. "Because development ran over schedule."
5. Ask why that happened. "Because we added features mid-project without adjusting the timeline."
6. Ask why that happened. "Because we have no process for evaluating scope changes."

Stop when you reach something you can actually fix. Here, the root cause isn't "bugs" — it's the missing change control process.

**Example:** Toyota, which developed this method, investigated why a machine kept stopping. Why? A fuse blew. Why? Bearing wasn't lubricated. Why? Oil pump wasn't working. Why? Pump shaft was worn. Why? No strainer, so metal chips got in. The solution wasn't replacing fuses — it was adding a strainer.

**Watch out for:** Two failure modes. First, stopping too early at a comfortable but superficial answer. "Why were we late? Bad luck." No. Keep digging. Second, branching into multiple "whys" at each level until you're drowning in complexity. Pick the most important cause at each step and follow that thread. You can always trace other branches later.

The 5 Whys works because problems rarely are what they appear to be. The late launch isn't really about bugs. The broken machine isn't really about fuses. The technique forces you past your first explanation to something you can actually change.
