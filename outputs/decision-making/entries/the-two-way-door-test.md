# The Two-Way Door Test
***Separating reversible choices from permanent commitments***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A framework for categorizing decisions based on whether they can be undone. One-way doors are permanent or extremely costly to reverse — hiring a VP, selling your company, moving to another country. Two-way doors can be walked back through — trying a new meeting format, testing a pricing change, taking a temporary assignment. The tool changes how much time and certainty you need before deciding.

**When to use it:** When you're stuck debating a decision, unsure how much analysis is enough. When a team is moving too slowly on small choices or too quickly on large ones. When you need to explain why some decisions deserve more rigor than others.

**How to do it:** 

1. Ask: "If this goes badly, can we reverse it? What would that cost?" Be specific — time, money, relationships, reputation.

2. If reversal is cheap and fast (hours to weeks, minimal cost), it's a two-way door. Decide quickly with less information. Bias toward action.

3. If reversal is expensive or impossible (months to years, major cost, burned bridges), it's a one-way door. Slow down. Gather more data. Get dissenting opinions. Sleep on it.

4. Write it down. "This is a two-way door because..." or "This is a one-way door because..." Forces clarity about what makes reversal hard.

**Example:** Jeff Bezos uses this explicitly at Amazon. Changing the layout of a product page? Two-way door — Amazon runs thousands of tests monthly, reverting the losers. Acquiring Whole Foods for $13 billion? One-way door — took years of deliberation, involved the board, required extensive modeling. The same leader treats these decisions completely differently because the doors are different.

**Watch out for:** Calling something a two-way door when it isn't. Sunk costs accumulate. Momentum builds. Relationships change. A "temporary" hire becomes hard to fire. A "trial" partnership creates dependencies. Test your reversal scenario: Would you actually walk back through? What would stop you? Also: some leaders overuse this framework to justify recklessness ("Everything's reversible!") or analysis paralysis ("Nothing's truly reversible!"). The tool works when you're honest about real reversal costs.
