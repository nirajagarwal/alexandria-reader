# Buffett's Bet on Coca-Cola
***When everyone else saw temporary trouble, he saw permanent value***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** October 1987. The stock market crashed. By 1988, Wall Street was still skittish, and Coca-Cola's share price had dropped significantly. The company faced real problems: new management under Roberto Goizueta, intense competition from Pepsi, health concerns about sugar, and the "New Coke" disaster still fresh in memory. Most investors saw a mature company in a saturated market with questionable prospects.

Warren Buffett saw something different. He'd been watching Coca-Cola for decades but never bought — the price was always too high. Now, in the aftermath of the crash, with everyone nervous, he could finally afford it. Between 1988 and 1989, Berkshire Hathaway invested $1.3 billion in Coca-Cola stock, eventually becoming the largest shareholder. It was Buffett's biggest bet yet, representing more than a third of Berkshire's stock portfolio.

**The decision:** Buffett ignored the temporary troubles and focused on permanent characteristics. Coca-Cola had unmatched global distribution, powerful brand loyalty in nearly every country, and a product that cost pennies to make but commanded premium pricing. The company earned high returns on capital and generated enormous cash flow. Most importantly, he believed people would still be drinking Coke decades into the future — the "moat" around the business was wide and deep.

He bought when others were fearful, but not blindly. He'd spent years understanding the business. He calculated what the company's future earnings would be worth, discounted back to present value. Even in 1988, with all the concerns, Coke was selling below his estimate of intrinsic value. The market was offering him a great business at a fair price.

**The outcome:** By 1997, Buffett's $1.3 billion investment was worth over $13 billion — a tenfold return in less than a decade. But he didn't sell. He held the position for decades, collecting growing dividends year after year. The investment became legendary, not just for the returns but for what it demonstrated: the power of buying quality businesses when the crowd is pessimistic.

**The lesson:** Great decisions often require three elements that Buffett displayed: patience to wait for the right price (he'd watched Coke for decades), conviction to act when others won't (buying during market fear), and clarity about what makes something valuable long-term (the moat, not the moment). Notice he didn't need to predict when sentiment would turn — he just needed to be right about the underlying business value. The market eventually came around to his view, but he would have been fine waiting years if needed. He wasn't making a bet on timing. He was making a bet on value that would compound regardless of short-term market mood.
