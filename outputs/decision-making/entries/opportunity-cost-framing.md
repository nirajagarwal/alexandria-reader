# Opportunity Cost Framing
***Every yes is also a no to something else***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A technique that forces you to think about what you're giving up when you say yes to something. Instead of asking "Is this worth the money?" you ask "What else could I do with this money?" Instead of "Should I take this job?" you ask "What am I not doing if I take this job?"

**When to use it:** When a decision looks obviously good on its own terms but you have a nagging feeling you're missing something. When you're stretched thin and everything seems urgent. When you're about to spend significant time, money, or attention and want to make sure it's going to the right place.

**How to do it:** 

1. Name the choice you're considering in concrete terms: "Spend $300 on this course" or "Accept this speaking invitation" or "Add this feature to the product."

2. Ask: "If I do this, what am I not doing?" List 3-5 specific alternatives. Not abstract categories like "other investments" but actual things: "Save for the house down payment" or "Weekend trip to see my sister" or "Two months of focused time on the current product."

3. Compare directly. Put the original option and the alternatives side by side. Which matters more right now? Which moves you closer to what you actually want?

4. Repeat the question one level deeper: "If I'm not doing that alternative, what does that cost me?" Sometimes the second-order effects matter most.

**Example:** Netflix in 2011 faced a choice about DVD distribution. The DVD business was still profitable and growing. The obvious question was "Should we invest more in DVDs?" But CEO Reed Hastings reframed it: "Every dollar and hour we spend on DVDs is a dollar and hour we're not spending on streaming." The opportunity cost wasn't just money—it was attention, engineering talent, and strategic focus. They chose streaming, took a short-term hit, and won the long game. The key wasn't that DVDs were bad; it was that streaming was what they were giving up by optimizing for DVDs.

**Watch out for:** This tool can create paralysis if you take it too far. Every choice has infinite opportunity costs if you look hard enough—you can always imagine something better you might do. Use it to clarify major tradeoffs, not to second-guess every decision. Also watch for false comparisons. The alternative needs to be realistic, not a fantasy of what you "could" do but never actually would.
