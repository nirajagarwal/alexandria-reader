# Kodak and Digital Photography
***When protecting today's profits blinds you to tomorrow's reality***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** In 1975, Steve Sasson, a young engineer at Kodak, built the first digital camera. It weighed eight pounds, captured 0.01 megapixels in black and white, and took 23 seconds to save an image to a cassette tape. Management's response? "That's cute — but don't tell anyone about it."

Kodak wasn't stupid. They understood digital photography would eventually matter. They held patents on digital imaging technology. Their research labs were excellent. But film was printing money — literally. Kodak dominated global film sales with 70% market share and even higher margins. Film plus the chemicals and paper for developing generated billions in recurring revenue. Every vacation, every wedding, every birthday meant Kodak sales.

The company spent decades managing the "transition" to digital. They released digital cameras but designed their strategy around protecting film revenue as long as possible. They assumed digital would supplement film, not replace it. They believed consumers would always want prints, which would preserve their paper and chemicals business. They were half-right — prints did matter, but not enough and not for long enough.

**The decision:** Kodak chose to delay. Not ignore — delay. They invested in digital technology but structured every decision around extending the film business. When they released digital cameras, they priced them high and marketed them carefully to avoid cannibalizing film sales. When they bought a photo-sharing site in 2001, they tried to build a business model around printing digital photos rather than embracing pure digital sharing.

Meanwhile, competitors with no film business to protect — Sony, Canon, Nikon, and eventually smartphone makers — could optimize purely for digital. They didn't need to balance new technology against old revenue. They just built better digital cameras at lower prices.

**The outcome:** Kodak filed for bankruptcy in 2012. A company that had pioneered digital imaging, that employed 145,000 people at its peak, that had been synonymous with photography itself — gone. Instagram, founded in 2010 with 13 employees, was acquired by Facebook for $1 billion the same year Kodak went bankrupt. The company that invented digital photography lost to a photo-sharing app.

**The lesson:** Sometimes the biggest threat to making a good decision is making a lot of money from your current approach. Kodak's problem wasn't ignorance — they saw digital coming. Their problem was that they saw it too clearly: they understood it would destroy their most profitable business. So they made the rational short-term choice repeatedly: protect today's revenue. Each individual decision made sense. The cumulative effect was catastrophic.

When you're facing a choice between protecting what's working now and preparing for what's coming next, ask yourself: am I making this decision, or is my current business model making it for me? The answer matters more than you think.
