# The 10/10/10 Rule
***Ask how you'll feel about this decision in 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A time-travel exercise for decisions. Before choosing, you imagine yourself at three points in the future — 10 minutes from now, 10 months from now, and 10 years from now — and ask how you'll feel about the choice you're about to make.

**When to use it:** When your immediate emotions are strong and you suspect they're distorting your judgment. When you're tempted by something that feels urgent but might not matter. When you're avoiding something uncomfortable that might be important. It's especially useful for decisions where short-term pain might bring long-term gain, or vice versa.

**How to do it:** 

1. State the decision clearly: "Should I [specific action]?"
2. Imagine it's 10 minutes after you've decided. How do you feel? Relieved? Regretful? Anxious? Satisfied?
3. Jump to 10 months. What's changed? Does this decision still register as important? Are you living with consequences or has it faded to background noise?
4. Leap to 10 years. Looking back, does this moment matter? Will you remember making this choice? What did it cost or give you?
5. Notice the pattern. If the feeling improves as you move forward in time, it's often worth the short-term discomfort. If it degrades, examine whether momentary satisfaction is worth future regret.

**Example:** Sarah gets an angry email from a colleague. She writes a sharp reply. Before sending, she uses 10/10/10. In 10 minutes: vindicated, slightly nervous. In 10 months: probably embarrassed, possibly dealing with damaged trust. In 10 years: won't remember the email, but might remember the relationship this damaged. She deletes the reply, waits an hour, writes something direct but not hostile. The immediate satisfaction fades; the working relationship survives.

**Watch out for:** This tool assumes you can predict future feelings, but you're probably worse at that than you think. You might underestimate how quickly you'll adapt to either success or failure. It also privileges long-term thinking, which isn't always right — sometimes the urgent matters more than the important. And some people use this to avoid all short-term discomfort, turning it into a procrastination engine. The rule works best as a check on impulse, not as a replacement for values or as permission to ignore present needs.
