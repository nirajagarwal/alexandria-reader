# The Planning Fallacy
***We underestimate how long things will take, even when we know we always underestimate.***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You estimate a project will take three weeks. Six weeks later, you're still working on it. This wasn't bad luck — you've done this before. Last time you estimated three weeks, it took seven. The time before that, your "two-month project" stretched to five. Yet somehow, this time felt different. This time you were being realistic.

The planning fallacy is our systematic tendency to underestimate task duration, even when we have clear evidence of our past underestimates. We predict based on the best-case scenario while conveniently forgetting the obstacles that always appear.

**Why it happens:** When planning, we imagine the work itself — the focused hours of actual doing. We don't imagine the client who needs three rounds of revisions, the software update that breaks everything, the week we spend sick, the teammate who quits mid-project.

We also fall victim to optimism bias. We want the project to be quick, so we construct a timeline where everything goes smoothly. Our brain treats this wish as a prediction.

Finally, we rely on the "inside view" — thinking about this specific project's tasks — rather than the "outside view" of how long similar projects actually took.

**How to spot it:** You're in the planning fallacy when:
- Your estimate is suspiciously clean ("exactly two weeks")
- You're planning as if nothing will go wrong
- Someone asks "What could delay this?" and you're annoyed by the question
- You remember past delays but think "this time is different"
- You've padded your estimate and still feel anxious it's too short

**How to escape:** Use the outside view. Ask: "How long did the last three projects like this actually take?" Use that average, not your optimistic projection of this one.

Add a "failure buffer." Identify the three most likely obstacles. Add time for each. If it feels like too much padding, you're probably close to realistic.

Break the work into smaller chunks and estimate those separately. Our errors compound less on small estimates. Estimating "write section one" is more accurate than estimating "write the whole report."

Finally, track your estimates versus reality. Keep a log. The pattern becomes undeniable, and the next time you estimate, your past self provides the outside view you need.

**Example:** In 1977, the Sydney Opera House opened — ten years late and 1,357% over budget. The original estimate: four years and $7 million. The reality: fourteen years and $102 million. The architects planned for the building tasks but not for the unprecedented engineering challenges of those dramatic concrete shells, or the political fights, or the design changes, or the acoustic complexities discovered mid-construction. They used the inside view of their ambitious design and ignored the outside view that groundbreaking buildings always take longer than planned.
