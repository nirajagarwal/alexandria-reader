# The Halo Effect
***When one good trait makes everything else look better***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You meet someone who's articulate and assume they're also competent. A company has a sleek website and you trust their product quality. An employee is punctual and you rate their strategic thinking higher. One positive impression bleeds into unrelated judgments, making everything about them seem better than it actually is.

**Why it happens:** Your brain wants consistency. When you see one strong trait, it feels wrong for other traits to be weak — so your mind quietly upgrades them. It's efficient but inaccurate. The halo effect is strongest when you're judging abstract qualities (intelligence, trustworthiness, potential) based on concrete observations (appearance, confidence, past success). Your mind fills gaps in knowledge with assumptions that match the overall impression.

**How to spot it:** You're making multiple positive judgments about someone or something, but you only have direct evidence for one or two of them. You find yourself thinking "they seem like the kind of person who..." without specific evidence. You're more confident in your overall assessment than you should be given what you actually know.

Ask yourself: What specific evidence do I have for each quality I'm attributing? Am I seeing these traits directly, or inferring them from something else?

**How to escape:** Force separation. Judge each quality independently. If you're hiring, assess communication skills from their writing samples, problem-solving from their work examples, and reliability from their references — don't let strong performance in one area inflate your ratings in others.

Use structured evaluation. Write down the specific criteria that matter before you meet the person or see the product. Rate each criterion separately based only on relevant evidence. The person who interviews well might still be disorganized. The beautifully designed app might still have terrible customer service.

Get contrary evidence. Actively look for ways the person or thing might be weak in areas where you're assuming strength. The halo effect weakens when you encounter information that doesn't fit the pattern.

**Example:** In 1946, psychologist Solomon Asch showed people descriptions of someone as "intelligent, skillful, industrious, warm, determined, practical, and cautious" versus the same list with "cold" instead of "warm." That single word reversed judgments about generosity, happiness, and popularity — traits that had nothing to do with temperature. The halo (or horns) from one trait colored everything else.

Venture capitalists consistently fall into this trap with founders who resemble previous successes. The founder went to Stanford like the last winner did? Suddenly their unproven business model looks more solid, their thin team looks more capable. One trait creates a halo that makes investors see strengths that aren't there yet.
