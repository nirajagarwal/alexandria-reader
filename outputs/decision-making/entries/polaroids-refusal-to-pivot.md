# Polaroid's Refusal to Pivot
***When protecting the present kills the future***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** In 1981, Polaroid dominated instant photography. Their cameras were cultural icons — the whir and hum of a developing photo was the sound of capturing memories. They employed 21,000 people and generated $3 billion in annual revenue, mostly from film sales. Each camera sold meant years of high-margin film purchases.

That same year, Sony demonstrated the first digital camera — the Mavica. Polaroid's own engineers showed leadership a working digital prototype. The technology was primitive, but the direction was clear: photos would eventually be bits, not chemicals.

Edwin Land, Polaroid's founder and CEO, had built the company on a simple insight — people want to see their photos immediately. Digital photography would fulfill that promise better than instant film ever could. His engineers wanted to lead the transition.

Land said no. So did his successors.

**The decision:** Polaroid chose to defend instant film rather than build the future of photography. They invested billions improving film technology — making it sharper, faster, more colorful. They filed patents to block competitors. They bought Helios, a struggling instant camera company, to expand market share in a dying category.

When they finally entered digital photography in 1996, they designed digital cameras that could print instant photos — trying to preserve their film business even in the new medium. Their digital strategy was "instant film, but with pixels first."

The reasoning seemed sound: film generated 70% of their profits. Customers loved instant photos. Digital quality was still poor. Why cannibalize a profitable business for an uncertain technology?

**The outcome:** Polaroid filed for bankruptcy in 2001. They filed again in 2008. The brand survives only as nostalgia, licensed to other manufacturers.

Meanwhile, companies that embraced digital photography — Canon, Nikon, Sony — thrived. Later, Apple and Samsung captured the market entirely by making cameras ubiquitous in phones.

Polaroid's engineers were right. Digital photography fulfilled Land's original vision better than instant film. But by the time Polaroid accepted this, they had no technical lead, no market position, and no resources to compete.

**The lesson:** The hardest decisions aren't between good and bad — they're between today's success and tomorrow's survival. When the choice is "protect what works now" versus "build what works next," the rational move feels like protecting the present. The revenue is real. The relationships are established. The alternative is uncertain.

But when the future threatens the present, defending the present doesn't make the future go away. It just ensures you won't be part of it.

The question isn't whether your successful business will eventually be obsolete. It will. The question is whether you'll obsolete it yourself, or wait for someone else to do it.

Polaroid could have led digital photography. They had the brand, the engineers, the insight into what people wanted from photos. They chose short-term profit over long-term survival.

That's not stupidity. That's the trap of success — when what works today blinds you to what's required tomorrow.
