# The Precommitment
***Deciding now so you won't have to decide later***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A precommitment is a decision you make in advance that constrains your future choices. You lock yourself into a path before the moment of temptation, confusion, or pressure arrives. Odysseus ordering his crew to tie him to the mast before hearing the Sirens' song. You set the rule when you're clear-headed, then follow it when you're not.

**When to use it:** When you know you'll face a predictable moment of weakness. When emotions will run high and judgment will suffer. When the stakes are significant but the right choice is obvious from a distance. Use it for recurring decisions where willpower alone has failed you before.

**How to do it:** 

First, identify the future decision point. Be specific about the circumstances: "When I'm angry at my business partner" or "When the market drops 20%."

Second, decide now what you'll do then. Make the rule concrete and unambiguous. Not "I'll be reasonable" but "I'll wait 24 hours before responding to any email written in anger."

Third, create friction for breaking the rule. Tell someone. Write it down. Set up automatic systems. Make it harder to violate the precommitment than to follow it.

Fourth, actually follow it when the moment comes. This is where most precommitments fail — you convince yourself this time is different.

**Example:** In 2008, Vanguard founder Jack Bogle watched his own portfolio lose 30% of its value in weeks. He'd spent decades preaching index investing and staying the course. Now he was watching his retirement savings crater. 

Years earlier, he'd made a precommitment: he would never check his portfolio balance more than once per quarter, and he would never sell in response to market moves. He'd built his investment mix when he was calm, and he'd promised himself he'd stick to it.

So he didn't check. Didn't sell. Didn't do anything. The precommitment made the decision for him. By 2012, his portfolio had recovered and grown beyond its 2007 peak. If he'd sold at the bottom, he'd have locked in devastating losses.

**Watch out for:** Precommitments can become straitjackets. The world changes, and sometimes the rule that made sense last year is genuinely wrong today. Build in a review mechanism — not an escape hatch for every moment of discomfort, but a genuine check on whether the underlying logic still holds. Also, don't precommit to something you're not truly willing to follow through on. A broken precommitment is worse than no precommitment — you learn you can't trust your own rules.
