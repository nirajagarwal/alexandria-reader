# Toyota's Andon Cord
***How stopping the line became Toyota's competitive advantage***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** In the 1950s, Toyota was a struggling automaker trying to compete with American giants like Ford and GM. These companies ran massive assembly lines where stopping production was unthinkable — every halt cost thousands of dollars per minute. The American approach: keep the line moving, fix defects later.

Taiichi Ohno, Toyota's production chief, faced a choice. He could follow the proven American model, or he could try something that seemed insane: give every worker the power to stop the entire production line by pulling a cord — the andon cord — whenever they spotted a problem.

His executives were skeptical. Workers might abuse it. Production would plummet. Competitors would widen their lead.

**The decision:** Ohno installed the cords anyway. More than that, he changed the culture. Pulling the cord wasn't just permitted — it was expected. When a worker pulled it, a supervisor had to come immediately. The team would swarm the problem, fix it, update the process so it couldn't happen again, then restart.

Ohno's reasoning: a defect caught on the line costs minutes. A defect caught at the dealer costs thousands. A defect that causes a recall costs millions. But the real cost was subtler — when workers couldn't stop the line, they stopped caring. They'd see problems, shrug, and let them pass. The organization was blind to its own failures.

**The outcome:** Production did slow at first. Workers pulled the cord constantly. But something unexpected happened: they pulled it less and less. Each pull led to a fix. Each fix prevented future pulls. Within months, the line ran faster than before — with fewer defects.

By the 1980s, Toyota's quality was legendary. American automakers sent executives to study what they called the "Toyota Production System." They saw the andon cords. They took notes. Some even installed their own cords.

Most failed to replicate Toyota's success. They had the tool but not the decision behind it: the willingness to value learning over the appearance of efficiency, to trust workers over protecting management control, to stop in order to go faster.

**The lesson:** The best decisions often look wrong in the moment. Giving workers the power to halt production seemed like surrendering control. It was actually claiming a different kind of control — control over quality, over learning, over long-term competitiveness.

The andon cord worked because it forced truth to surface immediately. Problems couldn't hide. This made the organization smarter with every pull.

When facing a decision that seems risky, ask: what am I really choosing between? Ohno wasn't choosing between speed and slowness. He was choosing between visible problems and hidden ones, between short-term efficiency and long-term capability.

The hardest part isn't making bold decisions. It's sticking with them through the initial chaos when they seem to be failing. Ohno's competitors saw the chaos and retreated. Ohno saw it and leaned in.
