# Outcome Bias
***We judge decisions by results, not by the information available when they were made.***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** A CEO greenlights an acquisition that everyone agrees looks brilliant. Three years later, it's a disaster. The board calls it "reckless." Meanwhile, a rival CEO passes on a similar deal that would have failed spectacularly. No one mentions his decision at all — it wasn't reckless, it was just Tuesday.

Same quality of decision-making. Opposite reputations. The only difference is luck.

**Why it happens:** Results are vivid and final. The decision process is murky and counterfactual. We can see the company's stock price. We can't see the alternate universe where the other choice played out. Our brains prefer concrete over hypothetical, so we work backwards from outcomes to judge the decision.

This gets worse under pressure. When something goes wrong, organizations need someone to blame. "It seemed reasonable given what we knew" doesn't satisfy that need. "Bad decision-maker" does.

**How to spot it:** Listen for these phrases:
- "How could they have been so stupid?"
- "It was obvious this would fail"
- "They got lucky"

The telltale sign: people sound more certain about what "should have been done" than anyone was at the time. If the decision looked genuinely unclear when made but obvious in retrospect, outcome bias is at work.

Check yourself: Would you judge this decision differently if the result had been reversed?

**How to escape:** Separate decision quality from outcome quality. Ask:
- What did they know when they decided?
- What reasonable person could have predicted this outcome?
- What was the range of possible results?
- If we ran this scenario 100 times, how often would this outcome occur?

Write down your reasoning before you decide. Not just the choice, but why it makes sense given current information, what could go wrong, what you're uncertain about. When results come in, compare the outcome to your reasoning, not to some revisionist "obviously you should have..."

When evaluating others: demand to see their thinking at the time. If they made a thoughtful bet that didn't pay off, that's different from a reckless gamble that happened to work.

**Example:** In 2000, Yahoo had the chance to buy Google for $1 million. They said no. Google is now worth over a trillion dollars. Was Yahoo's decision idiotic?

Not necessarily. In 2000, Google was a two-year-old search engine with no business model and serious competition from established players. Yahoo was already the dominant internet portal with its own search technology. Spending a million on an uncertain acquisition might have been premature. The decision to pass was defensible with the information available then.

But that's not the story we tell. We say Yahoo "missed" Google, as if the outcome was obvious. We judge them by knowing how the movie ends.
