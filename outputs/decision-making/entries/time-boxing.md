# Time Boxing
***Set a deadline before you start thinking, then stop when time's up***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** You give yourself a fixed amount of time to make a decision, then you make it when the timer runs out. Not when you feel ready. Not when you have perfect information. When the time is up.

**When to use it:** When you're stuck in analysis paralysis. When the cost of delay exceeds the value of more information. When you notice yourself researching the same question for the third time. When a decision needs to be "good enough" but you keep pushing for "optimal."

**How to do it:** Before you start deliberating, set a timer. For small decisions (which restaurant, which color), give yourself five minutes. For medium decisions (which job offer, which apartment), give yourself a week. For large decisions (which career path, whether to relocate), give yourself a month. Write down the deadline. When it arrives, decide with what you know. No extensions.

**Example:** Reed Hastings gives executives at Netflix a "context, not control" framework, but adds time constraints. When a team debates whether to greenlight a show, they get two weeks maximum. At the end, they decide yes or no. This prevents the endless "let's gather more data" spiral that killed projects at traditional studios. The result: Netflix produced *House of Cards* while competitors were still in market research. Not every show succeeds, but the speed of decision-making itself became a competitive advantage.

**Watch out for:** Some decisions genuinely need more time — don't time-box life-or-death medical choices or major financial commitments without proper due diligence. The tool works when the timer forces you to act on available information, not when it forces you to act without necessary information. Also watch for using artificially short timers to avoid difficult emotional work. "I gave myself five minutes to decide about the relationship" might mean you're running from the real conversation you need to have.
