# Overconfidence Bias
***We systematically overestimate how much we know and how right we are***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You're 90% sure the project will finish on time. You're certain you can spot a liar. You know exactly how your industry will evolve. Then reality delivers a different answer. Overconfidence isn't occasional arrogance — it's a built-in feature of how we think. We consistently rate our knowledge, abilities, and predictions as better than they actually are.

**Why it happens:** Our minds don't naturally track uncertainty well. When you know something, you forget what it felt like not to know it. When you make a prediction, you focus on reasons it might be right, not reasons it might be wrong. Add success to the mix — a few good calls, a promotion, some wins — and your brain starts treating past luck as future certainty. The feeling of confidence becomes evidence of correctness, when it's actually just a feeling.

**How to spot it:** Listen for phrases like "I'm sure," "definitely," "no way that happens," especially about complex situations. Watch for narrow ranges in estimates — "It'll cost $100,000, give or take $5,000" when you're building something you've never built before. Notice when you're making predictions without considering how often similar predictions have been wrong. Ask yourself: when was the last time you said "I don't know" or "I'm genuinely uncertain"?

**How to escape:** Force yourself to consider alternatives. Before committing to a forecast, spend five minutes listing reasons you might be wrong. Before a decision, ask "What would someone smarter than me worry about here?" Use reference classes — don't predict your project's timeline in isolation; look at how long similar projects actually took. Keep a decision journal and review your past predictions; nothing cures overconfidence like confronting your actual track record. And build in buffers — if you're 90% sure it takes three months, plan for four.

**Example:** In 1985, Coca-Cola launched New Coke after extensive taste tests showed people preferred it to both Pepsi and original Coke. The company was certain — the data was clear, the research was thorough. They discontinued the original formula. Within weeks, the backlash was massive. They'd been overconfident about what the taste tests measured. People weren't just buying a flavor; they were buying tradition, identity, nostalgia. Coca-Cola knew the data perfectly but overestimated how much the data mattered. Three months later, they brought back "Coca-Cola Classic."
