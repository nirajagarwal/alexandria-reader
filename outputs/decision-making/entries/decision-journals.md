# Decision Journals
***Write down what you expect before you decide — your future self will thank you***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A decision journal is a record of significant decisions made in the moment, capturing what you expected to happen and why. Not a diary of outcomes — a snapshot of your reasoning before you know how things turn out. You write down the decision, your expected outcome, the alternatives you considered, and your confidence level. Then you review it later to see where your thinking was sound and where it went wrong.

**When to use it:** For decisions that matter and recur. Career moves. Hiring choices. Investment decisions. Strategic pivots. Any situation where you'll face similar choices again and learning from this one would help with the next. Skip it for trivial decisions — you don't need to journal about which coffee to order.

**How to do it:**

1. When you make a decision, write it down immediately. Not later. Before the outcome reshapes your memory.

2. Record: What you decided. What you expect to happen (be specific — "revenue will grow 20%" not "things will improve"). Why you think this. What alternatives you considered and rejected. Your confidence level (60%? 90%?). What would change your mind.

3. Set a review date. Six months out. A year. Whatever matches the decision's timeframe.

4. When that date arrives, review honestly. What actually happened? Where was your reasoning right? Where wrong? What patterns emerge across multiple entries?

5. Keep journaling. The value compounds over time as you spot your recurring blind spots.

**Example:** When Satya Nadella became Microsoft CEO in 2014, he decided to embrace open source and cloud computing — a major shift from Microsoft's traditional stance. If he'd kept a decision journal, it might have read: "Betting on Azure and open-sourcing .NET. Expect: Azure revenue to exceed Windows within 5 years. 70% confident. Risk: alienating partners, cannibalizing existing business. Alternative considered: doubling down on Windows/Office traditional model — rejected because mobile shift makes this a declining foundation." Years later, reviewing would show: right about cloud growth (ahead of schedule), underestimated how fast shift would happen, didn't foresee how much developer goodwill open source would generate.

**Watch out for:** Hindsight bias is powerful. You'll "remember" being less confident than you were, or having considered risks you actually ignored. That's why you write things down immediately. Also resist the urge to journal only successes or only failures — you need both to calibrate. And don't make this a self-punishment exercise; the goal is learning, not judgment. Some good decisions fail, some bad ones succeed. Focus on the quality of your reasoning, not just outcomes.
