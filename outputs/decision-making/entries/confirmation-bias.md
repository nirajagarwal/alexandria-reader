# Confirmation Bias
***We see what we expect to see.***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You decide what you believe, then gather evidence that supports it while dismissing or never noticing evidence against it. A manager thinks a new hire is talented, so she remembers their good ideas and forgets their mistakes. An investor believes a company will succeed, so he reads the positive analyst reports and skips the critical ones. The conclusion comes first. The evidence follows obediently behind.

**Why it happens:** Your brain isn't designed to find truth — it's designed to reduce uncertainty and conserve energy. Once you form a belief, maintaining it is cheaper than rebuilding it. New information gets filtered: supporting evidence feels important and memorable, contradicting evidence feels like noise or exception. You're not lying to yourself. You're just seeing clearly in one direction and blurrily everywhere else.

**How to spot it:** Ask yourself what would prove you wrong. If you can't think of anything, or if every contrary signal seems explainable away, you're probably trapped. Notice which information you seek out. Are you reading both the positive and negative reviews? Talking to both supporters and skeptics? Or building a case? Another sign: everyone around you agrees with you. That's either remarkable luck or selective attention.

**How to escape:** Actively hunt for disconfirming evidence. Tell yourself, "I'm going to spend the next hour trying to prove I'm wrong about this." Call someone who disagrees and actually listen. Write down what would change your mind before you start gathering information — commit to the criteria when you're still neutral.

Keep a decision journal. Write what you believe and why. Return to it later. You'll see patterns in what you noticed and what you missed.

Ask others to steelman the opposite view — not to dismiss it, but to present it as compellingly as possible. Better yet, try to argue against yourself as persuasively as you can.

**Example:** In 2003, the CIA believed Iraq had weapons of mass destruction. They had some evidence: past programs, suspicious shipments, defector testimony. But they also had a belief, shaped by years of watching Saddam Hussein. When sources contradicted the WMD narrative, analysts found reasons to discount them — "unreliable source," "probably deception," "inconsistent with other intelligence." When sources supported it, even weak ones, they seemed credible. The conclusion felt obvious. The evidence was shaped around it. The invasion happened. The weapons were never found. The cost was hundreds of thousands of lives and trillions of dollars. The analysts weren't corrupt or stupid. They just saw what they expected to see.
