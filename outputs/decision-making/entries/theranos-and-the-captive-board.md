# Theranos and the Captive Board
***When prestigious directors failed to ask basic questions***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** In 2011, Theranos was burning through cash while developing blood-testing technology that didn't work. Founder Elizabeth Holmes assembled a board of directors unlike any other Silicon Valley startup: George Shultz (former Secretary of State), Henry Kissinger (former Secretary of State), James Mattis (Marine Corps general), Bill Perry (former Secretary of Defense), Richard Kovacevich (former Wells Fargo CEO). Average age: over 70. Medical or laboratory expertise: zero.

The board met quarterly. They received no detailed technical reports. They never visited the labs. When Tyler Shultz, George Shultz's grandson and a Theranos employee, raised concerns about fraudulent test results, the board never heard about it. Instead, Holmes sicced lawyers on him.

**The decision:** The board repeatedly chose to trust Holmes rather than verify her claims. They approved expansions into Walgreens and Safeway. They supported a valuation of $9 billion based on technology that—had they asked basic questions—they would have discovered didn't exist. When red flags emerged, they defended the company rather than investigating.

Why? Status worked against scrutiny. These were men accustomed to deference, to being the experts in the room. Holmes flattered them, called them for advice on geopolitics, made them feel important. She wore black turtlenecks and spoke of changing the world. The board saw themselves as supporting the next Steve Jobs, not as fiduciaries responsible for verifying claims about blood chemistry.

They also knew nothing about the field they were governing. A single board member with clinical laboratory experience would have asked: "Show me the validation studies. Where's the FDA approval? How does this finger-stick sample avoid hemolysis?" Instead, discussions centered on partnerships and growth strategy—downstream questions that assumed the technology worked.

**The outcome:** By 2015, a Wall Street Journal investigation exposed that Theranos ran most tests on standard machines, not its proprietary devices. The technology couldn't do what Holmes claimed. The company collapsed. Holmes faced criminal fraud charges. Patients received incorrect results that led to real medical decisions—stopped medications, unnecessary procedures.

The board? No criminal liability. But their reputations bore the stain of being fooled by questions they never asked.

**The lesson:** Prestige is not expertise. A board of generals can't govern a medical device company, no matter how distinguished their service. When you're making decisions as an advisor, director, or supervisor, ask: Do I actually understand the domain I'm governing? Do I have enough information to verify key claims, or am I just trusting someone's narrative?

The right question—"Show me the test results compared to standard labs"—would have ended this in 2011. Nobody with power asked it. They were captured by the founder they were supposed to oversee, chosen precisely because they wouldn't know what questions to ask.
