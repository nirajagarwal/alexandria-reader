# The Reversibility Check
***Some doors swing both ways. Know which kind you're opening.***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** Before making a decision, ask whether you can undo it. One-way doors require careful deliberation. Two-way doors deserve speed over certainty.

Jeff Bezos calls them "Type 1" and "Type 2" decisions. Type 1 decisions are irreversible or nearly so — you can't easily go back. Type 2 decisions are reversible — if you're wrong, you can return to where you started without catastrophic loss.

The key insight: these two types deserve radically different decision-making processes.

**When to use it:** Any time you're deciding how much time and energy to invest in a choice. When you're paralyzed by a decision that might not warrant the paralysis. When a team is moving too slowly or too recklessly.

**How to do it:**

First, ask the reversibility question: "If this goes wrong, can I get back to roughly where I am now?" Be honest about the answer.

Look for three things:
- **Time cost**: How long to reverse course?
- **Money cost**: What's lost if you backtrack?
- **Opportunity cost**: What doors close permanently?

If the answer is "yes, relatively easily" — it's a two-way door. Make the call with 70% confidence. Decide quickly. Learn from doing.

If the answer is "no" or "only at tremendous cost" — it's a one-way door. Slow down. Gather more information. Stress-test your reasoning. Get input from people who disagree.

For two-way doors, bias toward action. For one-way doors, bias toward thoroughness.

**Example:** Hiring your co-founder is a one-way door. You're legally and emotionally entangled; unwinding the relationship costs years and money and hurts. Interview extensively. Check references. Work together on a trial project.

Picking your team's project management software is a two-way door. Yes, there's switching cost, but you can migrate data and change tools in a quarter if it's not working. Choose based on current needs, try it for two months, reassess.

Most people treat both like one-way doors. The co-founder decision gets made over beers because "we vibe." The software decision consumes six weeks of committee meetings and vendor demos. Get the proportionality right.

**Watch out for:** Some doors look two-way but aren't. Taking a job seems reversible — quit if it's bad. But career momentum, reputation, and gaps in your resume make it stickier than it appears. Each industry has its own hidden one-way doors.

Also, reversibility changes with context. For a large company, most product features are two-way doors. For a startup burning runway, a feature that delays launch might be one-way — you run out of money before you can reverse course.

Check your assumptions about what's reversible. Then match your decision process to the door type.
