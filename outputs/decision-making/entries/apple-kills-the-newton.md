# Apple Kills the Newton
***When cutting your losses means killing your future***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** In 1997, Steve Jobs returned to Apple after twelve years away. The company was ninety days from bankruptcy, bleeding cash on too many products. Among them: the Newton, Apple's pioneering personal digital assistant. Launched in 1993, the Newton had become a punchline — its handwriting recognition was mocked in Doonesbury, sales were disappointing, and it was losing millions.

But the Newton had also evolved. By 1997, the MessagePad 2000 actually worked well. Developers were building for it. A loyal user base existed. And crucially, the Newton team had developed technologies that would prove fundamental to touchscreen computing — handwriting recognition, stylus input, a operating system designed for mobile devices. The Newton wasn't just a product; it was a research platform exploring the future.

**The decision:** Jobs killed it within weeks of returning. No gradual phase-out. No spin-off. Just gone. His reasoning was brutal and simple: Apple was dying, resources were finite, and the Newton wasn't core to saving the company. Every dollar spent on the Newton was a dollar not spent on the Macintosh. Every engineer on the Newton was an engineer not working on what would become OS X. The decision was binary: focus or die.

He was explicitly choosing the present over the future, the certain over the experimental. And he knew it. When engineers protested that they were building the next generation of computing, Jobs reportedly said: "If Apple dies, there won't be a next generation."

**The outcome:** Apple survived. The iMac launched in 1998 and became a hit. The company rebuilt its core business. Then, in 2007, Apple released the iPhone — a device that vindicated everything the Newton team had believed about touchscreen computing. But the iPhone used almost none of the Newton's actual technology. That decade-long head start was simply abandoned.

Meanwhile, Palm filled the PDA void. Microsoft poured resources into Windows Mobile. Both established positions in handheld computing that Apple had pioneered but surrendered. When Apple finally returned to mobile devices with the iPod and later the iPhone, it had to fight for space it had once owned.

**The lesson:** Sometimes the right strategic decision creates the wrong long-term outcome. Jobs was correct that Apple couldn't afford the Newton in 1997 — the company genuinely might have died otherwise. But that didn't make killing the Newton costless. It meant throwing away years of research, market position, and institutional knowledge about mobile computing.

This is the hardest kind of decision: when your best option still hurts. When you know you're sacrificing something valuable but can't afford to keep it. The discipline isn't choosing between good and bad — it's choosing between survival and potential, between today and tomorrow. Jobs made the only choice he could. But understanding it was necessary doesn't make it painless.
