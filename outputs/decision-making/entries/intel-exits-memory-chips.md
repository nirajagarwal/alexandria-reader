# Intel Exits Memory Chips
***When the market leader walks away from what made them great***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** In 1984, Intel dominated DRAM memory chips — the business that had made them successful since 1968. They invented the product. They built the market. Revenue was declining, but memory was their identity.

Andy Grove was COO. Gordon Moore was CEO. Japanese manufacturers had flooded the market with cheaper chips. Intel's memory division was bleeding money, but the company kept investing, kept fighting. Grove and Moore debated endlessly. Should they exit? Could they exit? What would Intel be without memory?

One afternoon, Grove asked Moore: "If we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what do you think he would do?" Moore answered immediately: "He would get us out of memories." Grove stared at him. "Why shouldn't you and I walk out the door, come back, and do it ourselves?"

They didn't act right away. The decision was obvious but impossible. Memory was the company. Grove later wrote: "We had meetings and more meetings, bickering and arguments, resulting in nothing but conflicting proposals." Middle managers kept advocating to stay in. The Japanese would stumble eventually, right? Intel had the technology to catch back up, didn't they?

**The decision:** Grove and Moore finally committed to exit memory chips completely. They would bet everything on microprocessors — a smaller business they'd started almost accidentally. They announced it internally in October 1985.

The organization revolted. Salespeople had no product to sell during the transition. Engineers felt betrayed — we're just giving up? The memory group was the company's heart. Competitors called it surrender.

Grove pushed through anyway. He later described it as "agonizing" and "wrenching." But once decided, he moved fast. Factories were retooled. Staff was reassigned or let go. The company's entire identity shifted in months.

**The outcome:** Within three years, Intel became the dominant microprocessor company. The 386 chip made them more profitable than they'd ever been in memory. By the 1990s, "Intel Inside" was one of the world's most valuable brands. The decision saved the company.

But it almost didn't happen. Grove and Moore knew the right answer for months before acting. They needed to imagine themselves as outsiders to see clearly what insiders couldn't admit.

**The lesson:** The hardest decisions aren't choosing between good and bad — they're letting go of what used to be good. Especially when it's your identity.

Grove's question works because it bypasses emotion: What would someone without our history do? That person sees the situation, not the story. They don't care that memory made Intel great. They care what will keep Intel alive.

When you're trapped debating the same choice endlessly, ask it. If someone replaced you tomorrow, what would they do? Then walk out the door and come back in as that person.
