# Lincoln's Cabinet of Rivals
***When the best decision is to include your enemies***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** November 1860. Abraham Lincoln has just won the presidency of a fracturing nation. Seven Southern states will secede within months. War looms. Lincoln needs a cabinet.

The conventional move: reward your supporters. Fill the room with loyalists who share your vision and owe you their positions.

Lincoln did the opposite. He offered cabinet posts to his three main rivals for the Republican nomination — men who had spent months declaring him unqualified. William Seward, who expected to be president himself, became Secretary of State. Salmon Chase, who considered Lincoln a backwoods lawyer, became Treasury Secretary. Edward Bates, who thought Lincoln too radical, became Attorney General.

These men didn't hide their contempt. Seward initially tried to run foreign policy around Lincoln. Chase openly positioned himself to replace Lincoln in 1864. Cabinet meetings were contentious, sometimes hostile.

**The decision:** Lincoln could have chosen easier relationships. He deliberately chose difficult ones. He wanted the strongest possible team, not the most comfortable one. He needed people who would argue, challenge, bring different perspectives — because the decisions ahead would be catastrophic if made in an echo chamber.

More than that: he needed to unite the Republican Party's factions. Each rival represented a wing of the party. Excluding them would have fractured the coalition before the war even began.

**The outcome:** The cabinet was chaotic. Seward and Chase feuded constantly. Lincoln spent enormous energy managing egos and conflicts. 

But when it mattered, it worked. Seward became one of Lincoln's closest advisors, helping navigate the diplomatic crisis with Britain during the Trent Affair. Chase, despite his ambitions, financed the war effectively. The cabinet's debates helped Lincoln pressure-test his thinking on everything from emancipation to reconstruction.

The Union survived. Barely. But it survived partly because Lincoln put competence and diversity of thought above comfort and loyalty.

**The lesson:** The hardest decisions benefit most from disagreement. When stakes are highest, surrounding yourself with yes-men is a luxury you can't afford.

This doesn't mean hiring people who undermine you. Lincoln's rivals all shared the essential goal — preserving the Union. They disagreed on everything else.

Ask: Am I avoiding this person because they're wrong, or because they make me uncomfortable? If your answer is comfort, you might need them in the room.

The cost is real. Managing strong personalities with competing ambitions takes energy that could go elsewhere. But for decisions that shape years or lives or organizations, the alternative — a team that never challenges you — is more dangerous than the friction.
