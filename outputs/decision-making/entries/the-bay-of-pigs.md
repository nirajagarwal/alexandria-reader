# The Bay of Pigs
***When group consensus overrides individual doubt***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** April 1961. John F. Kennedy, three months into his presidency, faced a plan inherited from Eisenhower: land 1,400 Cuban exiles at the Bay of Pigs to overthrow Castro. The CIA promised air support, popular uprising, easy victory. Kennedy's advisors — McNamara, Rusk, Bundy — all brilliant men. All nodding along. Arthur Schlesinger had doubts. So did Senator Fulbright. But in those meetings, with everyone else seeming certain, they stayed quiet or soft-pedaled their concerns.

The pressure was invisible but total. Kennedy was new. The CIA were experts. Questioning the plan meant questioning all that expertise, appearing weak, being the person who complicated things. Each advisor looked around the table, saw apparent consensus, and assumed everyone else knew something they didn't.

**The decision:** Kennedy approved the invasion. The doubters never spoke forcefully enough to break the consensus. Schlesinger later wrote he wished he'd pushed harder. But in the room, surrounded by confidence, his doubts felt like personal weakness rather than wisdom.

**The outcome:** Complete disaster. No air support arrived as promised. No popular uprising. The exiles were killed or captured within three days. Castro grew stronger. Soviet influence in Cuba deepened. Kennedy was humiliated publicly and haunted privately. He later said, "How could I have been so stupid?"

The answer wasn't stupidity. It was the structure of those meetings — smart people, similar backgrounds, all seeking agreement, none wanting to be the obstacle. Group cohesion defeated individual judgment.

**The lesson:** Consensus often masquerades as wisdom. When everyone in the room agrees, someone should get suspicious. Real confidence can absorb disagreement. False confidence needs everyone nodding.

After the Bay of Pigs, Kennedy changed how he made decisions. He started meeting with advisors individually, not just as a group. He brought in outsiders. He sometimes left the room so people would speak more freely. During the Cuban Missile Crisis eighteen months later, that same group made much better decisions under much higher stakes. Not because they got smarter, but because Kennedy redesigned how they decided.

If you're in a meeting where everyone agrees and it feels wrong to object — that feeling is data. If you're leading and everyone's nodding — be worried, not relieved. The Bay of Pigs is what happens when agreement becomes the goal instead of the byproduct of honest thinking.

Ask: Who isn't speaking? What aren't we questioning? What would we do differently if we had to explain this to someone outside this room?

The smartest group can make the dumbest decision if no one will risk breaking consensus.
