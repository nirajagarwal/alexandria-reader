# Bezos Leaves Wall Street
***Betting on an idea when the downside is bearable***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** In 1994, Jeff Bezos had what most would call a dream job. At 30, he was the youngest senior vice president at D.E. Shaw, a prestigious quantitative hedge fund in Manhattan. The money was excellent. The trajectory was clear. But he'd stumbled across a statistic: internet usage was growing at 2,300% per year. He became convinced there was an opportunity to sell books online — books first because you couldn't browse millions of titles in a physical store, but the internet made that catalog possible.

He told his boss David Shaw about the idea. Shaw listened, said it sounded like a good plan for someone who didn't already have a great job. Bezos spent weeks wrestling with the choice. He was walking away from a certain path of financial security and professional prestige for something that would probably fail — most startups do.

**The decision:** Bezos used what he later called a "regret minimization framework." He projected himself forward to age 80 and asked which choice he'd regret more: trying and failing, or never trying at all. The prospect of regret for *not* attempting the thing felt worse than the prospect of failure. He also assessed the downside concretely: he could likely return to Wall Street if the venture failed. He wasn't risking destitution. He told his wife MacKenzie they'd be moving to Seattle to start an online bookstore. She'd known him for six months.

He quit his job, drove cross-country, and wrote the business plan in the passenger seat while MacKenzie drove. He started Amazon in a garage, packing boxes himself. For the first year, every time someone made a purchase, a bell would ring in the office.

**The outcome:** Amazon nearly collapsed multiple times in its early years. The 2000 dot-com crash left it trading below $6 per share, down from $100. Many predicted bankruptcy. But the company survived, expanded beyond books, and transformed retail. By 2021, Amazon employed 1.6 million people and Bezos was briefly the world's richest person. More importantly for understanding the decision: even in the darkest early days, Bezos never expressed regret about leaving D.E. Shaw.

**The lesson:** When facing an uncertain opportunity, two questions clarify: What will I regret not trying? And what's the real worst case? Bezos didn't minimize the risk of failure — he expected it might happen. But he distinguished between two kinds of failure: the kind you can recover from, and the kind you can't. A failed startup was recoverable. A lifetime wondering "what if" wasn't. This framing doesn't work for every decision — some risks truly are catastrophic. But many decisions we agonize over have bearable downsides and unbearable regrets. The question isn't whether you might fail. It's whether you can live with not having tried.
