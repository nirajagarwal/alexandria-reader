# Escalation of Commitment
***Throwing good money after bad because you've already thrown so much***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You keep investing in a failing course of action because you've already invested so much. The project is clearly doomed, but you authorize another round of funding. The relationship isn't working, but you stay because you've already put in five years. The more you've committed, the harder it becomes to stop — even when stopping is obviously the right choice.

**Why it happens:** Your brain treats past investments as reasons to continue rather than as costs already paid. Admitting failure feels like wasting everything you've put in. There's also the fear of judgment — changing course looks weak, inconsistent, wasteful. And sometimes the people who made the initial decision are the ones evaluating whether to continue, creating a conflict between institutional wisdom and personal reputation.

Organizations make it worse. The manager who championed the initiative can't be the one to kill it. The team that spent two years building becomes invested in launching, regardless of market feedback. Success gets defined as "finishing what we started" rather than "achieving the goal we wanted."

**How to spot it:** Ask yourself: "If I were starting fresh today, knowing what I know now, would I make this same commitment?" If the answer is no, you're likely escalating. 

Watch for phrases like "We've come too far to turn back now" or "We just need to give it a little more time." Listen for justifications that reference past effort rather than future potential. Notice when the original goal has quietly shifted from "succeed" to "not look stupid for starting."

**How to escape:** Separate the decision-maker from the original champion. Bring in someone with no ego attached to the current path. Make sunk costs explicit — write down what's already been spent and formally declare it unrecoverable. Then evaluate only what happens from here forward.

Create decision checkpoints in advance. Before starting a project, write down what evidence would make you stop. When you hit those checkpoints, force the evaluation. "We'll invest six months. If we don't have X users by then, we stop." Then actually stop.

Reframe quitting as smart pivoting, not failure. Netflix started as a DVD-by-mail company. They didn't escalate commitment to physical media.

**Example:** Concorde, the supersonic passenger jet, became a cautionary tale. By the 1970s, the British and French governments knew the economics didn't work. Fuel costs made tickets unaffordable except for the ultra-wealthy. But they'd invested billions across decades. Politicians couldn't face the headlines: "Government Wastes Taxpayer Money on Failed Jet." So they kept funding it. Concorde flew for 27 years, never turning a profit, hemorrhaging money the entire time. It finally stopped in 2003 not because someone made the hard call, but because a crash forced their hand. The investment was already lost in 1970. They just refused to admit it for three more decades.
