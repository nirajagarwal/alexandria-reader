# The Pre-Mortem
***Imagine failure, then work backward to prevent it***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** A pre-mortem starts with a thought experiment: your decision has failed completely. The project collapsed. The hire was disastrous. The investment tanked. Now figure out why. By imagining failure before committing, you surface risks that optimism and group dynamics normally hide.

**When to use it:** Before major commitments — hiring key people, launching products, making large investments, restructuring teams. It's most valuable when stakes are high and you feel confident. That confidence is exactly what blinds you.

**How to do it:** 

1. **Set the scene:** "It's [time period] from now. Our decision has failed spectacularly."
2. **Write the story:** Everyone spends 5-10 minutes independently writing why it failed. Not might fail — did fail. Use past tense. Be specific.
3. **Share:** Go around the room. Each person reads their failure story.
4. **Extract risks:** List every distinct failure cause mentioned.
5. **Prioritize:** Which risks are most likely? Most damaging?
6. **Mitigate:** For top risks, decide: prevent, monitor, or accept?

**Example:** In 2019, a fintech startup planned to launch in Germany. The pre-mortem revealed: "We failed because our German hire quit after three months — turns out they wanted to be in Berlin, and we're remote-first." Another: "German banks blocked our integration because we didn't understand their compliance requirements." They delayed launch, hired a German regulatory consultant first, and made location expectations explicit in job offers. The launch succeeded where it likely would have stumbled.

**Watch out for:** Pre-mortems work because they license people to voice doubts. But that only happens if the leader genuinely welcomes bad news. If you're defensive, people will self-censor and you'll waste everyone's time. Also, don't let it paralyze you — every decision has risks. The goal is to see clearly, not to find reasons to never act.
