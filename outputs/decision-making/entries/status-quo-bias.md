# Status Quo Bias
***The default option wins — even when it shouldn't***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You stick with the current situation not because you've chosen it, but because changing feels harder than staying. The job you'd never take if offered today. The subscription you wouldn't buy now but haven't cancelled. The relationship that ended years ago in everything but name. You keep choosing what you already have, not through deliberate preference but through the path of least resistance.

**Why it happens:** Change requires action. It demands mental effort, carries risk, and often can't be undone. The present arrangement, however flawed, is known — its problems familiar, its routines established. Your brain treats "do nothing" as the safe bet, even when the status quo is slowly failing. Loss aversion amplifies this: giving up what you have feels worse than missing what you might gain. And if the current state resulted from an earlier active choice, abandoning it feels like admitting that choice was wrong.

**How to spot it:** Ask yourself: "If I weren't already in this situation, would I choose to enter it today?" If the answer is no — if you wouldn't take this job, start this project, or enter this arrangement fresh — you're likely trapped by status quo bias. Another signal: you find yourself defending the current state with "it's not that bad" or "at least I know what I'm dealing with" rather than with actual advantages. You're explaining why change is hard, not why staying is good.

**How to escape:** Make the status quo compete on equal terms. Write down what you'd gain by staying and what you'd gain by changing — force yourself to see inaction as a choice with its own costs. Set a decision date: "By Friday, I will either actively choose to stay or I will change." The deadline transforms drift into deliberation. For recurring situations, build in automatic review points. "Every January, I'll evaluate whether to keep this subscription, this commitment, this arrangement." Make staying require a fresh yes rather than just the absence of no.

**Example:** In the 1980s, New Jersey and Pennsylvania offered drivers different car insurance options. New Jersey made full tort the default (you can sue for damages) with an option to choose limited tort (lower premiums, restricted lawsuits). Pennsylvania flipped it — limited tort was default. Economically identical choices. But in New Jersey, only 20% switched to limited tort. In Pennsylvania, only 25% switched to full tort. The default won in both states. Four out of five drivers simply accepted whatever was already checked on the form, even though checking a different box cost nothing and the decision affected thousands of dollars and legal rights. The status quo shaped choices more than preferences did.
