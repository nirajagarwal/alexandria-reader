# Second-Order Thinking
***Ask not just "What happens?" but "And then what?"***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** Most people stop at the immediate consequences of a decision. Second-order thinking means tracing the chain further — considering the consequences of the consequences. It's asking "and then what?" repeatedly until you see effects that others miss.

**When to use it:** When a decision seems obviously good or bad. When everyone agrees on what to do. When short-term and long-term interests might diverge. Any time the stakes are high enough that you can't afford to be surprised by predictable downstream effects.

**How to do it:** 

1. Write down the obvious first-order effect of your decision
2. Ask: "If that happens, what happens next?" Write it down
3. Ask again: "And then what?" Keep going 3-5 steps
4. Look for reversals — places where the initial effect creates its opposite
5. Ask: "Who else will react to this, and how will their reaction change things?"

**Example:** A city considers banning plastic bags to reduce waste. First-order: fewer plastic bags in landfills. Second-order: people buy heavier reusable bags, or switch to paper bags that require more energy to produce. Third-order: without free bags, people buy plastic bags specifically for trash, increasing plastic bag sales. Fourth-order: stores raise prices slightly to offset lost bag revenue. The environmental benefit becomes ambiguous; the distribution of costs shifts to lower-income shoppers who reuse grocery bags for trash.

In 2011, Bangalore banned plastic bags. Fruit vendors who'd relied on plastic to keep produce fresh saw spoilage rates jump. They switched to paper, which got soggy. Then to cloth, which customers wouldn't return. The environmental win came with an economic loss that hit the poorest vendors hardest — predictable in second-order thinking, invisible in first-order.

**Watch out for:** Paralysis — you can always imagine another consequence. Stop at 3-5 steps or when you hit genuine uncertainty. Also watch for motivated reasoning: tracing only the consequences that support your preferred choice. The tool works only if you're genuinely curious about what happens, not just marshaling arguments.
