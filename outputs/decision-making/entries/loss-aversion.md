# Loss Aversion
***We feel losses about twice as strongly as equivalent gains***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You turn down a fair bet — 50% chance to win $110, 50% chance to lose $100 — because the pain of losing feels bigger than the pleasure of winning. You hold a declining stock too long because selling would "make the loss real." You stay in an unsatisfying job because leaving means giving up seniority, benefits, the known quantity. The potential loss looms larger than any potential gain.

**Why it happens:** Evolution wired us for a world where losses could kill you. Missing out on extra berries — unfortunate. Losing your food cache to theft — potentially fatal. Our brains still treat losses as existential threats even when they're not. The asymmetry made sense for survival. It makes less sense for modern decisions where losses are recoverable and gains matter.

**How to spot it:** You're weighing a decision and the first thing you think about is what you'll give up. You find yourself saying "at least I haven't lost anything yet" while something deteriorates. You can't clearly articulate what you'd gain — you're fixated on what you'd lose. You're holding onto something declining because selling means "admitting you were wrong."

Ask yourself: Am I treating this loss as irreversible when it isn't? Am I refusing a good opportunity because I'm too focused on what I'd give up?

**How to escape:** Reframe in terms of total position, not gains and losses. You're not deciding whether to "lock in a loss" by selling a bad stock. You're deciding whether you'd buy this stock today at its current price. If the answer is no, you should sell — the past investment is gone regardless.

Set a pre-commitment rule. Decide your exit point before emotions take over. "If the stock drops 15%, I sell." "If I'm still unhappy at work in six months, I'll start looking." Future you will want to rationalize staying. Present you needs to tie yourself to the mast.

Consider opportunity cost. Holding that declining stock isn't "avoiding a loss" — it's losing the opportunity to invest that money better elsewhere. Staying in that job isn't "preserving what you have" — it's giving up whatever you could have been building somewhere else.

**Example:** Blockbuster had everything to lose and Netflix had everything to gain. In 2000, Netflix offered to sell to Blockbuster for $50 million. Blockbuster said no. They couldn't see past the loss — cannibalizing their profitable late-fee business, abandoning their massive real estate investment, admitting the world had changed. By 2010, Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy. Netflix became worth billions. Blockbuster's loss aversion protected them from risk in the short term and guaranteed catastrophe in the long term. They were so focused on not losing what they had that they lost everything.
