# Anchoring
***The first number you see becomes the center of gravity for all numbers that follow***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You walk into a salary negotiation. The employer opens with $75,000. Suddenly, every number you think about clusters around that figure. $80,000 feels ambitious. $90,000 feels absurd. Even though moments before, you were thinking $95,000 was reasonable, that initial number has reset your sense of what's possible. The first number dropped an anchor, and now your judgment is tethered to it.

**Why it happens:** Your brain treats the first number as information, even when it shouldn't. It becomes a reference point, and you adjust from there rather than evaluating the situation fresh. This happens automatically — studies show even random numbers influence subsequent estimates. Show someone a wheel marked 1-100, spin it, then ask them to guess the percentage of African countries in the UN. The wheel's number affects their guess, despite being obviously irrelevant. If arbitrary numbers do this, imagine what happens with numbers that seem meaningful.

**How to spot it:** Ask yourself: "Where did that first number come from?" If you're clustering your thinking around it — treating it as reasonable, adjusting slightly up or down — you're probably anchored. Watch for phrases in your thinking like "well, they said X, so maybe X plus a bit..." The anchor isn't just affecting your estimate; it's defining your range of imagination.

**How to escape:** Before you see any numbers, write down your independent assessment. In that salary negotiation, decide your walk-away point and target before the meeting. For a house purchase, determine your maximum before seeing the listing price. For a project timeline, estimate the work before hearing the client's deadline. You need your own anchor.

When you're already anchored, force yourself to consider extremes. If they offered $75,000, seriously imagine asking for $120,000. Not to actually demand it, but to break the gravitational pull. Think from first principles: what's the value created, what's the market rate, what's your alternative? Rebuild your sense of reasonable from the ground up.

Get a second opinion from someone who hasn't seen the anchor. They'll often name numbers that surprise you — numbers that would have felt obvious before you got anchored.

**Example:** In 2008, Lehman Brothers was shopping itself to potential buyers. The asking price was around $4 billion. Barclays and Bank of America examined the books and walked away — even at that anchor point, the deal looked bad. When Lehman went to $2 billion, then $1 billion in desperation, it still found no buyers. The firm was worth less than zero. But for crucial days, everyone involved spent energy negotiating around those initial billions, asking "how much less than $4 billion?" instead of "should we be involved at all?" The anchor delayed the real question.
