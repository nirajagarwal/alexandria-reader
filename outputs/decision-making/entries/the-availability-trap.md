# The Availability Trap
***We judge what's likely by what we remember easily***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You overestimate the probability of events that come to mind readily. Plane crashes feel more dangerous than car accidents. Shark attacks seem common after watching Jaws. The colleague who spoke up in yesterday's meeting seems more competent than the one who's been quietly shipping code for months. Your mind mistakes "easy to recall" for "likely to happen."

**Why it happens:** Your brain is an efficiency machine. When judging probability, it doesn't calculate statistics — it asks "Can I quickly think of an example?" Vivid, recent, or emotional memories spring to mind faster. A dramatic news story sticks better than a statistic. One personal experience outweighs ten abstract facts. Evolution rewarded quick pattern-matching over accurate probability assessment.

**How to spot it:** Ask yourself: "Am I judging this because I can easily imagine it happening?" Notice when you:
- Make decisions right after hearing dramatic news
- Weigh a single vivid story more than steady patterns
- Feel certain about rare events because you read about one recently
- Dismiss real risks that haven't made headlines
- Change your assessment based on what you watched last night

**How to escape:** Make the invisible visible. Actively search for counter-examples. If you're worried about a startup failing because TechCrunch covered three bankruptcies this week, look up the actual survival rates. If hiring someone reminds you of a great employee, ask what percentage of people with those traits actually succeeded. Get base rates. Write down your reasoning before consuming news about the topic. Check whether dramatic recent events are driving your thinking.

Most powerful: invert it. Ask "What would make me think the opposite?" If plane crashes are top of mind, research the actual numbers. They're boring numbers, which is precisely why they don't spring to mind — and why they're more reliable.

**Example:** After the 2008 financial crisis, MBA applications surged. Finance seemed too risky — everyone could recall banks collapsing, friends losing jobs, dramatic headlines. Meanwhile, applications to law school had been climbing for years despite a saturated legal market. Why? No dramatic images. No Lehman Brothers moment. The slow accumulation of underemployed lawyers didn't create memorable stories. Five years later, law graduates faced worse employment odds than finance MBAs, but the availability of crisis images had shaped thousands of career decisions.
