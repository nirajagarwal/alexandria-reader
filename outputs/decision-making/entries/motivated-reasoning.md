# Motivated Reasoning
***We find what we're looking for***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** You reach the conclusion you wanted before you started thinking. A CEO sees every problem as requiring more aggressive expansion because that's his specialty. A manager finds reasons why the candidate she liked in the first thirty seconds is objectively the best choice. Someone evaluating a risky investment discovers that all the concerning data points are actually bullish signals. The decision feels reasoned, but the reasoning was working backward from a predetermined answer.

**Why it happens:** Your brain isn't a neutral computer. It has preferences, fears, and incentives. When you want something to be true — or when proving it true gets you something you want — your mind becomes a lawyer instead of a judge. It selectively gathers evidence, interprets ambiguity favorably, and dismisses contradictions. This isn't conscious deception. You genuinely believe you're being objective. That's what makes it dangerous.

**How to spot it:** Notice when analysis feels easy. Real thinking involves tension — evidence that doesn't fit, alternatives that have merit, uncertainty that persists. If every data point confirms what you hoped, you're probably not thinking, you're justifying. Watch for phrases like "obviously," "clearly," or "any reasonable person would see." They often mark places where you've stopped examining. Ask yourself: what would I need to see to change my mind? If the answer is "nothing" or feels theoretical, you're reasoning backward.

**How to escape:** Make your prediction explicit before you analyze. Write down what you hope is true. Then actively try to disprove it. Assign someone to argue the opposite case with full intellectual honesty. Seek out the smartest critic of your position and genuinely engage their arguments. Set decision criteria in advance — "I'll invest if X, Y, and Z are true" — before you look at whether they're true. Ask someone with different incentives to evaluate the same information. The goal isn't to eliminate preferences but to prevent them from corrupting your reasoning.

**Example:** In 2003, Kodak's leadership evaluated whether to invest heavily in digital photography. They had access to the same market data as everyone else — digital was obviously the future. But Kodak made money from film and printing, so their analysis kept finding reasons to protect the traditional business. Digital cameras needed better quality. Consumers weren't ready. The printing business would remain strong. Each argument had some truth, but together they formed a motivated conclusion: we can delay. By 2012, Kodak filed for bankruptcy. They weren't stupid — they were reasoning toward an answer that preserved what they had built, rather than reasoning toward what was true.
