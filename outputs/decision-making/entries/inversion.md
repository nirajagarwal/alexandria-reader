# Inversion
***Work backward from what you want to avoid***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** Instead of asking "How do I succeed?" ask "How do I fail?" Map the paths to disaster, then avoid them. Charlie Munger called this "inverting, always inverting" — the practice of approaching problems from the opposite end.

**When to use it:** When forward planning feels overwhelming or abstract. When you're stuck on "the right answer." When the stakes are high and failure is costly. Especially useful for complex decisions where success has many paths but failure has obvious patterns.

**How to do it:**

1. State your goal clearly
2. Flip it: "What would guarantee failure?"
3. List every way to fail — be specific and honest
4. For each failure mode, identify the warning signs
5. Design your approach to avoid these paths
6. Check your actual plan against the failure list

**Example:** Before Amazon launched AWS, they could have asked "How do we build the best cloud service?" Instead, they inverted: "How do we build something nobody will use?" The answers were clear — make it expensive, complicated, unreliable, hard to start, impossible to scale, lock customers in. AWS launched as the opposite: cheap, simple, reliable, easy to start, elastic, pay-as-you-go. They built the service by systematically avoiding every failure mode.

**Watch out for:** Inversion finds floor, not ceiling. It tells you how not to lose, not necessarily how to win big. Some opportunities require risk-taking that inversion might screen out. Also, focusing too much on avoiding failure can create analysis paralysis — sometimes you need to move forward despite risks. Use inversion to set boundaries, but don't let it paralyze action. The goal is informed boldness, not fearful avoidance.
