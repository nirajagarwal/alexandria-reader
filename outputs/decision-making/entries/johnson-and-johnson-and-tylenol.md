# The Tylenol Murders
***When seven people died from poisoned capsules, J&J chose to protect people over profits***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** September 29, 1982. Seven people in Chicago died after taking Extra-Strength Tylenol capsules laced with cyanide. Someone had opened bottles on store shelves, added poison, and returned them. Tylenol was Johnson & Johnson's most profitable product — $1.2 billion in sales, 37% of the pain reliever market, 17% of J&J's corporate profits.

The poisoning wasn't J&J's fault. The tampering happened after the bottles left their control. Their lawyers and some executives argued they had no legal liability. A limited recall of Chicago-area products would satisfy regulators. But CEO James Burke and his team faced a choice: minimize financial damage or maximize public safety.

**The decision:** Within days, J&J recalled all Tylenol capsules nationwide — 31 million bottles worth over $100 million. They stopped all Tylenol advertising. They developed tamper-resistant packaging. They offered to exchange capsules for tablets. Burke appeared on 60 Minutes and Donahue, speaking directly to the public about what J&J was doing and why.

The decision wasn't obvious. The poisonings were geographically contained. Tylenol's market share was collapsing — down to 7%. Marketing experts predicted the brand was dead. Board members questioned whether they were destroying shareholder value to chase an impossible standard of safety.

Burke invoked J&J's Credo, written forty years earlier by Robert Wood Johnson: "We believe our first responsibility is to the doctors, nurses and patients, to mothers and fathers and all others who use our products and services." Not shareholders first. Users first.

**The outcome:** Tylenol's market share recovered to 30% within a year. The tamper-resistant packaging became industry standard, later mandated by FDA regulation. J&J's reputation strengthened — surveys showed increased public trust. The poisoner was never caught, but no one else died.

The financial cost was real. But the alternative — a slow, defensive response — would have meant more uncertainty, more fear, more deaths if the tampering spread. And it would have permanently broken trust between J&J and the public.

**The lesson:** When your values are tested, the expensive choice often becomes the profitable one. Not because virtue guarantees reward, but because trying to split the difference between protecting people and protecting profits convinces no one. Burke didn't know Tylenol would recover. He decided that some gambles are worth taking even if they fail — that some decisions define who you are regardless of outcome.

The question wasn't "What will save the brand?" It was "What kind of company are we?" Answer that clearly enough, and the tactical decisions follow.
