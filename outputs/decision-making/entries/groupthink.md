# Groupthink
***When the desire for harmony silences better judgment***

> **Section:** Traps

**What happens:** A team reaches consensus too easily. Doubts go unvoiced. Warning signs get dismissed. Everyone nods along, and the decision feels inevitable — until it fails spectacularly. Groupthink turns smart people into a choir singing the same wrong note.

**Why it happens:** We're social animals. We want to belong, to maintain harmony, to be team players. When a group has strong cohesion — especially under a confident leader or time pressure — questioning the emerging consensus feels like betrayal. The psychological cost of dissent rises while the perceived benefit falls. It becomes easier to doubt your own judgment than to challenge the room.

The trap strengthens itself: each silent member assumes others agree, which makes their own silence more reasonable. The appearance of unanimity becomes evidence for unanimity.

**How to spot it:** Listen for these signals:

- Decisions feel too smooth, too obvious
- Critics are dismissed as disloyal rather than engaged
- Alternative views aren't explored, just explained away
- The team has a shared enemy or villain (which bonds members but narrows thinking)
- Self-censorship: you have doubts but don't voice them
- The group's confidence seems disconnected from the evidence

If someone finally raises an objection and the room goes quiet with surprise, you were probably in groupthink.

**How to escape:** Break the unanimity explicitly:

**Before deciding:** Assign someone to play devil's advocate — and give them real status to do it. Better yet, rotate the role so dissent becomes normal. Bring in outsiders to review the logic. Create anonymous channels for concerns.

**During discussion:** Leaders should speak last, not first. Invite the quiet voices directly: "Sarah, you look skeptical — what are we missing?" Separate idea generation from evaluation so criticism doesn't feel personal.

**After tentative consensus:** Schedule a second-chance meeting. Ask everyone to come back with one reason the decision could fail. Make reversibility part of the plan.

The goal isn't endless debate. It's ensuring that silence means agreement, not just harmony preservation.

**Example:** In 1961, President Kennedy's advisors planned the Bay of Pigs invasion of Cuba. The group was cohesive, confident, and insulated. Arthur Schlesinger Jr. had serious doubts but stayed quiet, later writing that he feared being dismissed as soft. The Attorney General silenced critics by questioning their loyalty. No one wanted to be the naysayer.

The invasion was a disaster: 1,400 Cuban exiles defeated in three days.

Kennedy learned. During the Cuban Missile Crisis eighteen months later, he deliberately stayed out of early meetings to avoid swaying the group. He brought in outside experts. He encouraged debate and protected dissenters. The decision-making was messier — and it prevented nuclear war.
