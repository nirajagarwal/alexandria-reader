# Scenario Planning
***Prepare for multiple futures instead of predicting one***

> **Section:** Tools

**What it is:** Scenario planning means creating 3-4 detailed, plausible stories about how the future might unfold, then deciding what you'd do in each. Instead of betting on a single prediction, you prepare for different possible worlds. Each scenario gets a name, a narrative, and specific implications for your decision.

**When to use it:** When you're making decisions with long time horizons where the future is genuinely uncertain. Product launches in emerging markets. Career pivots. Major investments. Climate-dependent plans. Anywhere predictions feel like guesswork but doing nothing isn't an option.

**How to do it:**

1. **Identify the critical uncertainties** — not everything that might change, but the 2-3 factors that would fundamentally alter your situation. For a restaurant owner: commercial real estate trends and remote work adoption.

2. **Create 3-4 distinct scenarios** — combine your uncertainties into coherent stories. Don't make them best/worst/middle. Make them different. Name them something memorable: "Office Renaissance," "Hybrid Sprawl," "Full Remote." Write a paragraph describing each world in 2025.

3. **Trace implications** — for each scenario, answer: What would success look like? What capabilities would matter? What would you wish you'd done today?

4. **Find robust moves** — identify actions that work across multiple scenarios. Also note any scenario that would require a dramatically different strategy.

5. **Set triggers** — define the early signals that would tell you which scenario is emerging.

**Example:** In 2007, Shell used scenario planning for oil prices. Instead of predicting a single number, they built scenarios around $40, $70, and $100+ per barrel. When prices spiked to $147 in 2008, they weren't caught flat-footed — they'd already worked through what that world meant for their investment and operational decisions. They had playbooks ready, not panic.

**Watch out for:** Scenarios can become an excuse for indecision — "we're ready for anything" sometimes means you're committed to nothing. The scenarios themselves can be unconsciously biased toward your preferences. And spending three months crafting beautiful scenarios is worthless if you never translate them into actual decisions. The point isn't the stories; it's making better choices today because you've thought seriously about tomorrow's possibilities.
