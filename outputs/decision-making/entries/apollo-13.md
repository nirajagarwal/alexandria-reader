# Apollo 13: "Failure Is Not an Option"
***How NASA turned a catastrophic malfunction into a successful rescue***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** On April 13, 1970, two days into their mission to the moon, Apollo 13's oxygen tank exploded. Commander Jim Lovell heard a bang and felt the spacecraft shudder. Then came the now-famous understatement: "Houston, we've had a problem."

The explosion crippled the Service Module that provided power, oxygen, and water. Three astronauts were 200,000 miles from Earth in a dying spacecraft. The Moon landing was immediately impossible. The question became whether they could survive long enough to get home.

Mission Control had to make dozens of critical decisions in hours, not days. Use the Lunar Module as a lifeboat? Risk running out of power? Attempt a faster but riskier return trajectory? Each choice had cascading effects on oxygen, battery life, and navigation.

**The decision:** Gene Kranz, the lead flight director, made the crucial framing choice immediately. "We've never lost an American in space," he told his team, "and we're sure as hell not going to lose one on my watch. Failure is not an option."

That wasn't bravado. It was a decision about how to decide. They would not waste time on backup plans or acceptable losses. Every calculation, every simulation, every choice would assume success was possible and work backward from there.

They powered down the Command Module to save battery for reentry. They moved the crew into the Lunar Module — designed for two people for two days, now housing three people for four days. They jury-rigged CO2 scrubbers using duct tape, plastic bags, and cardboard — making square canisters fit into round holes because "that's what we have to work with."

The crew performed a manual engine burn using Earth's horizon through their window for alignment — no computer guidance, just pilot skill and math done on paper.

**The outcome:** After 87 hours in a freezing, failing spacecraft, all three astronauts splashed down safely in the Pacific Ocean. What NASA called "a successful failure" — the mission failed, but everyone survived.

**The lesson:** Kranz's framing decision mattered more than any individual technical choice. By ruling out failure as an option, he shaped how hundreds of people allocated their attention and creativity. They didn't debate whether rescue was possible — they solved for how.

But notice what else happened: they improvised constantly. The plan changed hour by hour as new problems emerged. "Failure is not an option" didn't mean "stick to the plan." It meant "we will keep solving until we succeed or run out of physics."

The Apollo 13 decision-making model works when three conditions hold: the stakes are existential, the timeline is compressed, and you have genuine expertise to execute. Miss any of those, and Kranz's approach becomes either paralysis ("we must be perfect") or recklessness ("we can't consider failure").

Most decisions aren't Apollo 13. But when they are — when failure truly isn't acceptable and there's no time for contingency planning — frame it clearly, commit completely, and improvise relentlessly.
