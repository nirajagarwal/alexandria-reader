# FDR's First 100 Days
***When the nation was paralyzed by fear, Roosevelt decided to act — everywhere, all at once.***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** March 4, 1933. Franklin Roosevelt took office with the American economy in freefall. Banks were failing by the hundreds — customers lined up to withdraw their savings, which accelerated more failures. Unemployment had reached 25%. Industrial production had fallen by half. The previous president, Hoover, had tried cautious, limited interventions. They hadn't worked. Public confidence was gone. The country wasn't just in crisis — it was paralyzed by the fear that nothing could be done.

Roosevelt had no proven solution. The scale of the Depression was unprecedented. Economic theory offered competing diagnoses. His advisors disagreed on fundamental questions. But he had a conviction: action itself was better than paralysis. In his inaugural address, he spoke the line that would define his approach: "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself."

**The decision:** Roosevelt chose velocity over certainty. In his first 100 days, his administration proposed and Congress passed fifteen major laws. He closed all banks temporarily, then reopened them with federal backing to restore trust. He created jobs through public works programs. He regulated Wall Street. He reformed banking. He established support for farmers. He repealed Prohibition.

The programs contradicted each other sometimes. The National Recovery Administration tried to raise prices while the Agricultural Adjustment Act tried to lower them. Some initiatives would later be ruled unconstitutional. Roosevelt's team was making it up as they went. But the decision was to move fast, try multiple approaches, and signal unmistakably that the government was engaged.

**The outcome:** The banking crisis ended within weeks. Deposits flowed back. Industrial production began rising. Unemployment started falling — slowly, but the trend reversed. More important than any single program was the shift in mood. People believed something could be done because they saw something being done.

Not all the programs worked. Some created new problems. The economy wouldn't fully recover until World War II. But the 100 Days broke the psychological deadlock. It established that government could act decisively in crisis. It reset expectations about what was possible.

**The lesson:** In a true crisis, deciding to act can matter more than having the perfect plan. When paralysis is the main problem, momentum becomes the solution. Roosevelt couldn't know which programs would work — so he launched enough of them that some would. He treated experimentation as better than analysis-paralysis.

This isn't a license for recklessness. Roosevelt was operating in an emergency where doing nothing was obviously failing. But when the cost of delay is high and the path forward is uncertain, the bias toward action — trying multiple approaches, adjusting as you learn — can break through gridlock. Sometimes you decide to decide, then figure out the details while moving.
