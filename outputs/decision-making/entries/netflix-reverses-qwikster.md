# Netflix Reverses Qwikster
***When admitting failure is the best decision***

> **Section:** Stories

**The situation:** In September 2011, Netflix was riding high with 25 million subscribers. CEO Reed Hastings announced a dramatic split: streaming video would remain Netflix, but DVD-by-mail would become "Qwikster" — a separate service, separate website, separate billing. Customers wanting both would manage two accounts, two queues, two charges.

The announcement came after Netflix had already raised prices 60% by separating streaming and DVD plans. Customers were already angry. Now they'd have to use two websites, search twice for movies, and lose their unified rental history.

The backlash was instant and savage. Facebook comments ran 90% negative. Customers canceled in droves — 800,000 subscribers gone in one quarter. Stock price dropped from $298 to $77. Hastings was named one of the worst CEOs of the year.

**The decision:** Three weeks later, Hastings killed Qwikster. In a blog post titled "DVDs will be staying at Netflix.com," he simply reversed course. No complex explanation. No saving face. Just: "It is clear that for many of our members two websites would make things more difficult, so we are going to keep Netflix as one place to go."

The decision to reverse happened quickly once Hastings saw the customer data and reaction. Some board members reportedly wanted to push forward — they'd already announced it, already looked foolish, why look more foolish by reversing? But Hastings had a principle: listen to customers over your own ego.

**The outcome:** The reversal didn't immediately fix everything. Netflix still lost those 800,000 subscribers. The stock stayed depressed for months. But the hemorrhaging stopped. More importantly, Hastings preserved something crucial: customer trust that Netflix would listen.

Within eighteen months, subscribers were growing again. By 2013, Netflix had 44 million members. The company went on to transform into the streaming giant we know today. The Qwikster debacle became a teaching moment inside Netflix about user experience and humility.

**The lesson:** The cost of admitting failure is usually less than the cost of defending it. Hastings made a bad decision with Qwikster, but he made a good decision about his bad decision. He recognized the difference between commitment and stubbornness.

Three weeks feels fast for a corporate reversal, but it was slow enough that it cost 800,000 customers. The real skill isn't just reversing — it's reversing as soon as you have enough evidence, before pride or sunk costs take over.

When you've made a wrong choice, ask: What would I decide if this were someone else's mistake? That distance helps. Your ego wants to defend the original decision. Your customers, your team, your future self — they all want you to fix it.
