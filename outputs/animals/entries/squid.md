# Squid

A squid is a jet engine wrapped in skin, a brain distributed through tentacles, and a body that can rewrite its own surface in fractions of a second. Eight arms, two longer feeding tentacles, three hearts, and a beak sharp enough to cut through bone. They move by sucking water into a muscular cavity and forcing it out through a siphon—propulsion by controlled explosion. In the open ocean, where there's nothing to hide behind and nowhere to run, squid have evolved not camouflage but *active concealment*: the ability to become, moment by moment, whatever background they're crossing.

Their skin contains millions of chromatophores—pigment sacs ringed with muscle. When the muscles contract, the sac expands, flooding the skin with color. Yellow, red, brown. Beneath these sit iridophores and leucophores, cells that reflect and scatter light. The result: a squid can match sand, rock, coral, open water, or create patterns that have never existed before—waves of color that ripple across the body for reasons we don't fully understand. Alarm. Communication. Thought made visible. Unlike chameleons, which change slowly in response to their surroundings, squid transform in milliseconds, faster than the conscious mind can process. They don't camouflage *to* a background; they perform it, improvising in real time.

This happens despite the fact that most squid are likely colorblind. They have only one type of photoreceptor. Their world, visually, might be rendered in shades of brightness—and yet they match colors they cannot see. The leading theory: their skin itself acts as a distributed eye. Opsins, the light-sensitive proteins that enable vision, have been found in squid skin cells. The skin *sees* the light around it and adjusts accordingly, bypassing the brain entirely. The squid, in this sense, is less a creature with a surface than a surface that thinks.

We have eaten squid for millennia. The Romans called them *loligo*. Mediterranean fishers have harvested them under moonlight, drawing them up with lures that glowed phosphorescent in the dark water. In Japan, dried squid—*surume*—has been a preserved staple for centuries, chewy and intensely savory. In modern industrial fisheries, squid are caught by the millions. Humboldt squid off the coast of South America, Japanese flying squid in the Pacific, shortfin squid in the Atlantic. The global catch exceeds four million tons annually. Fried calamari, grilled on skewers, stuffed with rice, sliced into rings—the squid is one of the most consumed cephalopods on Earth, though we rarely think of it that way. To most people, it's seafood. To the ocean, it's a keystone: predator and prey, linking the deep scattering layer of krill and small fish to the larger hunters—tuna, sharks, whales—that depend on squid as fuel.

Squid themselves are relentless predators. They hunt in schools or alone, depending on the species. The Humboldt squid, Dosidicus gigas, grows to six feet and moves in aggressive packs, swarming prey with coordinated speed. Fishers who've hauled them onto boats report that the squid attack anything within reach—fish, nets, each other, human limbs. Their tentacles are lined with suckers ringed in sharp, chitinous teeth. The beak, hidden at the center of the arms, can sever flesh cleanly. In the deep sea, where some squid species grow to lengths exceeding forty feet—the giant squid, *Architeuthis dux*, recorded at over forty-three feet, the colossal squid, *Mesonychoteuthis hamiltoni*, potentially larger—they hunt in near-total darkness, using eyes the size of dinner plates, the largest in the animal kingdom, to detect the faint silhouettes of prey against the dim light filtering from above.

We know comparatively little about these deep-sea giants. No giant squid was filmed alive in its natural habitat until 2012, when a Japanese research team captured footage at over two thousand feet. What we know of them comes mostly from carcasses washed ashore or pulled from the stomachs of sperm whales. The whales dive deep to hunt them, and the squid fight back—sperm whales often bear circular scars, the marks of squid suckers, across their heads. Some researchers believe the whale's clicking sonar stuns the squid before the attack. Others think the squid simply cannot escape an animal that can hold its breath for ninety minutes and dive to depths where the squid have nowhere left to flee.

What squid have taught us is that intelligence does not require a backbone. Their nervous system is the most complex of any invertebrate: a central brain, large optic lobes, and crucially, a significant portion of their neurons distributed throughout their eight arms. Each arm can act semi-independently, tasting and exploring without waiting for orders from the brain. A severed squid arm will continue to hunt, grasping at prey. The brain delegates; the body decides. This is a form of intelligence we are only beginning to map—decentralized, reactive, embedded in flesh rather than localized in a skull.

In the lab, squid have been used to study neuroscience for nearly a century. The squid's giant axon—a nerve fiber up to a millimeter in diameter, roughly a hundred times thicker than human axons—allowed researchers in the 1930s and '40s to probe the mechanics of nerve transmission directly. Alan Hodgkin and Andrew Huxley used squid axons to demonstrate how action potentials work, the electrical signals that underlie all neural activity. They won the Nobel Prize in 1963. The squid, unintentionally, gave us the foundation of modern neuroscience.

And still, they remain fundamentally strange. They live fast—most species die within a year or two, growing rapidly, reproducing once, and then deteriorating as if programmed for obsolescence. They are soft-bodied and boneless, save for the beak and a thin internal structure called the pen, a ghostly remnant of the shells their ancestors once carried. In the open ocean, they are shapes without fixed form, creatures that dissolve into light and color, that think with their skin, that move like liquid given intent. We eat them, study them, and have barely begun to understand them.
