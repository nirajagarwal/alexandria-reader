# Roundworm

The most abundant animals on Earth are not insects. They are nematodes—roundworms—and they exist in numbers that make human population counts seem like rounding errors. Four out of every five animals alive is a nematode. They live in soil, in water, in the bodies of plants and animals. If every other organism on Earth vanished tomorrow, leaving only nematodes, you could still see the world's topography outlined in their distribution: mountains and valleys, forests and plains, all rendered in worms.

Nathan Cobb, writing in 1915, imagined this spectral landscape: "The location of towns would be decipherable, since for every massing of human beings there would be a corresponding massing of certain nematodes. Trees would still stand in ghostly rows representing our streets and highways." He was not exaggerating. A single spadeful of good soil contains thousands of them. A rotting apple: tens of thousands. Your body: potentially millions, depending on where you live and what you've encountered.

Most nematodes are microscopic, transparent, nearly featureless. They look like animated threads or the cursive marks of an alien script. A tube within a tube—that's the essential architecture. An outer body wall surrounds a digestive tract running mouth to anus, with a fluid-filled space between them that serves as a hydrostatic skeleton. No circulatory system, no respiratory system. Gas exchange happens through the skin. Food moves through the gut by simple muscular contraction. They are among the simplest bilaterally symmetrical animals, and yet they have colonized every conceivable environment: marine sediments five kilometers deep, Antarctic ice, hot springs, beer mats in German pubs.

What they lack in complexity they compensate for in efficiency. A nematode moves by thrashing its body in sinusoidal waves, propagating contractions down its length. On a solid surface this looks comical—they writhe and flip, making slow progress. But in films of liquid, in soil pores, between sediment grains, the motion becomes effective. They are built for squeezing through spaces barely wider than themselves.

The one you've heard of is *Caenorhabditis elegans*, a millimeter-long resident of rotting vegetation that became, in 1974, the first multicellular organism to have every cell in its body mapped. It has exactly 959 somatic cells in the adult hermaphrodite form. Not approximately—exactly. Every *C. elegans* develops the same way, cell by cell, division by division, in an invariant pattern. Researchers have traced the lineage of every cell from fertilized egg to adult worm. They know which neurons connect to which muscles, which genes activate in which cells. In 1998, *C. elegans* became the first animal to have its genome completely sequenced. In 2002, Andrew Fire and Craig Mello discovered RNA interference in *C. elegans*, work that won them the Nobel Prize.

The worm's transparency is part of its utility. You can watch development happen in real time under a microscope—cells dividing, neurons sprouting, organs forming. Researchers have used lasers to destroy individual cells and observe what happens. They've mapped the worm's entire nervous system: 302 neurons, roughly 7,000 synaptic connections. This is the only organism for which we have a complete neural wiring diagram, and we still cannot fully predict its behavior from that diagram alone.

But *C. elegans* is exceptional only in its fame. Most nematodes are parasites. They parasitize plants, fungi, insects, fish, birds, mammals. *Ascaris lumbricoides* infects over 800 million people, living in human intestines and growing to 30 centimeters long. *Wuchereria bancrofti* causes elephantiasis, blocking lymphatic vessels until limbs swell grotesquely. Guinea worm disease, caused by *Dracunculus medinensis*, involves a meter-long worm emerging slowly through a skin blister—the only treatment is to wind it, millimeters per day, around a stick. This is thought to be the origin of the Rod of Asclepius, the medical symbol: a worm on a stick.

The filarial worms are particularly inventive. They require two hosts—a human or other mammal, and an insect vector. *Onchocerca volvulus* causes river blindness: the adult worms live under human skin, producing millions of microfilariae that migrate through tissue. When they die in the eye, the immune response causes scarring and eventually blindness. Black flies transmit the infection. The parasite has co-evolved with a bacterium, *Wolbachia*, which lives inside the worm's cells and is necessary for its reproduction. Kill the bacteria and you kill the worm—this discovery has transformed treatment.

Plants suffer their own nematode plagues. Root-knot nematodes (*Meloidogyne* species) cause billions of dollars in crop losses annually. They inject saliva into root cells, inducing the plant to form galls—tumorous swellings where the worm feeds and reproduces. Cyst nematodes encase themselves in the hardened body of the female, which can survive in soil for years, waiting for a host plant to germinate.

Not all are parasites. Free-living nematodes are critical to soil ecology, consuming bacteria and fungi, mineralizing nutrients, moving through soil pores and creating channels for water and air. They are prey for larger organisms. They are part of the microbial loop that makes life on land possible. When you plant a garden, you are negotiating with nematodes—hoping to cultivate the beneficial ones, discourage the plant-parasitic ones, maintain a balance you can barely see.

What the roundworm teaches is ubiquity without visibility. They are everywhere, doing everything: decomposing, parasitizing, cycling nutrients, connecting ecosystems. They have been found in gold mines three kilometers underground, where they eat bacteria and reproduce in complete darkness. They have outlived extinction events. They will likely outlive us. And yet most humans will live entire lives without seeing one with unaided eyes, never knowing that these near-invisible threads constitute the actual fabric of animal life on Earth—ourselves being merely the conspicuous minority, the brief exception to their rule.
