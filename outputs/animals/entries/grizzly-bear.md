# Grizzly Bear

The grizzly bear can disembowel an elk with a single swipe, crush a bowling ball in its jaws, and sprint at thirty-five miles per hour uphill. It can also spend an entire afternoon turning over rocks to lick ants off its paws, one by one, with what appears to be infinite patience. This is the same animal.

*Ursus arctos horribilis*—the horrible northern bear—ranges across what remains of western wilderness from Alaska to Wyoming, though it once occupied the Great Plains from Mexico to Canada. An adult male weighs six hundred pounds on average, eight hundred in coastal regions where salmon are abundant. They can stand over eight feet tall. Their claws, which never retract, grow up to four inches long and are visible in their tracks, five parallel grooves ahead of the pad like a rake dragged through mud.

The scientific name captures something Europeans felt upon encountering them: horror, in the old sense of the word—not fear exactly, but a kind of metaphysical dread at confronting an animal that could kill you as casually as you might kill a mosquito. Lewis and Clark shot one grizzly eleven times before it died. Another pursued them into the Missouri River, where they finally killed it from a canoe. Lewis wrote that these bears were "furious and formidable" and that "the Indians may well fear this animal." The Corps of Discovery had been warned. They hadn't listened.

But the grizzly's power is less interesting than its intelligence. They remember berry patches decades after visiting them, can navigate hundreds of square miles of territory using landmarks and spatial memory that rivals a London cabbie's knowledge of the city. A study in Yellowstone found that grizzlies could distinguish between fifteen different species of pine cones and knew which ones produced nuts worth the energy of harvest. They learn from each other—in Alaska, young bears watch adults fishing and adopt the techniques of successful hunters: the dive, the stand-and-wait, the underwater snorkel approach with head submerged and rump in the air.

Their diet reveals something about the kind of intelligence this is. Grizzlies are opportunistic omnivores who eat roots, berries, grubs, fish, carrion, hoofed mammals they kill themselves, and occasionally each other. They'll eat seventy different plant species in a year. In spring, they dig up glacier lily bulbs and biscuitroot, excavating gardens the size of suburban yards. In late summer, they enter hyperphagia—a state of near-constant eating where they consume twenty thousand calories a day, gorging on huckleberries, buffalo berries, whitebark pine nuts. A single bear can eat forty thousand berries in a day. They remember where the bushes are heaviest, which slopes face south and ripen first, when to arrive. This is ecological knowledge, accumulated and precise.

The hibernation that follows is not sleep but a metabolic marvel. Heart rate drops from forty beats per minute to eight. They don't eat, drink, urinate, or defecate for seven months. Pregnant females give birth during hibernation—usually to twins—and nurse them while remaining largely unconscious. The cubs weigh one pound at birth. By the time the family emerges in spring, they weigh ten to fifteen pounds and are ready to follow their mother through a landscape that is, to them, entirely new.

This is when grizzlies are most dangerous to humans: a mother with cubs is evolution's answer to the question of how much violence one animal can summon in defense of another. She will charge anything, including animals five times her size. Most charges are bluffs—she stops short, woofing and clacking her teeth. But not always. Between 2000 and 2015, grizzlies killed forty-four people in North America. For context, moose killed thirty-five. Domestic dogs killed hundreds. Grizzlies attack far less often than their reputation suggests, but when they do, the damage is comprehensive. They are built to kill animals with thick hides and heavy bones.

We killed them for this capacity, systematically, until they were eliminated from 98 percent of their former range in the lower forty-eight states. By the 1970s, fewer than a thousand remained. The Endangered Species Act protected them; their numbers have since recovered to around two thousand. This is both a conservation success and a permanent diminishment. The grizzly now exists in islands of wilderness—Yellowstone, Glacier, the North Cascades, the Cabinet-Yaak. There is not enough habitat to connect these populations. They are genetically isolated, shrinking worlds unto themselves.

The Blackfeet knew them as *Real Bear* and considered them sacred. Warriors sought visions from them. Killing one required ceremony and apology. The bear's claws became necklaces that signified courage or spiritual power—or both, which were not distinguished. To wear grizzly claws was to carry a reminder that some beings cannot be dominated, only occasionally survived.

What the grizzly teaches is not about strength, though it possesses that absolutely. It's about competence—about being a thorough creature in a complex world. The bear knows things: where to dig, when to fish, which berries ripen first on which hillsides, how much fat it needs to survive winter. It investigates. A grizzly encountering something new will approach cautiously, test it with paws and nose, assess risk and reward. They are careful. They pay attention. This is why they endure.

In autumn, when they feed for twenty hours a day, grizzlies leave caches everywhere—half-eaten carcasses buried under dirt and branches, excavated root patches, berry bushes stripped to twigs. The landscape records their passage, their hungers. To walk where they've been is to see the world as a pantry with a thousand shelves, all mapped in a mind we'll never access. The grizzly moves through wilderness not as a visitor but as someone who knows every room.
