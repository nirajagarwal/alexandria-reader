# Mantis Shrimp

The mantis shrimp is not a mantis and not a shrimp. It is a stomatopod—a marine crustacean that has been perfecting violence for 400 million years, longer than trees have existed on land. There are over 450 species, ranging from thumb-sized to nearly a foot long, living in burrows and crevices throughout tropical and subtropical waters. They hunt. They see colors humans cannot name. And they strike with appendages that move so fast the water around them boils.

Two types dominate: spearers and smashers. Spearers have barbed, harpoon-like clubs that impale soft-bodied prey—fish, worms, other crustaceans. Smashers have hammer-like clubs used to pulverize hard-shelled animals: snails, crabs, mollusks. The peacock mantis shrimp, *Odontodactylus scylarus*, is a smasher. Its club accelerates at 10,400 g—more than a .22 caliber bullet. The strike itself takes three milliseconds. The impact generates a cavitation bubble that collapses with a secondary shockwave and a flash of light called sonoluminescence. If the first blow doesn't kill the prey, the shockwave often does.

The club is a marvel of biological engineering. The outer layer is made of hydroxyapatite, the same mineral in human bones and teeth, arranged in a helicoidal structure—layers rotated slightly like a spiral staircase—that distributes force and resists fracture. Beneath that is a region of biopolymers that absorbs and dissipates energy. Engineers at UC Riverside have studied this structure to design better body armor, aircraft panels, and football helmets. The mantis shrimp has been breaking shells for eons; we are only beginning to learn how.

They molt regularly, and after each molt, the club is temporarily soft. During this vulnerable period, many species retreat into their burrows and wait. They know their own limits. Aquarium workers know them too: mantis shrimp have been known to shatter the glass of their tanks. Not out of aggression—out of habit. They strike at movement, at shadows, at the geometry of their world.

But the violence is not the strangest thing about them.

The mantis shrimp has sixteen types of photoreceptor cells in its eyes. Humans have three: red, green, blue. Most mammals have two. Birds, with their rich color vision, have four or five. Sixteen seems absurd, excessive—a biological extravagance. For years, scientists assumed that mantis shrimp must see a kaleidoscopic range of colors far beyond human perception, a richer palette than anything we could imagine.

They were wrong.

Research by Hanne Thoen and her colleagues at the University of Queensland revealed that mantis shrimp are surprisingly bad at discriminating between similar colors. Humans can detect a wavelength difference of 1-4 nanometers; mantis shrimp need a difference of 25 nanometers or more. Their sixteen photoreceptors don't create a more nuanced picture—they create a faster one. Each receptor responds to a narrow band of color, allowing the mantis shrimp to recognize colors almost instantly, without the neural processing that human and mammalian brains require to compare signals from three photoreceptors. Speed over subtlety. Recognition over contemplation.

Their eyes move independently, each one containing trinocular vision—three separate regions that perceive depth on their own. They can see in twelve directions simultaneously. They detect linear and circular polarized light, a dimension of vision invisible to us, useful for detecting transparent prey and for communication. Some species have fluorescent markings on their bodies visible only in polarized light—signals meant for each other, unreadable to most predators.

To be a mantis shrimp is to inhabit a sensorium we cannot enter. Not richer, necessarily, but differently organized: a world of rapid color recognition, layered polarization, and geometric precision. Vision built not for contemplation but for action.

They are solitary, territorial, and remarkably monogamous. Some species pair-bond for up to twenty years, sharing a burrow and defending it together. They communicate through rumbles—low-frequency sounds produced by vibrating a specialized muscle. Rivals signal before fighting; mates signal before mating. When one partner dies, the survivor often remains in the burrow, alone.

The female carries her eggs in her front appendages, ventilating them with water, cleaning them, protecting them until they hatch. She does not eat during this time, which can last several weeks. The larvae drift as plankton before settling and beginning their own excavations, their own long practice of precision and force.

Fishermen in Southeast Asia and the Mediterranean have known them for centuries—sometimes as bycatch, sometimes as food. In Japanese and Mediterranean cuisines, they are steamed or grilled, the meat sweet and delicate. In Italian, they are *canocchie*; in Cantonese, *lai piu*—"pissing shrimp," named for the way they spray water when handled. They are not commercially farmed. They are too aggressive, too territorial, too likely to destroy each other and anything nearby.

Aquarium hobbyists both fear and desire them. Online forums fill with warnings and advice: use thick acrylic, not glass; don't put anything fragile in the tank; don't underestimate them. Some keepers report personalities—curiosity, apparent recognition of individual humans, even what looks like playfulness. Others report only destruction. One keeper described coming home to find every snail in the tank obliterated, their shells reduced to gravel, the mantis shrimp resting calmly in its corner.

They do not care what we think of them. They have been what they are since before the first vertebrates crawled onto land. They have refined their clubs, their color vision, their burrows, and their strikes across geologic time. We build armor inspired by their bodies; we try to decode the light they see. They continue to hunt, to mate, to dig, to strike—precise and pitiless and entirely themselves.
