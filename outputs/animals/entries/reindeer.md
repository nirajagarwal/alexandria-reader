# Reindeer

The reindeer is the only deer species in which females grow antlers. This fact alone should reorient how we think about these animals—not as the neutered servants of Christmas mythology, but as creatures whose basic biology confounds the mammalian pattern of male display and female receptivity. Both sexes carry bone crowns. Both sexes shed them. But they do so on different schedules, which means that in midwinter, when male reindeer have dropped their antlers and stand bare-headed in the snow, the females still wear theirs. Every reindeer pulling Santa's sleigh, if we're being honest about the timing, would be female.

This is a species designed for cold. The reindeer's hollow hair provides insulation so effective that its core body temperature can remain stable in conditions that would kill most mammals. Its hooves change with the seasons: soft and spongy in summer for traction on wet tundra, hard and sharp-edged in winter for breaking through ice to reach the lichen underneath. The nose, often remarked upon for its cartoonish size, is a countercurrent heat exchanger—incoming air is warmed by outgoing breath, conserving thermal energy while preventing frostbite of the nasal passages. In infrared photography, a feeding reindeer appears as a bright core surrounded by darkness. Almost no heat escapes.

They see ultraviolet light. This was discovered only in 2011 by a team studying Arctic animal vision at University College London. While most mammals have evolved to filter out UV wavelengths—which can damage the retina—reindeer retain sensitivity to them. This allows them to perceive lichen, which absorbs UV light and appears dark against the reflective snow, and to see the urine trails of predators, which glow in ultraviolet like runway lights. They navigate the monochrome Arctic by reading an invisible spectrum. The whiteout that blinds us is, to them, legible.

Humans have lived alongside reindeer for at least 20,000 years. The earliest evidence appears in cave paintings and carved antlers from southern France—records of hunting before agriculture, before domestication, before the concept of livestock existed. But approximately 3,000 years ago, somewhere in the subarctic forests and tundra of northern Eurasia, a shift occurred. People began not just hunting reindeer but following them, managing them, living as they lived. The Sámi of northern Scandinavia, the Nenets of Siberia, the Evenki and Chukchi—each developed elaborate systems of reindeer herding that required moving with the animals through their seasonal migrations, covering hundreds of miles between summer and winter pastures.

This is a different relationship than the ones we built with cattle or sheep. Those animals were bent to our geography, confined to our pastures, bred toward docility and stasis. Reindeer herding demanded the opposite: humans who would follow the animals' logic, accept their routes, adapt to their needs. The herders didn't so much domesticate reindeer as enter into negotiation with them. Reindeer are only semi-domesticated even now—genetically, they're nearly identical to wild caribou. They can't be fenced. They can't be fully controlled. What they permit is companionship, use, and a degree of management, but the fundamental bargain is that you go where they go.

They eat lichen. In winter, this becomes almost their entire diet—primarily *Cladonia rangiferina*, appropriately called reindeer lichen, a slow-growing symbiosis of fungus and algae that carpets the Arctic floor. Lichen grows at roughly five millimeters per year. A reindeer requires four to seven kilograms per day. The math of this is severe: a feeding area can support a herd for only so long before the lichen is depleted and requires decades to recover. This is why reindeer must migrate, why they can't stay still, why the relationship with them is always a relationship with movement.

The global population of domestic and semi-domestic reindeer is approximately 2.5 million. Most are in Russia—about 1.6 million, down from 2.4 million in 1990. The collapse coincides with economic changes following the Soviet Union's dissolution: state-supported herding gave way to market pressures, younger generations left for cities, traditional migration routes were disrupted by oil and gas development. In some regions, herding persists; in others, it has become economically unviable. The animals remain, but the human knowledge of how to live with them is eroding.

Climate change is altering the terms of existence for Arctic animals. Warmer winters bring rain that freezes into ice layers the reindeer can't break through to reach lichen. Earlier springs trigger plant growth before the calves are born, mistiming nutrition with need. The animals are adapted to extreme cold, not to variability. They can endure minus forty degrees. They struggle with unpredictability.

What the reindeer teaches is that domestication isn't always domination—that humans can form working relationships with animals that require us to change as much as they do, to accept limits, to follow rather than lead. The reindeer herders didn't conquer the Arctic; they learned to live in it by attending to an animal that already knew how. This is a knowledge being lost, and with it a model of subsistence that didn't demand the land submit but rather that people move with its rhythms, carried forward by hooves that grow sharp enough to crack ice, by noses that warm the frozen air, by eyes that read the invisible.
