# Duck

The duck's bill is not a bill. It is a soft, pliable apparatus studded with mechanoreceptors—touch-sensitive nerve endings packed so densely that a duck can find prey in murky water by feel alone, filtering mud through lamellae, the tiny comb-like structures along the bill's edges that strain out edible matter while water runs through. The bill looks rigid from a distance, looks like something you might call a beak, but touch one and you'll find it flexible, almost rubbery, covered in a thin layer of keratin over bone riddled with blood vessels and nerves. A duck does not peck. It sifts, strains, filters. It reads the world through its face.

There are over 120 species, distributed across every continent except Antarctica. Most people think "duck" and see a mallard—the male's iridescent green head, the female's mottled brown—but the family includes the eider, whose down humans have harvested for centuries to insulate bedding; the muscovy, native to Central and South America and kept domestically for meat; the merganser, whose serrated bill catches fish; the ruddy duck, whose male sports a bright blue bill during breeding season; the wood duck, whose plumage looks hand-painted. What unites them is not appearance but adaptation to water: webbed feet, waterproof feathers maintained by oil from the uropygial gland near the tail, and that specialized bill.

Humans domesticated ducks at least 4,000 years ago, probably in China and Southeast Asia, selecting certain mallards for size, egg production, and temperament. The Pekin duck—white, heavy-bodied, raised by the millions for meat—descends from Chinese stock brought to the United States in 1873. Sixty-five ducklings made the journey from Beijing to Long Island. From those birds came an industry. The American Pekin now produces more meat per bird than any other duck breed, grows to market weight in seven weeks, and has been engineered into a creature that can barely fly. Domestication is always a negotiation, but with ducks the terms were stark: we took their sky.

In medieval Europe, ducks were caught, penned, and fattened for the table, but they were also wild, migratory, common. They populated marshes, ponds, rivers. They were abundant enough to be unremarkable except at the moment of harvest. The duck decoy—a word derived from the Dutch *eendenkooi*, meaning "duck cage"—was a funnel-shaped trap built into wetlands, designed to lure wild ducks into progressively narrower channels until they could be netted. Hunters used tame ducks, trained dogs, even geometric arrangements of reeds to manipulate the flight paths of wild birds. The decoy exploited the duck's social instinct: where one lands, others follow.

Ducks migrate. Not all species, but many—mallards, pintails, teals—move thousands of miles between breeding and wintering grounds, navigating by landmarks, stars, magnetic fields, inherited routes. A mallard banded in Saskatchewan might winter in Louisiana. These flyways overlap with human geographies, and for centuries ducks have been a predictable, seasonal source of protein. The scale of the harvest was enormous. In the late 19th century, market hunters in the United States killed millions of ducks annually, shipping them by rail to urban markets until the practice was curtailed by the Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918. The law recognized something that industrial slaughter had made visible: ducks are migratory commons, belonging to no one nation, and their populations can collapse.

They can also think. A 2016 study published in *Science* found that ducklings—less than a day old—can grasp abstract concepts of "same" and "different." Researchers showed ducklings a pair of objects, then tested whether the ducklings would follow a new pair that matched in sameness or difference. They did. The ducklings generalized the concept, applying it to shapes and colors they'd never seen. This is relational thinking, something humans don't typically manage until age two or three. The duckling does it within hours of hatching, possibly because its survival depends on recognizing and following its mother in a chaotic, predator-rich world. Imprinting—the process by which a duckling bonds to the first moving object it sees—looks simple, but it requires sophisticated pattern recognition. The duckling must learn what "mother" means.

Ducks have also taught humans about hydrodynamics. In the 1950s, engineers studied the way ducks move through water with minimal drag, how their webbed feet fold on the forward stroke and spread on the backward stroke, how their bodies reduce turbulence. This research influenced designs for ship hulls, submarine propellers, and swimming fins. The duck is incidentally efficient. It is not trying to teach; it is simply solving the problem of being a duck in water. But humans, watching, learn.

Ducks are eaten everywhere they are found. In France, there is *confit de canard*—duck leg slow-cooked in its own fat. In China, Peking duck, its skin lacquered and crisp, served with pancakes and scallions. In the United States, duck is less common than chicken or turkey, but it persists in niches: Chinatowns, upscale restaurants, the occasional holiday table. The flavor is richer, darker, fattier. The meat tastes like the water the duck swam in, the life it lived.

And ducks die by the millions. Avian influenza, habitat loss, hunting, predation. Wetlands drained for agriculture eliminate breeding grounds. Climate change shifts migration timing, decoupling ducks from the seasonal abundance of food they evolved to exploit. Still, many species remain common. The mallard is among the most numerous wild birds on Earth. Ducks persist because they adapted to water, to migration, to humans. They are wary but not impossible to approach. They are edible but not easy to exterminate. They are familiar and, if you watch them long enough, strange.

They sleep with one eye open. Literally. Ducks practice unihemispheric slow-wave sleep—shutting down one half of the brain while the other remains alert, one eye closed, the other scanning for predators. A duck in a row of ducks will sleep more deeply in the middle, less so on the edges. The ones on the periphery take shifts. It is not altruism. It is arithmetic. The duck on the end is most likely to be eaten, so it sleeps least. The system is fair in the way nature is fair: no one wants to be on the edge.
