# Great White Shark

The great white shark grows teeth in conveyor-belt rows—up to 300 at a time, arranged in five lines that move forward as older teeth fall out. A single shark may go through 30,000 teeth in a lifetime. This is not waste but precision engineering: each tooth is a three-inch serrated blade designed to saw through muscle, fat, and bone, then be discarded. The shark doesn't maintain its weapons. It manufactures them continuously, like a factory that never closes.

*Carcharodon carcharias* reaches lengths of twenty feet and weights exceeding two tons, though reports of larger specimens persist in the gray literature of commercial fishermen and spearfishermen who have no reason to exaggerate and every reason to remember accurately. The shark is gray on top, white underneath—counter-shading that makes it nearly invisible when seen from above against the dark sea floor or from below against the bright surface. It has no swim bladder, the gas-filled organ that gives most fish buoyancy. Instead, its liver—which can weigh up to 500 pounds—is filled with oil. The shark must keep moving or sink.

This need for constant motion has been misunderstood as aggression. The great white doesn't hunt because it's angry. It hunts because it's swimming, and it's swimming because stopping means dying. Its metabolism runs hot—not quite warm-blooded but close, maintaining body temperature well above the surrounding water through a heat-exchange system in its blood vessels. This makes it faster than cold-blooded fish but demands enormous caloric intake. A large white shark needs roughly 30,000 calories a week, the equivalent of sixty steaks. It achieves this by eating seals.

The mechanics are well documented. The shark approaches from below, accelerating to speeds of twenty-five miles per hour. At the moment of impact, it extends its jaws outward—the entire snout lifts and the mouth protrudes forward—and rotates its eyes backward into its skull for protection. The first bite is investigative. Great whites have electrosensory organs called ampullae of Lorenzini, pores on the snout that detect the electrical fields generated by living creatures. But they cannot tell seal from surfer until they taste. Most human victims survive the first bite because the shark realizes its mistake and withdraws. It was expecting blubber. It got neoprene.

This fact has not calmed our ancient fear. The great white has been depicted as supernatural evil—in *Jaws*, in countless imitations, in the cultural imagination of anyone who grew up after 1975. Peter Benchley, who wrote *Jaws*, spent the last decades of his life advocating for shark conservation, trying to undo the damage his novel had done. He didn't entirely succeed. Great whites are now protected in most waters, but they were extensively hunted throughout the twentieth century, not for food or oil but for jaws and teeth sold as trophies. Shark populations declined by up to seventy percent in some regions.

What we've learned from studying them contradicts the mythology. Great whites are curious. They investigate objects—including boats, cages, and debris—by mouthing them gently. They are strategic. Researchers off Seal Island in South Africa documented great whites learning to anticipate seal swimming patterns and adjusting their ambush points accordingly. They are social in ways we're only beginning to understand. Tracked sharks return to the same hunting grounds year after year and have been observed tolerating the presence of other sharks in ways that suggest recognition or hierarchy.

Their intelligence is harder to quantify than a dolphin's or octopus's, but it exists in a form suited to their lives. They navigate thousands of miles between feeding and mating grounds using geomagnetic cues. Tagged specimens have crossed entire ocean basins. One female traveled from South Africa to Australia and back—a round trip of 12,000 miles—in nine months. They remember. One great white named Nicole has made the journey between South Africa and Australia multiple times, suggesting not just navigation but destination memory.

The great white's brain-to-body ratio is comparable to some mammals. The cerebellum—the region associated with coordination and motor control—is highly developed. So is the olfactory bulb. A great white can detect one drop of blood in 25 gallons of water and sense blood from miles away. But their most sophisticated sense may be electroreception. The ampullae of Lorenzini can detect electrical fields as weak as one-billionth of a volt. They use this to find prey buried in sand, to navigate using the Earth's magnetic field, and possibly to sense the emotional state of other animals—fear and stress alter bioelectric fields.

Humans kill approximately 100 million sharks per year, mostly for fins used in soup. Great whites are killed at a lower rate because their fins aren't prized, but they die as bycatch in nets and longlines. Current estimates suggest fewer than 3,500 great whites remain worldwide, though counting them is difficult—they're solitary, wide-ranging, and spend most of their time in deep water. Sharks attack roughly ten people per year globally. Humans kill one shark every three seconds.

The great white has survived for sixteen million years, predating the earliest hominids by a factor of ten. It has seen ice ages and warm periods, mass extinctions and radiations. It has outlasted megalodon, its giant ancestor. It is not a relic but a perfected design—precisely calibrated to its ecological niche, neither wasteful nor excessive. We call it a killing machine, but it's more accurate to say it's an eating machine, and eating is the work of staying alive. The terror we feel when we enter its habitat is appropriate. We aren't at the top of the food chain in the ocean. The great white is. The fear teaches us our place.
