# Cockroach

There are approximately 4,600 species of cockroach. Thirty are considered pests. The other 4,570 live in forests, caves, and leaf litter, pollinating plants, recycling nutrients, serving as food for countless predators. They are ecologically essential, evolutionarily ancient, and almost entirely invisible to us. We know only the thirty that have chosen to live where we live, eating what we eat, thriving in the infrastructures we build. These thirty we know intimately—which is to say, we know them as invaders of a space we consider ours.

The German cockroach, *Blattella germanica*, which likely originated in Southeast Asia despite its name, lives almost exclusively in human structures. It cannot survive winter outdoors in temperate climates. It has been carried everywhere humans have built heated buildings, traveling in cargo, luggage, and furniture. In evolutionary terms, it has domesticated itself to us—not as companion but as parasite, extracting value from our buildings and our waste while offering nothing we want in return. A single female can produce up to 400 offspring in her lifetime. They reach reproductive maturity in 36 days.

What makes a cockroach successful in human spaces is not resilience—the mythology of indestructibility is overstated—but flexibility. They eat almost anything organic: food, paper, glue, hair, each other. They can survive a month without food, two weeks without water. Their flat bodies compress to fit through cracks the width of a quarter. They run at speeds of 50 body lengths per second, which, scaled to human size, would be roughly 200 miles per hour. The cercal system—wind-sensing organs on their rear end—detects air movement from approaching threats and triggers escape responses in as little as 8.2 milliseconds. By the time your foot descends toward a cockroach, it has already begun to run. It perceives your approach before your conscious mind has completed the decision to strike.

They are also adapting in real time. Researchers have documented cockroach populations evolving glucose aversion—what tastes like food to ancestral roaches now tastes bitter to their descendants. This occurred because glucose was the attractant in many cockroach baits. The ones that found glucose repulsive didn't eat the poison. Within a few years, entire populations shifted. Evolution at observable speed, driven by human intervention.

This adaptability has made them objects of study. The nervous system of the American cockroach (*Periplaneta americana*) has become a model organism in neuroscience. Its relatively simple ganglionic system—distributed rather than centralized—allows researchers to study learning, memory, and neural signaling in ways that are difficult with more complex organisms. Cockroaches can learn to associate smells with food or danger. They can be trained. The gap between what we call simple and what we call complex narrows under scrutiny.

They are also, improbably, among the most attentive parents in the insect world. Some species give birth to live young rather than laying eggs. The Pacific beetle cockroach (*Diploptera punctata*) produces a protein-rich crystalline milk to feed its developing embryos—a structure that researchers have analyzed as one of the most nutritious substances known, containing more than three times the energy of buffalo milk. Other species guard their egg cases, and newly hatched nymphs remain with their mothers, feeding on her feces to acquire the gut bacteria necessary for digesting cellulose. This is not anthropomorphic projection. It is documented care: the allocation of parental resources to offspring survival.

Still, we kill them. We have always killed them. Romans used fermented fish sauce mixed with pepper as a roach repellent. Medieval households smoked out infestations with sulfur. By the 19th century, arsenical pastes and phosphorus-based poisons were common. The 20th century brought organophosphates, carbamates, pyrethroids—each one initially effective, each one eventually overcome by resistance. American households spend over $1.5 billion annually on cockroach control. The industry exists because the animals persist.

They persist, in part, because they were here long before us. Cockroach fossils date to the Carboniferous period, roughly 320 million years ago, when the first forests were spreading across the supercontinent Pangaea. They are older than dinosaurs, older than flowering plants. They have survived five mass extinctions. The mythology that they would survive nuclear war is exaggerated—radiation studies show cockroaches are not exceptionally resistant to fallout—but the broader point holds. They are survivors not because they are indestructible but because they are flexible, fecund, and willing to occupy margins.

What they teach us, then, is not about their nature but about ours. We have created environments—dense, warm, filled with organic waste—that are perfect for them. Every poorly sealed door, every crumb behind the refrigerator, every damp pipe and dark cabinet is an invitation. They live in the gaps of our infrastructure, the places we overlook. And our revulsion is telling. They do not bite, do not sting, carry few diseases compared to rats or mosquitoes. They are dangerous primarily as allergens—cockroach feces and shed skins are significant asthma triggers, particularly in low-income urban housing. But the visceral response they provoke exceeds rational assessment. They disgust us because they live where we live, eat what we eat, and remain fundamentally indifferent to us. They remind us that our homes are not sealed fortresses but leaky ecosystems. They are proof that we share the world with creatures whose flourishing depends on ours but who do not ask permission.

Thirty species. Thirty that we know. Four thousand five hundred and seventy that we don't. The question is not whether cockroaches will survive us. It is whether, in trying to eliminate them, we recognize what they reveal about the spaces we build and the lives we lead within them.
