# Vampire Bat

The vampire bat drinks blood exclusively. No fruit, no insects, no nectar—just blood, two tablespoons per night, every night, or it dies. This is the most specialized diet of any mammal. It's also, arguably, the most social.

Three species practice this: the common vampire bat, which feeds primarily on cattle and horses; the hairy-legged vampire bat, which prefers birds; and the white-winged vampire bat, also a bird specialist. All three live in Central and South America. The common vampire bat, *Desmodus rotundus*, is the one that concerns ranchers and captivates researchers. It weighs about an ounce. Its wingspan is seven inches. It can kill a cow over time, bleeding it gradually across weeks of nocturnal visits. It can also, in captivity, recognize individual human caretakers by their breathing patterns.

The bat lands near its prey—rarely on it. It approaches on foot, walking with a strange, crouched gait, using its thumbs and hind legs like a four-legged mammal that forgot how. Thermoreceptors in its nose detect blood flow beneath the skin, mapping heat signatures invisible to its eyes. It bites with teeth sharper than surgical steel, incisors that remove a divot of flesh so cleanly the animal often doesn't wake. The bat's saliva contains an anticoagulant called draculin—named, inevitably, after the Count—which prevents clotting for hours. It laps, rather than sucks. The tongue has grooves that channel blood into the mouth through capillary action. It drinks for perhaps twenty minutes, its stomach expanding, its weight doubling.

Then it must fly home. This is a problem. The bat is now too heavy. So it begins urinating immediately, even while still feeding, processing the blood's water content to reduce mass. Within two minutes of drinking, it's already producing urine. By the time it takes off, it's lighter, but not light enough to be safe. A vampire bat gorged with blood flies poorly, struggles to gain altitude, becomes vulnerable to predators. This is why they rarely feed alone far from the roost.

The roost—a hollow tree, a cave, an abandoned building—is where the strangeness becomes more complex. Vampire bats live in colonies of twenty to a hundred individuals, and they share food. Not occasionally. Regularly. A bat that has fed will regurgitate blood directly into the mouth of a bat that has not. This is one of the most well-documented cases of reciprocal altruism in mammals.

The sharing isn't random. Vampire bats remember who has helped them. Gerald Wilkinson, working in Costa Rica in the 1980s, demonstrated that bats preferentially regurgitate blood to individuals who have fed them in the past. They also share more with close relatives, but kinship alone doesn't predict sharing. History does. If you've helped me, I help you. If you've refused me, I don't. This is reciprocity with memory, a system that requires tracking past behavior and adjusting future action accordingly.

The stakes are high. A vampire bat can survive only about two nights without feeding. After that, starvation. Young bats are particularly vulnerable—their metabolism is faster, their margin smaller. Mother bats feed their young regurgitated blood for months. But when the mother fails to feed, others in the colony may step in. Adoptive feeding is common, though again, not random. Bats feed infants they've associated with before, infants whose mothers have shared with them.

This is sometimes framed as altruism, but it's more accurate to call it insurance. The bat that shares tonight may need sharing tomorrow. The system works because need is unpredictable—some nights, prey is scarce or alert, and even experienced bats return empty—and because bats live long enough (up to nine years in the wild, over twenty in captivity) for reciprocity to pay off. They form friendships, in the functional sense: long-term relationships with non-kin, based on repeated positive interactions.

We know this because researchers have studied captive colonies for decades, tracking who feeds whom, who grooms whom, who roosts near whom. Vampire bats spend hours grooming each other, particularly around the head and neck, where they can't reach themselves. Grooming predicts sharing. The bats with the most grooming partners are the most integrated into the food-sharing network. Social bonds literally keep them alive.

In the wild, humans have mostly seen them as pests. Vampire bats transmit rabies, a serious concern for livestock and humans. In Latin America, thousands of cattle die annually from rabies contracted through bat bites. Control programs cull bats, often indiscriminately, sometimes wiping out non-vampire species in the process. The common vampire bat, at least, is not endangered—its range has expanded with the spread of livestock, which provide abundant, predictable feeding opportunities. But the relationship is antagonistic. Ranchers see them as thieves. The bats see cattle as resources, warm and large and, compared to native prey, remarkably easy to approach.

Before cattle, they fed on tapirs, deer, pecaries. They probably still prefer wild prey, when available. But wild prey is declining. Domesticated prey is not. The vampire bat has become a creature of the Anthropocene, its survival increasingly tied to human agriculture, its population shaped by human decisions about which animals to kill and which to tolerate.

In the lab, though, they've taught us about cooperation. The food-sharing network of vampire bats has become a model for understanding how reciprocity evolves, how animals track social debts, how cooperation can persist in the absence of human-style morality or rules. The bat that shares blood isn't being generous. It's being strategic, in a system where strategy looks a lot like friendship.

They've also taught us about saliva. Draculin is being studied as an anticoagulant for human stroke patients. The bat that bleeds cows may, eventually, help dissolve clots in human brains. This is typical. We fear them, kill them, and then quietly borrow from their biology, extracting what's useful and discarding the rest.

The name "vampire" was inevitable—blood-drinking nocturnal creature with fangs. But the metaphor obscures as much as it reveals. Vampires, in myth, are solitary predators, immortal and aristocratic, draining victims dry. Vampire bats are communal, mortal, and remarkably gentle in their feeding. They take enough to survive, not enough to kill. And then they go home and share what they've taken, in a system of mutual aid that keeps the colony alive. The real animal is stranger, and more instructive, than the myth.
