# Pangolin

The pangolin walks on its hind legs with its front claws folded against its chest, tail held out for balance, looking less like a mammal than like a small dinosaur escaped from the Mesozoic. This posture—upright, deliberate, vaguely penitent—has made it one of the most trafficked animals on Earth. People want to eat it, grind its scales into powder, possess it. The pangolin's response to threat is to curl into a ball, scales overlapping like roof tiles, and wait. This defense works perfectly against leopards. Against humans with sacks, it fails completely.

There are eight species of pangolin, four in Asia and four in Africa, ranging from the size of a housecat to a medium dog. All are covered in keratin scales—the same protein that makes up human fingernails and rhino horn—that account for twenty percent of their body weight. A pangolin is essentially a mammal wearing medieval armor made of its own hair. The scales grow throughout its life, get damaged and shed and replaced. They're sharp enough to cut, and when threatened, a pangolin can turn itself into a ball of blades by tucking its head to its tail and interlocking the scales. Mother pangolins curl around their young. If a predator persists, the pangolin can deploy its scales as weapons, slashing with its tail.

But the primary function of those scales is not defense—it's digging. Pangolins are myrmecophages, ant and termite specialists, and they spend their nights excavating nests with claws that can tear through concrete-hard termite mounds in minutes. They have no teeth. The tongue does all the work: up to sixteen inches long in some species, stored in a sheath that extends back to the pelvis. When a pangolin feeds, this tongue flicks in and out of tunnels at astonishing speed, coated in sticky saliva produced by enormous salivary glands. A single pangolin can consume seventy million insects per year. Their stomachs contain small stones—gastroliths—that grind the insects into paste, since without teeth, digestion requires mechanical help.

They have almost no vision. The world of a pangolin is chemical and tactile: the scent trails of ant columns, the vibration of termites moving through galleries underground, the texture of earth giving way under claws. Their hearing is poor. They rely on smell to navigate, to find food, to identify each other. Each pangolin has scent glands near the anus that produce a secretion variously described as pungent, musky, and nauseating—a chemical signature that marks territory and communicates reproductive status. When researchers handle pangolins, the smell clings to them for days.

Chinese medicine texts from two thousand years ago describe pangolin scales as a remedy for various ailments, from poor circulation to demon possession. There is no evidence they work for anything. Keratin is keratin—eating pangolin scales is pharmacologically equivalent to eating your own fingernails. Yet demand persists, inflated by status and desperation. In Vietnam, pangolin meat is a luxury food, a display of wealth. In parts of Africa, it's bushmeat, protein in regions where protein is scarce. Between 2000 and 2019, an estimated 2.7 million pangolins were trafficked globally. All eight species are now listed as threatened with extinction.

The trafficking follows predictable routes. African pangolins are hunted with dogs, snares, and fire. Asian pangolins, rarer and more valuable, command higher prices—up to $3,000 per kilogram for scales on the black market. Poachers sell to middlemen; middlemen sell to criminal syndicates; syndicates smuggle across borders in shipping containers, luggage, and body cavities. In 2019, customs officials in Singapore seized 37.5 tons of pangolin products—representing approximately 60,000 animals—in two shipments. The pangolins had been boiled alive to remove their scales more easily.

What conservation exists struggles against the animal's biology. Pangolins don't breed well in captivity. They're stress-sensitive, prone to infection, and their dietary needs—live ants and termites, in enormous quantities—make zoos impractical. Reintroduction programs face the same problem: you can't just release a pangolin anywhere. It needs specific ant and termite species, specific soil for digging, specific conditions. And it needs to avoid humans, which it has not evolved to do. A pangolin's instinct when confronted is to freeze, curl up, become immobile. It makes capture trivial.

In 2020, scientists proposed that pangolins might be an intermediate host for the virus that causes COVID-19, suggesting that the pandemic could have originated in the wildlife trade that has decimated pangolin populations. The evidence remains disputed, the chains of transmission murky. But the hypothesis made the pangolin briefly famous, a symbol of zoonotic spillover and humanity's intrusion into animal systems. For a few months, people knew what a pangolin was. Then they forgot.

The animal continues: solitary, nocturnal, walking on its hind legs through forests and savannas, tongue longer than its body, insulated from the world by scales that protect against everything except us. It is the only mammal wholly covered in scales, an evolutionary experiment that worked for millions of years until it encountered industrial-scale greed. What the pangolin teaches is simple and unbearable: that uniqueness is not protection, that strangeness invites exploitation, that the rarest things are consumed first.
