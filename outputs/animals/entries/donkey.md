# Donkey

The donkey is an animal that has carried civilization on its back and received, in return, contempt. No creature has been more essential to human enterprise or more thoroughly mocked for its service. We made them plural through the word "ass"—a term that migrated from the animal to mean fool, then buttocks, then general-purpose insult. The donkey's reward for five thousand years of labor has been to have its name become synonymous with stupidity.

This is projection, not observation. *Equus asinus* descends from the African wild ass, domesticated in Nubia around 3000 BCE, and what humans interpreted as stubbornness is actually superior judgment. A horse, bred for speed and compliance, will run itself to death if pushed. A donkey will stop. It will plant its feet and refuse to move when exhausted, overloaded, or sensing danger. This is not stupidity—it's an intact self-preservation instinct that millennia of domestication never bred out. The donkey retained what the horse surrendered: the ability to say no.

Their intelligence manifests in memory and problem-solving. A donkey remembers routes, water sources, and individuals—both human and animal—for up to twenty-five years. They can learn to operate simple latches and gates, and they approach novel problems methodically, testing solutions rather than panicking. What looks like hesitation is assessment. A study by the Donkey Sanctuary in the UK found that donkeys could distinguish between photographs of familiar and unfamiliar donkeys, and showed preferences for images of individuals they knew, even after separation. They were, in a sense, looking at pictures of friends.

They are also intensely social, forming lifelong pair bonds with other donkeys. Separate a bonded pair and both animals will grieve—calling, refusing food, showing signs of depression that can last months. Yet we have historically kept them alone, one donkey to a farm, because we needed their labor but not their happiness. A solitary donkey is an animal in a state of permanent loss.

Physically, donkeys are built for endurance in harsh terrain. Smaller than horses, with upright manes and longer ears, they evolved for the deserts of North Africa—environments where heat tolerance and efficient water use meant survival. A donkey can lose up to thirty percent of its body weight in water and rehydrate in minutes, a feat that would put a horse into shock. Their hooves are narrower and harder than horses', shaped for rocky ground. Their thick hide resists thorns and sun. They can subsist on coarse vegetation that would starve other livestock. This is why they became the universal beast of burden: they thrive where other animals merely survive.

For most of human history, donkeys carried what needed carrying. They hauled grain in Mesopotamia, transported goods along the Silk Road, worked in mines and quarries, turned millstones, and bore soldiers' supplies. The Roman army relied on them; so did the pioneers crossing the American West. They carried water in deserts, ore in mountains, wounded soldiers in wars. In agricultural societies, a donkey often represented the difference between subsistence and starvation—the animal that could reach the distant well, bring goods to market, plow the marginal field.

Yet they appear in our stories as comic figures or symbols of foolishness. Bottom in *A Midsummer Night's Dream* wears a donkey's head. The Golden Ass is a man trapped in humiliating form. We laugh at their bray—a sound shaped by desert acoustics to carry across long distances, functioning as contact call and alarm. What sounds absurd to us is optimized communication for their environment. Even Eeyore, the most famous literary donkey, is defined by depression—as though perpetual melancholy were the natural state of donkeyhood rather than the consequence of isolation and neglect.

The only place donkeys received consistent respect was in Christian iconography, where they carried Mary to Bethlehem and Jesus into Jerusalem. The cross-shaped marking on a donkey's shoulders—the dorsal stripe intersecting with a line across the withers—became a legend: the sign that they had borne Christ. This is actually a genetic trait inherited from wild asses, but the myth contains an accidental truth. Donkeys did carry the foundations of religious civilizations. They transported the materials for temples, the goods that funded monasteries, the travelers spreading faiths across continents.

Today there are roughly fifty million donkeys worldwide, most in Asia and Africa, still performing the labor they've always done. In the developed world, they've become either pets or victims of the hide trade—killed by the millions to supply ejiao, a traditional Chinese medicine made from donkey-skin gelatin. The global donkey population has declined by half in some regions since 2000. An animal that has served humanity since the Bronze Age is being slaughtered for a product whose efficacy is unproven.

What the donkey teaches is the cost of undervaluing the essential. They have been infrastructure—biological machines that made trade routes, agriculture, and settlement possible. They are also individuals with strong emotions, long memories, and the capacity for both affection and judgment. The contempt we've shown them is the contempt civilizations show their foundations once they no longer have to think about them. We forget that someone had to carry the bricks.
