# Tiger

The tiger kills with precision. It does not chase its prey across open ground like a lion or harass it into exhaustion like wolves. Instead, it waits. A tiger can remain motionless for hours, its striped coat dissolving into tall grass and bamboo shadows, each stripe breaking the visual continuity of its body into fragments that the prey animal's brain cannot resolve into predator. When it moves, it does so in absolute silence—the pads of its feet absorb sound, its joints flex without crack or rustle. At the final moment, it explodes forward at speeds exceeding thirty-five miles per hour, closes its jaws around the throat or neck, and holds. The bite is not to tear but to suffocate or sever the spinal cord. A deer collapses. A wild boar goes still. The tiger drags the carcass—sometimes weighing more than the tiger itself—into cover and feeds for days.

There are fewer than 4,000 wild tigers left. A century ago there were perhaps 100,000. They once ranged from eastern Turkey to the Russian Far East, from the Caspian Sea to the Indonesian archipelago. Now they occupy roughly 7% of their historical range, confined to fragments: Indian national parks, Sumatran rainforests, the birch forests of Siberia where Amur tigers hunt wild boar in snow. Three subspecies have gone extinct in the last eighty years—the Caspian tiger, the Javan tiger, the Bali tiger. The South China tiger has not been seen in the wild since the 1990s. What remains is under siege: habitat loss, poaching, prey depletion, human-wildlife conflict. A tiger needs territory—a female requires at least twenty square kilometers, a male up to one hundred. As forests shrink and fragment, tigers find themselves pressed against villages, farms, roads. Sometimes they kill livestock. Sometimes they kill people. And then people kill them.

The tiger is the largest cat, heavier and longer than the lion, more solitary and more dependent on forest cover. An adult male Amur tiger can weigh 660 pounds. It is a hypercarnivore, eating almost nothing but meat—deer, wild pigs, water buffalo, occasionally bears or young elephants. It hunts alone, mostly at night, using vision adapted for low light and hearing acute enough to detect the snap of a twig from hundreds of meters away. A tiger's whiskers are embedded in tissue rich with nerves, creating a tactile map of space that allows it to navigate dense undergrowth in darkness. Its tongue is covered in sharp, backward-pointing papillae that strip meat from bone with the efficiency of a rasp.

Tigers do not roar to announce a kill or establish dominance in the manner of lions. They roar to communicate across distances in dense forest—a sound that carries for up to two miles, low-frequency enough to penetrate vegetation. They also chuff, a non-threatening greeting sound made by exhaling through the nose and mouth, used between mothers and cubs or during courtship. They spray urine mixed with scent gland secretions to mark territory, leaving chemical messages that other tigers read as clearly as text.

Humans have been obsessed with tigers for as long as there has been contact. In Hindu tradition, the goddess Durga rides a tiger, symbolizing power and protection. In Chinese culture, the tiger is one of the four sacred animals, representing courage and military prowess. Korean folklore speaks of the white tiger as a guardian spirit. During the British Raj, tiger hunting became a sport of imperial masculinity—maharajas and British officers competed to see who could kill the most. One prince claimed to have shot 1,150 tigers in his lifetime. The photographer and hunter Jim Corbett killed tigers that had turned man-eater, sometimes after they had killed dozens of people, then wrote books about it that are still read as wildlife literature. The tiger became simultaneously a symbol of wild nature's magnificence and an enemy to be dominated, a creature whose death proved human superiority.

What we have learned from tigers comes partly from their absence. The extirpation of tigers from large parts of Asia has created ecological cascades—prey populations explode without apex predators, vegetation changes, entire ecosystems shift. Reintroducing tigers, or protecting the forests where they remain, stabilizes these systems. The tiger functions as what ecologists call an umbrella species: protect enough forest for tigers and you protect habitat for thousands of other species. India's Project Tiger, launched in 1973 when tiger numbers had fallen to fewer than 1,800, has brought the population back to around 3,000 through aggressive conservation. It required relocating villages, restricting human access, funding anti-poaching patrols, and negotiating the complex politics of who gets to use the land.

A tiger perceives the world differently than we do. Its eyes have a high concentration of rod cells, maximizing sensitivity to low light at the expense of color discrimination during the day. It sees blues and greens well but struggles with reds—the forest at dusk is rendered in shades of blue-gray, movement more important than hue. A tiger does not think in terms of conservation status or symbolic meaning. It thinks, if we can call it thinking, in terms of territory, hunger, threat, opportunity. It knows the location of water sources, the trails prey animals use, the boundaries marked by the scent of neighboring tigers. It knows the feel of earth under its paws and the texture of a successful hunt.

What the tiger teaches is the cost of dominance. We have taken so much of the world for ourselves that there is barely room for a creature that needs space, silence, prey, distance from human activity. To keep tigers alive requires us to leave something alone—to not log, not build, not farm, not mine. It requires a kind of restraint that runs counter to the logic of expansion. Whether we can manage that restraint, whether we value the existence of tigers enough to let forests stand, will determine whether this animal persists or becomes another name in the long catalogue of what we eliminated because we could not share.
