# Scarab Beetle

In 1922, when Howard Carter opened Tutankhamun's tomb, he found among the gold and lapis lazuli a pectoral ornament: a winged scarab beetle holding the sun. The beetle was carved from chalcedony, its wings inlaid with carnelian and turquoise, its legs fashioned from gold. For three thousand years it had lain in darkness, this image of a beetle pushing a ball of light across the chest of a dead king. The Egyptians called it *kheper*—"he who comes into being"—and made it the hieroglyph for existence itself.

They were watching dung beetles, specifically *Scarabaeus sacer*, rolling balls of excrement across the sand. What they saw was not waste disposal but cosmology. The beetle pushed its sphere the way the sun god Khepri pushed the solar disk across the sky. Both performed the essential work of bringing tomorrow into being. The parallel wasn't fanciful—these beetles really do emerge from the earth fully formed, crawling from balls of dung buried in the ground. The Egyptians, who lacked microscopes and believed in spontaneous generation, concluded the beetles were self-created, needing no parents, achieving existence through their own effort. A reasonable inference from the evidence available. A beetle that makes itself from shit and rolls the sun.

The beetles themselves are engaged in something more practical. A male *Scarabaeus sacer* finding a fresh pile of dung will carve out a portion—mostly liquid—and roll it into a sphere about the size of an apple. He moves backward, pushing with his hind legs, navigating by the sun, moon, or when both are unavailable, the polarized light pattern of the sky. In 2013, researchers in South Africa fitted beetles with caps that blocked their view of celestial cues; the beetles lost the ability to roll in straight lines and instead circled endlessly. Remove the cap and the beetle reorients instantly. They are among the few insects known to navigate by the Milky Way—they need it to move their cargo away from competitors. Other beetles will fight for a finished ball rather than make their own.

The female follows a male with a promising sphere. If his ball is adequate, she'll mate with him and help roll it to a suitable burial site. Together they dig a chamber, install the dung ball, and separate—he returns to the surface to die or start again; she stays below. She lays a single egg in the dung ball, shaping it into a pear, and provisions it with a few more loads of excrement before sealing the chamber. The larva that hatches will spend its entire youth eating its way through this single meal, pupating in the empty shell, then digging upward to emerge as an adult beetle, black and hard-shelled, ready to find dung of its own.

This is what the Egyptians meant by self-creation. The beetle appears from nowhere, needing no visible parent, making itself. It was, in their cosmology, doing what the universe does: bringing form from formlessness, being from non-being. They carved scarabs by the millions—as amulets, seals, jewelry, funerary objects. The underside was inscribed with names, prayers, royal cartouches. To own a scarab was to carry a piece of cosmic machinery, a reminder that existence renews itself. The heart scarab, placed on a mummy's chest, was inscribed with Spell 30B from the Book of the Dead: "O my heart, do not stand up as a witness against me." The dead needed the beetle to roll them into the next life.

What scarabs actually eat is liquid nutrients from herbivore dung—mostly from cows, buffalo, elephants. They prefer fresh dung, arriving within minutes of deposition. Researchers in Texas found that dung beetles can reduce the mass of a cowpat by ninety percent in forty-eight hours. In Australia, where native dung beetles evolved for marsupial droppings, the introduction of millions of cattle in the 18th century created a dung crisis. Cowpats accumulated, breeding flies, fouling pasture. In the 1960s, Australia imported 23 species of dung beetle from Africa and southern Europe—Operation Scarab. The beetles established populations and now process an estimated 2.7 billion cattle pats annually, preventing an ecological catastrophe measured in shit.

The beetle's strength is disproportionate. A scarab can roll a ball ten times its own weight and pull loads up to 1,141 times its mass—the equivalent of a human dragging six double-decker buses. This strength evolved not for transport but for competition. Males fight for dung, females fight for males, and everyone fights for finished balls. The beetles have horns—in some species, elaborate pronged structures—used to flip rivals away from resources. Strength is reproductive fitness.

But the Egyptians weren't wrong about transformation. The beetle does change state—from egg to larva to pupa to adult—and it does so inside a sphere it will consume, hollowing itself out to make room for wings. It emerges from burial having eaten its entire world. The metaphor writes itself, which is why it lasted three millennia. Kings wanted scarabs on their chests because beetles know how to cross from one state to another, how to remake themselves in darkness, how to push the sun.

Modern entomology confirms the scarab's role as ecosystem engineer—aerating soil, cycling nutrients, reducing methane emissions from livestock waste, dispersing seeds. A 2012 study calculated the global economic value of dung beetles at approximately $380 million annually in ecosystem services. But the numbers don't explain the obsession. The scarab became sacred not because it's useful but because it enacts a story: burial, consumption, emergence, flight. The beetle treats shit as raw material for becoming. It finds the lowest substance and rolls it toward the sun. You can make a religion from less.
