# Frog

The frog begins as something else. It hatches from an egg as a tadpole—gilled, tailed, herbivorous, essentially a fish. Then it drowns its former self. The gills are absorbed into the body. Legs erupt from the sides. The tail shrinks and vanishes. Lungs develop where there were none. The jaw reshapes. The digestive system reorganizes from a long herbivore's gut to a short carnivore's tract. The eyes migrate forward and bulge outward. In a matter of weeks, the creature that swam through pond water eating algae becomes something that breathes air, hunts insects, and can leap twenty times its body length.

This is not growth. This is demolition and reconstruction.

Human cultures have noticed. The ancient Egyptians saw frogs emerging en masse after the Nile's floods and made Heqet, the frog-headed goddess, a symbol of birth and resurrection. Medieval alchemists used frogs in their diagrams of transformation—the opus from base matter to gold paralleled the tadpole's metamorphosis. In fairy tales, frogs are princes waiting to be restored, creatures trapped between states. But the real transformation is stranger than any spell. When a tadpole metamorphoses, it literally digests parts of itself. Cells that formed the tail commit apoptosis—programmed death—and are broken down to fuel the construction of legs. The creature eats its own childhood.

There are over 7,000 species of frogs, inhabiting every continent except Antarctica. They range from the thumbnail-sized *Paedophryne amauensis* of Papua New Guinea—at 7.7 millimeters, the world's smallest vertebrate—to the goliath frog of Cameroon, which can weigh seven pounds and span a foot. Despite this diversity, most share the basic architecture: permeable skin, protruding eyes, powerful hind legs. That skin is key. Frogs don't drink water—they absorb it through their skin, along with oxygen. This makes them exquisitely sensitive to their environment. A frog's skin is a membrane between interior and exterior that never fully closes.

This permeability is why frogs are dying. Since the 1970s, amphibian populations have collapsed worldwide. Over 200 species have gone extinct; another 2,000 are threatened. The causes are multiple—habitat destruction, climate change, pollution—but the most devastating is a fungus: *Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis*, or Bd. This chytrid fungus colonizes amphibian skin, disrupting its crucial functions. Infected frogs can't regulate water and electrolytes. They die of cardiac arrest. Bd has spread across six continents, likely via the pet trade and scientific specimen transport. In Panama's cloud forests, researchers have documented the wave of silence that follows Bd's arrival—streams that once rang with frog calls going quiet in months.

But frogs keep teaching us. In the 1950s, South African researchers discovered that injecting the urine of pregnant women into female African clawed frogs (*Xenopus laevis*) would cause the frogs to lay eggs within hours. The human chorionic gonadotropin hormone in the urine triggered ovulation. For decades, Xenopus was the living pregnancy test, shipped globally to hospitals and labs. The species is now established as an invasive in multiple countries—escaped lab frogs breeding in the wild, a testament to their adaptability and to the unintended consequences of our use of them.

Frog skin has yielded other treasures. The mucus that coats it contains antimicrobial peptides that protect against bacteria and fungi—chemical defenses produced by the skin itself. Researchers studying poison dart frogs found that their toxins, among the most potent known, come not from the frogs themselves but from their diet of mites and ants. In captivity, fed crickets instead, the frogs become harmless. The poison is borrowed, concentrated, advertised in brilliant colors: a chemical defense system assembled from the environment.

The frog's eyes see mostly movement. A frog can starve to death surrounded by motionless flies. Its visual system is tuned to detect changes—the flutter of wings, the crawl of prey. Each eye has a nearly 180-degree field of view, and together they provide almost complete spherical vision. The frog processes visual information differently than mammals; its retina does more computation before sending signals to the brain. In the 1950s and 60s, Jerome Lettvin and his colleagues at MIT studied the frog's visual system, mapping how different neurons responded to different types of motion. Their paper, "What the Frog's Eye Tells the Frog's Brain," became foundational to neuroscience. The frog taught us that perception is not passive reception but active construction—the eye doesn't simply record what's there but interprets, filters, transforms.

A frog's tongue is attached at the front of its mouth, not the back. When it strikes, the tongue flips forward and the tip makes contact first. The tissue is one of the softest biological materials known, ten times softer than human tongue. This allows it to deform around prey on impact. Recent high-speed photography by Alexis Noel and David Hu at Georgia Tech revealed that the tongue's saliva changes viscosity—it's thick and sticky during capture, then becomes thin and watery during retraction, allowing the frog to easily pull the prey into its mouth. The tongue operates faster than the human eye can track: some frogs extend and retract it in 15 milliseconds.

Frogs sing, though we hear it as croaking. The males call to attract mates, and each species has its own repertoire. The coquí frog of Puerto Rico gives the two-note call that provides its name: co-KEE. The first note warns other males away; the second attracts females. Some frogs have vocal sacs that inflate to amplify sound; the túngara frog's sac can triple its body size. Others, lacking vocal structures, have evolved alternatives. The Bornean flat-headed frog uses ultrasound—frequencies above human hearing—to communicate in noisy streams. We only discovered this in 2008, when researchers realized the frogs' mouths were moving in apparent silence and brought recording equipment that could capture higher frequencies.

To hold a frog is to feel that permeable skin, cool and damp, pulsing slightly with breath drawn through nostrils and pushed into lungs by swallowing. It's to hold a creature that has died and been reborn, that breathes through its skin, that sees in motion, that sings frequencies we cannot hear. The frog is a boundary creature—between water and land, between what it was and what it becomes. It lives in the margins, the places where worlds meet. And it is vanishing, not in evolutionary time but in human time, in decades, in the span of a single human lifetime. The frog that taught us about transformation is being transformed by us, its permeable skin absorbing what we've put into the world.
