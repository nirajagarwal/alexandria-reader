# Hippopotamus

The hippopotamus spends its days almost entirely underwater, eyes and nostrils breaking the surface like periscopes, and emerges at night to graze. This is the opposite of what you'd expect from an animal that looks like a living boulder. Four tons of mammal, barrel-bodied and short-legged, with a mouth that opens to 150 degrees and canine teeth that grow continuously throughout its life, reaching eighteen inches in males. The hippo seems built for land and violence, but it has chosen water and, for most of the day, stillness.

They are not related to pigs, despite the name—*Hippopotamus amphibius*, river horse—and despite the appearance. Genetic studies in the 1990s revealed that their closest living relatives are whales. Sixty million years ago, a common ancestor gave rise to both lineages. Whales went entirely aquatic, lost their legs, gained blowholes and echolocation. Hippos kept their legs but remained tethered to water, unable to swim in the traditional sense. They walk along river bottoms, their specific gravity allowing them to sink and stride through the murk like astronauts on a heavy planet. They can hold their breath for five minutes. Their eyes, ears, and nostrils sit atop their heads in a configuration that allows them to submerge almost entirely while still sensing the world above. Infant hippos nurse underwater, closing their ears and nostrils with muscular valves.

Ancient Egypt worshipped and feared them. The goddess Taweret, protector of childbirth, had the head and body of a pregnant hippo standing upright, because what better symbol for fierce maternal protection than the animal that kills more humans in Africa than any other large mammal? Hippos are territorial, aggressive, and fast—they can run at nineteen miles per hour despite weighing as much as a small car. Males fight with those continuously growing canines, battles that leave deep scars across heads and flanks. The wounds rarely become infected because hippo skin secretes a reddish, oily substance that acts as both sunscreen and antibiotic. For years, observers thought hippos sweated blood.

They are grazers, not predators, but they kill to defend territory and young. In 2014, a hippo in Niger attacked a boat, killing thirteen people. These incidents are not anomalies. Anyone who has worked near African rivers knows that hippos are more dangerous than crocodiles, which at least have the decency to look lethal. The hippo looks ridiculous until it's charging, and then it's too late to recalibrate your assumptions about herbivores and danger.

At night they leave the water and walk established paths to grazing grounds, sometimes traveling several miles to feed. They eat around eighty pounds of grass per night, cropping it short with their lips, not their teeth. Those massive jaws are for fighting, not feeding. The grazing hippo is almost quiet, a shadow moving through darkness, following trails worn smooth by generations of nightly walks. They defecate while simultaneously spinning their tails like propellers, spraying dung to mark territory. David Attenborough once called this "muck-spreading," which is accurate but fails to capture the baroque strangeness of watching a four-ton animal helicopter-spray its own waste across the landscape.

Their presence transforms ecosystems. Hippo dung feeds fish populations—in some African rivers, hippo excrement provides the nutrient base for entire food webs. When hippos were nearly eliminated from Queen Elizabeth National Park in Uganda during the 1960s and 70s, fish populations crashed. The river became clearer but less productive, a sterile clarity that supported less life. Reintroduction efforts in the 1990s restored the cycle: hippos eat on land, defecate in water, fertilize rivers. They are what ecologists call ecosystem engineers, though that term makes their role sound more intentional than it is. They're just living, and rivers have adapted to their living.

Colombia has a population of hippos descended from four animals Pablo Escobar imported to his private zoo in the 1980s. After his death in 1993, the hippos were left to their own devices. They thrived. The population now numbers over 130, making it the largest hippo population outside Africa. They have no natural predators in South America. Some ecologists argue they're filling a niche left empty by extinct megafauna—giant ground sloths, toxodontids—and may actually benefit local ecosystems. Others point to their territorial aggression and the impossibility of controlling a population that doubles every seven years. Colombia is attempting sterilization programs, but sterilizing a hippo is complicated surgery on a dangerous animal. The debate continues while the hippos breed.

They can live for fifty years in the wild. Donna, a hippo at the Mesker Park Zoo in Indiana, died in 2012 at age sixty-one. For decades, she was one of the oldest hippos in human care, which means humans have been keeping hippos in captivity for at least that long, studying their behavior, breeding them, learning that they can be trained to open their mouths for dental inspections but remain fundamentally undomesticated. You cannot make a hippo safe through familiarity. You can only make humans more careful.

The earliest known hippopotamus fossils date to sixteen million years ago. They once ranged across Europe and Asia. Cave paintings in France show hippos. Climate change and human hunting eliminated them from everywhere except sub-Saharan Africa. Now even there, populations are threatened—somewhere between 125,000 and 150,000 remain. Their ivory, those massive canines, is softer than elephant ivory but easier to carve, and therefore valuable. They are killed for meat, for teeth, for the crime of being in rivers that humans also want to use.

What the hippo teaches is that amphibiousness—existing between worlds—requires specific adaptations and exacts specific costs. They are neither fully terrestrial nor fully aquatic, too heavy to swim, too dependent on water to abandon it. They have made a strange life work for millions of years, which is more than most lineages can claim. But their flexibility has limits. The water they need is disappearing, degraded, contested. Being between worlds means you have twice as many places to lose.
