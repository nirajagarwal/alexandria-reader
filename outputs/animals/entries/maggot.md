# Maggot

The larva of the common blowfly can reduce a fifty-kilogram human corpse to bone in three weeks. It does this by liquefying tissue—secreting enzymes that break down proteins into a digestible soup—then absorbing the slurry through its mouth hooks. A single female fly lays up to three hundred eggs in a wound or orifice. Within hours they hatch. Within days the wound is a seething mass. Within weeks the flesh is gone.

This is not metaphor. This is function.

We call them maggots as if naming them something other than "larvae" makes them more bearable, or more contemptible. The word comes from Middle English *magot*, probably from Old Norse *maðkr*, meaning grub or worm. It's a word that sounds like disgust, and for most of human history disgust has been the appropriate response. Maggots meant rot. They meant death poorly handled, poverty, the battlefield, plague. They appeared where civilization failed—in untended wounds, in meat gone bad, in bodies left too long. Spontaneous generation, the idea that they emerged from decay itself rather than from eggs, persisted until Francesco Redi's experiments in 1668. People believed maggots were what putrefaction *became*, not what consumed it.

But a maggot is simply a feeding machine. It has no eyes, no legs, no recognizable face. It is a tube that processes decay with extraordinary efficiency. The common blowfly larva—*Lucilia sericata*, green and metallic as an adult—passes through three instars, molting between each stage, growing from translucent thread to thumbnail-sized grub in four to ten days depending on temperature. It breathes through posterior spiracles, crawls using rows of hooks, and possesses a kind of intelligence about its environment: maggots cluster together, generating heat through their collective feeding, which speeds decomposition and their own development. They're social in the way that efficiency is social.

Forensic entomologists can determine time of death by examining the species and developmental stage of maggots in a corpse. Blowflies arrive within minutes of death if conditions allow. Flesh flies come later. Beetles arrive last, after the wet work is done. The succession is predictable enough to serve as evidence in court. A second-instar *Lucilia* larva at a certain temperature means death occurred approximately forty-eight hours prior. This is knowledge purchased from thousands of bodies—murder victims, mostly, but also experiments with pig carcasses left to rot in cages, meticulously photographed and measured. Maggots testify. They are more honest than human witnesses.

The disgust reflex exists for good reason: maggots indicate contamination, disease, the presence of death. But they also perform a service that nothing else can match. In the fifteenth century, military surgeons noticed that soldiers with maggot-infested wounds healed better than those without. During the Napoleonic Wars, during the American Civil War, during the First World War, doctors observed the same thing but lacked the means to harness it systematically. Maggots ate only dead tissue, left living tissue intact, and somehow prevented infection. In 1928, William Baer, an orthopedic surgeon at Johns Hopkins, formalized what battlefield medicine had long known: sterile maggots, deliberately introduced to a wound, could clean it better than any scalpel.

Maggot debridement therapy fell out of favor with the advent of antibiotics, but antibiotics have begun to fail. Resistant infections now kill more people annually than HIV/AIDS. Maggots have returned, FDA-approved, shipped in sterile vials to hospitals where conventional treatment fails. A diabetic ulcer colonized by MRSA can be cleared in days. The maggots are applied in a "biobag"—a mesh sachet that contains them but allows their secretions through. Patients report a tickling sensation. The maggots dissolve the necrotic tissue, disinfect the wound with their antimicrobial secretions, and stimulate healing. Then they are removed. They have done exactly what they were designed by evolution to do: consume the dead.

This is not redemption. The maggot is not noble. It simply is. It processes decay because decay is its niche, the narrow slot in existence where it can extract energy and reproduce. A maggot doesn't care if the flesh is human or pig or deer. It doesn't distinguish between a corpse left in a ditch and a wound on a living leg. It consumes what is dead. That we find this useful, or horrifying, or both, says more about us than it does about the maggot.

They have taught us that decomposition is not an end but a process, a form of ecology. They have taught us that disgust can obscure function. And they have taught us—though we resist learning it—that our bodies are not different in kind from any other meat. A corpse is a resource. Maggots know this. They arrive with the efficiency of specialists, transform death into their own life, and move on. When we are gone, they will remain, doing what they have always done: eating the world's dead things, making room for what comes next.
