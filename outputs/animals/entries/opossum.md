# Opossum

The Virginia opossum, *Didelphis virginiana*, is North America's only marsupial—a biological oddity that wandered up from South America roughly three million years ago and decided the arrangement suited it. While its Australian cousins evolved into koalas and kangaroos, the opossum stayed small, scrappy, and profoundly unchanged. It is, in essence, a Cretaceous mammal still operating on Cretaceous software, which makes it either a spectacular failure of evolution or its greatest success story.

The animal has fifty teeth, more than any other North American land mammal. Its tail is prehensile, capable of gripping branches but too weak to support the animal's full weight for more than a few seconds, despite what cartoons suggest. It has opposable thumbs on its hind feet. Its body temperature runs cold for a mammal—around 94°F—which makes it a poor host for rabies. The virus, which thrives at higher temperatures, rarely gains purchase in opossum blood. Between 1990 and 2021, wildlife rehabilitation centers across the United States reported only three confirmed rabies cases in opossums out of thousands tested. You are more likely to contract rabies from a philosophical discussion about rabies than from an opossum bite.

The "playing possum" behavior is not a performance but a physiological response to extreme fear. The animal doesn't choose to collapse; its nervous system does. The opossum enters a catatonic state that can last from forty minutes to four hours. Its body goes rigid, its breathing slows to near-imperceptibility, its eyes glaze, its tongue lolls, and its anal glands release a foul-smelling green fluid that mimics the scent of decay. The animal is, during this time, genuinely unconscious—a state researchers call "tonic immobility." It's an involuntary shutdown, a biological surrender that happens to work as theater. Predators, confronted with what appears to be a rotting corpse, often lose interest. When the opossum wakes, it has no memory of the performance. It simply gets up and walks away.

Before this collapse, the opossum will try other strategies. It hisses, bares its fifty teeth, drools copiously, and may emit a series of guttural clicks. It will sway its head, fixing you with a stare that reads as either menacing or deeply confused. Usually it's both. The threat display is entirely bluff. The opossum has poor vision, limited speed, and no real weapons beyond those theatrical teeth. If cornered and unable to flee, it faints. This has made the animal a symbol of cowardice in American folklore, which fundamentally misunderstands both the animal and the nature of survival.

The opossum is a walking garbage disposal with a nearly omnivorous diet. Carrion, insects, snails, rodents, birds, eggs, frogs, fruits, nuts, grass, leaves—if it's organic and fits in its mouth, the opossum will eat it. This includes ticks. A single opossum can consume up to 5,000 ticks in a season, hoovering them off the ground and its own fur as it shuffles through underbrush. This made the opossum a darling of public health messaging in the 2010s, when Lyme disease rates were spiking. "Possums eat ticks!" became a conservation rallying cry, though subsequent research suggested the number might be lower—perhaps closer to 1,000 ticks per season. Still respectable. Still helpful. The opossum, unloved and frequently roadkilled, was suddenly reframed as an ally in the war against tick-borne illness.

They are also prodigiously fecund. A female opossum can give birth twice a year, producing up to twenty offspring per litter. The babies—called joeys, like their Australian relatives—are born after only twelve to thirteen days of gestation, each one about the size of a honeybee. Blind, hairless, and essentially embryonic, they crawl from the birth canal to the mother's pouch, a journey of roughly three inches that takes about five minutes and kills half of them. Those that survive latch onto one of the mother's thirteen nipples. If there are more joeys than nipples, the extras die. The ones that feed will stay in the pouch for two months, then ride on the mother's back for another month, clinging to her fur as she forages. By three months old, they're independent. By eight months, they're sexually mature. By two years, most are dead.

The average lifespan of a wild opossum is one to two years. In captivity, they can live four. This brief, frantic existence is a different strategy than the one pursued by longer-lived mammals—less investment per offspring, more offspring overall, faster turnover. The opossum is playing a numbers game, flooding the ecosystem with genetic experiments and letting attrition sort out the winners. It works. The species' range has expanded steadily northward over the past century, adapting to colder climates despite lacking the fur density of native mammals. They suffer frostbite on their naked ears and tails but keep going.

What the opossum teaches is this: you don't have to be fast, fierce, or particularly intelligent to persist. You can be slow, strange, and equipped with an ancient body plan that evolution forgot to update. You can faint when frightened, eat garbage, and die young, and still your species endures. The opossum survives not by dominating its environment but by fitting into the cracks other animals ignore, eating what others won't, and reproducing faster than predators can eliminate you. It's an inglorious strategy. It's also 70 million years old and counting.
