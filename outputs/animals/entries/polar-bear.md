# Polar Bear

The polar bear is a carnivore that evolved to hunt on ice, and the ice is disappearing. This fact—stark, quantifiable, accelerating—overshadows almost everything else we might say about *Ursus maritimus*. But before the bear became a symbol of climate catastrophe, before it appeared on documentary footage stranded on shrinking floes, it was already something remarkable: a land mammal that became marine, that turned the Arctic Ocean's frozen surface into hunting ground, that grew large enough to kill animals four times its size.

Adult males weigh between 900 and 1,600 pounds, occasionally more. Females are smaller—300 to 700 pounds—but still massive by the standards of most terrestrial predators. This size is maintained almost entirely on seal fat. Polar bears are hypercarnivorous, meaning more than 70 percent of their diet consists of meat, specifically the blubber of ringed and bearded seals. A single seal can provide a bear with enough calories to sustain it for a week. The bear hunts by waiting at breathing holes in the ice, sometimes for hours, motionless, until a seal surfaces. Then it strikes: a blow with the paw, teeth to the skull, immediate death if the bear is experienced. The bear eats the skin and fat first, often leaving the protein-rich meat for scavengers. In a landscape where calories are scarce and metabolic demands extreme, fat is the only currency that matters.

Their scientific name means "sea bear," and it's apt. Polar bears are classified as marine mammals—the only bear with this designation—because their survival depends on the sea ice that forms over the Arctic Ocean. They are strong swimmers, capable of covering 60 miles or more in open water, paddling with their massive forepaws while their hind legs trail as rudders. Their fur is water-repellent; their skin is black to absorb heat; beneath the skin lies four inches of blubber. Biologist Ian Stirling, who has studied polar bears for five decades, estimates they spend more than half their lives on sea ice, hunting, traveling, sometimes even mating. They are ice obligates. Without it, they cannot access seals efficiently. Without seals, they starve.

The Inuit have more than a dozen words for different types of sea ice, and they have lived alongside polar bears for millennia. The relationship has been one of both respect and predation. Polar bears were hunted for fur, meat, and fat; they also hunted humans, occasionally, when opportunity or hunger aligned. In Inuit cosmology, the bear possessed a soul that required ceremonial respect after death. Killing a bear was significant—spiritually, practically. The skin alone could mean survival through an Arctic winter. But the bear was also *nanuq*, a creature of power and patience, something not entirely separate from the human hunter but parallel to it.

Europeans who encountered polar bears in the 16th and 17th centuries were less impressed by kinship than by threat. Early accounts describe bears attacking explorers, boarding ships, besieging camps. The bears were curious, opportunistic, unafraid. Willem Barents, the Dutch navigator, watched a bear killed by his crew in 1596 and noted it weighed over 1,000 pounds, that its stomach contained only seaweed and undigested seal remains. Later expeditions brought bears back to Europe as attractions—caged, displayed, fought against dogs for entertainment. The bear became spectacle, exotic proof of the North's hostility.

What we have learned about polar bears since then has mostly complicated the narrative of apex predation. They are solitary except during mating season and maternal rearing. Females den in snowdrifts to give birth, usually to twins, in the middle of winter. The cubs emerge weighing less than two pounds, blind and helpless. The mother does not eat for months, sustaining herself and her cubs entirely on stored fat. When they emerge in spring, she has lost more than half her body weight. Her survival depends on finding seals quickly. Cubs stay with their mothers for two and a half years, learning to hunt—an education that involves long, patient watching, repeated failure, gradual refinement of technique. Polar bears are intelligent, capable of problem-solving and tool use in captivity, though these behaviors are rarely observed in the wild. Intelligence, for a polar bear, means reading ice, predicting seal behavior, conserving energy in an environment where waste is fatal.

The relationship between polar bears and climate change is not metaphorical. Sea ice in the Arctic has declined by roughly 13 percent per decade since 1979, according to NASA satellite data. The ice now forms later in autumn and melts earlier in spring, shortening the hunting season. Bears compensate—or try to. Some populations have shifted to scavenging: whale carcasses, seabird colonies, garbage dumps near human settlements. These foods are less calorie-dense than seal blubber. Studies in Hudson Bay show that when the ice-free season extends beyond four months, adult females lose enough weight to compromise reproduction. Cubs die. Populations decline. Nineteen subpopulations of polar bears exist worldwide, totaling perhaps 26,000 individuals. Several are already decreasing. Projections suggest that by mid-century, many of these populations will no longer be viable.

This is what makes the polar bear both symbol and subject. It is large, white, photogenic—easy to care about in the abstract. But it is also an ice obligate predator in an era when ice is becoming optional. It has taught us, unwillingly, what it means to be exquisitely adapted to a habitat that is ceasing to exist. The bear evolved over hundreds of thousands of years to exploit a niche that will, within a century or two, largely disappear. It did not choose this. It cannot adapt quickly enough. It is simply living the life it evolved to live while the ground—or rather, the ice—vanishes beneath it.
