# Electric Eel

The electric eel is not an eel. It is a knifefish, more closely related to catfish than to true eels, and it breathes air. Every ten minutes or so, it rises to the surface of the muddy South American rivers where it lives and gulps. Without access to air, it drowns. This fact alone should recalibrate our sense of what we're dealing with: a fish that must breathe like we do, living in water but tethered to the surface as surely as any whale.

But what makes *Electrophorus electricus* genuinely alien is not its breathing apparatus. It is the fact that it has converted roughly eighty percent of its body into a living battery. Most fish have muscles arranged for swimming. The electric eel has muscles arranged for generating voltage. Three specialized organs—the main organ, Hunter's organ, and Sachs' organ—run the length of its body, stacked electrocytes numbering in the thousands. Each cell produces only about 0.15 volts. Together, they can discharge up to 860 volts at one ampere. This is enough to stun a horse, stop a human heart, or light up a string of fifty LED bulbs, which researchers have demonstrated by wiring eels to Christmas lights for public television.

The eel uses this power with precision. Low-voltage pulses—ten volts or less—function as a kind of electric vision. The waters of the Amazon basin are dark with sediment. Eyes are of limited use. The eel navigates by generating an electric field around itself and sensing distortions in that field caused by nearby objects. It can detect a small fish at a distance of several feet, distinguish between a rock and a root, locate prey buried in mud. This is not echolocation but something stranger: the eel becomes its own radar system, living inside a three-dimensional map it constantly generates.

When it hunts, it shifts modes. Kenneth Catania at Vanderbilt University has spent years studying electric eel predation, often while standing in rubber waders thick enough to prevent his own electrocution. He has documented a two-stage attack. First, the eel releases a volley of high-voltage pulses—two or three millisecond bursts repeated hundreds of times per second. These pulses cause involuntary muscle contractions in nearby fish. The prey twitches, revealing its location. Then the eel strikes, clamping down with its jaw while delivering a sustained shock that immobilizes the target. The whole sequence takes less than a second.

More remarkably, Catania discovered that electric eels will leap from the water to attack threats they perceive at the surface. They curl their body so that both the positive and negative poles of their electric organ contact the target simultaneously, maximizing current flow. In his experiments, eels leapt repeatedly to shock a fake alligator head equipped with LED lights and conductive materials. Each leap delivered a shock; each shock was accompanied by a visible brightening of the LEDs. The eel was not thrashing randomly. It was climbing its own electric field, using its body as both weapon and wire.

Humans have known about electric eels since at least the 1600s, when Spanish and Portuguese colonizers in South America reported fish that could deliver shocks through spears and nets. Indigenous peoples of the Amazon had known far longer and had learned to avoid them, or to catch them with wooden implements, or to wait until the eel had exhausted itself discharging before attempting retrieval. In the eighteenth century, European naturalists began systematic study. Alessandro Volta, who invented the first true battery in 1800, explicitly modeled his design on the anatomy of electric fish, including the eel and the torpedo ray. He stacked discs of zinc and copper separated by brine-soaked cloth, mimicking the stacked electrocytes he had read about in anatomical descriptions. The electric eel taught us how to store and deliver electrical energy before we had any theory of electricity.

For most of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, the electric eel was studied primarily as a biological curiosity, a living demonstration of bioelectricity. But recent research has revealed depths we hadn't suspected. In 2019, a team led by C. David de Santana published evidence that what we thought was one species is actually three: *Electrophorus electricus*, *E. voltai*, and *E. varii*. The last of these, found in the Brazilian highlands, produces discharges of up to 860 volts—the strongest bioelectricity ever recorded. We had been grouping together distinct species for over two hundred fifty years, unable to tell them apart by sight.

The eel lives up to fifteen years in the wild, growing to lengths of six feet or more. It is solitary, somewhat sluggish, a poor swimmer because so much of its body is given over to organs unrelated to propulsion. It hunts primarily at night. During the day, it rests in muddy shallows, breathing at the surface, its electric field a faint hum around it. Fishermen know to avoid the water where eels are present. There are documented cases of drowning after electric shock, of horses knocked unconscious while crossing rivers, of entire nets of fish stunned simultaneously when an eel feels threatened.

What the electric eel teaches is that evolution will sacrifice almost anything for an advantage. Vision, mobility, muscle mass—all subordinated to the demands of the electric organ. The result is a creature that cannot swim well, cannot see clearly, and must surface regularly to breathe, but which commands its environment through a sense we do not possess and a weapon we struggle to survive. It is a fish that taught us to build batteries. It is a predator that hunts with manufactured lightning. It is proof that even in the mud and darkness, under ten feet of turbid water, life will find a way to shock the world into paying attention.
