# Vulture

A vulture's stomach is a vat of hydrochloric acid strong enough to dissolve metal, with a pH that hovers near zero—comparable to battery acid. This is what it takes to safely consume carrion days dead, bloated with botulism and anthrax, crawling with pathogens that would kill most other creatures within hours. The vulture eats death itself and transforms it into flight.

There are twenty-three species of vulture, split between the Old World birds of Africa, Asia, and Europe—which are true raptors, related to eagles and hawks—and the New World vultures of the Americas, which evolved from an entirely different lineage, possibly storks. This is convergent evolution at its most stark: two unrelated groups arrived at nearly identical solutions to the same ecological problem. Both developed bald heads (feathers would mat with gore), broad wings for soaring (efficient for covering vast territories in search of the dead), and those catastrophically acidic guts. Natural selection drew the same blueprint twice.

They are obligate scavengers, with one notable exception. Every vulture species feeds exclusively on carrion except the palm-nut vulture of Africa, which turned herbivore and eats mostly fruit. But the rest are nature's sanitation workers, and they are spectacularly good at their job. A wake of vultures—the collective noun, appropriately funereal—can strip a Thomson's gazelle to bones in twenty minutes. In doing so, they prevent the spread of disease that would otherwise propagate through soil and water, through scavenger mammals, through the entire ecosystem. Where vulture populations have collapsed, rabies and anthrax have surged. In India, the near-extinction of vultures in the 1990s led to a boom in feral dog populations feeding on rotting cattle carcasses, which led to tens of thousands of additional human deaths from rabies. The vultures had been performing a public health service worth an estimated $34 billion annually, and no one noticed until they were gone.

They were killed, mostly, by diclofenac—a common anti-inflammatory drug given to livestock. A cow dies, vultures feed, and the drug causes kidney failure in the birds within days. Three species of Indian vulture declined by more than 99 percent in less than a decade, one of the steepest population crashes ever recorded. By the time the cause was identified and the drug banned for veterinary use in 2006, millions of vultures were dead.

But even before diclofenac, vultures have been despised and misunderstood for millennia. In English, "vulture" is synonymous with predation, greed, opportunism—despite the fact that vultures do not kill. They wait. A "vulture capitalist" picks at the corpse of a failed business; a friend who "vultures" your fries swoops in on what you've abandoned. The metaphor is backwards: vultures do not create death; they arrive after, performing the inglorious work of cleaning up what others have left behind.

Ancient Egyptians saw them differently. The vulture goddess Nekhbet was a protector of pharaohs, her image worn on the royal crown. Vultures were believed to be exclusively female—a notion that persisted into medieval Europe—because no one had witnessed vulture courtship or mating. They seemed to reproduce by spontaneous generation, which made them symbols of virgin birth, purity despite their diet of rot. Early Christians associated vultures with the Virgin Mary for this reason. Purity and putrefaction, intimately linked.

Tibetan sky burials make explicit what vultures do everywhere implicitly: return the dead to the world. A human corpse is carried to a mountaintop, dismembered by a rogyapa—a body breaker—and offered to the vultures, who consume everything, bones and all. There is no waste, no grave. The body feeds the birds; the birds fly; the elements disperse. It is a kind of resurrection without metaphysics, an ecological afterlife. And it is becoming difficult to perform because Tibetan vultures, like those in India, are declining. Diclofenac again, and also habitat loss, and the slow creep of modernity into the mountains.

A turkey vulture—common across the Americas—can detect a single molecule of ethyl mercaptan, the gas released by decomposing flesh, from over a mile away. They fly low, zigzagging, following scent corridors invisible to us. Pipeline companies have used this: they add ethyl mercaptan to natural gas so leaks smell like rot, then watch where vultures circle. The birds are used as gas leak detectors, their noses repurposed for human infrastructure.

Vultures are also among the highest-flying birds on record. A Rüppell's griffon vulture collided with an aircraft over Côte d'Ivoire at 37,000 feet—seven miles above the earth, where the air is so thin that most creatures would lose consciousness in seconds. The bird died, but it had been flying at altitudes where only jets and condors dare. Why so high? Possibly to scan larger territories; possibly because updrafts at altitude allow them to soar for hours without a single wingbeat. They are endurance flyers, designed for efficiency over spectacle. A griffon vulture was tracked flying 350 miles in a single day without landing. Another flew 11 hours straight. They burn energy the way sloths do: barely.

Up close, they are unsettling. The hooked beak, the bare head slick with blood and gastric juices, the cold evaluative stare. But they are also fastidious. After feeding, vultures will find water and bathe, preening meticulously. Some species urinate on their own legs—the evaporating liquid cools them, and the ammonia kills bacteria picked up from carcasses. They are not filthy; they are clinical, built to interface with decay without succumbing to it.

We need them more than we know. Every ecosystem with large animals produces corpses, and something must deal with the dead. In the vultures' absence, bacteria and flies take over, spreading slower and less completely, leaving rot to fester. Vultures short-circuit that spread. They are a firebreak against plague. And they ask nothing in return but what we're already leaving behind.
