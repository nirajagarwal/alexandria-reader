# Lamb

The lamb arrives in spring, when fields green and air warms and the earth tilts back toward abundance. This timing is not accident but breeding—sheep evolved to give birth when grass returns, when milk flows easiest, when survival asks least of mother and young. Humans, noticing this, made it doctrine: the lamb became the animal of renewal, of sacrifice, of blood on doorposts and bodies on altars. We eat them at Easter and Passover not despite their youth but because of it—the lamb is the creature we kill when it's most innocent, most tender, most itself.

A lamb is a sheep less than one year old, though the word typically means much younger—the weeks-old animal still living on milk, still learning what grass is. Sheep (*Ovis aries*) are ruminants, born with four-chambered stomachs designed to extract nutrition from cellulose that would starve most mammals. The lamb enters the world with this machinery installed but not yet operational. For the first weeks it nurses, gaining weight rapidly—a healthy lamb may double its birth weight in ten days. Then the rumen awakens, microbes colonize the fermentation chambers, and the animal begins its real work: turning grass into flesh.

They are born knowing almost nothing but learn with startling speed. Within hours, a lamb can stand, walk, recognize its mother by voice and smell. Ewes and lambs establish bonds through a process called "imprinting" that occurs in the first few hours after birth—the ewe licks the lamb, memorizing its scent; the lamb learns the ewe's bleat among dozens or hundreds of others. Research by Keith Kendrick at the Babraham Institute showed that sheep can remember individual faces—both ovine and human—for years, and can distinguish between expressions. A lamb separated from its mother after those critical early hours may never bond; one left with her for even a day will remember her call months later, long after weaning.

Humans domesticated sheep somewhere between 11,000 and 13,000 years ago, probably in Mesopotamia, probably from the Asiatic mouflon. We bred them to be docile, woolly, and fat—wild sheep have hair, not wool, and carry most of their weight in muscle, not meat. The lamb is an artifact of this breeding: an animal that grows rapidly, fattens easily, and dies young. Most lambs raised for meat are slaughtered between four and twelve months old, well before they reach physical maturity at around two years. The meat is prized for its tenderness—muscle fibers haven't yet toughened, connective tissue remains soft, fat is white and mild.

The killing happens in industrial abattoirs or on-farm, depending on scale and region. In the United States alone, approximately 2.2 million lambs are slaughtered annually, a number dwarfed by the 9 billion chickens but significant in its cultural weight. The lamb carries symbolic freight that the chicken does not. Abrahamic religions center the lamb in foundational stories—Abraham's ram caught in the thicket, sparing Isaac; the Passover lamb whose blood marks the faithful; Christ as "Lamb of God," sacrificed for collective sin. The animal's youth is essential to the metaphor: innocence, purity, the unblemished offering.

This symbolism makes eating lamb an act shadowed by what it represents. The French serve *agneau de lait*—milk lamb, slaughtered at three to five weeks old, flesh pale as veal. The British prefer spring lamb, killed at three to five months when grass-feeding has just begun. The taste is mild, sometimes almost bland, requiring rosemary, garlic, or mint to assert character. What's prized is texture: the give of barely-worked muscle, the sweetness of youth.

Lambs are social animals, grazing in flocks, playing in small groups. Researchers observing Merino lambs documented what they called "play bouts"—spontaneous running, jumping, and gamboling that served no obvious survival function but appeared to be practiced for its own sake. These behaviors decrease as the animal ages; play is the province of the young, in sheep as in humans. A lamb will follow its mother, follow the flock, but also, if raised by humans from birth, follow people. Bottle-fed lambs become companion animals, greeting their caregivers with enthusiasm that looks remarkably like affection.

The lamb teaches us something about time and value. We have decided that certain animals are worth more dead at the beginning of their lives than alive at the middle or end. A mature sheep produces wool annually, can breed for years, serves the flock as experienced member. But the market values the lamb—the creature that hasn't yet done any of this—more highly. The logic is gustatory: younger flesh tastes better to us. But it's also theological, symbolic, aesthetic. We want the creature before it becomes worldly, before it toughens, before it knows enough to complicate our consumption.

That lamb you see in spring pastures, white against green, hopping stiff-legged in what looks like pure joy—it exists in a narrow window. In four months or six months or eight, it will be mutton, which we value less. Or it will become a ewe for breeding, a ram for genetics, an animal whose purpose shifts from flesh to reproduction. The lamb itself is a category that vanishes with time. You cannot be a lamb at two years old. The word describes not a species but a stage, a season, a state of not-yet-being what you'll become.

The animal knows none of this. It knows grass and milk, warmth and flock, the sound of its mother's voice in a field of voices. It knows how to run. What it doesn't know is that its youth is exactly what we value, that its innocence is precisely why it's useful, that spring—which for the species means survival—for the individual means the beginning of the countdown toward slaughter.
