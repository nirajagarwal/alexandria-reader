# Rat

The Norway rat, *Rattus norvegicus*, arrived in North America sometime in the eighteenth century, likely aboard grain ships from Europe, though it originated nowhere near Norway—the species evolved in northern China and spread west along trade routes, a parallel colonizer moving in the shadow of human expansion. It reached England around 1720, Paris by 1750, and crossed the Atlantic not long after. Within decades it had displaced the black rat, *Rattus rattus*, which had itself arrived centuries earlier with medieval merchants. The Norway rat was larger, more aggressive, a better swimmer, and crucially, preferred to burrow underground rather than nest in rafters and grain stores. It occupied a different niche—beneath us rather than among our stored goods—and from that position it has thrived in every human settlement of sufficient density to sustain it.

A brown rat can squeeze through a hole the width of a quarter. Its skeleton is partially collapsible, the ribs articulating in ways that permit temporary compression; the skull can fold enough to pass through gaps that seem impossible. This capacity for infiltration has made the rat unsurpassable as an urban animal. Sewer systems, subway tunnels, the cavities inside walls—these are rat highways, mapped and re-mapped by each generation through scent and memory. A single pair of rats can produce up to two thousand descendants in a year if conditions permit, though predation, disease, and competition usually prevent such exponential growth. Still, the math explains why rat populations recover so quickly after extermination efforts. Poison a building to zero and within months the population has returned, colonizers arriving from adjacent territories, sensing the sudden availability of resources.

They are neophobic—afraid of new things. A rat encountering an unfamiliar object in its territory will avoid it for days, sometimes weeks, circling, watching, waiting to see if other rats sicken or die. This is why effective rat poison must be slow-acting, undetectable until the trap is already sprung. Fast-acting poisons create "bait shyness"—the colony learns, and the trap becomes useless. This is social learning transmitted through observation and pheromones. Rats mark trails and food sources with urine, encoding information about safety and danger. A poisoned rat excretes stress hormones that warn others away. The colony is, in effect, a distributed nervous system, processing danger collectively.

Humans have killed rats for centuries—with terriers, with traps, with poison, with fire—but we have also made them indispensable. Wistar rats, bred from albino Norway rats captured in Philadelphia in 1906, became the foundation of modern laboratory science. Roughly eighty-five percent of medical research involves rats or mice. They're used to test drugs, study diseases, map neural circuits, investigate behavior. The Jackson Laboratory alone has produced hundreds of millions of rats, each one a living data point. The rat genome was sequenced in 2004, revealing that rats and humans share about ninety percent of their genes. A rat heart beats much like a human heart, scaled down. Rat neurons fire on the same principles. A rat can suffer, feel pain, experience stress—responses we can measure because they so closely resemble our own.

Researchers at the University of Chicago found that rats will free other rats from restraints even when there's no reward for doing so, even when freeing the other rat means sharing food. This looked like empathy, or at least a proto-social drive that prioritizes the distress of conspecifics. Other studies show rats laugh—not metaphorically but actually, emitting ultrasonic vocalizations at frequencies above human hearing when they're playing or being tickled. They dream. REM sleep studies show neural patterns consistent with memory consolidation, replays of the day's experiences. A rat running a maze will, during sleep, fire the same hippocampal sequences it used while navigating, sometimes in reverse, as if mentally retracing its steps.

In Paris, in 1899, there were rats enough that the city offered a bounty: ten centimes per tail. The scheme backfired—people began breeding rats for the reward. The same logic failure appeared in colonial India, where British authorities paid for dead cobras and created a cobra-farming industry. Incentives shape ecosystems in unexpected ways. Rats respond to what we provide: food, shelter, warmth, hiding places. Reduce those and the population falls. Increase them and the rats return, no matter how many you kill.

Rats carry leptospirosis, hantavirus, rat-bite fever, toxoplasmosis. They are vectors, though less so than popular imagination suggests. The Black Death, long blamed on rats, likely spread too fast to be primarily rat-borne—recent research suggests human ectoparasites, fleas and lice, may have been the main drivers. Still, rats are reservoirs for zoonotic disease, and their proximity to human waste and food makes them effective bridges between environments. A rat in a sewer and a rat in a kitchen represent the same species navigating radically different bacterial landscapes.

What rats teach is the cost of living alongside us. They are us, in a sense—adaptable, omnivorous, capable of thriving in degraded environments, comfortable with crowding and filth, neophobic but relentlessly curious, social but competitive, intelligent enough to learn and ruthless enough to survive. We built cities and the rats came. We built sewers and they moved in. We poison them and they evolve resistance. We study them to understand ourselves, and in doing so confirm the kinship we'd rather deny. The rat is the animal that refused to stay wild, that colonized human space without permission or domestication, that reminds us what thrives in the ruins of what we make.
