# Octopus

An octopus has nine brains—one central brain wrapped around its esophagus, and eight smaller brains, one in each arm. This is not a metaphor. Each arm processes sensory information independently, makes decisions about movement, and executes actions without waiting for instructions from headquarters. Cut off an octopus arm and it will continue hunting for food, bringing it to where the mouth would be if the body were still attached. The arm remembers what it's supposed to do even when separated from the creature that grew it.

This is not the only way the octopus violates our assumptions about how a body should be organized. Two-thirds of an octopus's neurons are in its arms. Its skin is covered with chromatophores—pigment sacs that expand and contract in milliseconds, allowing the animal to change color faster than a neural signal could travel from eye to skin. Some researchers now believe the skin itself might "see" light directly, changing color without input from the brain or eyes. The octopus is what intelligence looks like when it evolves along a path utterly separate from our own.

The last common ancestor we share with octopuses lived roughly 600 million years ago, a small flatworm creeping along the Precambrian seafloor. Since that split, vertebrates developed intelligence in centralized brains protected by bone. Cephalopods—the class that includes octopuses, squid, and cuttlefish—developed it in distributed networks, soft bodies with no skeleton at all except a hard beak. When an octopus solves a puzzle, it does so with a mind organized on entirely different principles than ours. Philosopher Peter Godfrey-Smith calls the octopus "probably the closest we will come to meeting an intelligent alien."

They solve problems. In laboratories, octopuses have learned to open childproof jars to get at crabs inside, navigate mazes, recognize individual human handlers, and spray water at lights that annoy them. In 2009, researchers at the Seattle Aquarium watched a giant Pacific octopus repeatedly short-circuit a light by squirting water at it until the bulb blew. When aquarium staff put the octopus in a different tank, it found that tank's light and did the same thing. The octopus dislikes bright lights and has learned how to turn them off.

But intelligence in an octopus is a different thing than intelligence in a primate. An octopus has no social life—most species are solitary, meeting other octopuses only to mate, sometimes eating their partners afterward. They do not raise their young. A female octopus lays between 50,000 and 200,000 eggs, guards them obsessively for months without eating, and dies shortly after they hatch. The tiny octopuses drift as plankton, then sink to the seafloor to begin their brief, solitary lives. Most species live one to two years. The giant Pacific octopus, largest in the world, lives five.

This raises questions about why an octopus would evolve such sophisticated intelligence. Social creatures—primates, cetaceans, corvids—develop complex cognition in part to navigate social relationships, to deceive and cooperate and remember who did what to whom. But an octopus lives alone and dies young. Its intelligence seems oriented entirely toward the immediate problems of survival: hunting, hiding, escaping. It is a mind built for the moment, not for memory or long-term planning.

And yet octopuses play. Researchers have observed them repeatedly releasing objects into tank currents and catching them, an activity with no obvious survival value. They recognize themselves in mirrors, suggesting some form of self-awareness. In the wild, octopuses have been seen carrying coconut shells or clamshells around to use as portable shelters—one of the few documented cases of invertebrate tool use. They seem to dream. Studies of sleeping octopuses show rapid changes in skin color and texture similar to REM sleep in mammals, suggesting something like dreaming might be occurring in that distributed, alien mind.

The octopus body itself is a marvel of mechanical impossibility. With no skeleton, an octopus can squeeze through any opening larger than its beak—the only hard part of its body. A 50-pound giant Pacific octopus can fit through a hole the size of a lemon. The arms, boneless and muscular, can bend in any direction, extend to several times their resting length, and manipulate objects with precision despite having no rigid structure to push against. Each of the eight arms has 200 to 300 suckers, each sucker independently controlled, each capable of tasting what it touches. An octopus exploring a crevice is not just feeling with its arms but tasting, each sucker sampling the chemical signature of everything it encounters.

This sensory richness exists in tension with the octopus's brief life. All that capacity for learning, for recognition, for solving novel problems—and then death within a year or two, sometimes months. It seems profligate, wasteful. But perhaps it suggests intelligence serves purposes we don't fully understand, that consciousness is not only an adaptation for long-term planning or social maneuvering but something more immediate—a way of being alert to the world, responsive to its textures and possibilities, even if only for a short time.

We have eaten octopus for millennia—roasted, boiled, grilled, dried. Mediterranean and Asian cuisines feature it prominently. But knowing what we now know about octopus cognition makes some people uneasy. Several countries, including the UK and Canada, have added octopuses to their lists of animals protected by animal welfare legislation. Some researchers argue we should not farm octopuses for food, that their demonstrated intelligence places them in a category deserving special ethical consideration.

The octopus asks us what we mean by mind. It has one, clearly, but built from different materials in a different pattern. It thinks without centralization, perceives without integrating all sensory input into a single stream, acts without our kind of unified self. And still it solves, plays, remembers, perhaps dreams. The octopus is proof that intelligence can evolve more than once, that consciousness might have multiple authors. Watching one move through water—arms flowing independent and coordinated, skin rippling through colors and patterns, eyes tracking you as you track it—you are looking at another solution to the problem of being aware. Strange kin, built from the same ancient cells but assembled according to an entirely different blueprint.
