# Earthworm

Charles Darwin spent the last years of his life watching worms. Not the grand voyage of the *Beagle*, not the furor over natural selection—just worms, moving through soil in pots he kept in his study. He played bassoon to them. He shone lights. He placed them on the piano while his son played, trying to determine if they could hear. In 1881, months before his death, he published *The Formation of Vegetable Mould Through the Action of Worms*, arguing that these blind, deaf creatures had quietly shaped the surface of England more profoundly than all of human history.

He was right. Earthworms—members of the phylum Annelida, segmented into repeating units like biological train cars—move ten to thirty tons of soil per acre annually in temperate regions. They are ecological engineers on a scale difficult to grasp. Each worm is a living plow, a mobile digestive system that transforms leaf litter and organic debris into humus, the dark, fertile component of soil. They tunnel constantly, creating channels that aerate earth and allow water to penetrate. Without them, much of the world's topsoil would be locked, compacted, lifeless.

Their body plan is almost absurdly simple. No eyes, no lungs, no hard parts. They breathe through their skin, which must stay moist—hence their aversion to sunlight, which desiccates. They're hermaphrodites, each possessing both male and female organs, though they still require a partner to reproduce. During mating, two worms align head-to-tail, held together by mucus, and exchange sperm packets. Later, each produces a cocoon from the clitellum, that thickened band near the head, and deposits eggs and stored sperm inside. The cocoon slips off over the worm's head like a rubber band and hardens into a protective capsule the size of a match head. In three weeks, juvenile worms emerge, already complete, just smaller.

What they lack in complexity they compensate for in durability. A common earthworm (*Lumbricus terrestris*) can live six years. Cut one in half and the head end will usually regenerate a new tail; the tail end dies. This regenerative capacity varies by species—some can regrow from either fragment—but the folk belief that you create two worms by cutting one is mostly wishful thinking. Still, worms survive routine violence. They endure winter by burrowing below the frost line, summer drought by entering dormancy, flooding by retreating to high ground or, failing that, surfacing en masse—which is why sidewalks swarm with them after heavy rain. Not, as commonly believed, because they're drowning (they can survive submerged for days) but possibly because they sense vibrations from predators or because surface movement is simply easier when it's wet.

Their navigational sense is rudimentary but sufficient. No brain, only a cluster of ganglia at the head end, yet they respond to light (avoiding it), chemical gradients (seeking food), and moisture. Darwin tested their preferences by offering them different leaves. They chose certain species over others and consistently grabbed leaves by the tip, the more efficient angle for dragging them underground. This isn't instinct alone but something closer to judgment—worms can learn. Recent studies show they navigate mazes faster after repeated trials, suggesting a capacity for memory that has no obvious neural substrate. How does a creature with no brain remember?

Humans have related to worms with a mixture of indifference and exploitation. For most of history, they were simply part of the earth, noticed only when turned up by the plow. Aristotle called them "the intestines of the earth." Cleopatra declared them sacred, forbidding their removal from Egypt. The Roman naturalist Pliny recommended them for treating ulcers, jaundice, and toothaches—remedies unsupported by evidence but revealing a recognition of their biological importance. In modern times, worms have become agricultural tools. Vermicomposting—using worms to break down organic waste—has become a small industry. Red wigglers (*Eisenia fetida*) process food scraps faster than any mechanical composter, converting garbage to fertilizer in weeks.

But earthworms are not uniformly beneficial. In northern forests of North America, where glaciation wiped out native earthworm populations, the introduction of European species has disrupted ecosystems. These invasive worms devour leaf litter too quickly, eliminating the spongy duff layer that many native plants and ground-nesting birds depend on. Forest understories thin. Salamander populations decline. What Darwin celebrated as soil improvement turns out to be ecological upheaval when worms enter ecosystems that evolved without them.

The worm's existence is narrow, circumscribed by moisture and darkness. It has no society, no communication, no awareness of other worms beyond the necessities of mating. Yet it perceives. Its skin contains photoreceptors that detect light. Its front end is dense with chemoreceptors and touch-sensitive cells. It tastes and touches the world continuously, living entirely in the present tense—no memory of yesterday's tunnel, no anticipation of tomorrow's leaf. It exists as pure process, a tube that transforms what it encounters.

Darwin watched them for forty years because he understood that the small and the overlooked often matter most. Worms taught him that gradual, invisible labor shapes worlds. That life persists not through grandeur but through repetition. That the earth is not a static stage but a living system, constantly remade by creatures we barely notice. In his final book, he calculated that all the soil in England had passed through worm bodies multiple times. The ground beneath our feet is not ground at all but residue—the accumulated feces of earthworms, compounded over millennia.

Every step you take, you're walking on what they've made.
