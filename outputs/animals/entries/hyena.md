# Hyena

The spotted hyena laughs because its larynx sits too high in its throat. The sound—that ascending giggle that has become shorthand for cowardice and treachery in a thousand films—is produced when air passes through an unusually narrow vocal tract under stress. It means excitement, frustration, or submission. It does not mean amusement. The hyena is not laughing at you, though it has every reason to.

For most of human history, we have gotten hyenas catastrophically wrong. Medieval bestiaries claimed they were hermaphrodites that changed sex with the phases of the moon. Aristotle, who should have known better, said they had a single rigid backbone that prevented them from turning their necks. African folklore sometimes cast them as sorcerers in animal form, corpse-eaters that walked between the world of the living and the dead. European colonizers called them cowards and scavengers, creatures too weak to hunt their own food, living off the kills of lions.

The truth is nearly the opposite of everything we've said about them.

Spotted hyenas—*Crocuta crocuta*, the largest and most studied of the four hyena species—are not scavengers primarily but hunters, successful on 75 percent of their hunts, a rate that makes lions look incompetent. They take down wildebeest, zebra, even young elephants, hunting in packs with sophisticated coordination. Their jaws generate 1,100 pounds per square inch of pressure, enough to crack open femurs that lions abandon. They eat every part of their prey—bone, horn, hide, hair—and digest it all. The only thing a hyena leaves behind is the hoofprint.

They are not hermaphrodites, but the females are masculinized to a degree unique among mammals. Female spotted hyenas have pseudo-penises—enlarged clitorises indistinguishable externally from male genitalia—through which they urinate, copulate, and give birth. This last act is as difficult as it sounds; the birth canal makes a 180-degree turn, and first-time mothers often lose their cubs. But this anatomy comes with advantages: females are larger than males, socially dominant, and run clans of up to eighty individuals structured in rigid matrilineal hierarchies. Daughters inherit their mother's rank. Males are immigrants, peripheral, tolerated. A low-ranking female outranks the highest-ranking male.

The complexity of hyena society rivals that of primates. Field biologists in Kenya's Masai Mara have tracked individual hyenas for decades, documenting alliances, betrayals, reconciliations, and sophisticated problem-solving. Hyenas recognize the voices of dozens of clan members, distinguish friend from rival at distances exceeding a mile, and coordinate hunts using vocalizations that vary by context—different calls for different prey, different strategies. They understand third-party social relationships: they know who's allied with whom, who owes whom a favor. In one study, hyenas outperformed chimpanzees on cooperative problem-solving tasks.

Their brains reflect this. Relative to body size, the hyena brain's frontal cortex—the region associated with executive function and social cognition—is proportionally larger than that of any carnivore except some primates. They have been observed using tools in captivity (though rarely in the wild, where their jaws make tools largely unnecessary). They recognize themselves in mirrors, a capacity once thought limited to great apes, dolphins, and elephants. And they mourn: females have been documented returning to the dens where their cubs died, calling for them, sometimes for days.

None of this has rehabilitated their reputation. The hyena remains the villain, the coward, the laughing fool. Disney's *The Lion King* cemented their role as snarling lackeys to evil. The Hausa people of West Africa still use "hyena" as an insult meaning shameless or stupid. In parts of Tanzania and Malawi, people believe hyenas are ridden by witches at night or that witches transform into hyenas to do their work. These beliefs have consequences: hyenas are poisoned, shot, burned alive.

Part of the problem is aesthetic. The hyena does not move like other predators. Its front legs are longer than its hind legs, giving it a slouching, loping gait that reads as ungainly, even servile. Its head is too large for its body, its muzzle too short, its ears too round. It looks assembled from spare parts. When it feeds, it is enthusiastic, messy, noisy—tearing, crunching, gulping. Lions feed with a certain regal composure. Hyenas feed like they mean it.

But perhaps the deeper problem is ontological. The hyena violates categories. It looks dog-like but is more closely related to cats and mongooses, part of the suborder Feliformia. Its females aren't female in any straightforward sense. It hunts but also scavenges; it's an apex predator but also a janitor. It is social but hierarchical, intelligent but brutal, nurturing but also capable of eating sick clan members alive. It doesn't fit our stories about how animals should be.

What the hyena teaches is that nature has no obligation to our narratives. The categories we impose—noble predator, lowly scavenger, male, female, cruel, kind—are ours, not the world's. The hyena simply is: an evolutionary solution to the problem of how to extract maximum nutrition from the African savanna while navigating one of the most complex social systems outside primates. That it disturbs us says more about our need for simplicity than about any failing on the hyena's part.

In northern Ethiopia, the town of Harar has made an uneasy peace with hyenas. For generations, "hyena men" have fed spotted hyenas by hand outside the old city walls, a practice that began as appeasement and has become spectacle. Tourists watch as hyenas take meat from human mouths. The hyenas are wild, not pets, but they have learned: here, humans are not threat but resource. Both species have adjusted. The laughter continues, misunderstood, meaning what it has always meant—I am here, I am alive, I will take what this world offers.
