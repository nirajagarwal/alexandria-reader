# Turkey

The turkey's wattle—that fleshy, vascularized appendage dangling from its throat—can change color in seconds. Blue when calm, red when agitated or displaying, white when frightened or sick. It is a mood ring of blood flow, a visible broadcast of internal state. The male turkey, strutting with tail fanned and feathers erected, is performing one of nature's more elaborate advertisements: *I am so healthy I can afford to be this conspicuous*. The display costs energy. The bright colors attract predators. Only a bird in excellent condition can sustain such flagrant visibility.

This is *Meleagris gallopavo*, native to North America, where it has lived for at least five million years. Two species exist: the wild turkey of forests and grasslands from Mexico to Canada, and the ocellated turkey of the Yucatan Peninsula, smaller and more iridescent. The wild turkey that Benjamin Franklin supposedly preferred as national bird—he didn't, quite, though he did note the bald eagle's poor character compared to the turkey's—is a different creature from the broad-breasted white that dominates American Thanksgiving tables. The wild bird can fly short distances at 55 miles per hour, roost in trees, and cover 25 miles in a day on foot. The domestic bird, bred for breast meat so heavy it throws off the bird's center of gravity, often cannot mate naturally. Artificial insemination is standard practice in industrial turkey production.

The Aztecs domesticated turkeys around 800 BCE. The Spanish encountered them in Mexico in the early 1500s and brought them to Europe, where they spread rapidly—France by 1520, England by 1540. The English colonists who arrived in North America in the 1600s brought European-domesticated turkeys with them, reintroducing to the continent a bird whose wild ancestors had never left. This loop of domestication created something peculiar: wild and domestic turkeys coexisting in the same landscape, one capable of flight and wariness, the other bred for docility and meat.

Wild turkeys nearly went extinct in the early 20th century. Habitat loss and unregulated hunting reduced the population to about 30,000 birds by the 1930s. Then came one of conservation's quiet successes: trap-and-transplant programs, habitat restoration, hunting regulations. The population rebounded to roughly seven million wild turkeys today. They have adapted to suburbs, learned to navigate human landscapes. In some neighborhoods, they are pests—aggressive, unafraid, blocking traffic. The turkey's recovery reveals something about adaptation: the species is not fragile. Given half a chance, it thrives.

The turkey's perceptual world is broader than ours in some dimensions, narrower in others. Turkeys have excellent color vision, better than humans—they see into the ultraviolet spectrum. Their daytime vision is sharp; they can spot movement at considerable distances. This is why wild turkeys are so difficult to hunt. They notice the smallest anomaly, the slightest motion. But their night vision is poor. At dusk, they fly into trees to roost, where they remain until dawn, essentially blind and vulnerable. This is when predators—great horned owls, raccoons, bobcats—take them.

Turkeys are more cognitively complex than their cultural reputation suggests. Studies show they can remember the locations of food sources, recognize individual humans (and respond differently to those who have treated them well versus poorly), and learn from watching other turkeys. Young turkeys, called poults, follow their mother for the first several weeks of life, learning what to eat, where to roost, how to respond to predators. The hen makes at least 28 distinct vocalizations; poults begin learning these calls while still in the egg, responding to their mother's sounds before they hatch.

The bird's association with stupidity stems partly from a persistent myth: that domestic turkeys will stare at the sky during rainstorms until they drown. This is false. The behavior sometimes observed—head tilted back, staring upward—results from a genetic condition called tetanic torticollar spasms, which affects some domestic turkeys and is entirely unrelated to weather. But the myth endures because it serves a purpose. We need the turkey to be stupid. It makes the eating easier.

Americans consume roughly 46 million turkeys each Thanksgiving, another 22 million at Christmas. The birds are slaughtered at 14 to 18 weeks old, far short of their natural lifespan of 3 to 5 years for wild turkeys, up to 10 in captivity. The average bird weighs about 30 pounds at slaughter, roughly double the weight of a wild turkey. This is the result of intensive selective breeding focused on a single trait: breast meat. The broad-breasted white turkey, developed in the 1960s, now represents 99% of commercial production. The breed grows so quickly that skeletal and cardiovascular systems struggle to keep pace. Heart attacks are common. Leg deformities are routine.

Yet there remains something undeniably impressive about a wild turkey in full strut. The fan of tail feathers, each one precisely aligned. The wings dropped and spread, primaries scraping ground. The head thrown back, wattle engorged, caruncles—those fleshy bumps covering head and neck—swollen and bright. The bird produces a low frequency sound, a boom felt as much as heard, created by expelling air from an inflated chest. It is absurd and magnificent simultaneously, which is perhaps the turkey's essential character: a bird that refuses to choose between comedy and dignity.

The turkey has taught us about selective breeding's capabilities and limits, about conservation's possibilities, about the gap between wild and domestic, between what an animal is and what we make it. It has taught us that we can engineer creatures to serve our purposes so thoroughly that they can no longer survive without us—and that wildness, given space, resurges. Mostly, though, the turkey teaches us about forgetting. We forget that the centerpiece of our national feast is a living creature with its own existence, its own senses and behaviors and history. We forget it so thoroughly that we can eat it once a year without thinking about what it was. This forgetting is perhaps what we do best with animals: transform them so completely into food, symbol, or resource that we no longer see what they actually are.
