# Chimpanzee

In 1960, Jane Goodall watched a chimpanzee strip leaves from a twig, poke it into a termite mound, and withdraw it covered with insects, which he ate. This observation—a wild animal making a tool—forced a redefinition. Before Goodall, tool use was considered the bright line separating humans from animals. After, we had to choose: expand the definition of human, contract the definition of tool, or admit that the line had always been arbitrary.

We chose the third option reluctantly. In the decades since, chimpanzees have been observed cracking nuts with stone hammers, fashioning spears to hunt bushbabies, using leaves as sponges to drink water from tree holes, and passing these behaviors down through what can only be called culture. Different chimpanzee communities have different traditions. Some fish for termites; others ignore them. Some crack certain nuts; others don't, even when the nuts and stones are available. The variations aren't genetic—they're learned, transmitted, maintained across generations. They are, in other words, cultural.

*Pan troglodytes*. The genus name means "all" or "every"—chosen because early taxonomists saw in apes something disturbingly universal. The species name means "cave-dweller," which is wrong; chimpanzees live in forests. But the error is revealing. We've always needed them to be other—primitive, bestial, less. We've needed distance.

The distance is approximately six million years, the time since our lineages split. We share 98.8% of our DNA. This number gets cited constantly, as if it settles something. It doesn't. The 1.2% difference contains everything that makes us human—language, imagination, the capacity to contemplate our own extinction. But the 98.8% contains everything else: hands that grasp, faces that express emotion, societies that navigate alliances, grudges, reconciliations.

A chimpanzee community numbers between twenty and 150 individuals, organized around a hierarchy of males who compete, cooperate, and betray one another with Shakespearean complexity. Females often migrate between communities, but males stay in their natal groups their entire lives—fifty years or more. They form coalitions, groom strategically, share meat after hunts, and sometimes overthrow alphas through coordinated rebellion. Frans de Waal, who spent decades observing them, titled his book *Chimpanzee Politics* without irony.

The politics can be lethal. Male chimps patrol their territory borders, and when they encounter lone individuals from neighboring groups, they kill them—not quickly, but with sustained brutality. These aren't defensive acts or fights over immediate resources. They're coordinated, premeditated raids. Over time, one community can systematically eliminate another, then expand into the vacated territory. Jane Goodall, who began her research believing chimpanzees were "rather nicer than human beings," watched a four-year campaign of systematic killing. She called it war.

They also kill their own. Infanticide happens, though less often than in some other primates. More disturbing are the cases of lethal aggression within communities—violence that serves no obvious evolutionary purpose, that looks less like strategy and more like rage, cruelty, murder.

And yet they also comfort one another. A chimpanzee who has lost a fight will be approached by others who embrace them, pat them, groom them. They share food, though not always fairly. They adopt orphans, though not always tenderly. Young males have been observed caring for infants unrelated to them after their mothers died—carrying them, sharing food, sleeping close—keeping them alive.

They recognize themselves in mirrors, one of a handful of species that pass this test of self-awareness. They understand what others know and don't know—theory of mind, the ability to imagine another's perspective. In experiments, they'll deceive humans about the location of hidden food, but only if they know the human watched them hide it. They track mental states.

Their communication is complex—dozens of distinct vocalizations, hundreds of gestures—but it isn't language. They can learn sign language or symbolic keyboards in captivity, acquiring vocabularies of a few hundred words, but they don't generate syntax, don't create new meanings by combining symbols recursively. The gap between their communication and human language remains profound, unbridged despite decades of attempts.

What they do have is a kind of intelligence that operates outside language: spatial, social, embodied. A chimpanzee can remember the location of dozens of fruit trees across miles of forest and know when each will ripen. They can predict how others will respond to threats, gifts, alliances. They can see a problem—termites in a mound, nuts too hard to crack—and invent a solution.

They're also among the most endangered great apes. A century ago, perhaps a million chimpanzees lived across equatorial Africa. Now there are fewer than 300,000, and populations continue to decline. Habitat loss, disease, bushmeat hunting. In some regions, they're killed for meat. In others, infants are captured for the pet trade, which requires killing the mother and often several other adults who try to defend her.

We've also used them as research subjects. Before the development of neuroimaging and computer models, thousands of chimpanzees were kept in laboratories, subjected to experiments on cognition, disease, addiction. Many lived their entire lives in cages. Some were infected with HIV or hepatitis. The research advanced human medicine significantly. It also depended on treating our closest relatives as expendable.

The United States stopped breeding chimpanzees for research in 1995 and officially ended invasive research in 2015. Many of the remaining lab chimps have been retired to sanctuaries. They'll live out their decades grooming one another, navigating social dramas, using tools, recognizing themselves. Some were born in forests and taken. Others know only captivity—raised by humans, trained to use symbols, asked questions about what they think and feel.

What they think, we can only partly know. They don't tell stories, don't ask questions, don't wonder about their origins. But they grieve their dead, console their allies, anticipate the future, remember the past. They live in a world of relationships, strategies, tools, and violence. A world that looks, from certain angles, disturbingly like a mirror.
