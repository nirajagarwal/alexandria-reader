# Wasp

A wasp is a flying insect that most people have decided to hate. This is understandable. Unlike bees, which die after stinging and therefore seem to exercise some restraint, wasps can sting repeatedly. Unlike bees, which make honey and pollinate crops and have been mythologized into industrious virtue, wasps seem to exist only to ruin picnics and build papier-mâché nests in places you need to reach. They are aggressive, territorial, and drawn to exactly the foods humans most enjoy outdoors—ripe fruit, sweet drinks, meat. The bee is the Protestant work ethic with wings. The wasp is a small, flying menace with no apparent purpose.

This assessment is wrong in almost every particular, but it has shaped human behavior for millennia. We have words for the systematic destruction of wasp nests—"abatement," "eradication"—that we don't use for bees. We teach children to remain calm around bees and to run from wasps. The difference matters. There are over 100,000 species of wasps, vastly outnumbering bees, and they are among the most important animals on Earth.

The wasp family includes everything from the tiny fairyfly, barely visible to the naked eye, to the tarantula hawk, which grows to two inches long and delivers one of the most painful stings known to science. Most wasps don't sting at all. Many are parasitic, laying eggs inside other insects—caterpillars, aphids, beetles—where the larvae devour their host from the inside out. Charles Darwin found this so disturbing he cited it as evidence against a benevolent creator. "I cannot persuade myself," he wrote, "that a beneficent and omnipotent God would have designedly created the Ichneumonidae with the express intention of their feeding within the living bodies of caterpillars."

But this is precisely what makes wasps essential. They are nature's pest control, regulating populations of insects that would otherwise devastate crops and forests. A single colony of social wasps—the yellow jackets and hornets that build the nests we fear—can consume thousands of insects per day. They hunt caterpillars, flies, aphids, and beetle larvae, paralyzing them with venom and carrying them back to feed their young. Without wasps, agricultural yields would plummet and ecosystems would destabilize. We would need far more pesticides than we already use, with all the attendant consequences for soil, water, and human health.

Yet wasps receive almost none of the recognition given to bees. This is partly because their work is invisible—they don't produce honey or beeswax, nothing humans can harvest—and partly because their life cycle seems cruel. But from the wasp's perspective, efficiency is not cruel; it's necessary. A parasitic wasp mother injects her eggs into a caterpillar not out of malice but because her offspring need protein to develop, and a living caterpillar provides it in controlled doses. The caterpillar continues eating, growing, fueling the larvae inside it until they're ready to emerge. It's a system perfected over millions of years, as old as flowering plants.

Some wasps have become so specialized they can only parasitize a single species. The genus *Megaphragma* includes wasps so small—around 200 micrometers—that they're nearly at the lower limit of insect size. At that scale, conventional insect anatomy doesn't work; there's not enough room for a complete nervous system. So these wasps have evolved neurons without nuclei, shedding cellular material to save space. They are functional animals with incomplete cells, which should be impossible. But wasps have repeatedly solved problems other insects haven't encountered, developing forms of navigation, architecture, and social organization that rival or exceed those of bees.

Paper wasps, which build those umbrella-shaped nests under eaves and branches, were making paper long before humans. They scrape wood fibers from dead trees and fence posts, chew them into pulp mixed with saliva, and spread the mixture into thin sheets that dry into a light, strong, waterproof material. The nests have thermoregulation systems—chambers that capture and circulate heat—and can house thousands of individuals organized into castes: queens, workers, drones. When a queen dies, workers can activate their ovaries and lay eggs, a form of reproductive flexibility rare in social insects.

Wasps also pollinate. Not as effectively as bees, in most cases, but for some plants wasps are the only option. Figs, which sustain countless species across the tropics, depend entirely on fig wasps. Each fig species has a corresponding wasp species; the wasp enters the fig through a tiny opening, pollinating it while laying her eggs inside. She dies there, her body absorbed into the fruit as it ripens. Every fig you eat contains the remnants of at least one wasp, dissolved but essential. The relationship is so specific that if fig wasps disappeared, so would figs—and with them, the fruit-eating birds, mammals, and insects that depend on them.

The economic value of wasps, calculated by ecologist Ryan Brock and colleagues, runs into billions of dollars annually—pest control, pollination, carrion removal. Yet they remain almost entirely unstudied compared to bees. Funding for bee research outpaces wasp research by orders of magnitude. We have detailed maps of honeybee cognition, communication, and social behavior, but vast gaps in our knowledge of wasps. We don't know how many species are declining, or how fast, or what the consequences might be. We've decided they're villains, so we don't look closely.

What we're missing is an animal of astonishing versatility and precision. A creature that taught us how to make paper, how to build cooling systems, how to control pests without poison. An insect that sees in ultraviolet, navigates by polarized light, and remembers the faces of individual wasps in its colony. When a wasp hovers near your plate at a summer barbecue, it's not attacking. It's hunting protein to bring back to larvae it will never see mature. It's doing work you don't notice, in a world you've decided doesn't matter. The wasp makes you uncomfortable not because it's aggressive but because it refuses to be useful on your terms. It persists in being exactly what it is.
