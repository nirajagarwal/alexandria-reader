# Anchoring: A Panel Discussion

*A conversation exploring how first numbers shape all subsequent judgments with Jordan Lee (moderator), Dr. Maya Patel (behavioral economist), Alex Chen (strategy consultant), and Dr. Sarah Morrison (cognitive psychologist)*

---

**Jordan Lee:** Welcome everyone to another episode of Decision Science Today. I'm Jordan Lee, and today we're diving into one of the most pervasive yet invisible forces in decision-making: anchoring. You know that feeling when you walk into a negotiation thinking you know what you want, but somehow the first number thrown out completely changes your perspective? That's anchoring at work.

I'm joined by three experts who see this phenomenon from very different angles. Dr. Maya Patel is a behavioral economist whose research focuses on cognitive biases and decision-making under uncertainty. Alex Chen is a strategy consultant who's seen anchoring play out in countless business negotiations and strategic decisions. And Dr. Sarah Morrison is a cognitive psychologist who studies the gap between how we think we think and how we actually think.

Let's start with the classic study that really put anchoring on the map. Maya, can you walk us through what Tversky and Kahneman discovered in 1974?

**Dr. Maya Patel:** Absolutely. It's one of those studies that sounds almost too bizarre to be true. They had people estimate what percentage of African nations belonged to the UN. But before they made their estimates, each person watched a rigged wheel of fortune spin and land on either 10 or 65. Completely random, completely irrelevant to UN membership, and everyone knew it.

**Alex Chen:** And yet it worked, right? The people who saw 10 averaged around 25 percent in their estimates, while those who saw 65 averaged 45 percent.

**Dr. Maya Patel:** Exactly. A meaningless number, presented seconds earlier, yanked their judgments in its direction. What's fascinating is that this wasn't subtle – we're talking about a 20 percentage point difference based on pure randomness.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** This is what I find most unsettling about anchoring research. These weren't naive participants who didn't understand what was happening. They knew the wheel was irrelevant. But their minds couldn't help using that number as a reference point. It reveals something fundamental about how our cognitive machinery works.

### Segment 1: The Invisible Influence

**Jordan Lee:** Sarah, you mentioned that participants knew the wheel was irrelevant, but they were influenced anyway. Why is anchoring so invisible to us when it's happening?

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** The anchor doesn't announce itself with fanfare. When we arrive at a judgment, it feels like our own organic conclusion. We're not consciously thinking, "Well, the wheel said 65, so..." Instead, our mind uses that number as a starting point and adjusts from there, but we only experience the final result of that process.

**Alex Chen:** I see this constantly in business. A client will come in saying they have a budget of, let's say, $500,000 for a project. Later, when we present options ranging from $300,000 to $450,000, they feel like they're getting a good deal. But if they'd said their budget was $200,000, those same options would feel expensive. The first number they mentioned anchored the entire conversation.

**Dr. Maya Patel:** Alex raises an important point about the adjustment mechanism. When people encounter an anchor, they don't just accept it wholesale. They recognize it might not be perfect and they adjust away from it. The problem is, the adjustment is almost always insufficient.

**Jordan Lee:** Can you give us an example of that insufficient adjustment?

**Dr. Maya Patel:** Sure. In another Tversky and Kahneman study, they asked people whether Gandhi was more or less than 140 years old when he died. Obviously, 140 is absurd – people know that. So they adjust downward and estimate around 67. But when the anchor was 9 years old, people adjusted upward to around 50. Gandhi actually died at 78, so both groups were wrong, but in predictably different directions.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** What's happening cognitively is that people anchor on the initial number and then search for reasons to adjust. But once they find a few reasons that seem sufficient, they stop. They don't keep adjusting until they reach the optimal answer.

**Alex Chen:** This is why preparation is so crucial in negotiations. If you walk in without your own well-researched anchor, you're vulnerable to whatever number gets thrown out first. I've seen deals where someone accepted terms they would have rejected if the negotiation had started differently.

### Segment 2: Anchoring in the Wild

**Jordan Lee:** Alex, let's dig deeper into how this plays out in real-world situations. You mentioned real estate and restaurants in your work – how do professionals weaponize anchoring?

**Alex Chen:** Real estate is probably the most obvious example. Agents will often show you an overpriced house first – not because they expect you to buy it, but because it makes everything else look reasonable by comparison. That $800,000 house makes the $600,000 house feel like a bargain, even if it's only worth $500,000.

**Dr. Maya Patel:** The restaurant menu example is interesting too. You'll see a $200 steak listed prominently, making an $85 ribeye seem modest. Most people never order the $200 steak, but its presence on the menu shifts everyone's reference point.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** What's particularly clever about these strategies is that they exploit our psychological machinery without feeling manipulative in the moment. You don't feel like you're being tricked when you choose the "moderately priced" option.

**Jordan Lee:** Maya, your research focuses on decision-making under uncertainty. Does anchoring become more powerful when we're in unfamiliar territory?

**Dr. Maya Patel:** Absolutely. Context is everything with anchoring. When you're uncertain – estimating something unfamiliar, negotiating in a new domain, making decisions without clear comparables – anchors gain tremendous power. Your mind is searching for footholds, and the anchor, however arbitrary, offers one.

**Alex Chen:** I see this with startups trying to price their services. They often have no idea what they should charge, so they latch onto whatever number they hear first – maybe what a competitor charges, or what a potential client mentions as their budget. That first number becomes their reality.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** There's also an interesting emotional component here. Uncertainty makes us uncomfortable, and an anchor reduces that discomfort by giving us a reference point. We're motivated to use it because it helps us feel more confident in our judgment, even when that confidence is misplaced.

### Segment 3: The Limits and Defenses

**Jordan Lee:** Now, anchoring isn't all-powerful, right? What are its limitations?

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** Extreme anchors can actually backfire. If someone opens with a price so outrageous it insults your intelligence, you might reject not just the number but the entire negotiation. There's a zone where anchoring works, but push beyond it and you can actually damage your position.

**Alex Chen:** I've seen this happen. A vendor once opened a negotiation with such an absurd price that my client immediately started looking for alternatives. The anchor was so far from reality that it signaled either incompetence or bad faith.

**Dr. Maya Patel:** Expertise also matters. Anchors work less well on people who have deep knowledge in the relevant domain. A seasoned real estate investor won't be as swayed by an amateur's asking price the way a first-time buyer might be.

**Jordan Lee:** But here's what I find almost disturbing – even knowing about anchoring doesn't make us immune to it, does it?

**Dr. Maya Patel:** Not at all. Tversky and Kahneman demonstrated this repeatedly. Even researchers who studied the effect – people who understood the mechanism completely – still fell prey to it in experiments. Intellectual awareness isn't enough to overcome this bias.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** This is actually quite common with cognitive biases. They operate at a level that's more fundamental than conscious reasoning. You can know all about optical illusions, but that doesn't stop you from seeing them.

**Alex Chen:** So what can we actually do about it? Because this sounds pretty hopeless.

**Dr. Maya Patel:** Well, awareness does create opportunities for intervention, even if it doesn't provide immunity. When a number appears early in a decision process, you can pause and ask: Where did this come from? Is it meaningful, or just the first thing I heard? Am I adjusting enough, or am I still in its orbit?

### Segment 4: Practical Strategies

**Jordan Lee:** Let's get practical. What strategies actually work for dealing with anchoring?

**Alex Chen:** The clearest defense is to generate your own anchor before anyone else can. Do the research, know the market, walk in with a number you've thought through independently. In salary negotiations, for example, the person who states their expectations first often does better than those who wait.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** That's a great point. By setting your own anchor, you're taking control of the reference point instead of letting someone else establish it for you.

**Dr. Maya Patel:** I'd add that it's worth doing this research from multiple angles. Don't just find one comparable and anchor on that. Look at ranges, consider different methodologies, try to triangulate toward a reasonable number.

**Jordan Lee:** What about when you can't prepare in advance? Sometimes these situations catch us off guard.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** In those cases, I recommend explicitly questioning the first number. Ask yourself: "If this number were different, how would that change my thinking?" Try to imagine the negotiation starting with a much higher or lower anchor and see if that shifts your perspective.

**Alex Chen:** Time can be your friend too. If possible, don't make decisions immediately after hearing an anchor. Sleep on it, discuss it with others who weren't present for the original conversation. Sometimes distance helps you see how much that first number was influencing your thinking.

**Dr. Maya Patel:** And consider bringing in outside perspectives. Fresh eyes can often spot when you're being anchored in ways you can't see yourself.

**Jordan Lee:** Alex, you work with teams a lot. Are there organizational strategies that help?

**Alex Chen:** Absolutely. We often have team members independently research and write down their estimates before we start discussing. That way, everyone has their own anchor before they hear others. It's not perfect, but it reduces the power of whoever speaks first.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** That's a smart approach. It's similar to techniques used in forecasting, where you want to capture everyone's initial judgment before groupthink sets in.

### Segment 5: The Bigger Picture

**Jordan Lee:** As we wrap up, I want to zoom out a bit. What does anchoring tell us about human decision-making more broadly?

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** It reveals that we're not the rational calculators we often imagine ourselves to be. Our judgments are deeply contextual, shaped by seemingly irrelevant factors in ways we don't recognize. That's humbling, but also important to understand.

**Dr. Maya Patel:** From an evolutionary perspective, anchoring probably served us well in many contexts. Using available reference points to make quick estimates was likely better than having no starting point at all. The problem is that our modern environment can exploit this tendency in ways that don't serve our interests.

**Alex Chen:** It also highlights why process matters so much in decision-making. The order in which information is presented, who speaks first, what examples are used – these seemingly minor details can fundamentally alter outcomes.

**Jordan Lee:** Maya, do you think we're getting better at recognizing and countering these biases as they become more well-known?

**Dr. Maya Patel:** In some domains, yes. I see more sophistication in negotiations, more awareness in business settings. But anchoring is so fundamental to how our minds work that I don't think we'll ever fully escape it. The goal is probably damage control rather than elimination.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** I agree. And honestly, I'm not sure we'd want to eliminate it completely. Using reference points to make judgments is often useful. The key is being more intentional about which reference points we use and recognizing when they might be leading us astray.

**Alex Chen:** That's a good way to think about it. It's not about becoming perfectly rational – it's about becoming more aware of our own mental processes and building in safeguards where it matters most.

### Closing: Key Takeaways

**Jordan Lee:** So as we close, let me ask each of you: what's the one thing you most want listeners to remember about anchoring?

**Dr. Maya Patel:** That anchoring is invisible but not inevitable. You can't always prevent it, but you can prepare for it by doing your homework and setting your own reference points before others set them for you.

**Alex Chen:** The first number has power, so make sure it's your number. Whether you're negotiating, estimating, or making decisions, try to establish thoughtful anchors rather than accepting arbitrary ones.

**Dr. Sarah Morrison:** Remember that knowing about anchoring doesn't make you immune to it, but it does give you a fighting chance. Build processes and habits that account for your psychological limitations rather than pretending they don't exist.

**Jordan Lee:** Those are excellent insights. The key seems to be combining self-awareness with practical strategies – understanding that we're all susceptible to anchoring while taking concrete steps to minimize its negative effects.

Thanks to Dr. Maya Patel, Alex Chen, and Dr. Sarah Morrison for this fascinating discussion. For our listeners, remember: once an anchor drops, everything moves toward it. Including you. The question is whether you'll choose that anchor or let someone else choose it for you.

Until next time, I'm Jordan Lee. Keep making better decisions.
