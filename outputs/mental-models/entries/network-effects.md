# Network Effects

***Why some products become impossible to unseat***

> **Section:** Systems & Feedback

In 1997, eBay had a problem that would make most startups jealous. They'd built an auction site that worked perfectly well. The code was solid. The user experience was good enough. But when venture capitalists looked at their business, they saw something that made eBay nearly impossible to compete with: every new user made the site more valuable to every other user.

If you wanted to sell something rare—a vintage guitar, a first edition book, a piece of specialized equipment—where would you list it? On the platform with ten potential buyers, or the one with ten thousand? And if you were hunting for that rare item, where would you look? The same calculation, reversed. Sellers went where the buyers were. Buyers went where the sellers were. Each group pulled the other in, and the gap between eBay and its competitors widened with every transaction.

This is a network effect: a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it. It's not just that the company benefits from more users—though it does. It's that each user benefits from all the other users. The value is in the network itself.

Network effects explain why you use the same messaging app as your friends, even if a better one exists. A messaging app with superior features but zero of your contacts is worthless. The app your friends are already on—even if it's clunky—is infinitely more useful. The technical quality matters less than the network size. This is why dominant messaging platforms are so hard to displace, and why new ones only succeed by capturing a distinct group all at once (gamers on Discord, professionals on Slack).

The effect compounds. Early in a network's life, growth is slow. Each new user adds modest value. But past a certain threshold—economists call it critical mass—the dynamic reverses. The network becomes a gravitational well. Users join because other users are there, which attracts more users, which makes leaving increasingly costly. The technical term is "positive feedback loop." The practical term is "moat."

Not all networks are equal. Direct network effects connect users to users: telephones, social networks, marketplaces. Each participant directly benefits from others participating. Indirect network effects work through complementary goods: more iPhone users mean more app developers, which means better apps, which means more iPhone users. The effect is real but mediated.

Network effects also vary in strength. Local networks—like a neighborhood NextDoor group—have natural limits. You don't benefit from users across the country. Global networks—like Twitter or YouTube—keep growing in value as they scale. Some networks suffer from congestion as they grow: too many sellers on eBay can make finding what you want harder. Too many users on a dating app can create choice paralysis.

Where network effects mislead: they're often confused with scale effects (lower costs per user as you grow) or brand effects (people choose you because they've heard of you). Those are real advantages, but they're not network effects. The test is simple: would this product be more valuable to you personally if more people used it? If not, it's not a network effect.

Network effects also create false security. Strong networks can be displaced if a new platform offers a powerful enough reason to switch *and* solves the coordination problem. Facebook replaced MySpace despite MySpace's network advantage. WhatsApp took users from SMS. The network effect is a moat, but not an impenetrable wall.

The practical implication: when evaluating a product, a company, or a market position, ask whether network effects are at work. Are users locked in by other users, or just by habit? Is the competitive advantage growing or stable? If you're building something new, can you create network effects, or will you compete on quality alone? The difference determines whether you're building a product or building a fortress.

The network doesn't care about fairness or quality. It rewards whoever gets there first and crosses the threshold. The best product doesn't always win. The product with the most users wins. That's not cynicism. That's physics.
