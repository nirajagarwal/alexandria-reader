# Jevons Paradox

***When efficiency makes us use more, not less***

> **Section:** Systems & Consequences

In 1865, William Stanley Jevons published *The Coal Question*, warning that Britain would run out of coal. This seems backward. Steam engines were becoming dramatically more efficient. James Watt's engine extracted far more work from each ton of coal than earlier designs. Shouldn't better efficiency mean less coal consumption?

Jevons observed the opposite. As engines became more efficient, coal use exploded. The reason was simple: efficient engines made coal-powered operations cheaper. Factories that couldn't afford steam power suddenly could. Industries that used a little steam power found it economical to use much more. New applications emerged. The efficiency gains didn't reduce demand — they unleashed it.

This pattern appears everywhere. LED bulbs use 75% less electricity than incandescent bulbs, but we install more lights. Cars get better gas mileage, so we drive farther and buy heavier vehicles. Video compression makes streaming cheaper, so we watch in higher resolution and on more devices. Agricultural efficiency doesn't mean less farmland — it means more people fed, which means more demand for food, which often means more farmland converted.

The paradox emerges from a basic economic dynamic. Make something cheaper and you make it more attractive. More attractive means more applications, more users, more reasons to consume. The efficiency savings get spent on additional consumption, not on using less.

This doesn't mean efficiency is bad. Jevons wasn't arguing against better steam engines. He was noting that efficiency alone won't reduce total consumption if demand is elastic — if lowering the cost increases how much people want.

The model clarifies why many sustainability efforts disappoint. A company reduces the energy cost of producing widgets by 30%. Sounds good. But if that cost reduction lets them sell widgets cheaper, they might sell 50% more widgets. Total energy use rises. The efficiency gain was real. The environmental benefit wasn't.

Where the paradox misleads: It doesn't always hold. If demand is inelastic — if people have a fixed need — efficiency can reduce consumption. Better insulation truly does reduce heating costs because most people heat their homes to a comfortable temperature and stop. Making water heaters more efficient reduces hot water energy use because people don't suddenly take longer showers just because each minute costs less.

The paradox also operates differently at individual and system levels. You personally might use your efficient car less. But someone else buys your old inefficient car and drives it. Someone else starts driving because gas is more affordable. The system-level rebound can overwhelm individual restraint.

The key question is always: Does efficiency reduce the effective cost? And if so, does that lower cost enable more consumption? For climate policy, this matters enormously. Carbon efficiency without quantity limits often means more total carbon. For personal decisions, it means recognizing that using something more efficiently might simply mean you use it more.

Jevons identified this in 1865 watching coal consumption surge alongside efficiency gains. A century and a half later, we're still surprised when the same pattern repeats with bandwidth, electricity, fuel, and materials. Efficiency is powerful. But if it makes something cheaper, don't assume it makes us use less.

**The takeaway:** Making something more efficient often makes it more abundant. Lower cost per unit can mean higher total use.
