# In-Group/Out-Group

***How belonging creates blindness***

> **Section:** Psychology & Behavior

In 1954, psychologist Muzafer Sherif took twenty-two eleven-year-old boys to Robbers Cave State Park in Oklahoma. None of them knew each other. They came from similar backgrounds—white, middle-class, Protestant families. Sherif split them into two groups and kept them separated for a week. Each group hiked, swam, and played baseball. They picked names: the Rattlers and the Eagles. They designed flags.

Then Sherif introduced the groups to each other through competitive games. Within days, the boys were burning each other's flags, raiding cabins, and refusing to eat in the same room. They called each other names. They stockpiled weapons—rocks and sticks. The staff had to physically separate them.

The boys weren't naturally aggressive. The groups weren't based on real differences. Sherif had simply created an arbitrary boundary—us and them—and let competition do the rest.

This is in-group/out-group thinking. The moment we identify with a group, we begin to see the world through its lens. Members of our group become "us"—more trustworthy, more intelligent, more deserving. Everyone else becomes "them"—less relatable, less credible, less important.

The mechanism is automatic. We don't decide to favor our group. Our brains do it for us. Studies show that even when groups are formed randomly—by coin flip or the color of a shirt—people immediately start preferring their own group members. They rate them as smarter, funnier, more moral. They remember their successes more than their failures. They interpret ambiguous actions charitably for insiders, suspiciously for outsiders.

This isn't conscious prejudice. It's perceptual warping. The group membership changes what we see and how we see it.

In-group thinking serves a purpose. Throughout human history, survival depended on cooperation within groups. The people who shared your resources, defended your territory, and raised your children were your group. Trust within that circle was efficient. Caution toward outsiders was prudent.

But the modern world multiplies these boundaries. We form in-groups around politics, professions, neighborhoods, sports teams, online communities. Each one creates a lens. And the more important a group becomes to our identity, the stronger the distortion.

The real danger isn't that we favor our own. It's that we stop noticing we're doing it. A hiring manager sees their alma mater on a resume and feels a flash of warmth—they don't register it as bias. A journalist reads a claim from someone in their political coalition and requires less evidence—they experience it as the claim simply being more credible. A doctor trusts a diagnosis from a colleague more than the same diagnosis from a nurse—and calls it clinical judgment.

The distortion is invisible from inside.

Out-group thinking has a mirror image. We don't just favor insiders—we actively discount outsiders. We hold them to higher standards of proof. We interpret their motives as self-serving. We notice their mistakes and forget their successes. In negotiations, we assume bad faith. In debates, we hear their arguments less carefully. Not because they're wrong, but because they're not us.

Sherif knew this. At the end of the Robbers Cave experiment, he tried to reduce the hostility. He brought the groups together for pleasant activities—meals, movies, fireworks. It didn't work. The boys stayed hostile.

What finally worked was a superordinate goal—a problem that required both groups to cooperate. Sherif arranged for the camp's water supply to fail. Then he had the food truck "break down" on the way to a picnic. The boys had to work together to fix the problems. Not immediately, but gradually, the hostility dissolved. They started seeing each other as individuals again.

The boundary hadn't disappeared. But it had been crossed enough times that it became permeable.

You can't eliminate in-group/out-group thinking. But you can notice when it's operating. When you automatically trust someone because they share your affiliation, pause. When you automatically doubt someone because they're in another camp, check if you're applying a double standard. The group lens isn't always wrong—but it's always there.

The question is whether you see through it, or whether you mistake it for clear vision.
