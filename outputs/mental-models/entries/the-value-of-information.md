# The Value of Information

***What's worth knowing before you decide***

> **Section:** Decision Making

In 1961, Howard Raiffa stood before a room of executives and asked them to imagine drilling for oil. You've identified a promising site. The drill will cost $100,000. If there's oil, you'll make $1 million. If there isn't, you lose everything.

But before you drill, you can run a seismic test. The test isn't free — it costs $20,000. And it isn't perfect — it's right about 80% of the time.

Should you buy the test?

Most executives said yes immediately. A few said no. Raiffa asked them to show their math. None of them had any.

This is the gap between knowing information might be valuable and knowing what it's actually worth. The Value of Information principle gives you the math to close that gap.

Here's how it works: Information is valuable only if it changes what you do. If you're going to drill regardless, the seismic test is worthless — you're paying $20,000 to learn something you won't act on. If you weren't going to drill under any circumstances, same problem. The test only has value if it might flip your decision.

The formal calculation asks: What would I decide without this information? What might I decide with it? What's the expected value of each path? The difference is what the information is worth.

Back to Raiffa's oil field. Without the test, you're gambling: 50% chance of oil (let's say), so expected value of $500,000 minus the $100,000 drilling cost. That's $400,000 — assuming you decide to drill.

With the test, you get to update your decision based on new evidence. If it says "oil," you drill and your odds improve to maybe 80%. If it says "no oil," you walk away and save your $100,000. Work through the probabilities, and the test is worth something like $35,000. You'd pay $20,000 for it gladly.

But change the scenario. Say the test costs $50,000, or your initial odds are already 90%, or you're drilling no matter what because you're contractually obligated. Suddenly that same test is worth far less, possibly nothing.

This framework clarifies decisions most people make on instinct alone:

Should you pay for a home inspection? Only if there's a realistic chance it reveals something that would change whether you buy.

Should you run another focus group? Only if it might tell you something that would change the product design.

Should you wait for more data before launching? Only if that data could actually shift your decision, not just make you feel better about the decision you've already made.

The last case is the most common trap. People collect information for comfort, not decision-making. They conduct studies they won't listen to, commission reports they've already decided to ignore, and demand "more research" as a way to delay action. None of this is value creation. It's procrastination with footnotes.

The principle also reveals when to stop gathering information. You keep researching until the cost of the next piece of information exceeds its expected value. This sounds obvious but is routinely violated. Companies spend millions on market research that won't change their strategy. Individuals agonize over small decisions, reading endless reviews for products that are functionally identical. The information they're gathering has negative value — it costs more in time and money than it could possibly return in better outcomes.

One warning: This model assumes you'll actually use the information. In practice, people often buy information and then ignore it because it contradicts what they want to believe. If you're going to override your data anyway, don't buy the data. You're just paying to feel rational.

The value of information is not what you learn. It's what you do differently because you learned it.
