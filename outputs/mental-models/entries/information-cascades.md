# Information Cascades

***When following the crowd becomes rational, even when the crowd is wrong***

> **Section:** Social Dynamics

In 1991, two economists walked into restaurants in Manhattan's SoHo district and watched people choose where to eat. They weren't looking at menus or prices. They were watching something simpler: who was watching whom.

They noticed a pattern. A person walking down the street would glance at two similar restaurants. One was empty. One had a few diners visible through the window. The person would choose the one with diners, even though they had no information about the food quality, no reviews, no recommendation. They were making a choice based entirely on the choices of strangers.

More interesting: the next person did the same thing. And the next. Within an hour, one restaurant would be filling while the other stayed empty. The difference between success and failure had nothing to do with the food. It came down to which restaurant happened to seat its first customers near the window.

This is an information cascade. It happens when people abandon their private information and follow the behavior of others, creating a chain reaction where everyone copies everyone else.

The mechanism is straightforward. You're deciding between two products. You don't know much about either. You notice that previous customers chose Product A more often than Product B. This is information—other people saw something you didn't. Following them is rational. They probably knew something.

Except they didn't. They were following the people before them, who were following the people before *them*. The cascade started with someone's random choice, or a choice based on information that was only slightly better than random. Everyone after that assumed the crowd knew something. The crowd assumed the same thing. Nobody actually knew.

Information cascades explain sudden fashion shifts, bank runs, political movements that materialize from nowhere. They explain why five nearly identical restaurants on the same block can have wildly different success rates. They explain why a mediocre product can dominate a market while a superior competitor withers.

The power of the cascade lies in its rationality. You are not being stupid when you follow the crowd. You are making a reasonable inference from limited information. The error is collective, not individual. Each person makes a defensible choice. The aggregate result is often absurd.

This is why early momentum matters so much. In markets with information cascades, the first few decisions carry enormous weight. A product that gets early adopters—even through luck—can trigger a cascade that makes it dominant. A restaurant that seats its first customers in the window may thrive while a better restaurant across the street fails. First impressions aren't just important; they can become self-fulfilling.

The model breaks down when people have strong private information or when they can observe outcomes directly rather than just choices. If you can taste the food before choosing the restaurant, the cascade weakens. If you have expertise in the domain, you're less likely to defer to the crowd. Information cascades are strongest when decisions are visible but outcomes are hidden.

They also reverse. A negative cascade can start as quickly as a positive one. One visible defection can trigger a stampede in the opposite direction. This is why reputation collapse often happens suddenly. For months or years, everyone assumes everyone else knows something good. Then someone publicly abandons the consensus. Others follow. The cascade runs backward.

The practical lesson: distrust early momentum. The first wave of adoption tells you less than you think. The crowd is often following itself in circles, mistaking its own footprints for a trail blazed by someone who knew the way.

When you see everyone moving in the same direction, ask what they actually know versus what they're inferring from everyone else moving in the same direction. Usually, it's the latter.
