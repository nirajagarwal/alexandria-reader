# The Seen and the Unseen

***Why the most important effects are often invisible***

> **Section:** Systems & Consequences

In 1850, the French economist Frédéric Bastiat watched a shopkeeper's son break a window. A small crowd gathered. Someone said, "Well, at least this is good for the glazier." Others nodded. The glazier would be paid six francs. He'd spend those francs at the baker, who'd spend them at the tailor. The broken window, they agreed, was actually good for the economy.

Bastiat saw something else. Yes, the glazier would receive six francs. But the shopkeeper had been saving those six francs to buy new shoes. Now he'd have a window but no shoes. The shoemaker had lost a sale. The economy hadn't gained anything—it had simply rearranged where the money went, while becoming one window poorer than before.

The crowd saw the glazier working. They couldn't see the shoes that would never be made.

This is the seen and the unseen: what happens is visible, but what doesn't happen because of it remains invisible. We count the jobs created by a new highway but not the businesses that fail when the old road empties. We see the industry saved by tariffs but not the consumers who pay higher prices. We notice the regulations that prevent harm but not the innovations they block.

The pattern appears everywhere. A city builds a stadium with tax dollars. The seen: construction jobs, a gleaming venue, packed games. The unseen: what those tax dollars would have built otherwise—better schools, repaired bridges, money left in taxpayers' pockets. Both exist. Only one is photographed at ribbon-cutting ceremonies.

The unseen isn't merely less obvious. It's fundamentally harder to perceive. What happens makes noise, creates headlines, generates photos. What fails to happen is silent. The restaurant that would have opened but couldn't get a permit. The medicine that would have been developed but wasn't funded. The career that would have flourished if the person hadn't taken the safe job.

Every decision has an opportunity cost—the value of the best alternative you didn't choose. But opportunity cost is entirely unseen. When you spend an hour in a meeting, you see yourself in the meeting. You don't see the report you'd have finished, the problem you'd have solved, the conversation you'd have had. The meeting feels like something. The alternative feels like nothing.

This asymmetry warps decision-making. Politicians favor policies with visible benefits and invisible costs. Managers favor projects with measurable outputs over unmeasurable prevention. Parents see the music lesson their child attends but not the unstructured play they miss. Everyone optimizes for what they can see.

The model breaks down when we become paralyzed by invisible possibilities. Every choice forecloses others. At some point you must act on available information rather than agonize over unknowable alternatives. The key is not perfect visibility—that's impossible—but conscious effort to consider what you're not seeing.

Before deciding, ask: What am I not seeing? What would happen if we did nothing? What would this money, time, or attention accomplish elsewhere? Who isn't in the room to advocate for the invisible alternative?

The most important effects are often the ones you have to deliberately imagine.
