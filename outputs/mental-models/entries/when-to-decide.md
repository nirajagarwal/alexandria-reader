# When to Decide

***The optimal moment between ignorance and irrelevance***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1943, Alan Turing faced a problem that defined modern intelligence work. Britain's codebreakers at Bletchley Park were reading German naval messages, but not fast enough. By the time a U-boat's position was decoded, the submarine had moved. Worse: if Britain acted on decoded messages too quickly or too obviously, Germany would realize their codes were broken and change them. Turing needed to decide when to use the intelligence. Too early meant insufficient information. Too late meant worthless information.

He chose a middle path. They would wait until they had enough decoded messages to predict patterns, but act before those patterns became obsolete. More importantly, they would only act when they could plausibly explain the action through other means—a "sighted" reconnaissance plane, a "lucky" depth charge pattern. The information was valuable only in a narrow window, and only if its source remained secret.

This is the essence of decision timing: the sweet spot between not knowing enough and knowing too late.

We think of decision-making as a question of what to choose. But often the harder question is when. Every decision has an information curve and an action curve. Early in any situation, you're ignorant. You don't know enough to choose well. But as time passes, the value of the decision itself decays. The job gets filled. The opportunity closes. The competitor moves first. The crisis metastasizes.

The optimal moment sits between these curves. You know enough to be better than random, but it's not too late to matter.

This creates an uncomfortable truth: you will always decide with incomplete information. The person who waits for certainty waits forever. Or more precisely, waits until the decision makes itself through inaction.

The skill is recognizing which kind of decision you're facing.

**Reversible decisions** favor speed. Jeff Bezos calls these "two-way doors"—you can walk back through if you're wrong. Here, the cost of being wrong is low and the cost of delay is high. Choose fast, learn fast, adjust fast. The startup that waits for perfect product-market fit before launching never launches.

**Irreversible decisions** favor patience. Marriage, mortgages, permanent moves. The cost of being wrong is high and the cost of waiting is relatively low. Here you gather information deliberately. You test. You simulate. You give time for your own thinking to evolve.

**Time-sensitive decisions** live between these extremes. The information curve is still climbing but the action curve is falling fast. This is Turing's situation. This is the job offer that expires. The house with other bidders. The medical diagnosis where delay matters but more tests would help.

Here, the discipline is knowing what information would actually change your decision. Not what's interesting. Not what's available. What would change your choice? If the answer is "nothing," decide now. If the answer is "something I can learn quickly," set a deadline and learn it. If the answer is "something unknowable," acknowledge the uncertainty and decide anyway.

The common failure mode is mistaking motion for progress. Gathering one more opinion. Running one more analysis. Scheduling one more meeting. This feels like good decision-making—thorough, careful, responsible. But often it's just postponement with paperwork.

The less common failure is moving too fast because patience feels like weakness. The executive who decides in the meeting to show decisiveness. The investor who jumps at the first promising deal. Speed is a virtue only when timing actually matters.

The question is not whether to decide. Time will make the decision for you if you don't. The question is whether you're in the window where deciding matters—when you know enough to be useful but not so much that it's too late to act.

Turing understood this. The decoded message had value only in that narrow span. Too early and the intelligence was fragmentary. Too late and the U-boat was gone. The art was recognizing the moment and taking it.

That moment exists in almost every consequential choice. The skill is learning to see it.
