# Cheap Talk

***When words cost nothing, they mean nothing***

> **Section:** Game Theory & Strategic Behavior

In 1987, economist Joseph Farrell published a paper that bothered people. He showed mathematically what everyone already knew but preferred not to say: most communication is worthless.

The problem isn't dishonesty exactly. It's that words, by themselves, have no inherent cost. A CEO can promise shareholders that growth is coming. A politician can swear their opponent is dangerous. A friend can insist they'll help you move. The speaker pays nothing for making the claim. When speech is free, it becomes cheap talk—communication that can be safely ignored because the speaker has no stake in its accuracy.

Farrell called this "cheap talk equilibrium." In game theory terms, it describes any situation where players communicate but their words don't bind them to action. The words might be true. They might be lies. They might be sincere but ultimately meaningless. What matters is that saying them doesn't change the speaker's payoffs.

Consider a used car salesman telling you a car has never been in an accident. Whether he's lying or telling the truth, the statement itself costs him nothing. Compare this to a warranty—a promise backed by real money. The warranty is costly to provide if the car is unreliable, which is precisely why it carries information. The cheap talk doesn't.

The model explains why diplomacy so often fails. When countries issue statements, warnings, and threats, they're engaging in cheap talk until something real is at stake. "We strongly condemn this action" means nothing when the condemning country plans to do nothing. Intelligence analysts learned during the Cold War to ignore statements and watch troop movements, military exercises, and resource allocations—the costly signals that revealed actual intentions.

Corporate communication swims in cheap talk. "We value transparency." "Customers come first." "We're committed to diversity." These statements cost nothing to make and nothing to abandon. Only when a company puts real resources behind a claim—opening its books, reshaping incentives, changing hiring practices—does the communication become credible.

Cheap talk isn't always useless. Farrell showed it can work when interests align. If we're both trying to meet for coffee and I tell you I'll be at the corner café at 3pm, you can believe me because we both want to coordinate. The talk is cheap, but it's not misleading because we have the same goal.

The problem emerges when interests diverge. A job candidate says they're a team player. An executive says the layoffs are over. A company announces a "commitment to quality." When the speaker benefits from you believing something that might not be true, cheap talk becomes noise.

This explains why actions speak louder than words. Actions have costs. Showing up to help someone move costs you a Saturday. Resigning from a board in protest costs you prestige and connections. Turning down a lucrative opportunity because it conflicts with your principles costs you money. These costly signals separate genuine commitment from empty speech.

Goodhart's Law operates as a special case of cheap talk. Once a metric becomes a target, claiming you've hit it becomes cheap talk unless verified. (See also: Goodhart's Law)

The model doesn't make us all cynics. It makes us better listeners. When someone speaks, ask: what would this cost them if it were false? If the answer is nothing, weight the words accordingly. Look instead for the costly signals, the actions that can't be faked, the commitments that bind.

The insight isn't that people lie constantly. It's that when words are free, they carry no information by themselves. Credibility requires cost.

**The thing to remember:** Trust what people do when it's expensive, not what they say when it's cheap.
