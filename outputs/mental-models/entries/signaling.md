# Signaling

***Why the costly parts matter most***

> **Section:** Incentives & Information

In 1973, economist Michael Spence won a Nobel Prize for explaining why getting a college degree might have nothing to do with what you learn in class.

He noticed something puzzling: employers paid college graduates more than high school graduates, even for jobs that didn't require any specialized knowledge from university. The degree seemed to work like magic — wave it at an employer, get paid more. But why would companies pay a premium for education that didn't teach job-specific skills?

Spence's answer: the degree itself wasn't valuable. The ability to get one was.

Finishing college signals something. It shows you can commit to a long-term goal. It shows you can navigate bureaucracy, meet deadlines, absorb information, pass tests. The content of your classes matters less than the fact that you completed them. The degree is expensive and difficult to obtain — and that's precisely what makes it valuable as a signal.

This is how signaling works. The cost is the point.

A signal is an action taken primarily to communicate information. It works because it's hard to fake. Anyone can claim to be hardworking or intelligent or reliable. But not everyone can spend four years and tens of thousands of dollars proving it.

The principle extends far beyond education. A peacock's tail is a signal. It's metabolically expensive, makes the bird more visible to predators, and serves no practical purpose for survival. That's exactly why it works. Only a healthy peacock can afford such costly ornament. The hen observes the tail and infers: this bird has good genes.

A warranty signals quality. A company that offers to replace defective products for ten years is betting its own money that the product won't fail. A company making junk can't afford that bet.

Expensive advertising signals market position. When a company spends millions on a Super Bowl commercial for a simple product, they're not just informing you the product exists. They're demonstrating they have resources to burn — which implies they have customers to burn them on.

The luxury watch, the corner office, the Ivy League degree on the wall — all signals. The person wearing or displaying them is communicating: I can afford this. And because it's costly, you believe them.

But here's where signaling gets interesting: it only works when the signal is hard to fake. The moment a signal becomes cheap enough for everyone to produce, it stops signaling anything.

This is why credentialism inflates. Once enough people have bachelor's degrees, the signal weakens. Employers start requiring master's degrees for the same positions. Not because the job changed, but because they need a more expensive signal to distinguish candidates.

The model reveals something uncomfortable: sometimes the most valuable part of an action is that it's difficult or expensive, not that it's useful. The student who learns nothing but graduates is signaling more effectively than the self-taught expert with no credentials. The company that burns money on advertising is signaling more than the one that quietly makes better products.

This is not a moral judgment. It's a description of how information flows in a world where everyone has incentive to exaggerate their qualities. When cheap talk won't do, costly signals emerge.

Look for signaling when actions seem wasteful but persist. When the expensive option is chosen over the effective one. When the hard way is valued more than the easy way. When you wonder why anyone bothers with something so inefficient.

The answer is often: because efficiency isn't the point. The cost is the message.
