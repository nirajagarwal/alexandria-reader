# Half-Life

***Why extreme outcomes soften over time***

> **Section:** Statistics & Probability

In 1906, Francis Galton published a paper that troubled him. He had measured the heights of 928 adult children and their parents. The children of very tall parents were, on average, tall — but not as tall as their parents. The children of very short parents were short — but not as short as their parents. Heights seemed to drift back toward the average.

Galton called this "regression toward mediocrity." The name stuck, though we now call it regression to the mean. But the phenomenon itself is older than statistics. It appears everywhere humans encounter variation.

A student aces one test and does worse on the next. A baseball player has a career year, then returns to form. A company posts record profits, then merely good ones. A patient tries a new treatment after their worst symptoms and improves — but would have improved anyway as their condition fluctuated.

The pattern is so common we rarely notice it. But when we do notice, we misread it. We invent stories. The student got cocky. The player lost his edge. The treatment worked. We see causation where there is only mathematics.

Here is what's actually happening: Extreme outcomes are rare because they require everything to align. Skill, yes, but also luck, timing, circumstance. When someone performs at the edge of possibility, they are likely experiencing an unusual cluster of favorable conditions. Those conditions don't persist. Not because anything changed about the person, but because extremes are, by definition, temporary deviations.

The tallest parents weren't just genetically tall. They also happened to land on the fortunate side of all the small random factors that influence height — nutrition, health, development timing. Their children inherit the genes but get a different roll of the dice on everything else. On average, they end up closer to the middle.

This is half-life thinking. After any extreme event, expect a movement back toward typical. The magnitude of the extremeness predicts the magnitude of the return. Not all the way back. Not immediately. But consistently, measurably, inevitably.

The mistake is assuming that change requires an explanation. A CEO takes over after record profits and earnings decline. She gets blamed. A new coach inherits a team after their worst season and they improve. He gets credit. But some of both outcomes would have happened anyway, pulled by the mathematics of variation.

This doesn't mean people have no impact. It means we systematically overestimate how much of an outcome is due to intervention versus natural fluctuation. The patient who recovers after trying a new supplement attributes the improvement to the supplement. Maybe it helped. But they started taking it precisely when they felt worst — the moment most likely to precede natural improvement regardless of intervention.

Where half-life thinking misleads: It can excuse real decline or dismiss genuine improvement. Not everything is regression. Sometimes the student really did stop studying. Sometimes the treatment really does work. The model tells you to be skeptical of extreme outcomes, but skepticism is not the same as certainty. You still need to investigate. You still need evidence.

The way to separate regression from real change is time and comparison. Does the pattern persist? How does it compare to a control group that experienced the same starting conditions? If the improvement (or decline) continues well past what regression would predict, you have found something real.

But most of the time, in most situations, half-life thinking is the better default. Extreme performances regress. Hot streaks cool. Today's outlier is tomorrow's closer-to-average. Not because of failure or success, but because that's what happens when randomness and skill combine.

The practical wisdom: Be wary of intervening right after extreme events. Wait to see what natural regression does first. When you succeed dramatically, expect the next attempt to disappoint slightly. When you fail spectacularly, expect some natural recovery. Don't over-praise, don't over-punish, don't over-correct.

Extreme outcomes are temporary, not because people change, but because extremes require everything to go right (or wrong) at once. That alignment is rare. Its absence is ordinary.
