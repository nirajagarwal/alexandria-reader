# Matthew Effect

***Why advantages compound and disadvantages cascade***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases & Heuristics

In 1968, sociologist Robert Merton noticed something strange about scientific credit. When multiple researchers made similar discoveries, the most famous one usually got all the recognition. Einstein's name appeared on papers alongside lesser-known collaborators, but only Einstein became a household name. The pattern repeated across disciplines: initial advantage bred further advantage.

Merton called this the Matthew Effect, after a line in the Gospel of Matthew: "For to everyone who has, more will be given, and he will have abundance; but from him who does not have, even what he has will be taken away."

The mechanism is simple. Early success creates opportunities for more success. The first-time author whose book sells well gets better promotional support for the second. The startup that raises an initial round finds the next round easier. The student who tests well early gets placed in advanced classes, which improve their scores further. Small differences in starting position amplify over time.

The effect appears everywhere. In professional hockey, players born in January, February, and March vastly outnumber those born later in the year. The reason: youth leagues use a January 1st cutoff. Kids born early in the year are older, bigger, and more coordinated than teammates born eleven months later. Coaches notice them. They get more playing time. The extra practice makes them better. By the time age differences disappear, the gap in skill has become permanent.

The same pattern holds in venture capital. Founders who went to Stanford or Harvard get meetings more easily. Those meetings lead to investments. The investments provide capital, connections, and credibility for the next round. Soon the pattern looks like evidence that elite school graduates make better founders. But the advantage was access, not ability.

The Matthew Effect operates in reverse too. One missed rent payment leads to late fees. Late fees make the next payment harder. Eventually comes eviction, which makes finding the next apartment harder, which increases housing instability. The initial disadvantage cascades.

What makes the effect powerful is that it looks earned. By the time we observe outcomes, the early advantage is invisible. We see a successful scientist, athlete, or entrepreneur and attribute everything to talent or hard work. We miss the compounding effect of an early break.

The model has limits. It explains patterns, not destinies. Plenty of January-born hockey players quit young. Plenty of people without prestigious degrees build successful companies. Initial disadvantage doesn't guarantee failure any more than initial advantage guarantees success. The Matthew Effect describes probability, not fate.

But understanding it changes how we evaluate success and failure. When someone pulls ahead, ask: what was their starting position? When someone falls behind, ask: what early setback triggered the cascade?

The effect suggests that small interventions early can have large effects later. Helping a struggling student catch up in third grade may prevent years of compounding difficulty. Giving a promising young researcher access to better equipment may accelerate their entire career. The leverage point is timing.

The next time you see extreme inequality of outcome, look for the Matthew Effect. The gap between winner and second place often traces back to a small, early difference that compounded over time. What looks like a chasm of ability may have started as a crack of circumstance.

Recognition compounds. So does obscurity. The difference often begins smaller than we think.
