# Chesterton's Fence

***Don't tear down what you don't understand***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1929, G.K. Chesterton posed a thought experiment. You come across a fence in the middle of a road. It seems pointless, an obstacle. The modern reformer says, "I don't see the use of this; let us clear it away." 

Chesterton's response: "If you don't see the use of it, I certainly won't let you clear it away. Go away and think. Then, when you can come back and tell me that you do see the use of it, I may allow you to destroy it."

The fence was built for a reason. Until you know that reason, you have no business removing it.

This appears everywhere. A company has a clunky approval process that frustrates new managers. It seems like bureaucratic waste—until you learn it exists because three years ago, a rushed contract cost the company millions. A codebase has a strange workaround that offends every programmer who sees it—until you discover it prevents a data corruption bug that took weeks to track down. A family has an odd tradition that embarrasses the younger generation—until someone explains it began after a tragedy, as a way to stay connected.

The pattern is always the same: something looks arbitrary from the outside, but it evolved to solve a problem you can't see.

The model works because systems are path-dependent. They carry the weight of past decisions, past failures, past crises. The fence isn't there by accident. It's there because at some point, someone discovered they needed it. Maybe the reason no longer applies. Maybe the fence can come down. But you can't know until you understand what it was preventing.

This is not an argument for never changing anything. Chesterton was clear: once you understand the fence's purpose, you can make an informed decision about removing it. Sometimes you discover the reason is obsolete. Sometimes you find a better solution. But "I don't understand why this exists" is not the same as "this serves no purpose."

The failure mode is obvious: refusing to change anything because "that's how we've always done it." That's not Chesterton's Fence. That's just inertia. The model requires genuine investigation. What problem was this solving? Is that problem still present? Can we solve it better?

The deeper insight: complexity often looks like stupidity. A system that has been running for years accumulates patches, exceptions, workarounds. From the outside, it looks like a mess. But each piece may be load-bearing. The only way to know is to ask why it's there—and keep asking until you get a real answer.

The fence is still standing because someone thought it should be. Find out who, and why, before you reach for the sledgehammer.
