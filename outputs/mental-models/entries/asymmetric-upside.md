# Asymmetric Upside

***When potential gains dwarf potential losses***

> **Section:** Risk and Reward

In 1997, Jeff Bezos wrote a letter to Amazon's shareholders. The company had just gone public, and most analysts thought he was insane. Amazon was losing money on every book it sold. The more it grew, the more it bled. Bezos wrote that he was optimizing for the long term, not quarterly earnings. He was willing to accept certain failure in the short term for uncertain success in the long term.

The math was simple: Amazon could lose only what it invested. But if it became the default platform for online retail? The upside was unlimited.

This is asymmetric upside. The situation where your maximum loss is capped but your maximum gain is open-ended. You can lose 100% at most. You can gain 1,000%. Or 10,000%. Or more.

The model shows up everywhere once you see it. A job interview costs you an afternoon. Getting hired might change your career. A conversation with a stranger costs you ten minutes. It might lead to a friendship, a business, or just ten minutes. Writing an essay costs you a few hours. It might reach no one or reach millions.

Venture capitalists live in asymmetric upside. They know most investments will fail. They build portfolios accordingly. If nine companies go to zero but one becomes Google, the fund returns exceed everything. The losses are bounded. The wins compound.

Nassim Taleb calls this "optionality." You pay a small, fixed price for the chance of unbounded reward. An option to buy stock at a set price has limited downside (the cost of the option) and theoretically infinite upside. You're not trying to predict the future. You're buying tickets to good surprises.

The opposite is asymmetric downside: capped gains, unlimited losses. Writing naked call options. Driving without insurance. Building on a floodplain. The upside is small and certain. The downside is catastrophic and uncertain.

Most people avoid asymmetric upside because they focus on the probability of failure, not the payoff structure. Yes, the new project will probably fail. Yes, the cold email will probably go unanswered. But when the downside is capped and the upside isn't, probability matters less than you think.

Where the model misleads: Not all uncapped upside is worth pursuing. You still need some minimum probability of success, or you're just buying lottery tickets. And "capped downside" can be deceptive. The cost might be small in dollars but large in time, attention, or opportunity. Starting ten businesses with capped financial risk but uncapped time commitment isn't asymmetric upside—your time is being shredded.

Also, payoffs must be truly uncapped. Writing a book has asymmetric upside only if the book can actually reach a large audience. Many systems pretend to offer uncapped rewards but have hidden ceilings.

The real insight is behavioral. Most people do the opposite. They seek comfortable situations with small, predictable gains and ignore rare, catastrophic losses. They optimize for looking smart in the short term. They avoid projects that will probably fail, even when the payoff from a single success would outweigh a dozen failures.

Asymmetric upside is the logic behind "take more shots." But it's not about quantity alone. It's about recognizing situations where the structure itself favors you. Where you pay retail for downside and get wholesale prices on upside.

Bezos wasn't predicting Amazon's success. He was building a company where success, if it came, would be exponential, and failure, if it came, would be finite. That's the trade worth making.

Look for bets you can afford to lose but can't afford not to make.
