# Adverse Selection

***Why the people who want the deal most are often the worst bets***

> **Section:** Information Asymmetry

In 1970, economist George Akerlof published a paper about used cars that would win him a Nobel Prize. The puzzle was simple: Why are used car markets so dysfunctional? Why do buyers assume every seller is hiding something?

The answer reveals a pattern that plays out everywhere information is unequal.

Imagine you're selling a used car. You know everything about it—every repair, every quirk, every warning sign. The buyer knows almost nothing. They can kick the tires and check the oil, but they can't see what you see.

Now suppose used cars fall into two categories: good ones and lemons. Good cars are worth $8,000. Lemons are worth $4,000. But buyers can't tell which is which before they buy.

If buyers assume a 50/50 chance of getting a lemon, they'll offer around $6,000—the average of $8,000 and $4,000. That's rational given their uncertainty.

But look what happens. If you own a good car worth $8,000, you won't sell for $6,000. You'll keep it or wait for a better offer. If you own a lemon worth $4,000, you'll happily take $6,000. It's a great deal for you.

So who shows up to sell? Overwhelmingly, the lemon owners.

Buyers notice this. They adjust their expectations down. Maybe they'll only pay $5,000. But that drives out even more good cars. The market spirals. Eventually, only lemons get sold, and buyers who might have paid fair prices for good cars simply exit the market.

This is adverse selection: when you can't tell good from bad, the bad drives out the good. The very act of offering to transact reveals information—and usually, it's bad news.

The used car market was just the beginning. The pattern appears anywhere one party knows more than the other.

Insurance companies face this constantly. People who know they're high-risk—chronic health issues, dangerous hobbies, risky driving habits—are more motivated to buy insurance than low-risk people. The insurer can't fully distinguish between them, so they set prices based on averages. But those average prices are too high for the healthy and too low for the sick. The healthy drop out. Premiums rise. More healthy people drop out. The insurance pool fills with exactly the people the company can't afford to cover.

Employers see it when hiring. The candidates most eager to accept any offer are often the ones with the fewest alternatives. The best candidates have options and walk away from lowball offers. If you only hire people desperate enough to say yes immediately, you've selected for desperation, not talent.

Even dating markets show this pattern. People still available late in life aren't necessarily worse partners—but the ones actively seeking any relationship, willing to overlook any incompatibility, might be revealing something about why previous relationships failed.

The model explains why so many markets develop elaborate screening mechanisms. Insurance companies require medical exams. Employers run background checks and interviews. Colleges demand transcripts and test scores. These aren't just bureaucracy—they're attempts to break the information asymmetry that causes adverse selection.

It also explains why credible signals matter so much. A car dealer offering a warranty is saying "I'm not selling a lemon." A job candidate taking a pay cut to join a startup is signaling confidence in the company. These costly signals separate good from bad because they're expensive to fake.

But screening and signaling are imperfect. They add friction and cost. Some excellent candidates can't afford to signal properly. Some lemons learn to game the signals. The fundamental problem—one party knows more than the other—never fully disappears.

The model breaks down when information becomes symmetric. If both parties have equal knowledge, adverse selection vanishes. This is why some markets invest heavily in transparency and disclosure. It's also why reputation systems exist—they convert private information into public knowledge over time.

The key insight: when you can't observe quality directly, the willingness to transact becomes information itself. And often, it's the wrong information. The people most eager to sell you something, to join your company, to enter your market, are disproportionately the ones you'd prefer to avoid.

The question isn't whether adverse selection exists in a market. The question is what signals and screens can overcome it—and whether those mechanisms work, or just create new ways to pretend.
