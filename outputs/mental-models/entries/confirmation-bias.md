# Confirmation Bias

***Why we see what we expect to see***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases

In 1960, psychologist Peter Wason gave subjects a simple sequence: 2, 4, 6. Their task was to discover the rule generating it. They could propose their own sequences, and Wason would tell them whether each fit the rule. Most subjects generated sequences like 8, 10, 12 or 20, 22, 24—and confidently announced the rule was "numbers increasing by two."

They were wrong. The actual rule was simply "any ascending numbers." The sequence 1, 2, 3 would have worked. So would 5, 17, 432. But almost no one tested these. They only tested sequences that confirmed what they already believed.

This is confirmation bias in its purest form: we seek evidence that supports our beliefs and ignore evidence that contradicts them. Once we form a hypothesis about how the world works, we become lawyers for that hypothesis rather than scientists testing it.

The mechanism is subtle. We don't consciously reject contradictory evidence—we simply don't look for it. A manager who believes a employee is incompetent notices every mistake and dismisses every success as luck. A investor convinced a stock will rise reads bullish analysis and skips bearish reports. A doctor with an initial diagnosis orders tests that would confirm it, not tests that would rule it out.

The bias operates in what we search for, what we notice, how we interpret ambiguity, and what we remember. If you believe vaccines are dangerous, you'll remember news stories about adverse reactions and forget the millions of safe vaccinations. If you believe your city is declining, you'll notice the closed storefronts and miss the new businesses opening. The world provides endless data. Confirmation bias is the filter that selects which data reaches your consciousness.

It's especially powerful because it feels like objectivity. The manager genuinely believes they're evaluating fairly—they're simply reporting what they observe. The investor thinks they're following the data. The doctor is practicing evidence-based medicine. None of them realize they're working with a biased sample of evidence.

This is why first impressions are so durable and conspiracy theories are so sticky. The initial belief shapes what evidence you encounter next, which reinforces the belief, which shapes what evidence you encounter after that. Each cycle makes the belief feel more solid while the foundation remains unchanged.

The antidote is not trying harder to be objective. The antidote is deliberately seeking disconfirming evidence. Wason's subjects who discovered the rule were those who tested sequences they thought would *fail*—and were surprised when they didn't. The manager needs to look for evidence the employee is competent. The investor needs to read the bear case. The doctor needs to ask, "What else could cause these symptoms?"

This feels unnatural. We experience the search for disconfirming evidence as arguing against ourselves. And in a sense, it is. But it's also the only way to discover whether our beliefs reflect reality or simply reflect themselves.

The physicist Richard Feynman described science as "bending over backwards to prove ourselves wrong." That's the standard. Not because we enjoy being wrong, but because finding out we're wrong is the only way to become less wrong.

Confirmation bias doesn't make us stupid. It makes us human. But recognizing it—and actively fighting it—is what makes us clear-thinking.
