# Law of Large Numbers

***Why flukes fade and patterns emerge***

> **Section:** Probability & Statistics

In 1689, Jacob Bernoulli discovered something that should have been obvious but wasn't: if you flip a coin long enough, the ratio of heads to tails will get closer and closer to 50-50. Not because the coin "remembers" previous flips and corrects itself. But because with more flips, the early flukes matter less.

He worked on this proof for twenty years. It appeared in *Ars Conjectandi*, published eight years after his death. His nephew called it "the golden theorem."

It's golden because it explains why insurance companies make money, why casinos always win, and why your friend who picked ten stocks that all went up is probably not a genius.

The Law of Large Numbers says that as the number of trials increases, the average of the results converges toward the expected value. The short-term is chaos. The long-term is pattern.

Consider life insurance. An actuary cannot predict when you will die. But given ten thousand people your age and health, they can predict with remarkable precision how many will die this year. One person is a mystery. Ten thousand is math.

This is why casinos don't fear lucky gamblers. Over a hundred hands, someone might win big at blackjack. The house edge is small—maybe 0.5%. But over ten million hands across all tables, that 0.5% becomes a mountain of profit. The casino doesn't gamble. It waits.

The law operates everywhere humans face randomness:

A restaurant has a few terrible weeks. The owner panics. But over a year, customer traffic regresses toward the mean. The flukes—a storm, a viral bad review, a competing restaurant's grand opening—wash out.

A new manager's team performs brilliantly for three months. Leadership calls her a high performer. But was it skill or variance? The Law of Large Numbers says: wait. If it's skill, the pattern holds. If it's luck, it fades.

A startup investor picks five companies. Four fail, one succeeds spectacularly. Is she skilled? Maybe. Or maybe she made fifty investments over ten years, and the Law of Large Numbers made her track record look more ordinary than that one year suggested.

The law reveals something uncomfortable: small samples lie. They lie in both directions—spectacular success and spectacular failure. The truth emerges only with repetition.

But here's where the model misleads:

It requires identically distributed events. The coin must be the same coin. If you flip a coin, then a weighted coin, then roll a die, the law doesn't apply. You're not accumulating evidence about one phenomenon.

Many things we care about don't have stable probabilities. Companies change. Industries evolve. A restaurant's "true" customer traffic isn't fixed—it shifts with competition, trends, reputation. The Law of Large Numbers assumes you're measuring the same thing repeatedly. Often, you're not.

It also doesn't tell you how large "large" is. Ten flips? A hundred? A million? The convergence happens, but the speed varies. A rare event might require enormous samples before patterns stabilize.

And crucially: the law speaks about averages, not individual outcomes. Even after a million flips, you can still get ten heads in a row. The law says nothing about sequences, only about long-run proportions.

The most dangerous misunderstanding is thinking the law implies correction—that after several heads, tails becomes "due." This is the gambler's fallacy. The law doesn't work backward. Past results don't make future results more likely to balance out. The balancing happens because the past becomes less important as the sample grows.

The real insight: small samples are dominated by noise. Large samples reveal signal. If you want to know what's actually happening, you need more data than you think. And if someone's making bold claims based on three examples, you're watching noise pretend to be signal.

One fluky restaurant week is chaos. One fluky year is a clue. One fluky decade is who you are.
