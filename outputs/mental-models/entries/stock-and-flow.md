# Stock and Flow

***Why bathtubs, bank accounts, and traffic jams follow the same logic***

> **Section:** Systems Thinking

In 1956, Jay Forrester sat in a meeting at General Electric, listening to managers argue about a factory's inventory problem. Orders were backlogged. The warehouse was full. Production lines ran at maximum capacity one month, then sat idle the next. No one could explain why.

Forrester, an engineer turned systems thinker, drew a diagram on the board. Not a flowchart of decisions, but something simpler: boxes and pipes. The box was inventory. The pipes were production flowing in, orders flowing out. When orders exceeded production, inventory dropped. When production exceeded orders, inventory rose. The chaos wasn't mysterious—it was mathematics.

"You're trying to control the level by adjusting the flows," he said. "But flows take time to change, and the level keeps moving while you wait."

This insight—that the world is made of stocks and flows—became the foundation of system dynamics, a field that now models everything from climate change to corporate strategy.

## The Model

A **stock** is an accumulation. It's the thing you can count at any moment: water in a bathtub, money in a bank account, knowledge in your head, cars on a highway, carbon in the atmosphere.

A **flow** is a rate of change. It's what moves into or out of the stock over time: water from the faucet and down the drain, deposits and withdrawals, learning and forgetting, traffic entering and exiting, emissions and absorption.

The stock changes only when the flows are unbalanced. If inflow exceeds outflow, the stock rises. If outflow exceeds inflow, it falls. If they match, the stock holds steady—a state called equilibrium.

This seems obvious when you first hear it. Of course a bathtub fills when the faucet runs faster than the drain. But most people, most of the time, think only about the stock. They look at their bank balance, not their spending rate. They worry about traffic density, not the rate cars enter the highway. They focus on the level, forgetting that the level is just history—flows determine the future.

The dangerous part: flows can be hard to see. A savings account might look healthy while spending quietly exceeds income. A company's revenue might appear strong while customer acquisition slows and churn accelerates. A lake might seem full while pollution flows in faster than natural processes can clean it.

And flows have momentum. You can't change them instantly. Even after Forrester's insight, GE's managers couldn't snap their fingers and sync production with demand. Factories take months to ramp up or down. Orders arrive in waves. The lag between decision and effect means the stock keeps moving in the wrong direction even after you've started fixing the flow.

## Where It Misleads

The model tempts you to treat everything as a stock-and-flow problem, but not everything accumulates predictably. Stocks can evaporate suddenly—trust, reputation, relevance. Flows can reverse without warning. And some stocks have tipping points where small changes in flow trigger massive shifts in behavior.

The bathtub metaphor also makes systems seem simpler than they are. Real systems have multiple stocks feeding into each other, feedback loops where stocks influence their own flows, and delays that make cause and effect hard to trace. Forrester's diagram was a starting point, not the whole picture.

## The Takeaway

When you see a problem, ask: What's the stock? What are the flows? Are you trying to change the level or the rate? Most confusion comes from mixing the two. Most solutions fail because they ignore lag. The stock is a photograph. The flows are a movie. If you want to predict where things are going, watch the movie.
