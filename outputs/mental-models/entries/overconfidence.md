# Overconfidence

***Why we're certain about things we barely understand***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases & Heuristics

In 1995, a man named McArthur Wheeler walked into two Pittsburgh banks in broad daylight and robbed them. He wore no mask. He made no attempt to hide his face from security cameras. When police arrested him later that night, he was genuinely shocked. "But I wore the juice," he said.

Wheeler had rubbed lemon juice on his face before the robberies. He believed that because lemon juice can be used as invisible ink, it would render his face invisible to cameras. He had even tested this theory by taking a selfie with a Polaroid camera. The photo was blurry—probably because he got juice in his eyes and held the camera poorly—and he took this as confirmation.

The case inspired psychologists David Dunning and Justin Kruger to study a phenomenon they'd noticed: people who lack competence in an area also lack the ability to recognize their own incompetence. Their experiments showed a consistent pattern. People in the bottom quartile of performance on tests of logic, grammar, and humor rated themselves as above average. The gap between their actual and perceived ability was largest for those who performed worst.

This is overconfidence in its purest form: a systematic tendency to overestimate our knowledge, abilities, and the precision of our beliefs. It appears across domains. Entrepreneurs overestimate their chances of success. Drivers rate themselves as above average. Doctors misdiagnose with more certainty than accuracy warrants. Students predict they'll finish assignments earlier than they do.

The mechanism works in both directions. Not only do we overestimate what we know, we also underestimate what we don't know. Ask someone to provide a 90% confidence interval for a quantity they're uncertain about—the length of the Nile River, the number of countries in Africa—and they'll give a range. Their answer falls outside that range about 40% of the time. Their 90% confidence interval performs like a 60% confidence interval.

This matters because decisions require calibration. If you're 80% confident about something you should be 50% confident about, you'll underinvest in alternatives and contingencies. You'll take risks you shouldn't take and skip preparations you need. The entrepreneur who thinks there's a 90% chance of success when the base rate is 10% will mortgage their house.

Overconfidence compounds with expertise in unexpected ways. Genuine experts tend toward better calibration, but only in the narrow domains where they have deep experience and fast feedback. A weather forecaster who sees the results of hundreds of predictions learns to calibrate. But take that same person out of their domain, and the pattern reverses. Experts often become more overconfident than novices because their knowledge in one area creates an illusion of broader competence.

The hardest cases involve complex predictions with delayed feedback. How will this market evolve? Will this product succeed? Here, even smart people with relevant experience show poor calibration. The feedback is too slow and too noisy to correct the bias. You can be wrong for years before you know it.

The corrective isn't eliminating confidence—you need some to act—but developing calibration. Track your predictions and their outcomes. When you say you're 70% sure, you should be right about 70% of the time. Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Ask not just "Why might I be right?" but "What would I need to believe to be wrong?" Distinguish between justified confidence backed by evidence and feedback, and the free-floating certainty that comes from not knowing what you don't know.

McArthur Wheeler's mistake was extreme, but the mechanism is universal. We all wear invisible juice sometimes. The question is whether we can notice it before the cameras do.
