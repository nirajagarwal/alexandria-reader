# Mean Reversion

***Why extreme outcomes soften over time***

> **Section:** Statistics & Probability

In 1886, Francis Galton measured the heights of 928 adult children and their parents. He expected tall parents to have tall children, short parents to have short children — heredity following a straight line. Instead, he found something stranger. The children of very tall parents were tall, yes, but not quite as tall as their parents. The children of very short parents were short, but not quite as short. The extremes softened. Galton called this "regression toward mediocrity." We now call it mean reversion, and it explains far more than height.

The pattern appears everywhere. A baseball player hits .350 one season — far above the league average — then drops to .290 the next. A fund manager beats the market three years running, then underperforms. A student aces one exam, then scores closer to their usual performance on the next. This isn't failure. It's statistics.

Extreme outcomes almost always contain two components: skill and luck. The .350 batting average reflects genuine ability plus fortunate timing, favorable matchups, balls that happened to find gaps. When luck regresses — as it must, being random — performance moves back toward the mean. The player didn't get worse. The universe stopped being unusually kind.

Mean reversion operates as a mathematical principle, not a mystical force. Think of it as probability correcting itself. If you flip a coin ten times and get eight heads, the next ten flips won't give you two heads to "balance things out." Each flip remains 50/50. But over time, as more flips accumulate, the overall ratio drifts back toward half heads, half tails. Not because the coin remembers. Because that's where the math lives.

This matters for how we judge performance. After a spectacularly good quarter, we praise the executive's new strategy. After a terrible quarter, we demand explanations and changes. But if the good quarter was partly luck running hot and the bad quarter was luck running cold, we're reacting to noise. The executive's actual skill level might not have changed at all. We're seeing mean reversion and calling it competence or incompetence.

The trap deepens with intervention. A struggling sales team gets intensive coaching. Performance improves. The coaching worked, right? Maybe. But if the team was selected for coaching *because* they were having an unusually bad stretch, some improvement was inevitable through mean reversion alone. The same effect appears in medicine. Patients seek treatment when symptoms are at their worst — which is often precisely when they're about to improve naturally. The treatment gets credit for what time would have delivered anyway.

Where mean reversion misleads most dangerously is in assuming it applies everywhere. Markets don't always revert to historical means. A declining industry doesn't bounce back just because it fell far. A relationship doesn't improve merely because it hit rock bottom. Mean reversion operates where randomness dominates outcomes. Where structural forces are at work — shifts in technology, changes in fundamentals, genuine decline — regression has no reason to appear.

The distinction matters. If a company's earnings collapsed because of temporary supply chain disruptions, mean reversion suggests recovery. If earnings collapsed because their product became obsolete, mean reversion promises nothing. The first involves luck. The second involves reality.

Recognizing mean reversion changes how you respond to extremes. That brilliant first draft you wrote? The next one probably won't feel as effortless. That terrible presentation you gave? The next one will likely go better. Not because you learned some profound lesson in between, but because extreme outcomes rarely repeat. The universe is returning you to your baseline.

The wisdom is in not overreacting. Don't rebuild your strategy after one exceptional quarter. Don't abandon your approach after one disastrous day. Give randomness time to even out. Let the mean reveal itself through repetition, not individual results.

Galton saw this in those children's heights and recognized something profound: we inherit not our parents' exact measurements but their distance from average, diluted by probability. The principle extends beyond heredity. Every extreme result you experience carries the seeds of its own moderation. Not always. Not inevitably. But often enough that betting on persistence means betting against mathematics.

When you see an outlier, expect regression. When someone performs brilliantly once, don't assume brilliance as the new baseline. When things go exceptionally wrong, don't assume catastrophe as the new normal. The extremes are lying to you, or at least exaggerating. The truth lives somewhere closer to the middle, waiting for enough time to reveal itself.
