# Path Dependence

***Why early choices constrain later possibilities***

> **Section:** Systems & Feedback

In 1872, the Boston and Albany Railroad needed to lay new track. Engineers chose a gauge — the distance between rails — of 4 feet 8.5 inches. Not because this was optimal. Because it matched the gauge of the railroads they were connecting to. Those railroads had chosen it because it matched English rail gauges. And England? Their early rail builders used the same jigs and tools that made horse-drawn tramways. The tramways used that width because it fit the wagon ruts in English roads. The ruts were carved by Roman war chariots, which were sized to accommodate two horses side by side.

By the time American engineers had the technology and knowledge to optimize track gauge from first principles, the choice was already made. Switching would mean rebuilding every train, every station platform, every piece of rolling stock. The path chosen centuries earlier by Roman chariot makers constrained what was possible for 19th-century railroad engineers.

This is path dependence: when early decisions, even arbitrary ones, lock in trajectories that become harder to escape over time.

The mechanism is simple. Each choice builds infrastructure — physical, social, or psychological. That infrastructure makes certain next steps easier and others harder. Choose a programming language for your startup, and every library, every developer hire, every integration depends on it. Leave it and you rewrite everything. Choose a degree and your next job likely relates to it. That job shapes your network, which shapes your next opportunity. Choose a city and your relationships form there, your routines adapt, your identity shifts to fit.

The path creates switching costs.

Path dependence explains why inferior solutions persist. The QWERTY keyboard was designed in 1873 to slow typists down — fast typing jammed mechanical typewriters. Once millions learned QWERTY and manufacturers standardized on it, switching to a faster layout (Dvorak, Colemak) never happened at scale. Not because QWERTY was better. Because the installed base made it too costly to change.

It explains why organizations calcify. Early founders set culture and process. Those attract certain people and repel others. The people hired reinforce the culture. Decades later, executives wonder why "that's how we've always done it" is the answer to every question. The path chosen at founding still determines what's possible.

But path dependence is not determinism. Paths can be escaped — with effort and cost proportional to how far down the path you've traveled. Programming languages can be changed if you're small enough or the benefit large enough. Dvorak users exist. Companies pivot. People retrain.

The question is not whether change is possible, but whether the benefit justifies the accumulated switching costs.

Where path dependence misleads: it can become an excuse for inaction. "We're locked in" is sometimes true and sometimes learned helplessness. The railroad gauge was genuinely locked in by 1872. Your career at thirty is not. Paths constrain but rarely eliminate options. The mistake is treating all path dependence as equally binding.

The deeper insight is timing. The earlier you are on a path, the cheaper it is to switch. Ten lines of code can be rewritten. Ten thousand cannot. A one-year relationship can redirect. A ten-year marriage cannot without upheaval. This makes early choices surprisingly important, even when you lack the information to make them well. Because they're reversible then and irreversible later.

Path dependence is why your first hires matter more than your tenth. Why your first principles matter more than your tenth iteration. Why the name you choose at birth — though you could change it — you probably won't.

The past doesn't just inform the present. It builds the rails the present runs on. Sometimes the rails lead where you want to go. Sometimes they lead to a place that made sense once but doesn't now. And sometimes you're moving so fast you don't realize you're on rails at all until you try to turn.

The question is not whether you're on a path. You are. The question is whether you've chosen to stay on it or simply never asked if another path exists.
