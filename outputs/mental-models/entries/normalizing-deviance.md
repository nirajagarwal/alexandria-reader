# Normalizing Deviance

***When the unacceptable becomes routine***

> **Section:** Systems and Failure

On January 28, 1986, the space shuttle Challenger broke apart seventy-three seconds after launch. Seven astronauts died. The cause was a failed O-ring seal in the right solid rocket booster. Engineers had warned about this exact failure mode. The cold weather that morning made the risk acute. But the launch proceeded anyway.

The problem wasn't that no one knew about the O-rings. It was that everyone had gotten used to them failing.

For years before Challenger, O-rings had shown damage after flights. Not catastrophic damage—just erosion, charring, blow-by. Each time, engineers investigated. Each time, they concluded the damage was within acceptable limits. Each time, the shuttle flew again successfully. The definition of "acceptable" quietly expanded. What once would have grounded the fleet became routine. The deviance normalized.

This is how systems fail. Not through a single catastrophic error, but through the slow accumulation of small compromises. A procedure gets skipped once because you're running late. Nothing bad happens. It gets skipped again. Eventually, the shortcut becomes the standard. The exception becomes the rule. The line between acceptable and unacceptable blurs, then disappears.

Normalizing deviance works through repetition and recency. Each time you get away with something, your brain recalibrates risk. The worst-case scenario hasn't happened yet, so maybe it won't. Success becomes evidence of safety rather than luck. Memory of the original standard fades. New people join who have only ever known the deviant behavior. They assume it must be fine—everyone does it this way.

The model explains why hospitals develop antibiotic-resistant infections, why financial institutions take on excessive leverage, why teams ship software with known bugs. The first violation feels significant. The tenth barely registers. By the hundredth, questioning it seems naive.

Organizations are particularly vulnerable. Individual caution gets worn down by collective habit. Speaking up means claiming that what everyone else accepts is actually dangerous. The person raising the alarm sounds alarmist. The system keeps running. Nothing breaks. Until it does.

The O-rings had failed safely nineteen times before Challenger. That track record became the argument for launching. The deviance had normalized so thoroughly that the absence of disaster was taken as proof the design was adequate.

Where the model misleads: Not every loosening of standards represents dangerous drift. Sometimes rules are genuinely overcautious. Sometimes shortcuts are legitimate optimizations discovered through experience. The challenge is distinguishing beneficial adaptation from creeping risk.

The key is the gap between stated principles and actual practice. When you say safety is non-negotiable but routinely negotiate it, deviance is normalizing. When the question shifts from "Is this safe?" to "Can we get away with it?", the standard has moved.

The takeaway: Watch what becomes routine. The dangerous moment isn't when someone proposes breaking the rules. It's when no one notices they're already broken.
