# Transaction Costs

***The hidden friction in every exchange***

> **Section:** Economics & Systems

In 1991, Ronald Coase won the Nobel Prize in Economics for asking a question that sounds almost naive: Why do companies exist?

Classical economics said markets were efficient. If you needed something done, you could hire someone to do it. Need a report written? Hire a writer. Need a logo designed? Hire a designer. Every task could be outsourced to whoever did it best and cheapest. So why did people form companies—stable organizations where employees did many tasks under one roof—instead of just contracting everything through the market?

Coase's answer was simple but profound: Because using markets isn't free.

Every market transaction has costs beyond the price paid. You have to search for the right person. Negotiate terms. Write a contract. Monitor the work. Enforce the agreement if something goes wrong. These transaction costs can exceed the value of the exchange itself. Sometimes it's cheaper to hire someone permanently than to keep negotiating with freelancers.

This insight—that friction exists in every exchange—applies far beyond business.

**The Model**

Transaction costs are everything required to complete an exchange beyond the direct price. They include:

- **Search costs:** Finding the right option (researching products, vetting candidates, comparing prices)
- **Negotiation costs:** Agreeing on terms (discussing price, setting expectations, writing contracts)
- **Monitoring costs:** Ensuring compliance (checking quality, tracking progress, managing performance)
- **Enforcement costs:** Handling disputes (legal fees, customer service, returns and refunds)

These costs explain why you don't change banks every time another offers 0.1% higher interest. Why you reorder from the same restaurant instead of researching every option. Why long-term relationships persist even when better alternatives theoretically exist. The cost of switching exceeds the benefit.

Transaction costs shape entire systems. They determine which activities happen inside organizations versus in markets. They explain why some industries consolidate while others fragment. They predict when technology will disrupt existing arrangements—usually when it dramatically lowers transaction costs.

Consider Uber. It didn't succeed because it invented ride-sharing. It succeeded because it eliminated most of the transaction costs in getting a ride. Before: search for a taxi (maybe call multiple companies), negotiate where to go before they'd agree to take you, worry about payment, have no recourse if something went wrong. After: tap, ride, automatic payment, rating system. The transaction cost of getting a ride dropped by 90%.

**Where It Misleads**

Not all friction is transaction cost. Some friction serves a purpose. The three-day waiting period for gun purchases is intentional delay, not inefficiency. The difficulty of canceling a gym membership is deliberate, not accidental. These aren't transaction costs to eliminate—they're features someone designed.

Transaction costs also aren't always bad. Sometimes friction protects. The effort required to change jobs prevents impulsive career moves. The difficulty of getting divorced gives people time to reconsider. The paperwork around lending money creates accountability. Some costs that look wasteful actually prevent worse outcomes.

The model can also obscure power dynamics. "High transaction costs" might mean "we made it deliberately hard for you to leave." "Reducing friction" might mean "we're removing safeguards." Lower isn't always better.

**The Takeaway**

Every exchange has hidden costs beyond the price tag. These costs shape which exchanges happen and which don't, which institutions emerge and which fade. When you wonder why markets work the way they do, look for the friction. When you want to change behavior, change the cost of transacting. The visible price is just the beginning.
