# Conservation Laws

***What stays constant reveals what matters***

> **Section:** Systems Thinking

In 1915, Emmy Noether proved something that changed physics forever. She showed that every symmetry in nature corresponds to a conservation law. If the laws of physics don't change over time, energy is conserved. If they're the same everywhere in space, momentum is conserved. The deepest truths about the universe, she revealed, are about what *doesn't* change.

Conservation laws tell us that in any closed system, certain quantities remain constant even as everything else shifts. Energy transforms from kinetic to potential, from chemical to thermal, but the total never changes. Mass converts to energy and back again, but the sum persists. Momentum transfers between objects, but the system's total stays fixed.

This matters far beyond physics.

Every system—economic, biological, social—has its own conservation laws. They're not always obvious, but finding them reveals the system's true structure. In a closed economy, one person's spending is another's income. In an ecosystem, energy flows through trophic levels but the total captured from the sun is bounded. In an organization, adding a role in one place means less attention or resources somewhere else.

The conservation law forces a question: If this quantity is fixed, where is it coming from? When a startup promises "move fast and break things," what's being traded? Usually: long-term stability for short-term speed, careful consideration for quick decisions. The total risk hasn't disappeared—it's been transferred to the future. When a government prints money, purchasing power isn't created—it's redistributed from existing holders to new ones. When you add a feature to software, complexity doesn't vanish—it shows up somewhere, in the code or the interface or the user's mental model.

The power of conservation thinking is in what it rules out. Perpetual motion machines violate conservation of energy—that's how we know they're impossible. Pyramid schemes violate conservation of value—wealth isn't created from nothing, so someone must lose. Any proposal that seems to create something from nothing is actually moving it, hiding it, or borrowing from the future.

This applies to personal resources too. Time and attention are conserved quantities. Every yes is a no to something else. Every new commitment draws from the same fixed pool. People who seem to "have it all" aren't violating conservation laws—they're either making tradeoffs invisible to you, operating at the edge of their capacity, or about to hit a limit.

But conservation laws can mislead. Not everything is conserved. Information isn't—it can be copied infinitely. Ideas compound rather than transfer. Love and trust aren't zero-sum. And "closed system" is usually an approximation. A business that seems to be trading long-term health for short-term gains might actually be tapping into a new market, breaking the apparent conservation law by opening the system.

The trick is identifying which quantities are actually conserved in your situation. In negotiations, trust might be conserved—gain it here, lose it there—or it might genuinely grow through good faith actions. In creative work, attention might be conserved within a day, but skill accumulates and breaks the conservation law across time.

When someone proposes a solution, ask: what's being conserved here? If they claim more of everything, they're wrong or the system is opening. If they say nothing is being traded, they haven't looked carefully enough. The wisest plans acknowledge the tradeoffs explicitly.

The conserved quantities reveal what the system fundamentally is. Find them, and you understand the constraints you're actually working within.
