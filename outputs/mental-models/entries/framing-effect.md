# Framing Effect

***The same choice, told two ways***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases

In 1981, two psychologists asked people to imagine an outbreak of an unusual disease expected to kill 600 people. They offered two programs to combat it.

Half the subjects saw this version:
- Program A: 200 people will be saved
- Program B: 1/3 probability that 600 people will be saved, 2/3 probability that no one will be saved

72% chose Program A. They wanted the sure thing.

The other half saw these options:
- Program C: 400 people will die
- Program D: 1/3 probability that nobody will die, 2/3 probability that 600 people will die

78% chose Program D. They couldn't accept the certain deaths.

Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman ran this experiment dozens of times with consistent results. The remarkable thing? Programs A and C are identical. So are B and D. Same numbers, same outcomes, different words. The frame—whether the choice emphasized lives saved or lives lost—determined what people picked.

This is the Framing Effect: logically equivalent information produces different decisions depending on how it's presented. The frame acts as a lens, highlighting certain aspects of reality while obscuring others. We don't evaluate choices in a vacuum. We evaluate them relative to how they're described.

The effect runs deeper than word choice. Consider time. "This sale ends tonight" and "You have six hours left" present the same deadline, but the first feels like information while the second feels like pressure. Or defaults: organ donation rates vary by country not because of different values, but because some countries require opting in while others require opting out. The frame sets the starting point, and most people never move from it.

Skilled framers know this. A surgeon can say "This procedure has a 90% survival rate" or "10% of patients don't survive." Both true. Radically different patient decisions. A company can describe layoffs as "rightsizing" or "eliminating positions" rather than "firing people"—same action, different emotional weight. Politicians frame tax cuts as "returning your money" or "defunding schools" depending on their position.

The mechanism seems to be loss aversion, our tendency to feel losses more intensely than equivalent gains. In Tversky and Kahneman's disease problem, the "lives saved" frame highlighted gains—we locked in the sure win. The "people will die" frame highlighted losses—we gambled to avoid accepting those deaths. The frame determined whether we saw the glass as half full or half empty, and that determined everything else.

But the Framing Effect isn't just about manipulation. Sometimes frames reveal truth rather than distort it. Describing carbon emissions in tons per year means nothing to most people. Say "equivalent to driving your car 10,000 miles" and the frame makes the abstract concrete. Good frames can clarify. Bad frames can deceive. The problem is we often can't tell the difference in the moment.

The effect breaks down when people slow down and consider multiple frames simultaneously. If you see both "200 saved" and "400 die" at once, you recognize they're the same. If you check the absolute numbers instead of reacting to percentages, the manipulation becomes visible. If you ask "How else could I describe this?" you escape the first frame's gravity.

But that takes effort most people won't make in most situations. We're cognitive misers. The first frame we encounter usually wins because reframing requires noticing we've been framed at all.

The deepest insight: there is no "unframed" way to present information. Every choice of words is a frame. Every emphasis is a decision about what to highlight. Even "just the facts" involves choosing which facts and in what order. The question isn't whether to frame—it's whether you're aware of the frame you're using and whether it serves understanding or manipulation.

When someone presents you with a choice, ask: How else could this be described? When you're presenting information, ask: What am I emphasizing, and what am I hiding? The frame shapes the decision. Notice the frame, and you take back control.
