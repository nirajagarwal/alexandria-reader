# Expected Value

***The mathematics of choosing when outcomes are uncertain***

> **Section:** Probability and Decision-Making

In 1654, a French nobleman named Antoine Gombaud, the Chevalier de Méré, had a gambling problem. Not addiction—he was losing at dice and couldn't figure out why. He'd devised what seemed like a brilliant strategy: bet that at least one six would appear when rolling a die four times. He won often enough to profit. Then he tried a variation: bet that at least one double-six would appear when rolling two dice twenty-four times. The math looked similar, but his money disappeared.

Frustrated, de Méré wrote to mathematician Blaise Pascal, who shared the problem with Pierre de Fermat. Their correspondence solved de Méré's puzzle and invented probability theory. The answer? The first bet had a 51.8% chance of winning. The second: only 49.1%. That three-percentage-point gap bankrupted a nobleman.

What Pascal and Fermat discovered was how to multiply probability by payoff to find the true value of an uncertain choice.

Expected value is the average outcome of a decision if you could repeat it infinite times. You calculate it by multiplying each possible result by its probability, then summing them all. 

A coin flip for ten dollars has an expected value of five dollars: 50% chance you win ten, 50% chance you win zero. (0.5 × $10) + (0.5 × $0) = $5. If someone charges you three dollars to play, you should. If they charge seven, you shouldn't. The math determines the decision.

This seems obvious with coins. It becomes powerful with careers, investments, and risks that most people evaluate by gut feeling alone.

Consider a startup offer: $80,000 salary plus equity that could be worth millions or nothing. Your safe corporate job pays $120,000. Which is better? Expected value forces you to be honest. If the equity has a 5% chance of being worth $2 million and 95% chance of worthlessness: (0.05 × $2,000,000) + (0.95 × $0) = $100,000. Add the salary and you get $180,000 expected value versus the corporate $120,000. The startup wins—if your probability estimate is honest.

The hard part is estimating probabilities for decisions you make once. Pascal had thousands of dice rolls. You get one career move. But even rough estimates beat ignoring the math entirely. Humans naturally focus on the most vivid outcome—the jackpot or the catastrophe—and underweight probability. Expected value forces you to hold both in mind simultaneously.

It also reveals why insurance companies profit and lottery players lose. A lottery ticket with a one-in-a-million chance of winning $500,000 has an expected value of fifty cents. If it costs two dollars, you lose $1.50 in expectation every time you play. The lottery isn't entertainment that sometimes pays. It's a consistent loss dressed up as hope.

The model has limits. It assumes you can absorb the losses. A bet with positive expected value but a small chance of losing everything you own is a bad bet—even if the math says play. This is why bankroll management matters in poker and why leverage destroys investors who are "technically right." Expected value tells you what to do across infinite repetitions, but you live in a world of finite resources and irreversible ruin.

It also assumes you can estimate probabilities. Garbage in, garbage out. Overconfidence produces beautiful calculations that justify terrible decisions. The model works best when you can calibrate against reality: track your estimates, see what happens, adjust. Without that feedback loop, you're de Méré before he wrote to Pascal—acting on intuition dressed up as mathematics.

But used honestly, expected value is a razor for cutting through uncertainty. It prevents you from rejecting good risks because failure is possible, and from embracing bad risks because success is imaginable. It turns "maybe" into a number, and numbers can be compared.

The Chevalier de Méré wanted to know why he kept losing. Pascal showed him he'd been playing the wrong game all along. The question wasn't whether he'd win or lose on any single roll. It was whether the bet was worth making at all.
