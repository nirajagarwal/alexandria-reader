# Satisficing vs. Maximizing

***Two strategies for decision-making — one seeks good enough, the other seeks the best***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1957, economist Herbert Simon was trying to understand why people make decisions that seem irrational by traditional economic standards. Classical theory assumed humans always optimize — always find the best possible solution. But Simon noticed something different in the real world.

He watched managers making hiring decisions. They didn't interview every possible candidate. They didn't compare all options on every dimension. Instead, they set a threshold — "We need someone with X experience and Y skills" — and hired the first person who cleared it. They stopped searching once they found "good enough."

Simon called this *satisficing* — a combination of "satisfy" and "suffice." It wasn't laziness or ignorance. It was rational behavior in a world where information is costly, time is limited, and the difference between the 90th percentile and the 99th percentile might not matter.

The alternative is *maximizing* — exhausting all options to find the absolute best. The maximizer compares every restaurant review before choosing where to eat. They research every model before buying a vacuum cleaner. They agonize over whether their partner is truly their soulmate or if someone better might exist.

On the surface, maximizing sounds superior. Who wouldn't want the best outcome? But research by psychologist Barry Schwartz found that maximizers are consistently less happy than satisficers. They experience more regret. They're more prone to depression. Even when they achieve objectively better outcomes, they feel worse about them.

The reason: maximizing has hidden costs. The search itself consumes time and energy. Every option you consider raises the bar. When you finally choose, you're haunted by the alternatives you didn't pick. The maximizer who researched fifty vacuum cleaners wonders if number fifty-one would have been better. The satisficer who bought the first one that met their needs is already vacuuming.

The trap deepens in a world of abundance. When you could choose from three job offers, maximizing was plausible. When LinkedIn shows you thousands of options, it becomes paralyzing. Maximizers in dating never quite commit — there might be someone better on the next swipe. Satisficers set their criteria and marry someone who meets them.

This isn't about settling. The satisficer's threshold can be high. They might want someone who makes them laugh, challenges them intellectually, and shares their values. But once they find that person, they stop searching. The maximizer wants all of that *plus* the assurance that no one better exists. That assurance never comes.

The model applies differently depending on reversibility. For decisions you can undo — which restaurant, which movie — satisficing saves time with little cost. For irreversible decisions — which career, which city — spending more time maximizing might be worth it. But even there, the goal isn't perfection. It's making a choice good enough that you can stop searching and start building.

Simon won the Nobel Prize partly for this insight. He showed that rationality isn't about perfect optimization. It's about making decisions that are good enough given the constraints you face. In a world of infinite options and finite time, "good enough" is often optimal.

The maximizer seeks the best vacuum cleaner. The satisficer buys one that works and spends Saturday at the park instead of reading reviews. Both end up with clean floors. Only one enjoyed their weekend.
