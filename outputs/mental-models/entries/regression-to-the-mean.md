# Regression to the Mean

***Why extreme outcomes soften over time***

> **Section:** Statistics and Probability

In 1886, Francis Galton measured the heights of 928 adult children and their parents. He expected tall parents to have tall children, short parents to have short children. The data confirmed this—but with a pattern he didn't expect.

The tallest parents had tall children, yes. But those children were, on average, shorter than their parents. The shortest parents had short children. But those children were, on average, taller than their parents. Every generation moved toward the middle.

Galton called this "regression toward mediocrity." We now call it regression to the mean. And it explains far more than height.

Extreme outcomes—in either direction—tend to be followed by more moderate ones. Not because anything has changed. Not because of correction or intervention. Simply because extreme results usually involve some combination of skill and luck, and luck doesn't persist.

A student scores unusually high on a test. Their next score will likely be lower. Not because they've learned less, but because the first score probably included some lucky guesses. A team has a spectacular season. Next year they'll probably perform closer to average. The players haven't gotten worse. The extremity itself was unsustainable.

The problem is how we interpret this movement. We see the high test score, praise the student, and when the next score drops, we think our praise caused complacency. We see the great season, give the coach a raise, and when performance dips, we question the contract. We see a patient's pain spike, try a new treatment, and when pain returns to normal levels, we credit the treatment.

Regression to the mean makes us believe we're seeing cause and effect when we're often just seeing the mathematics of variance.

Daniel Kahneman discovered this teaching Israeli flight instructors. The instructors insisted that praise hurt performance and criticism helped it. They had evidence: when they praised a smooth landing, the next landing was usually worse. When they criticized a rough landing, the next was usually better.

Kahneman realized they were observing regression, not causation. Exceptionally smooth landings are rare. They're followed by more typical landings—not because praise corrupted the pilot, but because exceptional is, by definition, temporary. Rough landings work the same way in reverse.

The instructors were learning exactly the wrong lesson from valid data.

This happens everywhere. Cities install speed cameras at dangerous intersections after a spike in accidents, then take credit when accidents decline. But accident rates fluctuate randomly. The spike was likely temporary regardless of intervention. Businesses hire consultants after a bad quarter, then credit the advice when results improve—even though most bad quarters are followed by better ones without help.

Regression to the mean doesn't eliminate actual effects. Training does improve pilots. Good management does improve businesses. Medical treatments do reduce pain. But these real effects are obscured by regression, which creates the illusion of effects where none exist.

The model has limits. It applies to outcomes with a random component, not to persistent traits or systematic change. If your baseline is shifting—if you're actually getting better at something, or if underlying conditions are deteriorating—regression to the mean won't predict what comes next. And it tells you nothing about individual cases, only about patterns across many observations.

But where it applies, it's humbling. Many things we take credit for or assign blame to are just noise reverting to signal. The spectacular success wasn't entirely skill. The devastating failure wasn't entirely incompetence. And the return to normal wasn't caused by whatever we happened to do in between.

**The takeaway:** Extreme outcomes contain more luck than typical ones. They don't predict similar extremes—they predict movement toward the average. Don't confuse that movement with causation.
