# Tradeoffs

***Every choice is also a refusal***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1975, Stephen Jobs and Stephen Wozniak couldn't build the computer they wanted to sell. They needed money. Jobs owned a Volkswagen van. Wozniak owned a programmable calculator, an HP-65, which at the time cost more than some used cars. Jobs sold the van. Wozniak sold the calculator. They raised $1,300.

What they gained: Apple Computer Company. What they lost: transportation and the calculator Wozniak used for his day job at Hewlett-Packard.

This is obvious, almost trivial. But the obviousness is the point. Every resource you commit to one purpose cannot serve another. Every hour you spend is an hour you don't spend differently. Every yes contains a no.

We call these tradeoffs, and they govern every decision that matters.

A tradeoff exists when choosing one thing means giving up another. Not when you can have both eventually or both partially, but when the act of choosing one necessarily diminishes your ability to have the other. Time is the clearest example: you cannot simultaneously attend two meetings. Money is another: dollars spent here are not spent there. Attention, energy, space, political capital—all operate under the same constraint.

The mental model sounds simple. It is simple. What makes it powerful is how consistently we ignore it.

We speak as if choices are additions. "We should also..." "Why not add..." "It would be good to include..." As if resources were infinite, as if time were elastic, as if decisions didn't exclude. Organizations create strategies that list ten priorities, which is to say they have none. People commit to more obligations than their calendars can hold, then wonder why they feel overwhelmed.

The model forces a different question. Not "What do we want?" but "What are we willing to give up to get it?"

When Ford designed the Model T, Henry Ford made it clear: "Any customer can have a car painted any color that he wants, so long as it is black." This wasn't arbitrary. Black paint dried fastest. Faster drying meant faster production. Faster production meant lower costs. Lower costs meant more people could afford cars. Ford traded customer choice for efficiency, and efficiency for accessibility. He knew what he was trading.

When you make a tradeoff explicit, you can evaluate it. Should we hire more engineers or more salespeople? Both would be useful. Both seem necessary. But the tradeoff is clear: engineering capacity versus market reach. You can reason about which matters more right now, given where you are and where you're trying to go.

When you pretend the tradeoff doesn't exist, you get strategic incoherence. You hire a few more engineers and a few more salespeople, neither group large enough to accomplish what you need. You spread resources across both without committing to either. You make no real choice at all.

The hardest tradeoffs are between good things. Not safety versus recklessness, but safety versus speed. Not quality versus garbage, but quality versus cost. Not focus versus distraction, but focus on this versus focus on that. The choice between bad and good is easy. The choice between good and good is strategy.

Some decisions do resolve over time. You can go to graduate school now and start a business later. You can save money this year and spend it next year. But even these are tradeoffs—present versus future, certainty versus option value. The resource being traded is time and opportunity cost, which are real even if less visible.

The model fails when you mistake a constraint for a tradeoff. If you're deciding between rent and food, that's not a tradeoff you're evaluating—it's poverty. True tradeoffs assume baseline resources and force allocation decisions. Recognizing which situation you're in matters.

Every successful strategy is a list of things you've decided not to do. Every coherent life is a series of refusals. What you're not building, not pursuing, not attending, not reading, not becoming. The clearer you are about what you're giving up, the better your judgment about whether the tradeoff is worth it.

Wozniak later said selling the calculator was worth it. Jobs sold and bought back several vehicles over the years. But in that moment, in 1975, they made the trade. They knew what they were trading. That clarity is what let them decide.
