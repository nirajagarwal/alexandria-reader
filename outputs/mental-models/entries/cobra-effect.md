# Cobra Effect

***When the solution creates more of the problem***

> **Section:** Unintended Consequences

During British rule in colonial India, Delhi had a cobra problem. Venomous snakes slithered through streets and homes. People were dying from bites. The government needed a solution.

They decided to incentivize the public. For every dead cobra brought to authorities, they'd pay a bounty. Simple economics: make cobra killing profitable, reduce the cobra population.

At first, it worked. Dead cobras poured in. Citizens collected their rewards. Officials congratulated themselves on the elegant market-based solution.

Then reports began filtering back. People weren't just hunting wild cobras. They were breeding them. Cobra farms appeared across the city. Why risk hunting dangerous snakes in the wild when you could raise docile ones at home, kill them on schedule, and collect guaranteed income?

When the British discovered the scheme, they canceled the program. The bounty disappeared overnight. And the cobra breeders, now stuck with worthless snakes, did the rational thing: they released them. Delhi's cobra population exploded beyond its original size.

The solution had manufactured the problem.

This is the Cobra Effect — when an incentive designed to solve a problem instead amplifies it. The intervention creates perverse behavior that defeats its own purpose. What looks like progress in one metric becomes regression in the actual goal.

The pattern appears everywhere. A hospital reduces patient wait times by adding more appointments — so doctors spend less time with each patient, miss diagnoses, and readmissions spike. A company pays developers per line of code written — so they write verbose, unmaintainable garbage. A school system ties funding to test scores — so teachers teach to the test and students stop learning to think.

The mechanism is consistent: You identify a problem. You measure something related to that problem. You create an incentive to move that measurement. People respond to the incentive in ways you didn't anticipate. The measurement improves while the underlying problem worsens.

The trap is mistaking the metric for the goal. Dead cobras are not the same as cobra safety. Wait times are not the same as quality care. Test scores are not the same as education. When you pay for the proxy instead of the outcome, you get the proxy — often at the expense of the outcome.

Goodhart's Law captures this precisely: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The moment you incentivize a metric, people start optimizing for that metric rather than the underlying goal. And humans are endlessly creative at gaming systems.

The Cobra Effect thrives where three conditions meet: The metric is easier to manipulate than the actual problem is to solve. The incentive is strong enough to make manipulation worthwhile. And the system has insufficient mechanisms to detect or prevent gaming.

This doesn't mean incentives are useless. It means simple incentives for complex problems are dangerous. The more degrees of freedom a system has — the more ways people can respond — the more likely an intervention creates perverse outcomes.

What to do instead? Measure multiple things. If you care about patient outcomes, track wait times AND readmission rates AND patient satisfaction. Make the game harder to game. Better yet, design systems where the incentive naturally aligns with the goal. Pay doctors for healthy patients, not busy calendars.

Watch for early warning signs. If your metric improves rapidly without apparent improvement in the underlying problem, you've probably created cobra farms. If people spend more time figuring out how to hit targets than doing the work, your incentives are broken.

The deepest lesson: Complex systems resist simple solutions. Every intervention ripples through a web of incentives, behaviors, and unintended consequences. The cobra breeders weren't criminals — they were rational actors responding to the rules they'd been given. Your system is only as good as its ability to survive contact with human creativity.

Before you design an incentive, ask: What's the cleverest way someone could game this? Then assume they'll find three you didn't think of.
