# Real Options

***Why keeping doors open has measurable value***

> **Section:** Decision-Making and Strategy

In 1977, Stewart Brand wanted to start a magazine but couldn't afford the printing costs. So he published the first issue of *CoEvolution Quarterly* as a photocopied booklet, sold it by mail, and waited to see if anyone cared. They did. Six months later, he printed a proper magazine. Two years later, it became a quarterly institution of counterculture thinking.

Brand didn't commit to the full vision upfront. He bought information. Each small step revealed whether the next one made sense. He kept his options open while reducing his uncertainty.

This is the logic of real options—a mental model borrowed from finance but useful far beyond it.

A financial option gives you the right, but not the obligation, to make a transaction later. You pay a small price now for the ability to decide later, when you know more. A real option is the same idea applied to any decision: you make a small, reversible move that preserves your ability to make a larger, irreversible one later.

The value isn't in making the decision. It's in *deferring* it while gathering information.

Consider software development. Amazon's architecture is famously modular—each team builds services that can plug into or detach from the larger system. This looks inefficient. Coordination is harder. Duplication happens. But it creates real options. If a service doesn't work, they can replace it without tearing down the building. If it works brilliantly, they can scale it independently. The "inefficiency" is a premium they pay for flexibility.

Or hiring. A company could hire someone full-time immediately, or they could start with a contract project. The contract costs more per hour, but it's a real option. They learn how the person actually works before committing to the full employment relationship. The premium is the price of information.

Real options have three components:

**The premium**: what you pay now to keep the option alive. Stewart Brand's photocopied booklet. Amazon's "inefficient" architecture. The contractor's higher hourly rate.

**The strike price**: what it will cost to exercise the option later. Printing a full magazine. Building the full system. Extending a job offer.

**The expiration**: the point at which you must decide or lose the option. If Brand waited too long, the moment would pass. If Amazon never committed to a design, they'd never ship.

The model reveals why certain choices that look wasteful are actually rational. Keeping two suppliers when one is cheaper. Maintaining expertise in an older technology while building a new one. Taking longer to decide when the decision is irreversible.

The key is proportionality. Real options are valuable when:
- Uncertainty is high
- The cost of the premium is small relative to the strike price
- Information will arrive before expiration

They become expensive when you're buying optionality you don't need. The person who never commits to anything, always "keeping their options open," pays premiums forever and exercises nothing. The company that endlessly pilots and tests but never scales is paying for information it already has.

The failure mode is treating all decisions as if they were irreversible. Some choices *can* be undone. The company that agonizes for months over an easily reversible decision—what color to paint the office, which project management tool to try—is paying an opportunity cost far higher than just picking one and learning.

Real options thinking asks: What is the smallest move that preserves my ability to make the larger one later? What information am I buying with this premium? When will I know enough to decide?

Stewart Brand's photocopied booklet was worth more than a business plan. It was a real option. And unlike most options, this one paid off.

**The thing to remember**: Before locking in an irreversible decision, ask whether a smaller, reversible move would teach you what you need to know. The premium you pay for flexibility is often cheaper than the cost of being wrong.
