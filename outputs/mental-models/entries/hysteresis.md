# Hysteresis

***Why the path you took determines where you are***

> **Section:** Systems & Feedback

In 1985, a group of macroeconomists noticed something strange about European unemployment. After the oil shocks of the 1970s drove joblessness to record highs, rates stayed elevated even after oil prices stabilized. Classical theory said unemployment should return to its "natural rate" once the shock passed. It didn't. France, which had 3% unemployment in 1973, still had 10% a decade later, long after the crisis ended.

The economists borrowed a term from physics: hysteresis. In magnetism, it describes how iron retains magnetization after the magnetic field is removed. The metal remembers where it's been.

Economies, they realized, remember too.

Here's how it worked. When companies laid off workers during the crisis, those workers lost skills. Their networks atrophied. Hiring managers viewed long unemployment gaps as red flags. Meanwhile, the companies that survived learned to operate lean. They discovered they could maintain output with fewer people. Both sides adapted to the new reality. When conditions improved, neither side moved back.

The shock was temporary. The changes were permanent.

This is hysteresis: when a system doesn't return to its starting point after a temporary force is removed. The path matters as much as the destination.

Think of a couple's first major fight. Before it, they spoke openly. After it, even though they reconciled, a new caution enters their conversations. The fight is over, but its effect persists. Trust, once broken, doesn't simply restore itself when the immediate conflict ends. The relationship has moved to a different equilibrium.

Or consider a company that cuts its research budget during a recession. When revenue recovers, the budget doesn't automatically return. The researchers have left. The institutional knowledge is gone. The company has learned to function without R&D. The temporary pressure created a permanent change.

The mechanism varies, but the pattern holds: systems with hysteresis are path-dependent. Where you end up depends not just on current conditions but on the route you traveled to get there.

This is why "just wait it out" often fails. If you lose your fitness during an injury, you can't just stop being injured and expect your conditioning back. The gap creates its own effects. Muscles atrophy. Habits break. Momentum dies. You have to rebuild, not merely wait.

Organizations show hysteresis in their cultures. A startup that grows slowly builds different social structures than one that scales explosively, even if they end up the same size. The rapid-growth company carries forward the chaos of its expansion—the weak systems, the hired-too-fast managers, the cowboy culture. These persist long after growth slows.

Financial markets exhibit hysteresis in valuations. After a crash, investors don't simply return to their pre-crash risk appetite when prices stabilize. Many sit in cash. Some never return. The crash rewired their psychology. The temporary event created lasting behavioral change.

The model misleads when you treat every persistent effect as hysteresis. Some systems have multiple stable states not because of path dependence but because the underlying conditions actually changed. Real structural shifts exist. The trick is distinguishing between a system that remembers its path and a system that has genuinely moved to new terrain.

Hysteresis also doesn't mean irreversibility. Path dependence doesn't equal permanence. You can build new paths. But you can't ignore the one you traveled. The European unemployment eventually did decline—but it took active policy intervention and two decades, not merely the passage of time.

The practical implication: prevention matters more than you think. Once you've traveled the path, you can't simply retrace it backward. The college student who takes five years off can eventually finish their degree, but they won't be the same student who left. The company that loses its top talent can eventually rebuild, but not by simply offering their old jobs back.

When you see a system that won't return to baseline after a shock, don't assume it's broken or irrational. It might be showing hysteresis. The system adapted to where it's been. The path changed it.

The metal remembers the magnet.
