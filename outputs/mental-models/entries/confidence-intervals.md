# Confidence Intervals

***Why certainty is a red flag***

> **Section:** Statistics & Probability

In 1936, Literary Digest magazine sent out 10 million ballots asking who Americans would vote for in the presidential election. Over 2 million people responded—an enormous sample. The magazine confidently predicted that Alf Landon would defeat Franklin Roosevelt in a landslide. Roosevelt won by the largest margin in modern history.

The same year, a young pollster named George Gallup surveyed just 50,000 people and correctly predicted Roosevelt's victory. Gallup understood something Literary Digest didn't: precision without accuracy is worthless. He also understood that all predictions contain uncertainty, and honest predictions acknowledge it.

A confidence interval is a range, not a point. It says: "Based on this data, the true value is probably between X and Y." The "probably" matters. A 95% confidence interval means that if you repeated the measurement many times, the true value would fall within your range about 95% of the time.

Literary Digest's mistake wasn't just their biased sample (they surveyed people from phone books and car registrations, skewing wealthy). It was treating their large sample as if it guaranteed certainty. Gallup's genius was building uncertainty into his method. He didn't claim to know exactly how many people would vote for Roosevelt. He claimed to know, with measurable confidence, the range within which the result would fall.

This distinction changes everything. When someone reports "52% support the policy," they're giving you a point estimate—a single number. When they report "between 48% and 56% support the policy (95% confidence interval)," they're giving you something more honest and more useful. The interval tells you the precision of the measurement. Narrow intervals mean high precision. Wide intervals mean genuine uncertainty.

Three factors determine interval width: sample size, variability in the data, and your chosen confidence level. Want a narrower interval? Collect more data, reduce noise, or accept less certainty. There's no free lunch. A 99% confidence interval will always be wider than a 95% interval for the same data—higher confidence requires acknowledging more uncertainty.

The counterintuitive truth: people who give you ranges are usually more trustworthy than people who give you points. The executive who says "revenue will be $10 million" is either ignorant or lying. The one who says "revenue will be between $8 and $12 million, and I'm confident of that range" understands their business.

But here's where it misleads: confidence intervals measure statistical uncertainty, not all uncertainty. Gallup's method couldn't account for people lying to pollsters or last-minute opinion shifts. The interval captures sampling error—the noise from not surveying everyone. It doesn't capture model error, measurement error, or future changes. A tight confidence interval built on wrong assumptions is precisely wrong, not approximately right.

This matters most when stakes are high. Medical trials report confidence intervals for treatment effects. Climate models report ranges for future warming. Economic forecasts show intervals for GDP growth. In each case, the range is the story. Anyone who strips it away to report a single number is either selling something or doesn't understand what they're measuring.

The point estimate gets the headline. The confidence interval gets the truth.
