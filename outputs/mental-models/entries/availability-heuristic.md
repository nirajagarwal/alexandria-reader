# Availability Heuristic

***Why the easiest examples feel like the only examples***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases and Heuristics

In the summer of 1975, psychologists Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman asked people a simple question: In English, are there more words that start with the letter K, or more words that have K as their third letter?

Most people said words starting with K were more common. They could easily think of examples: king, kitchen, kite, knowledge. Words with K in the third position? Much harder. Maybe... make? Awkward? The examples didn't flow.

The actual answer: There are roughly twice as many words with K as the third letter. But those words are harder to recall, so people assumed they were rarer.

This pattern—judging frequency by ease of recall—is the availability heuristic. When we estimate how common something is, we rely on how easily examples come to mind. The more available the memory, the more common we assume the thing is.

It's a reasonable shortcut. Usually, things that happen often are easier to remember. Common birds are easier to name than rare ones. Frequent causes of problems surface quickly when troubleshooting. Most of the time, availability correlates with reality.

But not always.

After a plane crash, people avoid flying and drive instead—even though driving is statistically far more dangerous. The vividness and media coverage of the crash make air disasters feel more common than they are. Car accidents, precisely because they're more common, fade into background noise. Availability and actual risk diverge, and our decisions follow availability.

The heuristic explains why people overestimate dramatic risks (shark attacks, terrorism, stranger abductions) and underestimate mundane ones (heart disease, car accidents, falls at home). Dramatic events make vivid memories. They're covered extensively. They generate emotional reactions. All of this makes them easier to recall, which makes them feel more probable than they are.

It also works in reverse. If something is hard to imagine or recall, we assume it's rare—even when it's not. Before the 2008 financial crisis, many economists and investors struggled to imagine a housing crash because recent memory contained only rising prices. The absence of available examples made the possibility feel remote.

The heuristic operates everywhere: In job interviews, where the most recent candidate feels like the strongest. In medical diagnosis, where doctors consider diseases they've seen lately over equally likely but less memorable conditions. In hiring decisions, where diversity looks like "lowering standards" if successful diverse employees are less visible than successful traditional hires.

Personal experience weighs especially heavily. If you know someone who got sick from a vaccine, that single vivid example can outweigh statistical evidence from millions of safe vaccinations. If your friend made money day trading, that feels more real than aggregate data showing most day traders lose money. One memorable story beats a thousand abstract datapoints.

The trap isn't that we use availability. The trap is that we don't notice we're using it. We feel like we're assessing actual frequency, but we're really assessing memory accessibility. We confuse "easy to recall" with "actually common."

The fix isn't to stop using availability—you can't. The heuristic runs automatically. Instead, ask: Why is this easy to remember? Is it actually common, or just vivid? Recent, or genuinely representative?

When making decisions that matter, availability should be the start of investigation, not the end. The examples that come to mind quickly are worth considering—but so are the ones that don't.

We think we're estimating the world. We're really reporting what memory makes easy.
