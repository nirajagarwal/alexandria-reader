# Convexity

***When small changes produce disproportionate results***

> **Section:** Mathematics & Systems

In 1952, a bond trader named Sidney Homer noticed something odd. When interest rates dropped by one percentage point, his bonds gained more in value than they lost when rates rose by the same amount. The relationship wasn't symmetrical. It curved.

Homer called this property "convexity." The name stuck because it described the shape of the curve when you plotted price against yield. But the real insight wasn't about bonds. It was about asymmetry.

Most people think in straight lines. Double the input, double the output. Cut the risk in half, cut the reward in half. This works for many things—miles driven, hours worked, apples bought. But it fails for anything that curves.

A convex relationship bends upward. Small changes at the extremes produce larger changes in outcome than the same changes at the center. This creates a powerful asymmetry: you gain more from favorable moves than you lose from unfavorable ones.

Consider physical training. Your first week of running improves your fitness dramatically. Your hundredth week barely moves the needle. The curve flattens as you approach your genetic limits. That's concave—diminishing returns.

Now consider network effects. Facebook with 10 users is worthless. With 100 million users, each additional user makes the network more valuable to everyone already on it. The curve steepens. That's convex—increasing returns.

Convexity appears everywhere once you notice it:

A startup's potential outcomes curve upward. You can lose your initial investment, but you cannot lose more than 100%. You can gain 10x, 100x, or more. The upside is convex.

Options contracts are deliberately convex. You pay a fixed premium for the right to buy or sell at a set price. If the market moves against you, you lose only the premium. If it moves in your favor, your gains are unlimited. The payoff structure bends in your direction.

Small teams often outperform large ones on novel problems. Adding the third person to a two-person team changes everything. Adding the hundredth person to a ninety-nine-person team changes little. Early additions are convex.

The key insight is that when you face convex outcomes, uncertainty becomes an advantage. Volatility helps you. A quiet, stable market leaves a convex position unchanged. A wild, swinging market activates the asymmetry.

This is why Nassim Taleb advocates for "barbell strategies"—extreme safety on one end, extreme risk on the other, nothing in the middle. The safe end protects you from ruin. The risky end captures convex upside. The middle just exposes you to volatility without the benefit of asymmetry.

But convexity has a cost. The bond trader pays for it through lower yields. The options buyer pays a premium. The startup founder pays in time and stress. You cannot get asymmetric upside for free. Someone is always selling you that convexity, and they want compensation.

And not all curves bend in your favor. Concave relationships work in reverse. Small favorable changes produce smaller gains than small unfavorable changes produce losses. Debt is concave—the pain of default exceeds the pleasure of lower interest rates. Reputation is concave—one scandal can erase decades of trust, but decades of good behavior add little to an already good reputation.

The critical skill is recognizing which type of curve you face. Are you in a situation where small changes compound in your favor or against you? Does volatility help or hurt? Are the extremes where the action happens, or is the middle where you want to be?

Homer's discovery in the bond market revealed a deeper truth: the world rarely moves in straight lines. Understanding the shape of the curve tells you whether to embrace uncertainty or avoid it, whether to optimize for stability or expose yourself to volatility, whether you're playing a game where small bets can produce outsize returns or where every mistake compounds against you.

The curve matters more than the direction.
