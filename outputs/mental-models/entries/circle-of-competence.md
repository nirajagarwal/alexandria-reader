# Circle of Competence

***What you actually know versus what you think you know***

> **Section:** Self-Knowledge

In 1965, Warren Buffett bought control of a struggling textile mill in New Bedford, Massachusetts. He knew textiles were dying in America. He knew the economics were terrible. But he thought he could turn it around through better management.

He couldn't.

For twenty years, Berkshire Hathaway's textile operations hemorrhaged money while Buffett watched helplessly. The business had moved offshore. The margins were razor-thin. No amount of good management could fix structural decline.

The irony: Buffett was already one of the best investors alive. He understood insurance, banking, and capital allocation better than almost anyone. But textiles? He was guessing.

Later, he would call it one of his worst decisions. Not because he lost money overall—he eventually pivoted Berkshire into the holding company that made him famous. But because he stayed in textiles for years trying to prove he could succeed outside his circle of competence.

**The Model**

Your circle of competence is the domain where you have genuine expertise. Not where you're interested. Not where you have an opinion. Where you actually know what you're doing.

Inside the circle, you understand the variables that matter. You can predict outcomes with reasonable accuracy. You know when you're wrong and can correct quickly. You can spot the difference between luck and skill.

Outside the circle, you're operating on surface knowledge, borrowed opinions, and overconfidence. You don't know what you don't know.

The size of your circle matters less than knowing its boundaries.

A programmer who deeply understands database systems has a small circle. But within it, she can solve problems others can't see. She knows which optimizations work and which are cargo cult rituals. She recognizes the signs of an architectural mistake before the system crashes.

The same programmer asked to redesign the company's sales process? Now she's outside her circle. She has opinions—everyone does—but she lacks the accumulated experience to separate good ideas from plausible-sounding disasters.

The trap is that competence feels like it should transfer. "I'm smart. I figured out databases. How hard can sales be?" Very hard, as it turns out. Because competence isn't intelligence. It's pattern recognition built through repeated exposure to a specific domain.

**When It Misleads**

The circle model has three failure modes.

First, you can draw your circle too small. You assume you don't know something when you actually have transferable skills. A journalist who thinks she can't write fiction because she's "not a creative writer" might be underestimating her circle.

Second, you can mistake adjacent knowledge for competence. Understanding economics doesn't mean you can trade currencies. They're related but distinct circles.

Third, circles change. What you knew five years ago might be obsolete. Technology shifts. Industries evolve. Your old circle becomes a donut—you remember the outdated version, which is sometimes worse than knowing nothing.

**Expanding the Circle**

Competence expands through deliberate practice and feedback loops. You take small steps outside your current boundary. You make predictions. You check results. You adjust.

But expansion is slow. There are no shortcuts. You can't read your way into a circle of competence—you have to build it through experience.

This is why successful investors like Buffett pass on deals they don't understand. Not because the opportunities are bad. Because operating outside your circle transforms every decision into a coin flip with career-ending downside.

The skill isn't just knowing what you know. It's having the discipline to say "I don't know" when you're outside your circle—even when the opportunity looks obvious, even when everyone else is piling in, even when saying no costs you money.

Buffett eventually learned this lesson. After textiles, he stuck to businesses he understood. He passed on technology stocks during the dot-com bubble, looking foolish as the market soared. Then the bubble burst, and his discipline was vindicated.

**The Takeaway**

Know where your circle ends. Stay inside it for important decisions. Expand it deliberately when you can afford the tuition of mistakes. And remember: having a small circle isn't a limitation if you know its boundaries. Operating outside your circle without knowing it—that's the trap.
