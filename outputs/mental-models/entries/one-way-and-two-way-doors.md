# One-Way and Two-Way Doors

***Not all decisions deserve the same agony***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1997, Jeff Bezos faced a problem that every successful company eventually encounters: decisions were slowing down. Amazon was growing fast, but the decision-making machinery was becoming bureaucratic. Executives scheduled meetings to discuss whether to have meetings. Small choices required lengthy memos. The company was treating every decision like it could end the business.

Bezos realized that most decisions weren't actually that dangerous. He started asking a simple question: "Is this a one-way door or a two-way door?"

A one-way door is a decision you can't easily reverse. Selling your company. Choosing a co-founder. Moving to a new country. Once you walk through, you're committed. These decisions deserve careful analysis, detailed planning, long discussions. The cost of getting them wrong is severe.

A two-way door is reversible. You can walk through, look around, and come back if you don't like what you see. Launching a new product feature. Trying a different pricing model. Reorganizing a team. If it doesn't work, you can undo it. These decisions should be made quickly by individuals or small groups with good judgment.

The problem is that organizations—and people—treat everything like a one-way door. We agonize over choices that don't actually matter much. We gather more information, consult more stakeholders, delay until we're certain. But certainty is expensive. The time spent perfecting a reversible decision is often worth more than the decision itself.

Bezos wrote this distinction into Amazon's annual letter to shareholders. He made it explicit: most decisions are two-way doors, and if you wait for perfect information on those, you'll move too slowly. The real skill is correctly categorizing which type of door you're facing.

Consider hiring. For a large company, hiring a senior executive is a one-way door. They'll reshape teams, make strategic calls, build relationships. If it's the wrong fit, extracting them is painful and expensive. That decision deserves multiple interviews, reference checks, careful deliberation.

But hiring a contractor for a three-month project? Two-way door. If it's not working after a month, you part ways. The mistake is correctable. So you can decide faster, with less process.

The model works in personal decisions too. Choosing a college major when you're locked into a four-year program? One-way door. Taking an online course to explore whether you like data science? Two-way door. Getting married? One-way door (legally and emotionally). Going on a date? Two-way door.

The key insight is that decision-making speed should match decision reversibility. When you treat a two-way door like a one-way door, you pay a hidden tax—not just in time, but in opportunity cost. Every decision delayed is a decision not to do something else.

Where it misleads: Some doors look two-way but aren't. Reputation decisions often feel reversible but leave permanent marks. Quitting a job seems reversible until you realize you can't get the same job back. The social cost of reversal can make a nominally two-way door functionally one-way. You need to account for the full cost of turning back, not just the mechanical possibility.

Also, calling something a two-way door can become an excuse for sloppy thinking. Yes, you can reverse the decision, but reversals still have costs—wasted time, confused stakeholders, eroded trust. The goal isn't to be reckless with two-way doors. It's to be appropriately decisive.

The real power of this model is that it forces explicit thinking about reversibility. Before you gather another round of input or schedule another meeting, ask: What kind of door is this? If it's two-way, someone with good judgment should just decide and move forward. Save your agony for the doors you can't walk back through.

Most doors are two-way. Act accordingly.
