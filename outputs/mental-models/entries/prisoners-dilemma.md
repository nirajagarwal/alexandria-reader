# Prisoner's Dilemma

***When individual logic leads to collective failure***

> **Section:** Game Theory

In 1950, two RAND Corporation mathematicians named Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher ran an experiment. They asked two colleagues to play a simple game repeatedly. Each round, both players chose to either cooperate or defect. The payoffs were structured so that mutual cooperation beat mutual defection, but if one player defected while the other cooperated, the defector won big and the cooperator lost big.

The rational choice in any single round was obvious: defect. Whatever the other player does, you're better off defecting. If they cooperate, you get the biggest payoff. If they defect, you at least avoid being the sucker.

But here's what happened: the players didn't always defect. They cooperated. And when they did, both ended up better off than if they'd followed pure logic.

This became known as the Prisoner's Dilemma, named after a story Albert Tucker invented to explain it. Two prisoners are interrogated separately. Each can betray the other or stay silent. If both stay silent, each gets one year. If both betray, each gets three years. But if one betrays while the other stays silent, the betrayer goes free and the silent one gets five years.

The dilemma is that individual rationality produces collective irrationality. Each prisoner's best move — regardless of what the other does — is to betray. But when both follow this logic, they both get three years instead of the one year they'd get from mutual silence.

The model appears everywhere. Two nations stockpiling weapons when both would be safer without them. Companies in a price war that destroys everyone's profits. Athletes taking performance-enhancing drugs that create an arms race with no winner. Climate change, where each country benefits from polluting while hoping others reduce emissions.

The structure is always the same: what's rational for me is harmful for us, but only if everyone does it. The trap is that you can't trust others to cooperate, so you don't either, and everyone ends up worse off than if cooperation were guaranteed.

The model gets more interesting when the game repeats. If you know you'll face the same person again, cooperation becomes rational. You can punish defection and reward cooperation. Robert Axelrod's famous computer tournaments in the 1980s found that "tit-for-tat" — cooperate first, then copy whatever the other player did last — beat more sophisticated strategies.

This explains why small communities often solve collective action problems better than large ones. Repeated interaction creates reputation. The one-shot rational choice stops being rational when you'll see each other tomorrow.

**Where it misleads:** Not every conflict is a Prisoner's Dilemma. Sometimes interests genuinely oppose each other — one person's gain really is another's loss. The model can make cooperation seem possible in situations where it isn't. It also assumes players have complete information about payoffs, which is rarely true. Real dilemmas are often murkier: unclear options, unknown consequences, asymmetric stakes.

The model also says nothing about fairness or ethics. The "rational" solution might be rational but still wrong. When pharmaceutical companies refuse to share vaccine technology during a pandemic, the structure is a Prisoner's Dilemma, but the ethical question transcends the game theory.

**Connections:** The Prisoner's Dilemma is the foundation for understanding Tragedy of the Commons and related to Principal-Agent Problems when interests misalign.

The Prisoner's Dilemma teaches you to spot situations where everyone follows individual logic into collective failure. The value isn't in having a clever name for it. The value is in recognizing the structure early enough to change the game — through repeated interaction, binding agreements, or changing the payoffs so cooperation becomes individually rational. Because the trap isn't that cooperation is impossible. It's that rationality alone won't get you there.
