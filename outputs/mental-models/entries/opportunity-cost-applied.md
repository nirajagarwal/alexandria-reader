# Opportunity Cost

***The invisible price of every choice***

> **Section:** Economics & Decision-Making

In 1990, Stephen King was offered $2,500 to write a short story for a horror anthology. He'd just published *Misery* and *The Stand*. His name on the cover would help sell books. Easy money for a few days' work.

He said no.

Not because he didn't need $2,500. Not because he disliked the editor. He said no because those few days had another use: working on his next novel. The $2,500 was real, but the cost of accepting it was whatever he wouldn't write instead. That invisible cost mattered more than the visible payment.

This is opportunity cost—what you give up when you choose one thing over another.

Every choice has two prices. The explicit price: what you pay in money, time, or effort. And the implicit price: what else you could have done with those same resources. The second price is harder to see but often more important.

A college student choosing a major sees tuition bills and textbook costs. She doesn't see as clearly the engineering salary she's forgoing by studying philosophy, or the philosophy she won't learn by studying engineering. A company allocating budget to Project A sees the $500,000 expense. It sees less clearly that Project B, which might have returned $800,000, now won't happen.

The invisible price explains why free things aren't actually free. A "free" two-hour webinar costs nothing in money but costs two hours—two hours that could have been spent reading, working, or sleeping. The time is the cost, and time spent is time that can't be spent again.

It explains why rich people behave differently about time than money. Bill Gates won't spend an hour arguing about a $100 charge because the hour is worth far more than $100 to him. The opportunity cost of his time exceeds the benefit of winning the argument.

It explains why keeping options open has a price. The entrepreneur who won't commit to one business idea, keeping three in play "just in case," pays in scattered attention and slower progress. She sees the safety of multiple options. She sees less clearly what she could build by betting everything on one.

Opportunity cost is present in every decision, but it's hardest to see when the alternative wasn't seriously considered. You don't feel the ache of a path not taken if you never pictured yourself taking it. The person who stays in a mediocre job for twenty years feels the paycheck every month but doesn't feel the compounding career growth from the job they didn't take. The foregone earnings and learning never appear on a ledger.

The clearest thinkers make opportunity costs visible. Before deciding, they ask: "If I do this, what am I not doing?" They make the invisible price explicit. They compare not just options against each other, but each option against what it displaces.

But opportunity cost misleads in one crucial way. It assumes that the alternative would have worked out. The person who agonizes over the job not taken imagines it would have been better. Maybe. But paths not taken remain hypothetical. The grass looks greener partly because you never have to mow it. Real choices require comparing actual options with realistic predictions of forgone ones—not with fantasy versions where everything breaks your way.

Opportunity cost is not about regret. It's about seeing clearly. Every time you say yes to something, you say no to everything else you could have done instead. The question is whether you're choosing with both prices in mind.

Stephen King wrote *Needful Things* instead of that short story. It sold over a million copies. He paid $2,500 to earn millions. He could see both prices. Most of us never check.
