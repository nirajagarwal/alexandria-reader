# Discounting

***Why the future feels cheaper than it should***

> **Section:** Economics & Incentives

In 1978, a team of psychologists asked people a simple question: Would you rather have $15 today or $50 in a year? Most chose the $15. Then they asked: Would you rather have $15 in five years or $50 in six years? Most chose the $50. The math hadn't changed—same gap, same ratio. But something about waiting *now* felt different than waiting *later*.

This is discounting: the tendency to value immediate rewards more than future ones, even when the future reward is objectively better. A dollar today is worth more to us than a dollar tomorrow. The pattern is so consistent it can be measured. The further away something is, the less we value it, following a predictable curve.

The effect is not small. Studies show people will accept $10 now over $15 in a week. They'll choose one apple today over two apples tomorrow. The immediate option exerts a gravitational pull that warps our sense of value. The future, even the near future, exists at a discount.

This made sense in ancestral environments. Resources in hand were guaranteed. Resources promised were not. A bird in hand was genuinely worth two in the bush because bushes could be raided, predators could intervene, promises could be broken. Evolution built in a bias toward the present.

But modern life is different. Most future rewards are actually quite reliable. The $50 in a year will almost certainly arrive. Your retirement account will compound. The degree you're working toward will open doors. Yet we still apply Stone Age discounting to these modern certainties. We choose the smaller, sooner option even when choosing poorly.

The mechanism is simple: the present feels real, the future feels abstract. You can see the $15 now. The $50 in a year is a number, a promise, a hypothetical. Even when you know intellectually that waiting pays better, the emotional weight tilts toward now.

This shows up everywhere. We skip exercise (future health discounted) for an extra hour of television (immediate pleasure). We defer saving (future security discounted) to maintain current spending (immediate comfort). We take the quick solution (immediate relief) over the durable fix (future stability). Every time, we trade a larger future good for a smaller present one.

The discount rate varies by domain. We discount money less than we discount health. We discount our own outcomes less than we discount outcomes for strangers. We discount predictable events less than uncertain ones. But the direction is always the same: closer means more valuable.

Where discounting misleads is in decisions where the future actually matters more than the present. Retirement savings compound over decades—the early contributions have exponential impact. Health habits accumulate—the difference between thirty years of exercise and thirty years of sedentary life is enormous. Education, relationships, reputation: all build slowly and pay out over time. These are domains where the math of discounting is backwards. The future reward isn't just bigger, it's *much* bigger, big enough that even after psychological discounting it should dominate. But it doesn't feel that way.

The workaround is to bring the future closer. Make it concrete. Monthly retirement projections instead of abstract compound interest. Photos of your future self instead of vague notions of aging. Scheduled check-ins instead of eventual goals. The less abstract the future, the less we discount it.

Or change the immediate option. Don't choose between exercising today and relaxing today. Choose between exercising today and feeling guilty today. The immediate comparison shifts, and suddenly the future-focused choice competes better.

The model's power is in revealing that what feels like preference is often just proximity. You don't actually value the candy bar more than your health. You just value *now* more than *later*. Once you see that pattern, you can design around it. You can't eliminate discounting—it's too deep in the wiring—but you can account for it. Build systems that work with the bias instead of against it. Automate the future-facing choice so it doesn't compete with the present one. Make the future feel close enough to matter.

The question isn't whether you discount the future. You do. Everyone does. The question is whether you'll let it determine your outcomes.
