# Schelling Points

***Where strangers meet without planning***

> **Section:** Coordination & Game Theory

In 1960, the economist Thomas Schelling asked students at Yale a simple question: You need to meet someone in New York City tomorrow, but you can't communicate with them. Where do you go?

Most chose the information booth at Grand Central Terminal. Same time too — noon.

They had never discussed this. They didn't know each other. But somehow, without words or signals, they converged on the same answer. Grand Central wasn't objectively better than Penn Station or Times Square. It was just *obvious* in a way that's hard to explain.

Schelling called these focal points — places, times, or solutions that people naturally coordinate around without explicit agreement. They work because humans aren't random choice machines. We're pattern recognizers with shared cultural knowledge. When we can't communicate, we guess what others will guess. And often, we're right.

The classic example isn't a meeting place but a number. If you and a stranger both have to pick the same number between 1 and 100 to win a prize, what do you choose? Most people pick 1, 50, or 100. The middle and the edges. The numbers that feel like they *mean* something.

This mechanism operates everywhere coordination is needed but communication is difficult. During World War II, paratroopers scattered over France would regroup at the first church they could find — not because churches were strategically important, but because everyone assumed everyone else would go there. After an earthquake cuts phone lines, family members head to a predetermined house, even if they never explicitly agreed on it. It was just understood.

Nations draw borders at rivers and mountain ranges not because these are natural political boundaries, but because they're features everyone can see and agree on. "This side of the river" is easier to coordinate around than "seventeen kilometers west of the old oak tree."

Markets create Schelling points too. When a new technology emerges — say, USB-C charging — it can become standard not because it's technically superior to every alternative, but because everyone expects everyone else to adopt it. The expectation becomes self-fulfilling. Traders coordinate on round numbers: a stock "breaking through $100" matters more psychologically than crossing $99.47, even though the numbers are arbitrary.

The power of these focal points is that they solve coordination problems without negotiation. But this is also their danger. They can lock in arbitrary or suboptimal solutions. QWERTY keyboards persist not because they're the best layout, but because everyone expects everyone else to use them. The first solution that achieves coordination becomes hard to dislodge, even when better alternatives emerge.

Schelling points mislead when we mistake salience for correctness. Just because everyone naturally gravitates toward a solution doesn't mean it's optimal. It means it's obvious. The meeting spot, the round number, the familiar standard — these win by being memorable, not by being best.

In negotiation, recognizing Schelling points gives you an advantage. Fifty-fifty splits feel fair even when they're not equitable. The original asking price anchors the discussion. The first proposal that sounds "reasonable" often wins not because it's correct, but because it's the focal point everyone can see.

The question Schelling really answered wasn't where people meet. It's how people coordinate when they can't talk. They don't optimize. They don't calculate. They look for the solution that everyone else will see as obvious. And in that shared obviousness, strangers find each other at Grand Central at noon.

The model teaches you to see invisible coordination mechanisms. But it also teaches you to question them. Just because everyone's meeting there doesn't mean it's the right place.
