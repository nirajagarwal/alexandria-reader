# Moral Hazard

***When protection changes behavior***

> **Section:** Economics & Incentives

In 2008, Jamie Dimon testified before Congress about JPMorgan Chase's role in the financial crisis. A senator asked him directly: If the government hadn't bailed out Bear Stearns, would banks have been more careful in their lending? Dimon paused. "Probably," he said.

That single word captures moral hazard.

The concept comes from insurance, where it was first observed in the 18th century. Fire insurance companies noticed something odd: insured buildings burned down more often than uninsured ones. Not because of arson, necessarily, but because people with insurance were less careful. They stored flammable materials differently. They checked their chimneys less often. The protection itself changed their behavior.

Moral hazard occurs when protection from consequences alters the actions that lead to those consequences. The person taking the risk no longer bears its full cost, so they take more of it. The bank lends recklessly because it knows the government will intervene. The driver speeds because airbags will cushion the crash. The gambler doubles down because someone else covers the losses.

The mechanism is simple: separate risk from consequence, and behavior shifts. It doesn't require malice or even consciousness. A perfectly honest person with comprehensive health insurance visits the doctor more often than they would if paying out of pocket. Not because they're trying to cheat the system, but because the price signal — the thing that normally moderates demand — has been removed.

This makes moral hazard distinct from ordinary bad behavior. It's structural, not personal. Change the incentives, and even good people respond. Which is why it's so hard to eliminate. You can't solve it by finding better people. You have to redesign the system.

The classic response is skin in the game: make sure people bear enough of the cost that they stay careful. Insurance policies have deductibles. Bank executives face clawbacks. Even small co-pays change how often people use medical services. The goal isn't to eliminate protection — insurance and safety nets serve real purposes — but to keep the person protected from gambling with house money.

But this creates a dilemma. Too much exposure and people can't afford the protection they need. Too little and they take dangerous risks. Every insurance policy, every guarantee, every promise of rescue walks this line. The financial crisis showed what happens when the line is drawn wrong: institutions became "too big to fail," which meant they were big enough to take catastrophic risks because someone else would pay for the cleanup.

Moral hazard also appears in less obvious places. Unlimited sick leave can reduce the stigma of staying home when genuinely ill, but it can also blur the line of necessity. Bankruptcy protection allows entrepreneurship by reducing downside risk, but it can enable reckless borrowing. Even playground safety equipment creates moral hazard — soft surfaces and rounded edges let children play more dangerously, which is sometimes exactly what happens.

The model reveals a truth about human behavior: we are exquisitely sensitive to consequences. Remove them or reduce them, and we recalibrate. Sometimes consciously, often not. The person who drives more aggressively after installing better brakes isn't thinking "I have better brakes, so I can drive faster." They just feel safer, and the behavior follows.

This is why policies that sound purely beneficial often have unexpected effects. Flood insurance increases building in flood plains. Deposit insurance enables riskier banking. Each protection solves one problem while creating exposure to another.

The key insight: protection is never neutral. It always changes the game. The question isn't whether to provide safety nets — sometimes we must — but whether we've designed them to keep people alert to risk. Because the moment someone believes they're fully insulated from consequences, they start making decisions they otherwise wouldn't.

When institutions are too protected, whole systems become fragile. When individuals are too exposed, lives become precarious. The work is finding the balance where people take reasonable risks without becoming reckless, where protection exists without creating the conditions for catastrophe.

Jamie Dimon's "probably" wasn't an admission of wrongdoing. It was an acknowledgment of reality: when you remove the cost of failure, failure becomes more likely.
