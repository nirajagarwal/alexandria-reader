# Feedback Loops

***What happens when outputs become inputs***

> **Section:** Systems

In 1854, London's Broad Street neighborhood was dying. Cholera had killed over 500 people in ten days, and no one knew why. The prevailing theory blamed "bad air" — miasma rising from the Thames. Dr. John Snow suspected something else.

He mapped every death. The pattern was unmistakable: they clustered around the Broad Street water pump. But here was the mystery — the pump water looked clean, tasted fine, and brewery workers who lived nearby weren't getting sick. Snow discovered why: the brewery workers drank beer, not pump water. And the brewery had its own well.

The pump's handle was removed on September 8th. The outbreak ended almost immediately. What had seemed like random tragedy was actually a feedback loop: contaminated water caused disease, which produced waste, which contaminated the water further. Breaking the loop broke the epidemic.

Feedback loops are everywhere because they're fundamental to how systems work. They occur when a process's output becomes its input, creating a cycle that either amplifies (positive feedback) or dampens (negative feedback) the original effect.

Positive feedback loops accelerate change. A product becomes popular, more people use it, which makes it more useful, which makes it more popular. A bank run starts with fear of insolvency, causing withdrawals, which creates actual insolvency, which justifies the fear. The name is misleading — "positive" doesn't mean good. It means self-reinforcing. These loops create exponential growth or collapse.

Negative feedback loops create stability. A thermostat sensing cold triggers heat, which warms the room, which stops the heating. Blood sugar rises, insulin releases, sugar drops. Predator populations grow, prey becomes scarce, predators decline, prey rebounds. These loops maintain equilibrium.

Most real systems contain both. Your reputation operates on positive feedback — one success leads to opportunities that create more success. But it also faces negative feedback — success breeds complacency, or competition, or scrutiny. Understanding which loop dominates at any moment reveals whether a situation will stabilize or spiral.

The critical question is: what controls the loop? In the Broad Street outbreak, the loop ran unchecked because no one understood the mechanism. Once Snow identified the pump as the connection point, intervention became possible. This is true generally: feedback loops are easier to influence at their connection points than at their extremes.

Where feedback loops mislead is in their timing. The delay between action and feedback can hide the loop entirely. It took days for cholera to manifest after someone drank contaminated water. By then, hundreds had drunk from the same pump. If the effect had been immediate, people would have avoided the pump naturally. Delayed feedback means you can't see the loop operating until it's already gained momentum.

They also mislead when we mistake correlation for causation in the cycle. Just because two variables rise together doesn't mean one is feeding back into the other. The brewery workers' immunity didn't come from being healthy workers — it came from drinking different water. Finding the true connection requires tracing the actual mechanism, not just the pattern.

Social media platforms run on positive feedback — engagement generates content, which generates engagement. This seems sustainable until the loop inverts: overuse causes burnout, which reduces quality, which drives users away, creating a downward spiral. The same structure that builds the system can destroy it. The loop doesn't care which direction it runs.

The power of understanding feedback loops is recognizing when you're inside one. When small changes keep amplifying — either growing or shrinking — you're likely in a positive loop. When things keep returning to baseline despite your efforts, you're fighting negative feedback. Neither is inherently better. But knowing which you face changes what actions make sense.

Snow didn't cure cholera in 1854. He broke a feedback loop. Sometimes that's enough.
