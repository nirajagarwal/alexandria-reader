# Common Knowledge

***What everyone knows that everyone knows***

> **Section:** Game Theory & Strategy

In 1997, a trader named Victor Niederhoffer lost $130 million in a single day. His hedge fund collapsed. The loss came from selling options on the Thai baht, betting it wouldn't collapse. It did. But the strange part wasn't the bet itself — plenty of smart people thought Thailand's currency was overvalued. The strange part was the timing. Niederhoffer held his position even as everyone else was running for the exits. He knew the baht was in trouble. What he missed was that everyone knew everyone else knew it was in trouble.

That difference — between knowing something and knowing that others know it — is common knowledge. And it changes everything.

Most information is private knowledge. You know your own plans. A doctor knows your test results. An executive knows her company's real numbers. This kind of knowledge affects your decisions, but it doesn't necessarily affect anyone else's.

Common knowledge is different. It's not just that multiple people know something. It's that they know that others know it. And they know that others know that they know it. The knowledge is public and recursive. Everyone can see everyone else seeing it.

This matters because humans don't just react to facts. We react to how we expect others to react to facts. And that expectation changes when information becomes common knowledge.

Consider a company with a weak CEO. For years, executives privately doubt his competence. Each one thinks they might be alone in their concern, or that speaking up would be career suicide. The CEO survives. But then he makes a visible mistake in an all-hands meeting. Suddenly everyone knows that everyone else saw it. The private doubts become common knowledge. Within months, he's forced out. The facts didn't change. The structure of the knowledge changed.

Or take a bank run. Many depositors might privately worry their bank is unstable. But as long as that worry stays private, the bank survives — most people assume others aren't worried, so they don't withdraw their money. But once the worry becomes common knowledge — a news story, a line outside a branch — the calculation flips. Now each depositor knows that others know the bank might be in trouble, which means they expect others to withdraw, which means they should withdraw too. The bank fails not because the underlying facts changed, but because the knowledge structure changed.

This is why scandals often seem to explode suddenly. The information was known by many people for years. But it was distributed privately, in whispers and winks. Once it becomes public — a news report, a lawsuit, a viral post — it becomes common knowledge. Now everyone knows that everyone else knows. The coordination problem dissolves. People can act on what they've long suspected.

The model explains why certain types of communication matter more than others. A rumor might spread widely without becoming common knowledge — you hear it from one person, but you don't know who else has heard it. A public announcement creates common knowledge instantly. Everyone receives the same information and knows that everyone else received it. This is why dictators care so much about controlling public gatherings and mass media. Private dissent is manageable. Common knowledge of dissent is regime-threatening.

Markets are particularly sensitive to this distinction. Prices aggregate private information — my belief about a stock, your belief, thousands of others' beliefs. But when information becomes common knowledge, it can trigger cascades. Everyone knows that everyone else knows, so everyone expects everyone else to act, so everyone acts immediately. This is why market crashes can be so swift and severe. The underlying facts may have been deteriorating for months. But once the deterioration becomes common knowledge, the coordination happens in minutes.

The model can mislead in two ways. First, not everything that seems like common knowledge actually is. You might think everyone knows something because it's "obvious" or "everywhere," but people attend to different things. The intellectual world you inhabit is not the world most people inhabit. Second, common knowledge can make you overcorrect. Just because everyone knows something doesn't mean they'll all act on it the same way, or at the same time. The coordination problem might still be real.

But understanding the distinction makes you better at reading situations. When you notice information becoming common knowledge, you know to watch for coordinated action. When you want to create change, you know that private persuasion isn't enough — you need to make the information public and recursive. And when you're navigating crises, you know that managing what people know isn't enough. You have to manage what people know that others know.

Victor Niederhoffer knew Thailand was in trouble. He failed to see that it had become common knowledge. That difference cost him everything.
