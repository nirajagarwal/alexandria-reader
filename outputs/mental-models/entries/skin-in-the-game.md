# Skin in the Game

***Why risk and reward must travel together***

> **Section:** Incentives and Consequences

In 2008, while millions of Americans lost their homes to foreclosure, the executives who had packaged and sold the toxic mortgages walked away with their bonuses intact. They had recommended risks they would never personally bear. Their incentives pointed one direction — maximize short-term profits — while the consequences of failure fell entirely on others.

This was not an accident of design. It was the design.

Skin in the game is the principle that people who make decisions should face the consequences of those decisions. Not just the upside. The downside too. When risk and reward travel together, decision-making becomes honest. When they separate, it becomes reckless.

The concept is older than economics. Ancient Babylonian law required that if a building collapsed and killed the owner's son, the builder's son would be executed. Brutal, yes. But it aligned incentives with outcomes in a way no contract could. Roman engineers stood under the bridges they built when the scaffolding came down. Ship captains were the last to leave sinking vessels. These weren't just traditions. They were information systems. They separated people who actually knew what they were doing from people who only claimed to.

The mechanism is simple: when you can't escape the consequences of being wrong, you become very interested in being right. Not right in theory. Right in practice. Right in the particular situation with all its messy details. You stop optimizing for looking smart and start optimizing for actual outcomes.

Modern life has found countless ways to break this connection. Financial advisors who earn fees whether their advice makes you money or not. Pundits who face no penalty for wrong predictions. Consultants who recommend strategies they'll never have to execute. Bureaucrats who write regulations they'll never have to follow. Each separation creates the same problem: decisions made by people who don't pay the price.

The absence of skin in the game doesn't just produce bad decisions. It produces a specific kind of bad decision — the one that looks good on paper but fails in reality. When you won't face the consequences, you can afford to ignore second-order effects, implementation challenges, and rare but catastrophic risks. You can optimize for the presentation instead of the outcome. You can confuse the map with the territory because you'll never actually have to walk the terrain.

But the principle cuts both ways. Skin in the game isn't just about downside risk. It's about genuine participation. The restaurant owner who eats in her own restaurant. The software engineer who uses the product she builds. The CEO who holds company stock through retirement. These aren't just aligned incentives. They're information flows. When you live with your own decisions, you notice what works and what doesn't. You feel the rough edges. You discover the problems that don't show up in metrics.

The model breaks down when consequences become too severe relative to decisions, creating paralysis instead of care. And not all expertise can be validated by personal risk — the archaeologist studying ancient civilizations can't exactly live through them. But for decisions that affect others, the question remains sharp: Does the person recommending this choice have anything real at stake?

The next time someone offers advice or sells a solution, ask a simple question: What do they lose if they're wrong? If the answer is "nothing," you're not getting their best thinking. You're getting their most convenient thinking. Real wisdom requires real stakes.

The collapse of 2008 didn't happen because people didn't understand risk. It happened because the people making the decisions about risk had found a way to make it someone else's problem.
