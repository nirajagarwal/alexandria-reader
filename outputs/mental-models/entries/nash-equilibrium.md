# Nash Equilibrium

***When everyone's best move depends on everyone else's best move***

> **Section:** Game Theory and Strategy

In 1950, a 21-year-old mathematician named John Nash submitted a 27-page doctoral dissertation that would reshape economics, biology, and military strategy. The idea was simple enough to explain in a paragraph but powerful enough to win a Nobel Prize: sometimes the most rational choice for everyone individually produces an outcome that's worse for everyone collectively.

Nash was studying games — not poker or chess, but any situation where your best decision depends on what others decide. He discovered something unsettling: there are moments when everyone is making their best possible choice given what everyone else is doing, yet no one can improve their position by changing strategy. The system is locked.

Consider rush hour traffic. You're stuck on the highway, barely moving. You notice an exit with a side route that looks faster. Should you take it? If you're the only one who thinks of it, yes — you'll save time. But if everyone has the same thought, the side streets become as congested as the highway. Eventually the system settles: some drivers take the highway, some take side streets, and everyone's commute is equally miserable. No single driver can improve their situation by switching routes. That's Nash Equilibrium. Everyone is doing their best, and everyone is stuck.

The equilibrium isn't about optimal outcomes. It's about stability. It's the configuration where no player has an incentive to deviate, even if a different arrangement would make everyone better off.

Take the classic example that made Nash famous: two prisoners, held separately, each offered a deal. Betray your partner and you walk free while they serve ten years. Stay silent and you both serve one year. If both betray, you each serve five years. The individually rational choice — the one that protects you regardless of what your partner does — is to betray. So both prisoners betray each other and serve five years, even though mutual silence would have given them each just one.

This pattern appears everywhere. Nations stockpile weapons they'd prefer not to build, because unilateral disarmament is worse than mutual armament. Companies slash prices in competitive markets until no one profits. Social media platforms optimize for engagement even when it degrades the user experience, because the first platform to prioritize quality over engagement loses users to competitors.

Nash Equilibrium explains why rational actors produce irrational systems. It's not that people are stupid or short-sighted. It's that the incentive structure makes certain outcomes inevitable. You can see the problem clearly, understand that everyone would benefit from cooperation, and still be unable to escape the equilibrium without changing the rules of the game itself.

This is where the model becomes most useful: it reveals when individual rationality needs external support. If you're trapped in a Nash Equilibrium, personal willpower won't save you. Neither will education or awareness. You need to change the incentive structure — add penalties for defection, rewards for cooperation, or binding commitments that make alternative strategies viable.

But Nash Equilibrium has limits. It assumes perfect rationality, which humans rarely display. It treats preferences as fixed, when they often shift. Most important, it identifies stable states but doesn't tell you whether they're good, fair, or worth maintaining. Some equilibria are efficient. Others are disasters everyone recognizes but no one can escape alone.

The model also multiplies. Most real situations have multiple Nash Equilibria — different stable configurations depending on expectations and history. Which one you land in matters enormously, but the model itself doesn't predict it. This is why conventions, norms, and focal points matter so much. They coordinate us toward specific equilibria.

When you see Nash Equilibrium operating, you're seeing the gap between individual and collective rationality. The question isn't whether people are making mistakes. The question is whether the game itself needs redesigning.
