# Anchoring

***Why the first number you hear shapes every number after it***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases

In 1974, psychologists Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman asked people to estimate what percentage of African nations belonged to the United Nations. Before answering, each person watched a wheel of fortune spin and land on a number between 0 and 100. The wheel was rigged. Some people saw it land on 10, others on 65.

The wheel had nothing to do with UN membership. Everyone knew this. Yet the number it showed changed their estimates dramatically. People who saw 10 guessed an average of 25 percent. People who saw 65 guessed 45 percent. A random number, presented seconds earlier, had yanked their answers in its direction.

This is anchoring: the first number you encounter becomes a reference point that pulls all subsequent judgments toward it, even when that first number is arbitrary or irrelevant.

The effect appears everywhere. Real estate agents show you an overpriced house first, and suddenly the merely expensive house feels reasonable. A restaurant lists a $200 steak on the menu, making the $85 ribeye seem modest. A software company announces a "50% off" sale, and you forget to ask whether the original price was ever real. Job candidates who name their salary expectations first often do better than those who wait — the first number spoken becomes the anchor around which negotiations revolve.

What makes anchoring dangerous is its invisibility. You don't feel yourself being influenced. The anchor doesn't announce itself. You simply arrive at a judgment that feels like your own, unaware that it was shaped by whatever number happened to come first.

The mechanism is partly adjustment. When you encounter an anchor, your mind uses it as a starting point and adjusts away from it. But the adjustment is usually insufficient. If someone asks whether Gandhi was more or less than 140 years old when he died, you know 140 is absurd. You adjust downward. But not far enough. People given this anchor estimate Gandhi died around 67. People given an anchor of 9 estimate around 50. The truth is 78. Both groups adjusted, but neither escaped the anchor's gravity.

Context makes it worse. When you're uncertain — estimating something unfamiliar, negotiating in a new domain, making a decision without clear comparables — anchors gain power. Your mind searches for footholds, and the anchor, however arbitrary, offers one.

The model has limits. Extreme anchors can backfire. If a car salesman opens with a price so high it insults your intelligence, you might reject not just the number but the entire negotiation. And anchors work less well on experts in their domain. A seasoned real estate investor won't be swayed by an amateur's asking price the way a first-time buyer might be.

Knowing about anchoring doesn't make you immune to it. Tversky and Kahneman demonstrated this repeatedly. Even researchers who studied the effect fell prey to it in experiments. But awareness creates a chance to intervene. When a number appears early in a decision, you can ask: Where did this come from? Is it meaningful, or just the first thing I heard? Am I adjusting enough, or am I still in its orbit?

The clearest defense is to generate your own anchor before anyone else can. Do the research. Know the market. Walk in with a number you've thought through independently. Because once an anchor drops, everything moves toward it. Including you.
