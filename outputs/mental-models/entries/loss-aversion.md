# Loss Aversion

***Why giving up hurts more than gaining feels good***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases

In 1979, psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky asked people a simple question: Would you accept a bet that gives you a 50% chance of winning $150 and a 50% chance of losing $100?

Most people said no.

Mathematically, this makes no sense. The expected value is positive—you should take the bet. But the rejection wasn't irrational in the way people usually mean. It revealed something deeper: losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good.

Kahneman and Tversky called this loss aversion. The pain of losing $100 outweighs the pleasure of winning $150, even though one is objectively larger than the other. Our emotional scales are tilted. Loss registers more intensely than gain.

This isn't a flaw to fix. It's a feature that usually serves us. In environments where losing food or shelter could mean death, being more sensitive to losses than gains kept our ancestors alive. The asymmetry protected them. It protects us too—most of the time.

But in modern decision-making, loss aversion often leads us astray.

Consider the investor who refuses to sell a declining stock. She's down 30%. Selling means accepting the loss—making it real, final. So she holds, hoping it will recover, even as better opportunities pass by. She's not thinking about future returns. She's avoiding the pain of admitting she lost.

Or the executive who keeps funding a failing project. Millions already spent. Canceling means those millions were wasted. So he authorizes another round of funding, then another. The loss grows larger because acknowledging the existing loss feels unbearable.

Or the person who stays in the wrong job, the wrong city, the wrong relationship. Leaving means giving up what they have. Staying means avoiding that loss, even if what they have is making them miserable. The known loss—the life they're not living—registers less than the imagined loss of what they'd give up.

Loss aversion makes us hold when we should fold. It makes us risk more to avoid accepting a loss than we'd ever risk to achieve an equivalent gain. It turns sunk costs into traps. (See also: Sunk Cost Fallacy.)

The asymmetry shows up everywhere. People work harder to avoid losing $100 than to gain $100. Companies fight harder to keep existing customers than to win new ones—even when the economics are identical. Negotiators make worse deals to avoid small concessions than to capture much larger gains.

Understanding loss aversion doesn't make the pain disappear. You'll still feel it. But you can notice when you're making a decision to avoid pain rather than to move toward something better. You can ask: Am I holding this position because I genuinely think it's the right call, or because selling means admitting I was wrong? Am I staying because this is where I want to be, or because leaving means losing what I've invested?

The key is recognizing that your emotional response—this feels like too much to give up—is information, not instruction. The discomfort of loss is real. But it's often misleading about what matters.

What you've already lost is gone. The only question that matters is: What's the best move from here?

**When it misleads:** Loss aversion assumes all losses are equal, but they're not. Losing your health is not the same as losing money. And sometimes what feels like a loss—quitting a job, ending a relationship, moving cities—is actually freedom from a sunk cost. The pain of the transition is temporary. The cost of staying is permanent.
