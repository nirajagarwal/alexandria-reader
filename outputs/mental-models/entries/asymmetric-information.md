# Asymmetric Information

***When one side knows more than the other***

> **Section:** Markets & Incentives

In 1970, economist George Akerlof wrote a paper about used cars that would win him a Nobel Prize thirty years later. The insight was simple but devastating: when buyers can't tell good cars from bad ones, the market falls apart.

Here's why. A seller knows whether their car is reliable or a lemon. The buyer doesn't. So buyers offer a price somewhere in the middle—too low for truly good cars, too high for lemons. Owners of good cars withdraw from the market. Only lemons remain. Buyers, sensing this, lower their offers further. More sellers exit. The market "unravels" until only the worst products trade, if any trade at all.

This is asymmetric information: one party knows something the other doesn't, and that gap changes everything.

It explains why health insurance is expensive for healthy people. Why companies spend millions on warranties and certifications. Why job interviews are so elaborate. Why you pay a premium for a "certified pre-owned" car even though certification costs the dealer almost nothing.

The information gap doesn't just affect price. It affects whether transactions happen at all.

Consider hiring. You need a software engineer. Candidates know their own skill level. You don't. You can't offer top salary to everyone—you'd go broke. But if you offer average salary, the best candidates go elsewhere. You're left interviewing people who couldn't get better offers. This is why companies create elaborate hiring processes: coding tests, multi-round interviews, reference checks. They're trying to reduce the information asymmetry. It's expensive and imperfect, but doing nothing is worse.

Or medical insurance. You know if you smoke, if you exercise, if your family has a history of heart disease. The insurance company doesn't, or didn't until recently. If they charge average premiums, healthy people opt out—why pay for coverage they won't use? This leaves the insurer with only sick people, forcing premiums up, driving more healthy people away. The market collapses unless someone—usually the government—forces everyone to participate.

The key insight is that information asymmetry creates incentive problems. The informed party can exploit their advantage. The uninformed party knows this and responds defensively. Both sides end up worse off than if information were shared.

Markets respond in predictable ways. Sellers signal quality through warranties, brands, certifications—costly actions that only high-quality providers would take. Buyers screen through testing, inspection, background checks. Third parties emerge: consumer reports, rating agencies, auditors. All trying to close the information gap.

But the gap never fully closes. And sometimes the attempted solutions create new problems. Rating agencies can be captured by those they rate. Certifications can become rubber stamps. Warranties can be written in impenetrable legal language. The asymmetry shifts but doesn't disappear.

The model has limits. It assumes the informed party knows they have an advantage and might exploit it. Sometimes they don't. It assumes information gaps can't be closed through conversation and trust. Sometimes they can. It assumes people respond rationally to incentives. Sometimes they're just trying to do right by others.

But those exceptions prove the rule. When you notice a market that seems needlessly complicated—insurance forms, job requirements, return policies—asymmetric information is often the reason. The complexity isn't bureaucratic sadism. It's the market trying to function despite fundamental uncertainty about who knows what.

The next time you buy something expensive secondhand, notice your hesitation. Notice how much you value seller reputation, return policies, third-party inspections. You're responding to asymmetric information. The seller knows if it works. You don't. And that gap shapes every term of the deal.
