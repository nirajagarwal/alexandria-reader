# When Not to Decide

***The strategic value of keeping your options open***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1962, a young computer scientist named Butler Lampson joined a startup called Berkeley Computer Corporation. The company offered him a choice: take a higher salary now, or accept stock options that might be worth more later. He chose the salary. The company went bankrupt eighteen months later. "Best decision I never knew I was making," he later said.

The decision looked binary. Salary or stock. But there was a third option Lampson didn't see at the time: wait. Delay the decision. Watch the company's trajectory for a few months before committing. He couldn't have predicted the bankruptcy, but he could have preserved the option to respond to new information.

Most decisions feel urgent. Your mind presents them as now-or-never choices. But many decisions improve with delay—not because you'll magically know more, but because the decision itself becomes clearer or unnecessary.

This is **optionality**—the value in keeping your choices open. It's not indecision. It's recognizing that some decisions cost you more in closed doors than they gain you in commitment.

Options have three sources of value. First, **irreversibility**. The more permanent a choice, the more valuable it is to delay. Hiring someone is harder to undo than trying a contractor first. Buying a house closes more doors than renting. Marriage is harder to exit than dating. When a choice is difficult to reverse, the option to wait is often worth more than the expected value of deciding now.

Second, **information arrival**. Some situations naturally clarify themselves. A job candidate's references come back. The market reveals whether customers actually want your product. Your body tells you if the medical treatment is working. When you can expect to learn something material in the near term, delay has quantifiable value. You're not procrastinating—you're paying a small cost (the waiting period) to make a better decision.

Third, **alternatives in motion**. Sometimes the best choice isn't on the table yet. The candidate you really want hasn't applied. The technology you need is six months from release. The person you're negotiating with might come back with a better offer. Deciding too early means settling for a local maximum when a better option is approaching.

The opposite is also true. Some decisions *lose* value when delayed. When a window is closing, optionality becomes a trap. If you're interviewing for a job and they need an answer by Friday, Thursday evening is not the time to keep your options open. If your competitor is six months ahead and moving fast, "wait and see" is just dignified surrender.

The question is not "should I decide?" The question is "what happens if I wait?" 

If waiting means you learn something material, or the decision becomes reversible, or a better option appears—then delay has value. The cost of waiting (the option fee) needs to be less than the value of the information or flexibility you gain.

If waiting means the opportunity vanishes, or the cost of delay compounds, or you're just avoiding discomfort—then optionality is expensive hesitation dressed up as strategy.

Lampson couldn't have known Berkeley Computer would fail. But he could have asked: "What will I learn in three months that I don't know now? What does waiting cost me? Can I structure this so I'm not locked in immediately?" The bankruptcy made his choice look brilliant. But the real mistake was treating a reversible decision as if it had to be made immediately.

The most expensive decisions are often the ones you didn't realize you were making—the moments you closed a door because you didn't notice it was worth keeping open.
