# Homeostasis

***Why systems resist the very changes they need***

> **Section:** Systems Thinking

In 1854, French physiologist Claude Bernard opened a rabbit and made a discovery that troubled him. He had expected the animal's blood temperature to drop as it sat exposed on his laboratory table. It didn't. Despite the cold room and open incision, the rabbit's body maintained 38 degrees Celsius with stubborn precision.

Bernard spent the next twenty years puzzling over this. How did living things hold steady in a changing world? He called it the "milieu intérieur" — the internal environment that organisms defend against external chaos. Later, physiologist Walter Cannon gave it a simpler name: homeostasis. The body as thermostat.

But the implications reach far beyond biology.

Every stable system — a body, a company, a democracy, a friendship — maintains itself through negative feedback loops. When something pushes the system away from its set point, forces activate to push it back. Temperature rises, you sweat. Blood sugar spikes, insulin flows. Stock prices climb too high, sellers emerge. A child acts out, parents respond with structure.

This is homeostasis: the automatic resistance to change.

It sounds good. Stability is good, right? But here's the problem: homeostatic systems don't distinguish between healthy equilibrium and toxic stasis. They just defend whatever normal has become.

A company develops an inefficient approval process. People complain. But the system has adapted to it — workflows, staffing, expectations. Any attempt to change it triggers antibodies: "This is how we've always done it." "We tried something like that once." "Let's form a committee to study it." The immune response isn't malicious. It's homeostatic. The organization is maintaining itself against the perceived threat of change.

An alcoholic tries to quit. Their body fights back with withdrawal symptoms. Their social circle — built around drinking — responds with invitation and pressure. "One drink won't hurt." "You're no fun anymore." The system reasserting its equilibrium.

You try to change a personal habit. Wake earlier, eat better, exercise more. For a few days, you succeed. Then the old patterns reassert themselves with surprising force. Fatigue. Rationalization. The comfort of familiar routines. Your life, like Bernard's rabbit, defends its set point.

Homeostasis explains why meaningful change is so hard. It's not that people lack willpower or systems lack good intentions. It's that stability has its own momentum. Every deviation from normal activates correction mechanisms, most of them invisible until they push back.

**Where homeostasis misleads:** Not all stability is health. A system can be homeostatic and still be dying. The rabbit maintains 38 degrees until the moment it doesn't. A marriage can maintain its routines — dinner at six, television at eight, silence in between — long after intimacy has died. The system is stable. The system is also broken. Homeostasis preserves structure, not vitality.

The model also suggests more resistance than really exists. Systems do change. They adapt, they learn, they transform. But transformation requires either overwhelming the homeostatic response or slowly shifting the set point itself. Revolution or evolution. Shock or patience. There is rarely a middle path.

Understanding homeostasis changes how you approach change. If you're trying to shift a system — an organization, a relationship, your own behavior — you're not just introducing something new. You're fighting an invisible force that wants to return things to normal. The question isn't whether the change is good. The question is whether you can sustain it long enough for the system to accept a new normal.

Bernard's rabbit taught us that living things don't just endure their environment. They defend against it. So do all the systems we build and inhabit. Stability is a feature, not a bug. Until the moment it becomes a cage.

The system wants equilibrium. Sometimes equilibrium is the enemy.
