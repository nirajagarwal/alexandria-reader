# Multiplicative Processes

***Why small differences compound into vast gulfs***

> **Section:** Systems & Emergence

In 1626, Peter Minuit bought Manhattan from the Lenape people for trade goods worth about 60 guilders — roughly $1,000 in today's money. Four hundred years later, Manhattan's land alone is worth over $1 trillion. Every economist knows this story. Few pause to notice what it actually demonstrates.

The common telling focuses on the bad deal. The real lesson is about multiplication.

If the Lenape had invested that $1,000 at a 7% annual return, it would be worth $3.6 trillion today. Not through cleverness or luck, but through time and compounding. The process that built Manhattan's value is the same process that would have made the payment worth more. What matters isn't the starting amount. What matters is whether growth multiplies.

A multiplicative process is one where each step's outcome depends on the previous step's result. Growth builds on growth. Decline accelerates decline. A 10% gain followed by another 10% gain doesn't equal 20% — it equals 21%. A 10% loss followed by another 10% loss doesn't equal -20% — it equals -19%. The difference seems small at first. Over time, it becomes everything.

Consider two writers. One publishes a book that sells 1,000 copies. The second publishes a book that sells 1,200 copies — 20% more. If both write ten more books and maintain the same ratio, the second writer doesn't sell 2,000 more books total. They sell over 110,000 more. The gap expands with each multiplication.

This is why initial advantages persist. It's why inequality grows even when opportunities seem equal. Two people start with similar skills. One gets slightly better opportunities early on. Those opportunities lead to slightly better outcomes. Those outcomes lead to more opportunities. The process feeds itself. After twenty iterations, the gap is not 20% — it's exponential.

The mechanism appears everywhere. Viral content doesn't spread through addition. Each share multiplies the potential audience. Cities don't grow by adding people linearly. Each new resident adds network effects that attract more residents. Technologies don't improve by fixed increments. Each advancement creates a platform for the next.

The math is deceptive because our intuition is additive. We expect things to grow by the same amount each period. Ten dollars, then twenty, then thirty. But multiplicative processes grow by the same *percentage*. Ten dollars, then eleven, then twelve-point-one. The early stages look similar. After enough time, the multiplicative process runs away.

This explains why small interventions matter so much early in a process. A 5% improvement in year one of a career has vastly more impact than the same 5% improvement in year twenty. The year-one improvement gets multiplied by every subsequent year. It's also why decline is so hard to reverse. Each period of contraction makes the next period smaller. You need larger and larger percentage gains just to return to where you started.

But multiplicative processes mislead in the other direction too. They make us overestimate the impact of recent changes. A company's revenue grows 50% in a year, and investors assume that's the new normal. But if the growth was multiplicative — built on a smaller base — maintaining it requires finding 50% more growth on a much larger number. The multiplication that created the explosion makes sustaining it nearly impossible.

The other failure mode: assuming multiplication continues forever. No process compounds indefinitely in the real world. Eventually, resources run out. Markets saturate. Networks reach their limit. The multiplicative phase ends, and growth becomes additive again, or stops entirely. Mistaking a multiplicative process for a permanent state leads to bubbles, bad predictions, and collapsed empires.

The model's power is in recognition. When you see a multiplicative process, you know small differences early matter more than large efforts later. You know advantages tend to compound, not equalize. You know what looks like exponential success might just be the middle of a curve that will flatten. And you know that in a multiplicative world, getting the direction right matters more than getting the magnitude right.

Manhattan wasn't won or lost in 1626. It was lost in the decision about what kind of process would follow.
