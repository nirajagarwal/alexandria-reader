# Inversion

***Solving from the opposite direction***

> **Section:** Problem-Solving Models

In 1912, a mathematician named George Pólya was stuck. He'd been wrestling with a proof for weeks, trying to show that a particular mathematical statement was true. Every approach led nowhere. Then he stopped trying to prove it was true and asked instead: what would make it false?

Within an hour, he had his answer. By assuming the opposite and looking for contradictions, the path became clear. The proof that had eluded him for weeks revealed itself when he reversed the question.

Pólya called this inversion, and he spent the rest of his career teaching it. Most problems, he argued, become easier when you flip them around. Instead of asking "How do I succeed?" ask "How would I fail?" Instead of "What should I do?" try "What should I definitely not do?"

The logic is simple but powerful. Failure modes are often more obvious than success paths. It's harder to list everything that makes a good life than everything that ruins one. Harder to define a great business than to spot a doomed one. When you invert, you trade a fuzzy positive goal for a crisp negative boundary. And negative boundaries are easier to see.

Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett's longtime partner, built his investment philosophy around inversion. "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I never go there," he would say, quoting an old joke. Before evaluating whether to buy a company, Munger listed all the ways it could fail. Bad management. Technological disruption. Regulatory changes. Excessive debt. If he couldn't eliminate these failure modes, he didn't invest. He wasn't trying to predict success. He was trying to avoid disaster.

This works because success has many forms, but failure patterns repeat. A startup can win in countless ways, but it dies for a handful of reasons: no customers, ran out of money, co-founders split, built something nobody wanted. A marriage can thrive in infinite configurations, but the failure modes—contempt, dishonesty, neglect—are depressingly consistent.

Inversion forces you to confront these patterns. When a friend asks "How do I have a better relationship with my kids?" the inverted question—"What would guarantee I ruin it?"—produces immediate answers. Be absent. Be critical. Make everything conditional. Never apologize. Once you see what not to do, the positive path often becomes obvious. Or at least clearer.

The method works in design too. Engineers call it "pre-mortem analysis." Before a project launches, the team imagines it has failed catastrophically. Then they work backward: what went wrong? The hard drive corrupted. The user interface confused everyone. The server couldn't handle the load. By inverting success into failure, they surface risks they'd otherwise miss until it was too late.

But inversion has limits. It reveals what to avoid, not always what to pursue. Knowing how not to go broke doesn't tell you how to get rich. Knowing what kills a marriage doesn't guarantee a happy one. Inversion clears the minefield; it doesn't draw the map.

And some problems resist inversion. Creative work, for instance. You can list everything that makes writing bad, but that doesn't produce good writing. The absence of failure isn't the same as the presence of success. Sometimes you need to aim at the target, not just avoid the obstacles.

Still, when you're stuck—when the forward path is murky and solutions won't come—flip the problem around. Ask what you're trying to prevent instead of what you're trying to achieve. The view from the opposite direction often shows what the direct approach obscures.

As Pólya discovered, some proofs only reveal themselves backward.
