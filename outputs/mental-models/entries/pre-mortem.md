# Pre-Mortem

***Imagining failure before it happens***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1986, psychologist Gary Klein was working with a fire department when he noticed something odd. Before every high-stakes operation, the commander would gather the crew and ask: "What could go wrong?" Not in a general, hand-waving way. Specifically. Concretely. The pump fails. The roof collapses. Someone misses the radio call. They imagined the disaster, then worked backward to prevent it.

Klein turned this into a formal technique. Before launching a project, assume it has failed. Completely, spectacularly failed. Now explain why. Write the failure memo. The market never materialized. The technology didn't scale. The key person quit. You ran out of money in month seven, not month twelve.

This is the pre-mortem. It flips the usual planning question. Instead of "What could go wrong?" you ask "What did go wrong?" The shift in tense changes everything.

When you ask what could go wrong, people hedge. They soften. They name obvious risks but avoid uncomfortable truths. They don't want to seem negative or disloyal. They don't want to jinx the project. So they list the safe failures: "We might miss the deadline" or "The budget could run over."

But when you declare the project dead and ask why, permission structures flip. Suddenly it's a puzzle to solve, not a critique to suppress. People name the real risks. The CEO's nephew who can't code. The dependency on a vendor everyone knows is flaky. The assumption that users will behave one way when they always behave another. Things people saw but didn't say.

A pre-mortem surfaces hidden knowledge. It pulls intuition into the open. Someone on the team knows the timeline is impossible but hasn't spoken up. Someone else has seen this exact failure mode in their last company. The pre-mortem gives them permission to share what they already know.

It also defeats a cognitive trap. When we plan, we envision success. We imagine the product launch, the positive reviews, the revenue growth. This feels motivating, but it's dangerous. Success narratives smooth over complications. They make the path look straighter than it is. A pre-mortem forces you to imagine the opposite. It makes failure vivid and specific. And specific failures can be prevented.

The technique works best when the failure is truly catastrophic in the scenario. Not "We missed the deadline" but "The company shut down." Not "Users didn't love it" but "Zero users signed up." Go big. Exaggerate. Then trace the path back. If zero users signed up, why? If the company shut down, what was the final blow?

The weaknesses are subtle. A pre-mortem can become a venting session if not structured. It can also create a self-fulfilling prophecy if the team internalizes the failure too deeply. The point is not to kill morale but to sharpen planning. Done well, a pre-mortem builds confidence. You've seen the failure modes. You've addressed them. Now you can move forward with clarity.

Some teams do pre-mortems formally: a written document, a structured meeting. Others use the technique in the moment. Before a big decision, pause. Assume it fails. Why? Even sixty seconds of pre-mortem thinking can surface a risk you wouldn't have named otherwise.

The fire commanders Klein studied didn't do pre-mortems to avoid risk. They did them to take risks intelligently. They knew the work was dangerous. They couldn't eliminate the danger. But they could name it, prepare for it, and decide whether to proceed anyway.

That's what a pre-mortem does. It doesn't stop you from acting. It stops you from acting blind.
