# Red Teaming

***Deliberately attacking your own plans to find what you missed***

> **Section:** Strategy & Decision-Making

In 1967, Israeli intelligence was convinced Egypt wouldn't attack. Every indicator said so. Egypt's military wasn't ready. Their economy was fragile. War would be suicide.

Lieutenant General David Elazar didn't trust the consensus. He assembled a small team and gave them an unusual assignment: become Egypt. Think like Nasser. Find a scenario where attacking Israel makes sense, no matter how unlikely it seems.

The team found one. If Egypt struck first, rapidly, before Israel could fully mobilize, they might inflict enough damage to force a negotiated settlement. It wasn't about winning militarily—it was about changing the political reality. The scenario seemed far-fetched. Elazar took it seriously anyway and quietly increased readiness along the border.

Six years later, in 1973, Egypt attacked on Yom Kippur using almost exactly that strategy. Israel survived, barely, because a handful of officers had practiced thinking like the enemy.

Red teaming is the systematic practice of challenging your own thinking by adopting an adversarial perspective. You deliberately try to break your plan, find its flaws, exploit its weaknesses—before reality does.

It starts with a role reversal. If you're launching a product, someone becomes the competitor trying to destroy it. If you're defending a position, someone becomes the prosecutor. If you're making a strategic plan, someone becomes the world trying to make it fail. The goal isn't to be negative—it's to be thorough. To find failure modes you can't see from inside your own assumptions.

Good red teaming requires genuine opposition. The red team needs permission to be merciless. If they're just going through the motions, offering gentle criticism that won't offend, the exercise is worthless. The value comes from real attempts to demolish the plan. "We could try attacking from the south" is useless. "If I wanted to destroy this company, I would do exactly three things, and here they are" is what you need.

The hardest part is receiving the attack. Every instinct pushes you to defend, to explain why the red team is wrong, to dismiss their scenarios as unrealistic. This is precisely the reflex that makes red teaming necessary. The point isn't whether their specific attack is likely—it's whether your plan has structural weaknesses. A good red team exercise should hurt a little. If it doesn't, you're not doing it right.

When NASA lost the Columbia shuttle in 2003, investigators found that engineers had tried to raise concerns about foam strikes during launch. They were dismissed. The shuttle had been hit before and survived. What would have happened if NASA had red teamed the foam problem—if someone had been tasked with proving, rigorously, that foam could kill the orbiter? They might have found what investigators later did: that at high velocity, foam could punch through heat shielding. The mission might have been saved.

Red teaming misleads when it becomes theater. Many organizations now "do" red teaming because it sounds rigorous. They run sanitized exercises where criticism is softened and fundamental assumptions are off-limits. They listen politely, make minor adjustments, and check the box. This is worse than doing nothing because it creates false confidence.

It also fails when the red team doesn't understand what they're attacking. Effective opposition requires context. You can't break a plan you don't understand. The best red teams study the subject as deeply as the planners did—then use that knowledge to destroy it.

The value of red teaming isn't just in finding specific flaws. It's in cultivating humility about your own perspective. When you practice thinking like your enemy, your competitor, your critic, you remember that your view is partial. That the world contains intelligent forces working against you. That your plan, no matter how good, has vulnerabilities you cannot see alone.

Before you commit to the strategy, the launch, the operation—let someone try to kill it. Then make it stronger. Or, sometimes, realize you shouldn't do it at all.

The question isn't whether your plan can work. It's whether it can survive contact with a world that isn't cooperating.
