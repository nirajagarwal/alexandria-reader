# Hindsight Bias

***Why everything seems obvious after it happens***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases

On October 19, 1987, the stock market crashed. The Dow Jones dropped 22.6% in a single day — the largest one-day percentage decline in history. In the months that followed, financial analysts wrote thousands of pages explaining exactly why it happened: overvaluation, program trading, trade deficits, rising interest rates. Each explanation was clear, logical, and convincing.

But almost none of these experts had predicted the crash beforehand.

Paul Slovic, a psychologist studying risk perception, noticed something strange when he reviewed the analysts' predictions from before the crash. The same people who now wrote with absolute certainty about what caused the collapse had expressed no such certainty when the crash was still in the future. Their pre-crash writing was tentative, qualified, unsure. Their post-crash writing read like they'd known it all along.

They hadn't. But they couldn't see that anymore.

This is hindsight bias: the tendency to see past events as having been more predictable than they actually were. Once we know what happened, we unconsciously rewrite our memory of what we thought would happen. The outcome seems inevitable. We tell ourselves we "knew it all along."

The mechanism is subtle. When you learn an outcome, your mind immediately begins constructing a narrative that makes that outcome sensible. It highlights evidence that pointed toward what happened and downplays evidence that suggested other possibilities. This happens automatically, below conscious awareness. Within minutes, the new narrative feels like what you always believed.

The bias extends beyond predictions. It distorts how we evaluate decisions. A CEO makes a risky investment that succeeds, and we call her brilliant. Another CEO makes the same investment and fails, and we call him reckless. We judge the decision by the outcome, not by the information available when the decision was made. We forget that the future was once unknown.

This creates a pernicious problem for learning. If past events seem inevitable, we can't learn to make better predictions. Why would we? We already "knew" what would happen. We miss the chance to calibrate our judgment, to see where our models of the world failed.

It also makes us overconfident. If we think we predicted the past accurately, we assume we'll predict the future accurately too. We remember our hits and forget our misses — or rather, we misremember our misses as hits.

The bias affects how we judge others as well. We evaluate past decisions with full knowledge of how they turned out. A doctor faces malpractice claims not because her decision was unreasonable given what she knew, but because a patient died and now every warning sign seems obvious. A military commander is criticized for a decision that led to casualties, even though it was defensible given the uncertainty he faced. We demand the past account for the future we now know.

The remedy is deliberate. Before you learn an outcome, write down your prediction and your confidence level. Be specific. "70% chance the project finishes on time." Not "probably." When the outcome arrives, compare what happened to what you predicted. This breaks the automatic rewriting. It forces you to see where you were actually uncertain.

When evaluating past decisions — your own or others' — ask: what did they know then? Not what seems obvious now, but what was knowable at the time. Reconstruct the uncertainty. Imagine the other outcomes that seemed possible before one became real.

The past is not inevitable. It only seems that way from here.
