# Goodhart's Law

***When targets become traps***

> **Section:** Systems Thinking

In 1986, British officials decided to tackle the problem of ambulance response times. The target was clear: ambulances should reach patients within eight minutes. Hospitals were measured, ranked, and rewarded based on this metric. Performance improved dramatically. Success.

Except patients weren't getting better care. They were getting faster ambulances that spent less time at the scene. Paramedics rushed through initial assessments. Some hospitals moved ambulances closer to high-traffic areas, leaving other neighborhoods underserved. A few even allegedly kept ambulances circling near likely emergency spots, burning fuel to pad their numbers. The measure went up. The thing being measured—quality of emergency care—did not.

This is Goodhart's Law in action.

Charles Goodhart was a Bank of England economist studying monetary policy in the 1970s. He noticed that whenever policymakers began targeting a specific economic indicator, that indicator stopped behaving the way it used to. The act of making something a target changed its relationship to the underlying reality. He formalized this observation: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

The mechanism is simple. A good measure is a proxy for something you care about but cannot directly observe or control. Emergency response time is a proxy for quality of emergency care. Student test scores are a proxy for learning. Lines of code are a proxy for programmer productivity. These proxies work as long as no one is optimizing specifically for them.

But make the proxy a target—attach rewards, rankings, or consequences to it—and something shifts. People are clever. They find ways to hit the target that don't serve the underlying goal. Paramedics speed up trips but skip assessments. Teachers teach to the test. Programmers write verbose code or split useful code into multiple files.

The proxy and the goal diverge. The measure becomes corrupted.

This happens everywhere. Wells Fargo employees opened millions of fake accounts to meet sales targets. Soviet factories produced millions of useless nails measured by weight, then millions of useless nails measured by quantity. Corporate managers hit quarterly earnings targets by delaying necessary investments. Researchers chase citation counts by slicing important work into minimum publishable units.

The pattern is always the same: identify what matters, create a simplified measure of it, make that measure consequential, watch people game it.

Goodhart's Law does not mean measurement is futile. It means measurement is dangerous when misunderstood. A measure is useful for diagnosis, not control. The moment you try to steer by a single metric, that metric detaches from what it was measuring.

The solution is not to avoid targets entirely. It is to understand what targets actually do. They focus attention and effort, yes—but they also create pressure to hit the target rather than serve the goal. Good systems use multiple measures, change them periodically, and maintain ways to check if the proxy still tracks reality. They treat measurements as imperfect signals, not as the thing itself.

The deepest version of Goodhart's Law is about meaning. A measure only means something in context. Response time matters because it correlates with outcomes. Test scores matter because they indicate learning. These correlations are not laws of nature. They are patterns that hold under certain conditions. Change the conditions—make the measure high-stakes—and the pattern breaks.

When you see a target being met but the underlying problem persisting, Goodhart's Law is likely at work. The question is not "Why aren't they hitting the target?" but "What are we actually trying to achieve, and is this measure still getting us there?"

Measures are maps, not territories. The moment you forget that, you start optimizing for the map.
