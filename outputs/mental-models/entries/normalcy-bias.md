# Normalcy Bias

***Why we underestimate catastrophe until it arrives***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases & Heuristics

On the morning of September 11, 2001, Rick Rescorla was already evacuating Morgan Stanley employees from the South Tower of the World Trade Center. The first plane had just hit the North Tower. Port Authority announcements told people to stay at their desks. Rescorla ignored them.

He'd been running evacuation drills for years. After the 1993 bombing, he'd concluded the towers were still vulnerable and that most people would freeze in a real emergency. His colleagues thought he was paranoid. That morning, he got 2,687 of Morgan Stanley's 2,700 employees out alive. He went back in to search for stragglers and died when the tower collapsed.

Rescorla understood something most people don't: we are wired to assume tomorrow will look like yesterday. Even when the evidence says otherwise.

This is normalcy bias — the tendency to underestimate both the likelihood of disaster and its potential impact. We see warning signs and interpret them as anomalies. We see unprecedented events and fit them into familiar categories. We wait for official confirmation when we should be moving.

The mechanism is partly evolutionary. For most of human history, stability was the norm. The sun rose. Seasons cycled. Social structures persisted. A brain that treated every unusual event as catastrophic would be paralyzed. Better to assume continuity and occasionally be wrong than to panic constantly.

But in a world with genuine discontinuities — financial crashes, pandemics, technological disruption — normalcy bias becomes dangerous. It's why people ignore evacuation orders during hurricanes. Why investors hold collapsing stocks convinced the market will "come back." Why organizations dismiss existential threats until it's too late.

The bias operates at multiple levels. Individually, we downplay personal risk. "That won't happen to me." Collectively, we downplay systemic risk. "The system has always recovered before." The larger and more abstract the threat, the stronger the bias. Climate change. Antibiotic resistance. Financial system fragility. All fit the pattern: slow-building, unprecedented, therefore easy to normalize away.

Normalcy bias also explains why disasters often have two waves of victims. The first wave doesn't see it coming. The second wave sees it but doesn't believe it. On 9/11, some people who escaped the South Tower after the first plane hit went back upstairs to get their belongings. The Twin Towers had stood for three decades. Buildings that size don't collapse. Except they do.

The antidote isn't paranoia. It's pre-commitment. Rescorla didn't decide to evacuate that morning — he'd decided years earlier what the triggers would be and what he'd do. He'd run the drills until they were automatic. When the moment came, there was no decision to make.

This works in other domains. The investor who sets stop-losses before buying. The organization that runs tabletop exercises for scenarios that seem unlikely. The person who has an escape plan even when everything seems fine. These aren't signs of excessive caution. They're insurance against the moment when your brain will tell you everything is probably okay.

Because everything is probably okay. Until it isn't. And the difference between those who survive discontinuity and those who don't often comes down to whether they planned for it when it still seemed impossible.

The human mind is built for a stable world. When that world ends, the mind needs time to catch up. People who survive catastrophes often describe a period of disbelief, even as the evidence overwhelms them. The best protection isn't trying to override that instinct in the moment. It's building responses that activate before belief catches up.

Normalcy bias is why the unthinkable remains unthinkable until it's unavoidable. And why the people who survive are usually the ones who thought about it anyway.
