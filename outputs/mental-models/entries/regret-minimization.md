# Regret Minimization

***The future-self test for hard decisions***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1994, Jeff Bezos had a problem most people would envy: a senior vice president position at a hedge fund, a six-figure salary, and a bonus coming in three months. He also had an idea that wouldn't leave him alone—selling books online. The internet was growing at 2,300% per year. The opportunity window was closing.

He couldn't calculate the odds. There was no spreadsheet that could tell him whether leaving Wall Street for a garage in Seattle made sense. So he tried something different.

He imagined himself at eighty years old, looking back. Would he regret missing out on the bonus? Not really. Would he regret missing out on participating in this thing called the internet that he thought was going to be a big deal? Absolutely. Would he regret trying and failing? Not even a little.

The decision became obvious. He called it his "regret minimization framework."

The model is simple: When facing a major decision, project yourself to the end of your life and ask which choice you're least likely to regret. Not which is safer. Not which others expect. Which leaves you with fewer what-ifs.

This isn't the same as choosing the exciting option. Adventure isn't always the answer. Sometimes the regret-minimizing choice is staying put—turning down the startup offer to keep the stable job while your kids are young, or not moving across the country to chase a relationship that feels shaky.

The power lies in the mental shift. Most hard decisions paralyze us because we're trying to predict outcomes we can't possibly know. Will the startup succeed? Will I like the new city? Will this relationship work out? We get stuck in analysis paralysis, weighing probabilities we're inventing.

Regret minimization sidesteps all that. It doesn't ask what will happen. It asks what you can live with not knowing. It trades impossible prediction for knowable psychology.

The eighty-year-old version of you has information you don't—they know how the story ends. But they also have something more valuable: perspective. From that vantage point, most of what feels huge right now shrinks. The money you passed up, the apartment you didn't get, the job that went to someone else—future-you probably won't remember. But the risks you didn't take? Those stick.

This is especially useful for Type 2 decisions—the reversible kind (see also: Reversible Decisions). If you can undo it or course-correct, the question becomes even simpler: Will you regret not trying?

**Where it misleads:** Regret minimization can romanticize risk. Not every leap is worth taking just because it makes a good story. And it assumes your eighty-year-old self values adventure and growth above all else. Some people genuinely won't regret the stable path. Some will regret the chaos and instability of constant pivoting.

It also doesn't work for decisions that aren't really about you—leaving a marriage with kids, abandoning a business partner, choices where other people's regrets matter too. The framework is fundamentally self-focused.

And sometimes the urgent drowns out the important. Your eighty-year-old self might not care about the quarterly earnings, but if the quarterly earnings determine whether the company survives to next year, you can't simply optimize for the long view.

But for those genuine crossroads—the job offer in another country, the career change that terrifies you, the creative project you keep postponing—regret minimization cuts through the noise. It reminds you that the pain of action is usually sharp and short. The pain of inaction often starts quiet and never quite goes away.

The real insight isn't about maximizing adventure. It's about aligning decisions with the self you're becoming, not just the self you are today. Your current fears and comforts will fade. The question is what you'll wish you had done when they do.
