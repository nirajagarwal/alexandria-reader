# Via Negativa

***Progress through subtraction***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1854, a London physician named John Snow faced a cholera outbreak killing hundreds in Soho. The prevailing theory blamed "miasma" — bad air. Snow suspected contaminated water but couldn't prove which source. He couldn't add a cure; antibiotics wouldn't exist for another century. So he removed something instead. He convinced authorities to disable the handle of the Broad Street pump. The deaths stopped within days.

Snow's insight was methodological: when you can't identify what to add, remove what might be harmful and watch what happens. The disease didn't need a cure. It needed an obstacle removed.

This is Via Negativa — the way of negation, of defining things by what they are not, of improving by subtraction rather than addition. It's the recognition that we often know what's wrong more clearly than what's right, and that removing the harmful can be more powerful than adding the beneficial.

The principle appears everywhere once you notice it. Michelangelo said he released David from the marble by removing everything that wasn't David. The Hippocratic Oath instructs "first, do no harm" — not "first, do good." Investment success often comes more from avoiding disasters than chasing winners. A healthy diet is perhaps better defined by what you don't eat than what you do.

Why does subtraction work so well? Because complex systems have more ways to fail than to succeed. A human body has millions of possible dysfunctions but only one state called "healthy." Adding things introduces new interactions, new failure modes, new complexity. Removing obvious harms simplifies the system, lets it find its own equilibrium.

Consider your own life. Your calendar fills with commitments. Your home accumulates possessions. Your routines accrete habits. Your mind collects worries. The default trajectory is addition — more meetings, more stuff, more obligations, more concerns. But improvement often lies in the opposite direction. What if the question isn't "What should I do?" but "What should I stop doing?"

The practice is harder than it sounds. We're biased toward action, toward doing something. Removal feels passive, even lazy. A doctor who prescribes ten medications seems more helpful than one who eliminates nine. A consultant who adds processes looks more valuable than one who strips them away. A parent who schedules activities appears more engaged than one who creates empty time.

This bias toward addition runs deep. When researchers ask people to improve a Lego structure, most add blocks rather than removing them, even when subtraction would work better. When organizations face problems, they create new committees rather than disbanding old ones. We treat absence as nothing rather than recognizing it as something.

Via Negativa doesn't mean passivity. It means strategic removal. It means asking: What is causing harm? What adds complexity without value? What obscures rather than clarifies? Then having the discipline to subtract.

The model has limits. Not everything wrong can be fixed by removal — you can't cure diabetes by eliminating insulin, or repair a bridge by removing concrete. Some systems need addition; a malnourished person needs food, not further restriction. And taken too far, Via Negativa becomes nihilism, the rejection of everything until nothing remains.

But as a first principle, especially in systems you don't fully understand, it offers remarkable power. When you don't know exactly what to do, you can often identify what clearly isn't working. Remove that. Then observe.

John Snow never cured cholera. He removed access to contaminated water. The epidemic ended anyway. Sometimes the most effective action is the one that subtracts.

**The clearest path forward often runs through what you can eliminate, not what you can add.**
