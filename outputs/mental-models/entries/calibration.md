# Calibration

***Why being right 70% of the time means knowing when you're wrong***

> **Section:** Self-Knowledge

In 1987, a group of doctors at Stanford Medical School were asked to estimate the probability that their diagnoses were correct. When they said they were "completely certain," they were wrong 40% of the time. When they said they were "80% sure," they were wrong more than half the time. These were experienced physicians with years of training. They weren't bad doctors. They were badly calibrated.

Calibration is the alignment between your confidence and your accuracy. A well-calibrated person who says "I'm 70% sure" is right about 70% of the time. A poorly calibrated person who says "I'm 90% sure" might be right 60% of the time, or 95%. The confidence doesn't match reality.

Most people are overconfident. Ask someone how likely they are to complete a project on time, they'll say 90%. Track actual completion rates, and it's closer to 50%. Ask a group of drivers if they're above average, and 93% will say yes. Half must be wrong.

But the problem isn't just being wrong. It's being wrong and not knowing how wrong you tend to be. The Stanford doctors didn't just make bad diagnoses. They made bad diagnoses while thinking they were certain, which is dangerous in a different way. A doctor who knows she's 60% sure will order another test. A doctor who thinks she's 100% sure will start treatment.

You can improve calibration through practice and feedback. Professional weather forecasters are remarkably well-calibrated because they make thousands of predictions and learn immediately whether they were right. When a forecaster says 30% chance of rain, it rains about 30% of the time. They've closed the loop between prediction and outcome enough times to know what 30% actually feels like.

The method is simple: make predictions with specific probabilities, track your results, notice your patterns. Keep a log. When you say you're 80% sure about something, mark it down. After fifty predictions at "80% confidence," count how many you got right. If the number isn't close to 40, your 80% means something other than 80%.

You'll discover your patterns. Maybe you're overconfident about things you want to be true. Maybe you're underconfident in areas where you have real expertise. Maybe your "pretty sure" means 60% and someone else's means 85%. The numbers themselves don't matter as much as knowing what they mean for you.

Where calibration misleads is in thinking it can be perfected or that it matters equally in all domains. You can be well-calibrated about weather but terrible about politics. You can be accurate about simple predictions and wildly off about complex ones. And some decisions don't benefit from precise probability estimates. You don't need to be 73.5% sure that you love someone.

The deeper value of calibration isn't in the numbers. It's in the honesty. Tracking your predictions forces you to face the gap between what you think you know and what you actually know. That gap is uncomfortable. Most people avoid it. But you can't improve what you don't measure.

A well-calibrated person knows the difference between "probably" and "almost certainly." They know when to gather more information and when to decide. They know which of their beliefs they can trust and which ones are closer to guesses. They don't pretend to know things they don't.

The most useful form of confidence isn't feeling certain. It's knowing how certain you should feel.
