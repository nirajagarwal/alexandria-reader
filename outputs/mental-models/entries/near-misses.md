# Near Misses

***Why barely avoiding disaster makes you take more risk***

> **Section:** Risk & Uncertainty

In 1986, NASA engineers reviewed telemetry from the Space Shuttle Challenger's previous flights. They found evidence of O-ring erosion—the rubber seals were being damaged by hot gases during launch. But the shuttle had always returned safely. The damage was noted, discussed, and ultimately rationalized. The O-rings had been compromised before, and nothing catastrophic had happened. Each successful landing made the next launch feel safer.

On January 28, 1986, the Challenger exploded 73 seconds after liftoff. All seven crew members died. The O-rings, weakened by unusually cold weather, had failed completely.

The engineers hadn't ignored the warning signs. They had seen them clearly. But twenty-four successful flights with damaged O-rings had taught them the wrong lesson. Each near miss felt like evidence of safety rather than accumulating danger.

This is how near misses distort judgment. When you narrowly avoid a bad outcome, your brain treats it as validation rather than warning. You walked away fine. The system held. It must be working.

A driver runs a red light and barely avoids a collision. Instead of thinking "I almost died," they think "I made it through." The near miss doesn't reduce the behavior—it reinforces it. Next time, they'll run the light a little faster, with a little less hesitation.

A company misses a regulatory deadline but faces no penalty. Management interprets this as the deadline being flexible rather than themselves being lucky. They miss the next one too, and the next, until eventually the regulator acts and the consequences are severe.

Near misses feel like success. They share many of the same features—you tried something risky, and you're still here, unharmed. Your brain files them away with the wins. But they're not wins. They're warnings wrapped in relief.

The mechanism is simple: outcomes teach faster than probabilities. If you touch a hot stove and get burned, you learn immediately. But if you touch a hot stove that's *almost* hot enough to burn you, you learn that touching hot stoves is fine. The lesson sticks until the temperature changes.

This is why casinos love near misses on slot machines. Two cherries line up, the third just barely misses. It feels close to winning, so you play again. Professional gamblers know the difference between near misses and actual probability. Novices don't. They remember the times they almost won and believe their luck is turning.

The pattern appears everywhere once you see it. The hiker who goes off-trail without proper gear and makes it back safely. The investor who holds a failing position too long but gets out before total collapse. The pilot who cuts a safety check short and lands without incident. Each time, the near miss whispers: *See? It's fine. You can do it again.*

The danger compounds because near misses are more common than disasters. You can experience dozens of near misses for every actual failure. Each one makes you slightly more confident, slightly more willing to push the boundary. The statistical warning—that you're operating in dangerous territory—gets drowned out by the experiential evidence that you keep surviving.

Organizations are particularly vulnerable. When a near miss happens to a group, it can become institutional memory. "We've done it this way before and nothing went wrong" becomes part of the culture. The knowledge that something *almost* went wrong gets lost or dismissed. Success is visible. Near misses require interpretation.

The fix requires deliberate reframing. Treat near misses as failures that got lucky. When you barely avoid an accident, ask: "What would have happened if one variable had been different?" Run the counterfactual. Calculate the cost of the disaster you avoided, and recognize that your actions made that disaster possible.

Some industries do this well. Aviation has mandatory reporting of near misses, and they're investigated like accidents. The assumption is that near misses and accidents differ only in outcome, not in cause. The pilot who caught a problem just in time isn't celebrated for skill—they're studied for what went wrong upstream.

The Challenger disaster wasn't caused by a single O-ring failure. It was caused by twenty-four successful interpretations of warning signs as evidence of safety. Each near miss made the next risk feel smaller. Until the system failed completely, and there were no more chances to learn.

When you narrowly avoid disaster, you've learned something important. Just not the lesson that feels true in the moment.
