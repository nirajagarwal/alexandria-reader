# Principal-Agent Problem

***When the person doing the work wants something different than the person paying for it***

> **Section:** Incentives and Systems

In 1975, Michael Jensen walked into a corporate boardroom and asked a simple question: "Why would a CEO care about maximizing shareholder value?"

The executives looked at him like he was insane. The CEO works for the shareholders. That's literally the job.

"Does he own the company?" Jensen asked.

No, they admitted. He owns maybe 1% of the shares.

"So when he makes a decision that costs shareholders $100 million but makes his life easier or secures his job for another five years, what stops him?"

Silence.

Jensen had identified something that seems obvious once you see it, but most organizations are built as if it doesn't exist: the person you hire to act on your behalf has their own interests. And those interests are not identical to yours.

This is the principal-agent problem. You're the principal. You hire an agent to do something for you. The agent knows more than you do, has more control than you do, and wants different things than you do. The gap between what you want and what they want is where the trouble starts.

A lawyer bills by the hour. You want the case resolved quickly and cheaply. She wants it to take exactly as long as it takes to maximize revenue without losing your business. A real estate agent gets 3% of the sale price whether your house sells for $500,000 or $505,000. That extra $5,000 matters to you. To her, it's $150 more for weeks of additional work.

The problem isn't that agents are evil. It's that they're human. They respond to incentives. And when the incentive structure points them away from your interests, that's where they'll go.

The classic solution is monitoring. Watch them closely. Track their work. Verify their decisions. But monitoring is expensive. Someone has to do it. And if you knew enough to monitor effectively, you probably wouldn't need to hire them in the first place.

The better solution is alignment. Make the agent's interests overlap with yours. Give the CEO stock options. Make the lawyer's fee contingent on winning. Pay the real estate agent more for higher sale prices above a threshold.

But alignment has its own problems. Stock options can incentivize short-term thinking and excessive risk-taking. Contingency fees can encourage lawyers to avoid cases that don't promise a big payout. Every attempt to align incentives creates new distortions.

The problem appears everywhere power is delegated. Doctors who own testing facilities order more tests. Fund managers who collect fees based on assets under management don't care if you make money, only that you don't pull your money out. Politicians who represent you in Congress spend more time fundraising and campaigning than legislating.

The worst cases combine low monitoring with weak alignment. A nursing home resident can't easily evaluate the quality of care. A patient under anesthesia can't watch what the surgeon does. A citizen usually can't tell whether their representative actually read the bill.

Where the principal-agent problem becomes truly dangerous is when everyone involved pretends it doesn't exist. We build systems assuming perfect alignment—that teachers optimize for student learning, that financial advisors optimize for client returns, that managers optimize for company success. Then we're shocked when the tests get gamed, the fees pile up, and the middle managers build empires.

The problem has no complete solution. It's baked into the structure of delegation. But recognizing it changes what questions you ask. Not "Is this person competent?" but "What do they actually want?" Not "Can I trust them?" but "What happens when our interests diverge?"

Every time you delegate authority, you create a principal-agent problem. The question is whether you're going to acknowledge it or be surprised by it later.
