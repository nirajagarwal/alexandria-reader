# Intellectual Humility

***The courage to be wrong***

> **Section:** Thinking About Thinking

In 1847, Ignaz Semmelweis discovered that doctors were killing their patients.

He was a Hungarian physician working in Vienna General Hospital's maternity ward, where one in ten women died of childbed fever after giving birth. The deaths clustered in the ward staffed by doctors and medical students. In the adjacent ward, staffed by midwives, mortality was three times lower.

Semmelweis noticed something odd: the doctors performed autopsies in the morning, then delivered babies in the afternoon. The midwives did not. He hypothesized that "cadaverous particles" on doctors' hands were poisoning the mothers. When he instituted mandatory hand-washing with chlorinated lime solution, mortality in the doctors' ward dropped to match the midwives' ward.

He had proven his case. He expected celebration.

Instead, the medical establishment rejected him. Senior physicians were offended by the suggestion that they—gentlemen, men of science—were vectors of disease. The theory contradicted accepted medical doctrine. Hand-washing was dismissed as superstitious nonsense. Semmelweis grew bitter and erratic. He was eventually committed to an asylum, where he died at 47, possibly beaten by guards.

His colleagues could not accept being wrong. It cost thousands of lives.

Intellectual humility is the recognition that your beliefs might be mistaken. Not in the abstract—everyone nods along to that—but in the specific case at hand. The thing you're certain about. The position you've argued for years. The identity you've built around being right.

It sounds passive, like weakness. It's not. Intellectual humility is active maintenance of accurate beliefs. It requires admitting when you're wrong, updating when you learn, and holding uncertainty where certainty doesn't exist.

This is harder than it sounds. Your brain didn't evolve to be accurate. It evolved to be confident. Confidence wins arguments, attracts allies, and signals status. Doubt does the opposite. So you become attached to your conclusions. You stop examining them. You defend them like territory.

Intellectual humility is the antidote. It means treating your beliefs as provisional—subject to revision when better evidence arrives. It means distinguishing between confidence in your reasoning and confidence in your conclusion. You can be rigorous in your thinking without being rigid in your beliefs.

It shows up in small ways:
- Saying "I don't know" when you don't
- Seeking out information that contradicts your view
- Changing your mind and saying so
- Acknowledging the limits of your expertise
- Recognizing that people who disagree might have good reasons

Where intellectual humility misleads: it is not relativism. Some positions are better supported than others. Some questions have right answers. Humility doesn't mean treating all claims as equally valid. It means proportioning your confidence to your evidence.

Nor is it performative doubt. Some people mistake constant uncertainty for wisdom. They won't commit to any position, which is another form of closed-mindedness—closed to the possibility that some things really are knowable.

The cost of lacking intellectual humility compounds. When you can't admit error, you can't learn from it. When you can't update your beliefs, they drift further from reality. When you can't say "I don't know," you fill the gap with guesses you defend as facts. Over time, you become less reliable to yourself and others.

Semmelweis was right, but he couldn't get anyone to listen because he couldn't navigate their inability to be wrong. Twenty years after his death, germ theory vindicated him. By then, tens of thousands had died needlessly.

The doctors who rejected hand-washing weren't stupid or evil. They were normal. They suffered from the same limitation we all face: the difficulty of holding our own certainty lightly.

The question isn't whether you'll be wrong. You will. The question is whether you'll admit it before the cost becomes unbearable.
