# Survivorship Bias

***Why the survivors tell incomplete stories***

> **Section:** Perception and Reality

During World War II, the U.S. military had a problem. Bombers were returning from missions riddled with bullet holes, and the damage wasn't random. Holes clustered on the wings, fuselage, and tail sections. The obvious solution: reinforce those areas with armor plating.

Abraham Wald, a statistician working with the Statistical Research Group, looked at the same data and reached the opposite conclusion. Reinforce the areas *without* bullet holes — the engines and cockpit.

His reasoning changed how the military thought about aircraft design. The planes they were examining had survived. Holes in the wings and tail meant a plane could take fire there and still make it home. The absence of holes in the engines meant something different: planes hit there didn't return. They were at the bottom of the ocean.

The military was studying success and trying to replicate it. Wald studied absence and understood what killed.

This is survivorship bias. We see what endured and mistake it for the whole picture. The failed, the defeated, the unlucky — they're not in the data set. They're not around to tell their story. So we draw conclusions from an incomplete sample and wonder why our strategies fail.

The startup founder who dropped out of college and built a billion-dollar company makes headlines. The thousands who dropped out and failed don't. If you study only successful dropouts, college looks like a terrible investment. If you could study everyone who dropped out, the pattern might reverse.

The mutual fund that posted 40% returns last year gets featured in financial magazines. The fund that collapsed and closed gets erased from the historical record. Investment researchers call this the "survivorship bias in fund performance" — at any given moment, you're only seeing the funds that didn't fail. The average return of "all funds" looks better than the average return of "all funds that ever existed."

The soldiers who came home told stories about what worked — the tactics that saved them, the equipment that held up, the decisions that kept them alive. But the full picture includes the soldiers who aren't there to debrief. Their tactics failed. Their equipment broke. Their decisions didn't pan out. Those lessons died with them.

Survivorship bias is especially dangerous when we study success. We read biographies of great leaders, trying to extract principles. We find patterns: they wake up early, they read voraciously, they take risks. But we're not reading biographies of failed leaders who did the exact same things. We don't know if the habits caused the success or simply came along for the ride.

The bias warps our sense of risk. Old buildings look beautifully constructed — solid wood, expert craftsmanship, built to last. We conclude they don't make them like they used to. But we're only seeing the buildings that survived. The shoddily constructed ones collapsed or were torn down decades ago. The average quality of old buildings was probably no better than today. We just don't see the failures anymore.

It sneaks into personal advice. "Follow your passion" sounds wise when it comes from someone whose passion paid off. It sounds different from someone whose passion led to bankruptcy. "Take bold risks" works when the risk succeeds. Both pieces of advice come from survivors. The question is whether they would have succeeded regardless, or whether survivorship bias is making their strategy look smarter than it was.

The antidote is asking: what am I not seeing? Who failed doing this same thing? Where is the missing data?

Wald could have reinforced the wings and fuselage like everyone else suggested. The planes would have gotten heavier, slower, less maneuverable. More would have been shot down. Instead, he looked at the empty spaces in the data and found the truth.

The survivors get the microphone. But the full story includes the silence.
