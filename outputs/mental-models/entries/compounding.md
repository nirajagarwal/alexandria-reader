# Compounding

***Small changes that don't look like much until they are***

> **Section:** Leverage and Scale

In 1626, Dutch colonists bought Manhattan Island from the Lenape people for goods valued at 60 guilders—about $24. For centuries, this was held up as history's shrewdest real estate deal. Then in 1992, a financial historian named Peter Minuit did the calculation everyone had missed.

If the Lenape had taken that $24 and invested it at a 7% annual return, by 1992 they would have had $30 trillion. That's more than the assessed value of all land and buildings in Manhattan.

The deal wasn't as clever as everyone thought.

Compounding is the mathematical process where growth feeds on itself. Each period's gain becomes part of the base for the next period's gain. Small percentages, given time, produce results that seem impossible.

Albert Einstein may or may not have called it "the eighth wonder of the world." The quote is probably apocryphal. But the mathematics are not.

Start with $100 at 10% annual growth. After one year: $110. After two years: $121, not $120. That extra dollar comes from the previous year's gain earning its own return. After ten years: $259. After thirty years: $1,745. After fifty years: $11,739.

The same mechanism works in reverse. A 10% annual loss turns $100 into $5 after fifty years.

Compounding explains why Warren Buffett has more wealth than almost anyone alive. Not because he generates higher returns than other excellent investors—he doesn't. Because he has compounded good returns for seventy years. He started investing at age eleven. Most of his net worth accumulated after age fifty.

The model applies far beyond money. Knowledge compounds: each thing you learn makes the next thing easier to learn. Relationships compound: small gestures of care accumulate into deep trust. Damage compounds: small betrayals erode trust until it vanishes. Fitness compounds: a little exercise today makes tomorrow's workout less difficult. Skills compound: each hour of practice makes the next hour more productive.

The mathematics are simple but the psychology is hard. Compounding requires two things most people struggle with: patience and consistency.

The patience problem is that compounding produces minimal results in the early periods. The difference between $100 and $110 feels trivial. The exponential curve starts flat. You have to endure long stretches where nothing seems to be happening.

The consistency problem is that compounding punishes interruption. Miss a few years and you lose not just those years but all the growth those years would have generated. One year at -50% requires two years at +50% just to break even. The math isn't symmetrical.

This is why almost no one successfully times the market. The best returns often come in brief periods. Miss those periods and compounding fails. The investor who panics during a crash and pulls out misses both the crash and the recovery. The crash was already priced in.

The model has limits. Nothing compounds forever. Real estate compounds until the city stops growing. Skills compound until you hit biological limits. Companies compound until they saturate their market. Every exponential curve eventually meets friction.

Compounding also assumes stability in the underlying system. Your investment compounds if the economic system persists. Your knowledge compounds if your field remains relevant. The Lenape's theoretical $30 trillion assumes centuries of stable property rights and financial institutions. Reality is messier.

But the core insight remains: time is the ultimate leverage. The person who starts earlier beats the person who tries harder. The company that grows steadily beats the company that grows erratically. The writer who publishes weekly beats the writer who publishes sporadically.

The question is not whether you can achieve 10% or 20% or 50% returns. The question is what you can sustain long enough for compounding to matter.
