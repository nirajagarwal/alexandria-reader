# Common Knowledge

***What everyone knows that everyone knows***

> **Section:** Social Dynamics

In 1997, South Korean banks began failing. Each bank knew it was in trouble. Each bank knew other banks were in trouble. But no one acted—until a single downgrade by a credit rating agency made the crisis public. Within weeks, the entire financial system collapsed.

The banks didn't learn anything new from the downgrade. They already knew they were in danger. What changed was that now everyone knew that everyone else knew. That difference—between private knowledge and common knowledge—triggered the panic.

Common knowledge isn't just information that many people possess. It's information that everyone knows, and everyone knows that everyone knows, and everyone knows that everyone knows that everyone knows. It's knowledge that has been made public in a way that establishes shared awareness of shared awareness.

This matters because people don't act on what they privately know. They act on what they believe others know. A tyrant can survive widespread private dissent as long as that dissent remains private. Each person fears they're alone in their opposition. But a single public protest—even a small one—can topple regimes by converting private knowledge into common knowledge. Now everyone knows that others feel the same way. Now resistance becomes thinkable.

The same mechanism governs social norms. Many people might privately disapprove of a practice, but if no one speaks up, the practice continues. Each person assumes they're the outlier. Then someone says the obvious thing out loud—at a meeting, in an article, in a conversation—and suddenly the norm shifts. The private knowledge was already there. Making it common changed everything.

Common knowledge explains why announcements matter even when they contain no news. When a CEO sends an all-hands email saying "We need to cut costs," every manager already knows this. But now every manager knows that every other manager knows. Coordination becomes possible. The announcement doesn't inform; it aligns.

Gossip functions this way too. The interesting thing about a rumor isn't usually the information itself—often it's old news. The interesting thing is that now it's circulating. Now everyone knows that everyone is talking about it. That changes the social calculation. What was ignorable becomes significant purely by becoming common knowledge.

This is why powerful actors work so hard to prevent certain information from becoming common knowledge, even when they can't keep it secret. Keeping dissent fragmented, keeping complaints individual, keeping knowledge private—these preserve the status quo far better than suppressing the information entirely. The information can exist as long as people don't know that others know it too.

The internet has made common knowledge easier to establish and harder to control. A tweet, a video, a leaked document—any of these can convert private knowledge into common knowledge almost instantly. This is why seemingly stable systems can collapse so suddenly. The conditions were already there. The common knowledge was the catalyst.

You see this in your own life. Something bothers you about a relationship, a job, a living situation. You think you're the only one who feels this way. Then someone else mentions it casually, and suddenly you realize: we all knew. We just didn't know we all knew. And now that it's common knowledge, everything changes.

The model has limits. Not every shared belief requires common knowledge to matter—private decisions based on private information can still accumulate into large effects. And establishing common knowledge doesn't guarantee action; people can know that everyone knows something and still not act on it.

But when you see sudden shifts in behavior, sentiment, or coordination—when the tipping point seems to come from nowhere—look for the moment when private knowledge became common knowledge. That's usually when the world changed.

**What everyone privately knows stays private until someone makes it public. Then it becomes the thing everyone knows that everyone knows. That's when things move.**
