# Reputation

***The compound interest of behavior***

> **Section:** Social Dynamics

In 1519, Hernán Cortés landed on the coast of Mexico with 600 men, facing an empire of millions. According to legend, he burned his ships. His men couldn't retreat. They had to win or die.

Except he didn't burn the ships. He sank them. And historians now doubt he even did that. The story probably emerged decades later, crafted by chroniclers who understood something Cortés himself knew well: sometimes the legend is more useful than the truth.

Cortés built his reputation as a man who would do anything to win. That reputation preceded him into every negotiation, every battle, every alliance. Indigenous groups allied with him partly because they believed the legend. His own men fought harder because they thought retreat was impossible. The story of the burned ships did more work than burning actual ships ever could.

This is reputation in action — not what you do, but what people believe you'll do next.

Reputation is a prediction market about your future behavior. Every action you take is a bet, and everyone watching is updating their odds. Will she deliver on time? Can he be trusted with sensitive information? Does this company actually care about quality, or just about looking like they do?

The mathematics are brutal. One kept promise adds a small amount to your credibility. One broken promise can erase years of trust. This asymmetry exists because people weight negative information more heavily than positive. A single betrayal is more informative than a dozen successful collaborations — it reveals what you're capable of when the stakes are high enough.

This is why reputation compounds. Early investments in reliability, honesty, or quality pay dividends for decades. The consultant who always delivers exactly what she promised gets recommended. The manufacturer who recalls a defective product voluntarily earns customer loyalty. Each action adds to the prediction, making the next positive action more believable and the next opportunity more accessible.

But reputation has a dark side. It creates lock-in. Once people believe something about you, contradictory evidence is harder to accept. The "difficult" employee struggles to shed that label even after changing their behavior. The company known for cheap products can't convince customers to try their premium line. You become what people expect you to be, and expectations are sticky.

This is where Cortés understood something subtle. Reputation isn't just built through direct experience. It's built through stories. The tale of the burned ships spread faster than any firsthand account of his actual military tactics. Stories compress complex behavior patterns into memorable narratives. "She's the one who stayed up all night to fix the client's problem" is more powerful than "She's generally reliable."

The strategic question becomes: which stories are being told about you?

Some people optimize for short-term impression management — looking good in the moment. They dress up failures as successes, take credit for others' work, or make promises they can't keep. This works until someone checks. And someone always checks. The internet made reputation more durable and more searchable. The impressive resume that doesn't match the references. The founder whose last three companies failed quietly. The pattern reveals itself.

Others play the long game. Warren Buffett's line: "It takes twenty years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it. If you think about that, you'll do things differently." He's describing the compound interest of consistent behavior. Berkshire Hathaway's reputation for honest dealing lets them make acquisitions faster than competitors, often without extensive due diligence. The reputation does the work.

The mistake is thinking reputation is about what you say about yourself. It's not. It's about what others say when you're not in the room. That conversation is happening whether you know it or not. The only question is what evidence you're giving them.

Reputation is neither fair nor always accurate. People misjudge. Stories distort. But over time, with enough interactions, reputation tends toward truth. The consultant who overpromises gets found out. The company that actually cares about customers builds loyalty. What you do in private eventually becomes public knowledge.

You can't fully control your reputation. But you can control the pattern of behavior that generates it. Every interaction is a deposit or withdrawal. The compound interest works both ways.
