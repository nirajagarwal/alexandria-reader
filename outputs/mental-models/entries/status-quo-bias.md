# Status Quo Bias

***Why we mistake familiarity for rightness***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases & Heuristics

In 1988, William Samuelson and Richard Zeckhauser ran a simple experiment. They gave people a choice: invest an inheritance in a moderate-risk company, a high-risk company, treasury bonds, or municipal bonds. Most spread their money across options. Nothing surprising.

Then they changed one thing. They told a second group the inheritance was *already* invested in the moderate-risk company. Now what? Same four options. Same potential returns. But this time, 47% left the money exactly where it was.

A third group heard the inheritance started in treasury bonds. Nearly 60% kept it there.

The only thing that changed was the starting position. Everything else—risk, return, rational calculation—stayed identical. Yet people overwhelmingly chose whatever they were told they already had.

This is status quo bias: our tendency to prefer the current state of affairs over change, even when change offers clear advantages.

It operates quietly, in the background of nearly every decision. Your phone plan is probably not the best one available. Your insurance policy hasn't been reviewed in years. Your retirement portfolio uses default allocations from when you started the job. You know these things could be optimized. You also know you're probably not going to do it.

The bias appears strongest when decisions feel complex or consequences uncertain. Given ten insurance options, most people stick with their current one rather than evaluate all ten. Given a choice between rearranging their life and keeping things as they are, most people keep things as they are—even if "as they are" is demonstrably suboptimal.

Part of this is simple inertia. Change requires effort. But status quo bias runs deeper than laziness. We perceive losses from changing as larger than equivalent gains. Giving up your current health insurance feels riskier than missing out on a slightly better plan, even when the math says otherwise. We weight the downsides of change more heavily than the upsides.

We also feel responsibility differently. If you stick with your current choice and it goes badly, you can blame circumstances. If you switch and it goes badly, you blame yourself. The action feels more culpable than the inaction, even when both are decisions.

Institutions exploit this constantly. Default options in retirement plans. Auto-renewal subscriptions. Opt-out organ donation systems. They know most people won't change the preset, so the preset becomes the outcome.

But the bias also protects us. Not every decision deserves analysis. Some defaults are well-designed. Some current arrangements work fine. The person who optimizes everything optimizes nothing—they spend their life comparing phone plans instead of using their phone.

The trap is not having a status quo. It's *defending* it without knowing why. It's the moment when "this is how we've always done it" becomes evidence rather than history. It's staying in the relationship because leaving feels like failure. It's keeping the investment because selling feels like admitting a mistake.

The question isn't whether to change. It's whether you chose the status quo or simply accepted it.

Ask: If I were starting from zero today, would I choose this? Your current job, your current city, your current approach to the problem. If the answer is no—or even "probably not"—then the only argument for staying is that you're already there. Sometimes that's enough. Often it isn't.

The bias makes us defend positions we never consciously selected. The best decisions don't feel like changes at all. They feel like finally doing what you already knew you should do.
