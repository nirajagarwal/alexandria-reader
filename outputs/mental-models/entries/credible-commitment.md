# Credible Commitment

***Why promises need teeth***

> **Section:** Strategy & Incentives

In 1519, Hernán Cortés landed on the coast of Mexico with a small force facing the vast Aztec empire. His men were outnumbered, far from home, and terrified. Some wanted to steal ships in the night and sail back to Cuba. Cortés had a problem: how do you convince frightened soldiers to fight when retreat remains possible?

He burned the ships.

With no way home, his men had one choice: win or die. The commitment became credible not through words or threats, but through irreversibility. Cortés eliminated his own options to change everyone's calculation.

This is the essence of credible commitment—a promise you make believable by limiting your ability to break it. Words are cheap. Anyone can say "I'll definitely do this" while keeping their options open. A credible commitment costs something to reverse. It burns the ships.

The principle operates everywhere decisions depend on trust. A company wants to convince suppliers to invest in specialized equipment for its products. Saying "we'll keep ordering for years" isn't enough. But signing a ten-year contract with penalties for early termination? Now the suppliers believe. The commitment gained teeth.

Consider why hostages work as negotiating leverage. The hostage-taker wants something—money, safe passage, political concessions. But why should anyone trust they'll release the hostages after getting what they want? They shouldn't. Except if the demand is something like safe passage, releasing hostages early makes the commitment credible. It demonstrates good faith when the taker still needs cooperation. The commitment becomes believable through vulnerability.

Or look at nuclear deterrence during the Cold War. The U.S. promised to defend Western Europe with nuclear weapons if the Soviet Union invaded. But would the U.S. really risk New York to save Paris? The commitment wasn't credible until America stationed troops in West Berlin. Now any Soviet invasion would kill American soldiers immediately, forcing the U.S. to respond. The troops were hostages to our own promise.

The model reveals why certain promises fail. A manager tells her team she won't micromanage this time. But she's said that before, then swooped in at the first sign of trouble. Why would anyone believe her now? She hasn't constrained herself. She's kept all her options open.

Contrast that with a manager who says "I'm traveling for two weeks, unreachable except for true emergencies" and actually goes somewhere without reliable internet. Now the team knows she can't micromanage even if she wants to. The commitment becomes credible through incapacity.

The most effective commitments are irreversible. Marriage vows gain power from the social, legal, and emotional costs of divorce. A published deadline creates commitment through reputation cost. Announcing a strategy publicly makes backing down embarrassing. Each raises the price of reversal.

But credible commitment has limits. You can overcommit, burning ships you might actually need. Cortés gambled everything on conquest—the strategy succeeded, but only barely. Had the Aztecs united against him immediately, he'd have destroyed his own escape route for nothing.

Commitments can also trap you. Once you've publicly declared you'll never negotiate with terrorists, what do you do when they take your citizens hostage? The commitment that prevented blackmail now prevents flexibility. Goodhart's Law operates here too: when you commit too rigidly to a metric or strategy, circumstances change and you're locked into the wrong choice.

The deeper lesson: people believe your actions more than your words. They believe what you can't easily undo more than what you can. When you need someone to trust your promise, ask yourself what you're willing to sacrifice to prove it. What option are you ready to eliminate? What ship are you prepared to burn?

Trust is built through constraint. The credible commitment is the one you can no longer escape.
