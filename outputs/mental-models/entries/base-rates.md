# Base Rates

***What the crowd knows that you're ignoring***

> **Section:** Statistics & Probability

In 1978, psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky asked a simple question: "Steve is very shy and withdrawn, invariably helpful but with little interest in people or the world of reality. A meek and tidy soul, he has a need for order and structure, and a passion for detail. Is Steve more likely to be a librarian or a farmer?"

Most people said librarian. The description fits perfectly. Shy, orderly, detail-oriented — that's librarian material.

But there are roughly twenty times more male farmers than male librarians in the United States. Even if Steve's personality matches the librarian stereotype, the overwhelming numerical reality suggests he's probably a farmer. The base rate — the general frequency of something in a population — swamps the descriptive details.

We do this constantly. We ignore what happens most of the time and focus on what feels specific to the case in front of us. A friend complains of a headache and stiff neck, and we think meningitis when tension is a thousand times more common. A startup founder has charisma and ambition, so we invest, forgetting that 90% of startups fail regardless of the founder's charm. Someone matches the profile of a terrorist, so security detains them, even though the base rate of actual terrorists is microscopically small.

Base rates are the statistical ground truth. They tell you what normally happens, absent any special information. And they're usually more predictive than the vivid details we fixate on.

The power of base rates comes from their stability. Individual stories shift and surprise us, but large numbers converge on patterns. If you're trying to predict whether someone has a disease, the baseline prevalence of that disease in the population is often your best starting point — better than symptoms, better than intuition, sometimes even better than initial test results if you haven't adjusted for base rates.

Doctors learn this the hard way. A test for a rare disease might be 95% accurate, but if only one in ten thousand people has the disease, a positive result still probably means you don't have it. The base rate is so low that even a good test produces more false positives than true positives. Ignoring this has led to unnecessary treatments, surgeries, and years of patient anxiety.

The same logic applies to predictions about people. If you're hiring and trying to predict job performance, the base rate matters: most candidates at a given level perform adequately, most new hires take time to ramp up, most people are neither spectacular nor disastrous. Yes, this particular candidate seems exceptional in the interview. But the base rate of interview impressions predicting job success is modest. Weight both pieces of information.

Lawyers exploit base rate neglect in court. They present vivid details — a witness's confident testimony, a defendant's suspicious behavior — and juries forget to ask: "How often does this kind of evidence appear in cases where the person is actually innocent?" The answer is often "quite frequently." The base rate of wrongful convictions should temper our confidence in any single piece of dramatic evidence.

Where base rates mislead is when you have genuinely diagnostic information — evidence that actually shifts probabilities. A startup founder with charisma is still subject to the 90% failure rate, unless they've already built and sold two companies. A patient's symptoms might be common, unless they just returned from a country where a rare disease is endemic. Base rates are your starting point, not your ending point.

The hard part is emotional. Base rates feel cold. Impersonal. They ignore the specific human in front of you. But that's exactly their value. They correct for the story your mind wants to tell. They ask: before you knew any details, what would you have predicted?

Start with the base rate. Then adjust for the evidence. Not the other way around.
