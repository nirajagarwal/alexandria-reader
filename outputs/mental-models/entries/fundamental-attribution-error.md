# Fundamental Attribution Error

***Why we blame character instead of circumstance***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases

In 1967, psychologists Edward Jones and Victor Harris asked students to read essays about Fidel Castro. Some essays praised him. Others condemned him. Then they asked a simple question: What does the writer really think?

The students said the pro-Castro writers must support Castro. The anti-Castro writers must oppose him. This seems reasonable — until you learn one detail the students were told up front: The essay writers had been assigned their positions. They had no choice.

Even knowing this, the students still judged. They couldn't help it. They saw a person arguing for Castro and thought "Castro supporter," ignoring the constraint that made the person argue that way.

This is the fundamental attribution error. When we watch other people, we explain their behavior through their character — their beliefs, personality, intentions. When we explain our own behavior, we point to circumstances — the situation we were in, the pressures we faced, the options we had.

Someone cuts you off in traffic. They're reckless, you think. Not "maybe they just got a call that their kid is in the hospital." A colleague misses a deadline. They're unreliable. Not "maybe their manager gave them three conflicting priorities." Someone snaps at you. They're mean. Not "maybe they just learned their father has cancer."

We do this constantly. We see the person but not the situation they're in. We see the action but not the constraints that shaped it.

The error feels like perception, but it's really interpretation. You see someone do something and your mind immediately generates an explanation. That explanation almost always starts with "the kind of person they are" instead of "the situation they were in."

This happens because situations are invisible. You see a person sitting in front of you. You don't see the mortgage payment they're worried about, the medication they forgot to take, the argument they had that morning. Character is visible. Circumstance is not.

The error compounds in hierarchies. A CEO thinks her employees lack initiative. She doesn't see that every time someone took initiative last year, they got publicly criticized. A teacher thinks a student is lazy. He doesn't know the student works two jobs and sleeps four hours a night. The person with power sees behavior. The person without power lives in the situation.

It works the other way too. When you explain yourself, you catalog constraints. "I was late because traffic was terrible." "I snapped because I'm exhausted." "I forgot because I'm overwhelmed." You're not wrong about the circumstances. You're just wrong to think other people's circumstances don't matter as much.

The error isn't about being wrong about character. Sometimes the reckless driver really is reckless. Sometimes the unreliable colleague really is unreliable. The error is assuming character first, situation second, if at all.

Military psychologists learned this studying friendly fire incidents. When a pilot mistakenly shoots down an allied plane, the immediate reaction is to blame the pilot. Bad judgment. Poor training. Recklessness. Then you study the incident. The IFF system malfunctioned. The communication was garbled. The rules of engagement had been changed two hours earlier and never reached the unit. The pilot made the same decision any pilot would have made with the information they had.

The fundamental attribution error makes us crueler than we intend and less effective than we could be. If you think someone is failing because of who they are, you replace them. If you understand they're failing because of the situation they're in, you change the situation. One approach cycles through people. The other fixes systems.

The antidote is simple but hard: Stop one second before you judge. Ask what situation could make you do what they just did. If you can imagine it, you've found the circumstance you were about to ignore.

We are all more circumstantial than we think. And everyone else is less intentional than they seem.
