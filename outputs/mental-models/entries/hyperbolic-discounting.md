# Hyperbolic Discounting

***Why we can't resist tomorrow's problems today***

> **Section:** Decision Making

In 1956, a Harvard economist named Robert Strotz noticed something strange in his own behavior. He had set aside money for retirement, locked into an account with penalties for early withdrawal. He knew this was rational. His future self would need that money. But when an unexpected expense arose, he found himself calculating whether the penalty was worth paying. His future self's needs felt abstract. The present problem felt urgent.

Strotz wasn't weak-willed. He was encountering a fundamental quirk in how humans value time.

Ask someone if they'd prefer $100 today or $110 tomorrow, and most choose the $100. Ask if they'd prefer $100 in a year or $110 in a year and a day, and most choose the $110. The wait is the same—one day. But the first choice feels immediate. The second feels distant. Both involve waiting, but only one triggers our impatience.

This is hyperbolic discounting. We don't discount future rewards at a constant rate, the way compound interest works. We discount them sharply when they're near and gently when they're far. The curve is steep at the beginning and flattens out over time, like a hyperbola.

The pattern shows up everywhere. We'll drive across town to save $10 on a $40 purchase but won't bother to save $10 on a $400 purchase, even though $10 is $10. We buy gym memberships we never use, certain that *this time* our future self will be motivated. We eat the dessert tonight and promise to start the diet Monday. Monday, when it arrives, is no longer Monday. It's today, and today has its own desserts.

The effect is strongest across the now/later boundary. A reward available right now activates different brain systems than a reward available in an hour. This isn't a bug. For most of human history, the future was uncertain. A calorie today was more valuable than a calorie next week because you might not survive to next week. Modern life has changed the odds, but not the wiring.

The problem is that we make commitments when rewards are far away, then break them when the rewards get close. We sign up for retirement contributions when retirement is decades off. We book morning meetings when morning is days away. When the moment arrives, the discount curve has shifted. What seemed obviously worthwhile now seems costly.

This is why commitment devices work. Strotz's penalty for early withdrawal was a bet against his future self's impatience. Automatic payroll deductions, preset exercise classes, public promises—all of these exploit the fact that distant-you and present-you apply different discount rates to the same outcome.

But hyperbolic discounting also misleads us about other people. We judge someone who can't quit smoking or save money as lacking willpower, when really they're facing the same discount curve we all face. The difference is often context, not character. Someone with stable income finds it easier to save than someone living paycheck to paycheck, not because they're more rational but because their present is less urgent.

The model also explains why "just this once" is such a dangerous phrase. One cigarette, one skipped workout, one impulse purchase—each feels like a small exception. But the discount curve makes every moment feel like "just this once." The future where these exceptions accumulate never feels as real as the present where we're making them.

You can't eliminate hyperbolic discounting. You can design around it. Automate decisions before they reach the steep part of the curve. Shrink the gap between action and reward. Make the future self's interests visible to the present self—not through willpower, but through structure.

The retirement account with early withdrawal penalties wasn't a failure of Strotz's reasoning. It was an acknowledgment of it.
