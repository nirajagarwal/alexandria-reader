# Lindy Effect

***Why old things tend to live longer***

> **Section:** Time & Survival

In 1964, a comedian named Albert Goldman wrote about the unspoken rules of the Stage Delicatessen on Seventh Avenue in Manhattan. Broadway performers gathered there between shows, and Goldman noticed something odd about their conversations. When discussing the likely future run of a television show, they used a simple heuristic: the longer a show had already been on the air, the longer they expected it to remain. A show in its first season might last one more. A show in its fifth season would probably run another five.

This seemed backwards. Intuition says that everything ages toward death — a show in its fifth season should be closer to cancellation than one just beginning. But the comedians understood something about survival. Things that have lasted a long time have already proven their resilience. They've survived the gauntlet. The longer something has been around, the longer it's likely to persist.

The science writer Nassim Taleb named this the Lindy Effect, after the delicatessen. It applies to anything non-biological that doesn't have a fixed mortality — ideas, technologies, institutions, books. A restaurant open for one year might close in the next. A restaurant open for fifty years will probably run another fifty. The Bible has survived two thousand years and will likely survive another two thousand. JavaScript frameworks released last month will probably be obsolete by next year.

The logic is survival of the unfittest. Every day something survives reveals information. It has weathered storms we can't see. It has resisted forces we don't understand. It contains something — durability, utility, adaptability — that less sturdy competitors lacked. Time is the test. Survival is the credential.

This is why old books matter more than bestsellers, why classical music outlasts pop hits, why ancient philosophies still guide modern lives. Not because old things are inherently superior, but because their survival proves something. A book published last year competes with millions of others. A book from two centuries ago has already won that competition. It earned its place by not disappearing.

The Lindy Effect explains why revolutions in technology often aren't. The paperless office was predicted in the 1970s. Paper remains. Email would kill postal mail. Letters still arrive. Every few years someone declares books dead. Books persist. Not because they're perfect, but because they're Lindy — they've survived long enough that betting on their continued survival becomes rational.

But Lindy has limits. It only applies to the non-perishable. Living things age toward death on a biological clock. A 90-year-old human is not likely to live another 90 years. A company with physical infrastructure ages differently than an idea. And Lindy says nothing about quality — terrible things can persist (bureaucracies, prejudices, wars) simply because they're self-perpetuating.

Lindy also misleads when systems change. A technology might be Lindy within its environment but fragile to disruption from outside. The horse and buggy had survived centuries, right until automobiles arrived. An institution can be ancient and still collapse when its sustaining conditions shift. Lindy predicts persistence, not immunity.

The practical insight: when choosing what to learn, read, or build upon, favor the Lindy. Study texts that have survived centuries over blog posts from last week. Learn tools and languages with decades of survival over the framework du jour. Not because old is automatically good, but because survival carries signal. The things that last have earned their longevity. They've been tested in ways nothing new has faced.

The comedians at the Stage Delicatessen weren't being cynical. They were being empirical. Time reveals what endures. And what endures is likely to endure further still.
