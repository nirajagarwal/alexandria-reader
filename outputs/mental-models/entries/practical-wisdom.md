# Practical Wisdom

***The judgment that no rule can teach***

> **Section:** Judgment and Decision-Making

In 2004, psychologist Barry Schwartz was interviewing hospital janitors for a study on workplace satisfaction. One custodian, Luke, described cleaning a young man's room in the intensive care unit. The patient had been in a coma for months. His father came every day and sat beside the bed.

Luke had already mopped the room that morning, but the father hadn't seen him do it. When the father stepped out briefly, Luke mopped it again.

"It wasn't about the rules," Luke explained. "The room was clean. But the father needed to see someone caring for his son."

This is practical wisdom — the ability to discern what matters in a particular situation and act accordingly, even when no rule tells you what to do.

Aristotle called it *phronesis*. It's not intelligence. It's not knowledge of principles. It's not following procedures. It's the capacity to read a situation accurately and respond with good judgment, balancing competing concerns that can't be perfectly balanced.

The hospital had procedures. Luke knew them. Mop each room once per shift. Don't waste time on unnecessary tasks. Efficiency matters. All reasonable rules. But Luke also understood something the rules couldn't capture: sometimes what looks like wasted motion serves a purpose that matters more.

Practical wisdom has two components working together. First, the perception to recognize what's actually at stake. Luke saw a grieving father who needed reassurance that his son mattered. Second, the skill to do something appropriate about it. Mopping an already-clean floor wouldn't normally make sense, but in that moment, it was exactly right.

You can't codify this. No manual can tell you when to break the efficiency rule to serve a deeper value. That requires judgment developed through experience, reflection, and an understanding of human needs that goes beyond metrics.

This is why practical wisdom erodes when organizations rely too heavily on rules, scripts, and procedures. A customer service representative following a script can handle standard situations efficiently. But when a caller has an unusual problem — or a standard problem in an unusual circumstance — the script fails. The representative needs permission and capacity to think: What's really going on here? What would actually help?

Too many rules and you disable judgment. People stop thinking about what matters and start thinking about whether they're following the rules. They lose the ability to see the difference.

But practical wisdom also erodes without moral grounding. A used car salesman might have excellent judgment about how to manipulate a customer's fears to close a sale. That's not wisdom. Practical wisdom aims at doing the right thing, not just the effective thing. It requires caring about the right ends, not just being skilled at choosing means.

This is why you can't teach practical wisdom the way you teach calculus. You can't learn it by studying principles, even good principles. You learn it by making decisions in real situations, noticing what happens, being corrected when you get it wrong, and gradually developing a feel for what matters.

Organizations that want people to exercise practical wisdom must give them room to use judgment. They must accept that sometimes the right thing looks inefficient. They must hire for character and judgment, not just competence. And they must remember that the goal isn't perfect consistency — it's appropriate response to varying circumstances.

The alternative is a world of rigid rules rigidly applied, where everyone follows procedures and no one takes responsibility for whether the result actually makes sense. Where the hospital room doesn't get mopped twice because the manual says once is enough.

**When organizations optimize for compliance rather than judgment, they get compliance. And they lose the thing that rules can never replace: people who can see what's needed and do it, even when — especially when — no rule tells them to.**
