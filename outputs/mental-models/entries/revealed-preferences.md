# Revealed Preferences

***What people do tells you more than what they say***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1938, economist Paul Samuelson noticed something obvious that no one had formalized: a person who buys an apple when they could afford an orange has revealed something about what they value. They didn't need to fill out a survey about fruit preferences. Their choice was the preference.

This was revolutionary because economics had been stuck trying to measure something invisible — utility, satisfaction, inner desire. Samuelson said: stop asking what people want. Watch what they choose.

The model works like this: If you consistently choose A over B when you could afford either, you prefer A to B. The preference is revealed by the choice, not stated before it.

This sounds simple. It is. But it cuts through an enormous amount of noise.

Consider someone who says they value work-life balance. They turn down flexible schedules. They check email at midnight. They cancel plans when a meeting runs late. Their stated preference is balance. Their revealed preference is career advancement, or perhaps fear of missing out, or status. The gap between the two is where self-deception lives.

Or: a city government says it prioritizes affordable housing. It zones for single-family homes. It blocks apartment construction. It requires expensive parking minimums. The words say one thing. The revealed preference — keeping property values high, keeping the neighborhood "character" intact — says another.

The model gives you a tool for cutting through what people claim to value and seeing what they actually value. And it works on yourself. You say you want to write a book. You haven't opened the document in three weeks. Your revealed preference might be comfort, or the identity of "wanting to write" without the difficulty of writing.

But the model has limits. It assumes people can afford their preferences. A person who buys ramen instead of steak hasn't necessarily revealed a preference for ramen. They've revealed a budget constraint. It assumes people have good information. Someone who chooses a predatory loan might prefer fair terms but not understand what they're signing. And it assumes consistency. People are not always consistent.

Most importantly: revealed preferences capture what people do under current conditions. They don't tell you what people would choose if conditions changed. The person who drives everywhere might prefer walkable neighborhoods — if they existed nearby, if housing there were affordable, if their job were accessible. The choice reveals preference within constraints, not preference without them.

Still, the model is clarifying. It forces honesty. When you see a gap between stated and revealed preferences, you've found something worth investigating. Either someone is deceiving you, or they're deceiving themselves, or they're facing constraints you don't see.

Apply it to your own life. Look at your calendar, your bank statement, your browser history. Those are your revealed preferences. Not your aspirations. Not your self-image. What you actually choose when the choosing happens.

The distance between who you say you are and what you reveal through your choices — that's the work.
