# Opportunity Cost

***The invisible price of every choice***

> **Section:** Economics & Decision-Making

In 1990, Katharine Graham faced a choice that would define her final decade. The Washington Post Company, which she'd led for twenty-three years, was thriving. Revenues were strong. The newsroom was confident. But Graham noticed something her competitors hadn't: cable television was about to explode, and her company owned nothing in it.

She could buy into cable systems. The deal would cost $350 million—not to build something new, but to buy what already existed. The money was there. The board would approve it. But that same $350 million could go toward international expansion, digital infrastructure, or simply stay invested in what was working.

Graham bought the cable systems. Five years later, she sold them for $2.7 billion.

The remarkable thing isn't the profit. It's what she gave up to get it. That $350 million wasn't spent on newsrooms in Asia, on building early web capabilities, on a dozen other strategic moves. Those paths closed the moment she chose cable. She won one future and surrendered all the others.

This is opportunity cost: the value of what you don't choose.

It's invisible in accounting. When Graham spent $350 million on cable, the ledger showed an expense and later an asset. It never showed the international bureau network that didn't get built, the digital team that wasn't hired, the investments that didn't happen. Those ghosts don't appear in any financial statement.

Which is why most people ignore opportunity costs entirely. We see the price tag on what we choose. We rarely see the price of what we pass up.

Every dollar spent is a dollar not spent somewhere else. Every hour given to one project is an hour taken from another. Every "yes" contains a hidden "no" to everything else you could have done in that moment. The real cost of a decision isn't just what you pay—it's what you could have gotten instead.

This matters most when choices look similar in price but differ in return. A company with $10 million to invest might put it in a project with 5% returns. The check clears. The project launches. Everyone feels productive. But if another option would have returned 15%, the real cost wasn't $10 million—it was the 10% gap multiplied over time. The visible cost hides the true cost.

The same mechanism works in time. An executive spending four hours daily in status meetings isn't just paying with those four hours. She's paying with whatever she'd create during those hours: strategy work, product decisions, team development. The calendar shows four hours. The opportunity cost might be a quarter's worth of leadership.

Or consider education. A graduate student spending five years on a PhD pays tuition, yes. But the larger cost is the five years of salary she didn't earn, the career momentum she didn't build, the compound interest she didn't accumulate. The degree might still be worth it—but only if its value exceeds everything that doctorate student gave up to get it.

This is where opportunity cost misleads. It suggests every choice has a single, calculable alternative. In reality, you're choosing between multiple futures, most of which you can't fully imagine. Graham didn't know with certainty that cable would return 7x. She couldn't calculate the precise value of international newsrooms she didn't build. Opportunity cost isn't a number you can look up. It's a discipline of asking: what else could this buy?

The clearest thinkers make this question automatic. Before committing money, time, or attention, they name what they're not doing. Not to second-guess every decision, but to make the trade-offs visible. The choice might still be right—but at least it's made with full awareness of the price.

Katharine Graham won her bet. But she knew she was betting. She saw what she was trading away. Most people don't. They count what they spend and ignore what they surrender, and then wonder why their resources never seem to go far enough.

Every choice costs twice: once in what you give, once in what you give up.
