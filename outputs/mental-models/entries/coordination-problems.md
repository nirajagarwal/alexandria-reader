# Coordination Problems

***Why groups fail even when everyone wants the same thing***

> **Section:** Systems & Incentives

In 1906, British climber Edward Whymper arrived at the base of a Peruvian peak with a team of Swiss guides. They all wanted to summit. They all knew the route. They all understood the dangers. Yet on summit day, the expedition fell apart before noon.

The problem wasn't courage or skill. It was timing. One guide wanted to start at 3 AM to avoid afternoon storms. Another insisted on 5 AM to let the snow firm up. A third thought 4 AM split the difference. Without agreement, they argued. Without a start time, no one prepared their gear. By the time someone finally called for departure at 6 AM, it was too late. The weather window had closed.

This is a coordination problem: a situation where everyone benefits from acting together, but no mechanism exists to ensure that everyone acts together. The outcome everyone wants requires simultaneous action. But without coordination, the default is failure.

The classic example is driving. Americans drive on the right. The British drive on the left. Neither system is objectively better—what matters is that everyone in the same place does the same thing. If half of drivers suddenly switched sides tomorrow, the result would be catastrophic. The benefit comes entirely from coordination, not from any inherent property of right versus left.

Coordination problems appear everywhere:
- Technology standards (VHS beat Betamax not because it was superior, but because more people coordinated around it)
- Meeting times (the "best" time is whichever time gets everyone in the room)
- Emergency responses (sirens work because everyone knows the sound means "get out of the way")
- Language itself (words only work because we agree on their meanings)

The tricky part is that coordination problems often disguise themselves as other types of problems. When a project fails because different teams used incompatible software, it looks like a technical failure. When a company misses a product launch because marketing and engineering worked on different timelines, it looks like poor planning. But underneath, it's coordination: everyone wanted success, but no one synchronized the actions required to achieve it.

Three features define coordination problems:

First, **mutual benefit**: everyone is better off if the group coordinates, and everyone is worse off if it doesn't. There's no conflict of interest, just a failure to align.

Second, **multiple equilibria**: there are several possible outcomes, and once you reach one, it becomes self-reinforcing. Drive on the right or the left. Use metric or imperial. Start the meeting at 9 AM or 10 AM. Any choice works if everyone makes the same choice.

Third, **switching costs**: even when a better coordination point exists, moving to it is expensive or dangerous. The U.S. could theoretically switch to the metric system. But the cost of changing every road sign, every recipe, every tool—while everyone relearns measurements—is enormous. The current system persists not because it's optimal, but because coordination is hard to change.

This last point explains why inferior standards often win. QWERTY keyboards were designed to slow typists down and prevent mechanical jamming. Dvorak layouts are faster. But QWERTY got there first, everyone learned it, and now the switching cost is too high. First-mover advantage in coordination problems is massive.

The solution to coordination problems is rarely better incentives—people already want to coordinate. Instead, you need **coordination mechanisms**: clear signals, default options, or central planning.

Some examples:
- Traffic lights eliminate the need for drivers to negotiate every intersection
- Project management software creates a single source of truth for deadlines
- Standards bodies (like USB-IF or IEEE) prevent technological fragmentation
- Convention itself—social norms that tell people what to expect

But here's where it gets subtle: too much coordination can be as harmful as too little. Over-coordinating creates rigidity. The most robust systems allow local variation while maintaining global alignment. The internet works because TCP/IP coordinates the essential protocols, but allows innovation everywhere else.

When you see a group struggling despite shared goals, ask: Is this a coordination problem? Are we failing not because we disagree, but because we haven't agreed on *how* to act together? Often the solution isn't more motivation or better ideas. It's just picking one path—any reasonable path—and getting everyone to walk it at the same time.

The tragedy of coordination problems is that they're solvable but often invisible. The summit was there. The route was clear. The weather held. They just never climbed together.
