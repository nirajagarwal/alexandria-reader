# Incentives

***Why people do what they do***

> **Section:** Human Behavior

In 1975, economists in Mexico City tried to solve a cobra problem. The city was overrun with the venomous snakes, so officials announced a bounty: cash for every dead cobra delivered to collection points. The policy seemed straightforward. More dead cobras meant fewer living ones.

Within weeks, cobra corpses flooded in. The program appeared to be working. Then someone noticed an unusual pattern in the deliveries. The same faces kept returning with fresh snakes, far more than any person could reasonably catch in the wild.

People had started breeding cobras.

The economics made sense. Why hunt dangerous snakes in the city when you could raise them safely at home? The bounty created a perverse incentive — it rewarded cobra production, not cobra elimination. When officials discovered the scheme and canceled the program, the breeders released their now-worthless inventory into the streets. Mexico City ended up with more cobras than before.

This is the fundamental insight about incentives: people respond to them, but not always in the way you intend.

An incentive is anything that influences behavior by changing the costs or benefits of a choice. Money is the obvious example, but incentives come in many forms: status, recognition, punishment avoidance, social approval, time savings, effort reduction. Whatever shifts the balance between "do this" and "don't do this" is an incentive.

The power of incentives lies in their simplicity. You don't need to change hearts and minds. You don't need to inspire. You just need to change what people gain or lose from their actions. Tax something and you get less of it. Subsidize something and you get more. Pay for performance and performance rises. Usually.

The trap is assuming the behavior you incentivize is the behavior you get. People don't respond to your intentions. They respond to the actual reward structure you create. And they're often more creative than you expect at finding the shortest path to that reward.

Pay teachers based on test scores, and some will teach to the test, narrowing education to what's measured. Pay salespeople by volume, and some will push products customers don't need. Pay programmers by lines of code, and you'll get bloated software. The metric becomes the target, and the target becomes the game.

This is Goodhart's Law at work: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The act of incentivizing something changes what that thing means.

But the problem runs deeper than gaming. Incentives don't just redirect existing motivation — they can replace it. Offer children money to read books, and reading becomes work. What was intrinsic becomes transactional. Remove the payment, and the behavior often stops entirely. The incentive crowds out the original reason for doing the thing.

The solution isn't to eliminate incentives. That's impossible. Incentives exist whether you design them or not. The status quo has incentive structures built in. The question is whether those structures produce the outcomes you want.

Good incentive design requires imagining how rational people would respond, then imagining how creative people would game it, then imagining what hidden motivations might be crushed. It means asking not just "What behavior do I want?" but "What behavior am I actually rewarding?" And those are often different questions with different answers.

The cobra breeders weren't irrational or malicious. They were responding precisely to the incentive provided. The failure was in the design. The city incentivized dead cobras without accounting for where dead cobras might come from.

When you see behavior you don't understand, look for the incentive. When you want behavior to change, change what people gain or lose from their choices. And when you create a new incentive, ask what you're really measuring and what might happen when people start optimizing for it.

The world runs on incentives. The question is whether they're running it toward something you want.
