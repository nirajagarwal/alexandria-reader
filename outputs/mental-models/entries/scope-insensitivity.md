# Scope Insensitivity

***Why our hearts can't scale with numbers***

> **Section:** Cognitive Biases & Heuristics

In 1992, researchers asked people how much they would pay to save migrating birds from drowning in uncovered oil ponds. One group was told that 2,000 birds died each year. Another group heard the number was 20,000. A third learned it was 200,000.

The willingness to pay barely changed. The median responses ranged from $80 to $88. A hundred-fold increase in bird deaths moved the needle by ten percent.

The psychologists called this scope insensitivity — our emotional response to a problem stays roughly constant even when the scale changes dramatically. We care about the birds. We just can't make ourselves care ten times more about ten times as many birds.

This isn't a quirk of bird conservation. When people are asked how much they'd pay to clean up polluted lakes, their answers barely shift whether the question is about cleaning one lake or cleaning fifty. Hospital administrators feel roughly as much urgency adding a hundred beds as adding ten. Politicians campaign on "cracking down on waste" whether the waste is millions or billions.

The pattern appears everywhere because it reflects how human compassion actually works. We evolved to respond to individuals, not statistics. A single child trapped in a well commands national attention. A famine killing thousands becomes background noise. As Stalin allegedly observed, "One death is a tragedy; a million deaths is a statistic."

Our emotional system simply wasn't built to scale linearly with numbers. When we encounter a problem, we generate a feeling — concern, sympathy, outrage — and that feeling determines our response. The feeling comes from mental images, stories, individual faces. It doesn't multiply by ten when the numbers do.

This creates predictable distortions in how we allocate resources and attention. A photogenic disease affecting thousands gets more research funding than a mundane one affecting millions. We spend enormous effort preventing rare, dramatic deaths while accepting common, invisible ones. Corporate scandals involving personal betrayal generate more fury than abstract policy failures with larger consequences.

The model explains why fundraising campaigns focus on individual stories rather than statistics. Charity organizations learned through trial and error what research confirmed: showing one suffering child raises more money than citing how many children are suffering. The single face makes the problem *real* in a way that numbers never can.

Where scope insensitivity misleads most dangerously is in comparing risks and choosing priorities. We assess threats by how they make us feel, not by multiplying probability times consequence. Terrorism feels more threatening than traffic accidents. Dramatic pollution events outrage us more than diffuse environmental damage. We demand absolute safety in some domains while tolerating massive preventable death in others.

The correction isn't to suppress emotion — that won't work and shouldn't work. It's to notice when numbers should matter and deliberately do the math. How many people does this actually affect? If we're comparing two interventions, what's the cost per life improved? When our feelings seem disconnected from the scale of the problem, that's the signal to slow down and calculate.

Organizations that understand scope insensitivity can exploit it. Politicians dramatize small problems and bury large ones in statistics. Marketers frame donations to maximize emotional resonance over efficiency. Media coverage follows the drama, not the death toll.

But individuals who recognize the pattern can correct for it. Before committing resources based on feelings, check whether those feelings track the actual magnitude. Ask: Am I responding to the problem, or to the way the problem was presented? Would I care twice as much if twice as many people were affected?

Our emotional responses won't scale naturally with numbers. But our decisions can.
