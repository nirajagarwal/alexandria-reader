# Signal vs. Noise

***Why most information is interference***

> **Section:** Information and Observation

In 1948, Claude Shannon was trying to solve a practical problem at Bell Labs: how to send messages through telephone wires without them getting garbled. Static on the line. Electrical interference. Crosstalk from other conversations bleeding through. Every transmission carried two things—the message you wanted to send (signal) and everything that corrupted it (noise).

Shannon's insight became the foundation of information theory, but the distinction he formalized has applications far beyond telephone wires. Every time you try to extract meaning from the world—reading financial news, evaluating a job candidate, analyzing your own performance—you face the same challenge. Some of what you observe reflects underlying reality. Some of it is random variation. Knowing which is which determines whether you're learning or being misled.

The problem is that signal and noise look identical in the moment. Both appear as data. Both feel informative. A stock price moves up three days in a row. Is that a signal that the company is undervalued and the market is correcting, or noise that means nothing? Your new marketing campaign launches and sales tick up 8%. Signal that it's working, or noise that would have happened anyway? A colleague snaps at you in a meeting. Signal about your relationship, or noise because they slept poorly?

The ratio matters more than the absolute amounts. A weak signal in low noise is detectable. A strong signal in high noise disappears. This is why controlled experiments work—not because they amplify signal, but because they reduce noise. Randomized groups. Held-constant conditions. Eliminating alternative explanations. When you can't run an experiment, you're stuck trying to extract signal from noisy data, which means you need larger samples, longer timeframes, and more patience than intuition suggests.

Most people do the opposite. They treat every fluctuation as meaningful. Every data point as evidence. Every change as a signal to act on. This is how you end up chasing phantoms—adjusting your strategy based on random variation, medicalizing normal childhood behavior, or seeing patterns in stock charts that predict nothing.

The world provides an endless stream of information. Most of it is noise. A competent meteorologist can predict tomorrow's weather better than next month's because the signal-to-noise ratio deteriorates with distance. The same applies to business forecasts, political polling, and personal decisions. The further out you look, the more you're modeling noise and calling it insight.

Learning requires distinguishing signal from noise, but the human mind wasn't optimized for this task. We're pattern-recognition machines in a world full of random variation. We see faces in clouds, agency in coincidence, trends in noise. This worked well enough when the cost of a false positive (seeing danger that isn't there) was lower than a false negative (missing danger that is there). It works poorly when trying to understand complex systems.

The discipline is to be skeptical of small samples, short timeframes, and dramatic fluctuations. To ask: what would I expect to see if this were pure noise? If the answer is "something very similar to what I'm seeing," you haven't found signal yet. You're looking at static and calling it a message.

This doesn't mean signal doesn't exist. It means most of what you observe at any given moment isn't signal. The CEO's quarterly platitudes. The pundit's confident explanation for yesterday's market move. Your own performance on a single day. The trick is knowing which questions generate high-noise answers (Why did the stock market move today?) and which allow signal to emerge (Has this company's competitive position changed over five years?).

Signal exists. But it's usually quieter, slower, and less dramatic than noise. Finding it requires patience, larger samples, and the humility to admit that most of the time, you're watching randomness and learning nothing.
