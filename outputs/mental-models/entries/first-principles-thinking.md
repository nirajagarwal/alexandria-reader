# First Principles Thinking

***Breaking complex problems down to fundamental truths and building up from there***

> **Section:** Problem Solving

In 2002, Elon Musk wanted to buy refurbished rockets to send experimental payloads to Mars. The cheapest he could find cost $8 million each — far more than his budget allowed. Most people would have accepted this as the price of doing business in aerospace, or abandoned the project entirely.

Instead, Musk asked a different question: What is a rocket made of? Aerospace-grade aluminum alloys, titanium, copper, carbon fiber. He pulled up commodity prices on his laptop. The raw materials cost about 2% of the rocket's sale price.

The gap between materials and finished product wasn't physics. It was process, bureaucracy, and industry assumptions that had calcified over decades. Within a few years, SpaceX was building rockets for a fraction of the traditional cost.

This is first principles thinking: the practice of breaking a problem down to its most basic, provable truths and reasoning up from there, rather than reasoning by analogy or convention.

Most of our thinking is analogical. We solve new problems by finding something similar we've seen before. "Podcasts are like radio on demand." "This startup is Uber for dog walking." This works well for incremental improvements. It fails when you need to question the assumptions that everyone else takes as given.

First principles thinking starts with stripping away everything that can be doubted. Descartes did this philosophically — what can I know with absolute certainty? — and arrived at *cogito, ergo sum*. In practical problem-solving, you're looking for the irreducible facts: laws of physics, proven constraints, fundamental truths about human behavior or mathematics.

The rocket example shows the method clearly:

1. **Question the assumption**: Rockets cost $8 million.
2. **Break it down**: What is a rocket, physically?
3. **Identify fundamentals**: Fuel tanks, engines, electronics, structure. Materials with known market prices.
4. **Rebuild from there**: If we manufacture these components differently, what becomes possible?

The gap between conventional wisdom and first principles is often where breakthrough innovation lives. Battery packs were expensive because that's what battery companies charged. But batteries are lithium, cobalt, nickel, and packaging. The commodity cost tells you the theoretical floor — everything above that is process and markup that can potentially be engineered away.

But first principles thinking is brutally difficult. It requires holding uncertainty longer than most people can tolerate. When Musk decided to build rockets from scratch, he had no proof it would work at that price point. He had physics and materials science and a hypothesis. The conventional approach — buy from established suppliers — carried far less psychological risk.

It also requires genuine expertise in the domain. You can't identify true first principles without understanding what's actually fundamental versus what's merely traditional. A non-physicist trying to rebuild rocket science from first principles will likely rediscover why certain design choices exist. The method isn't a shortcut around learning.

And some assumptions that look arbitrary are load-bearing. The FDA approval process seems bureaucratic until you remember thalidomide. Industry standards often encode hard-won lessons. First principles thinking can lead you to discard wisdom along with convention.

The discipline is most valuable when:
- Industry costs seem divorced from fundamental constraints
- Everyone says "that's just how it's done" without good reason
- You're trying to achieve something an order of magnitude different from the status quo
- Analogical thinking keeps leading you back to the same constraints

It's least useful when:
- The problem is truly novel and you lack foundational knowledge
- The conventional approach exists because of genuine constraints you're not seeing
- Speed matters more than optimality

The power of the method lies in its refusal to accept inherited frameworks. But that refusal must be earned through deep understanding, not assumed through ignorance. The question isn't "Why do we do it this way?" asked dismissively, but "Why do we do it this way?" asked with genuine curiosity and the patience to trace the answer down to bedrock.

Start with what you know to be true. Build from there. Everything else is negotiable.
