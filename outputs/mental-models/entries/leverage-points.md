# Leverage Points

***Where small changes produce outsized effects***

> **Section:** Systems Thinking

In 1972, Donella Meadows was asked to help solve world hunger. She was a systems analyst at MIT, trained to model complex problems with equations and computers. The question seemed straightforward: How do we grow more food?

But Meadows didn't start with food production. She started mapping the system—population growth, soil degradation, water supplies, fertilizer costs, distribution networks, political incentives, cultural eating patterns. What she found changed how she thought about solving any problem.

Most interventions, she realized, push on the obvious parts of a system and accomplish almost nothing. A few interventions—applied at specific points—could restructure everything downstream. She called these **leverage points**: places within a system where a small shift produces large, lasting change.

The key is knowing where to push.

Consider drug policy. The obvious intervention is enforcement—more police, harsher sentences, tighter borders. But enforcement is a weak leverage point. It addresses symptoms while the system regenerates the problem. Drug demand persists. Supply routes adapt. Prices fluctuate but markets endure.

A stronger leverage point: the feedback loops that reinforce addiction. Criminal records that prevent employment. Stigma that prevents treatment. Systems that isolate users rather than reintegrate them. Change these, and you alter the conditions that sustain the market itself.

The strongest leverage point of all: the mindset that created the system. The belief that drug use is primarily a criminal problem rather than a medical one. Shift that paradigm, and every other intervention—policy, funding, treatment—follows naturally.

Meadows identified twelve leverage points, ranked from weakest to strongest. At the bottom: numbers like subsidies, taxes, and standards. Useful, but they don't change the system's structure. In the middle: information flows, feedback loops, and the rules that govern who decides what. At the top: the power to transcend paradigms altogether—to see that every model is partial, every system is designed, and design can be changed.

The weakest leverage points are the most obvious. They're where most organizations and governments intervene because they're visible, measurable, and feel productive. "We added 10% more funding." "We hired 50 more staff." Numbers go up. Nothing fundamental changes.

The strongest leverage points are often invisible or dismissed as impractical. Changing minds. Restructuring goals. Altering the assumptions that everyone takes for granted. They're uncomfortable because they threaten existing power structures and identities. But they work.

This reveals why so many reforms fail. Leaders apply weak interventions to strong problems and wonder why the system reverts to its old behavior. They tune parameters when they should be redesigning structures. They add resources when they should be changing rules.

**Where the model misleads:** Not every system has accessible high-leverage points. Sometimes the paradigm is locked in place by incentives you can't shift, or power you don't have. And even when you find a leverage point, timing matters. Push too early, and the system isn't ready. Too late, and momentum has calcified. Leverage points are opportunities, not guarantees.

Also: identifying the leverage point is different from having the authority or resources to act on it. Meadows could see where to intervene in world hunger. She couldn't force nations to change their food systems.

**See also:** Second-Order Effects (what happens downstream), Feedback Loops (the mechanisms leverage points often target), Goodhart's Law (what happens when you optimize the wrong number).

**The takeaway:** Most change happens at the margins. But the leverage points that matter sit upstream—in rules, incentives, information flows, and paradigms. Push there, and the whole system shifts.
