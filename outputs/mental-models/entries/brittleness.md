# Brittleness

***When strength becomes fragility***

> **Section:** Systems & Complexity

In 1977, NASA engineers were proud of the Voyager spacecraft. Every component had been tested to absurd standards. The power systems could survive temperature swings of hundreds of degrees. The communications array could maintain contact across billions of miles. The guidance computer had triple redundancy. Nothing would fail.

Then someone asked: what if the antenna gets stuck?

It was an embarrassing question. The entire mission depended on a single motorized joint that pointed the antenna toward Earth. If it jammed, the spacecraft would drift through space forever, unable to receive commands or transmit data. All that redundancy, all that testing, and one frozen bearing could end everything.

They added manual override protocols and alternate communication methods. The Voyager missions succeeded. But the question revealed something essential about complex systems: optimization for one kind of stress often creates catastrophic vulnerability to another.

This is brittleness. A system is brittle when it performs flawlessly under expected conditions but breaks completely when conditions change. The opposite of brittle isn't weak—it's resilient. Brittle systems are often extremely strong right up until they shatter.

Consider a bridge built to carry 100,000 pounds. Engineers might design it to handle 150,000 pounds with barely perceptible strain. At 160,000 pounds, it collapses instantly. A more resilient design might start sagging at 120,000 pounds—giving visible warning before failure. The brittle bridge is "better" by conventional measures. It's also more dangerous.

Brittleness appears everywhere you find optimization. Financial portfolios engineered for maximum returns in normal markets can evaporate overnight in a crisis. Supply chains designed for perfect efficiency have no slack when one supplier fails. Organizations structured for speed in good times have no adaptation mechanism when the environment shifts.

The pattern is always the same: tight coupling, high performance, hidden fragility. Brittle systems work wonderfully until they don't work at all.

The human version is equally common. The straight-A student who can't function after receiving their first B. The athlete whose identity collapses with their first serious injury. The executive whose entire self-worth depends on quarterly performance. These aren't weak people—they're often exceptionally capable. But their strength has no give.

Resilience requires slack, redundancy, early warning systems. This looks like inefficiency. A company that maintains inventory buffers has higher costs than one running just-in-time. A person who maintains friendships outside work has less time for optimization. A system that operates at 70% capacity seems wasteful.

But when conditions change—and they always do—that slack becomes survival. The inventory buffer keeps production running during a supply disruption. The outside relationships provide support when the job ends. The unused capacity absorbs the unexpected spike.

The trap is that brittle systems are easier to build and measure. You can optimize directly for the thing you want. Resilience requires planning for scenarios you hope never happen. It requires accepting lower performance in normal times to ensure survival in abnormal ones. This feels like poor management until the crisis hits.

You can identify brittleness by asking: what happens at the margins? A resilient system degrades gradually. A brittle one looks perfect, then fails catastrophically. If there's no middle ground between "working" and "broken," you're looking at brittleness.

The question isn't whether your system is strong. It's whether that strength can bend.
