# Optionality

***The value of keeping doors open***

> **Section:** Strategic Thinking

In 1959, a Yale graduate named Fred Smith wrote a term paper arguing that the existing cargo shipping system was obsolete. His idea: create a hub-and-spoke network designed specifically for overnight delivery. His economics professor gave him a C, noting that the concept was interesting but unrealistic.

Smith didn't abandon the idea. Instead, he went to Vietnam as a Marine pilot, then business school, then worked in various roles. He kept the overnight delivery concept alive but didn't force it. He waited. In 1971, after deregulation opened possibilities in air freight, he founded Federal Express with $4 million in inheritance and $91 million in venture capital. The timing was finally right. FedEx now moves 6 million packages daily.

The paper wasn't wasted. The years between weren't either. Smith maintained optionality — the right, but not the obligation, to act when conditions improved.

**Options in Decisions**

Optionality means structuring your choices so that you benefit from good outcomes while limiting exposure to bad ones. It's asymmetric: the upside is large, the downside is capped.

A financial option gives you the right to buy stock at a set price. If the stock soars, you exercise the option and profit. If it crashes, you walk away. You lose only the small price you paid for the option itself. The option is valuable because it participates in gains while avoiding losses.

This asymmetry exists beyond finance. Taking a job at a fast-growing startup preserves your option to return to corporate work, but the reverse isn't always true — once you've been out of startups for years, that door narrows. Learning a second language opens opportunities in multiple countries. Staying physically fit maintains the option to do demanding things later. Saving money creates options when unexpected chances appear.

The pattern: pay a small, known cost now to preserve choices later when you have better information.

**Why We Undervalue It**

Optionality feels like indecision. Commitment feels like virtue. We're taught to "burn the boats" and "go all in." But commitment eliminates options. Sometimes that's exactly right — you can't learn piano while keeping your options open to quit. But often, we commit too early, before uncertainty resolves.

Consider someone choosing between two job offers. One pays slightly more but is highly specialized. The other pays less but builds broader skills. The specialized role might be the "rational" choice today. But the second preserves options: you can move between industries, pivot careers, start something new. In a changing world, that flexibility has enormous value.

We also undervalue optionality because it's invisible. The cost of an option is clear and immediate. The value is hypothetical and distant. You pay $1,000 to keep your apartment while traveling for six months. If you return, you saved nothing. If you don't return, you wasted $1,000. But what you actually bought was freedom — the option to choose, once you had better information about where you wanted to be.

**When It Misleads**

Optionality can become excuse-making. "Keeping your options open" can mean never committing to anything, never developing depth, never doing the hard work that requires saying no to alternatives. The person who studies five languages casually speaks none well. The entrepreneur with ten projects builds nothing substantial.

Nassim Taleb distinguishes between optionality and "drifting." Optionality means setting yourself up so that randomness works in your favor — placing many small bets where the upside is unbounded. Drifting means avoiding decisions entirely, hoping something will work out. One is strategic. The other is just avoidance.

Real optionality also requires resources. You need time, money, energy, or attention to keep options alive. The writer who won't commit to a book because she's "keeping options open" may just be burning runway. At some point, you must spend options to get anywhere.

Finally, some situations genuinely require commitment. You cannot have optionality in deep relationships. You cannot keep your options open about whether to care for your children. Certain domains demand that you close doors.

**The Takeaway**

Build choices that let you benefit from opportunity while limiting your exposure to disaster. But remember: options expire. The value is in using them when conditions shift, not in holding them forever while the world moves on.
