# Commitment Devices

***How we bind our future selves***

> **Section:** Decision-Making

In 1519, Hernán Cortés landed on the coast of Mexico with eleven ships, six hundred men, and ambitions that terrified even his own crew. The Aztec Empire stretched inland — wealthy, vast, and utterly unknown. His men, looking at those ships, saw their escape route. Cortés saw the problem.

So he burned the ships.

Not all of them, technically — he scuttled most and kept one to send back for reinforcements. But the message was clear: there would be no retreat. The only way home was through conquest. His men, stripped of their easiest option, committed fully to the invasion. They conquered the Aztec Empire.

It's a dramatic story, and history textbooks tell it as a tale of leadership. But Cortés was doing something more fundamental: he was using a commitment device. He was deliberately limiting his future options because he didn't trust his future self — or his men — to stay the course when things got hard.

A commitment device is any mechanism that restricts your future choices in order to help you follow through on your intentions. You lock yourself in before the moment of weakness arrives.

Odysseus did this when he ordered his crew to tie him to the mast so he could hear the Sirens' song without steering the ship onto the rocks. He knew he couldn't trust his future self to resist. The ropes were his commitment device.

The concept appears everywhere once you notice it. Someone trying to quit smoking throws away all their cigarettes. Someone trying to save money sets up automatic transfers to a savings account they can't easily access. Someone trying to finish a project tells everyone they know about their deadline. Someone trying to lose weight doesn't buy junk food in the first place.

The insight is simple but powerful: your current self often has better judgment than your future self will have. Right now, calm and rational, you want to save money. But tomorrow, tired and stressed, you'll want to buy something unnecessary. Right now you want to exercise. But when the alarm goes off at six a.m., you'll want to sleep. Right now you're committed to your goal. But when the work gets hard, you'll want to quit.

Commitment devices work because they change the cost structure of backing out. Cortés didn't make his men more courageous — he made retreat more expensive than advance. The smoker who announces their quit date to everyone they know hasn't eliminated cravings — they've added embarrassment to the cost of failure.

The best commitment devices are proportional to the temptation. For small temptations, small barriers work: putting your phone in another room while you work, or asking the waiter not to bring the bread basket. For larger temptations, you need stronger constraints: freezing your credit cards in a block of ice, or signing a contract with financial penalties for missing your deadline.

But commitment devices are not foolproof. They work best when the goal is clear and the temptation is predictable. They work poorly when circumstances genuinely change. If Cortés had needed to retreat for legitimate strategic reasons, those burned ships would have been a catastrophic error. If your automatic savings transfer leaves you unable to pay an emergency medical bill, the commitment device has harmed you.

The other failure mode is overconfidence. People tend to overestimate their future willpower and underestimate future obstacles. They set up commitment devices that are too rigid, then break them when reality intrudes, and lose faith in the entire concept.

The best use of commitment devices is humble. You're not declaring yourself weak-willed. You're recognizing that willpower is a limited resource and that environments shape behavior more than intentions do. You're making the smart choice now so you don't have to make the hard choice later.

Cortés burned his ships and conquered an empire. But most of us aren't conquistadors. We're just trying to finish the project, skip the dessert, or get to the gym. The principle is the same: when you don't trust your future self, sometimes the wisest thing you can do is eliminate the option to fail.
