# Coase Theorem

***When property rights are clear, people bargain their way to efficiency***

> **Section:** Economics & Incentives

In 1960, economist Ronald Coase published a paper that annoyed nearly everyone. The Federal Communications Commission had been agonizing over radio frequency interference. When two stations broadcast on nearby frequencies, their signals could bleed into each other. The FCC's solution: detailed regulations specifying who could broadcast where, elaborate technical requirements, lengthy approval processes.

Coase looked at this mess and asked a simple question: Why not let them work it out themselves?

His argument: If property rights are clearly defined and people can negotiate freely, they'll reach an efficient outcome regardless of who initially holds the rights. Give Station A the right to broadcast? Station B will pay them to stop if the interference is costly enough. Give Station B the right to clean airwaves? Station A will pay for the privilege of broadcasting if their revenue justifies it. Either way, the frequency ends up in the hands of whoever values it most.

The theorem states: In the absence of transaction costs, the allocation of resources will be efficient regardless of the initial assignment of property rights. The parties will bargain until no further mutually beneficial trades exist.

This sounds almost magical. It suggests that ownership doesn't matter—that the invisible hand extends even to negotiations between two parties. But Coase wasn't claiming magic. He was pointing out something most economists had missed: that people negotiate.

Consider a factory that pollutes a river, harming a downstream fishery. The standard economic solution involves taxes, regulations, or government intervention. But if the fishery owns the right to clean water, the factory can pay them for the privilege of polluting. If the factory owns the right to pollute, the fishery can pay them to install scrubbers. In both cases, pollution happens only if the factory's gains exceed the fishery's losses. The efficient outcome emerges from bargaining.

The catch—and Coase emphasized this—is "in the absence of transaction costs." Transaction costs are everything that makes bargaining difficult: information asymmetries, negotiation expenses, enforcement challenges, the sheer number of parties involved. These costs are rarely absent.

When a factory pollutes a river affecting thousands of downstream users, transaction costs explode. Organizing those users, determining each person's harm, negotiating compensation—the costs of these activities can exceed the value of the bargain itself. This is why we have environmental regulations. Not because bargaining couldn't theoretically work, but because it can't practically work when transaction costs are high.

The theorem's real insight isn't that bargaining solves everything. It's that clear property rights enable solutions that central planning often can't see. When a company wants to build a cell tower and homeowners worry about property values, the existence of clear property rights means they can negotiate: maybe the company pays affected homeowners, or agrees to aesthetic modifications, or places the tower differently. Without clear rights—if neither side knows who has the legal claim—they litigate for years, burning money on lawyers while the tower sits unbuilt.

The theorem also reveals when government intervention helps. If transaction costs are high—many affected parties, information problems, enforcement difficulties—then regulations or taxes can approximate what bargaining would achieve if bargaining were feasible. The question isn't "government or markets?" but "which approach has lower transaction costs?"

Where it misleads: The theorem can make efficient outcomes seem inevitable, when they depend entirely on those usually-not-absent transaction costs. It can also obscure distributional concerns—who ends up paying whom matters to the parties involved, even if efficiency is the same. A fishery might reach the same pollution level whether they pay the factory or vice versa, but one outcome leaves them poor and the other leaves them whole.

The takeaway: Clear property rights enable bargaining, and bargaining tends toward efficiency. But only when people can actually negotiate without prohibitive costs. When transaction costs are low, let people work it out. When they're high, that's when regulation earns its keep.
