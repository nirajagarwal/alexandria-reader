# Reversible vs. Irreversible

***When doors close behind you***

> **Section:** Decision Making

In 1994, Jeff Bezos had a comfortable job at D.E. Shaw, a quantitative hedge fund in New York. He was the youngest senior vice president in the firm's history. But he'd noticed something: web usage was growing at 2,300% per year. He wanted to start an online bookstore. His boss tried to talk him out of it.

Bezos used what he called a "regret minimization framework." He projected himself forward to age 80 and asked which choice he'd regret more: trying and failing, or never trying at all. The answer was clear. He quit and drove to Seattle.

What made this decision easier was recognizing what kind of decision it was. If the bookstore failed, he could get another finance job. The door wasn't locked. But if he stayed at D.E. Shaw and the internet opportunity passed, that door would close forever. One choice was reversible. The other was not.

Some decisions let you walk back through the door. Others seal it behind you.

A reversible decision is one where the consequences can be undone or where you can return to roughly your starting position. You try a new restaurant. You don't like it. You don't go back. No permanent harm. You hire someone. They don't work out. You part ways. The team recovers. These decisions deserve speed. You gather basic information, make the call, and learn by doing. If you're wrong, you correct course.

An irreversible decision is one where the consequences are permanent or where undoing them carries massive cost. You have a child. You sell your company. You burn a bridge with someone who trusted you. You can't simply return to the state before. These decisions deserve time. You gather more information. You sit with discomfort. You imagine living with both outcomes.

The mistake most people make is treating reversible decisions like irreversible ones. They agonize over restaurant choices. They hold three-hour meetings about the office coffee supplier. They wait for perfect information on decisions that can be corrected next week. This creates organizational paralysis and personal anxiety.

The more dangerous mistake is treating irreversible decisions like reversible ones. "We can always pivot." "If it doesn't work out, we'll figure something out." Sometimes you can't. The bridge is burned. The money is spent. The years are gone.

Amazon institutionalized this distinction. Bezos divided decisions into "Type 1" (irreversible, one-way doors) and "Type 2" (reversible, two-way doors). Type 1 decisions move slowly up the hierarchy. Type 2 decisions are pushed down to whoever is closest to the information. The result: speed where it matters, care where it counts.

The same logic applies to personal choices. Dating someone is reversible. Marrying them is not truly reversible — divorce is possible, but costly in every sense. Taking a job in a different city is more reversible than having a child. Posting something angry online feels reversible but often isn't. Screenshots exist forever.

The hard part is that irreversibility isn't binary. It's a spectrum. Most decisions carry some degree of permanence. The question is how much. A tattoo is more reversible than it used to be, but laser removal is expensive and incomplete. Moving to a new city is reversible, but the relationships you left behind may not be. Even "trying out" a new career requires time you can't get back.

This is where the model becomes most useful. Before deciding, ask: can I undo this? What would it cost to reverse? What would I lose that I can't replace?

If the answer is "not much," move fast. Gather minimal information. Decide. Learn. Adjust.

If the answer is "a great deal," slow down. What information would change your mind? What are you assuming that might be wrong? Can you run a smaller, more reversible experiment first? What would you tell someone you love facing this choice?

The deepest irreversibility is time. You can't get back the year you spent on something that didn't matter. You can't relive your twenties once they're gone. Many decisions are reversible in theory but irreversible in practice because of the time they consume. This is why the opportunity cost of an irreversible decision is often another irreversible decision you didn't make.

Bezos could have gotten another finance job. But he couldn't get back the years when the internet was young and the field was open. That door would close. He walked through it.

Not every decision reveals its nature immediately. Some doors lock behind you quietly. The way to live with this: move fast on the reversible, slow on the irreversible, and stay honest about which is which.
