# Herding

***Why crowds move in the same direction—and over cliffs***

> **Section:** Social Dynamics

In 1637, a single tulip bulb in Amsterdam could cost more than a house. Not because tulips had suddenly become more useful, but because everyone was buying them. Prices had climbed for months. Neighbors spoke of fortunes made overnight. A merchant who'd paid ten guilders for a bulb in January sold it for fifty in March. By November, rare varieties traded for amounts that would buy entire estates.

Then, in February 1638, someone failed to find a buyer. Then another. Within weeks, the market collapsed. Bulbs that had commanded a year's wages sold for the price of an onion. Fortunes evaporated. The only thing that had changed was that people stopped buying because other people stopped buying.

This is herding—the tendency to follow what others are doing, especially when uncertainty is high and information is scarce. It's not irrational. Often, following the crowd is the smart move. If everyone is running toward the exit, you probably should too. If every restaurant on the street is empty except one, that one likely has better food.

The mechanism is simple: we use other people's behavior as information. When we can't assess quality or risk directly, we look for social proof. This works because crowds often aggregate distributed knowledge. A line outside a bakery signals something good. A stampede away from danger signals threat.

But herding has a critical flaw: it can become self-reinforcing, detached from underlying reality. The tulip bulbs didn't become more valuable—the behavior of buying made them appear valuable, which prompted more buying. The information loop closed on itself.

This happens in financial markets constantly. A stock rises because people are buying it. More people notice the rise and buy in. The rise accelerates, not because the company's fundamentals changed, but because the buying itself generates the signal to buy more. Until it doesn't.

Herding explains bank runs. One person withdraws their savings because they doubt the bank's solvency. Others see the withdrawal and doubt spreads. More people withdraw. The bank, which was solvent when the first person doubted it, becomes insolvent because everyone acted on that doubt simultaneously.

It explains fashion, fads, and moral panics. It explains why restaurants succeed or fail in their first months—often based not on food quality but on whether they appeared full or empty on opening weekend.

The dangerous moment in herding is the cascade—when private information gets overwhelmed by public action. You might suspect the tulips are overpriced, but if everyone around you is getting rich buying them, you override your own judgment. You join the herd. When enough people do this, the herd's direction becomes disconnected from reality. Everyone is following everyone else, and no one is following the underlying facts.

Herding also protects from blame. If you buy tulip bulbs and lose everything alone, you're a fool. If you lose everything because everyone else did too, you had bad luck. The same psychology appears in hiring, investing, and product launches. "Nobody ever got fired for buying IBM" was shorthand for: it's safer to fail conventionally than succeed unconventionally.

The model misleads when you assume herds are always wrong. Sometimes the crowd knows something you don't. The trick is distinguishing informational cascades—where the crowd is following real signals—from behavioral cascades, where the crowd is following itself.

Ask: What information are people actually responding to? If the answer is "other people's behavior," you're likely in a herd. If the answer is verifiable facts, the crowd might be right.

The tulip buyers weren't stupid. They were responding rationally to the signals around them. But those signals had become untethered from value. The bulbs were worth something because people believed they were worth something. When belief broke, value vanished.

Herding is powerful because it's usually useful, occasionally catastrophic, and almost impossible to resist. The person who stays out of the herd might be wise or might miss out. The only certainty is that when the herd turns, it turns all at once.
