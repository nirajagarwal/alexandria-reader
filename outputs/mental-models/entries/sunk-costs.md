# Sunk Costs

***Why past spending shouldn't determine future choices***

> **Section:** Decision Making

In 1971, the British and French governments faced a problem. The Concorde supersonic jet—their joint venture to dominate commercial aviation—was hemorrhaging money. Development costs had ballooned to ten times the original estimate. Market analysis showed the plane would never turn a profit. Airlines weren't interested in buying it. The economic case for continuing was nonexistent.

They built it anyway. And kept flying it for thirty-four years, at a loss.

The reasoning was simple and wrong: "We've already spent so much, we can't stop now."

This is the sunk cost fallacy in action. Once you've invested money, time, or effort into something, that investment is gone. It's sunk. The rational choice is to ask: Given where I am now, what's the best path forward? But that's not what humans do. We look backward instead of forward. We let past spending—which we cannot recover—dictate future decisions.

The fallacy shows up everywhere. You sit through a terrible movie because you paid for the ticket. You finish a meal you're no longer enjoying because you ordered it. You stay in a failing relationship because you've already invested three years. You pour more money into a renovation that's spiraling out of control because you've already spent so much.

The logic feels intuitive: "If I stop now, all that money/time/effort will have been wasted." But here's the truth: it's already wasted. The only question is whether you'll waste more.

Economists call unrecoverable past costs "sunk costs." The rule is simple: ignore them. Make decisions based solely on future costs and benefits. If you're in a movie theater thirty minutes in and the film is unwatchable, the ticket price is irrelevant. The question is whether the next ninety minutes are better spent there or elsewhere.

This sounds obvious. It's not. The sunk cost fallacy is one of the most persistent errors in human judgment. We feel the pain of loss more acutely than we should. Admitting that past investment was a mistake feels like doubling the loss. So we throw good money after bad, hoping to justify what we've already spent.

Businesses are especially vulnerable. A company invests millions in a product line that's clearly failing. Executives know it's doomed, but shutting it down means admitting the investment was a mistake. So they keep funding it, hoping something will change. The longer they wait, the more they lose, and the harder it becomes to stop. The sunk cost compounds.

The Concorde burned billions that could have gone elsewhere. Every hour it flew unprofitably was justified by the billions already spent. The past trapped the future.

The remedy is a question: If I weren't already invested in this, would I choose to invest now? If you hadn't already bought the ticket, would you pay to see the rest of this movie? If you hadn't already spent two years on the project, would you start it today? If the answer is no, you have your decision.

This is harder than it sounds. It requires acknowledging loss. It requires seeing your past self's decision as separate from your present self's choice. It requires accepting that waste is final—that the only thing you can control is what you waste next.

The Concorde finally stopped flying in 2003. Not because the governments admitted the mistake, but because the costs became impossible to ignore. Thirty-two years late.

Don't let the past hold your future hostage. The money is gone. The time is spent. The only question is what you do next.
