# 1947 — Glycogen Metabolism (Gerty Cori)

The body runs on sugar. Every movement, every thought, every heartbeat draws on glucose — but the body cannot store glucose directly. It converts the sugar to glycogen, chains of glucose molecules packed into liver and muscle, ready to be broken down when energy is needed. In 1947, Gerty Cori shared the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for discovering exactly how this happens: the chemical steps, the enzymes, the intermediate compounds that make it possible for a body to stockpile fuel and spend it on demand.

She was the first woman to win a Nobel in science. The prize recognized three decades of work done almost entirely in collaboration with her husband, Carl Cori. They published together, worked side by side in the same laboratory, and shared credit for every discovery. The Nobel committee split the prize three ways: half to Carl and Gerty Cori jointly, and half to the Argentine physiologist Bernardo Houssay for related work on hormonal regulation of sugar metabolism.

Gerty Theresa Radnitz was born in Prague in 1896, the eldest of three daughters in a Jewish family. Her father managed sugar refineries — an irony she would later note, given where her career led. She was educated at home until age ten, then attended a girls' school that taught languages and arts but almost no science. At sixteen, she decided she wanted to study medicine. The only path was the gymnasium, the academic secondary school required for university admission, but her schooling had left her unprepared. She taught herself Latin, mathematics, physics, and chemistry in a single year and passed the entrance examination.

At the German University of Prague's medical school, she met Carl Cori, another medical student. They married in 1920, the year they both received their degrees. The marriage was a true partnership. "I believe that in art and science the personalities of the two partners are of paramount importance," she would write later. "Our collaboration began thirty years ago when we were still medical students and continued throughout our lives."

Europe in the early 1920s offered little to Jewish scientists. They emigrated separately — Carl first, to work at a research institute in Buffalo, New York; Gerty six months later, after completing a position in Vienna. In Buffalo, she took a job as an assistant pathologist at the State Institute for the Study of Malignant Diseases. Carl worked at the same institute. They were finally in the same place, but the work was cancer research, distant from their shared interest in metabolism.

They pursued that interest anyway, in their spare time, on minimal equipment. The institute disapproved. The director warned Gerty that working with her husband would harm his career. Collaborating too closely with a woman, even his wife, would make him look weak. She was told to work independently or stop.

She did neither. They kept working together. By 1928, they had mapped the cycle of carbohydrate metabolism: how glucose travels from the bloodstream to muscle, gets broken down for energy, converts to lactic acid, returns to the liver, and reforms into glucose again. It became known as the Cori cycle. It was foundational work, and it was clearly joint. That year they published a summary paper in the *Journal of Biological Chemistry* under both their names, Cori and Cori, alphabetical and equal.

They moved in 1931 to Washington University School of Medicine in St. Louis, where Carl had been offered a chair in pharmacology. Gerty was not offered a faculty position. The university hired her as a research associate — a title without tenure, without salary security, without voting rights in the department. She earned a fraction of her husband's pay. This was the job she would hold for the next dozen years, through the most productive period of their research.

The Cori cycle explained the path of glucose through the body, but not the mechanism. What happened inside the liver cell? What enzymes were involved? What intermediate compounds formed along the way? To answer those questions required working with tissue extracts, isolating enzymes, identifying chemical structures — work at the level of molecules, not organs.

In 1936, they isolated a new compound from muscle tissue: glucose-1-phosphate. It was a phosphorylated form of glucose, an intermediate step in both the synthesis and breakdown of glycogen. This was the missing link. They called it "Cori ester." The compound was proof that glycogen metabolism proceeded through a series of reversible chemical reactions, each catalyzed by a specific enzyme. The body could build glycogen or break it down depending on which enzymes were active.

Over the next decade, working with a growing team of researchers, they mapped the entire pathway. They identified phosphorylase, the enzyme that breaks down glycogen into glucose-1-phosphate. They found the enzyme that converts glucose-1-phosphate to glucose-6-phosphate, and the one that synthesizes glycogen from glucose-1-phosphate. They crystallized phosphorylase and studied its structure. They showed how the system worked in reverse: how the body could take glucose, add phosphate groups, and assemble glycogen chains.

The work had immediate medical relevance. Certain diseases — some forms of diabetes, some childhood metabolic disorders — involved failures of glycogen metabolism. Understanding the enzymes made it possible to identify where the breakdown occurred. Four types of glycogen storage disease are still named for the Coris and their collaborators.

Gerty was the driving force behind the enzyme work. Carl focused more on the physiological side — whole-body metabolism, hormonal regulation. But every major paper listed both names. Their laboratory became a training ground for biochemists. Six researchers who worked with the Coris later won their own Nobels.

In 1944, Washington University finally promoted Gerty to associate professor of biochemistry. In 1947, the same year as the Nobel, she became a full professor. She had worked at the institution for sixteen years before receiving a faculty title.

The Nobel Prize ceremony was in Stockholm in December 1947. Gerty was forty-one. She was already ill with a rare blood disorder, myelofibrosis, which had been diagnosed three years earlier. Her doctors gave her ten years at most. She continued working as her condition worsened: refining the understanding of enzyme structure, mentoring students, writing papers. The disease slowly destroyed her bone marrow.

She kept her laboratory until the end. By 1957, she was too weak to work full days but still came in when she could. She died in October that year, at sixty-one. Carl outlived her by thirty years. He never remarried.

"For a research worker," Gerty Cori once said, "the unforgotten moments of his life are those rare ones, which come after years of plodding work, when the veil over nature's secret seems suddenly to lift and when what was dark and chaotic appears in a clear and beautiful light and pattern."

The light was literal: watching a test tube full of muscle extract turn cloudy as glycogen formed from simple sugars, the first direct observation of the synthesis happening outside a living cell. The pattern was the pathway, the sequence of enzymes and intermediates, the chemical logic of how a body stores and spends its fuel. She and Carl had lifted the veil. The Nobel recognized both of them for it, but the academy's records show what everyone in their field already knew: the work was joint, the insight shared. The first woman to win a Nobel in science won it as a partner, not a pioneer apart.
