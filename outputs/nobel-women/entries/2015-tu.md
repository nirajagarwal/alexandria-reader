# 2015 — Artemisinin and Malaria (Tu Youyou)

In 1969, in the middle of China's Cultural Revolution, a pharmaceutical chemist named Tu Youyou received an assignment: find a cure for malaria. The disease was killing soldiers in Vietnam. Western drugs were failing as parasites evolved resistance. She was handed ancient medical texts and a near-impossible mandate — extract something from traditional Chinese medicine that modern science had missed.

Six years later, she had it. Artemisinin, drawn from sweet wormwood, would become the most effective antimalarial treatment in history. It would save millions of lives, mostly in Africa and Southeast Asia. And it came from a method no one else would have thought to try.

Tu was born in 1930 in Ningbo, on China's eastern coast. Her father, a banker, named her after a line in the *Book of Songs*: "Deer cry out, eating the wild *youyou*." The plant referenced was mugwort, a cousin to the wormwood she would later make famous. She contracted tuberculosis as a teenager — two years in bed, watching her schooling slip away. The illness shaped her path: when she recovered, she chose pharmacy. She wanted to make medicines.

By 1969, she was a mid-level researcher at the Academy of Traditional Chinese Medicine in Beijing. Not a senior figure. Not politically connected. But methodical, tireless, and willing to do the work others wouldn't. When Project 523 — China's secret antimalarial research program — needed someone to screen traditional remedies, she volunteered.

The challenge was formidable. Malaria parasites had developed resistance to chloroquine and other standard treatments. Half the world's population lived in malaria zones. In Vietnam, disease was sidelining more soldiers than combat. And traditional Chinese medicine, while rich in centuries of observation, offered no chemical clarity. Tu's task was to bridge that gap: take folk knowledge and turn it into something reproducible, testable, effective.

She started with the texts. The *Handbook of Prescriptions for Emergencies*, written in 340 CE by Ge Hong, mentioned sweet wormwood (*Artemisia annua*) for treating intermittent fevers — a symptom pattern consistent with malaria. The prescription was simple: soak a handful of wormwood in water, wring it out, drink the juice.

Tu extracted compounds from sweet wormwood using standard methods. Boiling. Ethanol. None worked. The extracts showed little antimalarial activity in mice. She moved on to other plants, testing hundreds, circling back periodically. Something about the wormwood kept nagging her. The ancient text was too specific. Intermittent fevers. That precision suggested real observation.

Then she reconsidered the method. Ge Hong's prescription didn't call for boiling — just soaking in cold water. Heat, she realized, might be destroying the active compound. In 1971, she tried a low-temperature extraction using ether. The result was immediate and dramatic: 100% inhibition of malaria parasites in mice.

She called the extract qinghaosu — "essence of sweet wormwood." Later, the world would know it as artemisinin.

Testing the compound required certainty about its safety. No animal data would be enough — someone had to try it first. Tu volunteered herself. She and two colleagues took the extract in escalating doses, monitoring for toxicity. Only after establishing safety in their own bodies did they proceed to clinical trials. The first human trials, conducted in workers and patients in Hainan Province, showed rapid parasite clearance and fever reduction. Artemisinin worked where other drugs had failed.

The discovery's elegance lies in its mechanism. Artemisinin contains an unusual peroxide bridge — a molecular structure rare in nature. When the malaria parasite digests hemoglobin, it releases iron. That iron reacts with artemisinin's peroxide bridge, generating free radicals that destroy the parasite from within. It's precise, fast-acting, and effective against drug-resistant strains. The parasite's own biology triggers the drug's lethality.

Tu isolated and characterized artemisinin's structure by 1972. But getting the world to notice took decades. China was isolated during the Cultural Revolution; scientific communication with the West was minimal. Project 523's work was classified. Tu published papers, but under collective authorship — individual credit was politically suspect. When China began opening to international research in the 1980s, artemisinin's story slowly emerged.

The World Health Organization endorsed artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) in 2001. By then, artemisinin had already saved countless lives in Southeast Asia. Its adoption in Africa followed. Malaria deaths, which peaked around 2004, began a steep decline. Between 2000 and 2015, malaria mortality dropped by 60% globally. Artemisinin was the primary driver.

Recognition came late. Tu received the Lasker Award in 2011 — the first Chinese scientist so honored. The Nobel Prize followed in 2015. At 84, she traveled to Stockholm, the first female Chinese national to win a Nobel in science. Her Nobel lecture was characteristically straightforward: she described the work, acknowledged her colleagues, and quoted Ge Hong. "Success," she said, "is a collective effort."

The achievement is double-layered. First, the chemistry: isolating a structurally novel compound from a complex plant extract, determining its mechanism, making it reproducible. Second, the vision: seeing traditional knowledge not as folklore but as data — centuries of human observation awaiting systematic investigation. Tu bridged worlds that often ignore each other.

Some controversies linger. Who deserves credit for artemisinin's discovery? Tu led the work, isolated the compound, and published first. But others contributed: chemists who refined derivatives, clinicians who conducted trials, researchers who worked in parallel. The Nobel Prize went to Tu alone, an unusual departure from Project 523's collective ethos. Some colleagues felt overlooked. Tu has acknowledged the team effort while standing by her central role.

Resistance is emerging. Artemisinin-resistant malaria parasites have appeared in Southeast Asia, a reminder that evolution doesn't stop. The drug remains effective when used in combination therapies, but vigilance is required. Tu has called for continued research and careful drug deployment. The fight against malaria, she notes, is never finished.

Sweet wormwood grows wild across temperate zones. Farmers now cultivate it commercially; synthetic artemisinin is under development to stabilize supply. The plant Ge Hong wrote about in the fourth century — the plant in Tu Youyou's childhood poem — now anchors global public health. Millions of people are alive because she read an old text carefully and thought to try cold water instead of heat.
