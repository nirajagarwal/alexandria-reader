# 2023 — Human Rights in Iran (Narges Mohammadi)

The citation came from inside Evin Prison. Narges Mohammadi had spent most of the previous decade behind bars, yet she continued documenting torture, organizing hunger strikes, and amplifying the voices of other prisoners. When the Norwegian Nobel Committee announced her name in October 2023, she was serving multiple sentences totaling more than thirty years. Her teenage children accepted the prize in her place. It was the fifth Peace Prize awarded to someone in prison, and the second to an Iranian woman fighting the Islamic Republic's repression.

The award recognized not a single campaign but a life spent confronting a system built on suppression. For more than two decades, Mohammadi had been among Iran's most prominent human rights defenders, facing arrest after arrest for demanding freedom of expression, women's rights, and an end to the death penalty. By 2023, she had been imprisoned thirteen times, convicted five times, sentenced to death row, and subjected to physical abuse. Each release brought only temporary freedom before the next arrest. The pattern became the story itself — a regime that could not silence her, and an activist who would not stop.

Mohammadi came to activism through physics. Born in 1972 in Zanjan, she studied the subject at university in Qazvin, where she also began writing about women's rights. After graduation, she worked as an engineer but increasingly turned toward human rights work. In 2003, she joined the Defenders of Human Rights Center, an organization co-founded by Shirin Ebadi, who would win the Peace Prize that same year. Mohammadi became the center's vice president, coordinating campaigns against the death penalty and documenting cases of political prisoners.

The work made her a target. Her first arrest came in 2010 — eleven years in prison for "acting against national security." Released after a year, she returned immediately to activism. Another arrest in 2015 brought a sixteen-year sentence. The authorities seemed to believe that longer terms would break her. Instead, she turned prison into a platform.

Inside Evin, she wrote. Letters, essays, reports — documentation of conditions that the regime wanted hidden. She described the torture of women protesters, the abuse of political prisoners, the psychological warfare of solitary confinement. She organized fellow inmates, coordinating responses to injustice within the walls. When prison authorities moved to suppress protests in 2022, she helped organize a collective statement from inside. When the regime executed prisoners, she made their names known.

The 2022 protests made Mohammadi's imprisonment especially pointed. After twenty-two-year-old Mahsa Amini died in custody of the morality police — arrested for improper hijab — Iran erupted. Women led demonstrations under the slogan "Woman, Life, Freedom," burning headscarves and facing live ammunition. The regime's response was brutal: hundreds killed, thousands arrested. Mohammadi, already imprisoned, became a voice for the movement from inside Evin, documenting the treatment of arrested protesters and calling out executions meant to terrorize.

The Nobel Committee cited her "fight against the oppression of women in Iran and her fight to promote human rights and freedom for all." The phrasing linked her long record of advocacy with the specific moment of the Amini protests — a recognition that her work had prepared the ground for that uprising and continued to sustain it from behind bars.

Her husband and children had been living in France since 2015, refugees themselves from the regime's pressure. They had not seen her in years. Her son Kian and daughter Ali read her statement at the Nobel ceremony: "I write this message from behind the high, cold walls of a prison... The more of us they imprison, the stronger we become."

The Iranian government dismissed the prize as political interference. State media called it a Western provocation. The award came with no prospect of release — the regime had already demonstrated that international pressure would not move it to free her. Previous laureates imprisoned at the time of their awards — Carl von Ossietzky, Andrei Sakharov, Lech Wałęsa, Aung San Suu Kyi, Liu Xiaobo — experienced varying outcomes. Some eventually went free; others died in custody or, like Suu Kyi, later fell from grace. Mohammadi's fate remained uncertain.

What was certain was the pattern she had established: document, organize, endure. The work she had done before imprisonment equipped others to continue it. The work she continued during imprisonment showed that suppression had limits. The regime could confine her body but not contain the information she gathered or the example she set.

By 2023, Iran's human rights situation had worsened. Executions had increased. Protests were met with lethal force. Women who removed their headscarves faced arrest. The regime's grip seemed tighter than ever. Yet the structure Mohammadi helped build — networks of documentation, international attention, organized resistance — persisted. The prize recognized work that would outlast any single person, including its laureate.

She had written from prison that her imprisonment was "a small price to pay for defending human values." The statement was both defiant and pragmatic — an acknowledgment that the work demanded this cost, and that she had chosen to pay it repeatedly. The Nobel Committee's decision honored not just her courage but her strategic clarity: that documenting oppression, naming victims, and refusing silence could shift power even when power seemed absolute.

Mohammadi remained in Evin Prison after the award. The work continued.
