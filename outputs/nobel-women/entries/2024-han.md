# 2024 — Confronting Historical Trauma (Han Kang)

Han Kang writes about violence with a peculiar stillness. Her novels circle wounds that will not close: a woman who stops eating after witnessing a massacre, a boy beaten to death by his teacher, bodies piled in a government building in Gwangju. The prose is spare, almost clinical, yet it conveys something unbearable. When the Swedish Academy awarded her the Nobel Prize in Literature, they cited "her intense poetic prose that confronts historical traumas and exposes the fragility of human life." The citation is accurate but incomplete. What makes Han Kang's work devastating is not just that she confronts trauma — it's how she renders consciousness under its weight.

She was born in 1970 in Gwangju, South Korea, a city whose name would come to mean something specific in Korean history. Her father, Han Seung-won, was a novelist. The family moved to Seoul when Han Kang was nine. She studied Korean literature at Yonsei University, wrote poetry first, then fiction. Her early work was well-regarded in Korea but not widely translated. That changed in 2016 with the English publication of *The Vegetarian*, a novel written a decade earlier.

*The Vegetarian* begins with a marriage. The narrator, a middling office worker, has chosen a wife who seems unremarkable — "completely unremarkable in every way." Then she stops eating meat. Not as a political statement or health choice, but because of dreams. Violent dreams. The refusal escalates: she won't eat animal products, then cooked food, then eventually almost nothing. Her husband is bewildered, then angry. Her family intervenes with force. The novel shifts perspective — to her brother-in-law, who becomes obsessed with her, then to her sister, who watches her disappear. What could be read as anorexia narrative or body horror is something stranger: a woman trying to erase her own creatureliness, to become plant rather than animal. To stop being a thing that consumes and is consumed.

The violence in Han Kang's work is both spectacular and domestic. *The Vegetarian* contains a scene of family violence so abrupt and brutal that readers often have to stop. But the real horror is quieter: the steady erasure of a woman who cannot articulate what she's fleeing. Han Kang writes consciousness from inside, in fragments, with a precision that makes the unbearable legible. The prose is translucent. Nothing is explained; everything is shown.

*Human Acts* goes to the wound directly. The novel is set during and after the Gwangju Uprising of May 1980, when students and citizens protested martial law and were massacred by government troops. Hundreds died; the exact number is still contested. Han Kang was too young to remember directly, but the event saturated her childhood. "I couldn't help but ask myself," she wrote later, "what it means to be human, and in what circumstances a person can be stripped of humanity."

The novel begins in the provincial office where bodies are laid out. A middle-school boy searches for his friend among the dead. The prose is achingly specific: the smell, the temperature, the work of tending corpses. The narrative shifts through perspectives — the dead, the living, those who survived and carry it. A woman tortured in prison. An editor censored for decades. The boy's mother, decades later. Each chapter is a different voice, a different angle on the same rupture. The technique risks fragmentation, but Han Kang holds it together through the sheer intensity of attention. She writes as though bearing witness were a moral act.

The elegance of Han Kang's work lies in its restraint. She does not explain trauma; she enacts how it lives in bodies and language. Her sentences are short, declarative, but they accumulate into something overwhelming. There is almost no metaphor. The violence is literal. The body is literal. A woman becomes a tree. A boy is a corpse. The dead speak in second person: "You. You wander those streets." This refusal of distance is what makes the work feel urgent rather than literary.

*The White Book* is even more stripped down. Written after her sister's death — a sister who died as an infant before Han Kang was born — it is a meditation on whiteness, on things that are white: snow, salt, shrouds, infant clothes. The book is part memoir, part essay, part prose poem. It circles grief that precedes memory, loss that structures a life. "I possessed no map of this time," she writes. "Nothing to take hold of, nothing to lean against." The form itself becomes a kind of searching.

She returned to historical trauma in *We Do Not Part*, which engages with the Jeju Uprising of 1948, another massacre largely silenced in official history. Tens of thousands killed in anti-communist purges. Again: bodies, silence, the difficulty of speaking about violence when language itself has been compromised. Han Kang's obsessions are consistent. What happens to people when violence is inflicted by the state. How do you carry knowledge of atrocity. What does it cost to be a body in a world where bodies are destroyed.

Her work is sometimes called "poetic," but that suggests decoration. Han Kang's prose is poetic in the original sense: it makes language do more than communicate. It enacts. A sentence about washing a corpse becomes about care under impossible conditions. A dream about flowers becomes about the desire to escape being human. The style is deceptive. It seems simple. It is not.

She lives in Seoul, teaches creative writing, avoids publicity. In interviews, she is careful, almost reticent. "I wanted to understand what it means to be human," she said of *Human Acts*. Not to explain, not to redeem — to understand. The distinction matters. Her novels do not offer consolation. They do not resolve. They insist on staying in the unbearable place.

The Nobel committee's citation emphasizes historical trauma, but Han Kang's range is wider. *The Vegetarian* is about a woman, a marriage, a body. *The White Book* is about absence and tenderness. What unites the work is the question she keeps asking: What does it take to stay human when everything pushes toward erasure or violence? She does not answer. She writes the question over and over, in different registers, with a ferocity disguised as calm.

She is the first Korean woman to win the Nobel in Literature, the 18th woman laureate in the category. South Korea celebrated; the prize felt overdue to those who knew her work. Internationally, the award brought new attention to contemporary Korean literature, a lineage of writers engaging with the country's violent twentieth century. Han Kang writes from within that history, but her concerns are not regional. The fragility of human life is not a Korean problem. The difficulty of bearing witness is universal. What she offers is a way of writing that does not flinch, does not console, and does not look away.

"I thought the only way to face it," she said of writing *Human Acts*, "was to go into it, completely into it." That sentence could describe all her work. The reader does not emerge comforted. But something has been seen, and that seeing feels necessary.
