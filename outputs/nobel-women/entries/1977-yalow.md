# 1977 — Radioimmunoassay (Rosalyn Yalow)

The amounts were impossibly small — billionths of a gram floating in blood. Insulin. Growth hormone. Hepatitis virus. Before Rosalyn Yalow's technique, they were invisible to measurement. After it, medicine could see what it had only guessed at. Diabetes, thyroid disease, growth disorders, viral infections, drug levels in the bloodstream — all became quantifiable. The tool she invented became so fundamental that within fifteen years, radioimmunoassay tests were running in hospital labs worldwide, millions of times a year.

She built it to solve one specific problem: whether Type 2 diabetics produced their own insulin.

Yalow came to physics through pure stubbornness. Born in the Bronx in 1921, she was an undergraduate at Hunter College during the Depression, graduating in 1941. Graduate schools in physics didn't want women. She was accepted to one program — as a secretary who might take courses on the side. Instead, she took a teaching position that came with space at the University of Illinois. She was the only woman in the College of Engineering's faculty, the first since 1917.

After a doctorate in nuclear physics and a wartime stint teaching at Hunter, she ended up at the Bronx Veterans Administration Hospital in 1947. The VA was building a radioisotope unit — one of the first medical applications of the nuclear physics that had just won the war. Yalow was hired to set up equipment and train physicians. She met Solomon Berson, a young internist brilliant enough to match her intensity. They began collaborating. The partnership would last twenty-two years.

Berson wanted to understand adult-onset diabetes. The standard theory held that Type 2 diabetics couldn't make insulin — their pancreases had simply failed. But insulin therapy often stopped working in these patients. Why would bodies that lacked insulin develop resistance to it?

Yalow suggested they measure insulin directly in blood. The problem was scale. Insulin circulates in amounts measured in picograms — trillionths of a gram. Chemical tests couldn't detect it. Even the newest techniques were millions of times too crude.

But she was a nuclear physicist. Radioactive tracers could detect vanishingly small quantities. And she understood antibodies — proteins the immune system produces to bind specific molecules. Combine the two: let antibodies grab insulin, mark the insulin with radioactive iodine, measure the radiation.

The elegant part was the competition. Mix blood plasma with antibodies and radioactive insulin. If the blood contains unlabeled insulin, it competes with the radioactive insulin for antibody binding sites. Measure how much radioactive insulin gets bound — less binding means more insulin in the blood. By calibrating against known quantities, you could measure insulin levels in a single drop of blood.

They published the first results in 1959. The paper was initially rejected — the journal's reviewers didn't believe the method could work. When it finally appeared, it confirmed that Type 2 diabetics did produce insulin. Their problem wasn't production but resistance. The finding reshaped diabetes treatment.

The technique mattered more than the specific result. Yalow and Berson realized they'd invented something universal. Any substance that provoked an antibody response could be measured the same way. Hormones, drugs, viruses, vitamins — anything. The method was exquisitely sensitive and stunningly simple. It required no elaborate equipment, just antibodies, radioactive labels, and careful technique.

They didn't patent it. Yalow later explained the decision matter-of-factly: "We wanted it used as widely as possible." Within a few years, researchers worldwide were adapting radioimmunoassay to measure growth hormone, thyroid hormone, steroids, hepatitis antigens. The Food and Drug Administration began requiring RIA tests for blood bank screening in 1972. Drug development leaned on it to measure how compounds moved through the body. Endocrinology — the study of hormones — was transformed from guesswork into precision medicine.

Berson died in 1972 of a heart attack, at fifty-four. Yalow refused to let the Nobel Committee name the technique after both of them; the original papers bore both names and always would, but she would not claim shared credit for work he couldn't receive recognition for. She named her laboratory the Solomon A. Berson Research Laboratory and continued working there for the next thirty years.

The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine came in 1977, the first for a woman in medicine since 1947. Yalow shared it with two other researchers for unrelated work; her half recognized radioimmunoassay. She was the second woman to win in physics-adjacent sciences, after Marie Curie.

In her Nobel lecture, she described the method's power in characteristically direct terms: "The ability to measure the unmeasurable opened the door to understanding." It also opened doors she hadn't needed opened — suddenly institutions that had ignored her were eager to honor her. She accepted appointments, awards, lectures. She never softened her manner. Asked about barriers women faced in science, she replied: "If you are good enough, they will let you in. But you have to prove yourself more."

She kept working into her eighties, still at the Bronx VA. The technique she'd invented had become so standard that physicians ordering tests no longer thought about its origins. Medical students learned to interpret RIA results without knowing who developed them. In her office, she kept the original equipment: glass beakers, a radiation counter, handwritten notes from 1959. Simple tools. Impossible measurements.

Radioimmunoassay has since been largely replaced by newer techniques — enzyme immunoassays, chemiluminescent assays, mass spectrometry. But the principle remains: use biological specificity to measure the invisible. The method that made modern endocrinology possible was designed by a nuclear physicist who wanted to answer one question about diabetes and ended up giving medicine the ability to see what had been unseeable.
