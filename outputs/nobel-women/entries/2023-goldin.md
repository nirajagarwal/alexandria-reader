# 2023 — Women in the Labor Market (Claudia Goldin)

The puzzle wasn't why women earned less than men — everyone knew they did. The puzzle was why the gap persisted even as women's education caught up, then surpassed men's. Why it narrowed in some professions but not others. Why it widened after women had children, but only in certain careers. Claudia Goldin spent four decades tracing women's economic history in America, and what she found wasn't a single story of discrimination or progress. It was something more intricate: the collision between jobs structured for workers with no other obligations and lives that rarely worked that way.

Goldin didn't theorize from aggregate statistics. She built databases no one else had bothered to construct. Graduation records from the 1890s. Census manuscripts listing occupations by hand. Surveys tracking cohorts of women through decades of work. She wanted to know not what women earned on average, but what individual women actually did — when they worked, when they stopped, when they returned, and what changed in between.

What emerged was a pattern. Through most of the twentieth century, women's labor force participation followed a predictable arc: they worked before marriage, left when children came, sometimes returned when children grew. Each generation shifted the curve — working a bit longer, returning a bit sooner — but the basic shape held. Then, in the 1970s, something broke. Women stopped leaving. The curve flattened into a plateau, and for the first time, career became something many women could sustain across their adult lives.

But participation wasn't the same as equality. Even as women flooded professional fields — law, medicine, business — the wage gap barely budged in the top quartile. Goldin traced the divergence to a feature of certain jobs that economists had overlooked: the premium for long hours and immediate availability. A lawyer billing eighty hours a week earned far more per hour than one working forty. A consultant on call for clients commanded higher rates than one keeping boundaries. Jobs that rewarded temporal flexibility — working any time, all the time — penalized workers who couldn't or wouldn't comply.

This wasn't about skills or credentials. Women with identical education and experience to men earned less in finance, law, and corporate management, but the gap was smaller in pharmacy, veterinary medicine, and some technical fields. The difference? The latter had moved toward standardization. Clients didn't request specific pharmacists; one veterinarian could cover for another; tasks could be divided across shifts. The work itself didn't demand always-on availability, so part-time or flexible arrangements didn't carry steep penalties.

Goldin called this the "greedy work" problem. Certain professions had organized themselves to extract maximum hours and availability, rewarding those who gave them. The structure wasn't designed to exclude women — it predated their entry into these fields — but it had that effect once women were more likely to shoulder caregiving responsibilities. The gap wasn't primarily about discrimination in hiring or promotion. It was about compensation structures that assumed workers had no competing obligations.

The data showed this most clearly in the trajectories of MBA graduates. Men and women from top programs started at similar salaries and positions. Ten years later, men earned far more — but the divergence tracked almost entirely to hours worked and career interruptions, mostly driven by childbirth and childcare. Women who remained childless, or whose partners took primary caregiving roles, had earnings nearly identical to men's. The penalty wasn't for being a woman; it was for taking time or setting boundaries, which women disproportionately did.

Goldin documented one more shift: the rise of joint career couples. Through most of the twentieth century, married women's work was secondary to their husbands' careers — families moved for his job, organized around his schedule. By the 1990s, a growing fraction of couples included two professionals with careers of equal potential. This meant more negotiation, more compromise, and for women, often more constrained choices. The couple that moved for his promotion. The woman who took the flexible job so someone could manage school pickups. Individual decisions that aggregated into statistical patterns.

Her historical work revealed something economists had missed: the college graduation gap had flipped. For decades, men earned bachelor's degrees at higher rates. By the 1980s, women had caught up. By 2000, they had surpassed men, and the gap kept widening. Women now earned nearly 60 percent of bachelor's degrees, the majority of master's degrees, and half of doctorates and professional degrees. Yet the wage gap persisted, especially at the top of the earnings distribution.

This was Goldin's central insight: the gender gap in earnings wasn't a relic of past discrimination gradually fading away. It was actively reproduced by the structure of work in high-paying professions. Fixing it wouldn't come from getting more women into college or graduate school — that had already happened. It would require changing how jobs themselves were designed, moving away from structures that rewarded greedy work and toward ones that allowed substitution, flexibility, and more even distribution of hours.

"The gap is not due to the fact that women are not good enough," she said. "The gap is due to the structure of work." She pointed to professions that had already changed as proof that transformation was possible. Pharmacy had restructured in ways that made the gender wage gap nearly disappear. Technology enabled continuity of care without requiring the same provider always. Corporate practices shifted away from client ownership toward team-based work.

The Nobel committee awarded her prize "for having advanced our understanding of women's labor market outcomes." The citation noted that her work had "provided the first comprehensive account" of women's earnings and participation over two centuries. But the recognition was less for discovering that inequality existed than for explaining why it persisted in forms that standard economic models couldn't capture.

Goldin's work didn't offer simple solutions — she was a scholar, not an advocate. But it reframed the problem. The wage gap wasn't primarily about convincing employers to value women equally; many already did. It was about confronting the temporal demands of certain careers and asking whether the premium for greedy work was necessary or just habitual. Whether flexibility had to mean penalty. Whether jobs could be redesigned to allow different ways of contributing without sacrificing competence or commitment.

She had spent her career in archives and datasets, constructing the historical record that made sense of the present. The result was a body of work that illuminated not just women's economic history, but the structure of work itself — how it had evolved, what it rewarded, and what it made almost impossible to combine.
