# 2023 — mRNA Technology (Katalin Karikó, Drew Weissman)

The body treats foreign RNA as an invader. Inject synthetic messenger RNA into a cell and the immune system attacks — inflammation, shutdown, no therapeutic effect. For two decades, this was the insurmountable problem. Katalin Karikó kept working on it anyway, convinced that if mRNA could be delivered safely, it could teach cells to make any protein: vaccines, cancer treatments, therapies for genetic diseases. In 2023, she and Drew Weissman won the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for discovering how to make that possible.

They found the solution in 2005: modify a single building block of the RNA molecule. Replace uridine with pseudouridine — a naturally occurring variant — and the immune system no longer recognizes the RNA as foreign. The modified mRNA slips into cells, delivers its instructions, and disappears without triggering inflammation. It was an elegant insight born from basic research into how cells distinguish self from non-self. Neither Karikó nor Weissman could have known it would become the foundation for vaccines that would reach billions of people.

Karikó arrived at the University of Pennsylvania in 1989 as a research assistant professor, having left Hungary with her husband, their two-year-old daughter, and nine hundred British pounds sewn into the child's teddy bear. She had been working on mRNA since her doctoral studies in Szeged — trying to use synthetic RNA to direct protein production in cells. It was unfashionable science. DNA was the glamorous molecule; mRNA was unstable, difficult to work with, and triggered immune reactions. Grant applications were rejected. In 1995, Penn demoted her from the tenure track. She stayed anyway, moving from lab to lab, taking whatever position would let her continue the work.

Drew Weissman joined Penn's immunology faculty in 1997. He had trained with Anthony Fauci at the NIH, working on HIV vaccines. He met Karikó at a photocopier — the origin story they would recount in dozens of interviews two decades later. They started talking about dendritic cells, about RNA, about the immune response. Weissman had the immunology expertise; Karikó had been making synthetic RNA for years. They began collaborating.

The breakthrough came from systematic testing. They synthesized RNA with different modified nucleosides — natural variants of the standard building blocks — and measured how dendritic cells responded. Pseudouridine stood out. RNA containing it produced almost no inflammatory response. Cells still translated it into protein, but the immune system no longer attacked. The paper appeared in *Immunity* in 2005. It was elegant work, clearly significant for anyone working on RNA therapeutics. It did not cause a sensation.

For years, the discovery remained primarily academic. Karikó and Weissman published follow-up papers showing that the modification also made mRNA more stable and increased protein production. A few small companies licensed the technology. Penn filed patents but showed limited interest in developing it. Karikó left Penn in 2013 to become a vice president at BioNTech, a German startup working on mRNA cancer vaccines. Weissman remained at Penn, continuing to refine the approach.

When SARS-CoV-2 emerged in late 2019, two companies had the tools to respond quickly: BioNTech, where Karikó was senior vice president, and Moderna, which had licensed technology built on the Karikó-Weissman work. Both designed vaccine candidates within days of the viral sequence being published. The vaccines used modified mRNA encoding the coronavirus spike protein, packaged in lipid nanoparticles that protected the fragile molecules and delivered them into cells. Clinical trials moved at unprecedented speed. By December 2020, both vaccines were authorized for emergency use.

The scale of what happened next has few parallels in medical history. Within two years, more than five billion people received mRNA COVID vaccines. The technology that had struggled for funding became one of the most consequential achievements in modern medicine. It worked not because of any unique property of the coronavirus but because the basic science was sound: modified mRNA could safely deliver genetic instructions into human cells.

The immediate impact was measured in prevented hospitalizations and deaths during a pandemic. The longer-term implications extend further. Clinical trials are now underway for mRNA vaccines against influenza, HIV, malaria, and multiple cancers. The technology can be rapidly adapted to new targets — all you need is the genetic sequence of the protein you want cells to produce. Therapeutic applications beyond vaccines are being explored: treating genetic diseases by supplying missing proteins, directing immune responses against tumors, regenerating damaged tissue.

Karikó's path to Stockholm was not the traditional academic trajectory. She never led her own laboratory, never achieved tenure, spent years working in positions well below her contributions. "I was not the first author, I was not the last author, I was always in the middle," she said in one interview after the Nobel announcement. "I thought that was okay." The comment captures something of her temperament — persistence without bitterness, focus on the work rather than the recognition.

The Nobel committee's citation praised "their discoveries concerning nucleoside base modifications that enabled the development of effective mRNA vaccines against COVID-19." The "enabled" is precise. Karikó and Weissman did not develop the vaccines themselves, did not invent lipid nanoparticle delivery, did not run the clinical trials. They solved the fundamental problem that had kept mRNA therapeutics from working: how to deliver synthetic RNA without triggering an immune attack. Everything that followed built on that foundation.

The story became a media narrative during the pandemic: the underestimated scientist whose work saved millions of lives. It is not false, but it is incomplete. Karikó was underestimated, certainly — demoted, denied grants, working for years in positions beneath her abilities. But she was also supported: by collaborators like Weissman, by the infrastructure of a major research university, by small companies willing to license early-stage technology, by funding mechanisms that, however imperfect, sustained decades of basic research. The breakthrough required both the insight and the persistence, and the environment that made both possible.

When asked what kept her going through the years of rejection and demotion, Karikó's answer was consistent: she believed mRNA could work, and she wanted to see it work. Not as vindication, not for recognition — because the science was beautiful and the applications were worth pursuing. The Nobel Prize arrived thirty-eight years after she began working on mRNA, eighteen years after the key discovery, in the aftermath of a pandemic that made the significance impossible to ignore. The work had been extraordinary all along. It simply took the world that long to notice.
