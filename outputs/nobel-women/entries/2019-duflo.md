# 2019 — The Economics of Poverty (Esther Duflo)

The randomized controlled trial had revolutionized medicine: divide subjects randomly into treatment and control groups, measure the difference, know what actually works. Esther Duflo brought that revolution to development economics. Not theories about poverty — experiments. Not assumptions about what helps the poor — evidence. The work that earned her the 2019 Nobel in Economics, shared with Abhijit Banerjee and Michael Kramer, transformed how we understand and address global poverty by insisting on a simple, radical question: does it actually work?

At forty-six, she became the youngest person ever awarded the economics prize, and only the second woman. The achievement was methodological as much as substantive — she didn't just study poverty differently; she changed how poverty could be studied.

The traditional approach to development economics had operated at high altitude: macroeconomic policies, foreign aid packages, grand theories about what poor countries needed. Duflo descended to ground level. She asked specific, answerable questions. Do bed nets reduce malaria? Does deworming improve school attendance? Do microloans help the poor start businesses? Can conditional cash transfers increase vaccination rates?

Each question became an experiment. In rural Kenya, in urban India, across dozens of countries, she and her collaborators worked with local organizations to randomize interventions — some villages got the program, others didn't. Then they measured. Rigorously. The results often surprised.

Take the bed net puzzle. Public health experts knew insecticide-treated nets prevented malaria. But should governments distribute them free or charge a small fee? The fee advocates argued that people value what they pay for — free nets would go unused, wasted. The experiment showed otherwise. Free distribution dramatically increased coverage. People who received free nets used them just as much as those who paid. The fee wasn't ensuring commitment; it was creating a barrier.

Or education. The instinct was to focus on the big structural problems — teacher training, curriculum reform, new schools. Duflo's experiments found that sometimes smaller, stranger interventions worked better. In Kenya, treating intestinal worms improved school attendance more cost-effectively than hiring more teachers. The worms made children sick and tired; treating them was cheap. The effect was larger than many expensive educational reforms.

The work required unusual range. Duflo combined economic theory with field pragmatism. She understood statistical methodology and institutional politics. She could design an experiment that satisfied academic rigor while remaining implementable in villages without electricity. She worked with local partners who understood the context — what was feasible, what was culturally appropriate, what might actually help.

The approach had critics. Some argued that randomized trials were expensive, slow, and answered only narrow questions. How do you randomize a national economic policy? Others worried about ethics — was it right to withhold potentially helpful programs from control groups? Duflo responded that the alternative was worse: implementing programs based on ideology or intuition, with no idea whether they helped or harmed. "We need to be modest about what we know," she said. "The randomized trial is a way of being honest about our ignorance."

She'd arrived at MIT for graduate study in 1994, already skeptical of grand theories. She'd grown up in Paris, studied history and economics at École Normale Supérieure, worked briefly in Russia during its chaotic transition to capitalism. She'd seen firsthand how economic policies could fail spectacularly, how the distance between theory and reality could swallow good intentions.

At MIT, she found mentors who valued empiricism over elegance. She began running experiments in India with Banerjee, whom she would later marry. They founded the Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab (J-PAL) in 2003, which became the institutional home for randomized evaluations in development economics. By 2019, J-PAL had completed hundreds of studies across dozens of countries.

The cumulative picture that emerged from this work was nuanced. Poverty wasn't one problem but many, each requiring its own solution. Some interventions worked; many didn't. Context mattered enormously — what helped in Kenya might fail in India. But patterns appeared. Small, targeted interventions often outperformed large, systemic ones. Poor people made rational decisions given their circumstances; they weren't irrational or improvident. Access to basic health care and education could transform outcomes. Cash transfers, properly designed, helped. Microloans, the darling of development circles, rarely helped people escape poverty — they were useful financial tools, nothing more.

The method spread beyond economics. Governments began using randomized trials to test policies. NGOs demanded evidence before scaling programs. The World Bank shifted resources toward interventions with proven impact. Development economics became a science in ways it hadn't been before.

Her Nobel lecture focused on what remained unknown. "We know how to eliminate malaria," she said. "We don't know why Nigeria hasn't done it." The technical problems had solutions; the political and institutional barriers remained. The experiments answered what works; they couldn't force implementation.

The work continued expanding. She studied tax collection, corruption, political participation, gender discrimination. Always the same method: randomize, measure, learn. Always the same insistence: test your assumptions, count what matters, be honest about uncertainty.

The deeper contribution was epistemological. Duflo showed that economics could be modest and rigorous simultaneously, that admitting ignorance about grand theories didn't mean abandoning ambition, that the slow accumulation of reliable knowledge could matter more than elegant systems. The revolution wasn't in the findings — those would keep changing as more experiments ran. It was in the method: a way of knowing that traded theoretical elegance for empirical honesty.

"Poverty is not one thing," she insisted. "It is a million small things." The randomized trial couldn't solve poverty. But it could answer the million small questions, one experiment at a time. For that patient, relentless empiricism — for insisting that development economics ground itself in what actually works — Esther Duflo earned recognition as one of the field's transformative figures.
