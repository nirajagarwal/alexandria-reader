# 2011 — Women's Rights and Peace (Leymah Gbowee, Ellen Johnson Sirleaf, Tawakkol Karman)

Three women on three continents received the call from Oslo in October 2011. Ellen Johnson Sirleaf, president of Liberia, had spent the morning governing a fragile peace. Leymah Gbowee, the Liberian peace activist who had helped end a civil war, was in New York. Tawakkol Karman, journalist and protest leader, was in Yemen — where the Arab Spring had become a bloody stalemate and she had been jailed three times that year already. The Nobel Committee's citation bound them together: "for their non-violent struggle for the safety of women and for women's rights to full participation in peace-building work."

It was the first time three women had shared the Peace Prize. It was also a prize given in the middle of the story, not at its end.

## The Mass Action

Leymah Gbowee organized the movement that the world would remember as the "sex strike," though that was never quite the point. By 2002, Liberia had been at war for more than a decade. Charles Taylor's government and rebel forces fought over territory while civilians — overwhelmingly women and children — bore the consequences. Rape was systematic. Displacement constant. The international community had mostly looked away.

Gbowee, a social worker trained in trauma counseling, had been working with women survivors in the displacement camps outside Monrovia. She had her own history with the war: four children, an abusive relationship, the collapse of any normal life. What she saw in the camps was pattern, not random violence. War was a system, and women were its fuel and its refuse.

She began organizing at the Lutheran church she attended. Christian women, then Muslim women. The dividing lines that the war had weaponized became irrelevant. They wore white — the color of peace — and gathered at the fish market on a highway where Taylor's motorcade passed daily. Hundreds of women sitting in the sun, praying and singing. Then thousands.

The sex strike came later, mostly as theater — a way to get the men's attention, which it did. But the real pressure was the mass action itself: a continuous, visible, female presence that made invisibility impossible. When peace talks finally began in Accra, Ghana, in 2003, the women followed. They surrounded the building. When negotiations stalled — warlords posturing, nothing agreed — the women blocked the doors. No one was leaving until there was a peace agreement.

"We have shed too many tears," Gbowee told the negotiators through the door. "It is time for you to act."

They did. Taylor went into exile. The war ended. Gbowee had already moved on to the next thing: trauma healing, leadership training, the hard work of what comes after violence stops.

## The President

Ellen Johnson Sirleaf was seventy-seven when she won the Nobel, and had already lived several lifetimes. Educated at Harvard's Kennedy School, she had worked at the World Bank and Citibank, held ministerial positions in Liberia, run for president once before, and done time in prison — twice — for criticizing the dictators who preceded Taylor. She spent most of the war years in exile, returning in 1997 to run for president and lose to Taylor himself.

When she ran again in 2005, it was into ruins. The economy had collapsed. Infrastructure was gone. International institutions had withdrawn. Unemployment hovered near 80 percent. Gbowee and the Women of Liberia Mass Action for Peace campaign backed her, delivering crucial votes. Sirleaf won.

She inherited a country that barely existed as a state. No electricity grid. No running water in most of the capital. Government ministries operating out of buildings with no roofs. Her first actions were pragmatic: securing debt relief, bringing back international partners, reopening schools. Technocratic work, unglamorous. The kind of thing that doesn't make headlines unless it fails.

By 2011, Liberia was still poor, still fragile, but functioning. Schools open. Some infrastructure returning. Foreign investment trickling back. Sirleaf had been nicknamed "Iron Lady" — not for warmth but for the capacity to make unpopular decisions and survive them. The Nobel came weeks before her re-election campaign. Critics called it interference. She won anyway.

The prize recognized her presidency, but also the decades before: the exile, the imprisonment, the refusal to accept that military strongmen and warlords were Liberia's only possible future.

## The Tent

Tawakkol Karman was thirty-two, the youngest laureate in Peace Prize history at that point. She was a journalist, mother of three, and the face of Yemen's protest movement — which, in October 2011, was still underway and increasingly dangerous.

She had started organizing years earlier, founding Women Journalists Without Chains in 2005 to document human rights abuses under President Ali Abdullah Saleh's regime. Regular protests at Sana'a University. Regular arrests. When the Arab Spring reached Yemen in early 2011, Karman was already a known figure, already under surveillance, already uninterested in backing down.

She set up a protest tent outside Sana'a University in January. The tent became the center: organizing point, media hub, the physical space where the opposition gathered daily. Karman was there constantly, leading chants, giving interviews, sleeping in the tent when the crowds grew too large to disperse. The government called her a traitor. Conservative clerics said worse — a woman in public, unmarried men around, no male guardian, speaking to foreign journalists without permission.

She kept the tent open.

The Nobel announcement came while Yemen's uprising was turning into civil war. Saleh, wounded in a bombing, had gone to Saudi Arabia for treatment but refused to resign. Protesters were being shot in the streets. Karman used the Nobel platform to demand international pressure. "This is a prize for Yemen," she said, "for the Yemeni people, for their revolution, for their aspirations."

Saleh would resign two months later — a partial victory that settled nothing. The civil war that followed would make 2011 look like prologue.

## The Prize and Its Timing

Nobel Peace Prizes often honor work completed. This one honored work in progress. Sirleaf's presidency was midstream. Karman's revolution unfinished. Even Gbowee's Liberia — a decade past the war's end — remained deeply unstable. The Committee's gamble was that recognition might strengthen fragile gains.

For Karman, the prize offered some protection — though she would be forced into exile four years later when Yemen collapsed entirely. For Sirleaf, it provided international validation that helped her survive corruption scandals and the Ebola crisis that would hit Liberia in 2014. For Gbowee, it expanded her platform for trauma-healing work across West Africa.

The prize was also explicitly feminist: not just about peace but about women's role in making it. The citation emphasized "women's rights to full participation" in peace processes — a pointed rebuke to the pattern of excluding women from negotiations even when women had driven the movements that made negotiations possible.

Critics noted that Sirleaf's presidency had its own complications: corruption allegations, nepotism charges, an economy still dependent on foreign aid. Karman's coalition included Islamist groups whose vision of women's rights did not align with Western feminism. Gbowee's sex strike had been more media narrative than actual tactic.

The Committee was awarding not perfection but effort. Three women who had refused the standard scripts: the strongman, the militia, the foreign intervention. Who had organized, governed, and protested instead. Who had stayed, when exile was easier.

"We must continue to unite in sisterhood," Gbowee said in her Nobel lecture, "to turn our tears into triumph, our despair into determination, our fear into fortitude."

The tears had never been the point. The organizing was.
