# 1997 — The Landmine Ban (Jody Williams)

By October 1997, when Jody Williams received the Nobel Peace Prize, over 120 nations had signed the treaty banning anti-personnel landmines. The International Campaign to Ban Landmines, which she coordinated, had taken six years to build a global movement that circumvented the usual channels of international diplomacy. The treaty itself — drafted, negotiated, and signed without the participation of the world's major military powers — represented something new: proof that non-governmental organizations could reshape international law when governments would not.

Williams came to the work through Vermont's immigrant rights movement in the 1980s. Teaching English to refugees from Central America, she heard stories of what American-funded wars looked like on the ground. She moved to organizing, then to working with relief organizations in El Salvador and Nicaragua. By 1991, when two organizations — Vietnam Veterans of America Foundation and Medico International — decided someone needed to coordinate a campaign against landmines, Williams had a decade of experience building coalitions and a reputation for getting things done.

The problem was vast and mostly ignored. An estimated 110 million landmines lay buried in 64 countries. They killed or maimed someone every 20 minutes — long after wars ended, long after armies left. Most victims were civilians: farmers, children, refugees returning home. The weapons were cheap to make and nearly impossible to remove. Major powers manufactured them; developing nations bought them; humanitarian groups treated the wounded and tried to clear minefields one painstaking device at a time.

Williams started with a list and a fax machine. The International Campaign to Ban Landmines began as six organizations in October 1992. She ran it from a spare bedroom in her Vermont house, building a network of NGOs, coordinating their efforts, raising funds, and keeping everyone focused on a single goal: not regulation, not reduction, but an outright ban. No exceptions for so-called "smart" mines. No delay. By 1997, the campaign included over 1,000 organizations in 60 countries.

She understood that the moral case alone would not be enough. The campaign documented the human cost — the children who lost limbs, the farmers who lost livelihoods, the aid workers killed trying to clear fields. But it also built the practical argument: landmines were militarily ineffective weapons that cost more to remove than to deploy, killed indiscriminately, and violated the laws of war. The campaign recruited military officers, mine-clearance experts, and medical professionals. It enlisted celebrities and recruited survivors who could speak from experience.

The breakthrough came from finding governments willing to lead where major powers would not. Canada's foreign minister Lloyd Axworthy announced in October 1996 that his government would host negotiations for a treaty, with or without the U.S., Russia, and China. Williams and the campaign had convinced enough mid-sized and smaller nations that a ban was both morally necessary and practically achievable. The "Ottawa Process" moved faster than traditional arms control diplomacy — 14 months from first meeting to signed treaty.

The United States lobbied hard against it. The Clinton administration wanted exceptions for Korea, for "smart mines," for delayed implementation. Williams refused to budge. "The whole point," she said, "was to make it unacceptable to use these weapons, period." The treaty opened for signature in December 1997 with 122 nations signing immediately. The U.S., Russia, China, India, and Pakistan stayed out. The treaty entered into force anyway.

She was 47 years old, still working from Vermont, when the Nobel committee announced her prize, shared with the ICBL itself. The speed of the recognition was unusual — the treaty had been signed weeks earlier, not yet ratified. But the committee saw what had happened: a new model for international action. Williams had proven that a network of activists, properly organized and strategically focused, could outmaneuver governments and weapons manufacturers.

The work did not end with the treaty. Twenty-five years later, more than 55 million stockpiled landmines had been destroyed. Mine casualties dropped from over 20,000 per year in the 1990s to under 5,000 by 2020. Clearance continues in dozens of countries. Over 160 nations have joined the treaty. The major powers still have not — they maintain stockpiles and reserve the right to use landmines in future conflicts. But the norm has shifted. The weapon Williams called "a cheap, easy, and effective way to terrorize civilians" is now recognized as unacceptable by most of the world.

Williams herself remained blunt about what the campaign achieved and what it did not. "We got the treaty," she said years later. "We didn't get all the governments. We didn't end the suffering. But we made it harder to ignore and harder to justify." She stayed active in disarmament work, co-founded the Nobel Women's Initiative, and continued to push for bans on other weapons. Asked once how she managed to coordinate 1,000 organizations across 60 countries, she said: "You call people back. You answer the faxes. You show up. And you never, ever accept that the way things are is the way they have to be."
