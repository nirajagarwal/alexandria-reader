# 2014 — The Brain's GPS (May-Britt Moser)

A rat runs through a box, foraging. Electrodes thinner than hair record from single neurons in its brain. On the monitor, a pattern emerges: as the rat crosses certain points in space, specific cells fire. Not randomly — geometrically. The cell fires when the rat reaches the corners of invisible hexagons tiling the floor like bathroom tiles. Move the rat to a different room, and the same cells fire in the same hexagonal pattern, scaled and rotated to fit the new space. The brain contains a coordinate system. May-Britt Moser had found the grid cells.

This was 2005, at the Kavli Institute for Systems Neuroscience in Trondheim, Norway. Moser and her then-husband Edvard Moser had been chasing the question of spatial navigation for over a decade. How does the brain know where it is? How do we construct maps of our surroundings and navigate through them? Thirty years earlier, John O'Keeffe had discovered "place cells" — neurons that fire when an animal occupies specific locations. But place cells alone couldn't explain the system. They showed the brain represented space, but not how it computed spatial relationships. The Mosers went looking for the mechanism.

They found it in the entorhinal cortex, a structure adjacent to the hippocampus. The grid cells there don't mark individual places — they mark the metric structure of space itself. Think of longitude and latitude lines on a globe, except in the brain they're hexagonal. Each grid cell fires at multiple locations, but those locations are perfectly spaced, forming a repeating triangular pattern. Different cells have different grid spacings, from fine to coarse, creating a neural coordinate system that works at multiple scales. Together, these grids allow the brain to calculate position, distance, and direction with remarkable precision.

The elegance is in the mathematics. Hexagonal grids are the most efficient way to tile two-dimensional space — they provide maximum coverage with minimum overlap. The brain arrived at the same solution that honeybees use for their combs. But the brain's grids do something more: they're layered. Small grids capture fine details; large grids capture overall structure. The combination gives you both local precision and global coherence. You can navigate a building (large grid) and find a specific book on a specific shelf (small grid) using the same neural machinery.

May-Britt Moser came to neuroscience through psychology. Born in 1963 in Fosnes, Norway, she grew up in a small farming community. At the University of Oslo, she studied psychology, then shifted toward the biological mechanisms of behavior. She met Edvard Moser as an undergraduate; they married and built a scientific partnership that would last decades. They trained together, first in Oslo, then with O'Keeffe at University College London, learning the techniques of recording from single neurons in behaving animals. In 1996, they moved to Trondheim to establish their own laboratory.

The work required technical mastery and theoretical insight in equal measure. Recording from individual neurons as an animal moves freely through space is surgically and technically demanding. The rats must be trained, the electrodes positioned with sub-millimeter precision, the signals isolated from noise. Then comes the analysis: extracting patterns from thousands of neural firing events, determining whether those patterns represent genuine computational structure or coincidence. The Mosers developed new methods for both recording and analysis, pushing the technology forward to match their questions.

The discovery of grid cells transformed the field. It suggested that the brain builds internal models of external space through a universal coordinate system, independent of landmarks or specific locations. This has implications far beyond navigation. The entorhinal cortex connects to the hippocampus, which is central to memory. Spatial representation and memory formation appear to share neural machinery. Damage to the entorhinal cortex is an early feature of Alzheimer's disease — patients lose the ability to navigate familiar places before they lose other cognitive functions. Understanding grid cells might illuminate how memory itself is structured.

Since 2005, the Mosers' laboratory has continued to dissect the spatial navigation system. They've found "head direction cells" that act like a compass, "border cells" that map boundaries, "speed cells" that track movement velocity. Each discovery reveals another component of the brain's positioning system. The picture that emerges is of a sophisticated, multi-layered computational architecture — not a simple map, but a dynamic system that updates in real time as you move through the world.

May-Britt Moser is known for a methodical, rigorous approach to science. She designs experiments to eliminate alternative explanations, runs extensive controls, questions results until they hold up to scrutiny. She's built a large, collaborative laboratory where students and postdocs from around the world work on different aspects of the spatial navigation system. The scale of the operation reflects the scale of the question: to understand how the brain represents space requires understanding not just individual cells but the networks they form and how those networks interact with behavior.

The 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was shared among May-Britt Moser, Edvard Moser, and John O'Keeffe, recognizing the combined work that revealed the brain's positioning system. By then, the Mosers had divorced but continued to run adjacent laboratories in Trondheim, collaborating scientifically. They remain close colleagues, sharing resources and ideas while pursuing somewhat different research directions.

"Without the grid cells, we would have no sense of place," May-Britt Moser said in her Nobel lecture. "The grid cells provide the brain with a universal metric for space — a coordinate system that allows us to find our way, to remember where we have been, and to plan where we will go." This is the work: discovering that the brain contains geometry. Not metaphorically — literally. The firing patterns of cells in the entorhinal cortex implement coordinate transformations. We navigate the world by running calculations on an internal grid.

The implications extend to artificial intelligence. Understanding how biological brains solve spatial navigation informs the design of autonomous vehicles, robots, and AI systems that must move through and represent three-dimensional environments. The principles — hierarchical grids, place representation, boundary detection — turn out to be useful beyond biology.

May-Britt Moser continues to lead the Kavli Institute, now focused on questions at the intersection of space, memory, and consciousness. The grid cell discovery opened doors; what lies beyond them is still being explored. How do grid cells develop? How do they adapt to new environments? How does spatial representation interact with other cognitive functions? Each answer generates new questions. That's the nature of fundamental discovery — it doesn't close a field, it reveals how much territory remains.
