# 1946 — International Peace (Emily Greene Balch)

In 1946, the Nobel Committee awarded its first Peace Prize in six years. The war had ended; millions were dead; Europe was in ruins. The prize went to Emily Greene Balch, seventy-nine years old, half-blind, with little public profile outside pacifist circles. She had spent thirty years arguing that war could be prevented through patient, unglamorous institution-building — the kind of work that rarely makes headlines and almost never seems to succeed. Then came two world wars. By 1946, she had every reason to feel vindicated in her defeat. Instead, the committee recognized her as prophetic.

Balch had been an economist before she became a peace activist, though the distinction mattered less in her mind than it did to her institutions. She'd studied at Bryn Mawr and in Paris and Berlin, then joined the faculty at Wellesley College in 1896, teaching political economy with particular attention to immigration, labor conditions, and social policy. She was a Progressive in the American mold — empirical, reformist, convinced that injustice yielded to careful study and institutional pressure. Her 1910 book *Our Slavic Fellow Citizens* was groundbreaking sociology, based on months of fieldwork among Slavic immigrants in American industrial cities. She wanted to understand them, not as a social problem, but as people navigating impossible circumstances. The book was meticulous, humane, and free of the nativism poisoning much contemporary immigration discourse.

The First World War unraveled that world. In 1915, as Europe descended into mechanized slaughter, Balch joined Jane Addams and other women reformers at The Hague for the International Congress of Women. The gathering was audacious in its timing and modest in its demands: that neutral nations mediate; that women have a voice in peace negotiations; that international institutions be built to prevent future wars. Balch was among the delegates who traveled afterward to warring and neutral capitals, urging leaders to consider terms for peace. They were received politely and ignored completely. Still, the congress planted seeds — it would become the Women's International League for Peace and Freedom, with Balch as one of its architects.

What cost her career was consistency. When the United States entered the war in 1917, Balch opposed it. She didn't just dissent quietly; she spoke, wrote, organized. Wellesley College, which had tolerated her radical economics and her activism on labor and immigration, found pacifism intolerable. In 1918, after twenty-two years on the faculty, her appointment was not renewed. She was fifty-one, with no pension, no fallback. The loss was institutional and personal — she'd loved teaching, loved her students. But she didn't recant, and she didn't stop working.

The two decades that followed were spent in the unglamorous machinery of international cooperation. Balch moved to Geneva to work with the Women's International League for Peace and Freedom, becoming its international secretary-treasurer. She wrote reports, organized conferences, lobbied diplomats. She was particularly focused on the League of Nations — not as an idealist who believed it would end all conflict, but as a pragmatist who thought functioning international institutions might prevent the next catastrophic war. She studied disarmament proposals, drug trafficking, the international opium trade, the status of Haiti under U.S. occupation. She was detail-oriented, patient, willing to work in committees and subcommittees where nothing glamorous ever happened.

Her vision was practical internationalism: not world government, but structures that made cooperation possible and communication routine. She believed nations would continue to have interests, conflicts, rivalries — but that those could be managed through law, mediation, transparent process. She wasn't naïve about power. In 1926, she published *Occupied Haiti*, an incisive critique of American imperialism that combined economic analysis with moral argument. The U.S. had occupied Haiti since 1915, ostensibly to stabilize it, actually to control it. Balch documented how the occupation served American financial interests while impoverishing Haitians and destroying their sovereignty. The book was uncomfortable reading for her compatriots; it came from someone who knew how empires justified themselves.

By the 1930s, with fascism rising and the League of Nations collapsing, Balch's work looked increasingly futile. She kept at it anyway. When the Second World War came, she was in her seventies, watching the catastrophe she'd spent her life trying to prevent. Still, she worked. She focused on what could be salvaged: refugee relief, post-war planning, the possibility of building something better from the ruins.

The Nobel Committee's citation in 1946 recognized her as "the spiritual leader" of the international women's peace movement — phrasing that might have made her wince. Balch was more comfortable with budgets than banners. But the award acknowledged something genuine: she had helped build institutions that survived the wars and would shape the post-war order. The United Nations, for all its flaws, owed something to the vision she and others had articulated decades earlier — that international conflict required international structures, that peace was something you built, not something that simply arrived when the fighting stopped.

In her Nobel lecture, delivered by written statement because she was too frail to travel, Balch was unsentimental. She acknowledged the failures — two world wars, countless dead, the persistence of nationalism and militarism. But she also argued for persistence: "We are not asked to subscribe to any utopia or to believe in a perfect world just around the corner. We are asked to be patient with necessarily slow and groping advance on the road forward, and to be ready for each step ahead as it becomes practicable." She had no illusions about human nature or political possibility. She simply believed that the alternative — doing nothing, accepting war as inevitable — was unacceptable.

She lived eight more years, dying in 1961 at ninety-four. By then, the Cold War had replaced the hot one, nuclear weapons loomed over everything, and the United Nations seemed as fragile as the League had been. The peace she'd worked for had not arrived. But the institutions she helped build — flawed, compromised, perpetually inadequate — still existed. In a century that murdered millions in the name of competing ideologies, that was something. Balch had never promised utopia. She'd promised that it was possible to try.
