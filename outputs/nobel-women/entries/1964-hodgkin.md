# 1964 — The Structure of Biomolecules (Dorothy Crowfoot Hodgkin)

The insulin molecule contains 777 atoms arranged in a precise three-dimensional architecture. For thirty-five years, Dorothy Crowfoot Hodgkin worked to see it — not with her eyes, but through the diffraction patterns that crystals made when X-rays passed through them. When she finally solved the structure in 1969, five years after receiving the Nobel Prize, she had spent most of her adult life learning to read the language crystals speak.

The 1964 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized work already decades deep: the structures of penicillin, vitamin B12, and other biochemically vital molecules. But Hodgkin's achievement was larger than any single structure. She developed the methods that made it possible to see the molecular architecture of life itself.

X-ray crystallography works through inference. You cannot see atoms directly. You grow a crystal of the substance you want to study, shine X-rays through it, and capture the pattern of scattered rays on photographic film. The pattern — a constellation of spots, each with a specific intensity — contains encoded information about where every atom sits in three-dimensional space. The challenge is working backward from the diffraction pattern to the structure that produced it.

When Hodgkin began this work in the late 1920s, the technique was new and limited. Structures with a few dozen atoms could be solved. Complex organic molecules — the machinery of living cells — seemed beyond reach. The mathematics was brutal. Each reflection in the diffraction pattern represents a wave with both an amplitude (measurable from the spot's intensity) and a phase (not directly measurable). Without the phases, you cannot reconstruct the structure. With hundreds or thousands of reflections, each missing its phase, the problem becomes a labyrinth.

Hodgkin learned to navigate it. She began as a student at Oxford and Cambridge, one of the few women in crystallography, working with J.D. Bernal — a brilliant, erratic figure who recognized her talent immediately. In 1934, Bernal obtained crystals of pepsin, a digestive enzyme. Hodgkin took the first X-ray photographs. The patterns were beautiful and incomprehensible — thousands of reflections, far more complex than anything yet solved. They showed that protein crystals could diffract X-rays in an ordered way, proving that proteins had definite structures. But what those structures were remained unknown.

She returned to Oxford, set up her own lab, and began working on smaller molecules she might actually solve. Penicillin came to her during World War II. The antibiotic had been discovered by Alexander Fleming, but its chemical structure was unknown — and contested. Chemists disagreed about which atoms connected where. Knowing the structure would make synthetic production possible, crucial for the war effort. Hodgkin grew crystals and collected data.

The penicillin structure resisted solution for years. The phase problem loomed. Then Hodgkin began using a technique called isomorphous replacement: she would soak crystals in solutions containing heavy atoms like gold or mercury. The heavy atoms would lodge in specific spots in the crystal without changing the overall structure. By comparing diffraction patterns with and without the heavy atoms, she could extract phase information. It was painstaking — each heavy atom derivative required months of work. But it gave her a way through.

In 1945, she solved penicillin's structure. The molecule contained a beta-lactam ring — a square of atoms that most chemists had considered too unstable to exist. The discovery resolved the chemical debates and provided the foundation for synthetic production. It also demonstrated that X-ray crystallography could tackle molecules of significant complexity.

Vitamin B12 was larger: 181 atoms, including a single cobalt at its center. The molecule was essential for making red blood cells; deficiency caused pernicious anemia. Hodgkin began working on it in 1948. The structure took eight years. She used isomorphous replacement, incorporated early computers to handle the vast calculations, and developed new mathematical approaches to refine atomic positions. When she published the structure in 1956, it was the most complex molecule ever solved by X-ray crystallography. The Nobel committee cited it specifically, calling it "a magnificent scientific achievement."

By then, she had already turned to insulin — larger still, medically crucial, and maddeningly difficult to crystallize in forms that diffracted well. She would spend decades on it, working through interrupted health (rheumatoid arthritis that twisted her hands and required canes, then a wheelchair) and institutional limitations (Oxford gave her a laboratory but no permanent position for years; she worked as a university demonstrator, poorly paid, until finally made a professor in 1960).

She never complained about any of it. Colleagues remember her extraordinary gentleness, her patience with students, her generosity with credit. When her husband, Thomas Hodgkin, took a position in Ghana and later in Zambia, she stayed in Oxford near her lab, visiting him during academic breaks. They made it work. She raised three children, often bringing them to the laboratory. She continued working through pain that made holding a pen difficult.

What drove her was simpler than ambition: she wanted to see the structures. "I was captured for life by chemistry and by crystals," she said. The diffraction patterns were puzzles, beautiful in themselves, but what lay behind them was the molecular architecture of life — the precise arrangement of atoms that made penicillin kill bacteria, that made B12 essential for blood, that made insulin regulate sugar in the blood.

The insulin structure, when she finally solved it in 1969, revealed a molecule that could change shape — an active machine, not a static sculpture. It helped explain how the hormone worked and opened pathways for designing synthetic versions to treat diabetes. But for Hodgkin, the achievement was also simply seeing what had been invisible: 777 atoms arranged with elegant precision, a structure thirty-five years in the making.

She remained active until near the end of her life, though arthritis eventually confined her to a wheelchair. She mentored generations of crystallographers, many of them women drawn to the field by her example. She worked on science policy, on arms control, on building scientific institutions in developing countries. She corresponded with scientists behind the Iron Curtain during the Cold War, insisting that science transcended politics.

When asked about being one of the few women in her field, she tended to deflect. She had been fortunate, she said — fortunate in her teachers, her collaborators, her husband's support. The barriers she faced were real but not the story she wanted to tell. The structures were the story. The patterns that revealed, with patient work and rigorous mathematics, the hidden architecture of molecules essential to life.

"I seem to have spent much more of my life not solving structures than solving them," she once remarked. Perhaps. But the structures she did solve opened a field. X-ray crystallography would go on to reveal the structure of DNA, of ribosomes, of thousands of proteins and drugs. Much of modern molecular biology rests on the methods Hodgkin developed and the persistence she demonstrated. The invisible made visible, atom by atom.
