# 2025 — Democratic Rights in Venezuela (María Corina Machado)

The disqualification came in June 2023. Venezuela's supreme court, controlled by the Maduro regime, barred María Corina Machado from holding public office for fifteen years. The pretext was administrative irregularities — the kind of charge autocrats level when they cannot tolerate opposition. Machado, an industrial engineer turned political leader, had spent two decades building a movement for democratic restoration in Venezuela. The regime knew what polls showed: she would win.

She ran anyway. Through the summer and fall, she campaigned across a country hollowed by economic collapse and mass exodus. Where roads had deteriorated beyond use, she walked. Where formal rallies were banned, supporters gathered at intersections and market squares. By October, opposition primaries drew over two million voters despite government intimidation. Machado won with 93 percent. The regime responded by arresting campaign staff, raiding headquarters, and threatening anyone who associated with her.

In January 2024, the government replaced her on the ballot with a surrogate candidate — an attempt to satisfy international pressure for elections while ensuring defeat. Machado refused to concede. She crisscrossed Venezuela, speaking to crowds in working-class neighborhoods that had once formed Chavismo's base. The economic devastation — hyperinflation, power outages, hospitals without medicine — had shattered old loyalties. Opposition to Maduro was no longer the province of the comfortable middle class. It ran deep in the barrios, among the families who had fled or watched their children leave.

The July 28 election was not free, but neither was it entirely controlled. Opposition volunteers staffed thousands of polling places, photographing tally sheets before government officials could confiscate them. By midnight, these sheets — uploaded, verified, and published online — showed the opposition winning by a landslide. The regime announced Maduro's victory hours later, offering no evidence. Venezuelans poured into the streets. Security forces responded with bullets. Over two dozen died in the first week; two thousand were arrested.

Machado went underground. For months she moved between safe houses, appearing in videos and organizing resistance while dodging arrest. The government offered rewards for information on her whereabouts. She continued coordinating the opposition's documentation of electoral fraud and mobilizing international pressure. The United States, European Union, and multiple Latin American governments recognized the opposition's victory and demanded transparency. Maduro held power but had lost legitimacy beyond his own apparatus.

Machado's path to opposition leadership began far from politics. Born in Caracas in 1967, she studied industrial engineering and founded Súmate, a voter rights organization, in 2002. Súmate collected signatures for a recall referendum against Hugo Chávez — legal under Venezuela's constitution but treated as subversion by the government. She faced charges of conspiracy and treason. The case dragged through courts for years before quietly collapsing.

Elected to the National Assembly in 2010, she refused the accommodations other opposition figures made with the regime. When invited to the Organization of American States in 2014, she called for international action against Maduro — a stance that prompted the Assembly to strip her seat. The move was procedurally dubious but politically revealing: the regime saw her clarity as dangerous.

What Machado understood, and articulated relentlessly, was that Venezuela's crisis was not policy failure but systemic looting. The Chavista government had converted state institutions into mechanisms for personal enrichment and political control. Oil revenue that should have built prosperity instead funded repression and lined the accounts of officials who stashed billions offshore. Constitutional rights existed on paper while courts rubber-stamped whatever the executive demanded. Elections were theater designed to maintain international recognition while ensuring predetermined outcomes.

This analysis put her at odds with opposition figures who sought dialogue and power-sharing. Machado rejected negotiation with a regime she considered criminal. The stance made her uncompromising, sometimes isolated, but it resonated with Venezuelans exhausted by false promises of democratic transition. By 2023, she led the largest opposition movement in the country's modern history.

The Nobel Committee's decision to award the Peace Prize in October 2025 recognized not victory but endurance. Machado had not restored democracy in Venezuela; Maduro remained in power, backed by military loyalty and support from Cuba, Russia, and China. But she had sustained mass mobilization under conditions that broke other movements. The documentation of electoral fraud — verified by independent observers and recognized internationally — made Maduro's claims of legitimacy untenable outside his circle of autocratic allies.

"Democracy is not negotiable," she said in a message accepting the prize from hiding. "The Venezuelan people have voted, they have sacrificed, and they will not be erased by force. We will continue until our rights are restored."

The award arrived amid questions about whether peaceful resistance could prevail against a regime willing to use violence indefinitely. Venezuela had held elections that it lost and refused to honor. Opposition figures were in prison, in exile, or underground. The economy had contracted by seventy-five percent since 2013; seven million people had fled. Yet crowds still gathered when Machado appeared, and the tally sheets documenting fraud circulated globally, evidence that could not be erased.

The Nobel recognized what authoritarian regimes most fear: an opposition that will not quit, that documents abuses, that organizes without waiting for permission, that claims legitimacy from popular support rather than armed force. Machado had built that opposition in a country where every institution worked to crush it. Whether it would succeed remained uncertain. That it persisted was achievement enough to honor.
