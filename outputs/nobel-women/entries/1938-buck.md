# 1938 — China Through Western Eyes (Pearl S. Buck)

Pearl Buck made rural China legible to the West. Her novels, especially *The Good Earth*, presented Chinese peasant life not as exotic spectacle but as universal human experience — farmers worrying over harvests, families negotiating marriages, individuals caught between tradition and change. When the Swedish Academy awarded her the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1938, they cited "rich and truly epic descriptions of peasant life in China and for her biographical masterpieces." She was the first American woman to win.

The citation barely hints at the complexity. Buck straddled two worlds her entire life and never fully belonged to either. Born in West Virginia in 1892, she moved to China at three months old when her parents — Presbyterian missionaries — returned to their station in Zhenjiang. She grew up speaking Chinese before English, played with Chinese children, learned Confucian classics from a tutor. Her first language of emotion was Mandarin. Yet she was always the foreign child, the missionary daughter, the outsider looking in.

She absorbed China through immersion but understood it through bifocal vision. Her childhood nurse told her folk tales. Her mother taught her English literature. She witnessed the Boxer Rebellion's violence as a child, fled with her family, returned. She attended boarding school in Shanghai, college in Virginia, then returned again to China. In 1917 she married John Lossing Buck, an agricultural economist, and spent years living in small towns in northern China, often the only Westerners for miles. She taught English literature at Chinese universities while her husband studied farming techniques.

The marriage gave her access to rural life most Western missionaries never saw. She lived among farmers, watched crops fail, saw hunger close up. She learned to read the social structures — the weight of filial piety, the precarity of women's lives, the way drought could unravel everything. When she began writing fiction in the late 1920s, she drew from what she'd witnessed and what she'd been taught to see.

*The Good Earth*, published in 1931, became a phenomenon. The novel follows Wang Lung, a poor farmer who rises to wealth through luck and labor, then watches his sons abandon the land. Buck wrote it as realism — this is what life looked like for millions of Chinese peasants — but American readers received it as revelation. Here was China presented without the stereotypes of inscrutable Orientals or opium dens, without missionary condescension or colonial fantasy. Wang Lung worried about the same things as any farmer: weather, children, survival. The book sold millions, won the Pulitzer Prize, was adapted for stage and screen.

China scholars have debated Buck's accuracy ever since. She wrote as an intimate outsider — fluent in the culture but not of it, sympathetic but shaped by missionary worldview. Some Chinese intellectuals dismissed her work as simplistic, catering to Western tastes for pastoral exoticism. Others credited her with humanizing China at a moment when anti-Chinese racism was policy in America. Both things could be true.

What's undeniable is reach. Buck published over a hundred books in her lifetime — novels, biographies, children's stories, political essays. She wrote at ferocious speed, often a book a year, sometimes more. Much of it was potboiler fiction, churned out for income. But her best work — *The Good Earth*, the biographies of her parents (*The Exile* and *Fighting Angel*), novels like *Dragon Seed* about the Japanese invasion — carries documentary weight. She was reporting from inside a culture during its period of revolutionary transformation.

The Nobel came at a strange moment. Buck had left China permanently in 1934 as political tensions escalated, settling in Pennsylvania. She was enormously famous in America but increasingly focused on activism over artistry. She'd divorced her first husband, married her publisher, and begun advocating publicly for civil rights, women's rights, and against the racism she saw in America's treatment of both Chinese and Black citizens. By 1938 she was as known for her politics as her novels.

The Swedish Academy's citation praised her "rich and truly epic descriptions" but also noted her biographical works, which it called "masterpieces" of "rare insight and sympathy." The biographies were more experimental than the novels — her portrait of her mother's loneliness as a missionary wife, her father's rigid certainty, written with cool psychological clarity. They reveal Buck's own ambivalence about the missionary project, the cost it exacted from everyone involved.

The literary establishment's response was mixed. Some critics felt the prize recognized popularity over literary merit, that Buck was a skilled storyteller but not an artist on the level of previous laureates. Others saw the Academy making a statement during rising global tensions — honoring a writer who'd built bridges between cultures at a moment when war was fracturing them. Japan had invaded China the year before; Buck had been vocal in condemning Japanese aggression and advocating for Chinese refugees.

In her Nobel lecture, Buck spoke about the novel as democratic art form, accessible to common readers. She praised Chinese storytellers who performed in marketplaces, connecting with peasant audiences. "The Chinese novel," she said, "was written by the people and for the people. It was created by the story tellers of the street." She positioned herself as continuing that tradition in English, writing not for literary critics but for anyone who would read.

After the prize, Buck's literary reputation gradually declined while her activism intensified. She adopted children — eventually nine, several of mixed race — and founded Welcome House, one of America's first international interracial adoption agencies. She wrote ceaselessly, published constantly, much of it forgettable. She spoke out against McCarthyism, supported the civil rights movement, advocated for people with disabilities (her first daughter had been severely intellectually disabled, which Buck wrote about in *The Child Who Never Grew*).

Critics who dismissed Buck often pointed to her later work's uneven quality, the sense that she'd become more propagandist than artist. But the early novels retain their power. *The Good Earth* still sells. It remains, for better and worse, the book through which many Western readers first encountered China as something other than abstraction. Whether that encounter was accurate matters less than that it was human — that Buck insisted peasants in Anhui province felt hunger, love, ambition, grief the same as farmers in Kansas.

She died in 1973, largely out of literary fashion. Her reputation has been slowly reconsidered — not as a great stylist but as a writer who accomplished something rare: making one culture visible to another at a moment when visibility mattered. She wrote China for Americans during the Depression, during war, during the early Cold War when China became enemy. She kept insisting on the humanity of people her readers were being told to fear or forget.

"I love people," Buck once said. "I am never so happy as when I am among them, unknown and unpretentious." The paradox of her work: she made China known precisely by refusing to pretend she could fully know it, writing always from the position of sympathetic witness rather than authority. The view from the threshold.
