# 2009 — The Ribosome (Ada Yonath)

The ribosome is the cell's protein factory, a molecular machine present in every living thing from bacteria to humans. It reads genetic instructions and assembles amino acids into proteins — the molecules that do nearly everything in biology. For decades, scientists knew ribosomes were crucial. They could isolate them, study their function, watch them work. But no one could see their atomic structure. The machinery was too large, too flexible, too prone to falling apart under the electron beam. Without structure, fundamental questions remained unanswered: How exactly does the ribosome read RNA? How does it ensure accuracy? How do antibiotics jam bacterial ribosomes without killing us?

Ada Yonath answered those questions by doing what others thought impossible: she crystallized the ribosome and solved its structure at atomic resolution.

The technical challenge was staggering. Proteins crystallize when their molecules pack together in regular arrays, forming lattices that diffract X-rays in patterns scientists can decode. Small, stable proteins crystallize readily. The ribosome is neither small nor stable. It's a complex of RNA and proteins, huge by molecular standards, with moving parts that shift during the work of translation. Getting it to form ordered crystals seemed unlikely. Getting those crystals to survive the intense X-ray beam long enough to collect data seemed worse.

Yonath started in the late 1970s at the Weizmann Institute in Israel, working with ribosomes from bacteria that lived in extreme environments — the Dead Sea, hot springs. She reasoned that if the organisms could survive harsh conditions, their ribosomes might be inherently more stable. She tried thousands of crystallization conditions. When she finally got crystals, they were tiny, fragile, and diffracted poorly. The field was skeptical. The ribosome was too big, too complex. She should work on something more tractable.

She kept going. In the 1980s, she developed a technique called cryo-crystallography — flash-freezing crystals in liquid nitrogen before exposing them to X-rays. The cold stabilized the crystals, slowed radiation damage, bought time to collect data. It worked, but barely. The resolution was still too low to see individual atoms.

The breakthrough came incrementally through the 1990s. Better crystals. More stable ribosomal preparations. Improved freezing techniques. Synchrotron X-ray sources that provided more intense, focused beams. Heavy atom markers that helped solve the phase problem — the mathematical challenge of turning diffraction patterns into three-dimensional structures. Yonath's group published the first structures of ribosomal subunits at atomic resolution in 2000 and 2001. The large subunit from *Haloarcula marismortui*. The small subunit from *Thermus thermophilus*. Together, they revealed the ribosome's architecture: the RNA core that catalyzes peptide bond formation, the channels where messenger RNA and transfer RNA enter and exit, the binding sites where antibiotics interfere.

The structure explained mechanisms that had been black boxes. The ribosome's active site — where amino acids link together — is made entirely of RNA, not protein. This supported the hypothesis that life began in an "RNA world," with RNA molecules serving as both genetic material and catalysts before proteins evolved. The structure showed how the ribosome ensures accuracy: transfer RNAs dock in precise positions, with the geometry of the binding site favoring correct matches and penalizing errors. It revealed how bacterial ribosomes differ from human ones in ways that antibiotics exploit — why streptomycin kills bacteria but leaves our cells alone.

Pharmaceutical companies used Yonath's structures to design new antibiotics. Resistance was spreading; the old drugs were failing. Knowing exactly how existing antibiotics bind allowed chemists to modify them or design new molecules that target resistant strains. The ribosome structures became foundational to rational drug design against bacterial infections.

Yonath's path to this work was neither straight nor easy. Born in Jerusalem in 1939 to a poor family, her father died when she was eleven. She worked from childhood — tutoring, cleaning, whatever paid. She studied chemistry at Hebrew University, then earned a doctorate in X-ray crystallography at the Weizmann Institute. After postdoctoral work in the United States, she returned to Israel and set up her ribosome project. Funding was scarce. The goal seemed quixotic. She worked for years producing results that looked like failure: poor crystals, low resolution, skeptical reviewers.

The field was competitive. Thomas Steitz at Yale and Venkatraman Ramakrishnan at the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology in Cambridge were pursuing the same target with different strategies. All three groups raced through the late 1990s, sharing methods at conferences while guarding key details. When structures started appearing in 2000, it was clear all three had succeeded through different approaches. The Nobel Committee recognized this in 2009, awarding the prize jointly to Yonath, Steitz, and Ramakrishnan for their work on ribosome structure.

Yonath was the first Israeli woman to win a Nobel Prize in science and the first woman to win the chemistry prize in 45 years. She continued working after the award — refining structures, studying ribosome assembly, pursuing antibiotics. She kept her lab at Weizmann, mentored students, lectured widely about the ribosome and the state of antibiotic resistance.

The ribosome structures represent something beyond a single biological insight. They're an example of structure determining function at the most fundamental level — seeing the machine clearly enough to understand how it works. Yonath spent decades on a problem others dismissed, developing techniques that are now standard across structural biology. Cryo-crystallography became routine; high-resolution structures of massive complexes became possible.

"At the beginning, I was curious how nature does such a splendid job," she said. "This curiosity became an obsession." The obsession produced one of the defining achievements of molecular biology: the atomic portrait of life's protein-making machinery.
