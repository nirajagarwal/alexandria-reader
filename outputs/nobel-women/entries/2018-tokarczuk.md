# 2018 — Crossing Boundaries (Olga Tokarczuk)

The Nobel Committee called it "a narrative imagination that with encyclopedic passion represents the crossing of boundaries as a form of life." Olga Tokarczuk writes novels that resist being novels in the conventional sense — books that wander across time, perspective, and genre with the confidence of someone who knows exactly what she's building, even if it looks like deliberate disorder. Her work moves between eighteenth-century anatomists and modern-day travelers, between fairy tale and documentary, between the mythic past of her native Poland and the restless present of a continent in motion.

She had been writing for decades before the Nobel, producing more than a dozen books that made her one of Poland's most celebrated — and most controversial — contemporary writers. The Nobel recognized not a single masterpiece but a career-long project: the creation of a literary form capable of holding the complexity of how people actually live, move, and make meaning across borders of nation, time, and belief.

## The Form She Built

Tokarczuk's breakthrough came with understanding that the novel didn't need to pretend to coherence it couldn't honestly claim. *Flights* (2007), the work that brought her international recognition and the International Booker Prize, is built from 116 fragments. Some are a few sentences; others run several pages. They include: meditations on travel and motion, the story of a woman who disappears during a family vacation, a plastinated human nervous system on display in a museum, the preserved heart of Chopin, a seventeenth-century anatomist obsessed with preserving bodies, advice on how to travel alone, reflections on airports.

The book has no single protagonist, no conventional plot. What it has instead is a method: the accumulation of stories and observations around a set of obsessions — movement, preservation, the relationship between body and self, the impulse to stop time. The fragments echo and refract each other. You read by accretion, not by following a line.

"In a myriad of ways," she wrote, "we try to make sense of the world, to measure it, to divide it into little fragments, so it is easier to take into the mouth and swallow, so that it can be digested, and what you can't digest has to be vomited up."

This wasn't experiment for its own sake. It was form following insight: that experience itself is fragmentary, that we live in multiple temporalities at once, that meaning emerges not from a single story but from pattern and repetition across many.

## The Historical Imagination

If *Flights* mapped the present, *The Books of Jacob* (2014) took on history with similar ambition. The novel runs to nearly a thousand pages in English translation, spanning the eighteenth century across Poland, the Ottoman Empire, and Habsburg lands, following the real historical figure of Jacob Frank — a Jewish merchant who became a religious leader, claiming to be the Messiah, converting his followers first to Catholicism, then building a syncretic movement that drew from Jewish mysticism, Christianity, and Islam.

Tokarczuk tells the story from dozens of perspectives: Frank himself, his followers, his enemies, church authorities, Jewish community leaders, noblewomen, peasants. The book moves between letters, testimonies, travel narratives, and omniscient observation. It reconstructs an entire world — the multi-ethnic, multi-religious complexity of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth before nationalism reduced that complexity to single stories.

The novel argues implicitly against the kind of historical narrative that simplifies the past into clear lines of national or religious identity. Frank's movement thrived precisely in the spaces between established categories. Tokarczuk's form — shifting perspective, refusing to settle on a single authoritative voice — embodies that multiplicity.

"I am more of a guide," she said of her role as novelist, "than a judge." The narrator in *The Books of Jacob* occasionally addresses the reader directly, acknowledging the limits of what can be known, the gaps in the historical record, the impossibility of certainty about motivation or meaning.

## The Personal and Political

Tokarczuk grew up in southwestern Poland, near the Czech border, in a region that had belonged to Germany before World War II. The landscape itself was palimpsest — German street signs painted over, empty churches, layers of history incompletely erased. Her parents were both teachers; she studied psychology, then worked as a therapist before committing fully to writing.

Her early novels drew on Polish magical realism, a tradition of blending myth and history, but she was also reading broadly: Latin American writers, particularly the structural experiments of Borges and Cortázar, Jung's psychology, anthropology, travel writing. She was interested in how people make meaning, in the stories cultures tell themselves, in the difference between official history and lived experience.

This led her into controversy. In a 2014 speech accepting a literary prize, she described Polish history as including periods "when we were aggressors, when we carried out terrible deeds." The statement — a reference to Polish participation in anti-Jewish violence — provoked outrage from Polish nationalists, death threats, and years of public attacks. The country's ruling party used her as a symbol of liberal intellectual betrayal of Polish national pride.

She continued writing and speaking. When she won the Nobel in 2018 (announced in 2019 along with the 2019 prize, after a scandal had delayed the 2018 award), the Polish government's response was notably cool. The culture minister offered perfunctory congratulations; state media emphasized the controversy over her statements about Polish history.

## Mythology and Modernity

Her novels draw heavily on myth and fairy tale, but not as escape from the contemporary. *Drive Your Plow Over the Bones of the Dead* (2009) is narrated by a Blake-reading former engineer living in a Polish mountain village, investigating a series of deaths she believes are revenge killings by animals. It's simultaneously a murder mystery, an environmentalist manifesto, and a portrait of an eccentric woman whose marginal status lets her see what others ignore.

*Primeval and Other Times* (1996), one of her earliest novels, tells the story of a village called Primeval from multiple perspectives and across several generations, weaving in folk tales, dreams, and the appearance of mythological figures alongside historical events like World War II. Place becomes protagonist as much as any individual character.

This was her method: take the concerns of contemporary literature — migration, identity, environmental crisis, the failures of nationalism — and address them not through realism but through structures borrowed from myth, travel writing, anatomy textbooks, and fairy tale. The fragmentation isn't postmodern play; it's an argument about how to see whole.

## The Tenderness

"I came to the conclusion," she wrote in her Nobel lecture, "that culture, and in particular literature, is a sort of reservoir for tenderness, a tenderness that has the ability to expand our horizons of understanding and create space for the people we don't know, those who seem alien or who frighten us."

The word kept appearing in her work and interviews: tenderness. Not sentimentality, but something closer to careful attention, to the work of seeing across difference. Her novels contain violence, cruelty, the full weight of history's brutalities, but they insist on the possibility of connection — between strangers, across time, across the boundaries of nation or belief that claim to be absolute.

This was why form mattered so much. A single-perspective novel reinforces the illusion that one consciousness, one story, can contain truth. Tokarczuk's fragmentary structures, her proliferation of voices, her refusal of narrative authority — these were formal choices in service of an ethical commitment. To see more than one story at once, to hold multiple truths simultaneously, required a different kind of book.

## After the Nobel

The prize brought international visibility and Polish backlash. State television ran features questioning whether she deserved the honor. She continued to speak about migration (she supported taking in refugees during Europe's migration crisis), about women's rights (she supported abortion rights in a country where abortion is heavily restricted), about LGBT rights. She became unavoidable in Polish cultural debates, a figure onto whom conservatives projected anxieties about liberal cosmopolitanism.

Her work continued appearing in translation — more than twenty languages now. Readers who discovered her through *Flights* went back to earlier novels unavailable in English. *The Books of Jacob* finally appeared in English in 2021, with translator Jennifer Croft handling the massive text. Tokarczuk remained based in Poland, in the mountains near the Czech border, the region that had shaped her sense of place as multilayered, contested, impossible to reduce to simple national story.

She described herself as writing for readers who might not exist yet, for a future when the questions she was asking would be more widely understood. The Nobel suggested that future had arrived, that enough readers recognized in her work not experimental difficulty but something closer to necessity — a form adequate to the world's actual complexity, a way of seeing that didn't flatten or simplify.

"We live in a disintegrating reality," she said in an interview. "The role of the artist is to create new connections, to show connections that already exist but are not obvious."
