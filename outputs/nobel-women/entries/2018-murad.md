# 2018 — Against Sexual Violence in War (Nadia Murad)

In August 2014, ISIS fighters swept through the Yazidi villages of northern Iraq. They separated the men from the women. The men were killed. The women and girls were taken. Nadia Murad, twenty-one years old, from the village of Kocho, was among them. She was bought and sold, raped, tortured, held captive for three months before escaping. Two years later, she stood before the UN Security Council and described, in precise detail, what had been done to her. She named the acts. She named the men. She refused every euphemism.

The 2018 Nobel Peace Prize recognized Murad for her testimony — and for transforming testimony into campaign. She did not speak once and retreat into privacy. She spoke everywhere: the United Nations, the European Parliament, universities, refugee camps, wherever microphones and audiences gathered. She detailed the systematic use of rape as a weapon. She documented the slave markets where Yazidi women were displayed and sold. She demanded that ISIS be prosecuted for genocide. She insisted the world stop calling it tragedy and start calling it what it was: organized sexual violence as military strategy.

Murad shared the prize with Denis Mukwege, a Congolese surgeon who had treated tens of thousands of rape survivors. The pairing was deliberate. The Nobel Committee was recognizing not just individual courage but a movement: the effort to make sexual violence in conflict a prosecutable war crime, not an inevitable byproduct of war.

The Yazidis are a small religious minority in Iraq, practitioners of an ancient faith that blends elements of Zoroastrianism, Christianity, and Islam. ISIS declared them infidels. When they took Kocho, they gathered the villagers in the school. They demanded conversion. The Yazidis refused. The massacre began. Murad's mother was among the dead. Six of her brothers were shot. The women were loaded onto trucks.

What followed was a system. ISIS had established protocols for the distribution of women as spoils of war, supported by religious rulings that classified non-Muslim women as permissible slaves. Murad was taken to Mosul, where fighters inspected and selected women. She was given to a militant who beat her when she resisted. She was sold to a judge who raped her daily. She was passed among men who treated her as property. She attempted escape; she was caught, beaten, gang-raped as punishment. On a second attempt, three months in, she succeeded — helped by a neighboring family who risked their own lives hiding her.

She reached a refugee camp in Iraqi Kurdistan. Then Germany, where she was accepted as a refugee. She might have disappeared into the quiet life of survival. Thousands of Yazidi women did exactly that, rebuilding in silence. Murad chose otherwise. In December 2015, she spoke at a rally in Stuttgart. In December 2016, at the UN. Her testimony was forensic. She did not generalize. She recounted specific acts, named individual perpetrators, described the structure of the enslavement system. "They took our jewelry and IDs," she said. "They checked our clothes and our mouths, searching for anything we might have hidden. And then they took us."

The decision to testify came at cost. Yazidi culture prizes female modesty; speaking publicly about rape meant transgressing deep taboos. Murad's own community did not universally celebrate her visibility. Some survivors who wanted privacy resented the attention she drew. ISIS placed a bounty on her head. She required security wherever she traveled. And she traveled constantly: more than a dozen countries in the year before the Nobel announcement alone.

Her advocacy had a specific aim: legal accountability. She wanted ISIS fighters prosecuted for genocide, sexual violence classified as a war crime, and reparations for survivors. She pressed the UN to investigate. She lobbied governments to support tribunals. In 2016, she was appointed the UN's first Goodwill Ambassador for the Dignity of Survivors of Human Trafficking. In 2018, she published *The Last Girl*, a memoir that detailed her captivity and became an international bestseller.

The legal work advanced slowly. In 2021, a German court convicted an ISIS member of genocide for the murder of a Yazidi girl — the first genocide conviction related to the Yazidi persecution. Other prosecutions followed, mostly in European courts under universal jurisdiction statutes. But the majority of ISIS fighters faced no trial. The women they enslaved — estimates range from 3,000 to 10,000 Yazidis taken in 2014 — mostly remain unaccounted for. Some were killed. Some are presumed still held. Some, freed or escaped, live now in camps or diaspora.

Murad's testimony made visible what had been treated as unfortunate collateral damage. She insisted that sexual violence was not a side effect of war but a weapon of war, deployed with intent. "They did this to destroy us," she said. "To erase our identity, to ensure we could never return." The Nobel Committee, in awarding the prize, explicitly linked this recognition to international law: "Both laureates have made a crucial contribution to focusing attention on, and combating, such war crimes."

The prize did not end the campaign. Murad established the Nadia's Initiative, focused on rebuilding Yazidi communities and advocating for survivors of sexual violence. She continued to testify — before courts, parliaments, international bodies. She argued that protection for refugees was meaningless if it did not include justice for the crimes they fled. She pushed for what she called "accountability architecture": treaties, courts, enforcement mechanisms that made sexual violence prosecutable regardless of where it occurred or who committed it.

Critics argued that visibility could be its own trap — that elevating individual survivors like Murad turned structural violence into personal narrative, making the story about resilience rather than policy. Murad pushed back. "I don't speak for inspiration," she said. "I speak for justice." The testimony was not about her. It was evidence. The memoir was not catharsis. It was a record. "If I don't speak," she said, "there is no proof."

At the Nobel ceremony in Oslo, Murad wore white. She spoke for twenty-six minutes. She recounted the day Kocho fell, the months of captivity, the escape. She described what it meant to stand before the world and recount one's own rape. "I did not want to share my story," she said. "I wanted to sleep. I wanted to forget. But I had to do something." Then she turned to the audience — kings, diplomats, laureates — and said: "If you want to know what you can do, I will tell you. You can help us get justice."

The Yazidis call what happened in 2014 *firman* — annihilation. It was the seventy-fourth genocide in their history. This time, a survivor stood on a world stage and demanded the world call it by its name.
