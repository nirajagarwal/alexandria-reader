# 1986 — Nerve Growth Factor (Rita Levi-Montalcini)

In 1986, when Rita Levi-Montalcini received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, she was seventy-seven years old and still running her laboratory in Rome. The work being honored had begun forty years earlier in a makeshift laboratory she'd built in her bedroom during the Second World War, while Mussolini's racial laws barred her from Italian universities and German bombs fell on Turin. She had discovered nerve growth factor — a protein that directs the growth and survival of nerve cells — using fertilized chicken eggs, sewing needles fashioned into surgical instruments, and a determination that bordered on defiance.

The discovery would reshape neurobiology and open the field of growth factors, revealing that development is not simply genetic destiny but an ongoing conversation between cells and the molecules that guide them.

## The Bedroom Laboratory

Rita Levi-Montalcini was born in 1909 to a Sephardic Jewish family in Turin. Her father, a mathematician and engineer, initially discouraged his daughters from professional careers — he thought marriage and motherhood incompatible with intellectual work. Rita convinced him otherwise at twenty, enrolling in medical school at the University of Turin. She studied under Giuseppe Levi, a renowned histologist whose exacting standards and passion for cellular architecture shaped her approach to research. Histology — the study of tissue structure under the microscope — required patience, keen observation, and an almost aesthetic sense for what cells revealed about function.

She graduated in 1936, planning an academic career in neurology and psychiatry. Then Mussolini's government enacted racial laws. By 1938, Jews could not hold university positions. Giuseppe Levi, also Jewish, fled to Belgium. Rita stayed in Italy, working briefly in a private clinic before the war made that impossible too.

In 1940, facing the choice between emigration and idleness, she made a third option. She would continue her research at home. Her bedroom became a laboratory. She built an incubator for chicken eggs, sterilized instruments in a pressure cooker, and fashioned micro-surgical tools from watch springs and sewing needles. The microscope was borrowed. The question she chose to pursue came from her mentor: how do nerve fibers find their way to the correct targets during embryonic development?

She began by replicating experiments from Viktor Hamburger, an American embryologist who had shown that removing limb buds from chick embryos caused nerve cells in the spinal cord to die. Hamburger had concluded this was due to lack of mechanical stimulation. Working alone under her microscope, examining tissue sections stained to reveal nerve fibers, Levi-Montalcini saw something else. The nerve cells weren't failing to develop — they were developing normally, then dying. Death was part of the process, not an accident of missing limbs. This was programmed cell death, though that concept wouldn't have a name for decades.

The work continued through bombings and hiding. When Allied air raids intensified, the family moved to a cottage in the countryside. The laboratory moved with her. When German forces occupied Italy in 1943, the family went underground in Florence, living on forged papers. The microscope stayed behind. After the liberation, she worked briefly as a physician treating refugees and war victims, then returned to Turin. Giuseppe Levi had survived; together they prepared manuscripts from her wartime research.

## The Letter from St. Louis

In 1946, Viktor Hamburger read one of those manuscripts. He invited Levi-Montalcini to Washington University in St. Louis for what she assumed would be a one-semester visit to refine the observations she'd made in her bedroom. It became thirty years.

Hamburger suggested they examine mouse tumors known to grow networks of nerve fibers when grafted onto chick embryos. The tumors — sarcomas — seemed to attract and stimulate nerve growth in unnatural ways. If they could understand why, they might understand the normal process. Levi-Montalcini grafted tumor tissue onto chick embryos and watched what happened.

What happened was extraordinary. Nerve fibers from the embryo invaded the tumor like roots seeking water, but they also overgrew massively in areas far from the graft — in sympathetic ganglia that normally develop their own modest networks. Nerve cells were multiplying and extending fibers in response to something the tumor was producing. Not a structural target, not mechanical stimulation — a chemical signal.

She called it a "nerve growth promoting agent." It was specific: only certain types of nerve cells responded. It was potent: tiny amounts of tumor tissue triggered dramatic growth. And it suggested something new — that developing nerves don't just follow pre-laid paths but respond to signaling molecules that guide and nourish them.

## Snake Venom and Salivary Glands

The next step was to isolate the substance. Levi-Montalcini brought in Stanley Cohen, a biochemist, in 1953. Cohen had the chemical skills she lacked. Together they worked to extract and purify the growth-promoting factor from mouse tumors. The work was meticulous — testing fractions of tumor extract on embryos, measuring nerve fiber growth, refining the preparation.

They needed a test to prove they'd isolated a protein, not just a cellular debris artifact. Cohen suggested adding snake venom to their preparations — venom contained enzymes that broke down proteins. If the growth-promoting activity disappeared after venom treatment, they'd know it was a protein. They added the venom. The nerve fibers grew anyway. In fact, they grew *more*.

The snake venom itself contained nerve growth factor — in far higher concentrations than the tumor. It was an accidental discovery born from an experimental control, the kind of surprise that science occasionally offers to those prepared to see it. If venom contained the factor, what else might? They tested salivary glands from adult mice, reasoning they might share biochemistry with venom glands. The salivary glands were rich in nerve growth factor. Suddenly they had a plentiful source for isolation and study.

By 1960, Cohen had purified the protein. It was a moderately sized molecule — about 26,000 daltons — that bound to specific receptors on nerve cells and triggered growth. Injecting NGF into newborn mice caused their sympathetic nervous systems to grow enormously. Injecting antibodies that neutralized NGF caused those same systems to degenerate almost completely. Here was proof: NGF wasn't just present during development — it was necessary.

Levi-Montalcini focused on understanding which cells made NGF, where it acted, and how it guided nerve development. Cohen turned to biochemical mechanisms and later discovered another growth factor, EGF (epidermal growth factor), opening a new field. They worked in parallel more than together after the initial years, but the collaboration had been essential.

## The Architecture of Growth

What made NGF conceptually important was what it revealed about development. Before NGF, nerve development was understood largely as a genetic program: genes specified structure, and structure unfolded according to plan. NGF showed that development was also a matter of cells responding to their environment — that the environment itself, through signaling molecules, instructed cells on whether to grow, differentiate, or die.

Nerve cells extend fibers toward targets that produce NGF. The supply of NGF is limited, and not all nerve cells that reach a target survive — only those that secure enough NGF persist. This is why removing a limb bud causes nerve cell death: the target producing NGF is gone, so cells that would have innervated it die. Development is sculpted by survival as much as growth, and NGF controls both.

The discovery opened a field. Researchers found other growth factors — proteins that guide the development of blood vessels, skin, immune cells, nearly every tissue. The concept of growth factors as signaling molecules became central to understanding development, wound healing, cancer, and neurodegenerative disease. NGF itself became a therapeutic target: could supplementing NGF slow the progression of Alzheimer's or Parkinson's disease? The clinical promise has been complicated, but the science has been foundational.

## The Roman Laboratory

Levi-Montalcini spent half of each year in St. Louis and half in Rome, where she built a second laboratory at the Italian National Research Council. She never married, describing herself as wedded to science. She called her work "a beautiful adventure." In interviews, she emphasized luck and curiosity over ambition, though her career was marked by both.

She received the Nobel Prize in 1986, sharing it with Stanley Cohen. The Swedish Academy recognized not just the discovery of NGF but the conceptual shift it represented: that chemical signals between cells were essential orchestrators of development. She was the fourth woman to win the prize in Physiology or Medicine, and at seventy-seven, among the oldest laureates in the category.

In her Nobel lecture, she described the wartime bedroom laboratory without sentimentality: "The bedside table became my lab bench, and the bedroom became my laboratory." She credited the constraints as clarifying. When resources are minimal, the question must matter.

She kept working into her nineties, studying NGF's role in the immune system and collaborating on research at her Rome laboratory. She died in 2012 at the age of 103, having lived through Fascism, war, exile, and the transformation of neuroscience. Her legacy is the growth factors themselves, and the insight that development is not just a program but a conversation — between genes and environment, between cells and the signals that guide them toward survival or death.
