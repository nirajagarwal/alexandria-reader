# 2009 — Governing the Commons (Elinor Ostrom)

When Elinor Ostrom learned she'd won the Nobel Prize in Economics, she was in her campus office at Indiana University, preparing for a seminar. The call came at 5:30 in the morning. "Are you really awake?" the voice asked. She was. But the news — the first woman to win the economics prize in its forty-year history — took longer to register. She'd spent her career studying how people solve problems together. Now she faced a new one: explaining to the world that much of what economists believed about human cooperation was wrong.

The conventional wisdom, circa 1968, was tidy. When people share a resource — a fishery, a forest, a pasture — they destroy it. Each person takes as much as possible before others can. The tragedy of the commons, ecologist Garrett Hardin called it, invoking a parable of overgrazing. The solution, according to theory, was equally tidy: either privatize the resource or put government in charge. These were the only options rational people could accept.

Ostrom went looking for the real commons. Not thought experiments. Actual villages, fisheries, irrigation systems, forests. She found Swiss Alpine meadows managed by communities for centuries. Japanese mountain forests harvested sustainably across generations. Irrigation systems in Spain and the Philippines where farmers coordinated water allocation without central authority. The tragedy was avoidable. It was being avoided, daily, in hundreds of places around the world.

The question was how. Not whether cooperation was possible — she'd seen it — but what made it work.

She began collecting cases systematically. The work required ethnography, institutional analysis, game theory, field observation. She traveled to Nepal to study irrigation. To Maine to understand lobster fisheries. To Kenya to examine groundwater management. Each case was a natural experiment in governance. What conditions allowed communities to manage resources collectively without collapsing into either chaos or exploitation?

Patterns emerged. Successful commons had clear boundaries — you knew who was in and who was out. Rules matched local conditions rather than being imposed from above. Those affected by rules had a say in making them. Monitoring happened, but by the community itself or someone accountable to it. Sanctions for rule-breaking escalated gradually. Conflicts were resolved locally. And crucially: outside authorities didn't interfere with local arrangements.

None of this fit economic orthodoxy. Standard theory assumed people were self-interested utility maximizers. You couldn't trust them with common resources because they'd inevitably cheat. But Ostrom found that people often weren't simply maximizing individual gain. They were embedded in communities. They cared about reputation. They developed norms of reciprocity. They punished free-riders not because it made narrow economic sense but because fairness mattered.

Her 1990 book *Governing the Commons* laid out the framework. It was dense, careful, built from hundreds of specific cases. The central insight was deceptively simple: the tragedy of the commons wasn't inevitable. It was a failure of institutional design. Given the right rules, people could govern shared resources themselves — neither through markets nor through states, but through what she called "polycentric governance."

Polycentric meant multiple centers of authority, nested and overlapping. A fishing community might set daily catch limits while a regional body managed seasonal closures and a national government enforced territorial waters. No single authority commanded everything. Instead, governance happened at multiple scales, each adapted to particular problems.

This contradicted decades of policy. Governments had nationalized forests and fisheries, assuming local communities couldn't manage them. International development agencies imposed standardized solutions — privatize land, centralize control — without regard for existing arrangements. The results were often disastrous. Resources degraded. Communities that had managed successfully for generations lost authority. Ostrom's work provided both explanation and alternative.

She'd come to economics late and sideways. Born in Los Angeles in 1933, she'd studied political science at UCLA because women weren't particularly welcome in economics departments. She worked as a personnel officer. When she returned to graduate school, faculty assumed she'd study local government administration — a suitable topic for a woman. She did, but her dissertation on water management in Southern California became the foundation for decades of research on collective action.

She built her career at Indiana University, where she and her husband Vincent founded the Workshop in Political Theory and Policy Analysis. The name was deliberate — a workshop, where people actually made things. Collaborative, interdisciplinary, focused on problems rather than disciplinary boundaries. Young researchers from around the world came to study governance in practice. The Workshop became a hub for fieldwork, cross-cultural comparison, and theory that stayed tethered to reality.

Ostrom's methods were unfashionable in economics. She didn't work from elegant mathematical models. She resisted grand theories that explained everything. Instead she built middle-range frameworks, tested against messy reality. She used case studies, field experiments, agent-based modeling — whatever tools illuminated actual behavior. Some economists found this insufficiently rigorous. But she was studying phenomena that formal theory had simply assumed away.

Her work extended beyond natural resources. The principles applied wherever people faced collective action problems: managing urban policing, maintaining open-source software, organizing neighborhood associations. Any situation where individual incentives diverged from group welfare but where neither markets nor hierarchical control provided obvious solutions.

The Nobel committee cited her "analysis of economic governance, especially the commons." The prize startled many economists. Ostrom wasn't in the mainstream. She'd never held a position at Harvard, MIT, or Chicago. She worked outside the dominant paradigms. But her empirical rigor was undeniable, and her findings had reshaped thinking about property rights, public goods, and institutional economics.

She shared the prize with Oliver Williamson, whose work on firms and transaction costs complemented hers. But the attention focused on Ostrom — the first woman, yes, but also the anomaly. Here was an economist who'd spent her career watching people cooperate when theory said they shouldn't.

The policy implications were profound. If communities could manage commons effectively under the right conditions, then wholesale privatization or nationalization weren't the only options. Local knowledge mattered. Existing institutions mattered. Top-down solutions often destroyed working arrangements. Better governance meant strengthening communities' capacity to solve their own problems rather than assuming they couldn't.

This wasn't romantic localism. Ostrom documented failures too — commons that did collapse, communities that couldn't coordinate, experiments that ended badly. Her point was that outcomes depended on institutional design. Get the rules right, and cooperation was sustainable. Get them wrong, and tragedy followed. But "right" couldn't be determined abstractly. It had to fit context, history, ecology, culture.

She continued working after the Nobel, more visible but unchanged in approach. She pressed for better integration between fieldwork and theory. She advocated for polycentric responses to climate change — multiple scales of governance rather than a single global treaty. She defended complexity against the seduction of simple models.

Elinor Ostrom died in 2012, three years after the prize. Her legacy lives in policy — in conservation programs that involve local communities, in development approaches that respect existing institutions, in recognition that people aren't just rational actors maximizing utility. It lives in method — showing that careful empirical work could challenge fundamental assumptions. And it lives in the mundane miracle she documented: people governing themselves, solving problems together, avoiding tragedy not through markets or states but through rules they made and followed because the alternatives were worse.

"There are no panaceas," she liked to say. Every commons is different. Every solution must be crafted for context. The work is hard, particular, never finished. But it's possible. That was the insight. Not optimism about human nature. Not faith in spontaneous order. Just evidence, accumulated case by case, that under conditions we could specify, cooperation works.
