# 2022 — Click Chemistry in Living Systems (Carolyn Bertozzi)

Carolyn Bertozzi figured out how to do chemistry inside the human body without killing anything. She built molecules that could snap together in living cells like Lego bricks — fast, clean, and harmless to everything around them. The method let scientists attach fluorescent tags to sugars on cancer cells, track diseases as they moved through tissue, and build new tools for medicine in places chemistry had never worked before. It was chemistry redesigned for biology's constraints, and it opened a door that had been locked for a century.

The 2022 Nobel Prize in Chemistry recognized three people who made "click chemistry" real. Barry Sharpless and Morten Meldal developed the original concept: molecular reactions so reliable and efficient they worked like clicks, no mess. Bertozzi took it further. She made click chemistry work where it mattered most — inside living organisms, where standard chemistry would be toxic or impossible.

Traditional organic chemistry is messy. Reactions need heat, pressure, catalysts, organic solvents. They produce byproducts. Run that inside a living cell and you'd kill it. Bertozzi needed reactions that worked in water, at body temperature, without copper or other metals that cells can't tolerate. And they had to be selective enough to click only with the target molecules, ignoring the thousands of other things floating around in biological soup.

She found the answer in strained molecules. Take a ring of carbon atoms and force it into an unnatural shape — bend it, twist it, make it want to snap back. That strain stores energy. When the right partner molecule comes along, the ring springs open and locks together with it, releasing that energy. No catalyst needed. No toxic byproducts. Just two molecules clicking together in a biological environment that would have destroyed conventional chemistry.

Bertozzi called it "bioorthogonal chemistry" — reactions that are orthogonal to biology, running parallel to life's own chemistry without interfering. The word was precise. This wasn't just chemistry in living systems; it was chemistry invisible to them.

The earliest applications were visual. Bertozzi wanted to see glycans — the complex sugars coating the outside of cells — which play roles in everything from immune response to cancer metastasis but were nearly impossible to study in living tissue. She designed molecular probes with two parts: a sugar mimic that cells would incorporate into their glycan coats, and a chemical handle that could click with a fluorescent dye. Feed the sugar to cells, let them build it into their surface, then add the dye. Click. Suddenly the glycans glowed, and you could watch them in real time.

It worked. For the first time, scientists could see how glycans changed as cells became cancerous, how they helped tumors evade the immune system, how they varied from tissue to tissue. The method spread fast. Researchers used it to track proteins, lipids, nucleic acids. Anything you could tag with Bertozzi's chemical handles became visible in living systems.

Then came therapeutic applications. If you could click molecules together inside the body, you could build drugs on site — deliver two harmless pieces separately and let them assemble into the active compound only at the target location. Or click antibodies to tumor cells with precision. Or use click chemistry to modify antibodies for better drug delivery. The method became a tool for building next-generation cancer treatments.

Bertozzi grew up the youngest of three daughters in a Boston suburb. Her father was an MIT physicist; the household air was scientific. She played football in high school — defensive end, the only girl on the team. At Harvard she studied chemistry but spent as much time playing keyboard in a rock band. The dual interests — precision and creativity, structure and improvisation — stayed with her.

Graduate school at Berkeley under Mark Bednarski pointed her toward glycobiology, the chemistry of sugars. Glycans were the most complex and least understood molecules in biology. Most chemists avoided them. Bertozzi went straight in. After a postdoc, she returned to Berkeley as faculty in 1996, and built a lab around a single question: how do you study molecules on the surface of living cells without destroying the cells?

The obstacles were standard for a young academic — grants to write, labs to equip — but magnified. Berkeley's chemistry department had never tenured a woman. Bertozzi was out as a lesbian in an era when that still cost professional capital. She worked through it: published prolifically, mentored students, collaborated widely. Tenure came in 2002.

By then bioorthogonal chemistry was established. The method kept expanding. Bertozzi moved to Stanford in 2015 to direct a new institute focused on translating chemical tools into medicine. She held positions in chemistry, chemical engineering, and pediatrics. The cross-disciplinary spread was the point — bioorthogonal chemistry lived at the intersections.

The Nobel came twenty-five years after her first papers on the method. Sharpless, who shared the prize, had already won once before for different work. (He became only the fifth person to win two Nobel Prizes in science, and the first to win twice in chemistry for different discoveries.) Meldal had developed copper-catalyzed click chemistry independently. Bertozzi brought it to life.

"I was sitting at my kitchen table," she said of the phone call from Stockholm, "and I genuinely could not believe it." Then back to work. The prize was recognition, not conclusion. Bioorthogonal chemistry was still opening new ground: better probes, new therapeutic strategies, methods for imaging diseases in ways impossible a decade ago.

The elegance is in the constraint. Bertozzi didn't make chemistry more powerful by adding force; she made it more precise by removing everything unnecessary. Strained rings and careful design replaced heat and catalysts. The result works where nothing else can — in the intricate, crowded, water-filled environment of living cells, where life's own chemistry continues undisturbed and our new reactions run quietly alongside.

That's the achievement: chemistry that plays by biology's rules. Click.
