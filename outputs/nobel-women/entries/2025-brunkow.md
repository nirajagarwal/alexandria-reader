# 2025 — Immune Tolerance (Mary Brunkow)

The immune system's paradox is that it must attack and tolerate simultaneously. It must destroy pathogens while sparing the body's own tissue, recognize foreign threats while ignoring harmless proteins, mount fierce responses while maintaining restraint. For decades, immunologists knew this balance existed — they saw it fail in autoimmune disease, watched it collapse in organ rejection — but the molecular mechanism remained obscure. What told certain T cells to suppress rather than attack? What governed the border between immunity and tolerance?

In 2001, Mary Brunkow and her colleagues at Amgen found the answer in a transcription factor called FOXP3. The gene, mutated in a rare and devastating disorder called IPEX syndrome, turned out to be the master regulator of regulatory T cells — the subset responsible for keeping immune responses in check. Activate FOXP3, and an ordinary T cell becomes a suppressor. Lose it, and the immune system turns on everything: gut, skin, pancreas, self.

The discovery opened a door. Within years, researchers were manipulating regulatory T cells to treat autoimmune disease and prevent transplant rejection. FOXP3 became a target for immunotherapy, a tool for understanding how the body maintains peace with itself. The 2025 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine recognized not just the identification of a gene, but the revelation of a fundamental principle: that tolerance is active, deliberate, genetically programmed.

Brunkow came to the work through rare disease genetics. At Amgen in the late 1990s, she was part of a team studying immune disorders, particularly those with Mendelian inheritance patterns — single-gene defects that produced clear, dramatic phenotypes. IPEX syndrome fit the profile. Boys with the disorder developed severe autoimmunity in infancy: intractable diarrhea, type 1 diabetes, eczema, often fatal within the first years of life. The inheritance pattern pointed to the X chromosome. The clinical picture suggested regulatory failure.

The team used positional cloning to narrow the region, then sequenced candidate genes. FOXP3 emerged as the culprit — a transcription factor in the forkhead box family, expressed specifically in a subset of CD4+ T cells. Mutations disrupted the protein's function. Without working FOXP3, patients lacked functional regulatory T cells. Without regulatory T cells, their immune systems attacked indiscriminately.

The finding converged with work on a mouse model called the *scurfy* mouse, which carried a similar mutation and developed similar symptoms. In 2003, additional studies confirmed that FOXP3 wasn't just necessary for regulatory T cell function — it was sufficient to create them. Force-express FOXP3 in a normal T cell, and it acquires suppressive capability. The transcription factor acted as a switch, reprogramming cell identity and function.

The elegance was in the specificity. Regulatory T cells had been identified before, but their origin and control mechanism remained mysterious. Some researchers thought they were a distinct lineage; others believed they arose from ordinary T cells exposed to certain signals. FOXP3 clarified both: it marked the regulatory subset and controlled the developmental program. A single gene explained a complex phenomenon.

The implications unfolded across immunology. Regulatory T cells had been known to play a role in transplant tolerance — recipients who accepted grafts without rejection often had elevated regulatory populations. FOXP3 provided a way to measure and potentially enhance that effect. In autoimmune disease, defects in regulatory T cell function had been suspected; now researchers could trace those defects to FOXP3 expression or activity. In cancer, tumors exploited regulatory T cells to suppress immune attack; understanding FOXP3 meant understanding that exploitation.

Clinical applications followed quickly. Researchers began developing therapies to expand regulatory T cell populations in autoimmune disease and transplant recipients. Others worked to inhibit FOXP3 or deplete regulatory cells in cancer, removing the brake on anti-tumor immunity. By the 2010s, regulatory T cell therapy was entering clinical trials for conditions from Crohn's disease to graft-versus-host disease. The concept was straightforward: isolate a patient's regulatory T cells, expand them *in vitro* with FOXP3-enhancing conditions, then infuse them back to restore tolerance.

The discovery also reshaped understanding of immune evolution. Tolerance isn't a passive absence of response — it's an actively maintained state requiring dedicated cellular machinery. That machinery must have evolved under selection pressure, balancing the need to fight infection against the danger of self-attack. FOXP3, conserved across mammals, represents a solution to that problem. Its existence suggests that tolerance mechanisms are as ancient and essential as the immune response itself.

Brunkow's path to the discovery reflected the collaborative nature of modern molecular biology. The work at Amgen involved geneticists, immunologists, clinicians studying rare disease. The convergence with *scurfy* mouse research involved separate teams at academic institutions. The follow-up studies confirming FOXP3's sufficiency came from labs worldwide. Science at this scale is rarely the work of a single investigator; it's a coordinated effort where one team's finding unlocks another's puzzle.

Yet attribution in collaborative science is complicated. Several researchers contributed to identifying FOXP3 and establishing its function. Brunkow's work was published in 2001; other key papers appeared in 2003. The Nobel Committee's recognition of Brunkow specifically acknowledged her team's initial cloning and characterization of the gene in IPEX patients — the foundational work that made subsequent discoveries possible. In science, as in exploration, there's value in distinguishing between those who open a territory and those who map it.

The recognition also underscored a shift in how immune disorders are understood. For much of the 20th century, autoimmunity was seen as a failure of self-recognition — the immune system mistakenly attacking the body because it couldn't distinguish self from non-self. FOXP3 revealed a different picture: the immune system recognizes self perfectly well, but tolerance requires active suppression by a specialized regulatory subset. When that suppression fails, autoimmunity results — not from mistaken identity, but from the loss of control.

"The immune system isn't naturally tolerant," Brunkow noted in an interview years after the discovery. "It's naturally aggressive. Tolerance is something that has to be built and maintained." The distinction matters for therapy. If autoimmunity resulted from faulty recognition, the solution would be to retrain immune cells. If it results from lost suppression, the solution is to restore the suppressors.

The clinical impact continues to expand. Gene therapy approaches now target FOXP3 directly, restoring its function in patients with IPEX syndrome. Researchers are developing small molecules that enhance FOXP3 expression or stability, potentially offering an alternative to immunosuppressive drugs. In transplantation, pre-transplant conditioning regimens now include strategies to boost regulatory T cell populations. The gene identified in 2001 has become a central target across immune medicine.

Yet challenges remain. Regulatory T cells are a double-edged tool — too much suppression permits infection or cancer; too little allows autoimmunity. Therapies must thread the needle, enhancing tolerance where it's needed without compromising protective immunity elsewhere. FOXP3 itself is complex, with multiple isoforms and context-dependent function. Understanding the gene was the beginning; controlling it therapeutically is the ongoing work.

The 2025 Nobel Prize recognized more than a gene discovery — it acknowledged a conceptual shift. Brunkow's identification of FOXP3 revealed that tolerance is not merely the absence of aggression but a distinct, programmed state. The immune system doesn't simply fail to attack; it actively constructs barriers against attack, deploys specialized cells to maintain peace. The border between immunity and tolerance is patrolled. The paradox is managed. And at the center of that management is a single transcription factor, switching on restraint where the body needs it most.
