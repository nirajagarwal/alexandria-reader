# 1911 — Radium and Polonium (Marie Curie)

She isolated radium. Not discovered — Pierre and Marie Curie had done that together, along with polonium, years earlier, sharing the 1903 Physics prize with Henri Becquerel. But in 1911, the Chemistry prize recognized something harder: Marie Curie had proven these elements existed by extracting them in pure metallic form from tons of pitchblende residue. Eight years of brutal physical labor, chemical precision, and conceptual clarity. The result was a sliver of metal that glowed in the dark and two atomic weights no one could dispute.

The work began in 1898 in a converted shed at the École de Physique et de Chimie in Paris. No funding, minimal equipment, drafty and leaking. The Curies had noticed that pitchblende — uranium ore — was more radioactive than pure uranium could account for. Something else was in there. Marie suspected new elements. Finding them meant processing literal tons of ore, dissolving, precipitating, crystallizing, separating, measuring radioactivity at each stage to track where the mystery elements concentrated.

Polonium, named for Marie's native Poland, came first. Then radium, far more intensely radioactive. Both existed in infinitesimal quantities — a few decigrams per ton of ore. Marie did most of the physical work herself: stirring boiling solutions with an iron rod nearly as tall as she was, hauling sacks of ore residue, working in all weather because the shed had no ventilation system and the fumes were toxic. Pierre handled theory and some measurements; Marie handled isolation.

By 1902 they had a decigram of radium chloride, enough to calculate an atomic weight: 225. Close to the modern value of 226. The Physics prize followed in 1903. But radium chloride is a salt, not an element. Skeptics — and there were many, especially regarding work by a woman — could argue Marie hadn't proven the element existed, only that the salt did. Perhaps she'd made a measurement error. Perhaps radium was a compound, not an element.

So she kept working. Pierre died in 1906, killed by a horse-drawn wagon in a Paris street. Marie continued. To isolate pure radium metal required electrolysis: passing current through molten radium chloride to separate the metal from the chlorine. Radium is viciously reactive, corroding instantly in air or water. She worked in an atmosphere of hydrogen gas, using a mercury cathode. In 1910, working with André Debierne, she succeeded. A tiny globule of silvery-white metal, soft like lead, glowing faintly blue in the dark.

She prepared a sample of pure radium chloride and determined its atomic weight with meticulous precision: 226.45. She measured its properties: melting point, spectrum, chemical behavior. She documented everything with the rigor of a chemist who knew her work would be scrutinized more harshly than a man's. The 1911 Chemistry prize recognized "the discovery of the elements radium and polonium, by the isolation of radium and the study of the nature and compounds of this remarkable element."

The timing was complex. As the Swedish Academy deliberated in Stockholm, Paris newspapers were running front-page coverage of Marie's affair with physicist Paul Langevin, married though separated. The scandal was vicious: cartoons, accusations of homewrecking, nationalist attacks on the foreign-born woman who'd seduced a French scientist. She received death threats. Her daughters were harassed. Svante Arrhenius, a Swedish chemist on the Nobel committee, wrote suggesting she decline the prize or at least skip the ceremony until the scandal died down.

Marie refused. She went to Stockholm in December 1911 and delivered her Nobel lecture herself. Her topic: the isolation of pure radium. She described the chemistry methodically, citing measurements, explaining techniques. She spoke of Pierre — his contributions, his insights — more generously than most widows might. She did not mention the scandal. She did not apologize for existing. She accepted the prize as recognition of work done, and done well.

"I could tell you many things about radium and radioactivity," she said in the lecture, "but I believe it would take too much time." Instead she focused on the chemistry: fractionation methods, spectrum analysis, atomic weight determination. The elegance was in the precision. Radium existed not because she wanted it to, but because she'd proven it: isolated it, weighed it, characterized it, made it undeniable.

The prize made her the first person to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences. The only person, still, to win in both Physics and Chemistry. She was 44. Her hands were scarred from radiation burns — they'd thought the glowing tubes were merely curious, not dangerous. She would die of aplastic anemia in 1934, her bone marrow destroyed by decades of radiation exposure. Her papers, her cookbooks, even her furniture remain too radioactive to handle without protection.

But in 1911, she had her sliver of radium metal, its atomic weight precisely measured, its existence no longer disputable. She'd taken radioactivity from an interesting phenomenon to a field of study, from Pierre and Marie's collaboration to her own achievement. The Chemistry prize recognized what she'd actually done: not co-discovery, but isolation. The hardest part. The part that required eight years in a freezing shed, stirring toxic solutions, breathing poisonous fumes, doing the unglamorous work that transforms speculation into fact.

Radium would later be used in medicine — cancer treatment, sterilization — and in countless glow-in-the-dark products before its dangers were fully understood. Polonium would become a neutron source in nuclear physics and, grimly, a poison for assassination. The Curies' work opened radioactivity as a field, trained a generation of researchers, and transformed physics and chemistry both. Marie's institute in Paris became a center for radioactivity research, training dozens of scientists, including her daughter Irène, who would win her own Chemistry prize in 1935.

"One never notices what has been done," Marie wrote in a letter after Pierre's death, "one can only see what remains to be done." What remained, in 1911, was proving that radium existed not as an idea or a measurement but as a physical reality: metal you could hold, weigh, study. She did that work alone, and the Chemistry prize was hers alone. The first of its kind for a woman — and earned twice over.
