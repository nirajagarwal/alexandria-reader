# Colors in Soap Bubbles

A soap bubble catches sunlight and gleams with bands of shifting color — purple, green, gold. No pigment creates these hues. The bubble's wall is thinner than a bacteria, just molecules of soap and water, yet it splits white light into a spectrum. This iridescence reveals how waves interfere with themselves, why some colors survive while others vanish, and what happens when matter becomes thin enough that light must interact with both its surfaces at once.

**Light Meets Itself Coming Back**

When light hits a soap film, part reflects off the outer surface immediately. The rest enters the film, crosses to the inner surface, reflects there, and exits. These two reflections — outer and inner — are now slightly out of sync. If their wave peaks align, they amplify each other: bright color. If peak meets trough, they cancel: darkness. Which happens depends on the film's thickness and the light's wavelength. At any spot on the bubble, some colors constructively interfere while others destructively cancel, producing the bands.

**The Film Thins as It Falls**

Gravity pulls the soapy water downward. The top of a bubble becomes thinner than the bottom, creating vertical bands of color that shift as the film drains. Where the film thins to about 25 nanometers — roughly forty soap molecules — the path difference becomes less than any visible wavelength. All colors cancel. The bubble appears black. This happens right before it pops: the film simply cannot hold itself together below this threshold. The black region spreads downward from the top like an advancing shadow.

**Robert Hooke Saw Order in 1665**

Robert Hooke, examining soap bubbles with early microscopes and careful lighting, recognized that the color patterns were regular and repeatable, not random play of light. He measured the thickness of thin films using his observations and mathematical reasoning, arriving at estimates remarkably close to modern values despite lacking our tools. In *Micrographia*, he proposed that colors arose from light reflecting from both surfaces of the film. Isaac Newton later expanded this work, using soap films and air gaps between glass plates to investigate what he called "Newton's rings" — concentric colored circles revealing thickness variations. Neither man fully understood light waves, but both saw the bubble as an instrument for investigating nature's hidden scales.

**Oil Slicks Share the Principle**

The same interference creates rainbow swirls on wet pavement where oil has spread thin. Insect wings shimmer with structural color when microscopic layers interfere with light. The blue of a morpho butterfly comes not from pigment but from nanoscale architecture on its wing scales. Anti-reflective coatings on camera lenses use destructive interference — a thin layer precisely calibrated so reflected waves cancel, making the glass seemingly invisible. In each case, order at microscopic scales translates into visible color through wave interference. The soap bubble simply makes the principle luminous and temporary.

**The Color Sequence Repeats**

As a bubble film thins from top to bottom, colors appear in a predictable order: first yellow and magenta, then blue and green, then red and cyan, cycling through progressively higher interference orders. Each cycle corresponds to the film thinning by about half a wavelength of visible light — roughly 250 nanometers. A thick bubble can show multiple cycles of this pattern stacked vertically. The progression reveals the film's thickness gradient with precision: you're watching nanometer-scale changes written in color across a surface you can hold in your hand.

**Monochromatic Light Simplifies to Bands**

View a bubble under a sodium vapor lamp, which emits only yellow light at 589 nanometers, and the swirling colors vanish. You see only alternating bright yellow and dark bands — places where that specific wavelength constructively or destructively interferes. This simplified pattern reveals the pure geometry of interference without white light's complexity. It's how physicists first proved that interference was wave behavior: single-wavelength sources produce the regular, alternating patterns that wave theory predicts. The bubble becomes a demonstration apparatus, turning abstract wave mechanics into something you can blow from a wand.
