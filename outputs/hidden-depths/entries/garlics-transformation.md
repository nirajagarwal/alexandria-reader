# Garlic's Transformation

Raw garlic burns. Roasted garlic spreads like butter and tastes almost sweet. These aren't flavor variations — they're different chemical compounds. The same clove contains the potential for both, determined entirely by what happens in the first seconds after cutting. Understanding garlic means understanding a cellular suicide mechanism that's been 100 million years in the making.

**The Separated Lovers**

Inside an intact garlic clove, two compounds live in different cellular compartments: alliin (an amino acid) in the cytoplasm, alliinase (an enzyme) in specialized vacuoles. The cell wall keeps them apart. When you cut or crush garlic, these barriers rupture. The enzyme and amino acid meet. Within seconds, alliinase converts alliin into allicin — the pungent, sulfur-rich compound responsible for raw garlic's bite. This isn't decay. It's a defense system activating at the moment of injury.

**The Ten-Minute Window**

Allicin is unstable. It begins transforming immediately into a cascade of other sulfur compounds, each with different properties. After ten minutes at room temperature, much of the allicin has converted to ajoene and various polysulfides — milder, more complex. After twenty minutes, different compounds dominate. This is why letting chopped garlic rest before cooking produces less harsh results. The incendiary allicin has already degraded into something gentler, without heat's intervention.

**Heat's Intervention**

Above 140°F (60°C), alliinase denatures. The enzyme stops working. If you heat whole garlic cloves or quickly blanch minced garlic, alliin and alliinase never meet — or barely meet — and allicin never forms. What you taste instead is the amino acid's mild, slightly sweet profile. This is roasted garlic's secret: heat applied fast enough that the defense system never fully activates. The garlic browns through Maillard reactions, developing nutty, caramelized notes, but the sulfurous bite never emerges.

**The Evolutionary Bargain**

Garlic evolved this two-part defense because it works. Allicin disrupts bacterial cell walls, prevents fungal growth, deters most herbivores. It's a chemical weapon stored safely until needed. But the system created an opening: cook garlic before crushing it, and the weapon stays locked. Humans discovered this loophole thousands of years ago. We've been exploiting it ever since, selecting for garlic varieties with higher alliin concentrations — more potential bite — while perfecting techniques to control when and how that potential releases.

**Louis Pasteur's Footnote**

In 1858, Louis Pasteur noted garlic's antibacterial properties, though he didn't know why. It wasn't until 1944 that Chester Cavallito isolated allicin and identified it as the active agent. Cavallito was researching penicillin alternatives during World War II — antibiotics were desperately needed. Allicin showed promise in vitro but proved too unstable for medical use. It degrades within hours, even in ideal storage. Nature designed it for immediate, local defense, not long-term weaponry. Modern research has circled back: some scientists study garlic's sulfur compounds as potential antibiotic adjuvants, ways to make existing drugs more effective against resistant bacteria.

**The Cooking Gradient**

Between raw and roasted lies every technique: Sliced thin and sautéed briefly, garlic develops some allicin but heat stops the cascade early — sharp but not aggressive. Minced and cooked slowly in oil, it mellows as both heat and time transform the compounds. Confit — submerged in oil below 200°F for hours — produces something entirely different: the gentle treatment allows some enzymatic activity while heat prevents the harshest compounds from forming. Each method is a negotiation between the plant's defense and the cook's goal, playing variables of time, temperature, and physical damage against each other.
