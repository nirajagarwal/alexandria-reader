# Brighter Than Galaxies

For a few weeks in 1987, astronomers watched a single dying star in a nearby galaxy outshine 100 million suns. Supernovae — the violent deaths of massive stars — routinely release more energy in seconds than our sun will produce in its entire 10-billion-year lifetime. These aren't just bright; they're the universe's way of transmuting elements, seeding space with the raw materials for planets and people. What actually happens when a star explodes?

**Collapse in Under a Second**

When a massive star exhausts its nuclear fuel, its iron core — roughly the mass of our sun compressed to the size of Earth — collapses. In less than a quarter second, it shrinks to a sphere 20 kilometers across. The collapse happens so fast that the outer layers don't immediately follow. They hover, briefly, over an abyss. Then the core rebounds — either as a neutron star or, if massive enough, continuing its collapse into a black hole. The rebound sends a shockwave outward at 30,000 kilometers per second. Everything above the core — layers of silicon, oxygen, carbon, helium — explodes into space.

**The Neutrino Storm**

Most of a supernova's energy — 99% — doesn't emerge as light. It floods out as neutrinos, ghostly particles that barely interact with matter. In the first second of core collapse, a supernova releases 10^58 neutrinos. When SN 1987A exploded 168,000 light-years away, detectors on Earth registered exactly 24 neutrinos passing through. Twenty-four, from a storm of unfathomable density. Those neutrinos had been traveling since humans were beginning to paint on cave walls. They arrived three hours before the light, having left the core before the shockwave broke through the star's outer layers.

**Remembering Stan Woolsey**

Stanford Woolsey spent fifty years modeling how stars die. In the 1970s, he and his colleagues ran the first computer simulations that successfully produced supernova explosions — proving that the shockwave mechanism could work. His 1988 paper on SN 1987A, published months after the explosion, predicted details later confirmed: the pre-supernova star was a blue supergiant, its core had formed a neutron star. Woolsey died in 2024, but his models still guide observers. He once said the hardest part wasn't the physics but accepting that nature was "so extravagant, so wasteful" — building massive stars only to destroy them.

**Every Element Heavier Than Iron**

The core collapse creates conditions that exist nowhere else: temperatures exceeding 100 billion degrees, densities where atomic nuclei touch. Here, in microseconds, neutron capture processes create elements impossible in normal stellar burning. Gold, platinum, uranium — everything heavier than iron in your phone, your wedding ring, your bones — formed in supernova explosions or colliding neutron stars. The gold standard, literally, comes from stellar death. A single supernova can forge more gold than a sun-like star could produce burning for a trillion years. We are, demonstrably, made of dead stars.

**The Light Curve's Secret**

After the initial explosion, a supernova's brightness follows a predictable curve: a sharp rise, then a gradual decline over weeks. This fade isn't the explosion dimming — it's radioactive decay becoming visible. The explosion synthesizes unstable isotopes, especially nickel-56, which decays to cobalt-56, then to iron-56. Each decay releases gamma rays that heat the expanding debris, making it glow. By measuring how quickly a supernova fades, astronomers can calculate how much radioactive material it produced — a direct census of nucleosynthesis. The light curve is a receipt for creation.

**The Crab's Witness**

In 1054, Chinese and Japanese astronomers recorded a "guest star" visible in daylight for 23 days. The Crab Nebula, its remnant, now spans 11 light-years and continues expanding at 1,500 kilometers per second. At its center: a neutron star spinning 30 times per second, a stellar core so dense that a teaspoon would weigh a billion tons. The nebula itself glows from that pulsar's energy, not from the original explosion. We're watching the aftermath of something that finished nine and a half centuries ago, still bright, still violent, still teaching us how stars die.
