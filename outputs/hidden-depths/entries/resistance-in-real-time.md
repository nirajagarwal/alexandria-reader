# Resistance in Real Time

When you flip a light switch, electricity doesn't flood the wire like water bursting through a dam. It moves — but barely. The electrons themselves drift at about a millimeter per second, slower than a snail. Yet the light comes on instantly. What you're seeing isn't electrons racing to the bulb. It's something stranger: a wave of permission propagating at nearly the speed of light through a wire already full of waiting electrons. Resistance is what shapes that permission into useful work.

**The Copper Highway**

Inside a copper wire, electrons move like commuters in a packed subway car. Each electron is surrounded by other electrons and copper atoms vibrating with heat. To drift forward, an electron must squeeze past atoms, collide with them, transfer energy. These collisions are resistance. A thicker wire gives more paths around obstacles — lower resistance. A longer wire means more obstacles total — higher resistance. Temperature matters because hotter atoms vibrate more violently, blocking the path more effectively. At absolute zero, some materials lose resistance entirely: electrons glide without collision in a state called superconductivity.

**Georg Simon Ohm's Gamble**

In 1827, a German physics teacher named Georg Simon Ohm published a radical claim: the current flowing through a conductor is proportional to the voltage across it and inversely proportional to its resistance. The scientific establishment rejected it. His mathematics looked suspiciously like heat flow — too simple, too mechanical for the mysterious force of electricity. Ohm lost his teaching position. He survived on temporary jobs for years. Only in the 1840s, when other researchers independently confirmed his law, did recognition come. By then, Ohm's Law — V=IR — had become the foundation for electrical engineering. He was finally appointed to a professorship at 65.

**The Lightbulb's Secret**

Edison's breakthrough wasn't the first incandescent bulb. It was finding the right resistance. Too little resistance, and current surges through the filament without heating it enough to glow — wasted energy. Too much, and almost no current flows — darkness. Edison tested six thousand materials before settling on carbonized bamboo: high resistance that forced electrons to work so hard pushing through that the filament heated to 2,500°C, hot enough to glow white. Modern tungsten filaments follow the same principle. The resistance is carefully calibrated so that the electrical energy converts to light at precisely the rate that makes the bulb practical and economical.

**Where Energy Goes**

Resistance isn't loss — it's transformation. When electrons collide with atoms in the wire, they transfer kinetic energy. The atoms vibrate faster. That vibration is heat. This is why wires warm up, why your phone charger gets hot, why heating elements glow. Power companies hate this. In long-distance power lines, resistance means billions of dollars of electricity radiating away as waste heat before reaching homes. That's why they transmit at extremely high voltages: higher voltage means lower current for the same power (P=VI), and since heat loss depends on current squared (P=I²R), reducing current dramatically cuts waste. Step-down transformers near your home convert it back to safe voltages.

**The Transistor's Dance**

A transistor is resistance made controllable. It's a valve where a small voltage changes the resistance through a semiconductor pathway. Apply voltage to the gate, and resistance drops — current flows. Remove it, and resistance spikes — current stops. This binary switching, billions of times per second, is computation. The processor reading these words right now contains billions of microscopic resistors, each one a transistor whose resistance changes according to the logic of your software. The heat your laptop exhausts is resistance made visible: all those collisions, all that transformed electricity, radiating away.

**Lightning's Path**

Lightning doesn't choose the shortest path to ground — it chooses the path of least resistance. Air is an excellent insulator, but when electric fields get strong enough, electrons rip free from air molecules, creating a channel of ionized plasma with much lower resistance. Lightning follows this channel, branching where plasma branches, zigging where resistance zags. Sometimes that path runs through a tree (wet wood conducts better than dry air), sometimes through a person (salt water in our bodies conducts well), sometimes through nothing but sky until it finds a way down. The visible flash is the resistance of air converted to light and sound as 30,000 amperes force through.
