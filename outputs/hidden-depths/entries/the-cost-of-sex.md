# The Cost of Sex

Sex is expensive. Not socially or economically — biologically. Every creature that reproduces sexually pays a steep price: they must find a mate, coordinate reproduction, and pass on only half their genes to each offspring. Asexual organisms simply copy themselves, keeping all their genes and reproducing twice as fast. So why does nearly every complex organism bother with sex at all?

**The Two-Fold Cost**

In a population of asexual females, every individual produces offspring. Introduce sexual reproduction and suddenly half the population — males — produces no offspring directly. A sexual female uses resources to create sons who will never give birth. Her asexual neighbor, producing only daughters, doubles her genetic representation each generation. This is the "two-fold cost of sex," identified by John Maynard Smith in 1978. Sexual reproduction should be eliminated by natural selection within fifty generations. Yet it persists across almost all complex life.

**The Red Queen**

Leigh Van Valen, studying fossil extinction patterns in 1973, noticed something strange: a species' age didn't predict its survival. Ancient lineages died at the same rate as recent ones, as if running constantly just to stay in place. He called this the Red Queen hypothesis, after the character in *Through the Looking-Glass* who tells Alice, "It takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place." Sex, Van Valen argued, is how species run. Parasites and diseases evolve rapidly. Asexual organisms produce identical offspring — identical targets. Sexual reproduction shuffles genes, creating variation. Each generation, some offspring have new combinations that parasites haven't yet adapted to defeat.

**Alexey Kondrashov's Ratchet**

In 1988, Russian geneticist Alexey Kondrashov calculated what happens to mutations in asexual populations. Each individual accumulates small copying errors. Without sex, every mutation passes to all offspring. Bad mutations pile up — what's called Muller's ratchet, after Hermann Muller. The population's genetic quality clicks down, notch by notch, irreversibly. Sex breaks the ratchet. When two parents combine genes, some offspring get fewer mutations than either parent carried. The worst combinations die; the best survive. Sex is quality control, purging errors that asexual reproduction can only accumulate.

**The Bdelloid Exception**

Bdelloid rotifers are microscopic animals that have not had sex in 80 million years. They dry up when conditions are harsh, blowing as dust, then rehydrate and continue living. Matthew Meselson discovered in 2000 that their extreme desiccation tears their DNA apart. When they rehydrate, DNA from different individuals — and even other species — gets incorporated as cells repair the breaks. They've evolved a different solution: horizontal gene transfer that mimics sex's genetic shuffling without needing mates. Their exception proves the rule: if you can't shuffle genes sexually, you must find another way or accumulate your way to extinction.

**The Peacock's Tail**

Sexual reproduction creates another cost: sexual selection. Males must compete for females, leading to elaborate traits that harm survival. The peacock's tail is heavy, bright, and alerts predators. Yet it persists because peahens choose males with the most extravagant displays. Amotz Zahavi's handicap principle, proposed in 1975, explains why: only healthy males can afford such costly ornaments. The tail is honest advertising of genetic quality. Sex doesn't just maintain genes — it judges them, ensuring that every generation undergoes scrutiny that asexual reproduction never faces.

**The Geographic Pattern**

Near the poles, asexual reproduction becomes more common. Many arctic plants reproduce through runners or bulbs, not seeds. Some cold-water fish are asexual. The pattern holds across species: where parasite diversity is low and conditions stable, asexual reproduction succeeds. In the tropics, where parasite pressure is intense and competition fierce, sex dominates. The geographic distribution maps the cost-benefit calculation. Sex isn't universally necessary — it's necessary where the biological arms race runs fastest, where standing still means extinction.
