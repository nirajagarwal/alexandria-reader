# Slime Mold Intelligence

A single-celled organism with no brain solves mazes, builds efficient transport networks, and makes decisions that parallel human engineering. The slime mold *Physarum polycephalum* challenges our assumptions about what thinking requires. Intelligence, it turns out, doesn't need neurons — or even separate cells.

**A Body That Remembers**

*Physarum* is technically a plasmodium: thousands of nuclei sharing one giant cell that can span several feet. It moves by rhythmic contractions, pulsing cytoplasm through branching tubes at speeds up to 4 centimeters per hour. When researchers place food sources in a pattern matching Tokyo's suburbs and set the slime mold at the city center, it builds a network nearly identical to the actual Tokyo rail system — optimizing for both efficiency and redundancy. It accomplishes in hours what took human engineers decades and billions of dollars. The organism has no map, no plan, no central processor. It simply grows, retracts unsuccessful branches, and reinforces successful paths until an elegant solution emerges.

**Learning Without Neurons**

Tetsu Saigusa's 2008 experiment seemed simple: blow cold air (which *Physarum* dislikes) at regular intervals as the organism crosses a bridge toward food. After three repetitions, something remarkable happened. When the air stopped, the slime mold hesitated at the expected time anyway — then continued when nothing came. It had learned a temporal pattern and formed a predictive model. The mechanism appears to be chemical: the stress of cold air triggers molecular changes that propagate through the cytoplasm. These changes persist, creating a kind of distributed memory written in the concentration gradients of signaling molecules. No synapses. No neural circuits. Just chemistry that encodes experience.

**Toshiyuki Nakagaki's Mazes**

In 2000, Toshiyuki Nakagaki at Hokkaido University placed *Physarum* at the entrance of a maze with food at the exit. The slime mold initially filled the entire maze, exploring every path. Then it began retracting from dead ends, consolidating into a single thick tube that traced the shortest route from entrance to exit. Give it a maze with multiple valid paths and it finds them all, maintaining redundant connections in case one route is damaged. This isn't random exploration followed by luck. The organism is computing optimal solutions through a process called "spatial externalization of memory" — it literally embodies its problem-solving in physical structure. Each tube's thickness represents the value of that route.

**The Blob's Fusion**

Cut a slime mold in pieces and each fragment survives independently. But place two *Physarum* fragments together and they recognize each other as members of the same strain — then fuse into one organism. When Audrey Dussutour fused a trained slime mold (taught to cross a bridge covered in bitter salt) with a naive one, the combined organism inherited the learned behavior. The trained fragment physically transferred its chemical memory state to the untrained one. Knowledge passed not through genetics or neural signals, but through direct cytoplasmic contact. The implications are unsettling: in some sense, these organisms can teach each other by merging.

**Evolutionary Antiquity**

Slime molds belong to the Amoebozoa, a group that diverged from our lineage roughly 1.5 billion years ago — long before neurons evolved. They inhabited Earth for a billion years before the first animal drew breath. This means their problem-solving abilities didn't arise as a simplified version of brain-based intelligence. They represent a completely independent evolutionary solution to environmental challenges. Studying *Physarum* doesn't show us how simple brains work. It reveals that intelligence itself is substrate-independent — a pattern that can emerge from any system capable of forming, remembering, and responding to chemical gradients over time.

**Collective Computation**

What looks like a single organism is actually a liquid computer. Each region of the plasmodium acts as both memory and processor, with information flowing through the pulse patterns of cytoplasmic streaming. When one area encounters food, the contractions in that region speed up, pulling resources toward the discovery. When an area proves fruitless, contractions slow and the organism retracts. The result is a distributed optimization algorithm running in real time, with no central control. This architecture is now inspiring actual computers: "physarum machines" that simulate slime mold networks to solve routing problems in telecommunications. Biology-inspired computing usually means mimicking brains. Here, we're learning from something that predates brains by a billion years.
