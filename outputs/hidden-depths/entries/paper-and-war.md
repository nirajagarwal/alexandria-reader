# Paper and War

The mass production of paper didn't just enable bureaucracy and literacy — it fundamentally transformed how wars could be fought. Before cheap paper, armies moved slowly, commanded by officers who could see the battlefield. After, they exploded in size and complexity, coordinated by written orders across distances that made direct command impossible. The history of modern warfare is inseparable from the history of paper becoming cheap enough to throw away.

**The Prussian Revolution**

In the 1850s, the Prussian General Staff invented something new: war planning as a literary genre. Officers spent peacetime writing elaborate contingency plans for hypothetical conflicts, complete with railway timetables and supply schedules. When war came in 1866 and 1870, these paper plans allowed Prussia to mobilize hundreds of thousands of men in days, moving them to precise locations via written orders. Their opponents, still relying on verbal commands and ad hoc coordination, were overwhelmed before they understood what had happened. Paper had made complexity manageable.

**The Quartermaster's Problem**

Armies march on their stomachs, and before paper, that limited their size absolutely. A Roman legion needed supplies within visual range or short messenger distance. The Duke of Wellington personally approved every major supply decision in Spain. But the American Civil War deployed 2.1 million Union soldiers across a continent, supplied by a web of contracts, requisitions, invoices, and shipping manifests. The Union Quartermaster General's department generated more paperwork in a month than Napoleon's entire administration produced in a year. Paper made scale possible.

**Helmuth von Moltke's Doctrine**

Moltke the Elder, chief of the Prussian General Staff from 1857 to 1888, understood that paper changed command itself. No plan survives contact with the enemy, he said, so he developed Auftragstaktik: mission-type orders. Write the objective, not the method. Trust subordinates with written guidance, not verbal micromanagement. His officers carried orders they could read, interpret, and adapt independently. The paper itself became a tool for decentralized decision-making, allowing armies to operate coherently across distances that made real-time command impossible.

**The Killing Forms**

World War I perfected the bureaucracy of death. Every shell fired required a written request, approved up the chain, recorded in triplicate. Artillery barrage patterns were plotted on paper maps, translated into firing tables, distributed to gun crews. The Battle of the Somme consumed 1.7 million shells in the opening week — each one tracked by serial number in ledgers that still exist. The paper trail allowed unprecedented coordination, but it also made war industrial, reducing human decisions to processed forms. Paper didn't just enable the scale; it made the scale feel manageable.

**Information Warfare**

Code-breaking in World War II was fundamentally a paper problem. The volume of intercepted messages was so vast that only systematic written analysis could find patterns. Bletchley Park employed 9,000 people, most doing paper-based clerical work: transcribing intercepts, cross-referencing indices, maintaining card catalogs. Alan Turing's machines were important, but the institutional infrastructure was paper. The Allies read Enigma not because they were smarter, but because they could process more paper faster. Intelligence became an industrial product.

**The Digital Transition**

Modern militaries still drown in paper despite computers. The U.S. military generates over a billion pages annually. Why? Because paper creates accountability through physical trace. A digital file can be altered; a signed order cannot. Legal authorities still rest on paper documents. And in combat, paper works when batteries die, networks fail, and EMP weapons detonate. The military's resistance to going paperless isn't backwards — it's recognition that paper's physical permanence remains a feature, not a bug, in high-stakes coordination.
