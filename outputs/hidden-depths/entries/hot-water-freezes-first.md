# Hot Water Freezes First

Under the right conditions, a container of hot water placed in a freezer will turn to ice before an identical container of cold water. This fact contradicts basic thermodynamics — the hot water has further to cool — yet it happens reliably in kitchens and laboratories worldwide. The phenomenon has been observed for millennia, denied by physics for centuries, and remains incompletely explained today. Why should taking the long route sometimes get you there first?

**Mpemba's Ice Cream**

In 1963, a Tanzanian schoolboy named Erasto Mpemba was making ice cream in cooking class. Impatient, he put his hot milk mixture in the freezer before it cooled. When he checked later, his ice cream had frozen solid while his classmates' pre-cooled mixtures were still liquid. His teacher dismissed it as confusion. A visiting physicist, Denis Osborne, heard Mpemba's insistence and decided to test it. They published together in 1969. The effect now bears Mpemba's name — a reminder that observing carefully matters more than credentials.

**Ancient Knowledge**

Aristotle noted it in *Meteorologica*: "The fact that water has previously been warmed contributes to its freezing quickly." Medieval alchemists documented it. Francis Bacon mentioned it in 1620. Descartes tried to explain it with vortices. But as thermodynamics formalized in the 19th century, the observation was dismissed as experimental error or folk myth. The idea that heat could be an advantage in cooling violated the field's core logic. For three centuries, textbooks ignored what anyone with a freezer could verify.

**Evaporation's Price**

Hot water loses mass faster through evaporation. Less water means less total heat to extract, which can offset the higher starting temperature. In open containers, this effect is substantial — a hot sample can lose 15-20% of its mass before freezing begins. But the Mpemba effect occurs in closed containers too, where evaporation is impossible. Mass loss explains some cases, but not the phenomenon itself. It's a contributing factor, not the mechanism.

**Supercooling's Trap**

Pure water can cool below 0°C without freezing — a metastable state called supercooling. It needs a nucleation site (an imperfection, a dust particle, a scratch) to trigger crystallization. Cold water is more likely to supercool than hot water. Hot water, through its cooling journey, deposits dissolved gases and minerals on container walls — creating nucleation sites. It also experiences more convection, stirring up existing nucleation points. When measurement begins at 0°C, the cold water may still be liquid while the hot water has already triggered freezing.

**Hydrogen Bond Networks**

Water molecules form temporary hydrogen bonds in constantly shifting networks. Hot water's bonds are stretched and strained. When cooling begins, some researchers propose these strained configurations relax into ice's crystal structure more readily than the denser hydrogen-bond networks in cold water. The hot water's molecular disorganization might paradoxically organize into ice faster. This hypothesis is controversial — the mechanism isn't proven — but experiments measuring bond energy distributions show differences between samples with hot versus cold starting temperatures.

**The Unresolved Question**

No single mechanism explains all observations. The effect is real but maddeningly inconsistent — it depends on container shape, volume, freezer temperature, water purity, dissolved gases, and factors not yet identified. Some experimental setups show it clearly; others don't. In 2016, the Royal Society of Chemistry offered a prize for the best explanation; the winning entry invoked convection patterns and supercooling but acknowledged gaps. Physics hasn't solved this. A phenomenon anyone can observe in a home freezer still waits for complete understanding.
