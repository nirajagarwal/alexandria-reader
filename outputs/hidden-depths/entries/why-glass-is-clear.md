# Why Glass Is Clear

Glass surrounds us — in windows, screens, bottles, lenses. We use it precisely because we can see through it. But glass is made from sand, which is opaque. Heat sand until it melts, let it cool, and suddenly light passes through as if nothing is there. This transformation from opacity to transparency seems impossible. It reveals something fundamental about how matter interacts with light, and why most solid things block our view while a few strange materials don't.

**The Electron Problem**

Light is electromagnetic radiation. When it hits matter, it interacts with electrons orbiting atoms. In most materials, electrons absorb photons of visible light, jump to higher energy levels, then release that energy as heat. The light is gone. But glass is made primarily of silicon dioxide — the same stuff as sand and quartz. In this molecular arrangement, the electrons are bound so tightly to their atoms that visible light doesn't have enough energy to move them. The photons pass through without being absorbed. The material is transparent not because light ignores it, but because its electrons can't grab hold.

**The Grain Boundary**

Sand isn't transparent even though it's silicon dioxide. The difference is structure. Sand is crystalline — millions of tiny quartz crystals packed together. Each crystal is actually transparent. But light bends slightly when crossing from one crystal to another, and at each of thousands of boundaries, some light scatters. The cumulative effect is opacity. When you melt sand into glass, you destroy the crystal structure. Glass is amorphous — a frozen liquid with no repeating pattern, no grain boundaries. Light encounters only smooth, continuous material. It travels straight through because there's nothing to scatter it.

**The Ultraviolet Cutoff**

Glass is transparent to visible light but not to all light. Ultraviolet photons carry more energy — enough to excite those tightly-bound electrons. Glass blocks UV light, which is why you can't get a suntan through a window. Different glasses block different wavelengths depending on composition. Add iron oxide and you get green glass that blocks some visible red. Add lead and you get crystal that's especially transparent because lead's heavy electrons interact differently with light. The transparency of glass isn't absolute. It's a window in the electromagnetic spectrum, carefully defined by electron energy levels.

**Angelo Barovier's Secret**

For most of history, glass was colored and cloudy. Then in 1450, Angelo Barovier of Murano created *cristallo* — glass so clear it looked like rock crystal. His method remained secret for decades. The key was purity. Medieval glass contained iron, carbon, and other impurities with loosely-bound electrons that absorbed light. Barovier used the purest materials he could find and added manganese dioxide to neutralize remaining iron. He created glass with so few absorbing defects that light passed through almost perfectly. Clarity wasn't about the silicon dioxide itself. It was about removing everything else.

**The Speed Change**

Light slows down in glass. In vacuum it travels at 300,000 kilometers per second. In glass, about 200,000. This happens because light isn't really "slowing" — it's being absorbed and re-emitted by electrons billions of times per second. Each absorption-emission cycle takes time. The light you see emerging from glass has been destroyed and recreated countless times during its journey. The transparency of glass depends on these electrons re-emitting photons in exactly the same direction they came from. Any material where electrons re-emit randomly would be opaque. Glass is clear because its electrons are both weak enough to let most light pass and precise enough to preserve direction.

**The Obsidian Exception**

Volcanic glass — obsidian — is opaque and black. It's still glass, still amorphous silicon dioxide. But it contains enough iron, magnesium, and other impurities that their electrons absorb visible light. The structure allows transparency; the chemistry prevents it. This reveals the dual requirement: glass is clear because its structure eliminates scattering *and* its composition eliminates absorption. Both must be true. Amorphous structure opens the door. Chemical purity walks through it.
