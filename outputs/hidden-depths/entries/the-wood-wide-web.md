# The Wood Wide Web

Stand in a forest and you're standing above an internet. Beneath your feet, trees are trading resources, sending warnings, and nurturing their young through a network that makes our fiber optics look crude. The forest isn't a collection of individuals. It's a superorganism, and the fungi threading through the soil are its nervous system.

**The Cable Layers**

Mycorrhizal fungi wrap around tree roots in sheaths or penetrate between root cells, sometimes occupying 30% of a root's tissue. One pinch of forest soil contains several miles of these fungal threads—hyphae finer than root hairs, reaching into spaces roots can't access. The fungus trades minerals and water for the sugars only trees can make through photosynthesis. But this isn't a simple swap between two partners. A single fungal network can connect dozens of trees across acres, creating a mesh that lets nutrients flow from any tree to any other.

**The Market Makers**

In 1997, ecologist Suzanne Simard proved that paper birch and Douglas fir exchange carbon through their fungal connections. She grew trees in pairs, covered some with opaque bags, others with clear ones, and traced isotope-labeled carbon moving underground. The shaded birch received carbon from the sunlit fir. When conditions reversed in fall—birch still photosynthesizing, fir dormant—the flow reversed too. The trees were balancing each other's books through the season. Later work showed similar exchanges across species boundaries throughout forests: oak to maple, pine to spruce, a continuous reallocation of resources.

**The Mother Trees**

Old trees function as hubs in the network, connected to hundreds of others through especially dense fungal links. Simard calls them "mother trees." They don't just participate in the network—they shape it. When young seedlings establish nearby, mother trees pump carbon into them through the fungi, particularly into their own offspring, which they appear to recognize chemically. Seedlings growing in the shadow of large trees, where photosynthesis should barely sustain them, thrive on this subsidy. Cut down the mother trees and the death rate of nearby seedlings spikes. The forest is investing in its own succession.

**The Warning System**

When giraffes browse on African acacias, trees downwind begin pumping tannins into their leaves within minutes—long before the giraffes reach them. The first trees release ethylene gas, a chemical alarm. In 2013, researchers found temperate forests use the fungal network for similar warnings. When Douglas firs are attacked by budworm, their neighbors increase production of defensive enzymes before the insects arrive. The warning travels through the fungi, apparently as chemical signals, though the exact molecules remain unknown. Trees receiving the message increase their resistance to subsequent attack. The forest, warned, braces itself.

**The Carbon Vault**

Every forest contains more carbon underground than above. The mycorrhizal fungi themselves hold roughly 70% of the carbon stored in forest soil. When trees pump carbon into these networks, much of it doesn't flow to other trees—it stays in the fungal tissue or leaks into the surrounding soil as organic compounds. In effect, trees are paying fungi to build carbon banks. The system is so effective that researchers estimate mycorrhizal networks hold 5 billion tons of carbon globally—equivalent to 20% of annual human CO₂ emissions. Protect the network and you protect a planetary carbon vault.

**The Broken Links**

Clearcuts sever the fungal network. Spores remain in soil, but the living hyphal connections die within months without host trees. When foresters replant, seedlings must rebuild the network from scratch—no mother trees subsidizing them, no warnings flowing about pests, no fungal highways already laid. Survival rates drop. Growth slows. It can take decades for the complexity to return, and some fungal species, the long-lived ones forming the network's backbone, may never reestablish. We've been harvesting forests as if they were tree farms. We were cutting the wiring.
