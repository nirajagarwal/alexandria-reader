# Light Through Glass

When sunlight hits a window, most of it passes straight through. We call glass "transparent" and move on. But light doesn't simply go through glass — it's absorbed, re-emitted, slowed down, and bent. Every pane is doing something strange to light, and the clarity we take for granted is the result of quantum mechanics playing out trillions of times per second.

**Glass Isn't a Thing**

Glass has no crystal structure. Pour molten sand and it cools into a frozen liquid — atoms locked in disorder. This matters because light interacts with matter through its atomic structure. Crystals scatter light predictably; glass, being structurally chaotic, scatters it barely at all. The photons that enter emerge in nearly the same direction they started. We experience this molecular confusion as transparency.

**Every Photon is Absorbed**

Light doesn't pass through glass. Each photon is absorbed by an electron, which jumps to a higher energy state, then immediately re-emits an identical photon as it drops back down. This happens in about 10⁻¹⁵ seconds — so fast that we perceive continuous transmission. The glass you're looking through has destroyed and recreated every photon thousands of times across its thickness. You're seeing new light that remembers the old.

**The Speed Changes**

Light travels through vacuum at 299,792,458 meters per second. In glass, it slows to about 200,000 kilometers per second. This isn't friction — it's the cascading delay of absorption and re-emission. Light doesn't "slow down" so much as it takes a longer path through a forest of interactions. This speed change is why lenses work: light entering glass at an angle bends, a phenomenon Willebrord Snellius quantified in 1621 using ratios of these speeds.

**Ibn al-Haytham Saw It First**

In 1021, the Iraqi mathematician Ibn al-Haytham wrote *Book of Optics*, the first work to explain light as something that travels from objects to eyes, not the reverse. He demonstrated that light bends at boundaries between air and glass, refracts through lenses, and reflects predictably. His experiments with glass spheres and crystal shapes founded the science of optics eight centuries before Europe caught up. Every lens owes something to his deliberate investigations.

**UV Stops Cold**

Glass is transparent to visible light but opaque to most ultraviolet. The electrons in silicon dioxide can't absorb and re-emit UV photons — the energy gaps in their structure are wrong. UV photons hit glass and their energy converts to heat, never emerging. This is why you can't get a sunburn through a closed window. The glass isn't "blocking" UV; it's consuming it. Different glasses have different cutoff frequencies depending on their composition. Quartz glass, purer than window glass, lets UV through.

**Color Lives in the Edges**

White light entering a prism separates into rainbow because different frequencies slow differently in glass. Red light, with its longer wavelength, interacts less with the glass structure and slows least. Violet, higher energy and shorter wavelength, slows most. They refract at slightly different angles — a fraction of a degree, but enough. Newton demonstrated this in 1666, then proved colors weren't "added" by the glass by recombining the spectrum back into white with a second prism. The glass reveals what was always there, traveling together.
