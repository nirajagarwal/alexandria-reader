# The Moon's Pull

The ocean rises and falls twice a day, pulled by a force quarter-million miles away. We call them tides, treat them as background — something beaches have. But tidal forces shape far more than coastlines. They slow Earth's spin, push the Moon away, and govern which planets can hold moons at all. The Moon's pull is remaking both bodies, and has been since they were born.

**Two Bulges, Not One**

The ocean doesn't just rise on the Moon-facing side. It bulges on both sides of Earth simultaneously. The Moon pulls water toward itself on the near side, yes — but it pulls Earth itself slightly harder than the water on the far side, leaving that water behind. Two bulges, always opposite each other. The solid Earth moves through them as it spins, which is why most coasts get two high tides daily.

**The Friction Tax**

Those bulges drag across the seafloor and continental shelves. The friction is tiny — but over billions of years, tiny friction becomes a brake. Earth's rotation is slowing by about 2.3 milliseconds per century. Four hundred million years ago, in the Devonian, a day lasted 21.8 hours. The year had 400 days. Coral fossils from that era show daily and yearly growth rings proving it. We're still slowing down.

**Where the Energy Goes**

Energy can't vanish. Earth's lost rotational energy doesn't disappear — it transfers to the Moon's orbit as angular momentum. The Moon is receding from Earth at 3.8 centimeters per year, measured precisely by laser ranging to mirrors astronauts left on the surface. Four billion years ago, just after the giant impact that formed it, the Moon loomed perhaps fifteen times larger in the sky, orbiting in just 20 hours. Each body is still adjusting to that violent beginning.

**George Darwin's Deduction**

In 1879, Charles Darwin's son George — a mathematician, not a biologist — worked out the mathematics of tidal evolution. He realized that if tides pushed the Moon outward, running the process backward meant the Moon must have once been much closer. He calculated it had probably separated from Earth itself. The giant impact theory proved him half-right: the Moon did form from Earth's mantle, but ripped out by collision, not fission. Still, George Darwin saw in the equations what wouldn't be observed for another century.

**The Roche Limit**

Tidal forces don't just push moons away. Bring a moon close enough — typically about 2.5 times the planet's radius — and tidal forces tear it apart. The planet pulls harder on the near side than the far side, and eventually the difference exceeds the moon's own gravity holding it together. Saturn's rings exist because something — probably several somethings — crossed this threshold, called the Roche limit after Édouard Roche who calculated it in 1848. Mars's moon Phobos orbits inside its Roche limit, slowly fragmenting.

**Tidal Locking**

The Moon doesn't rotate from our perspective — the same face always points Earthward. This isn't coincidence. Early in its history, the Moon spun independently. But Earth's gravity raised tidal bulges in the Moon's rocky body, creating internal friction. That friction acted as a brake until the Moon's rotation synchronized with its orbit: tidally locked. Earth is being locked too, just more slowly. In perhaps 50 billion years — if the Sun doesn't expand and swallow both first — Earth and Moon will face each other permanently, their day and month both about 47 current days long.
