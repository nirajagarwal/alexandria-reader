# Pilgrim Time

When we imagine the Pilgrims landing at Plymouth Rock in 1620, we picture a scene lit by November sun. But the light falling on that Massachusetts shore wasn't the November light we know. England had switched calendars in 1752, and when historians backdated events, they created a peculiar distortion: we remember the Pilgrims in a month they never experienced. The question of when they actually arrived opens onto something stranger — how we built our relationship with time itself, and what we lost in the translation.

**The Julian Drift**

Julius Caesar's calendar, introduced in 45 BCE, calculated a year as 365.25 days. Close, but not quite right — the actual solar year is 365.2422 days. That 11-minute annual error accumulated. By 1620, the Julian calendar ran ten days ahead of the seasons. The Pilgrims landed on November 11 by their reckoning, but the solar calendar — the rhythm of actual seasons — called it November 21. Spring equinoxes wandered earlier. Easter, tied to astronomical events, drifted from its moorings. The gap widened like a wound that wouldn't close.

**The Papal Decree**

Pope Gregory XIII corrected course in 1582. His astronomers, led by Aloysius Lilius, proposed removing ten days from October 1582 — Thursday the 4th became Friday the 15th. They refined the leap year rules: years divisible by 100 wouldn't be leap years unless also divisible by 400. The math worked. But Protestant England refused to follow a papal decree. For 170 years, England lived in calendar exile, celebrating Christmas eleven days after Catholic Europe. When the Pilgrims sailed, England was an island not just of geography but of time itself.

**The English Conversion**

By 1752, the gap had widened to eleven days. Parliament finally surrendered to astronomical reality. Wednesday, September 2, 1752, became Thursday, September 14. Riots allegedly erupted — "Give us our eleven days!" — though evidence is thin. The real violence was quieter: every date before September 1752 exists in two forms. George Washington was born February 11, 1731 (Old Style) but celebrated February 22, 1732 (New Style) after the switch. The past fractured into parallel chronologies, each technically correct, both historically true.

**The Double Dating**

For those 170 years, English documents often carried double dates: "March 24, 1620/1" — Old Style 1620, New Style 1621. The year itself was contested. England began the new year on March 25 (Lady Day, the Annunciation). The Gregorian calendar started January 1. So January through March existed in a liminal zone, belonging to different years depending on who was counting. The Mayflower departed in September 1620 (everyone agreed), but the Pilgrims first sighted Cape Cod on November 9, 1620 (Old Style) — which might also be called November 19, 1620 (Gregorian), or by strict Old Style logic, November 9, 1619/20.

**The Modern Memory**

We remember the Mayflower landing on November 11, Old Style — the date the Pilgrims themselves recorded. But November 11 in their calendar was astronomically November 21 in ours. Thanksgiving, set by Lincoln in 1863 as the last Thursday in November, lands near where the Pilgrims actually experienced their arrival — late November's angle of light, its bare trees and cold harbors. The holiday accidentally corrected what historical record preserves. We celebrate in their season, not on their date. The calendar reformed, but memory kept the old numbers.

**The Question of Now**

Even corrected, the Gregorian calendar still drifts. We'll need one fewer leap day every 3,200 years. Atomic clocks measure time more precisely than Earth's rotation, so we add leap seconds — 27 since 1972. The Pilgrims landed in a moment their calendar couldn't quite capture, and we remember that moment in numbers that don't quite align with the experience. Time itself is exact. Our containers for it leak. Every date in history is a story we tell about when something happened, and the story changes depending on who's counting, and when they counted it, and whether they trusted the Pope.
