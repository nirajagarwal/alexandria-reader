# When Magnets Die

A magnet can last centuries, holding paper clips to your refrigerator through decades of use. Yet every magnet carries within it the seeds of its own undoing. The force that seems so permanent is actually a precarious alignment of countless atomic compasses, all pointing the same way — until something makes them stop.

**The Critical Temperature**

Heat a magnet to 770°C and it loses its grip instantly. This isn't damage or wear — it's a phase transition as absolute as ice melting. Above this Curie point, named for Pierre Curie who discovered it in 1895, thermal energy overwhelms the quantum forces holding electron spins aligned. The atoms still contain magnetic moments, but they tumble randomly, their fields canceling out. Cool the metal back down and the magnetism doesn't return by itself; you've erased the pattern. A forgotten magnet in a house fire becomes ordinary steel.

**The Slow Fade**

Even at room temperature, magnets weaken. Stray magnetic fields nudge domains out of alignment. Mechanical shock — dropping a magnet — jolts atoms loose from their neighbors' influence. Cheaper magnets lose 1-2% of their strength per decade just sitting on a shelf. The edge domains, where magnetic regions meet, are especially vulnerable. They're like border towns with divided loyalties, easily flipped by passing influences. This is why strong permanent magnets are stored with "keeper" bars across their poles — creating a closed magnetic circuit that shields internal domains from external interference.

**Demagnetization By Design**

Jewelers and watchmakers routinely kill magnets. A mechanical watch exposed to a strong field — an MRI machine, a loudspeaker — becomes magnetized and runs erratically. The hairspring, coiled and recoiling thousands of times per hour, develops sticky spots where coils attract each other. The fix: pass the watch through a decreasing alternating magnetic field. The domains flip rapidly — north, south, north, south — with each reversal weaker than the last, until the oscillations fade to randomness. The watch emerges demagnetized, its steel rendered magnetically neutral again.

**Neodymium's Weakness**

The strongest permanent magnets — rare-earth magnets made from neodymium, iron, and boron — are also the most fragile. They shatter if dropped. They corrode in humid air, their nickel coating the only thing between power and rust. Most critically, their Curie point sits at just 310°C. Leave a neodymium magnet in a hot car on a summer day, and it weakens noticeably. Use one in a motor that heats up during operation, and its strength degrades irreversibly. Engineers working with these magnets must constantly balance their extraordinary power against their vulnerability to the conditions that make motors and generators hot.

**The Oldest Magnets**

Lodestones — naturally magnetized magnetite — can hold their field for millennia. The ancient Greeks knew them; Chinese navigators used them in compasses by the 11th century. These rocks were magnetized by lightning strikes, their domains aligned in microseconds by enormous current. What preserves them is isolation: buried in earth, untouched by other fields, stable in temperature. Excavate a lodestone from an archaeological site and it still pulls iron. But the same stone, passed through many hands, dropped, heated by the sun, gradually loses what the lightning gave it. Even stone magnets, given enough time and disturbance, forget which way is north.

**Eternal Temporary**

Every permanent magnet is actually temporary — just on a timescale longer than human attention spans. The very thing that makes magnetism useful — that it persists without power, without maintenance — also makes its eventual decay invisible until it matters. The refrigerator magnet that no longer holds. The compass that points slightly wrong. The hard drive losing data not to corruption but to simple drift. They all eventually succumb to entropy's patient work, unaligning what was once ordered, returning organized fields to chaos. Nothing stays magnetized forever.
