# Cities Think Like Brains

When traffic slows on one street, it speeds up on another. When a neighborhood gentrifies, distant rents rise. When a factory closes, restaurants fail blocks away. Cities seem to process information, respond to damage, even learn from experience. The metaphor feels apt — but it's not a metaphor. The mathematics are identical.

**The Same Equations**

In 1952, Alan Turing wrote down equations describing how chemical signals spread and interact to form patterns in embryos — spots on leopards, stripes on zebras. In 2010, urban theorist Michael Batty noticed something strange: the same equations, unchanged, describe how crime hotspots form, how retail clusters emerge, how slums sprawl. Both systems follow reaction-diffusion dynamics. A stimulus appears (neurotransmitter, new business). It spreads. It triggers responses. Patterns emerge. The mathematics don't care whether the substrate is neurons or neighborhoods.

**Geoffrey West's Discovery**

Physicist Geoffrey West spent years measuring cities: their road networks, electrical grids, patent outputs, crime rates. He found power laws everywhere. Double a city's population, and you don't just double its restaurants — you get 2.15 times as many. Patents increase 2.27 times. Crime rises 2.16 times. The exponents held across cultures, climates, centuries. West realized: cities scale like organisms. Not because they're designed that way. Because energy and information flow through constrained networks in both, and physics doesn't negotiate.

**The Suprachiasmatic Nucleus**

Deep in your hypothalamus, 20,000 neurons fire in sync to generate your circadian rhythm. They're not following orders from a central clock. Each neuron has its own period — some run 23 hours, others 25. They synchronize by watching their neighbors, adjusting their firing rates until the whole network pulses as one. This is how New York's theater district maintains its schedule, how Mumbai's dabbawalas deliver 200,000 lunches daily with one error per 6 million deliveries. No master plan. Local interactions generating global coherence.

**Phantom Traffic Jams**

A driver taps their brakes on a crowded highway. The car behind brakes harder. The one behind that brakes harder still. The wave propagates backward at 12 mph, creating a jam with no accident, no obstacle — just a shockwave of information traveling through a network at capacity. Neuroscientists call these "spreading depolarizations" when they happen in the brain during migraines. The mechanism is identical: excitable systems amplifying and propagating disturbances. Your migraine aura moves across your visual cortex at exactly the speed phantom traffic crawls.

**Jane Jacobs Saw It First**

In 1961, Jane Jacobs described cities as "problems of organized complexity" — too many variables for simple cause-and-effect, but patterned, not random. She was describing emergence before the term existed. She noticed how sidewalks stay safe not through policing but through "eyes on the street" — distributed monitoring, like immune systems detecting pathogens. How shops depend on foot traffic that depends on residential density that depends on mixed use that brings shops. Feedback loops. No neighborhood "decides" to thrive. The decision emerges from interactions too numerous and rapid to trace.

**Where the Metaphor Breaks**

Cities don't have cell membranes. Information leaks everywhere. A brain can't annex neighboring skulls when it needs more processing power. Cities can sprawl. Neurons fire in milliseconds; neighborhoods gentrify over decades. The timescales differ by factors of millions. And yet: a neuron responding to its neighbors through chemical signals and a restaurant responding to foot traffic through profit margins are both processing information about their environment and adjusting behavior accordingly. The implementation differs. The computation is the same.
