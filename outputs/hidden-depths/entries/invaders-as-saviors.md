# Invaders as Saviors

We talk about invasive species as ecological disasters — and many are. But some arrivals transform degraded landscapes into functioning ecosystems again, filling niches that native species abandoned or can no longer fill. The same processes that make invasion destructive can, in wounded places, become restoration. The line between ecological villain and unlikely hero depends entirely on context.

**The Goat That Saved Galápagos**

When giant tortoises vanished from parts of the Galápagos, their ecological role — dispersing seeds, clearing vegetation, creating disturbance patterns — disappeared with them. Then feral goats arrived, eating the same plants, trampling the same paths, maintaining the same clearings. By 2005, conservationists faced a paradox: removing the invasive goats might collapse what remained of the degraded ecosystem. Some islands kept small goat populations specifically to maintain the disturbance regime until tortoises could be reintroduced. The invader was holding the niche open.

**Tamarisk and the Southwestern Flycatcher**

For decades, tamarisk trees were public enemy number one in the American Southwest — water-guzzling invaders choking rivers where native willows and cottonwoods once thrived. Then ornithologists noticed something unexpected: the endangered Southwestern Willow Flycatcher was nesting almost exclusively in tamarisk thickets. The native vegetation was gone. The invasive had become the only available habitat. Removing tamarisk now meant choosing between ideological purity and actual bird survival. Conservation biology doesn't offer clean answers when the invader becomes the last refuge.

**Charles Elton's Blind Spot**

When Oxford ecologist Charles Elton wrote *The Ecology of Invasions* in 1958, he established the framework we still use: invasives destabilize, natives stabilize. But Elton studied primarily intact ecosystems. He didn't account for systems already broken by logging, agriculture, fire suppression, or extirpation of keystone species. In those contexts, the assumption that native equals stable no longer holds. Sometimes an invasive species is filling a functional role that native species once filled but can no longer provide. The ecosystem isn't choosing incorrectly; it's choosing what's available.

**The Novel Ecosystem Problem**

Ecologists now recognize "novel ecosystems" — communities with no historical precedent, mixing natives and non-natives in combinations that never existed before but nonetheless function. California grasslands are 99% non-native species, yet they prevent erosion, sequester carbon, and support wildlife that native bunch grasses no longer can. You cannot restore what was there before; the conditions that sustained it are gone. The choice becomes: accept the functioning novel ecosystem or maintain a degraded native one. Neither is "natural" in any meaningful sense.

**Kudzu's Second Act**

Kudzu — the vine that ate the South — is being reconsidered by some restoration ecologists. In abandoned industrial sites and strip-mined lands too contaminated for native plants, kudzu stabilizes soil, sequesters carbon, and begins rebuilding organic matter. It's a pioneer species for the Anthropocene, thriving where nothing else will. Once it's done the dirty work of initial soil remediation, slower-growing natives can establish. The invasion becomes the first stage of recovery, not its opposite. The plant doesn't change; what changes is our recognition that some damage requires aggressive response.

**The Humility Question**

The invasive-as-savior phenomenon forces a deeper question: Are we managing ecosystems for ecological function or for historical fidelity? If an invasive species is providing the ecosystem services that natives once did — erosion control, water filtration, habitat structure — does its geographic origin matter more than its ecological role? There's no universal answer. Each case demands we ask: What is this landscape capable of now, not what was it once? Sometimes the invader is the only species still willing to do the work.
