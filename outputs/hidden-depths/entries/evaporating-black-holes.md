# Evaporating Black Holes

Black holes seem permanent — gravity so extreme that nothing, not even light, can escape. Yet Stephen Hawking proved in 1974 that black holes aren't eternal prisons. They leak. They shrink. Given enough time, they vanish completely, releasing everything they've swallowed back into the universe as radiation. This quantum evaporation transforms our understanding of what black holes actually are: not cosmic vacuum cleaners running forever, but temporary concentrations of mass with expiration dates.

**Quantum Foam at the Horizon**

Space isn't empty. Quantum mechanics reveals that even perfect vacuum seethes with particle-antiparticle pairs blinking into existence and annihilating within 10⁻²¹ seconds. Usually this matters to nothing. But at a black hole's event horizon, these pairs can be torn apart. One particle falls in. The other escapes as real radiation. The black hole loses the mass-energy of the captured particle. To an outside observer, the hole glows. The smaller the black hole, the more extreme this quantum foam becomes, and the faster it radiates.

**Hawking's Mathematics**

Stephen Hawking was 32 when he combined quantum field theory with general relativity and found something no one expected: black holes have temperature. A solar-mass black hole radiates at 60 nanokelvins — barely above absolute zero. But temperature means energy loss, and energy loss means the hole shrinks. His equation showed that radiation intensity increases as mass decreases. The final moments of evaporation release more energy than a supernova. Hawking called it his greatest discovery. Other physicists initially thought it was impossible.

**The Lifespan Problem**

A black hole with the sun's mass would take 10⁶⁷ years to evaporate — vastly longer than the universe's current 13.8 billion years. Stellar-mass black holes won't finish evaporating until long after the last stars have died. But primordial black holes — planet-sized or smaller, potentially formed in the Big Bang's first seconds — follow different timelines. A black hole with Earth's mass evaporates in 10⁵⁰ years. One with a mountain's mass? Already gone. Any with an initial mass under a billion tons has already exploded somewhere in the cosmos.

**The Information Paradox**

If black holes evaporate completely, what happens to the information about everything that fell in? Quantum mechanics says information can't be destroyed — it must be preserved somehow. But Hawking radiation appears completely random, thermally scrambled, carrying no trace of what created it. This is the black hole information paradox, still unresolved after fifty years. Some physicists argue information encodes subtly in the radiation's correlations. Others suggest it remains at the event horizon. Still others propose the whole framework needs rebuilding.

**Jacob Bekenstein's Entropy**

Before Hawking's radiation, a young graduate student named Jacob Bekenstein insisted that black holes must have entropy — a measure of disorder and hidden information. His advisor, John Wheeler, was skeptical. But Bekenstein showed that a black hole's entropy equals its surface area divided by four, measured in Planck units. This means information isn't volumetric but holographic, encoded on the boundary. When Hawking proved black holes radiate, he confirmed Bekenstein was right: black holes obey thermodynamics. They're thermal objects that can reach equilibrium, emit energy, and eventually die.

**Detecting the Invisible**

No one has observed Hawking radiation directly. For stellar-mass black holes, it's drowned by the cosmic microwave background, which is thousands of times hotter. But physicists have created analogues: sound waves in flowing fluids that mimic event horizons. In 2016, Jeff Steinhauer generated acoustic black holes in ultracold rubidium atoms and detected the equivalent of Hawking radiation in phonons. These laboratory analogues can't prove black holes evaporate, but they demonstrate the quantum mechanism works exactly as Hawking predicted.
