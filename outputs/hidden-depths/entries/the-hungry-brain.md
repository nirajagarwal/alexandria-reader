# The Hungry Brain

Your brain is two percent of your body weight but consumes twenty percent of your energy — more than your heart, lungs, and kidneys combined. This massive fuel demand shapes everything from why you crave sugar when studying to why evolution kept our brains from growing even larger. The organ that lets you think about energy is itself energy's most extravagant user.

**Glucose or Nothing**

Unlike your muscles, which can burn fat directly, your brain runs almost exclusively on glucose. It metabolizes about 120 grams daily — equivalent to the sugar in three cans of soda, though it prefers a steady trickle rather than spikes. During intense concentration, certain brain regions can increase their glucose consumption by up to 50 percent. This pickiness is why low blood sugar makes you foggy and irritable: your brain is literally running out of fuel, and unlike other organs, it can't easily switch to alternatives.

**The Twenty-Watt Thinker**

Your brain operates on roughly 20 watts — about the same as a dim lightbulb. That's remarkable efficiency for 86 billion neurons constantly firing. But in absolute terms, it's still enormous. While resting, your brain uses about 0.3 calories per minute. During challenging mental work — chess, calculus, writing — this rises only slightly, perhaps to 0.4 calories per minute. The brain's baseline activity is so high that thinking harder barely registers. Most of the energy maintains the basic electrical charge across neuron membranes, whether you're pondering philosophy or staring at a wall.

**The Expensive Tissue Hypothesis**

Anthropologist Leslie Aiello proposed in 1995 that human brains could only grow larger because our guts grew smaller. We evolved shorter, more efficient digestive systems by eating higher-quality food — cooked meat, processed tubers — which freed up energy. Our guts and brains are both metabolically expensive tissues. In primates, there's a clear trade-off: bigger brains correlate with smaller digestive systems. Humans are the extreme case. We literally became intelligent by offloading digestion to external processing: cooking, grinding, fermenting.

**The Childhood Energy Crisis**

Between ages five and seven, children's brains consume 60 percent of their total resting metabolism — more than at any other age. This coincides with the slowest period of physical growth in childhood. The body essentially stops investing in size to pour resources into rewiring the brain. Growth in height nearly stalls while the frontal cortex undergoes massive reorganization. This may explain why children in this age range need more sleep and show greater vulnerability to malnutrition. The brain is demanding so much energy that other systems must wait.

**The Ketone Escape Hatch**

During starvation, the liver produces ketone bodies — small molecules that can cross the blood-brain barrier and partially fuel neurons. After several days without food, ketones can provide up to 70 percent of the brain's energy needs. This metabolic flexibility probably saved countless ancestral humans during famines. But it comes with trade-offs: ketone metabolism produces less energy per unit oxygen, and very high levels cause dangerous blood acidification. The brain's preference for glucose isn't arbitrary. It's optimized for normal conditions, with ketones as an emergency backup.

**Evolution's Energy Ceiling**

Why didn't brains evolve to be even larger and more powerful? Energy. The human brain already pushes against fundamental limits. A woman's pelvis can barely accommodate an infant's head — hence our uniquely difficult births. Larger brains would require even longer childhoods to reach maturity, since brain development is energetically costly. And the cardiovascular system struggles to meet the brain's demands: even brief interruptions in blood flow cause permanent damage. We've likely reached the maximum size that metabolism and anatomy can support.
