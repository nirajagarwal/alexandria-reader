# Trillions Pass Through You

Every second, about 100 trillion neutrinos stream through your body. They come from the sun, from deep space, from the Earth itself. They pass through you, through buildings, through the entire planet without stopping. You are, right now, as transparent as glass to the universe's most abundant particle.

**Ghost Particles**

Neutrinos barely interact with matter. A single neutrino could pass through a light-year of lead — about six trillion miles of solid metal — with only a 50% chance of hitting anything. They have almost no mass, no electric charge, and travel at nearly the speed of light. When Wolfgang Pauli first proposed their existence in 1930, he called them "that terrible thing" and apologized for suggesting something so impossible to detect. He was sure no one would ever prove him right.

**The Sun's Exhaust**

Every neutrino from the sun begins as nuclear fusion in the core. When hydrogen atoms fuse into helium, they release energy — light, heat, and neutrinos. The light takes 100,000 years to random-walk its way out through the dense solar interior. The neutrinos leave immediately, passing through 432,000 miles of sun as if it weren't there. Eight minutes later, they're passing through you. They carry information about what's happening in the solar core right now, not 100,000 years ago.

**Clyde Cowan's Patience**

In 1956, physicists Clyde Cowan and Frederick Reines set up two massive water tanks next to a nuclear reactor in South Carolina. They waited. Nuclear reactors produce neutrinos by the trillions, and very occasionally — about once every twenty minutes in their detector — one would collide with a proton in the water. A flash of light. A cascade of particles. After months of watching, they had enough flashes to send Pauli a telegram: "We are happy to inform you that we have definitely detected neutrinos." Pauli interrupted a meeting to read it aloud and order champagne. He had twenty-three years.

**Deep Ice**

The IceCube Neutrino Observatory is a cubic kilometer of ice beneath the South Pole, threaded with 5,160 optical sensors frozen into the glacier. It's watching for the rare neutrino that hits an atom in the ice hard enough to produce visible particles. In 2013, it detected two neutrinos with more energy than anything the sun could produce — energy levels requiring cosmic catastrophes. One came from 3.7 billion light-years away. These particles traveled across half the observable universe, through galaxies and nebulae and empty space and Earth itself, until one random atomic nucleus in Antarctic ice finally stood in the way.

**What Mass Means**

Neutrinos were supposed to be massless. In 1998, Super-Kamiokande — a 50,000-ton water tank buried under a mountain in Japan — proved they weren't. Neutrinos come in three types, or "flavors," and they oscillate between them as they travel. Only particles with mass can oscillate like this. The discovery meant rewriting fundamental physics. The exact mass is still unknown, but it's tiny: if a neutrino were scaled up to the mass of a golf ball, an electron would weigh as much as the Earth.

**The Earth Glows**

Your body is slightly radioactive. Potassium-40 in your bones, carbon-14 in your tissues — natural isotopes that slowly decay. When they do, some release neutrinos. The Earth itself generates about a trillion trillion neutrinos per second from radioactive decay in the mantle and crust. Stand anywhere on the planet: neutrinos from the rock beneath you are rising up through your feet. Neutrinos from the rock on the far side of the planet are falling down through your head. You are standing in a sphere of radiation that treats matter as if it were empty space.
