# Faster When Cold

Heat usually speeds things up — chemical reactions double with every 10°C rise, metabolism quickens, molecules move faster. But some things break this rule. Superconductors carry electricity without resistance only near absolute zero. Helium becomes a superfluid that climbs walls and flows through impossible gaps. And in the ordinary world, closer than you think, cold makes certain processes *faster* than heat ever could.

**Glass Becomes Liquid**

Glass isn't solid. It's a supercooled liquid, frozen mid-flow. At room temperature, a window pane flows downward at roughly one atom per millennium. Heat it, and it flows faster — but only to a point. Cool glass below its transition temperature and something strange happens: its viscosity drops. The atoms, locked in their disordered arrangement, can suddenly rearrange more easily. The glass becomes paradoxically more fluid as it gets colder, until it hits another threshold and locks again. Windows are slowest at the temperature we keep them.

**The Mpemba Effect**

In 1963, Tanzanian student Erasto Mpemba noticed his hot ice cream mix froze faster than his classmate's cold batch. His teacher dismissed it. His physics professor mocked him. But when they tested it, hot water *did* freeze faster than cold — sometimes. Forty years later, we still don't fully know why. Evaporation removes mass. Convection currents differ. Dissolved gases escape. Supercooling delays freezing in cold water but not in water that started hot. Multiple mechanisms compete. Cold water *should* win, but hot water knows shortcuts.

**Quantum Tunneling at Zero**

Nuclear fusion requires extraordinary heat — millions of degrees to ram hydrogen nuclei together hard enough to overcome their electromagnetic repulsion. Except at absolute zero, fusion happens anyway. Quantum tunneling lets particles pass through energy barriers they shouldn't be able to cross. At higher temperatures, thermal motion interferes, scattering particles before they can tunnel. At temperatures near zero Kelvin, particles sit still enough that quantum effects dominate. They don't need kinetic energy to fuse. They can simply *appear* on the other side of the barrier. Cold fusion exists — just not the way anyone hoped.

**The Leidenfrost Effect Inverted**

Drop water on a hot skillet and it dances on a cushion of its own vapor, insulated from the heat. This Leidenfrost effect slows evaporation — the droplet lasts longer at 300°C than at 100°C. But reverse it: cool a surface to liquid nitrogen temperatures and drop water on it. The water freezes instantly, but a thin vapor layer forms underneath from the temperature shock. The ice hovers and slides, friction nearly eliminated. The colder the surface, the faster things can move across it. Speed skaters know this. Their blades melt ice microscopically, but the colder the ice, the thinner the melt layer, the faster they glide. Too warm and they sink. Too cold and there's no melt at all. The sweet spot is just below freezing, where cold enables speed.

**Bose-Einstein Condensates**

Cool atoms to a few billionths of a degree above absolute zero and they stop behaving as individuals. They blur into a single quantum entity, all occupying the same state, all acting as one. Information propagates through the condensate instantaneously — faster than any classical signal could travel. Photons pass through it at 17 meters per second, then reemerge at light speed. Time dilates within it. The condensate exists in a regime where "faster" loses meaning, where quantum coherence replaces classical motion, where the cold doesn't slow things down but erases the boundaries that made "slow" and "fast" coherent concepts.

**Chemical Reactions Without Energy**

Most chemistry follows the Arrhenius equation: reaction rates double with every 10°C increase. But some reactions accelerate in the cold. Polymerization of certain monomers proceeds faster at -40°C than at room temperature because side reactions are suppressed and the desired molecular alignment becomes favorable. The interstellar medium, at 10 Kelvin, hosts complex organic chemistry that shouldn't be possible — molecules forming not despite the cold but because of it. The cold eliminates competing pathways. It doesn't provide energy for reactions; it removes distractions.
