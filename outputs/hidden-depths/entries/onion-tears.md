# Onion Tears

You cry when you cut an onion. Everyone knows this. But what's actually happening is a chemical weapon evolved over millions of years, triggered by a cascade of enzymatic reactions in the seconds after your knife breaks the onion's cells — and your tears are exactly the defense the plant intended.

**A Sulfur Bomb**

Onions store two chemicals separately: amino acid sulfoxides in their cells and an enzyme called alliinase in their cell membranes. When your knife slices through, these meet. The enzyme converts the sulfoxides into sulfenic acid, which instantly rearranges into syn-propanethial-S-oxide — a volatile molecule light enough to float from cutting board to eye in seconds. This is the lachrymatory factor, discovered and named by Swiss chemist Eric Block in 1978. It's chemically distinct from the compounds that make onions smell or taste like onions. Its only function is attack.

**The Irritation Mechanism**

When syn-propanethial-S-oxide reaches your eye, it dissolves in the tear film covering your cornea. There it reacts with water to form sulfuric acid. Dilute, but enough. The corneal nerves detect this acidity as irritation. They signal the lachrymal glands, which flood the eye with tears — not the emotional tears that contain stress hormones, but reflex tears, essentially salt water. The goal: dilute and flush away the irritant. You blink, you tear up, you turn your head. Exactly what would happen if a small animal tried to take a bite.

**Underground Arsenal**

The onion is a bulb: a storage organ the plant builds underground, packed with energy for next season's growth. It's valuable and vulnerable. The lachrymatory factor is one part of a broader chemical defense system. Other sulfur compounds give raw onions their sharp bite, deterring herbivores that might dig them up. But these same compounds, transformed by cooking heat, become the sweet, savory flavors humans prize. The plant's deterrent became our delicacy. We farm onions globally now, propagating the weapon that makes us cry.

**Catalina's Onion**

In 2002, plant scientists at House Foods Corporation in Japan, led by Shinsuke Imai, succeeded in breeding a "tearless" onion. They used traditional cross-breeding to reduce the gene responsible for producing the lachrymatory factor synthase enzyme. The onion still has sulfoxides — still tastes like an onion — but produces 50-70% less of the airborne irritant. Released commercially as "Smile Ball" onions in Japan, they remain rare elsewhere. Most suppliers consider the tears a mark of freshness: a crying cook means a potent bulb, full of the sulfur compounds that deliver flavor.

**The Workarounds**

Chefs develop their own defenses. Chilling onions slows the volatilization — cold molecules move less, reach your eyes more slowly. Cutting under running water or near a flame draws the vapor away from your face. A sharp knife matters most: it breaks fewer cells per slice, releasing less alliinase and sulfoxide into contact. The electric onion goggles sold online work by simple physics: a sealed barrier the molecules cannot cross. But most cooks just accept the tears as transaction cost. The irritation fades in minutes. The flavor lasts.

**Evolutionary Success**

The onion's chemical weapon has made it one of the most successful plants in human history. We cultivate 100 million tons annually worldwide. The tears are proof of potency: this bulb concentrates enough sulfur compounds to defend itself against mammals, insects, fungi. That we cry and return to slice another means we've evolved our own adaptation — not genetic but cultural. We value the flavor enough to endure the pain. The onion wins either way: we spread it across continents, ensuring its genes persist in quantities no wild population could achieve. The weapon worked.
