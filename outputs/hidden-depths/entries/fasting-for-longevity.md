# Fasting for Longevity

Your body treats food as a signal. When you eat, cellular machinery ramps up — grow, divide, build. When you don't, that same machinery downshifts into maintenance mode: repair damaged proteins, recycle broken parts, fortify defenses. The question isn't whether fasting extends life. In worms, flies, and mice, the answer is yes, dramatically so. The question is what's actually happening during those hungry hours, and whether the mechanisms that add years to a lab mouse's life translate to the years you might want to add to yours.

**The Janitor Shift**

Stop eating for 12-16 hours and your cells activate autophagy — literally "self-eating." Damaged mitochondria, misfolded proteins, cellular debris that accumulates during normal operation: autophagy tags it, wraps it in membranes, and breaks it down for parts. Yoshinori Ohsumi won the 2016 Nobel Prize for mapping this process in yeast. The system exists in every eukaryotic cell, conserved across a billion years of evolution. Feed constantly and autophagy idles. Fast and it accelerates. This isn't starvation damage. It's spring cleaning.

**The Okinawa Data**

For decades, residents of Okinawa, Japan lived longer than almost anyone — many past 100, with low rates of cancer and heart disease. They also practiced *hara hachi bu*: eating until 80% full, maintaining mild caloric restriction throughout life. Population studies suggested their longevity came partly from consuming 20% fewer calories than mainland Japanese. Then younger generations abandoned the practice, adopted Western diets, and Okinawan longevity advantages began disappearing. The natural experiment suggests caloric restriction matters, though whether through fasting windows or simple moderation remains unclear.

**Valter Longo's Mice**

At USC, gerontologist Valter Longo bred mice genetically identical except for feeding schedules. One group ate freely. Another fasted every other day. A third followed his "fasting-mimicking diet" — five days monthly of restricted calories. The fasting mice lived 30-40% longer. Their tumors grew slower. Their neurons resisted age-related damage. Their immune systems stayed younger. When Longo moved to human trials, participants showed reduced IGF-1 (a growth factor linked to cancer), improved insulin sensitivity, and decreased inflammation markers. Not forever, not a cure. But measurable shifts in disease risk.

**The Insulin Problem**

Every meal spikes insulin, the hormone that tells cells to store energy. High insulin blocks fat-burning, promotes fat storage, and keeps cellular growth pathways active — specifically mTOR, which tells cells to build and divide rather than repair. Constant eating means constant insulin, which means mTOR stays on. Fasting drops insulin to baseline. Growth pathways quiet. Repair pathways activate. In metabolic terms, you toggle from anabolic (building) to catabolic (breaking down and recycling). Cancer cells, which require constant growth signals, particularly hate this toggle. Healthy cells tolerate it well.

**The Circadian Angle**

Your liver, pancreas, and gut don't work on your schedule — they work on an ancient light-dark cycle. Satchin Panda at the Salk Institute restricted mice to eating within an 8-hour window, without changing total calories. The time-restricted mice stayed lean, showed better glucose control, and resisted fatty liver disease. The mechanism: circadian clock genes govern metabolism. Eating around the clock confuses these genes. Fasting for 12+ hours nightly lets the clock run its program — repair overnight, process fuel during the day. In human trials, even without caloric restriction, time-restricted eating improved metabolic markers.

**The Translation Problem**

Mice live two years. Humans live eighty. Mice in labs eat processed chow and face no predators. Humans eat varied diets in complex social settings. When you extend a mouse's life by 30%, you add six months. Translate that percentage to humans and you add 24 years — which seems unlikely. Human trials show metabolic improvements: better insulin sensitivity, reduced inflammation, weight loss. But longevity? The studies tracking mortality take decades, and fasting research in humans only started seriously in the 2010s. We have mechanisms that should work. We have biomarkers moving in the right direction. We don't yet have the bodies.
