# Moral Convergence

Across cultures, across centuries, strangers who never met developed remarkably similar moral codes. Murder is wrong. Reciprocity matters. Children deserve protection. But why? If morality is just cultural invention, why does it keep inventing the same things? The answer reveals something about what we are — and what human societies need to be.

**The Ultimatum Game**

In 1982, economist Werner Güth created a simple experiment. One player gets $100 and proposes a split. The other accepts or rejects — if they reject, nobody gets anything. Standard economic theory predicts rational acceptance of any offer, even $1. Instead, across dozens of cultures, people consistently reject offers below 30% and propose splits near 50-50. We carry neural circuitry that values fairness even at personal cost. The instinct runs deeper than calculation.

**Axelrod's Tournament**

Political scientist Robert Axelrod invited game theorists to submit strategies for repeated Prisoner's Dilemma in 1980. Fourteen programs competed. The winner? "Tit for Tat" — cooperate first, then mirror what the other player does. The simplest strategy won because cooperation with punishment for defection outperforms both pure cooperation and pure defection. Natural selection discovered this solution millions of years before game theory formalized it. We didn't invent reciprocity. We inherited it.

**The Hadza Exception**

Among Tanzania's Hadza foragers, researchers found something unexpected. Unlike agricultural societies, Hadza players in the Ultimatum Game offered less and accepted less. They split resources more unequally. This isn't moral failure — it's adaptation. In nomadic groups with fluid membership, you share with today's campmates but tomorrow you're elsewhere. Strong fairness norms evolve where you face the same people repeatedly. The Hadza prove the rule: moral convergence reflects stable social structures, not universal human nature alone.

**Kohlberg's Stages**

Psychologist Lawrence Kohlberg interviewed boys about moral dilemmas across cultures in the 1950s and found developmental stages appearing in the same sequence everywhere. Young children obey to avoid punishment. Adolescents conform to social approval. Some adults reach principled reasoning about universal rights. Critics noted his research focused on Western boys, but cross-cultural studies confirmed the pattern holds across societies. We don't just learn morality; we grow into increasingly sophisticated versions of it, constrained by how human cognition develops.

**The Monogamy Puzzle**

Eighty-five percent of human societies permit polygyny, yet most marriages worldwide are monogamous. Why? Economist Joseph Henrich traced how societies that suppressed polygyny — early Christianity, later Confucianism — channeled male reproductive competition into economic productivity. More unmated males meant more crime and violence. Monogamous norms reduced male-male competition, lowered crime, increased trust between unrelated males, and enabled larger-scale cooperation. Moral rules that seem arbitrary often solve coordination problems invisible until you model them.

**The Incest Universal**

Every known human society prohibits sexual relationships between close kin. The biological cost is clear — recessive genetic disorders compound rapidly — but how did pre-genetic societies know? They didn't need to. Natural selection embedded the "ick factor," the Westermarck effect: people raised together in early childhood rarely become sexually attracted. The moral rule articulates an instinct already in place. We feel disgust, then construct the prohibition. The convergence happens at the emotional level first.
