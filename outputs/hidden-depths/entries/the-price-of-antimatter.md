# The Price of Antimatter

When physicists at CERN produce antimatter, they're creating the most expensive substance ever made. A single gram would cost roughly $62.5 trillion to produce — if we could even make that much. This isn't just the world's priciest material. It's a window into why our universe exists at all, and why the symmetry that should have destroyed everything instead left us behind.

**Making Nothing from Something**

At CERN's Antiproton Decelerator, protons traveling at 99.9% the speed of light slam into a metal target. The collision spawns particle-antiparticle pairs — including antiprotons. But the conversion is brutally inefficient. For every 10 million protons accelerated, roughly ten antiprotons emerge. The rest becomes heat, radiation, and other particles. You're using staggering amounts of energy to create tiny amounts of matter's mirror image, then spending more energy to slow those antiprotons down from near-light speed to something you can trap. The $62.5 trillion per gram figure assumes you could maintain this process continuously for centuries.

**The Magnetic Bottle**

Antimatter cannot touch ordinary matter. One antiproton meeting one proton annihilates both in a flash of gamma rays. So CERN stores antiprotons in a device called a Penning trap — a magnetic bottle made of precisely tuned electromagnetic fields. The antiprotons spiral endlessly in ultra-high vacuum, never touching the walls. CERN has kept antiprotons stable for over a year this way. The longest-lived storage contained about 300 antiprotons — roughly 0.0000000000005 grams. The traps themselves cost millions to build and maintain. The vacuum must be better than outer space. One stray molecule would destroy your entire stock.

**Dirac's Prediction**

In 1928, physicist Paul Dirac was solving equations describing how electrons behave at high speeds. The math kept producing two solutions: one for the electron everyone knew, another for something identical but opposite. Dirac initially thought this ghost particle was the proton. He was wrong — it was a new particle entirely. Carl Anderson discovered it in cosmic ray tracks in 1932: the positron, the electron's antimatter twin. Dirac had predicted antimatter from pure mathematics, never imagining anyone would make it. He received the Nobel Prize at 31, the youngest theoretical physicist to win it.

**The Missing Universe**

The Big Bang should have created equal amounts of matter and antimatter. The equations are symmetric — for every particle, an antiparticle. They should have annihilated each other completely, leaving only light. Instead, for every billion matter-antimatter pairs, one extra matter particle survived. We are that tiny asymmetry, that one-in-a-billion surplus. Physicists call this the baryon asymmetry problem. No one knows why the imbalance exists. Every human, every star, every galaxy is the residue of a symmetry that somehow, for reasons still unknown, broke.

**The Antimatter Rocket**

A matter-antimatter engine would be the ultimate fuel. Einstein's E=mc² means complete annihilation converts 100% of mass to energy. Chemical rockets convert about 0.0000001%. Nuclear fission manages 0.1%. Antimatter annihilation is absolute. One gram of antimatter meeting one gram of matter releases the energy of the Hiroshima bomb. A spacecraft with a few hundred grams could reach a significant fraction of light speed. NASA has studied designs. The problem isn't the physics — it's the $125 trillion fuel bill for two grams, and nowhere to store it.

**At NASA Goddard's Lab**

Physicist Gerald Smith spent decades designing antimatter traps that could survive a rocket launch. His team calculated that with better production methods, you might get costs down to $62.5 billion per gram — still impossibly expensive, but a thousand times better. Smith died in 2021. His designs remain theoretical. CERN's entire annual antimatter production would power a light bulb for minutes. But the engineering challenges are real problems, not physics problems. That's the difference between impossible and merely impractical.
