# Scars and Their Absence

We think of scars as the body's permanent record — injury written into skin that never fully forgets. Yet fetal wounds heal without a trace, and certain tissues regenerate perfectly while others seem incapable of repair. The question isn't why we scar, but why we ever stop healing the way we once did.

**Before Twenty-Four Weeks**

A fetus wounded in the womb heals without scarring. The skin closes seamlessly, cell by cell, as if nothing happened. This isn't magic — it's a different kind of healing entirely. Fetal skin contains higher ratios of type III collagen, laid down in an organized lattice rather than the haphazard crosshatch of adult repair. The inflammatory response is muted; fewer immune cells swarm the wound. What looks like primitive healing is actually more sophisticated than what comes after. We don't gain the ability to scar. We lose the ability not to.

**The Inflammation Question**

Scarring begins with inflammation. When adult skin tears, the body floods the wound with immune cells: neutrophils first, then macrophages, releasing chemical signals that transform nearby fibroblasts into myofibroblasts — contractile cells that pull wound edges together and pump out collagen at emergency speed. This saves lives. It also guarantees scars. The collagen gets deposited fast and messy, oriented for strength rather than architecture. Fetal wounds, by contrast, attract fewer inflammatory cells and maintain lower levels of transforming growth factor beta (TGF-β), the molecular foreman of scar formation. Less inflammation means less scarring, but adult wounds would gape open without it.

**Margaret Strickland**

In 1988, surgeon Margaret Strickland documented scarless fetal healing in human patients undergoing prenatal surgery. Operating on fetuses to correct spina bifida and other conditions, she noticed that surgical incisions made before the third trimester healed invisibly. After twenty-four weeks, they began leaving marks. This window became medically significant: it suggested that scarless healing wasn't just possible but was the body's original strategy. Strickland's work helped establish that scarring is an evolutionary trade-off — a faster, cruder repair system that sacrificed perfection for speed as organisms grew more complex and vulnerable to infection.

**Mouth and Eye**

Two tissues in adults heal with minimal scarring: the inside of the mouth and the cornea of the eye. Oral mucosa repairs quickly and cleanly, leaving barely visible marks even from deep cuts. The reasons echo fetal healing — saliva contains growth factors that dampen inflammation, and the tissue maintains a wetter environment with less mechanical tension. The cornea has no blood vessels and limited immune response, forcing it to heal through a quieter cellular reorganization. Both tissues suggest that scarless healing isn't lost, just suppressed. The body still knows how; it simply can't afford to use that knowledge everywhere.

**The Naked Mole Rat**

This wrinkled rodent, which lives in underground colonies and can survive for thirty minutes without oxygen, also heals wounds with almost no scarring. Its secret appears to be hyaluronic acid — the same molecule used in skin creams, but present in naked mole rat tissue at concentrations five times higher than in mice. High molecular weight hyaluronic acid creates a viscous extracellular matrix that modulates inflammation and guides cells to organize more precisely during repair. The animal's resistance to cancer and extended lifespan (over thirty years, ten times longer than similar-sized rodents) may share molecular roots with its healing abilities. All three traits involve careful regulation of cell division and tissue remodeling.

**The Regeneration Gradient**

Humans sit on a spectrum of regenerative ability. We regrow liver tissue, fingerprints sometimes return after burns, and children under ten occasionally regenerate fingertips if the nail bed remains intact. Yet we cannot regrow limbs like salamanders or repair spinal cords like zebrafish. The difference lies partly in our immune complexity. More sophisticated immune systems produce more aggressive inflammatory responses, which accelerate healing at the cost of precision. Salamanders can afford slower, more organized repair because their regenerative blastema — a mass of dedifferentiated cells at the wound site — operates in a relatively inflammation-free zone. We traded regeneration for speed, and speed for survival in a world where open wounds meant death.
