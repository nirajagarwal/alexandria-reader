# René Girard

The best way to understand René Girard is to imagine him at a faculty meeting. Not speaking—listening. Watching the mechanisms of academic rivalry play out in real time: the careful positioning, the subtle put-downs disguised as questions, the way prestige flows through the room like an electric current. Everyone performing independence while desperately tracking everyone else. Girard saw what they couldn't see about themselves: they were all wanting the same thing because the others wanted it.

He came to this insight backwards, through literature. A young Frenchman teaching at Indiana University in the 1950s, assigned to teach novels he barely knew, Girard started noticing a pattern. In *Don Quixote*, *Madame Bovary*, *The Red and the Black*, the protagonist doesn't desire directly. They desire through someone else. Don Quixote wants what Amadis of Gaul wanted. Emma Bovary wants what the romance novels promised. Julien Sorel wants what Napoleonic ambition prescribed. Desire, Girard realized, is borrowed. We learn what to want by watching others want it.

He called it "mimetic desire" and spent the next fifty years watching it everywhere. In corporate boardrooms and high school cafeterias. In academic departments where the fiercest battles were fought over the smallest stakes. In his own life—the way he'd moved from history to literature to anthropology, always chasing something he couldn't quite name, always aware that his ambitions were shaped by what others found important.

The theory kept expanding. If we desire what others desire, we compete for the same objects. Competition breeds rivalry. Rivalry needs an outlet. What do communities do with the violence that mimetic desire generates? They find a victim. One person, blamed for everything, expelled or killed, and suddenly the community feels united again. The scapegoat mechanism, Girard argued, was the secret engine of human culture. Every mythology, every sacrifice, every taboo—all ways of managing the violence baked into wanting.

His colleagues thought he was insane. An outsider making grand claims about everything from Greek tragedy to nuclear deterrence. He had no methodology, no proper training in anthropology, no respect for disciplinary boundaries. He was a literary critic writing about sacrifice. A theorist of culture who read ancient texts like detective novels. He made totalizing claims that serious scholars knew better than to make.

But Girard had found something that wouldn't let him go. He saw the pattern everywhere because it *was* everywhere. In Freud's Oedipus complex (the son doesn't want the mother; he wants what the father has). In market bubbles (everyone buying because everyone's buying). In ideological fervor (we don't believe; we believe what believers believe). Once you saw it, you couldn't unsee it.

Then came the religious turn. If the scapegoat mechanism powered archaic religion, what happened when a victim refused to stay silent? What if someone was killed, and their followers claimed the killing was unjust? Christianity, Girard argued, was the great revelation of the scapegoat mechanism—not another myth but the unmasking of all myths. The Gospels told the story from the victim's side. And in doing so, they planted a seed that would slowly undermine the entire sacrificial order.

This made him friends in strange places and enemies in expected ones. Some Christians embraced him; others were offended by his reduction of faith to anthropological mechanism. Atheists liked his account of archaic religion but hated his Christian apologetics. Academics resented his success with general readers. He was too religious for the secularists, too theoretical for the theologians, too interdisciplinary for everyone.

He didn't care. Or rather, he cared desperately but kept going anyway. He moved from Johns Hopkins to Buffalo to Stanford, always slightly apart, always watching. He accumulated followers—intense, devoted readers who found in his work a key to everything. He also accumulated critics who pointed out the gaps, the overgeneralizations, the way his theory explained too much and therefore explained nothing.

The criticism wasn't wrong. Girard's theory was unfalsifiable in the way grand theories tend to be. Any counterexample could be folded back into the mechanism. People don't desire mimetically? That's because they're hiding their models. Communities don't scapegoat? That's because the mechanism has been internalized, sublimated, redirected. The theory became a lens that colored everything it touched.

But that didn't make it useless. Girard had seen something true about the social nature of desire, even if he'd overstated its explanatory power. He'd noticed how much of what we call individual preference is actually socially mediated. How rivalries escalate in predictable ways. How groups achieve unity through exclusion. These insights didn't require accepting the whole edifice of mimetic theory or the Christian apologetics bolted onto it.

In his later years, he became obsessed with apocalypse. If the scapegoat mechanism was breaking down—if we could no longer unify through sacrifice—then violence had nowhere to go. Nuclear weapons made total destruction possible at the moment when the old outlets for violence were closing. Without the safety valve of scapegoating, we were headed for either complete annihilation or the Christian revelation fully realized: genuine peace through renouncing mimetic rivalry.

He died in 2015, convinced that his work had been ignored by the people who most needed it. He was probably right, though not in the way he imagined. His books sold. His ideas influenced writers, entrepreneurs, tech thinkers—people outside the academic mainstream he'd never quite joined. Peter Thiel cited him. Evangelical intellectuals embraced him. His name appeared in Silicon Valley reading lists.

But the academic reception remained cool. Too speculative, too ambitious, too willing to build theories that claimed to explain everything from Sophocles to suicide bombers. The very thing that made Girard compelling to general readers—the way he connected everything—made him suspect to specialists.

He would have recognized the mechanism. Academic prestige is rivalrous, model-based, defensive of boundaries. His work threatened too many territories at once. Of course they resisted. Of course they found reasons to dismiss him. That was the theory.

What remains is harder to assess. Not the complete system—few people accept mimetic theory whole. But the core insight about desire: that we want through others, that our autonomy is shot through with imitation, that what looks like individual preference is often social mimicry. Once you see it, you can't unsee it. And maybe that's enough. Not a theory of everything, but a way of noticing what we do to each other when we all start wanting the same thing.
