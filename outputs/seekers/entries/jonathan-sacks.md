# Jonathan Sacks: The Rabbi Who Rebuilt a Tradition

In 1990, a forty-two-year-old philosophy lecturer became Chief Rabbi of the United Hebrew Congregations of the Commonwealth. Jonathan Sacks inherited a British Judaism that was hemorrhaging members, relevance, and confidence. The Orthodox establishment expected him to manage decline with dignity. Instead, he spent the next twenty-two years trying to make an ancient tradition speak to a secular age without diluting what made it ancient.

He shouldn't have been capable of it. Born in 1948 to immigrant parents in post-war London, Sacks grew up in a moderately observant home where Judaism meant community, not theology. At Cambridge, studying philosophy, he dated a Catholic woman. Then, at twenty-one, he heard a lecture by Rabbi Menachem Schneerson—the Lubavitcher Rebbe—and something shifted. Not a road-to-Damascus conversion, but a recognition that the intellectual life he loved and the tradition he'd received weren't enemies. Six months later, he was wearing a kippah full-time. The girlfriend was gone.

What makes this interesting is that Sacks didn't become a fundamentalist. He became more observant *and* more intellectually ambitious. He studied at Jews' College and received rabbinic ordination. He also got a doctorate in philosophy from King's College London, writing on the ethics of tradition and modernity. Most rabbis who engage philosophy end up either abandoning Orthodoxy or abandoning philosophy. Sacks kept both, and the tension between them became his subject.

The Chief Rabbinate gave him a platform but also a set of constraints that would have made a less agile thinker miserable. British Judaism is fractious—ultra-Orthodox, modern Orthodox, Liberal, Reform, secular—and the Chief Rabbi speaks for only one slice: the United Synagogue, the mainstream Orthodox community. To his right were Haredi rabbis who considered him dangerously accommodating. To his left were Progressive Jews who considered him irrelevant. The center was evaporating. And yet Sacks acted as if Judaism could be a big tent, writing books and giving lectures that addressed *all* Jews, and often non-Jews as well.

His method was unusual. He didn't argue from authority—"The Torah says X, therefore Y." He argued from consequence: What kind of society do we want? What kind of people? Then he'd reach back into Jewish sources—Talmud, Midrash, medieval commentaries—and show that they'd been thinking about these questions for millennia, and their answers still worked. Sometimes better than modern answers.

Take *The Dignity of Difference*, published in 2002. Sacks argued that religious diversity isn't a problem to be solved but a feature of a world designed for difference. God speaks multiple languages; revelation isn't a zero-sum game. It was an elegant answer to the question of pluralism, rooted in Genesis 11—the Tower of Babel—and in kabbalistic ideas about divine self-limitation. It was also, from the perspective of traditional Judaism, borderline heretical.

The book provoked a firestorm. Ultra-Orthodox rabbis in Israel denounced him. The London Beth Din—the rabbinical court—pressured him to revise the text. Sacks rewrote parts of the second edition, softening claims that other religions might represent valid paths to God. He defended the changes as clarifications, not capitulations, but the episode revealed the tightrope he walked. He wanted Judaism to engage modernity, but he couldn't afford to alienate the traditionalists who granted him legitimacy.

This tension ran through his entire tenure. He promoted women's learning—supporting the establishment of yeshivas for women, encouraging women's leadership—but within Orthodox parameters. Women couldn't be rabbis. They couldn't read from the Torah in his synagogue. When feminist critics called this insufficient, he agreed it was insufficient, but said Orthodox Judaism changes slowly, through argument and example, not decree. When traditionalists accused him of going too far, he reminded them that the Talmud itself is a record of argument, and that stagnation is a kind of death.

His real contribution was the writing. Over three decades, he produced more than thirty books—on ethics, on covenant, on the relationship between religion and science, on Jewish history. They weren't academic works, though they were erudite. They were written for the educated layperson, Jewish or not, who wanted to think seriously about what it means to live well. He appeared on the BBC, wrote for *The Times*, gave TED talks. He became the public intellectual British Judaism hadn't had since Isaiah Berlin, though in most ways he was Berlin's opposite: observant where Berlin was secular, communitarian where Berlin was liberal, confident that tradition contained wisdom where Berlin was skeptical of all absolutes.

The books are uneven. The best—*The Dignity of Difference*, *Not in God's Name*, *Morality*—are genuinely original, drawing unexpected connections between ancient texts and contemporary crises. The weaker ones repeat themselves, recycle metaphors, lean too heavily on inspiring anecdotes. But even the weaker books do something important: they model a way of being religious that's intellectually serious, morally engaged, and unapologetic. In an age when religion is often presented as a choice between fundamentalism and irrelevance, Sacks showed a third path.

He paid for it. The criticism from the right never stopped. Some Haredi rabbis refused to share a platform with him. Others questioned his halakhic rulings, his theology, his willingness to engage with non-Orthodox Jews. From the left came complaints that he was too cautious, too political, too unwilling to challenge structures that marginalized women and LGBTQ Jews. Both sides were probably right. Sacks was trying to hold together a community that was pulling apart, and the center can't hold forever.

In 2013, he stepped down as Chief Rabbi. By then he was sixty-five, exhausted, and ready to write without institutional constraints. The next seven years were extraordinarily productive. *Not in God's Name* tackled religious violence. *Morality* diagnosed the collapse of shared values in late modernity. He lectured, taught, mentored. In 2020, he was diagnosed with cancer. He died six months later, on November 7, 2020, the week of the U.S. presidential election. In eulogies, speakers noted the symbolism: Sacks had spent his life arguing for the possibility of civil disagreement, of covenant over contract, of society as something more than a collection of individuals pursuing self-interest. The world he left behind seemed to have stopped listening.

But the books remain. So does the example: a thinker who believed that tradition could be both preserved and renewed, that the resources for addressing contemporary problems might be found in texts written two thousand years ago, and that the work of translation—making ancient wisdom legible to modern ears—was worth a life. He didn't resolve the tension between tradition and modernity. He lived inside it, and made something valuable from the friction. Whether that's enough depends on what you think intellectual life is for.
