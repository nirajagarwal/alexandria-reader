# Desmond Tutu

The photograph shows an old man in purple vestments, dancing. Not swaying ceremonially—dancing, with his whole small body, at the inauguration of a president who had spent twenty-seven years in prison. The year is 1994, and Desmond Tutu has just witnessed something he spent decades insisting was possible: a transfer of power in South Africa without a bloodbath.

He was always smaller than you'd expect—five foot four, maybe 135 pounds—and the disproportion between his physical presence and his voice became a fact about him. When he spoke, something came through him that seemed too large for the body. Not just volume, though he could fill a cathedral. Authority of a particular kind: the authority of someone who has looked at an abyss and refused to believe it was the final word.

Born in 1931 in Klerksdorp, a gold-mining town, he wanted to be a doctor. His family couldn't afford medical school. He became a teacher instead, loved it, quit after three years when the government imposed "Bantu Education"—a system explicitly designed to prepare Black children for servitude. "I couldn't be party to that," he said later, in the flat tone he used for describing the unbearable.

The priesthood was not a retreat. It was where he found his weapon.

He was ordained in 1960, the year of Sharpeville, when police shot sixty-nine people in the back for protesting pass laws. The massacre announced a new phase: the apartheid government had stopped pretending. Tutu was thirty-one, studying theology in London, and the question that would reorganize his life was forming: How do you remain human in a system designed to make you less than human?

His answer, worked out over decades, was counterintuitive: You insist on the humanity of everyone. Including the oppressor.

This was not sentiment. It was strategy rooted in theology. Tutu took seriously the claim that humans are made in the image of God—*all* humans, including architects of apartheid. This meant two things: Black South Africans possessed an inalienable dignity that no law could erase, and white South Africans were destroying their own humanity by constructing the system. Both needed liberation.

"I am not interested in picking up crumbs of compassion thrown from the table of someone who considers himself my master," he wrote. "I want the full menu of rights."

He became general secretary of the South African Council of Churches in 1978, and the position made him something unprecedented: a Black leader with institutional protection, international connections, and a platform the government couldn't easily silence. He used it to say things that could get you killed. He called apartheid evil, not unfortunate. He called the government illegitimate, not misguided. He met with the ANC in exile and told them their cause was just.

The government took his passport—twice. He traveled anyway. When they returned it, he went to accept the Nobel Peace Prize in 1984 and came home to press harder.

What made him dangerous was not just his moral clarity but his refusal of bitterness. Bitterness would have been justified. The government had declared his existence illegal, his marriage invalid, his children second-class. But bitterness would have confirmed the apartheid logic: that humans are essentially tribal, that reconciliation is naive, that might makes right.

He would not give them that confirmation.

When students asked why he didn't hate white people, he told them about the white priest who visited him in the hospital when he was a boy, sick with tuberculosis—Father Trevor Huddleston, who tipped his hat to Tutu's mother, a domestic worker. "That was the first time I saw a white man raise his hat to a Black woman," Tutu said. "I never forgot it."

The gesture mattered because it revealed something the system tried to hide: that the lines were drawn, not given. That a different world was possible because humans had made this one.

But the cost of optimism in a violent system is exposure. He presided over funerals for people killed by police. He threw himself over young men about to be necklaced by crowds. He walked into confrontations between protesters and security forces, knowing the purple cassock was thin protection. "I'm scared stiff much of the time," he admitted. "But I can't let them see that."

In 1985, at a funeral in Duduza, he prevented a murder by literally shielding the suspected informant with his body while a mob surrounded them with gasoline and tires. The footage shows him shouting at people he'd just eulogized others with, his face contorted with rage and grief. "I will not allow this to happen!" he screamed. Later he wept in his car.

This is the complexity the easy invocations of "Tutu" miss: his gentleness was real but it was not softness. It was ferocity in the service of a vision that insisted on treating people as capable of transformation.

When Nelson Mandela was released in 1990, Tutu was there. When the first democratic elections came in 1994, he was there, dancing in his vestments. And when Mandela asked him to chair the Truth and Reconciliation Commission, he accepted a job that would nearly destroy him.

The TRC was an impossible task: address decades of torture, murder, and systemic violence without either kangaroo courts or amnesia. Tutu's framework was theological: confession, forgiveness, ubuntu—the Nguni concept that a person is a person through other persons. We are bound together, even perpetrator and victim. Especially perpetrator and victim.

For two years he listened to testimony. Police officers describing how they burned bodies while having a barbecue nearby. Mothers learning how their sons had been tortured. Perpetrators asking for amnesty. Victims asked if they could forgive.

The cameras caught him weeping, head in hands. He wept often. The criticism came from all sides: too soft on perpetrators, too hard on the liberation movements, too religious, not legal enough. He absorbed it and kept going.

"Without forgiveness, there is no future," he said. "But without justice, there is no forgiveness."

Whether the TRC succeeded is still debated. Tutu believed it did something necessary: it made the truth public. "We have looked the beast in the eye," he said. The country did not collapse into civil war. That mattered.

In later years he took up causes that put him at odds with former allies: gay rights, Palestinian liberation, assisted dying for the terminally ill. He was consistent: if the logic was that every human bore the divine image, then it applied everywhere or nowhere. This won him no prizes from people who wanted selective application.

His last years included Parkinson's, repeated hospitalizations, and the diminishment that comes for prophets who live long enough. He died in December 2021, ninety years old, having seen the impossible thing happen and also having seen how much remained undone.

At his funeral, they played recordings of his laughter. It was frequent, high and giggly, and it punctuated even his darkest sermons. People who expected gravitas were sometimes unsettled by it—laughter in the face of apartheid seemed inappropriate. But the laughter was not denial. It was defiance of a different kind: the insistence that joy was not a reward for justice achieved but a resource for justice pursued.

He had looked at the abyss and then laughed at it, a small man in purple robes, dancing because the thing everyone said was impossible had happened and might happen again.

The question he lived with—how do you remain human in an inhuman system—he answered with his life: You insist on seeing the humanity in everyone, including your enemy, until the insistence becomes reality or kills you. He did the first and survived. That's rarer than it sounds.
