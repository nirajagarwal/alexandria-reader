# Dorothy Day

She kept a diary. In it, alongside prayers and the daily operations of running a soup kitchen, she recorded her horror at her own irritability. "I am impatient, critical, biting of tongue," she wrote in 1963. She was sixty-five and had been at it for thirty years—the movement, the houses, the paper, the whole impossible project of trying to live as though the Sermon on the Mount were practical instruction.

The impatience was real. People who worked with her remember it. She could freeze you with a look. She had standards, and she enforced them, and she didn't always do it gently. This was the same woman who insisted on calling the filthy, often drunk men who came through the Catholic Worker houses "ambassadors of God."

She came to this from elsewhere. The important thing to know about Dorothy Day is that she arrived at radical Christianity from the bohemian left of Greenwich Village in the 1920s. She had been a journalist, a socialist, a friend to Eugene O'Neill and Hart Crane. She had lived with a man, gotten pregnant, had an abortion. Later she had another lover, Forster Batterham, and with him a daughter, Tamar. The birth of her daughter broke something open. She had the child baptized Catholic. Batterham, an anarchist who despised religion, left her. She grieved him for years.

This is the crossing. She became Catholic in 1927, at thirty, without any clear sense of what she would do with it. The Church in America meant respectability, upward mobility, Cardinal Spellman blessing bombers. It meant Irish cops, not social revolution. For five years she had her faith and her past and no way to join them.

Then in December 1932, Peter Maurin showed up. He was twenty years older, a French peasant intellectual who had read his way through the Church Fathers and came out the other side believing that Christianity meant voluntary poverty, works of mercy, and the overthrow of the wage system. He talked in memorized paragraphs. He slept on park benches. He wanted to start a newspaper.

She listened. By May 1933, they had the first issue of *The Catholic Worker*—a penny a copy, eventually circulating 150,000. The paper opposed capitalism and communism both, advocated pacifism, personalism, distributism, agrarian communes, and the works of mercy performed by hand. It made no sense as a political program. As a way of life, it made a kind of luminous, impossible sense.

The houses followed. The first Catholic Worker house opened in Day's apartment when she started letting homeless women sleep there. Then they rented space. Then more space. By 1936, there were thirty-three houses around the country. They served meals, provided beds, and asked nothing in return. No means tests, no preaching before feeding, no separating the deserving from the undeserving poor. If you showed up hungry, you ate. This was not social work. Social work involved professional distance. This involved living with the people you served, eating the same food, sleeping in the same buildings, breathing the same smells.

The smell was considerable. Unwashed bodies, cheap wine, untreated illness, the sour smell of despair. The houses were loud, chaotic, frequently dangerous. People stole from each other. They got in fights. They urinated in corners. Day cleaned up the mess, and she insisted that everyone else clean it up too, and she kept insisting that these were ambassadors of God.

The pacifism cost more than the poverty. When World War II came, the Catholic Worker opposed it. This was not a popular position, even among Catholics who loved the paper. Circulation dropped from 150,000 to 30,000. Houses closed. Staff left. She held the line. She opposed the war and the draft and the internment of Japanese Americans, and she opposed them in print, regularly, while the FBI watched her and fellow Catholics called her a traitor.

After the war, she went further. She opposed civil defense drills in New York City, refused to take shelter when the sirens went off. She got arrested for this, repeatedly. She was in her fifties and sixties by then, being hauled off to jail for sitting on a park bench during an air raid drill. She opposed the Cold War, opposed nuclear weapons, opposed the entire logic of deterrence. She went to jail in 1957, 1959, 1961. The judge told her she was being foolish. She agreed that it looked foolish.

The great refusal of her life was to make Christianity reasonable. She wouldn't do it. She insisted that the gospel meant what it said—feed the hungry, shelter the homeless, love your enemies, turn the other cheek. Not in some spiritualized sense. In the actual sense. With actual enemies. With people who smell bad. With people you don't like.

This made her both admired and impossible. Young radicals in the 1960s loved the Catholic Worker. Day loved them less than they expected. When they wanted to simplify the politics, make it about capitalism or imperialism or some single theory, she pulled them back to the works of mercy. When they stopped going to Mass, she noticed. When they slept together casually, she noticed that too. She had done those things, decades ago, and she knew what they cost.

She opposed abortion. This confused people who wanted her to be simply left-wing. She opposed it on the same grounds she opposed war: you don't solve problems by killing. The child in the womb, the enemy in the trench—both were human, both made in the image of God, both deserving of life. The consistency was what mattered to her, not the political alignment.

By the end she had become a figure. Bishops who had once denounced her now praised her. The National Catholic Reporter called her a saint. She hated that. "Don't call me a saint," she said. "I don't want to be dismissed that easily." She knew what the word meant: someone whose life was exceptional, therefore not relevant to yours.

She died in 1980, at eighty-three, in Maryhouse on East Third Street. She had been living in community with the poor for forty-seven years. The houses continue—more than two hundred of them now, still serving free meals, still offering beds, still refusing government funding, still operating on donated money and donated labor and the stubborn insistence that the gospel means what it says.

Her diaries from those last years keep circling back to her failures. She was too harsh with people. She lost her temper. She didn't love enough. She kept trying.

What remains is not a social program. The Catholic Worker doesn't scale. It doesn't replicate easily. It doesn't offer policy solutions. What it offers is an existence proof: that it's possible to take the Sermon on the Mount literally and build a life around it, possible to live with the poor rather than merely help them, possible to oppose both capitalism and communism without retreating into apolitical piety, possible to be a pacifist in wartime, possible to be Catholic and radical in an America where those words didn't fit together.

She did it badly. She was impatient, critical, biting of tongue. She did it anyway. That's the testimony.
