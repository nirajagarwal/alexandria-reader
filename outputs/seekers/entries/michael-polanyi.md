# Michael Polanyi: The Chemist Who Chose Exile

In 1933, Michael Polanyi was a distinguished professor of physical chemistry at the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute in Berlin, publishing groundbreaking work on reaction kinetics and X-ray crystallography. By 1948, he had abandoned chemistry entirely and was teaching social studies at the University of Manchester, writing about things he had no formal training in: epistemology, political philosophy, the nature of scientific discovery. 

What happened in between was not a career change but a defection.

## The First Crossing

Polanyi was born in 1891 in Budapest, into the kind of assimilated Jewish family that produced an improbable number of geniuses in that window before the world collapsed. His father was a railway engineer; his mother ran a salon. His brother Karl became an influential economic historian. His nephew was John Polanyi, who won a Nobel Prize in chemistry—for work building on Michael's.

Michael himself was a doctor first, serving in the Austro-Hungarian army during the First World War. He treated typhus patients on the Serbian front and contracted the disease himself. During his convalescence, unable to do anything else, he wrote a paper on thermodynamics that caught Einstein's attention. He was twenty-six.

After the war, he chose chemistry. Not medicine, not the law his father had wanted—chemistry, which was then revealing the structure of matter itself. By the late 1920s, he was doing world-class work on adsorption and reaction rates, the kind of work that builds toward a Nobel. He had crossed from medicine into fundamental science, from Budapest to Berlin, from a collapsing empire into the center of European intellectual life.

Then the Nazis came.

## What He Refused

Polanyi left Germany in 1933, before he had to. He was offered a chair in physical chemistry at Manchester—not a promotion, not an upgrade, but refuge. He took it. Britain was safe. Manchester was provincial. He could continue his research.

But something had shifted. In Berlin, he had watched people he respected—scientists, intellectuals—accommodate themselves to obvious lies. Not crude lies. Sophisticated ones, dressed in the language of historical necessity and collective purpose. In the Soviet Union, which he visited in 1935, he saw the same pattern in a different costume: scientists proclaiming that pure research was bourgeois individualism, that science must serve the Five-Year Plan, that party doctrine determined what was true about genetics or linguistics or economics.

What disturbed him wasn't the lies themselves but the intellectuals who defended them. Smart people, trained in rigor, who suddenly insisted that objectivity was a mask for class interest, that truth was whatever served the revolution or the Reich. They had not been coerced into these positions; they had reasoned themselves there, or said they had.

In 1936, the Soviet economist Nikolai Bukharin published an essay arguing that scientific truth was determined by social utility. Pure research was a luxury of capitalism. Soviet science would be directed toward concrete social needs. Polanyi read it and understood he was facing something more dangerous than political repression: the claim that the whole idea of independent inquiry was suspect, elitist, a cover for power.

He could not let this stand.

## The Second Crossing

In 1948, at fifty-seven, Polanyi resigned his chemistry chair. Manchester gave him a new title—Professor of Social Studies—with no department, no students, no clear responsibilities. He had walked away from the field where he had authority to write about things he had been thinking about in the margins: how scientists actually make discoveries, what tradition means, whether society could be rationally planned, what we mean when we say we know something.

His colleagues thought he'd lost his mind. You don't abandon a distinguished scientific career to become an amateur philosopher. You don't trade certainty for speculation, authority for irrelevance.

But Polanyi had concluded that the crisis was philosophical, not political. The scientists who bent themselves to serve totalitarian regimes had done so because they believed a story about knowledge: that it was instrumental, that it existed to serve external purposes, that "objectivity" was a myth masking power. If that story was wrong—if knowledge had a different character entirely—then the case against it had to be made philosophically, not just morally.

He spent the next two decades making it.

## Personal Knowledge

*Personal Knowledge*, published in 1958, was Polanyi's attempt to describe how we actually know things, as opposed to how philosophers said we should. The book is dense, strange, difficult. It moves from chemistry to anthropology to art history to Gestalt psychology. It argues that all knowledge is personal—not subjective, not relative, but inescapably shaped by the knower's tacit judgments, embodied skills, commitments.

A scientist learning to see an X-ray crystallography pattern is not just acquiring information. She is being initiated into a tradition, trained to perceive distinctions she could not see before, submitting herself to standards she did not invent. The knowledge is objective—the pattern is really there—but her access to it is mediated by tacit powers that cannot be fully articulated. "We can know more than we can tell."

This was not relativism. Polanyi was arguing for the reality of discovery, not its impossibility. But he was insisting that discovery happened through persons, embedded in traditions, exercising judgment. The ideal of knowledge as a set of explicit, impersonal rules applied mechanically was not just unrealistic; it was false to the phenomenon. Worse, it was dangerous. If you convinced people that real knowledge worked that way, they would distrust the actual knowledge they had—the embodied, traditional, personal kind—and become vulnerable to anyone offering certainty through formulae.

The book was reviewed politely and mostly ignored. Philosophers found it amateurish. Scientists didn't see why they needed it. Polanyi had written a book arguing that the knowing subject could not be eliminated from knowledge—at precisely the moment when Anglo-American philosophy was working hard to eliminate it.

## What Remained

Polanyi died in 1976, having spent the last part of his life elaborating ideas that had no natural home in any discipline. He was too philosophical for scientists, too scientific for philosophers, too concerned with tradition for progressives, too insistent on inquiry for conservatives. *Personal Knowledge* is still in print, read by people who suspect that the standard accounts of knowing don't match their experience.

His other contribution was the concept of tacit knowledge—the idea that we know how to do many things without being able to say how we do them. Riding a bicycle, recognizing a face, diagnosing a disease: the knowledge is real and specific, but it cannot be reduced to rules. This idea has influenced fields from artificial intelligence to organizational theory to the philosophy of science, often in ways Polanyi would not have recognized.

What Polanyi saw, and what made him cross from chemistry into philosophy, was that the freedom to inquire was not self-justifying. It required a substantive account of knowledge—one that acknowledged its personal character without collapsing into relativism, that honored tradition without freezing into dogma, that recognized the authority of reality without pretending knowers were transparent to it.

He gave up a secure position in a field where he had authority to make that case. The case was not widely accepted. But he made it anyway, because the alternative—a world where inquiry was purely instrumental, where truth was whatever served power—was intolerable.

He had already left one country to escape that world. The second crossing was deeper: leaving the work he was trained for to defend the possibility of the work itself.
