# Wendell Berry

In 1964, Wendell Berry left New York. He had a Guggenheim Fellowship, a teaching position at NYU, a book of poems forthcoming from a major press, and all the literary connections a young writer could want. He returned to Kentucky, to Henry County, to a hillside farm his great-great-grandfather had worked, and bought it for $3,500. He was thirty years old. His friends thought he'd lost his mind.

The farm had been worn out by tobacco and neglect. The house had no central heating, no indoor bathroom. Berry and his wife Tanya fixed what they could and learned the rest. They planted trees on eroded slopes, fenced pastures, apprenticed themselves to their Amish neighbors. Berry wrote in the mornings and worked the land in the afternoons. He has not left.

This wasn't romantic agrarianism. It was an intellectual and spiritual discipline disguised as farming. Berry had seen something while teaching on the coasts—a severance between words and consequences, between intelligence and place. The literary world talked endlessly about technique and voice and influence. Nobody talked about where the food came from or what the writer's life did to the ground beneath it.

"I had made a choice that instructed me," he wrote later. The choice was to submit to the terms of a place, to let the place teach him rather than imposing his ideas upon it. The farm wasn't a subject for poetry. It was a form of attention, a way of knowing that couldn't be achieved through abstraction.

He began writing essays. *The Unsettling of America* (1977) attacked industrial agriculture with a precision born from knowing both what farming was supposed to be and what it had become. He could name the machines, cite the yields, trace the subsidies. But the real argument was about membership—the idea that health means belonging to a place and a community over time, accepting limits, paying attention to consequences.

The academy didn't know what to do with him. He wrote about farming, but he wasn't an agricultural scientist. He wrote about economics, but he wasn't an economist. He wrote about culture, but he rejected the progressive assumptions that structured cultural criticism. He was serious about Christianity but suspicious of institutional religion. He defended rural life but attacked rural extractivism. He loved tradition but distrusted nostalgia.

His own tradition was agrarian, Jeffersonian, Southern—but purged of its slavery and racism through discipline and attention. He refused the easy available positions. Urban intellectuals wanted him to celebrate the rural poor as repositories of authentic culture. He wouldn't. Rural people wanted him to defend their way of life against coastal condescension. He did, but not by excusing what needed to change. He praised sustainable farming and attacked the Farm Bureau. He defended the dignity of physical work and condemned the economy that made that work economically impossible.

The FBI opened a file on him in the 1960s after he protested the Vietnam War. He resigned from the University of Kentucky in 1977 over strip mining, then returned years later when he could teach one semester a year and farm the rest. He has spent fifty years writing against the machine—industrial agriculture, industrial education, industrial war, industrial everything—from a place most people drive through without stopping.

The writing kept coming: essays, poems, novels, stories. The Port William stories created a fictional Kentucky community spanning two centuries, tracking how membership frayed and what held anyway. The essays moved from agriculture to forestry to education to technology to war, always returning to the same question: What does it mean to belong responsibly to a place?

He opposed both Gulf Wars, the invasion of Iraq, the entire apparatus of American empire. After 9/11, when most writers rushed to declare their positions, Berry wrote "Thoughts in the Presence of Fear"—seventeen short sections that began by saying the attacks were made possible by the same economy that was destroying American land and community. He was savaged for it. He didn't back down.

In his eighties now, he still farms with horses on the steepest slopes, still writes by hand and has his wife type the manuscripts, still refuses email and lives without a computer. This isn't Luddism—it's choosing the scale at which he can pay attention and be accountable. "I don't think 'Wendell Berry' would be an improvement on me," he said about email addresses.

The work has influenced people who would never consider farming—urban designers, theologians, environmentalists, writers trying to figure out how to live responsibly in late capitalism. But it's also created a cult of Berry, admirers who want to romanticize his choices without understanding what they cost or what they require. He has been sharp about this. The point isn't to become Wendell Berry. The point is to belong somewhere completely enough that the place can teach you what you need to know.

His longest fight has been against strip mining in Kentucky. For forty years he has written, testified, organized, sued. Big coal knows his name. The mountains are still being destroyed, but slower than they would have been. He counts this as success—not victory, but the work that must be done whether it succeeds or not.

Near the end of *Jayber Crow*, his finest novel, the narrator says: "The world has a way of circling back and surprising you. Your old teachers never leave you. The same things come around and come around." Berry has stayed on his hillside long enough to see the circles complete, to know his neighbors' children and grandchildren, to watch trees he planted grow to maturity. He has submitted to the discipline of place long enough that place could teach him what books couldn't.

He once wrote that the question is not how to save the world but how to live in it. On a worn-out Kentucky farm, for sixty years, he has been answering.
