# Hildegard of Bingen

The visions began when she was three. Flames of living light. She saw them in the waking world, laid over ordinary things—trees, people, the stones of the wall. She learned quickly not to speak of them. When she mentioned them to her nurse, the woman's face changed. So Hildegard kept quiet, and the visions continued, and she tried to understand what she was seeing while pretending to see nothing at all.

At eight, her parents gave her to the church. Not unusual for a tenth child, especially a sickly daughter prone to strange episodes. She was enclosed with Jutta, an anchoress who lived in a cell attached to the monastery at Disibodenberg. The cell had a window for receiving food, a window facing the church altar, and that was about it. Jutta taught her to read Latin. The visions did not stop.

For three decades, Hildegard kept her secret. She learned music, medicine, the hours of prayer. She took vows. The visions intensified—not hallucinations (she saw the ordinary world clearly at the same time) but a kind of doubled sight, images of cosmic structure superimposed on reality. Still she said nothing. When Jutta died in 1136 and the women elected Hildegard as magistra, she was forty-two and had spent most of her life silent about the core fact of her experience.

Then in 1141, the command came within a vision: Write what you see and hear. She resisted. She was a woman, unlearned (this was her word, not accurate—she read Latin fluently but hadn't had formal theological training). She had no authority. The visions pressed harder. She fell ill, paralyzed. She understood the illness as refusal meeting demand. When she finally agreed to write, the paralysis lifted.

She needed help. Her Latin was functional but not elegant, and what she was attempting was enormous. She brought in Volmar, a monk who served as secretary and scribe. For ten years they worked on *Scivias*—"Know the Ways"—a text of twenty-six visions documenting the structure of creation, the fall, redemption, the end of time. She saw the cosmos as concentric spheres of light and meaning. She saw the soul as a tent. She saw sin as darkness, salvation as viriditas—greenness, the force of life.

The writing itself was a crossing. Women did not produce theological texts. They received visions, yes—female visionaries were a recognized category, though a suspect one. But they didn't compose systematic theology. They didn't claim to see the architecture of creation and presume to describe it. Hildegard did exactly this. She wrote in the voice of Living Light speaking directly to the reader: "Listen therefore and receive what you see and hear, write it down for others."

She needed authorization. She wrote to Bernard of Clairvaux, the most influential religious figure in Europe. He responded cautiously but positively. She pressed further, getting her text reviewed at the Synod of Trier in 1147. Pope Eugenius III read portions aloud to the assembly and gave his approval. She had, improbably, official permission to speak.

The floodgates opened. She wrote music—not simple chant but elaborate compositions with wild melodic leaps, longer ranges than normal, texts she wrote herself. She composed a morality play, possibly the first of its kind. She wrote on medicine, creating an encyclopedia of natural healing that mixed acute observation (she correctly described the circulation of blood) with cosmic symbolism. She wrote more theology. She founded her own monastery at Rupertsberg, over the objections of the monks at Disibodenberg who didn't want to lose the prestige her presence brought.

She traveled. This was almost unheard of for a cloistered woman. Between 1158 and 1171, in her sixties and seventies, she made four preaching tours through Germany. She preached to clergy, to crowds, to monasteries. She called out corruption in the church, lazy clergy, priests who cared more for comfort than souls. "You who are in authority," she wrote, "you rush around in stupid circles like blind dogs." She could say these things partly because the visions gave her authority that transcended gender, and partly because she was too old and too famous to easily silence.

She corresponded with everyone—four popes, Bernard, Emperor Frederick Barbarossa, kings, abbots, nuns, people seeking healing or advice. Her letters are specific and fierce. To one young woman struggling with desire for a particular person: "Your heart is like a wall with a hidden gate in it, and you keep going through that gate." To a priest uncertain of his vocation: "Your soul is like a stone that wants to be a fruit." She saw through people, and she told them what she saw.

The medicine is extraordinary. Her *Physica* and *Causae et Curae* document hundreds of plants, animals, stones, their properties and uses. Some of it is period-standard (swallow dung for eye problems), but much shows careful observation. She wrote about female sexuality with a frankness unusual for the time, describing pleasure and explaining conception. She understood illness as connected to mental and spiritual states—"when the soul is burdened, the body grows weak."

The cosmology was her real obsession. She saw everything as alive with divine energy, structured in relationships of correspondence. The human being was a microcosm, a miniature version of the cosmos. The elements in creation matched elements in the body. Music followed cosmic ratios. Prayer worked because it aligned human vibration with divine frequency. Modern readers sometimes try to make her a proto-scientist or a proto-feminist. She was neither. She thought completely within her moment, but she thought with an intensity that broke open new spaces within that moment's limits.

She fought with Frederick Barbarossa over papal authority. She resisted bishops who tried to control her monastery. Near the end of her life, her community buried an excommunicated man in their cemetery. The diocesan authorities ordered her to exhume the body. She refused. They placed the entire monastery under interdict—no sacraments, no music, no liturgy. For Hildegard, who heard music as the cosmos praising itself, the silence was devastating. She was eighty years old. She wrote to the prelates, defending her position in a letter that mixed theological argument with barely controlled fury. Eventually the interdict was lifted, but the episode shows how far she would go when pushed.

She died in 1179 at eighty-one, her monastery flourishing, her writings circulating. Two biographies appeared almost immediately. The canonization process began but stalled, repeatedly, over centuries. She was finally declared a saint in 2012, made a Doctor of the Church—official recognition that her theological writings carried authority.

The visions never stopped. To the end, she saw the flames of living light, the doubled sight of ordinary things transfigured. She had learned to live inside them, work inside them, speak from inside them. What drove her was the demand to make visible what she saw, to translate the wordless light into forms others could receive. What she crossed was the boundary between private vision and public speech, between woman and authority, between experience and theology. What it cost was her health repeatedly, and a life spent arguing for the right to say what she knew. What remains is a body of work unlike anything else in medieval Europe—fierce, strange, systematic, alive with what she called viriditas, the greening force that makes things grow.
