# Martha Nussbaum

In 1972, Martha Nussbaum sat in a seminar room at Harvard, pregnant with her first child, listening to a professor explain why women couldn't really do philosophy. Their bodies got in the way. Their emotions clouded judgment. Philosophy required a kind of abstract purity that the female condition precluded.

She was already reading Greek tragedy in the original, already thinking about what those plays knew that analytic philosophy had forgotten: that human beings have bodies, vulnerabilities, needs. That we're animals who can think, not pure minds temporarily housed in meat. That the capacity for reason doesn't separate us from our fragility—it makes us more aware of it.

Twenty years later, she would publish *The Fragility of Goodness*, showing how ancient philosophy grappled with what modern philosophy tried to wish away: that being human means being subject to luck, catastrophe, and forces beyond your control. That the good life isn't something you achieve through pure reason and then possess securely. That love, grief, and dependence aren't obstacles to human flourishing but central to what flourishing means.

But in 1972, she was still working out whether she belonged in philosophy at all.

## The Classical Turn

Nussbaum came to philosophy through theater and Greek. As an undergraduate at NYU, she studied acting, immersed herself in classical languages, wrote plays. She loved the texts but chafed at how they were taught—as museum pieces, dead languages for the cultivation of the genteel. She wanted to know what Aeschylus and Euripides understood about rage, loss, recognition. What happened in those moments when a character suddenly *saw*.

Philosophy seemed to promise tools for that investigation. So she went to Harvard for graduate school, entering a department dominated by figures like W.V.O. Quine, who saw philosophy as continuous with natural science. Rigorous, technical, committed to logical clarity and skeptical of woolly appeals to emotion or literature.

She learned the tools. She could parse formal logic, track arguments through dense technical prose, hold her own in seminars where precision mattered more than passion. But she kept returning to the tragedies, where precision looked different. Where wisdom came through recognition scenes, where the most important truths couldn't be separated from their dramatic rendering.

Her dissertation on Aristotle's *De Motu Animalium* was technically accomplished, philosophically serious—and already beginning to think about embodiment, about what it means that thinking happens in bodies that move, need, respond.

## The Vulnerability Question

The real breakthrough came when she stopped treating literature as decoration for philosophical arguments and started seeing it as a form of moral philosophy.

*The Fragility of Goodness* took up plays, poems, and philosophical dialogues together—not as examples of abstract principles but as different ways of investigating the same terrain. What do we owe to each other? What role does luck play in the good life? How do we respond to the fact that the people we love can suffer, leave, die?

The Stoics thought you could achieve invulnerability through philosophy. Control what's within your power—your judgments, your will—and be indifferent to everything else. Don't love anything you can lose. The wise man is self-sufficient, needing nothing outside himself.

Nussbaum thought this was both psychologically impossible and ethically impoverished. We're not made for self-sufficiency. A good human life requires external goods: friends, family, health, political circumstances that allow flourishing. These are real goods, not just preferred indifferents. And because they're external, they're subject to luck. You can do everything right and still lose what matters most.

This isn't a design flaw to be fixed. It's what gives human life its particular shape and weight. The capacity to love depends on vulnerability. Deep friendships matter precisely because they involve stakes. Grief isn't a failure of philosophy—it's what love turns into when the beloved dies.

She was saying philosophy had spent two millennia trying to transcend a condition it should have been illuminating.

## Chicago and the Public Turn

In 1995, Nussbaum moved to the University of Chicago Law School and the Philosophy Department simultaneously—a joint appointment that signaled where her work was headed. Not just toward understanding human vulnerability but toward building institutions and policies that take it seriously.

She started writing about disability, animal welfare, constitutional law. If human beings are vulnerable, dependent creatures who need certain conditions to flourish, what does that mean for how we organize society? For education, healthcare, legal rights?

With Amartya Sen, she developed the capabilities approach: a framework for thinking about justice that asks not just about resources distributed but about what people are actually able to do and be. Can they participate in political life? Form attachments? Use their senses, imagination, and thought? The question isn't whether we've given everyone an equal pile of stuff but whether we've created conditions for genuine flourishing.

This meant taking seriously things liberal political philosophy often bracketed: emotions, care work, the body, dependence. A just society isn't one that treats everyone as if they were independent contractors negotiating from positions of strength. It's one that acknowledges that we're all, at various points, dependent—as children, as elderly people, as sick people, as people who love.

The work made her influential in legal and policy circles, often in tension with her academic reputation. Some philosophers thought she'd gone soft, traded rigor for relevance. Some activists thought her approach too tame, not radical enough. She kept writing books at a pace that seemed designed to make both camps uncomfortable: *Hiding from Humanity* on the political role of emotions, *Frontiers of Justice* on disability and animal rights, *Not for Profit* on education, *The Monarchy of Fear* on anger and political life.

## The Rage Problem

Throughout, she circled back to anger. What is it? What does it want? Is it ever productive?

For a long time, the answer looked like this: anger at injustice is warranted, even necessary. It motivates change, signals boundaries, refuses complicity. The anger of the oppressed isn't the same as the anger of the oppressor. Some rage is righteous.

Then in *Anger and Forgiveness*, she shifted. Even warranted anger, she argued, tends toward a logic of payback—the desire to see the wrongdoer suffer. This doesn't undo the wrong. It doesn't repair what was broken. It just adds more suffering to the ledger.

What works better is what she called Transition-Anger: anger that moves toward addressing the wrong, changing conditions, preventing recurrence. Anger that doesn't stop at wanting the other to hurt but pushes toward something constructive.

The book landed badly with many readers. To people involved in social justice movements, it sounded like tone policing. Don't be angry, be productive. Control yourself. Several pointed out that Nussbaum was advising the oppressed to regulate their anger while saying little about the rage of the powerful.

She insisted she wasn't policing anyone's emotions, just investigating their logic. But the tension revealed something about her project: the distance between philosophical investigation and political necessity, between what makes sense in principle and what it feels like to be told to manage your rage when the people harming you face no consequences.

## The Private and Professional

Nussbaum's personal life kept leaking into the work, though she rarely discussed it directly. Her first marriage, to Alan Nussbaum, was troubled; her daughter Rachel later described an upbringing shaped by her mother's drive and her father's alcoholism. The marriage ended badly. So did her second, to the economist Cass Sunstein.

She worked constantly—teaching, writing, speaking, consulting. Colleagues described her as formidable, brilliant, and demanding. Some found her generous; others found her domineering. She seemed to have little patience for the distinction between working and living. Philosophy wasn't something she did; it was what she was.

In *Upheavals of Thought*, she wrote about her mother's death, about sitting by the bedside, about what it means to love someone who's disappearing. The prose is careful, philosophical—but you can feel the undertow. This is what the Stoics couldn't account for: the devastation of presence becoming absence, the way love binds you to what you can't control.

## The Cross-Disciplinary Thing

Nussbaum's reach—philosophy, classics, law, literature, feminism, animal rights—made her difficult to categorize. Classicists thought she was too philosophical. Philosophers thought she was too literary. Legal scholars appreciated the capabilities approach but worried about its application. Political theorists debated whether emotions belonged in public reason.

She seemed unbothered by disciplinary gatekeeping. The questions she followed didn't respect boundaries, so why should she? If you wanted to understand how human beings actually live—not how ideal rational agents might behave under perfect conditions—you needed tragedy and novels and law cases and empirical research and philosophical argument.

This made her work enormously influential and also exposed her to criticism from multiple directions simultaneously. Analytic philosophers complained about insufficient rigor. Continental philosophers complained about insufficient attention to power. Empiricists wanted more data. Activists wanted more fire.

She kept writing, crossing borders, refusing to stay in her lane.

## What Remains

The philosophical establishment never quite knew what to do with Nussbaum. She won major awards, held prestigious chairs, influenced policy debates worldwide. But she also remained slightly outside—too interdisciplinary, too public, too willing to write op-eds and testify before Congress and engage with non-philosophers.

What she left is a body of work that takes seriously a simple, radical idea: that being human means being vulnerable, and that philosophy shouldn't try to transcend this fact but to think through it. That the capacity for reason doesn't free us from need but makes us capable of understanding what we need and why. That a good life isn't one that's protected from loss but one that's open to the loves and commitments that make loss possible.

The capabilities approach is now used by development agencies, courts, and policymakers. Students read *Cultivating Humanity* in education programs and *Not for Profit* in debates about the humanities. *Hiding from Humanity* and *The Monarchy of Fear* shape discussions about emotion in political life.

But the deeper contribution may be the example: of someone who followed questions across every boundary that might have contained them, who thought philosophy mattered enough to bring it into the messiest, most difficult territories of human life. Who kept asking what we owe to each other, knowing there's no answer that doesn't involve acknowledging our own fragility and the fragility of those we love.

She's still writing, still crossing boundaries, still defending the humanities against those who think education should be purely instrumental. Still insisting that literature matters, that emotions matter, that the body matters, that animals matter, that disability matters, that vulnerability isn't weakness but the ground of our shared humanity.

Still a Stoic's nightmare: someone who thinks philosophy should make us more aware of what we can't control, not less.
