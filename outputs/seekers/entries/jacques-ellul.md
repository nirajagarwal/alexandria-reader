# Jacques Ellul

The law professor worked on the Bordeaux docks during the Occupation, unloading crates to avoid starving. At night he wrote *The Theological Foundation of Law*. The Gestapo came looking for him twice. He hid Jews in his house. When the war ended, he had tuberculosis and a manuscript arguing that technique—not just technology, but the entire apparatus of modern efficiency—was reorganizing human existence at a level deeper than politics.

He published it in 1954 as *La Technique*. The book argued that modern society had become autonomous, self-directing, indifferent to human purposes. Technique was not a tool humans used but an environment they inhabited. It demanded efficiency as its sole criterion. It transformed everything it touched—work, politics, education, sex—into problems requiring technical solutions. It was beyond ideology. Capitalism and communism were both expressions of the same technical imperialism.

The French academy ignored it. When it appeared in English a decade later as *The Technological Society*, American intellectuals noticed. Aldous Huxley wrote the introduction. The New York Times called it "disturbing." Students in the 1960s read it and recognized their world—the university as machine, the war as system, the revolution itself becoming technique.

Ellul kept teaching law in Bordeaux. He never took a position in Paris. He stayed on the periphery, physically and intellectually. He was a Protestant in Catholic France, a Marxist who rejected Marx's faith in progress, a Christian who distrusted the institutional church, a technological critic who used technology. When students asked why he owned a car and a television if technique was enslaving humanity, he said: "I'm describing the water we're drowning in. That doesn't mean I can breathe it."

He wrote fifty books. They divided into two streams that never fully merged: the sociological analysis of technique and the theological reflection on freedom. The sociology had no explicit God. The theology had no explicit politics. Critics on the left found his Christianity reactionary. Critics on the right found his Marxist analysis suspect. The academy didn't know what to do with someone who wouldn't choose.

He was born in Bordeaux in 1912, son of a small business owner who went bankrupt. The family was nominally Orthodox Christian but essentially secular. At eighteen, reading Marx, he became a communist. At nineteen, reading the Bible, he became a Christian. The combination should have been incoherent. For Ellul it was generative. Marx taught him to see structure. Christianity taught him that structure wasn't final.

In the 1930s he organized with the Front Populaire. When war came, he joined the Resistance, first in propaganda, then hiding refugees. The technical apparatus of occupation showed him how thoroughly modern systems could colonize conscience. Good people doing their jobs became the mechanism of evil. The system required no malice, only competence.

After the war he worked in the mayor's office, implementing urban reform. He quit after a year. Reform became administration. Administration required technique. Technique undermined the human purposes that had justified reform. The circle closed. He returned to the university, convinced that modern politics was trapped in a logic it couldn't escape.

*The Technological Society* described that trap. Technique was rational, cumulative, self-augmenting. Once you solved a problem technically, the solution became the foundation for the next technique. There was no reversing it, no stepping outside. Political revolution changed who controlled the apparatus; it didn't change the apparatus. The Soviets were as committed to technical efficiency as the Americans. Both organized society around productivity, measurement, optimization. The content of ideology mattered less than the form of organization.

He didn't argue for returning to pre-technical society. That was impossible and probably undesirable. He argued for consciousness. Technique worked by making itself invisible, presenting its demands as simple rationality. Naming it, seeing it, was the first act of freedom.

The theological work explored what freedom might mean in a technical civilization. Not freedom *from* technique—that was romantic fantasy. Freedom *within* it. Freedom as grace, as gift, as the possibility that emerged when you stopped believing technique could save you. His theology was dark, dialectical, anti-systematic. God was hidden. The church was compromised. Christianity in modernity was mostly propaganda, technique in religious dress. Real faith meant rejecting every system, including theological ones.

He angered everyone. The right wanted him to defend tradition. The left wanted him to endorse revolution. He did neither. Tradition had been colonized by technique—the church ran like a corporation. Revolution had been colonized too—the radicals planned the future with flow charts. The only position left was permanent refusal, what he called "criticism without possibility."

He meant it literally. He didn't think the technical system could be reformed or replaced. He thought it would probably continue until it collapsed under its own weight. In the meantime, the human task was to remain human: to love, to play, to make art, to pray, to refuse the demand for total efficiency. These weren't solutions. They were acts of witness.

In Bordeaux he gardened. He wrote. He taught students who mostly disagreed with him. He stayed married to the same woman for fifty-seven years. He died in 1994, still refusing every system, still working, still hoping without optimism that freedom might find a way to survive.

Posthumously his reputation fractured. The ecology movement claimed him—he'd warned about technological destruction of nature. The localists claimed him—he'd argued against globalization. The traditionalists claimed him—he'd criticized modernity. Tech critics claimed him—he'd anticipated surveillance, social media, the totalization of data.

He would have rejected all of it. The problem with being claimed is that claiming is itself a technique. You extract the useful parts, systematize them, turn them into a program. That's exactly what he spent fifty years arguing against.

What remains is less a body of work than a posture: the refusal to make peace with necessity. Ellul believed the technical system was enormously powerful, probably unstoppable, and absolutely not inevitable in the sense of being necessary or good. Those three things together—the recognition of power, the acknowledgment of probable defeat, the refusal to concede legitimacy—defined his witness.

He wasn't hopeful. He didn't think critique changed much. He kept going anyway, kept writing, kept naming the water, kept insisting that drowning wasn't the same as breathing. Whether that's pessimism or realism or a strange kind of faith depends on whether you think witness matters when it doesn't produce results.
