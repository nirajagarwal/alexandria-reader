# Rowan Williams

The Archbishop of Canterbury sat in his study at Lambeth Palace, translating Russian poetry. Outside, reporters waited for a statement on women bishops, or sexuality, or whatever crisis had arrived that week. Inside, Williams worked through the syntax of a Pasternak verse, attending to what the words actually said before they calcified into positions.

This was not procrastination. For Williams, the work of theology began in that kind of attention—slowing down enough to hear what was being said before deciding what you thought about it. Which made him possibly the worst-equipped person imaginable to lead the Anglican Communion through its decade of near-dissolution.

He became Archbishop in 2002, a Welsh poet-theologian known for books nobody pretended were easy to read. *The Wound of Knowledge* traced Christian spirituality through the tension between what can be said about God and what can't. *On Christian Theology* wrestled with language, embodiment, and the problem of speaking truthfully about things that exceed our capacity for speech. His lectures were famous—or infamous—for sentences that folded back on themselves, qualifying, nuancing, opening rather than closing.

The church wanted a leader. They got a contemplative who thought leadership might mean creating space for difficult conversations rather than winning them.

The sexuality debates nearly destroyed him. Not because he couldn't take a position—he had one, relatively progressive by Anglican standards—but because he believed the church had to stay together through the disagreement. This meant absorbing attacks from both sides. Conservatives saw him as weak, unwilling to defend orthodoxy. Progressives saw him as cowardly, sacrificing justice to preserve institutional unity.

Both might have been right. Or both might have missed what Williams was attempting: not compromise as failure but communion as a practice more fundamental than being correct. He believed the church was bound by something deeper than shared opinions—by baptism, Eucharist, the disciplines of attention that formed people into a body. He thought the body could hold disagreement without fracture.

He was probably wrong. The Anglican Communion splintered anyway. African bishops boycotted Lambeth Conferences. American dioceses fled to form their own structures. Williams spent ten years trying to hold together a thing that was already coming apart, each attempt earning him contempt from people who wanted him to choose their side.

What made it theological rather than just political failure was that Williams never stopped doing the other work—the writing, the teaching, the translation. Even as he negotiated with bishops who thought he was destroying the church, he published *Dostoevsky: Language, Faith and Fiction*, tracing how the novels make visible the cost and gift of recognition. He wrote about icons, about the desert fathers, about what it means to read Scripture as spiritual discipline rather than just source material for arguments.

The gap between these two modes—institutional management and contemplative attention—never closed. His supporters blamed the institutional pressures for preventing his best work. His critics noted that he'd accepted the job. Both observations held.

He stepped down in 2012, exhausted and probably relieved. Returned to Cambridge as Master of Magdalene College, where he could be what he'd always been: a teacher attending to difficult texts with students who'd chosen to be there. Continued writing—on Bonhoeffer, on justice, on the theological imagination. His recent work circles questions of how communities sustain practices of attention when everything militates toward speed, certainty, conclusion.

His books remain difficult. Not ostentatiously so—Williams isn't showing off. They're difficult because the questions are difficult, because easy answers flatten what needs to stay complex. Reading him requires the same discipline he practices: slowing down, staying with the tension, resisting premature resolution.

The question that drives Williams is about attention itself—how we attend to God, to texts, to each other, to reality as it presents itself rather than as we need it to be. Everything he's written circles this: the patience required to let something speak before we speak about it, the discipline of waiting when every instinct says to move, conclude, decide.

He learned this from poets and novelists as much as from theologians. From Welsh-language poetry, where he first encountered language working at the edge of what it could carry. From Dostoevsky, whose characters struggle not with ideas but with the impossible proximity of other people. From the contemplative tradition, which treats slowness not as failure of decision but as form of faithfulness.

The cost wasn't just the decade at Lambeth. The cost was watching people assume that because he wouldn't fight the way they wanted him to fight, he wasn't fighting at all. Being called weak when he was attempting something harder than strength: creating conditions where people could hear each other before they denounced each other.

He probably failed. The church split anyway. But what remains isn't just the institutional record—it's the body of work that kept insisting there was something worth attending to beneath the positions, something that required different practices than arguing and winning.

Williams is still writing, still translating, still teaching. The hair is whiter, the face more lined. He moves slower, speaks softer. But the questions haven't changed: What does it mean to attend? What practices form us into people capable of real recognition? How do we speak truthfully about things that exceed our speech?

These aren't questions that get answered. They're questions that, if attended to properly, reorganize how you live. Williams has been following them for fifty years. The path has led through poetry, theology, institutional catastrophe, and back to the disciplines that made him: reading slowly, writing carefully, translating what resists translation, creating space for attention in a world that mostly wants conclusions.

Whether that constitutes success or failure probably depends on what you think theology is for. Williams thinks it's for truth, not victory. Which means the measure of his work isn't whether he won but whether he saw clearly and said truthfully what he saw. By that standard, the verdict is still open. We're still reading. Still deciding whether what he saw was real or just the vision of someone who couldn't do what the job required.
