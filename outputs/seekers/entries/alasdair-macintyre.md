# Alasdair MacIntyre

In 1977, Alasdair MacIntyre gave a lecture at Boston College that began: "Imagine that the natural sciences were to suffer the effects of a catastrophe." Not a nuclear war exactly, but something worse—a social collapse that destroyed not just laboratories but the very ability to understand what laboratories had been for. Future generations would inherit fragments: equations they couldn't derive, instruments they couldn't operate, terms whose meanings had been lost. They would speak of "neutrinos" and "mass" while performing rituals that resembled science but had become, essentially, theater.

This wasn't a thought experiment about physics. It was a diagnosis of moral philosophy in the twentieth century.

MacIntyre was forty-eight, already on his third career, his fourth country, and his sixth university. He'd been a Marxist, an Anglican, briefly sympathetic to British linguistic philosophy, then repelled by it. He'd taught in Manchester, Leeds, Essex, Princeton, Brandeis. He'd written books on Marxism, Freud, Hegel, the history of ethics. Nothing had stuck. Colleagues found him brilliant and impossible—someone who changed positions too often, argued too hard, seemed to be searching for something the profession couldn't provide.

The lecture became *After Virtue* (1981), a book that announced the Enlightenment project of grounding morality in universal reason had failed, catastrophically, and that we were all arguing in a language we no longer understood. When someone invoked "rights" or "justice" or "utility," they weren't making claims that could be rationally adjudicated—they were expressing preferences with the grammar of objectivity. Moral philosophy had become emotivism in disguise, and modern liberal society was a wasteland of incommensurable claims with no shared framework for resolution.

The contemporary reader could be forgiven for hearing this as postmodern relativism. It was the opposite. MacIntyre's claim was that the fragments *meant* something once, within traditions that gave them life. And one tradition in particular—Aristotelian virtue ethics as developed through medieval Christianity—had been abandoned too quickly. The modern self, stripped of telos and communal identity, pursuing self-invention in a market of lifestyles, was a disaster. What we needed wasn't new theory. It was recovery.

This was not a popular diagnosis in analytic philosophy departments.

*After Virtue* made MacIntyre famous and more unemployable. Universities that celebrated pluralism discovered they preferred the kind that didn't question pluralism's foundations. MacIntyre moved to Vanderbilt, then Notre Dame, then Duke, often leaving under circumstances his colleagues described vaguely as "difficult." He argued in seminar rooms like a medieval disputant—devastating, ungenerous, uninterested in making his opponents feel heard. Students emerged shaken. The work mattered; working with MacIntyre was often miserable.

The intellectual position got stranger. In *Whose Justice? Which Rationality?* (1988), he argued there was no neutral rationality standing above traditions—you reasoned *within* a tradition, using its concepts and standards. This sounded relativist until you noticed he also believed traditions could fail, degenerate, be shown inadequate by their own standards or defeated in encounter with rivals. He wasn't saying all traditions were equal. He was saying the Enlightenment's claim to have transcended tradition was itself a tradition, and a failing one.

Then, in his sixties, after decades writing as a sophisticated philosophical historian, MacIntyre published *Dependent Rational Animals* (1999). The title was deliberate—we are animals, embodied, vulnerable, dependent from birth to senescence. Modern moral philosophy pretended to be about rational autonomous agents making choices. But most of human life was about dependence: infancy, illness, old age, disability. The virtues that mattered most weren't heroic self-assertion but the patient, unglamorous work of care. His mother had been disabled; he'd seen it.

The book arrived like a stone through the window of both liberal and communitarian theory. For decades, MacIntyre had been arguing about tradition and rationality and the metaphysics of morals. Now he was saying: you've all been theorizing about the wrong thing. Look at childhood. Look at dementia. Look at what it takes to raise a child or care for someone who will never be independent. Those dependencies aren't exceptions to moral life—they're the center of it.

By then he'd returned to Catholicism, the faith of his Scottish childhood, abandoned in his twenties. He arrived at it through Aquinas, which is a very MacIntyre way to return to anything—intellectually first, arguing his way back through seven centuries of theology and philosophy. He attended Latin Mass, wrote about animals and Aquinas, developed positions on medical ethics and disability that derived from virtue theory and drove colleagues in bioethics to distraction.

In his eighties, still teaching, he published essays on political economy and ecology that argued—with characteristic refusal to soften the blow—that late capitalism was incompatible with human flourishing, that the local economy was the only moral economy, that the nation-state was a dead end. The positions were now so far outside academic consensus that younger scholars sometimes cited him as a figure from intellectual history, like someone citing Duns Scotus.

MacIntyre never founded a school, exactly, though "MacIntyrean" became a term in ethics. He was too prickly, too willing to abandon positions once they'd served their purpose, too uninterested in building institutions. What he left was an example of what rigorous philosophical thinking looked like when it refused to accept the boundaries of its professional moment—when it followed questions about the good life and rational justification wherever they led, even if they led to medieval monasteries and local economies and the scandal of human dependency.

He died in 2024, ninety-five years old, having spent seven decades arguing that modern moral philosophy was based on a mistake and that the resources for repair lay in traditions most of his colleagues considered obsolete. The profession didn't follow him. But it couldn't ignore him. He'd shown that the emperor had no clothes, and even those who thought he was wrong about the solution couldn't quite shake the diagnosis.

Some lives reorganize themselves around a question. MacIntyre's question was: what if everything we think about ethics since the Enlightenment rests on a catastrophe we don't remember? He built an entire career on that question, burning through positions and universities, following the logic wherever it went. The answer he arrived at—that we needed not progress but recovery, not liberation but tradition, not autonomy but acknowledgment of dependence—was almost perfectly calculated to alienate everyone.

He offered it anyway.
