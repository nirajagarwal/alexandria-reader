# Abraham Joshua Heschel: The Rebbe Who Marched

When Abraham Joshua Heschel flew back from Selma in March 1965, a reporter asked him to talk about the march. "I felt my legs were praying," he said.

It was a strange thing for a theologian to say. Strange and precise. For Heschel, the body was never separate from the spirit, action never separate from devotion, the street never separate from the sanctuary. He had spent twenty years trying to explain this to American Jews who wanted religion to be either rational or emotional, either universal or particular, either intellectual or activist. He kept insisting it had to be all of it, all at once, or it wasn't religion at all.

He was born in Warsaw in 1907, descended from Hasidic dynasties on both sides. This wasn't metaphorical lineage—his father was the Rebbe of Medzibozh, his ancestors included the Maggid of Mezeritch. He grew up expecting to inherit a world. In photographs from his childhood, he looks like what he was: a boy being prepared for spiritual authority in a tradition twelve generations deep.

But the Warsaw where he came of age was not Medzibozh. He studied at the University of Berlin, wrote a dissertation on prophetic consciousness, moved in circles where Buber was a contemporary and Neo-Kantianism was the serious philosophy. When the Nazis deported him to Poland in 1938, he was caught between worlds—too modern for the traditionalists, too traditional for the moderns, his Hasidic lineage both credential and liability.

He got out six weeks before the invasion. His mother and three sisters did not.

In New York, teaching at Hebrew Union College and later Jewish Theological Seminary, he wrote books that read like nothing else in mid-century American theology. *Man Is Not Alone*. *God in Search of Man*. *The Sabbath*. They weren't apologetics and they weren't scholarship exactly. They were attempts to translate a way of being religious that American Judaism had largely abandoned—ecstatic, embodied, attentive to wonder—into a vocabulary that modern intellectuals might hear.

"Wonder rather than doubt is the root of all knowledge," he wrote. In a culture committed to demystification, this was heresy. He meant it literally. The prophets didn't argue for God's existence; they testified to having been grasped by something that unmade and remade them. Religion began not in questions but in being questioned, not in searching but in being found.

This wasn't mysticism as escape. Heschel reserved his sharpest words for religion that consoled but didn't disturb. "The opposite of good is not evil," he wrote. "The opposite of good is indifference." The prophets screamed because God cared about specific things—widows, orphans, the exploitation of the poor, the corruption of worship into performance. "What manner of man is the prophet?" he asked. "A sweeping condemnation, unnerving and frightening."

He took this seriously enough to risk his position. In 1965 he was sixty, established, respected. He could have stayed home, written letters of support, let younger people march. Instead he linked arms with Martin Luther King Jr. and walked through Selma. The photograph shows him in his yarmulke and dark suit, white beard brilliant in the Alabama sun, looking utterly out of place and utterly present.

The Jewish establishment was horrified. What was a scholar doing in a protest march? Wasn't he supposed to be above politics? But Heschel had spent his career arguing that this distinction was false. "Racism is Satanism," he wrote. "It is an outrage that prayer in America is debatable while discrimination is accepted." For him, marching was not a departure from theology but its enactment. The prophets hadn't only spoken; they had embodied their message until it consumed them.

Vietnam was worse. While colleagues wrote careful position papers, Heschel called the war "blasphemy." Not mistaken policy—blasphemy, a religious category, an offense against God. He testified before the Senate. He joined Clergy and Laymen Concerned About Vietnam with Niebuhr and King. He kept pushing, kept naming, kept refusing to let religion retreat into safety.

The cost was real. Conservative Jews saw him as a radical. Secular radicals saw him as religious. He was too demanding for liberals, too prophetic for traditionalists, too particular for universalists, too universal for particularists. His insistence that Hasidic depth and prophetic justice belonged together left him comprehensible to almost no one.

He also wrote things that now look troubling. His opposition to intermarriage was absolute. His view of women's roles was traditional. His few writings on Christianity show both generosity and limitation. He contained contradictions he never fully resolved—the Hasidic rebbe and the modern intellectual, the mystical Jew and the social prophet, sharing one body that was itself part of the message.

His last book, published in 1972, was *A Passion for Truth*. It juxtaposed two figures: the Kotzker Rebbe, who withdrew from the world in pursuit of absolute authenticity, and Kierkegaard, who did something similar in Copenhagen. Heschel spent three hundred pages circling the question of whether truth required withdrawal or engagement, isolation or incarnation. He never resolved it. Six months after publication, he died.

What remains is a body of work that still reads like an interruption. "We worry about what to do with our time," he wrote, "but the real issue is what to do with ourselves." The Sabbath as palace in time rather than space. Prayer as "an invitation to God to interfere in our lives." The prophets as people who couldn't stop themselves from speaking even when speaking destroyed them. Judaism as something that happened in your legs, your hands, your willingness to show up when showing up cost something.

Photographs of Heschel show a man with fierce eyes and a white beard, looking like he'd just arrived from the nineteenth century or maybe the first. In his face you can see both the Hasidic court and the protest march, both the study house and the street. He spent his life insisting they were the same place, that the prayer was the walking, that you couldn't have one without the other.

American religion mostly didn't believe him. It wanted the prayer or the walking, the tradition or the justice, the particular or the universal. Heschel kept offering both, kept embodying the connection, kept showing up with his whole self until there was nothing left to give.

"When I was young," he said near the end, "I used to admire clever people. Now that I am old, I admire kind people." It was the closest he came to a program. Not clever. Not impressive. Kind—which for Heschel meant attentive, responsive, unable to walk past suffering without feeling your legs begin to pray.
