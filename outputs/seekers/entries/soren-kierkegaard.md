# Søren Kierkegaard

The young man wrote under pseudonyms, published books attacking each other, and signed the attacks with different names. He did this in Copenhagen, a city small enough that everyone knew it was him. In 1843, the same year, three books appeared: *Either/Or* (signed "Victor Eremita"), *Fear and Trembling* (signed "Johannes de Silentio"), and *Repetition* (signed "Constantin Constantius"). They disagreed with each other. They argued different positions on love, faith, ethics, despair. Readers trying to determine what Søren Kierkegaard believed found themselves in a maze where every exit led back to the entrance.

This was deliberate. "I am not a Christian," he wrote in his journal, even as he published some of the most penetrating Christian reflections of the century. The point was not to hide but to refuse the easy position: the expert, the professor, the authority handing down conclusions. He wanted readers to feel their way through the questions themselves, to encounter the difficulty of existing rather than just thinking about existence.

The architecture of this refusal had a foundation: Regine Olsen. He met her when she was fourteen, proposed when she was seventeen, and then broke the engagement after a year. The breaking took planning. He needed her to reject him, not the other way around, so he made himself rejectable—cold, erratic, cruel. It worked. She married someone else. He spent the rest of his life writing about her, or not about her but about what happened to him when he chose his work over his life.

The question at the center was whether a person could live religiously in the modern world. Not whether they could believe in God—plenty of people in Copenhagen believed in God, attended church, considered themselves Christians. The question was whether faith was compatible with the kind of existence being constructed around him: rational, comfortable, explainable, a matter of correct doctrine and social position.

His answer was no. Faith was not comfort. It was terror. Abraham, walking up the mountain to sacrifice Isaac, couldn't explain himself to anyone, not even to Sarah. He moved past the ethical—past what could be justified in rational or moral terms—into a direct, inexplicable relation with God. If you could explain it, it wasn't faith. If it made sense within the community's moral framework, it wasn't the movement of faith. "The knight of faith," Kierkegaard wrote, "looks like a tax collector." He goes to work, loves his wife, eats his dinner. Inwardly, he has made the movement that puts everything at stake. But no one can see it. There's no outward sign.

This was not reassuring theology. Danish Christianity in the 1840s was reassuring—cultured, refined, integrated with social life and national identity. Bishop Mynster presided over it with dignity. Kierkegaard's father, a wealthy merchant haunted by guilt, attended Mynster's services. The bishop spoke beautifully about Christ. The congregation felt elevated. Christianity was a civilizing force, a source of meaning that complemented rather than threatened ordinary life.

Kierkegaard saw this as a lie. Not a deliberate lie—worse. An unconscious one. Christianity had been domesticated, turned into something that asked nothing. The "leap of faith" had become a comfortable step into an accepted position. No one was offended by Christ anymore. No one found Christianity absurd. This meant Christianity was no longer Christianity.

The books appeared in rapid succession through the 1840s. *The Concept of Anxiety* (1844), *Stages on Life's Way* (1845), *Concluding Unscientific Postscript* (1846). Each approached the question from a different angle, in a different voice. The aesthetic life—the life of sensation, irony, possibility—was taken seriously, explored, shown to collapse into despair. The ethical life—duty, marriage, social responsibility—was honored and then shown to be insufficient for the religious. The religious couldn't be reached by climbing a ladder of development. It required a break, a paradox, the acceptance of absurdity.

In 1846, a humor magazine called *The Corsair* began mocking him. It was a scandal sheet, and Kierkegaard had been safe from it because the editor admired his work. When Kierkegaard deliberately provoked an attack—he wanted to be mocked, wanted to experience what it felt like to be the exception—the magazine obliged. They mocked his walk, his trousers, his hunched back. Children on the street shouted at him. For someone who already felt isolated, who already understood himself as fundamentally alone with his questions, the public ridicule confirmed what he knew: truth was not socially successful.

His father had told him something as a child. The old man, out walking with young Søren, had once cursed God as a boy while freezing on the Jutland heath. This curse, the father believed, explained everything—his financial success (undeserved), the deaths of his children (punishment), the melancholy that soaked the household. Søren inherited this melancholy, this sense of being wrong somehow, marked. It made him brilliant. It also made ordinary happiness impossible.

When Regine married, Kierkegaard was at the wedding. Not inside—outside, watching. He couldn't stop watching. Years later, he saw her on the street and bowed. She nodded back. These tiny movements contained everything: the life he didn't have, the choice he'd made, the cost of being the person who asks rather than the person who lives.

The last years turned combative. In 1854, Bishop Mynster died, and Kierkegaard's restraint died with him. He published article after article attacking the Danish Church. Not for theological error but for fraudulence—for calling itself Christian while making Christianity easy, respectable, compatible with wealth and comfort. "Christianity does not exist," he wrote. The priests were liars. The congregations were playing church. Real Christianity was suffering, it was offense, it was martyrdom. Anything less was cosplay.

He collapsed on the street in October 1855. They took him to the hospital. He was forty-two years old, his body used up. When a pastor came to give him communion, Kierkegaard refused unless it came from a layperson. The pastors weren't real Christians, he said. The pastor left. On November 11, Kierkegaard died, alone in the hospital room, having refused the comfort of the church he'd spent his life thinking about.

At the funeral, his brother Peter—also a pastor—gave the eulogy. A crowd had gathered. At the graveside, a young man interrupted the proceedings to read a statement criticizing the church for claiming Kierkegaard as its own. The priests tried to stop him. The crowd argued. Even in death, Kierkegaard was the occasion for conflict about what it meant to believe.

The books remained, riddled with pseudonyms, arguments, contradictions. You couldn't extract a system from them because that was the point. They forced the reader into the position Kierkegaard occupied: alone, without authority, making a choice that couldn't be justified to anyone else. The question wasn't what to think about Christianity. The question was whether you were willing to exist as a Christian, which meant existing in passion, in fear, in the absurdity of infinite hope directed at something that made no rational sense.

He'd paid for the question with Regine, with ordinary life, with his body. What remained was a body of work that refused to be a body of work—that insisted instead on being an occasion for each reader to confront their own existence. Not in general. Not as an interesting philosophical problem. But directly, personally, alone. As he had been alone. As everyone finally is.
