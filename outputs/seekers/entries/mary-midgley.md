# Mary Midgley

The philosophy department at Oxford in the late 1930s was not designed for women. It barely tolerated them. Mary Scrutton arrived there to find a world of men who assumed philosophy belonged to them—not because they'd thought about it, but because thinking about it would have seemed unnecessary.

She became a philosopher anyway. But she took an odd route: marriage, children, twenty years away from academic philosophy while raising three sons in Newcastle. When she finally published her first book, she was fifty-eight. The men who'd dismissed her were already retired or dead.

*Beast and Man* appeared in 1978. It argued something unfashionable: that human beings are animals, that this matters philosophically, and that ignoring it had made philosophy stupid. Not wrong in small ways—stupid in large ones. The idea that humans are blank slates, that culture explains everything, that we can understand ourselves without understanding our evolutionary inheritance: all of this was, she suggested, wishful thinking dressed as sophistication.

The book enraged people. She was accused of biological determinism, of justifying whatever is, of being a reactionary. She was none of these things. She was asking philosophy to look at what ethology and evolutionary biology had learned about social animals—including the intensely social animal that philosophizes. She thought this might illuminate problems philosophy had been stuck on for centuries.

She was right, but saying so in 1978 required crossing a boundary. The social sciences had spent decades insisting that biology was irrelevant to human behavior. To suggest otherwise was to invite association with Social Darwinism, eugenics, the claim that inequality is natural. Midgley knew this. She argued anyway.

Her method was to take ideas seriously enough to examine what they actually said. When Richard Dawkins published *The Selfish Gene*, she read it carefully and then wrote a review pointing out that genes are not selfish—selfishness is a psychological state, genes don't have psychology, and talking as if they do smuggles metaphysical claims into biology while pretending to be rigorously materialist. The review was sharp. Dawkins was furious. But she was right about the metaphysics.

She kept doing this. When scientific materialists claimed that consciousness is an illusion, she asked what's doing the being-fooled if consciousness doesn't exist. When animal rights philosophers built theories on the capacities that separate humans from animals, she pointed out that the capacities that matter ethically are the ones we share. When postmodernists declared that there's no truth, only power, she noted that this claim itself purports to be true.

None of this was contrarian posturing. She genuinely believed that ideas have consequences and that bad ideas produce bad consequences even when held by brilliant people. Especially then.

Her style was conversational, almost chatty. She used metaphors from plumbing and cooking. She mentioned her garden. Academic philosophers found this suspicious. Philosophy is supposed to sound difficult. Midgley thought difficulty was often a sign of confusion, not depth. When you understand something clearly, you can say it clearly. If you can't say it clearly, you might not understand it.

This did not make her popular in professional philosophy. She published in popular venues—newspapers, magazines, books for general readers. She wrote about animals, about the environment, about science, about the myths we live by. She won prizes, sold books, influenced people outside the academy. Inside it, she remained marginal.

She didn't seem to mind. Or rather, she minded—she was human—but she kept working. She thought philosophy had become too specialized, too technical, too removed from the questions people actually face. Returning it to those questions was more important than professional acceptance.

What drove her was impatience with nonsense. Not intellectual disagreement—she could live with that—but with ideas that violated common sense while pretending to be more sophisticated than common sense. The blank slate theory violated common sense. So did the claim that animals are machines. So did the idea that we can understand ethics without understanding the kind of creature for whom ethics matters.

She trusted common sense, but not naively. She knew it could be wrong. But she thought philosophy's job was to start with common experience and refine it, not to replace it with abstractions that ignore what everyone knows. Everyone knows that humans are animals. Everyone knows that consciousness exists. Everyone knows that some things matter more than others. A philosophy that denies these things isn't sophisticated; it's lost.

The crossing she made was from technical academic philosophy to something messier and more useful. She gave up the prestige of difficulty for the possibility of being understood. She gave up the safety of narrow expertise for the risk of ranging across disciplines. She paid for this—fewer citations, less professional recognition, dismissal as a popularizer.

What she gained was influence beyond philosophy. Environmentalists read her. Scientists read her. People trying to think about how to live read her. She reconnected philosophy to the rest of thought, and to life.

Her last book appeared when she was ninety-one. She kept writing almost until she died at ninety-nine. She was still impatient with nonsense, still using metaphors from cooking, still insisting that philosophy matters because life matters and philosophy is supposed to help us think about life.

She wrote once that wisdom is not the same as knowledge. Knowledge is about facts; wisdom is about their meaning. Philosophy had become very good at knowledge—at technical arguments, formal systems, specialized debates. It had become less good at wisdom. Midgley thought this was a mistake. Not because wisdom is easy, but because it's what we need.

The men who'd dismissed her at Oxford had been very knowledgeable. Some of them had been brilliant. But they'd built a philosophy that couldn't look at animals without embarrassment, couldn't think about the body without distaste, couldn't engage with science without condescension. They'd made philosophy smaller by making it purer.

She made it larger by making it messier. She brought the animal back into the philosopher, the body back into the mind, the garden back into the theory. She insisted that we are creatures thinking, not pure intellects observing from nowhere. And she demonstrated that this makes philosophy more rigorous, not less—because it forces us to acknowledge the standpoint from which we think.

What remains is a body of work that refused the choice between rigor and relevance. She showed that you can be precise about things that matter, accessible without being shallow, interdisciplinary without being undisciplined. She left a model of philosophical work that starts with life and returns to it, that takes science seriously without being scientistic, that engages the public without pandering.

She also left an example: twenty years away from the profession, then returning to reshape it. The gap wasn't a loss; it was preparation. She raised children, lived in the world, thought about things while doing other things. When she returned to philosophy, she brought all of it with her—the messiness, the concreteness, the impatience with abstractions that don't illuminate life.

The profession is still catching up.
