# Meister Eckhart

In the winter of 1327, an aging Dominican friar sat in Avignon waiting to be condemned. Eckhart von Hochheim—Master Eckhart to his students, Meister Eckhart to history—had been summoned before the papal court to answer charges of heresy. He had preached in German to ordinary people about a God beyond God, about becoming empty enough for divinity to be born in the soul, about the possibility of union so complete that the difference between creature and Creator dissolved. The Archbishop of Cologne had collected his most dangerous sentences. Twenty-eight propositions. Some he had said. Some he hadn't, but could have.

Eckhart was sixty-seven. He had taught at Paris twice, the highest honor for a medieval theologian. He had administered his order's German province. He had preached hundreds of sermons that left listeners trembling with recognition of something they hadn't known they knew. Now he stood accused of misleading simple people, of saying the unsayable, of crossing lines that existed to keep Christians safe from the vertigo of their own tradition's mystical core.

He died before the verdict came. The bull condemning seventeen of his propositions was published in 1329, speaking of him in the past tense. "We have been informed," it began, with the careful vagueness of institutional documents, "that the late Eckhart..."

---

The young Eckhart would not have seemed destined for controversy. Born around 1260 in Thuringia, he entered the Dominicans as a teenager—the order of preachers, the order of Thomas Aquinas, defenders of orthodox theology against heretical movements. He was brilliant in the approved ways. He mastered the tools: Aristotelian logic, scholastic disputation, the careful architecture of systematic thought. His Latin commentaries on scripture and theological treatises are dense, technical, orthodox. He knew exactly what could be said and how to say it within the boundaries of acceptable discourse.

But he was also preaching. In German. To communities of Beguines—lay religious women, uncloistered, often educated, suspect to church authorities precisely because they claimed direct access to religious experience outside institutional channels. He preached to Dominican nuns. He preached to townspeople in Strasbourg, Frankfurt, Cologne. And in German, free from the constraints of academic Latin, he said things that couldn't quite be unsaid.

"God must become I and I must become God, so completely that this 'he' and this 'I' share one 'is.'"

"The eye with which I see God is the same eye with which God sees me."

"God is not good, nor better, nor best. Whoever says God is good, wrongs him as much as if he called the sun black."

This wasn't the warm devotionalism of contemporary mystics like Mechthild of Magdeburg, with their bridal mysticism and passionate longing. Eckhart's language was colder, stranger, more absolute. He spoke of *Gelassenheit*—releasement, letting-go, a self-emptying so complete that nothing remained to obstruct the divine breakthrough. He distinguished between *Gott*—God as conceived, God with attributes, God as object of devotion—and *Gotheit*, the Godhead, the desert of divinity beyond all concepts and names.

"God acts," he preached, "the Godhead does not act."

To reach the Godhead required what he called *Abgeschiedenheit*—detachment, but more radical than mere asceticism. Not just giving up possessions but giving up the self that could possess. Not just surrendering the will but surrendering the distinction between my will and God's will. In the ground of the soul—what he called the *Seelengrund* or the *Fünklein*, the little spark—human and divine shared an origin before all division.

"There is something in the soul so closely akin to God that it is already one with him and need never be united to him."

His listeners understood this wasn't poetry. He meant it literally—or beyond literal, in a way that made the literal/figurative distinction irrelevant. He was describing a topology of consciousness where the usual safety barriers between Creator and creature, infinite and finite, uncreated and created, simply weren't there. Or rather, they appeared in the realm of multiplicity, of created things, but in the ground where the soul met God, they dissolved.

The church had language for this kind of talk. *Pantheism*. *Antinomianism*. The claim that the soul could become God was the claim that made Marguerite Porete's *Mirror of Simple Souls* a heretical text; she had been burned at the stake in 1310, less than two decades before Eckhart's trial. Eckhart knew this. He had probably read her work. He kept preaching anyway.

---

What drove him past safety? Not rebellion. He genuinely believed he was teaching orthodox Christianity—its secret heart, the thing Aquinas knew but couldn't quite say in Latin's careful architecture. When challenged, he invoked Augustine, Dionysius, Bernard. He was right that they had said similar things. But they had said them carefully, with qualifications, in contexts where the institutional frame held firm. Eckhart stripped the qualifications away. He wanted people to *experience* what the tradition preserved as metaphor.

"Theologians may quarrel," he preached, "but the mystics of the world speak the same language."

This was perhaps his deepest transgression—not what he said but his conviction that ordinary people, women, laity, anyone willing to undergo the inner poverty he described, could reach these heights. The mystical experience he pointed toward wasn't reserved for theological experts or authorized saints. It was the birthright of consciousness itself.

"God expects but one thing of you," he told them, "and that is that you should come out of yourself insofar as you are a created being, and let God be God in you."

The Archbishop's case against him came down to this democratic impulse. These propositions, the indictment argued, "could lead many simple people into error." Simple people. The phrase recurs in medieval heresy trials. It means: people who shouldn't be told certain things, who might mistake direction for arrival, metaphor for method, possibility for permission.

Eckhart defended himself carefully in Avignon. He submitted written defenses parsing his sermons, showing how each proposition could be read orthodoxly. He insisted that anything erroneous came from the hearer's misunderstanding. But he also made a remarkable statement: if he had said anything heretical, he retracted it, because he could not persist in error. Yet he claimed never to have taught anything heretical in intention. The defense is both submission and evasion—I retract whatever was wrong, but nothing I said was wrong.

He didn't live to hear himself condemned. The bull *In agro dominico* listed seventeen articles as heretical or suspect. Many were genuine things he'd said, stripped of context. Some were distortions. The effect was to push Eckhart to the edge of the tradition—present but dangerous, influential but unreliable.

---

For centuries his work survived underground. The Rhineland mystics—Tauler, Suso—preserved his insights in safer language. Some sermons circulated anonymously. Protestant mystics like Jakob Böhme breathed his air without knowing his name. Romantic philosophers, especially in Germany, rediscovered him: Hegel claimed him, Schopenhauer too. Each generation found different Eckharts—the proto-German idealist, the Buddhist-before-Buddhism, the phenomenologist of consciousness, the panentheist, the apophatic radical.

Modern scholarship has tried to domesticate him, showing how his Latin works are orthodox, how his German sermons must be read charitably, how his paradoxes resolve in proper theological context. Some of this is true. Some of it misses the point. Eckhart at his most characteristic was saying something that strained language to breaking. The paradoxes don't resolve. They point beyond resolution to an experience he insisted was real.

"I pray God to rid me of God."

That sentence, perhaps his most notorious, captures the doubleness that made him dangerous. The God he prayed to and the God he wanted to be rid of were not the same God. One was the God of devotion, attributes, relationship. The other was the abyss beyond God, the nothing that was everything, the desert where even the word "God" became an obstacle to what it named.

He was not a systematic thinker, despite his training. His thought moved in circles, spirals, contradictions held in paradox. Students who demanded consistency missed the point. He was trying to describe something that couldn't be described, using the very concepts that had to be dissolved to reach it. The only way forward was through language to language's limit, then through the limit into silence.

"The most beautiful thing which a person can say about God would be for that person to remain silent from the wisdom of an inner wealth."

Yet he kept speaking. Hundreds of sermons. He couldn't stop. The silence at the heart of his vision required speech to point toward it. This was the doubleness at his core—preaching against preaching, speaking to enable silence, using words to destroy words' claim to capture reality.

---

What remains is troubling and alive. Eckhart's influence runs through unexpected channels—to Luther's theology of grace, to Angelus Silesius's mystical poetry, to Heidegger's phenomenology, to D.T. Suzuki's Zen Buddhism, to Thomas Merton's contemplative Christianity. Each found in him permission to think past available categories.

The Catholic Church never rescinded the condemnation, but it has softened. In 1992, the Master General of the Dominicans initiated a rehabilitation process. In 2010, Timothy Radcliffe, another Dominican, wrote that "Eckhart is surely a saint." The case remains open. The Meister remains liminal—in the tradition but at its edge, orthodox in intention but heretical in implication.

Perhaps this is where he belongs. He crossed boundaries that existed to keep theology stable: between apophatic and cataphatic, mystical and institutional, elitist and democratic, East and West, orthodox and heretical. He paid the cost—condemnation, diminished reputation, centuries of obscurity. But he left a deposit that won't dissolve: the insistence that Christianity contains within itself resources for radical self-transcendence, that the mystical ground is democratically available, that God at the deepest level is not an object of devotion but the abyss in which all distinction, even between self and God, vanishes.

His last recorded words, in his Latin defense at Avignon, were practical: "I may err but I may not be a heretic—for the first has to do with the mind and the second with the will."

It's a technical distinction. It's also true to his life. Everything he said emerged from the conviction that he was pointing toward reality, not inventing it. The union he described wasn't achievement but recognition. The poverty he preached wasn't impoverishment but freedom. The nothingness he named wasn't nihilism but fullness beyond category.

"The seed of God is in us. Given an intelligent and hard-working farmer, it will thrive and grow up to God, whose seed it is; and accordingly its fruits will be God-nature."

He believed this. It got him condemned. It made him a Seeker.
