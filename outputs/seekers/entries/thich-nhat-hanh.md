# Thich Nhat Hanh

The monk who became a refugee learned to walk slowly because he had nowhere safe to go quickly.

In 1966, Thich Nhat Hanh traveled to the United States to speak against the war consuming his country. He told American audiences something they didn't expect from a Buddhist monk: that neutrality in the face of suffering was not peace but cowardice. "If you wish to be neutral in situations of injustice," he said, "you have chosen the side of the oppressor." The South Vietnamese government immediately barred him from returning. The North Vietnamese, whom he also criticized, wanted him dead. He was forty years old and would not see Vietnam again for thirty-nine years.

He had become what he would later name: a person without a country to go home to, practicing a Buddhism without a monastery to protect it.

He was born Nguyen Xuan Bao in central Vietnam in 1926, entered monastic life at sixteen, and by his twenties was already pushing at the boundaries of Vietnamese Buddhism. The tradition he inherited was ancient, contemplative, removed from the world. He wanted it to descend from the mountains. In 1956, he founded a magazine called *Engaged Buddhism*—the first time that term appeared in print. Not Buddhism applied to social problems. Buddhism that recognized no separation between practice and the world.

When war came, he refused the choice between sides. The Viet Cong killed his students for refusing to support them. The South Vietnamese government killed them for the same reason. In 1963, a young nun he knew, Nhat Chi Mai, publicly immolated herself in Saigon, calling for peace. He wrote: "The fire that burns the body of Nhat Chi Mai continues to burn in my heart, and I will carry it there all my life."

The question that reorganized his life was simple and impossible: how do you practice peace when peace is not permitted?

His answer: you become the practice.

In exile in France, he established Plum Village, a monastery that looked nothing like a monastery. No high walls. No separation from the village. Laypeople came for weeks or months, lived alongside monastics, then returned to their lives. He taught walking meditation by walking—slowly, on ordinary paths, paying attention to each step. He taught eating meditation by eating—slowly, in silence, noticing the food. Everything became practice because everything was already practice; you just had to stop long enough to notice.

The teachings were simple to the point of offense. Breathe. Notice you're breathing. When you're angry, say "I am angry" and breathe. When you wash dishes, wash dishes. American students brought him complex questions about consciousness and enlightenment. He told them to drink their tea.

This simplicity concealed ruthless clarity. In *Being Peace*, he wrote: "If we are not happy, if we are not peaceful, we cannot share peace and happiness with others, even those we love, those who live under the same roof. If we are peaceful, if we are happy, we can smile and blossom like a flower, and everyone in our family, our entire society, will benefit from our peace."

It sounds like sentiment until you grasp what he's actually saying: your peace is not your private achievement. It's a political fact. Every moment of presence or absence from presence reverberates. The opposite of war is not the absence of war but the presence of people who have learned to be present.

He developed the practice of Beginning Anew—a structured way for communities to acknowledge harm, express appreciation, and speak difficult truths. Monastics practiced it. Families practiced it. He was designing liturgy for the mess of actual human relationship, the places where Buddhism's traditional focus on individual enlightenment fell silent.

In 2001, after the September 11 attacks, he wrote to President George W. Bush, urging him not to respond with violence. He understood that letter would accomplish nothing politically. He sent it because the practice was to send it. "Terrorism is the result of hatred, violence and misunderstanding," he wrote. "Putting all our resources into military action will not end terrorism... The root of terrorism should be identified and carefully removed."

The question his work raises: what if the deepest politics is the one you enact with your nervous system?

He was not naïve. He knew that mindfulness could be co-opted, commodified, reduced to an app that helps you tolerate injustice more calmly. His response was not to protect the teaching but to make it more specific. He wrote explicitly about the environmental crisis, about economic injustice, about racism. In *The Art of Power*, he distinguished between domination and true power, which he defined as the capacity to make people less afraid.

The cost of his seeking: he lived in permanent exile for four decades. His students in Vietnam practiced under surveillance, were arrested, had their monasteries confiscated. He couldn't visit his dying mother or attend his father's funeral. When he finally returned to Vietnam in 2005, it was as a visitor, not a citizen. The government allowed him in only because he had become too famous to exclude, and they watched him closely.

In 2014, at eighty-eight, he had a severe stroke that left him unable to speak. He had written, "The practice of Zen is to eat, breathe, cook, carry water, and scrub the toilet—to infuse every act of body, speech, and mind with mindfulness." Now he could no longer speak to teach it. He returned to Vietnam in 2018 to die in the temple where he had been ordained as a novice monk seventy-nine years earlier. He sat in his wheelchair in the garden, silent, still practicing the only teaching that remained: this moment. Just this.

What remains is not a system of thought but a set of practices anyone can do. Walk and know you're walking. Breathe and know you're breathing. Look at the person suffering in front of you and know that their suffering is yours. It sounds trivial until you try to do it for five minutes. Then you discover what he discovered: that attention is the most difficult political act, and the most necessary.

He died in January 2022 at Tu Hieu Temple in Hue, age ninety-five, having reorganized Vietnamese Buddhism, created a global movement, and demonstrated that the line between contemplation and action exists only for people who are not paying attention. His last teaching was his living: that you can lose your country, your language, your mobility, your speech, and still have the one thing that cannot be confiscated—this breath, this step, this moment of presence that is somehow, inexplicably, enough.
