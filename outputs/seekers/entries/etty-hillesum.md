# Etty Hillesum

The young woman who would die at Auschwitz at twenty-nine kept a diary that reads like someone learning to see. Not the world—she could see that fine—but through it, past the surface of things to what she called "the deep well within." The war was closing in. German Jews were being deported from Amsterdam. Food was scarce, movement restricted, yellow stars mandatory. And Etty Hillesum was writing about how to live.

She wasn't pious. She'd studied law and Slavic languages, read Rilke and Dostoevsky, had lovers, worked as a tutor. She was messy, passionate, prone to headaches and self-dramatization. In March 1941, at twenty-seven, she began keeping a diary at the suggestion of Julius Spier, a psychologist and refugee who taught her to read hands and who became her teacher, therapist, and lover. The diaries start ordinary enough—anxieties, moods, relationships—but something happens as she writes. The writing becomes a practice, and the practice opens something.

"There is a really deep well inside me," she wrote early on. "And in it dwells God. Sometimes I am there too. But more often stones and grit block the well, and God is buried beneath. Then He must be dug out again."

This became her work: the digging out. Not through withdrawal or transcendence but through attention. She practiced noticing—the light on a wall, the face of a friend, the jasmine blooming outside her window. She practiced staying present to her own difficulty without collapsing into it. She read mystics—Augustine, the Gospels, Meister Eckhart—but she wasn't escaping into them. She was learning a way to live with catastrophe without denying it or being destroyed by it.

The catastrophe was accelerating. July 1942: mass deportations began. Jews were being rounded up, sent first to Westerbork, the transit camp in the northeast Netherlands, then by train to the east. Most who went east didn't return. Etty could have gone into hiding. Friends offered. She refused. In July, she took a job with the Jewish Council, then volunteered to go to Westerbork itself—to be with her people, as she put it, where they were suffering.

Westerbork was a waiting room for death. Barracks in the mud, insufficient food, disease, and every Tuesday the trains departing for Poland. Etty worked in the camp hospital, helped people write letters, sat with the dying. She smuggled letters out describing what she saw—the panic, the separations, the cattle cars. She could have saved herself. She didn't.

Her letters from the camp are astonishing documents. She doesn't flinch from the horror—the woman going mad, the old man who can't walk being dragged to the train—but she also keeps noticing beauty. The sky. A scrap of kindness. She was developing what she called "a seeing heart": the capacity to witness suffering without turning away and without being consumed by rage or despair.

"I don't feel I'm in anybody's clutches," she wrote from Westerbork. "I feel safe in God's arms." This sounds like piety, but read the diaries and it's stranger than that. God for Etty wasn't rescue. God was what remained when everything else was stripped away—the bare fact of being alive, the capacity to love, the refusal to hate. "If we don't preserve some inner space," she wrote, "however small, some breathing room, then we'll be crushed."

She knew what was coming. "I know and share the many sorrows that a human being can experience, but I do not cling to them; they pass through me, like life itself, as a broad eternal stream, they become part of that stream, and life continues."

In June 1943, her parents were deported. She got herself transferred to the transport list to stay with them. On September 7, they were on the train to Auschwitz. A farm worker near the tracks found a postcard she'd thrown from the train: "We left the camp singing." 

She died at Auschwitz on November 30, 1943. Her parents died there too. The diaries and letters survived because she'd given them to friends.

What Hillesum left wasn't philosophy—she wasn't systematic—and it wasn't theology, exactly. It was something more like a record of learning to be present. She called it "living life a thousand times over." Not transcending suffering but meeting it directly, not with resignation but with a quality of attention so fierce and loving it amounted to resistance.

She could be naive. Her faith that individual transformation could answer collective evil was not borne out. She underestimated the machinery she was caught in. She died, and her death didn't mean anything redemptive in the usual sense. Six million died. The trains kept running.

But the diaries insist on something. That even under conditions designed to make humans into things, the capacity for attention remains possible. That seeing truly—yourself, others, the jasmine, the horror—is a way of refusing to be crushed. That interior freedom exists even when exterior freedom is gone, and that interior freedom matters.

"I shall try to help You, God, to stop my strength ebbing away," she wrote, "though I cannot vouch for it in advance. But one thing is becoming increasingly clear to me: that You cannot help us, that we must help You to help ourselves."

This was her discovery: that the divine wasn't elsewhere. It was here, now, in the actual, requiring human hands to do its work. That being present—truly present, with what Simone Weil called "attention"—was itself a form of resistance and even a kind of freedom.

The night before she was deported, she wrote: "We should be willing to act as a balm for all wounds." This from a woman standing at the edge of the gas chambers. Not because she didn't know where she was going. Because she did.
