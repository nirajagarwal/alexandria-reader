# Simone Weil

In June 1936, Simone Weil walked away from her position teaching philosophy at a girls' lycée in Bourges and took a job at the Renault factory in Boulogne-Billancourt. She had argued for years that intellectuals could not speak truthfully about working-class life without experiencing it. Now she would find out what standing at a press for ten hours did to the soul.

She lasted eight months. The work broke her. The noise, the speed, the transformation of human beings into extensions of machines—it wasn't what she'd expected, though she wasn't sure what she'd expected. She had chronic headaches that made thought nearly impossible. Her clumsiness, which had always been a family joke, became dangerous around industrial machinery. She burned herself repeatedly. Other workers treated her sometimes with kindness, sometimes with contempt for the bourgeois girl playing at poverty.

But something happened during those months that she would spend the rest of her short life trying to articulate. The affliction—she would always use that word, *malheur*, not suffering, not hardship, but affliction—entered her. Not as an idea to be analyzed but as a spiritual fact to be endured. She wrote later that slavery had marked her with its brand, that she had become, if only partially and temporarily, a thing.

This was exactly what she'd wanted. And it nearly destroyed her capacity to want anything.

---

Weil was brilliant in the way that makes other brilliant people uncomfortable. Born in 1909 to an affluent Jewish family in Paris, she was reading Racine at six, had mastered Greek by adolescence, and at fifteen became convinced she would never contribute anything original to human knowledge—a conviction triggered by comparing herself to her brother André, a mathematical prodigy. She contemplated suicide. She recovered by deciding that truth mattered more than genius, that access to truth was democratic.

This belief would reorganize everything. If truth was available to all, then the distance between the philosophy professor and the factory worker was not a matter of capacity but of attention. And attention required not just intellectual focus but a particular quality of waiting, of emptying oneself, of becoming nothing so that reality could enter undistorted.

At the École Normale Supérieure, she earned the nickname "the Red Virgin"—red for her politics, virgin for what her peers assumed about her complete uninterest in conventional feminine life. She joined demonstrations, infuriated administrators, refused to wear stockings or jewelry, dressed in shapeless clothes, chain-smoked, and argued that pacifism was a moral absolute. Then, in 1936, when the Spanish Civil War began, she concluded that some things were worth fighting for and went to Barcelona to join the anarchist militia.

She lasted a month. She stepped in a pot of boiling oil during a cooking accident and had to be evacuated. Her nearsightedness, always severe, made her dangerous with a rifle. But the deeper problem was that she discovered she had no talent for hating, even fascists. She watched the anarchists murder a priest. She found she could not sustain revolutionary violence in the presence of particular human faces.

---

The factory year came after Spain, and it marked a division in her life. Before: a brilliant, difficult young woman with left-wing politics and mystical leanings. After: someone whose attention had been reorganized around the possibility of contact with the real.

She began to think seriously about Christianity, though she had been raised in an agnostic household that considered religion a form of superstition. In 1937, while visiting Assisi, she found herself compelled to kneel in the chapel where Saint Francis had prayed. The next year, at Solesmes during Holy Week, she attended services while enduring one of her crushing headaches. The pain, she wrote later, allowed the beauty of the Gregorian chant to enter her directly, to reach "a point beyond pain, where the soul ceases to defend itself."

She never sought baptism. She refused it, repeatedly, despite yearning for it with an intensity that bled through her letters to the priests and theologians she argued with. She could not join the Church while it claimed exclusive possession of truth. She could not join while it had blessed violence. She remained, deliberately, at the threshold—which was, she came to believe, exactly where she belonged. The Church needed people at the threshold, she thought, people who could speak to those outside because they remained partially outside themselves.

---

In 1942, she fled France for New York with her parents, then almost immediately began plotting her return. She got to London, where she hoped to be dropped into occupied France to help the resistance. The Free French gave her desk work instead. She wrote obsessively—notebooks, essays, letters that ran to dozens of pages. She was trying to think through what a post-war civilization would need to be rooted in: not rights, which could become another form of power, but obligations. Not belief systems but attention to the good.

Her health had never been strong. Now it deteriorated rapidly. The headaches worsened. She refused to eat more than she imagined those under occupation could eat—a gesture that was either solidarity or suicidal asceticism, depending on who's assessing it. In April 1943 she collapsed and was diagnosed with tuberculosis. In August she died in a sanatorium in Kent. The coroner's report listed the cause of death as "cardiac failure due to myocardial degeneration of the heart muscles due to starvation and pulmonary tuberculosis." She was thirty-four.

The verdict, unofficial but widely held: suicide by starvation, or close enough.

---

What she left behind has the quality of something excavated rather than composed. The posthumous books—*Gravity and Grace*, *Waiting for God*, *The Need for Roots*—were assembled from notebooks and letters. They feel less like arguments than like reports from the boundary. She wrote about attention as the rarest and purest form of generosity. About affliction as the condition that reveals whether the soul will turn toward truth or toward illusion. About how power makes human beings invisible to each other and how the only counter is a quality of looking that refuses to unsee.

She was difficult in ways that remain difficult. Her asceticism bordered on the pathological. Her writings on Judaism contain passages that are, generously read, tortured, and less generously read, self-hating in ways that become unbearable. She refused obvious help—medical treatment, adequate food—with a stubbornness that looks like pride disguised as humility. Her life can be read as a long argument with embodiment itself, and embodiment won.

But the difficulty is inseparable from what she saw. She understood something about attention that most philosophy misses: that it requires not adding but subtracting, not grasping but waiting, not asserting the self but emptying it. She understood affliction not as a topic for ethical reflection but as the place where the soul is tested—not in the sense of whether it will endure but whether it will continue to see truly while enduring.

"The love of God is pure when joy and suffering inspire an equal degree of gratitude," she wrote. She was not recommending this. She was reporting from a place most people have the good sense not to go.

Whether she should have gone there is a different question. But she went, and she brought back word, and the word is still strange and still true, and we still don't quite know what to do with it.
