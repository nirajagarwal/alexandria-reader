# Iris Murdoch

She kept notebooks—scores of them, densely packed with philosophical arguments, novel plots, character sketches, reminders to herself about attention. In one she wrote: "Art and morals are one. Their essence is the same. The essence of both of them is love. Love is the perception of individuals. Love is the extremely difficult realisation that something other than oneself is real."

This wasn't piety. It was the central problem of her life.

Iris Murdoch wrote twenty-six novels, most of them featuring tangled love affairs, philosophical conversations in pubs, and characters who discovered too late that other people were not characters. She also wrote dense works of moral philosophy arguing against the dominant trends of her time. The novels made her famous. The philosophy made her unfashionable. She didn't care. The two projects were one project: how do you learn to see what's actually there?

She was born in Dublin in 1919 to Anglo-Irish parents, grew up in London, went to Oxford in 1938. Wartime Oxford was strange—half-empty, everyone waiting. She read classics, joined the Communist Party (briefly), fell in love with women and men, sometimes simultaneously. Her notebooks from this period are full of attempts to understand desire, to map its logic. She wanted a theory. The problem was that desire kept escaping the theory.

After Oxford she joined the civil service, then UNRRA, the UN relief agency. In 1945 they sent her to Belgium and Austria. She worked in displaced persons camps—thousands of people who had survived but had nowhere to go back to. The camps were filthy, chaotic, impossible. She helped people fill out forms, tried to reunite families, dealt with the daily fact that bureaucracy could not touch what had happened to these people.

The experience destroyed something in her. Or built something. She came back unable to take seriously the philosophical fashion of the moment, which was logical positivism—the idea that meaningful statements had to be verifiable, that ethics was just emotion dressed up as argument. She'd just watched civilization collapse and been rebuilt by people filling out forms while thinking about whether to kill themselves. The positivists' tidy categories seemed obscene.

In 1948 she went back to Oxford, to study philosophy formally this time. She read everything—Plato, Kant, Hegel, the existentialists. She also met the new philosophy coming from Cambridge: Wittgenstein's ordinary language philosophy, which held that most philosophical problems dissolved if you just looked at how words actually worked. She found this both brilliant and insufficient. Yes, look at how words work. But also look at what's really there, which is harder than it sounds.

Her first novel, *Under the Net*, appeared in 1954. It seemed like an existentialist romp—young man in London, love affairs, coincidences, philosophical conversations. Critics called it charming. They missed what she was doing. The book was about a man who treated other people as characters in his own narrative and kept being shocked when they turned out to have their own lives. The whole plot was an education in attention.

She married John Bayley in 1956. He was an Oxford don, shy, brilliant, deeply kind. The marriage was unconventional—she had affairs, he knew, it caused pain, they stayed together. People who knew them disagree about whether this was liberation or damage or both. Her novels are full of marriages like this: arrangements that work until they don't, sustained by affection and cowardice and genuine love all mixed together.

The novels kept coming, almost one a year. *The Bell* (1958), *A Severed Head* (1961), *The Black Prince* (1973), *The Sea, The Sea* (1978, which won the Booker). The plots were melodramatic—secret affairs, sudden deaths, mystical experiences in gardens. The prose was lucid, almost plain. The combination let her do something unusual: use plot machinery to expose the mechanics of self-deception.

Her characters were always making grand gestures, having revelations, deciding to change. The revelations were usually wrong. Not because truth didn't exist but because seeing it required a discipline most people couldn't sustain. You had to look away from yourself long enough to see that other people were real, not supporting characters. This was, she thought, the same work that art does and morality requires.

The philosophy establishment didn't know what to do with her. Her big book, *The Sovereignty of Good* (1970), argued against everything fashionable. She said that morality wasn't about choices in crisis moments but about the slow, patient work of learning to see clearly. She said that freedom wasn't about the will but about attention. She said that good was real, not just a name for preferences. She invoked Plato without apology at a time when Plato was considered hopelessly outdated.

The book was short—three essays, ninety pages. It was also devastating. She didn't argue against her opponents directly; she just described a different picture. Imagine morality is like learning to see. You practice. You fail. You practice more. Gradually the world reveals itself as it is, not as you need it to be. This isn't therapy, it's metaphysics. The Good is real. Love is attention to reality. Art helps because it gives us practice looking at something other than ourselves.

The academic philosophers mostly ignored this. The novelists understood it immediately.

She kept teaching at Oxford—undergraduates, tutorials, the weekly grind of reading bad essays. She kept writing—mornings at her desk, the notebooks filling up. She kept the affairs, which hurt Bayley and which she couldn't seem to stop. The novels examined this pattern obsessively: people who understand, theoretically, that their actions harm others, but who can't quite make that understanding real enough to change their behavior.

In the early 1990s she began to lose words. Not forget them—lose them, as if they were falling out of her brain. At first it seemed like ordinary aging. Then it got worse. The notebooks became harder to read, the entries shorter, more repetitive. By 1995 she couldn't write anymore.

The diagnosis was Alzheimer's. The woman who had spent fifty years thinking about attention, about the discipline of seeing what's real, about love as the recognition that other people exist—she was losing the ability to recognize anything.

Bayley took care of her. He wrote about it later, in *Iris: A Memoir*, which some people found beautiful and others found obscene—this private thing made public. He described helping her dress, reminding her who people were, watching her become someone simpler. She died in 1999.

The question the work keeps asking is: How do you see what's actually there? Not what you want to be there, not what the theory says should be there, but what *is* there—the concrete, messy, recalcitrant reality of other people.

She thought the answer was attention. Sustained, patient, unselfing attention. Art could train you in it. Morality required it. Love was nothing else.

The discipline she described is almost impossibly hard. She knew this. Her own life demonstrated it—the affairs, the self-deception, the gap between understanding and action. The notebooks are full of reminders to herself to look, to really look, followed by evidence that she hadn't.

What remains is the insistence that it matters. That seeing clearly is possible and also necessary. That love is real work, not a feeling. That other people are not characters in your story but the whole point.

The novels are still in print. The philosophy is taught more now than it was in her lifetime. The notebooks are at Oxford, available to scholars. What they record is not triumph but the attempt—fifty years of trying to look away from herself long enough to see what was real.
