# Franz Kafka

The insurance lawyer kept meticulous records. He arrived at his desk at 8:30 each morning, assessed workers' compensation claims, wrote careful reports in clear German prose, and departed at 2:00 in the afternoon. His superiors found him competent. His colleagues found him courteous. No one at the Workers' Accident Insurance Institute for the Kingdom of Bohemia suspected that the thin man with the good posture was spending his afternoons sleeping, his evenings eating almost nothing, and his nights—the hours between 10:30 PM and sometimes 6:00 AM—writing stories about men who turned into insects, about execution machines, about a hunger artist who simply couldn't find food he liked.

The two lives were supposed to remain separate. "My job is unbearable to me," Kafka wrote to Felice Bauer in 1912, "because it conflicts with my only desire and my only calling, which is literature." But the separation was never clean. The insurance work taught him how institutions process human beings into cases. The bureaucratic forms he drafted by day became the bureaucratic nightmares his characters inhabited by night. The man who wrote about K.'s futile attempts to reach the Castle spent his working hours navigating the actual castle of Habsburg bureaucracy.

He wrote in German in Prague, already a problem. Prague's German-speaking Jews occupied a narrow strip of identity—not quite Czech, not quite German, not quite part of the Yiddish-speaking Jewish masses from the east. Kafka felt this suspension in his bones. "What do I have in common with Jews?" he asked his diary. "I hardly have anything in common with myself." He studied Hebrew late in life, dreamed of emigrating to Palestine, collected Yiddish folktales, but the ground beneath his feet remained permanently uncertain.

The writing happened in the gap. Everything he published in his lifetime could fit in a slim volume. Most of what he wrote, he considered failure. He asked his friend Max Brod to burn the manuscripts after his death—the novels, the stories, the parables, nearly everything. Brod didn't. The instructions were clear; Brod ignored them. This disobedience gave the world *The Trial*, *The Castle*, *Amerika*, and the stories that have become synonyms for modern alienation.

Kafka's father Hermann was a large man who had clawed his way from village poverty to Prague respectability through sheer force. He ran a fancy goods shop, wore his body as evidence of triumph, and could not fathom his thin, anxious son who wanted to write stories instead of strengthening the family business. The "Letter to His Father," written when Franz was thirty-six and never sent, runs to forty-five pages of surgical self-analysis: "My writing was all about you; all I did there, after all, was bemoan what I could not bemoan upon your breast."

The letter diagnosed the wound but didn't heal it. Nothing healed it. Kafka got engaged to Felice Bauer twice, broke it off twice. He got engaged to Julie Wohryzek, broke it off. He lived with Dora Diamant near the end, perhaps the only sustained intimacy of his adult life, and by then he was already dying. Tuberculosis had been diagnosed in 1917—not coincidentally, shortly after one of the broken engagements. He recognized immediately what it meant: "The illness also has its advantages," he wrote. "It makes certain decisions easier, decisions that would otherwise have required superhuman strength."

The disease bought him permission to leave Prague, to reduce his insurance hours, to justify his distance from normal life. But it was killing him. He moved through sanatoria in the mountains, trying different air, different treatments, different diets. He became increasingly vegetarian, increasingly obsessed with chewing each bite properly. He lost weight he didn't have to lose. The body became another site of impossible demands.

The stories don't resolve. They stop. Gregor Samsa dies as an insect while his family feels relief. Joseph K. is executed "like a dog" without ever learning his crime. The hunger artist starves because "I couldn't find the food I liked. If I had found it, believe me, I should have made no fuss and eaten my fill like you or anyone else." K. never reaches the Castle. The runner cannot find the doctor the message that he's finally arrived—the Emperor has sent a message but it will never be delivered.

Critics have spent a century explaining what it means. Kafka is about totalitarianism (he died before Hitler came to power). Kafka is about Judaism (he barely practiced). Kafka is about alienation under capitalism (he kept his insurance job until tuberculosis made it impossible). Kafka is about father-son conflict (as if that explained the執法). Every interpretation captures something; none captures it all.

What the work does is make visible a particular kind of impossibility. Not the impossibility of external barriers you might overcome, but the impossibility built into the structure of being itself. His characters inhabit worlds where the rules exist but remain inaccessible, where authorities pass judgment without stating charges, where the crime is obscure but the guilt is certain. They try to understand, to comply, to reach the next level of appeal. The trying is the trap.

He knew this about his own writing. The famous breakthrough came in September 1912, when he wrote "The Judgment" in a single night, and when he finished at 6:00 AM he felt he had achieved something. But achievement brought its own problem: now he knew what the work required. He could produce pages of perfection and pages of garbage, and the gap between them made the garbage unbearable. "I need solitude for my writing," he wrote, "not 'like a hermit'—that wouldn't be enough—but like a dead man."

By 1924 the tuberculosis had spread to his larynx. Speaking became painful, then impossible. He communicated by writing notes. He was forty years old. He died in a sanatorium outside Vienna on June 3rd, weighing perhaps ninety pounds, unable to eat or drink because it hurt too much.

Max Brod gathered the manuscripts Kafka had wanted burned and began publishing them. The thin man who had published a few slim volumes in his lifetime became, posthumously, essential. The adjective "Kafkaesque" entered languages worldwide to describe the particular nightmare of rational systems producing irrational outcomes. The work that felt like failure to its author became the map dozens of writers used to navigate the twentieth century.

He didn't live to see any of it. He died thinking he had failed, that the work was incomplete, that he hadn't managed to write the thing he'd stayed up all those nights trying to write. The seeking continued until his body couldn't sustain it. What remained was the record of the seeking itself: the painstaking documentation of what it feels like to live in a world whose rules you cannot learn but whose judgment you cannot escape.
