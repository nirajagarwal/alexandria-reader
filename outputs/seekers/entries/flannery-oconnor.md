# Flannery O'Connor

The peacocks screamed at Andalusia, her mother's dairy farm in Milledgeville, Georgia, where Flannery O'Connor spent the last thirteen years of her life dying of lupus. She raised over forty of them. Visitors remarked on the noise, the mess, the iridescent violence of the birds. O'Connor watched them drag their impossible tails through the red clay and wrote stories about people receiving grace like a blow to the head.

She was twenty-five when the lupus announced itself. Climbing stairs in a train station, her arms suddenly too weak to lift her luggage. The disease had killed her father when she was fifteen. Now it would kill her too, but slowly. The doctors gave her steroids and crutches and sent her back to her mother's farm, back to Georgia, back to the place she'd been trying to escape through literature and distance.

Before the disease, she'd been heading somewhere else. Iowa Writers' Workshop. New York City. Connecticut, living in a garage apartment on the property of Robert and Sally Fitzgerald, translating medieval French, writing furiously, attending daily Mass at a church where nobody knew her. She was becoming the kind of writer who lived in the North and visited the South for material. The lupus made her the kind of writer who lived in the South and couldn't leave.

What it gave her was limitation, and limitation gave her everything. Forced into proximity with the thing she'd been fleeing—the rural Georgia of her childhood, her mother's farm, the pious Catholic enclave in the Protestant Bible Belt—she stopped trying to write like anyone else. The freaks and prophets that populated her fiction weren't sociological observations anymore. They were the view from her bedroom window, the people she saw on her rare trips to town, the voices in her own head magnified by illness and isolation.

She wrote every morning for three hours, never more. The lupus wouldn't permit more. Revision took months, years. She produced thirty-one stories and two novels in her lifetime. Every sentence cost something. She made the cost visible in the prose—hard, clean, stripped of ornament. No word wasted because she couldn't afford waste.

The violence in her fiction disturbed everyone. A Bible salesman steals a woman's wooden leg. A grandmother causes her family's murder by recognizing the escaped convict too late. A teenager drowns trying to baptize a child against his will. A woman's artificial leg gets stolen in the hayloft where she thought she was seducing someone. Critics called it grotesque. O'Connor called it realism. "You have to make your vision apparent by shock," she said, "to the hard of hearing you shout, and for the almost-blind you draw large and startling figures."

What she meant: grace isn't gentle. The modern world had made itself deaf to the possibility of transformation, blind to the action of God in the everyday. So her stories had to hit hard. The moment of recognition—what she called the moment when "the character's vision is purified"—often came through catastrophe. The Misfit shoots the grandmother just as she reaches toward him with something like love. Hazel Motes blinds himself to see more clearly. Mrs. Turpin in "Revelation" has her certainty shattered by a girl who calls her a warthog from hell, then sees the souls of good country people like herself bringing up the rear in a heavenly procession, their virtues burned away.

She was writing from inside Christian orthodoxy in an age that found Christian orthodoxy embarrassing. The letters show her trying to explain this to readers who thought her stories were attacking faith rather than embodying it. To one correspondent she wrote: "I am a Catholic peculiarly possessed of the modern consciousness, that thing Jung describes as unhistorical, solitary, and guilty." To another: "I see from the standpoint of Christian orthodoxy. This means that for me the meaning of life is centered in our Redemption by Christ and what I see in the world I see in its relation to that."

The standpoint cost her readers. It also gave her the only readers she wanted—the ones who could receive what she was actually offering. Not comfort. Not affirmation of their liberal politics or their vague spirituality. A vision of reality in which God acted and people were responsible and grace came violently or not at all.

She died at thirty-nine, in 1964, the lupus finally finishing what it had started. Her mother found her lying on the floor, unable to get up. At the hospital, the doctors tried to operate on a benign fibroid tumor, but she went into shock. A priest gave her last rites. She died the next day, conscious almost to the end.

What remains is the fiction—two novels and those thirty-one stories, every one of them dense as a fist. And the letters, collected after her death, showing a mind of ferocious intelligence and unexpected tenderness. She could be funny in the letters, gossipy even, complaining about the literary world, describing the peacocks, making jokes about her crutches and the lupus. The correspondence with "A" (later revealed to be Betty Hester) ran for years, covering everything from Catholic theology to the proper care of chickens.

She knew what she was doing, narrowing her life to the farm and the work. "The writer operates at a peculiar crossroads where time and place and eternity somehow meet," she wrote. "His problem is to find that location." Lupus found it for her. Andalusia, Milledgeville, Georgia. A bedroom overlooking peacocks and cattle. Three hours every morning. A body failing and a mind burning and the absolute conviction that the material world was all you had to show what couldn't be seen.

Her stories still hit like they're supposed to. The shock hasn't worn off. People still call them violent, grotesque, disturbing, too harsh. She'd count that as success. The hard of hearing need shouting. The almost-blind need large and startling figures. And grace, when it comes, rarely comes gentle.
