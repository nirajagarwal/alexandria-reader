# Mitochondrial Function

Every cell in your body runs on energy produced by tiny organelles inherited exclusively from your mother—ancient bacteria that merged with our ancestors a billion years ago and never left. Understanding how these cellular power plants work reveals why some people stay vigorous into old age while others deteriorate, and what distinguishes resilience from decline.

## What It Is

Mitochondria are the energy-producing organelles inside nearly every cell in your body—hundreds to thousands per cell depending on energy demands. They convert nutrients from food into ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the molecular currency that powers everything from muscle contraction to neurotransmitter synthesis. Each mitochondrion contains its own circular DNA, inherited maternally, distinct from nuclear DNA. This isn't just cellular machinery—it's a symbiotic relationship with what were once independent bacteria. Tissues with high energy demands (heart, brain, muscles, liver) contain the most mitochondria. When people talk about "metabolic health," they're largely talking about how well these organelles function.

## The Mechanism

Mitochondria produce ATP through oxidative phosphorylation, a process occurring across the inner mitochondrial membrane. Nutrients (primarily glucose and fatty acids) enter a series of reactions called the electron transport chain. As electrons pass through protein complexes, they pump protons across the membrane, creating an electrochemical gradient. This gradient drives ATP synthase, a molecular turbine that generates ATP. The process requires oxygen—hence "oxidative"—and produces carbon dioxide and water as byproducts. It also generates reactive oxygen species (ROS), molecules that can damage cellular components. Healthy mitochondria maintain quality control through mitophagy, selectively destroying damaged organelles and replacing them through biogenesis. This balance between damage and renewal determines mitochondrial function. Exercise, particularly high-intensity training, triggers biogenesis signals—your cells respond to energy stress by building more power plants.

## The Evidence

Doug Wallace's research established that mitochondrial DNA mutations accumulate with age and contribute to decline in energy production and increased oxidative damage. Studies show mitochondrial function decreases approximately 8% per decade after age thirty. David Sinclair's work on NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide), a crucial cofactor in mitochondrial energy production, reveals declining levels correlate with aging markers; supplementation with NAD+ precursors improved mitochondrial function in animal models, though human translation remains under investigation. Mark Tarnopolsky's research demonstrated that endurance exercise maintains mitochondrial DNA integrity and increases mitochondrial density even in elderly subjects—master athletes in their seventies showed mitochondrial profiles resembling sedentary people in their twenties. Research on caloric restriction consistently shows improved mitochondrial efficiency and reduced oxidative damage across species, mediated partly through AMPK and sirtuins activating mitochondrial quality control pathways.

## When It Fails

Mitochondrial dysfunction manifests as inadequate ATP production and excessive oxidative damage. Primary mitochondrial diseases—caused by inherited mutations—can be devastating, affecting high-energy organs like brain and heart. But acquired mitochondrial dysfunction develops gradually in most people through accumulated damage, declining biogenesis, and impaired quality control. The result is cellular energy deficit: neurons that can't maintain synapses, muscles that fatigue easily, immune cells that can't mount effective responses. Excessive ROS production damages proteins, lipids, and DNA, creating a degenerative cycle. This dysfunction underlies multiple age-related conditions—Parkinson's and Alzheimer's both show severe mitochondrial impairment in affected neurons. Insulin resistance involves mitochondrial dysfunction in muscle and liver cells. Chronic fatigue syndrome patients consistently show abnormal mitochondrial function. The fundamental problem: cells can't generate enough energy to maintain themselves.

## A Case

Consider what happens to skeletal muscle mitochondria in sedentary aging versus active aging. Researchers biopsied muscle tissue from seventy-year-old sedentary individuals and compared it to tissue from similarly aged endurance-trained athletes and young sedentary controls. The sedentary elderly showed approximately 50% reduction in mitochondrial content, decreased oxidative capacity, and accumulated mitochondrial DNA mutations. Their muscle cells were literally running on half power. The master athletes, however, maintained mitochondrial density and function nearly equivalent to young sedentary subjects—the trained seventy-year-olds had mitochondria that functioned like those of inactive thirty-year-olds. Gene expression analysis revealed sustained activation of PGC-1α, the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis, in the athletes. This wasn't genetic advantage—when previously sedentary elderly subjects began supervised exercise training, mitochondrial markers improved significantly within six months. The machinery responds to demand.
