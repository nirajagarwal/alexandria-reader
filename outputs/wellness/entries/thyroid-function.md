# Thyroid Function

A butterfly-shaped gland at the base of your neck regulates the metabolic rate of nearly every cell in your body. When thyroid function falters—even slightly—the effects cascade through energy production, temperature regulation, cognitive function, and dozens of other systems that depend on precise hormonal signaling.

## What It Is

The thyroid gland produces two primary hormones: thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3). These hormones don't trigger specific actions like adrenaline or insulin. Instead, they set the basal metabolic rate—the speed at which cells convert nutrients into energy and perform their basic functions. T3 is the active form, binding to nuclear receptors in cells throughout the body to regulate gene expression. T4 serves primarily as a reservoir, converted to T3 as needed. The system operates through a feedback loop involving the hypothalamus and pituitary gland, which monitor circulating hormone levels and adjust production accordingly. This hierarchical control system (hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis) maintains metabolic homeostasis across varying conditions.

## The Mechanism

The process begins with iodine, which the thyroid concentrates from the bloodstream and combines with the amino acid tyrosine to synthesize thyroid hormones. The pituitary gland releases thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), which prompts the thyroid to release T4 and T3. Most cells contain deiodinase enzymes that convert T4 to T3 locally, allowing tissue-specific regulation. Once T3 enters a cell's nucleus, it binds to thyroid hormone receptors that interact with DNA, influencing the transcription of genes involved in metabolism, protein synthesis, and oxygen consumption. This affects mitochondrial activity, the sodium-potassium pump that maintains cellular electrochemical gradients, and the expression of metabolic enzymes. The hypothalamus monitors T3 and T4 levels, releasing thyrotropin-releasing hormone when levels drop, which signals the pituitary to increase TSH production—a negative feedback loop that maintains stability.

## The Evidence

Research has established thyroid hormones as master metabolic regulators. Antonio Bianco's work at the University of Chicago demonstrated that tissue-specific conversion of T4 to T3 allows different organs to fine-tune their metabolic activity independently. Studies measuring resting metabolic rate show that hypothyroid patients can experience a 15-40% reduction, while hyperthyroid individuals may see increases of similar magnitude. Brain imaging research by Peter Whybrow at UCLA revealed that even subclinical hypothyroidism—TSH levels above normal but below diagnostic thresholds—correlates with measurable decreases in cerebral blood flow and cognitive performance. Epidemiological data shows thyroid dysfunction affects women nine times more frequently than men, with autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's disease) being the leading cause in developed countries. The Framingham study found subclinical hypothyroidism in 8-10% of adults, increasing with age. Optimal TSH ranges remain debated, with some researchers arguing that the current reference range (0.4-4.0 mIU/L) is too broad and masks subtle dysfunction.

## When It Fails

Hypothyroidism manifests as systemic metabolic slowdown: persistent fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain despite unchanged diet, cognitive sluggishness, constipation, dry skin, and hair loss. The heart rate slows, cholesterol rises, and myxedema—protein and mucopolysaccharide accumulation in tissues—causes facial puffiness. In severe cases, myxedema coma represents life-threatening metabolic collapse. Hyperthyroidism produces the opposite cascade: anxiety, tremor, heat intolerance, weight loss despite increased appetite, rapid heartbeat, and muscle weakness. Graves' disease, an autoimmune condition where antibodies mimic TSH, drives overproduction. Both extremes disrupt reproductive function, bone density, and cardiovascular health. The insidious nature of gradual thyroid decline means many people attribute symptoms to aging, stress, or poor sleep rather than hormonal dysfunction. Subclinical cases may persist for years before crossing diagnostic thresholds, accumulating metabolic damage.

## Cases

In the 1990s, researchers discovered an epidemic of hypothyroidism in the English town of Great Barrington. Environmental investigation revealed contamination of the water supply with thyroid-suppressing chemicals, but the cluster also highlighted how thyroid dysfunction often goes unrecognized. Many residents had adapted to gradual metabolic decline, normalizing symptoms as "just how I am."

Clinical studies of T3/T4 combination therapy versus T4-only treatment illuminate individual variation in deiodinase enzyme activity. Some patients maintain normal T4 and TSH but convert poorly to T3, experiencing persistent symptoms despite standard treatment showing "normal" labs. This led to research by Francesco Celi at Virginia Commonwealth University on genetic polymorphisms affecting deiodinase enzymes, explaining why identical TSH levels produce different clinical pictures in different people.

Severe iodine deficiency in landlocked regions historically caused endemic cretinism—profound cognitive impairment when maternal hypothyroidism affected fetal brain development. Salt iodization programs eliminated this in most developed nations, but WHO estimates that 2 billion people globally remain iodine-deficient, with measurable impacts on IQ and developmental outcomes.
