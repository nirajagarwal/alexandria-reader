# Inflammation

Your body's first response to injury or infection is a carefully orchestrated alarm system. Inflammation summons resources, isolates threats, and initiates repair. When working correctly, it's one of medicine's miracles—the reason a paper cut heals and a cold doesn't kill you. When it misfires or never turns off, it becomes the common mechanism behind diabetes, heart disease, Alzheimer's, and autoimmune disorders.

## What It Is

Inflammation is the immune system's rapid response protocol—a cascade of chemical signals and cellular activity triggered by damage or infection. Acute inflammation is the familiar swelling, redness, heat, and pain that appears after injury. It's self-limiting, lasting hours to days while tissue repairs. Chronic inflammation persists for months or years, often at low intensity, damaging tissues while attempting to protect them. This distinction matters: acute inflammation heals wounds and fights infections; chronic inflammation creates the wounds it pretends to heal. The same system operating under different time scales produces opposite outcomes.

## The Mechanism

When tissue damage or pathogen presence is detected, damaged cells release alarm molecules—cytokines, histamines, prostaglandins. These signals dilate blood vessels and increase permeability, allowing plasma and white blood cells to flood the affected area. Neutrophils arrive first, within minutes, engulfing bacteria and debris. Macrophages follow, cleaning up dead cells and releasing more cytokines. The swelling physically isolates the problem. The heat increases metabolic activity. The pain enforces rest. Pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-6 and TNF-alpha coordinate this response systemically, triggering fever and fatigue—whole-body shifts that prioritize fighting infection over normal activity. Resolution requires anti-inflammatory signals: molecules like lipoxins and resolvins that tell immune cells the job is finished, initiating tissue remodeling and return to baseline.

## The Evidence

Rudolf Virchow identified inflammation's cellular basis in 1858, but its role in chronic disease emerged slowly. Epidemiologist Paul Ridker's work in the 1990s established that elevated C-reactive protein—a marker of systemic inflammation—predicted heart attacks independent of cholesterol levels. The CANTOS trial (2017) demonstrated that an anti-inflammatory drug reduced cardiovascular events in people with high CRP but normal cholesterol, proving inflammation itself drives disease. Neuroscientist Beth Stevens at Harvard showed how inflammatory microglia—brain immune cells—prune synapses excessively in Alzheimer's and schizophrenia. Research on metabolic inflammation revealed that visceral fat releases inflammatory cytokines, creating insulin resistance—obesity and diabetes linked through inflammation. The consistency across diseases suggests chronic inflammation isn't a symptom but a mechanism: the immune system attacking the body it's meant to protect.

## When It Fails

Failure takes two forms. Insufficient acute inflammation—immunosuppression from disease or drugs—allows infections to spread unchecked. More common is inflammation that never resolves. Triggers include persistent infection, autoimmune misidentification of self-tissue as threat, continuous tissue damage (smoking, high blood sugar), and visceral fat secreting inflammatory signals. The inflammatory state becomes self-perpetuating: cytokines attract more immune cells, which release more cytokines, which damage tissue, which triggers more inflammation. In atherosclerosis, inflamed arterial plaques rupture and clot. In joints, persistent inflammation degrades cartilage. In the brain, inflammatory microglia attack neurons. The system designed for short-term crisis response causes long-term destruction when it can't turn off.

## A Case

Randy Shekelle was a healthy 45-year-old attorney when he developed what seemed like a persistent flu in 2008. Blood tests revealed extremely elevated CRP—over 100 mg/L, where normal is below 3. Doctors found no infection, no autoimmune markers, no obvious cause. But CT scans revealed extensive coronary calcification—his arteries looked like those of a 70-year-old. Despite normal cholesterol and blood pressure, inflammation was destroying his cardiovascular system. Investigation eventually traced the source to chronic periodontitis—severe gum disease creating a continuous bacterial load. Treatment of the oral infection, combined with anti-inflammatory medication, brought his CRP down to 2 mg/L within six months. The case illustrated inflammation's dual nature: a local infection triggering systemic destruction far from its source, but reversible when the underlying trigger was addressed. Shekelle's arteries stopped deteriorating. His "flu" symptoms resolved. The inflammation that nearly killed him turned off once it had nothing left to fight.
