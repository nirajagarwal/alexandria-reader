# Sex Hormones

The chemical messengers that shape reproduction, metabolism, mood, and cognition don't simply govern sexuality—they orchestrate dozens of physiological systems from adolescence through aging. Understanding how testosterone, estrogen, and progesterone actually work reveals why hormonal shifts affect everything from bone density to decision-making, and why their influence extends far beyond what their name suggests.

## What It Is

Sex hormones are steroid hormones produced primarily in the gonads (testes and ovaries) and adrenal glands that regulate reproductive function and influence metabolic, skeletal, cardiovascular, and neurological systems. The three main classes—androgens (testosterone), estrogens (estradiol, estrone, estriol), and progestogens (progesterone)—are present in all sexes but in different concentrations and ratios. Both males and females produce testosterone and estrogen; what differs is the amount and the target tissue response. These hormones operate through receptor binding in cells throughout the body, making them systemic regulators rather than merely reproductive signals. The hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis controls their production through feedback loops that respond to circulating hormone levels, stress, nutrition, and environmental cues.

## The Mechanism

Sex hormones work by binding to specific receptors inside cells, forming complexes that enter the nucleus and directly influence gene expression. When testosterone binds to androgen receptors or estrogen to estrogen receptors, they activate transcription of specific genes that produce proteins affecting cell function. This is why effects take hours to days—gene expression is slower than neurotransmitter signaling. The HPG axis begins when the hypothalamus releases GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone), triggering the pituitary to release LH (luteinizing hormone) and FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone), which stimulate the gonads to produce sex hormones. These hormones then feed back to suppress further GnRH and LH/FSH release—a negative feedback loop maintaining homeostasis. Aromatase enzymes convert testosterone to estradiol in fat tissue, brain, and other tissues, meaning testosterone serves partly as a precursor. The hormones also bind to carrier proteins in blood (sex hormone-binding globulin), with only the unbound "free" fraction biologically active at any moment.

## The Evidence

Research consistently demonstrates sex hormone influence beyond reproduction. Barbara Sherwin's work at McGill established that estrogen affects verbal memory and executive function in postmenopausal women, with receptor density in hippocampus and prefrontal cortex explaining the mechanism. Peter Celec's research shows testosterone influences spatial cognition, risk tolerance, and competitive behavior through androgen receptor activity in specific brain regions. The Framingham Heart Study documented how declining testosterone in aging males correlates with increased cardiovascular risk, reduced muscle mass, and metabolic syndrome—though whether low testosterone causes these conditions or results from them remains debated. Susan Davis's research demonstrates that testosterone in women affects sexual desire, muscle function, and bone density at physiological levels far below male ranges. The Women's Health Initiative revealed complex effects of hormone replacement therapy: while estrogen protects bone density and may benefit cognition when started near menopause, timing matters—starting years after menopause showed different cardiovascular outcomes, suggesting a "critical window" for some protective effects.

## When It Fails

Hypogonadism—inadequate sex hormone production—produces distinct cascades depending on timing. Prepubertal onset prevents normal sexual development and growth spurt. Adult-onset causes decreased bone mineral density (increasing fracture risk), reduced muscle mass and strength, metabolic changes including insulin resistance, mood alterations, and sexual dysfunction. In women, premature ovarian insufficiency (before age 40) not only causes infertility but accelerates cardiovascular disease risk and bone loss due to estrogen deficiency. Conversely, excess androgens in women (as in polycystic ovary syndrome affecting 6-12% of women) disrupts ovulation, causes insulin resistance, and produces hirsutism and acne through androgen receptor activation in hair follicles and sebaceous glands. The feedback loops can fail: pituitary tumors may overproduce LH/FSH, or hypothalamic dysfunction from extreme weight loss or excessive exercise suppresses GnRH, halting the entire cascade. Aromatase excess in males converts too much testosterone to estrogen, causing gynecomastia and metabolic dysfunction.

## Cases

The Minnesota Starvation Experiment (1944-45), though primarily about caloric restriction, documented dramatic hormonal shifts. As healthy men lost 25% of body weight, testosterone levels plummeted and remained suppressed even during refeeding—sexual interest vanished, testicular size decreased, and psychological effects (depression, irritability, obsessive food thoughts) correlated with hormonal changes. This revealed how energy availability governs the HPG axis through leptin signaling: the body shuts down reproduction when resources can't support it.

Anne Fausto-Sterling's research on athletes with differences of sexual development (DSD) illuminated hormone receptor variation. Caster Semenya has 46,XY chromosomes and internal testes producing male-typical testosterone, but complete or partial androgen insensitivity syndrome (CAIS/PAIS) means androgen receptors don't respond normally. Her body developed phenotypically female despite high testosterone—demonstrating that hormone effects require functional receptors, not just circulating hormones. This explains why testosterone levels alone don't determine athletic performance in DSD cases—tissue responsiveness matters as much as hormone concentration.

The cognitive effects of menopause reveal estrogen's neural role. Roberta Brinton's research shows that estrogen withdrawal affects mitochondrial function in neurons, reducing glucose metabolism in brain regions including the hippocampus. This explains why some women experience "brain fog" during menopause—not psychological but metabolic at the cellular level. Yet hormone replacement therapy doesn't universally restore function, supporting the "critical window hypothesis": estrogen receptor expression changes during the transition period, potentially closing the window for some neuroprotective effects if treatment begins too late.
