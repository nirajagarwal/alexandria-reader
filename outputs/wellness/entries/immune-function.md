# Immune Function

Your body distinguishes self from non-self billions of times per day, mounting precisely calibrated responses to threats while leaving your own cells unmolested. This discrimination between danger and safety, pathogen and protein, determines whether you fight off infection or develop autoimmunity, clear cancer cells or let them proliferate. Immune function is the difference between resilient health and chronic inflammation.

## What It Is

The immune system comprises innate defenses—barriers, phagocytes, inflammatory responses—that react immediately to generic threats, and adaptive immunity—T cells and B cells—that learn to recognize specific pathogens and remember them for decades. It operates through chemical signaling (cytokines, interferons), cellular armies (neutrophils, macrophages, lymphocytes), and antibody weapons manufactured in bone marrow and lymph nodes. The system extends beyond blood cells to include gut barrier integrity, skin microbiome, mucous membranes, and the lymphatic network that drains tissues. It's both a defense force and a surveillance system, constantly sampling what enters the body and deciding whether to tolerate or attack.

## The Mechanism

Innate immunity responds within hours. Epithelial barriers block entry; if breached, pattern-recognition receptors identify molecular signatures common to bacteria or viruses. Macrophages engulf invaders. Neutrophils release toxic chemicals. Inflammatory cytokines raise temperature and increase blood flow, creating hostile conditions for pathogens while recruiting more immune cells.

Adaptive immunity takes days but provides precision. Dendritic cells present fragments of invaders to T cells in lymph nodes. Helper T cells coordinate the response. Killer T cells destroy infected cells. B cells manufacture antibodies—Y-shaped proteins that bind to specific pathogens and mark them for destruction. Some B and T cells become memory cells, persisting for years, which is why you don't get chickenpox twice.

The system must be exquisitely calibrated. Too weak, and infections overwhelm you. Too aggressive, and it attacks your own tissues (autoimmunity) or overreacts to harmless substances (allergies). Regulatory T cells suppress excessive responses, maintaining tolerance to food proteins, beneficial bacteria, and self-antigens.

## The Evidence

Polly Matzinger's danger model, proposed in the 1990s, showed the immune system responds not just to foreign molecules but to danger signals from damaged cells—explaining why it tolerates the fetus (foreign but non-dangerous) while attacking transplanted organs. Her work revealed immunity as context-dependent, not simply self/non-self discrimination.

The hygiene hypothesis, supported by epidemiological data from Erika von Mutius and others, demonstrates that excessive cleanliness in childhood correlates with higher rates of asthma and allergies. Children raised on farms, exposed to diverse microbes and endotoxins, develop more robust immune regulation. The immune system requires microbial education.

Graham Rook's "old friends" hypothesis refined this: we evolved with certain microorganisms—gut bacteria, helminths, environmental mycobacteria—that trained regulatory pathways. Their absence in modern environments leaves the immune system untutored, prone to misfiring. Studies show inflammatory bowel disease, multiple sclerosis, and Type 1 diabetes rise in populations undergoing rapid modernization.

Research on psychoneuroimmunology by Janice Kiecolt-Glaser demonstrates that chronic stress elevates cortisol, which suppresses lymphocyte proliferation and antibody production. Caregivers of dementia patients show delayed wound healing and reduced vaccine responses—stress literally weakens immune defenses.

## When It Fails

Immunodeficiency leaves you vulnerable to opportunistic infections. HIV destroys helper T cells, the coordinators of adaptive immunity, allowing normally harmless organisms to become life-threatening. Primary immunodeficiencies, genetic disorders affecting specific immune components, reveal how each part matters—patients lacking antibodies suffer recurrent bacterial infections; those missing complement proteins cannot clear certain bacteria.

Autoimmunity occurs when self-tolerance breaks down. In Type 1 diabetes, T cells destroy insulin-producing pancreatic cells. In rheumatoid arthritis, immune cells attack joint tissue. In multiple sclerosis, they target myelin sheaths protecting neurons. These failures often involve genetic susceptibility triggered by environmental factors—infections, diet, stress, or toxin exposure that disrupt regulatory mechanisms.

Chronic inflammation, where the immune system remains activated without acute threat, damages tissues over time and associates with cardiovascular disease, cancer, neurodegeneration, and metabolic dysfunction. It's not dramatic immune failure but constant low-grade activation that wears the body down.

## A Case

In the 1980s, researchers studying parasitic worms noticed patients with multiple sclerosis in regions with high helminth infection rates had milder symptoms. This observation led Joel Weinstock to conduct trials giving Crohn's disease patients controlled infections with *Trichuris suis*, pig whipworms that colonize temporarily without causing disease. Results showed reduced inflammation—helminths secrete molecules that promote regulatory T cell development and dampen aggressive immune responses.

Separate studies of inflammatory bowel disease patients moving from developing to developed countries found disease onset coincided with helminth eradication. The mechanism became clear: these "old friend" parasites had coevolved with humans for millennia, and their presence shaped immune regulation. Without them, the system lacks proper calibration.

Meanwhile, research on sleep deprivation by Luciana Besedovsky showed that a single night of poor sleep reduces T cell adhesion molecules needed to attach to infected cells, measurably weakening immune surveillance. Subjects given hepatitis A vaccine after sleep restriction produced 50% fewer antibodies than well-rested controls. The immune system requires sleep's restorative processes to function—memory cell formation happens during deep sleep stages, growth hormone released nocturnally supports immune cell proliferation.
