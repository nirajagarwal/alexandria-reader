# Margery Kempe

Around 1373, in the port town of King's Lynn, England, a woman named Margery Kempe was born into a prosperous merchant family. She married young, bore fourteen children, ran a brewing business, attempted a milling enterprise, and by all accounts lived an ordinary life for a woman of her class—until a profound religious conversion in her late twenties transformed her into something her society didn't quite know what to do with: a laywoman claiming direct mystical experience of Christ, weeping uncontrollably in public, and traveling on pilgrimage while still married.

She was loud, difficult, probably insufferable to be around, and she dictated what became the first autobiography in English.

## The Experience

Kempe's mystical life began after a mental breakdown following the birth of her first child. She describes seeing devils with "mouths all inflamed" and being tormented for months until Christ appeared to her, sitting on her bedside "in the likeness of a man, most seemly, most beauteous, and most amiable," asking why she had forsaken him. The vision restored her sanity and marked the beginning of what would become an overwhelming intimacy with the divine.

Her experiences centered on what she called "cries"—loud, uncontrollable weeping and roaring that would overtake her during Mass, at holy sites, or when contemplating Christ's Passion. "She would cry marvelously loud and weep and sob full hideously," she dictates, aware of how this appeared to others. These episodes could last for hours. She saw visions of Christ's birth, watched Mary at the foot of the cross, spoke with God the Father in her mind, and experienced what she understood as mystical marriage to the Godhead.

The content was often domestic and tender. Christ tells her, "I must be intimate with you and lie in your bed with you." She has conversations with Mary about motherhood. She experiences profound grief over the Passion as if she were actually present. Her mysticism was not abstract theology but immediate, embodied, relational—and her culture found this deeply suspicious in a laywoman.

## The Path

Kempe's practices were those available to a laywoman: frequent confession, daily Mass when possible, long hours of meditation on Christ's life and Passion, pilgrimage to Jerusalem, Rome, Santiago de Compostela, and various English shrines. She negotiated a chastity agreement with her husband after years of marriage and many children—paying his debts in exchange for his consent to celibacy.

She wore white clothing, which she understood as a divine command but which marked her as claiming special status. She fasted rigorously. She wept. She talked—to priests, to other pilgrims, to anyone who would listen about her visions and about sin and repentance. Her path was not monastic or eremitic but public and peripatetic, carried out in the world while claiming direct access to divine revelation.

## The Teaching

Kempe had no systematic teaching because she couldn't write—she was illiterate. What she taught was by example and conversation, and the teaching was simply this: a laywoman, a mother, a failed businesswoman, could have direct encounter with God. Her *Book*, dictated to two different scribes years after the experiences, validates the inner life of an ordinary person against a church that largely reserved mystical authority for the educated, the celibate, and the male.

She instructed by insisting on the legitimacy of her own experience. When priests questioned her, she cited Scripture back to them from memory. When fellow pilgrims found her weeping intolerable, she reminded them that Mary's grief was greater. She taught that intense emotional response to the divine was not hysteria but a gift—"the gift of tears" that the church itself recognized in saints, even if it didn't recognize it in her.

## The Reception

Kempe was investigated for heresy multiple times. The early 15th century was the age of Lollardy in England—religious dissent that threatened church authority—and a laywoman claiming direct revelation, preaching about sin, and wearing white was immediately suspect. She was examined by the Archbishop of York, by various bishops and magistrates, accused of being a Lollard, threatened with burning.

She survived these encounters partly through quick wit and theological knowledge, partly through her ability to secure testimonials from respected clergy including Julian of Norwich, whom she visited and who apparently affirmed her experiences. Julian told her, she reports, "The Holy Ghost moves never a thing against charity."

But she remained controversial even among the devout. Fellow pilgrims tried to abandon her. Her crying disrupted services. Priests refused her communion. Even sympathetic clergy found her exhausting. The monk Richard of Caister supported her but also likely helped shape her *Book* to make it more acceptable to church authorities.

The *Book* itself disappeared for five hundred years. The single surviving manuscript was discovered in 1934, and suddenly scholars had this extraordinary document—an illiterate woman's account of her own inner life, her travels, her negotiations with male authority, her claims to divine intimacy, all recorded in her own voice through her scribes.

## The Legacy

Kempe made visible what had always existed but rarely been recorded: the religious experience of laywomen, the mysticism of the embodied and domestic, the validity of excessive emotion as spiritual path. She didn't found an order or inspire a movement, but she left a document that scholars of women's spirituality, medieval mysticism, and autobiography mine endlessly.

Medieval historian Caroline Walker Bynum notes how women mystics often emphasized bodily, affective piety—experiencing Christ through identification with his suffering body rather than through intellectual contemplation. Kempe is perhaps the most extreme example of this mode in English tradition.

Modern psychological readings suggest possible diagnoses—religious mania, temporal lobe epilepsy, post-traumatic stress from childbirth, histrionic personality disorder. All may contain truth. None erases what she actually reported: encounters with a presence she understood as divine, experiences of overwhelming love and grief, a transformation of consciousness that gave meaning to an otherwise ordinary life.

She stands as the awkward, necessary reminder that mystical experience doesn't only come to the educated, the cloistered, the refined—and that the church often didn't know what to do with the mystics it couldn't control.
