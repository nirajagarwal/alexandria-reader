# Bonaventure

A Franciscan friar teaching theology at the University of Paris in the 1250s, Giovanni di Fidanza - called Bonaventure - constructed what may be the most systematic map of the mystical ascent ever written. He was both scholar and contemplative, university master and later Minister General of his order, writing dense academic treatises while insisting that theology without prayer was merely philosophy. His *Itinerarium Mentis in Deum* - "The Soul's Journey into God" - treats mystical experience as a structured ascent through six stages of illumination, each building on the last, each requiring both intellectual clarity and experiential depth.

Bonaventure joined the Franciscans at Paris around 1243, studied under Alexander of Hales, and witnessed the early struggles to preserve Francis of Assisi's radical poverty while managing a growing international order. He never abandoned the intellectual life for pure contemplation - he united them. His mysticism was Franciscan: creation is a book God wrote, every creature a word pointing back to its source. The ascent to God moves through creation, not away from it. "Whoever is not enlightened by such splendor of created things is blind," he wrote. "Whoever is not awakened by such outcries is deaf."

The *Itinerarium* maps six stages. First, observe God's traces in the external world - the order and beauty of creation. Second, find God's image reflected in your own sensory experience. Third, discover God's image in your interior faculties - memory, understanding, will. Fourth, contemplate God's being as refracted through these reformed faculties. Fifth, gaze upon the divine unity - Being itself. Sixth, rest in the darkness beyond all concepts, where God is known by "unknowing." The seventh stage is mystical rest, the Sabbath beyond striving, but Bonaventure was careful: this is grace, not technique. "Let the intellect be stilled," he instructed. "Let prayer wholly cease from speaking."

He described the experience as excess - a movement beyond what reason can encompass. Following Pseudo-Dionysius, he called the culmination a "darkness," but it arrives after maximum light, not instead of it. Understanding doesn't cease; it's overwhelmed. "If you ask how these things come about," he wrote, "question grace, not instruction; desire, not intellect; the cry of prayer, not pursuit of study; the spouse, not the teacher; God, not man; darkness, not clarity." This is a mystic speaking from experience, not just systematizing others' reports.

Bonaventure grounded his theology in Christ as the *exemplar* - the pattern and possibility of union. The Incarnation isn't just redemption; it's the revelation that divinity and humanity can interpenetrate without destruction. Francis receiving the stigmata - Christ's wounds appearing on his body - was for Bonaventure the supreme historical proof that such union is real. He wrote his *Life of St. Francis* not as hagiography but as demonstration: here is what full conformity to Christ looks like. The wounds weren't metaphor.

His teaching career ended abruptly in 1257 when he was elected Minister General of the Franciscans, then fracturing over how strictly to follow Francis's insistence on absolute poverty. Bonaventure navigated the controversy by distinguishing *use* from *ownership* - Franciscans could use things without possessing them - a solution that preserved the order but outraged the rigorist "Spiritual" faction. He was consumed by administration, writing letters and constitutions while longing for contemplation. "I confess before God," he wrote near the end, "that what makes me love the state of poverty most is that it keeps us free for God."

Made Cardinal Bishop of Albano by Pope Gregory X in 1273, he died the following year at the Council of Lyons, attempting to reunite the Eastern and Western churches. He was fifty-three. The reconciliation failed. His body was buried in the Franciscan church at Lyons; his head was later moved to a reliquary and lost during the French Revolution.

Medieval Franciscans called him the *Seraphic Doctor* - the angelic theologian who proved that rigorous thought and mystical experience were not enemies but collaborators. Bernard McGinn describes his achievement as making "the intellectual life itself a mystical path." Ewert Cousins's translation and introduction to the *Itinerarium* restored Bonaventure to modern readers in 1978, revealing a thinker who prefigured contemporary interest in embodied spirituality and creation-centered mysticism. His insistence that matter can mediate the divine - that creation is not obstacle but gateway - influenced everything from Ignatian spirituality to Teilhard de Chardin's cosmic vision.

The *Itinerarium* remains one of the clearest navigational charts through mystical ascent ever written - not because it makes the journey easy, but because it acknowledges exactly what the soul must traverse and what it must finally surrender. Bonaventure never pretended the map was the territory.
