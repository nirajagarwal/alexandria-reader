# Julian of Norwich

Medieval England's most original theologian was an anchoress locked in a cell attached to St. Julian's Church in Norwich. Between 1373 and her death sometime after 1416, the woman we know only as Julian wrote the first book in English by a woman - an extended meditation on sixteen visions she received during a near-fatal illness at age thirty. What she produced was radical: a systematic theology built not on scholastic argument but on direct vision, centering on a God who is love and only love, who cannot be angry, and in whom "all shall be well."

## The Visions

On May 8, 1373, Julian lay dying. A priest brought a crucifix to her bedside. As she gazed at it, her pain suddenly ceased and the visions began. Over several hours, she saw Christ's passion in vivid, physical detail - blood running down his face from the crown of thorns, his body drying and dying on the cross. But these were not mere Passion meditations. Within and through Christ's suffering, she saw the entire created world "as it were a hazelnut in the palm of my hand" and understood that it lasts because "God loves it." She saw Christ's blood falling on the earth like raindrops. She saw him showing her his side wound and saying, "See how I loved you."

The visions lasted about five hours. Then Julian recovered. She spent the next twenty years contemplating what she had seen.

## The Theology

Julian's *Revelations of Divine Love* exists in two versions - a short text written soon after the visions, and a long text completed around 1393 after two decades of prayer and reflection. The long text is one of medieval Christianity's most daring theological works.

At its center is Julian's insistence that God cannot be angry. "I saw no wrath except on man's side, and that he forgives in us." This contradicted the dominant theology of her time, which emphasized divine judgment, wrath, and retribution. Julian saw none of it. In her visions, Christ suffers *with* humanity, not against it. God is not split between justice and mercy - there is only love, and what looks like judgment is our own misperception.

She went further. God is not only Father but Mother: "As truly as God is our Father, so truly is God our Mother." This wasn't soft sentiment - Julian meant it ontologically. Christ mothers us, feeds us, teaches us. "The mother can give her child to suck of her milk, but our precious Mother Jesus can feed us with himself." The maternal Christ nurses the soul with the Eucharist, disciplines through suffering, and delights in the soul's growth.

## The Problem and the Promise

Julian faced the theological problem that wrecked her contemporaries: if God is all-powerful and all-loving, why does sin and suffering persist? She had no systematic answer. Instead, she recorded what she was shown: "Sin is necessary, but all shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of thing shall be well."

She didn't explain away evil or suffering. She acknowledged that the church taught one thing (the necessity of judgment) while her visions showed another (the impossibility of divine wrath). She was shown that there is "a deed that the Holy Trinity shall do on the last day... and what the deed shall be and how it shall be done is unknown to all creatures beneath Christ." Some secret reconciliation exists between God's love and the reality of sin - but she was not shown what it is, only that it exists.

This was not universal salvation as a doctrine but universal salvation as vision - she saw it, she didn't deduce it.

## The Cell

Sometime after recovering from her illness, Julian became an anchoress - a woman permanently enclosed in a small cell attached to a church, living a life of prayer and counsel. The cell had three windows: one to the church (to hear Mass and receive communion), one for necessities to be passed in, and one through which she could speak to those who came seeking guidance.

The anchorhold was not quite solitary confinement. Julian had servants, perhaps a cat, and clearly access to books - she knew patristic theology, scholastic arguments, mystical texts. People came to her window. Margery Kempe, another English mystic whose visions were considerably more dramatic and less welcome, visited Julian around 1413 and recorded being counseled to test whether her experiences drew her closer to God and neighbor. Julian told her to be patient with doubters: "The same Holy Spirit that revealed truth to you will give others grace to believe you."

## The Reception

Julian wrote in Middle English, not Latin - a choice that marked her work as devotional rather than academic, accessible rather than scholarly. She circulated only in manuscript for centuries. The first printed edition appeared in 1670, but she remained obscure outside specialist circles until the twentieth century. T.S. Eliot quoted her in *Four Quartets* ("And all shall be well"), and suddenly scholars and seekers rediscovered her.

Modern readers have variously claimed her as a proto-feminist, a universalist, a mystic psychologist, and an orthodox contemplative. The medieval church investigated her but apparently cleared her - she remained in her anchorhold until her death, which occurred sometime after 1416 when historical records fade.

Feminist theologians have recovered her maternal God-language. Mystics prize her insistence that contemplative theology comes from experience, not argument. Process theologians note her non-wrathful God. Orthodox critics point out she never denies hell outright - just that she can't see how God's love and eternal damnation coexist.

## The Legacy

Julian left no school, founded no order, trained no disciples. But her anchorhold's third window - the one through which she counseled visitors - models mystical practice made available. She didn't withdraw from the world to hoard experience but to metabolize vision into wisdom others could use.

Her "all shall be well" has become modern Christianity's most quoted mystical phrase, often reduced to bland reassurance. Julian meant something harder: not that nothing bad will happen, but that some reconciliation exists that we cannot yet see - that God's love will prove foundational in ways we cannot imagine. She refused explanations. She reported visions.

The *Revelations* remain the most systematic theology built on direct experience in Christian history. A woman in a cell, contemplating visions received during a brush with death, produced theology that two centuries of universities had not: a God who is only love, who cannot be angry, who mothers the soul, and in whom every created thing will somehow, impossibly, be made well.
