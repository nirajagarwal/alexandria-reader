# Brother Lawrence

Nicholas Herman was born around 1614 in French Lorraine, served as a soldier, took a musket ball to the sciatic nerve that left him limping for life, worked as a footman to a treasury official, and at midlife entered a Carmelite monastery in Paris as a lay brother. He was assigned to the kitchen. He died in 1691, having spent roughly forty years washing dishes, mending sandals, and cooking meals for his community. He wrote almost nothing himself. He is remembered because he talked about God while doing the dishes, and someone wrote it down.

What Brother Lawrence practiced was continuous conversation with God while performing menial tasks. Not prayer before work or after work, but prayer *as* work. He called it "the practice of the presence of God" - a phrase that has become so common it obscures how strange his claim was: that peeling potatoes and scrubbing pots could become direct encounter with the divine, no less than formal liturgy or contemplative silence.

The texts that preserve his teaching are secondhand. Joseph de Beaufort, vicar general to the Archbishop of Paris, visited the monastery kitchen multiple times in Lawrence's later years, took notes on their conversations, and collected a handful of Lawrence's letters. These were published in 1692, a year after Lawrence's death, as *The Practice of the Presence of God*. The book is a slim compilation - conversational fragments, letters, and de Beaufort's recollections. But it has never gone out of print.

Lawrence claimed no visions, heard no voices, experienced no ecstasies. His mysticism was relentlessly ordinary. He described his method: begin the day by offering all work to God. When the mind wanders - and it will wander constantly - bring it back. Not with strain or self-recrimination, but gently, as many times as necessary. "The time of business does not with me differ from the time of prayer," he told de Beaufort. "In the noise and clatter of my kitchen, while several persons are at the same time calling for different things, I possess God in as great tranquility as if I were upon my knees."

This was not, he insisted, a technique that required years of training or theological sophistication. It was simpler than that: turn attention toward God, constantly, in the midst of whatever you're doing. He compared it to learning a skill - awkward at first, then gradually natural. What made it mystical was his claim about what happened when the practice deepened. The sense of God's presence became so continuous that he stopped needing to remind himself. "It would be as difficult for me not to think of God," he wrote, "as it was at first to accustom myself to it."

Lawrence's path had no special practices beyond this relentless redirection of attention. He was skeptical of excessive mortification, complicated spiritual methods, or anxious introspection. "We ought not to be weary of doing little things for the love of God," he said, "who regards not the greatness of the work, but the love with which it is performed." He stayed in the kitchen. He praised God while turning an omelet.

His teaching generated no school, no movement, no monastic reform. He was not condemned, not canonized, not particularly controversial within his own community. The Discalced Carmelites noted his death in their records with the same brevity they gave everyone. De Beaufort's book brought him wider attention, but Lawrence himself never sought it. He was consulted sometimes by people with spiritual questions; he answered briefly and went back to his pots.

What makes his witness significant is its radical accessibility. Medieval mystics like Eckhart or John of the Cross described towering architectures of ascent through purgation, illumination, union. The desert fathers fled to caves. Lawrence stayed in the kitchen and said God was fully there. His claim was that contemplative union - the state mystics sought through years of intense practice - was available to anyone who would practice simple attention in the middle of an ordinary life. Wash the dishes. Mend the sandals. Think about God. Keep thinking about God. That's the method.

The practice has influenced Quaker silent waiting, evangelical "practicing the presence," charismatic "prayer without ceasing," and secular mindfulness adapted from religious sources. Frank Laubach's twentieth-century "game with minutes" - trying to think about God at least once per minute throughout the day - is a direct descendant. Thomas Kelly's *A Testament of Devotion* expands Lawrence's kitchen into modern professional life. The same essential claim: continuous attention transforms ordinary action into prayer.

Scholars treat Lawrence less as a theologian than as an exemplar of lay mysticism - the Christianity available to the illiterate, the busy, the unordained. Caroline Walker Bynum and others have noted how medieval women mystics, denied clerical authority, claimed divine encounter through domestic labor and bodily experience. Lawrence, male but lowborn and uneducated, offers a parallel: mysticism for people who can't afford a hermitage.

What he left was a method so simple it's hard to take seriously. Turn your mind toward God. Do it constantly. Keep doing it while you work. Eventually the gap between prayer and life collapses. Brother Lawrence spent four decades testing whether this was true. His testimony, preserved in fragmentary conversations, is that it was.
