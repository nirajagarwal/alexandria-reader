# Amma Syncletica

Around 270 CE in Alexandria, a wealthy young woman from a prominent Macedonian family refused marriage and inheritance. Syncletica would not become a society matron or enter a respectable convent. After her parents died, she gave away her fortune, cut her hair, and disappeared into a family crypt outside the city with her blind younger sister. There, in a tomb, she lived for perhaps eighty years – long enough to become one of the most frequently quoted teachers of the desert tradition, though she never sought students and barely left the dead.

The *Apophthegmata Patrum* – the Sayings of the Desert Fathers – includes twenty-seven sayings attributed to her, making Syncletica one of only three *ammas* (mothers) preserved in that collection alongside hundreds of *abbas* (fathers). These sayings don't describe visions or ecstatic experiences. They describe warfare. "In the beginning there are a great many battles and a good deal of suffering for those who are advancing towards God," she taught, "but afterwards, there is ineffable joy. It is like those who wish to light a fire: at first they are choked by the smoke and cry, and by this means obtain what they seek. So we must kindle the divine fire in ourselves through tears and hard work."

She knew what she was talking about. According to her *Life*, written perhaps a century after her death and modeled explicitly on Athanasius's *Life of Antony*, Syncletica spent years in a tomb wrestling with demons that manifested as bodily symptoms – burning fever, putrefying mouth cancer, gangrenous lungs. The text may blend hagiographic convention with actual ascetic hardship, but the teaching that emerged from that darkness was precise. She understood contemplative practice as a craft requiring specific technique: "Just as the most bitter medicine drives out poisonous creatures, so prayer joined to fasting drives evil thoughts away."

Her aphorisms cut through spiritual pretense. On fasting: "If you are living in a monastery, do not move to another place, for it will harm you greatly. If a bird deserts the eggs she was sitting on, she prevents them from hatching." On stability: "It is dangerous for anyone to teach who has not first been trained in the 'practical' life." On spiritual ambition masking pride: "We who have chosen this way of life must obtain perfect temperance. It is true that among seculars also there is temperance, but it is incomplete, since they indulge in other things."

She was particularly alert to the ways women practicing asceticism attracted male attention – the voyeurism around female renunciation, the eroticization of female fasting. Better to stay hidden, she counseled. Better to remain anonymous. The tomb kept her safe from spectators while she worked.

Syncletica taught in a lineage that scholar Benedicta Ward traces back to Antony but developed distinctive emphases. Where male desert abbas often fled alone into the wilderness, Syncletica remained in community with her sister, caring for her until death. Where the fathers sometimes gloried in extreme asceticism, Syncletica warned against measuring holiness by outward severity: "There are many who live in the mountains and behave as if they were in the town, and they are wasting their time. It is possible to be a solitary in one's mind while living in a crowd, and it is possible for one who is a solitary to live in the crowd of his own thoughts."

The question of what she actually experienced in that tomb – what encounters she had in decades of prayer in darkness among the dead – goes largely unrecorded. She did not traffic in extraordinary phenomena. Her sayings point toward something more foundational: the dismantling of the false self through ascetic practice, the slow burning away of ego-attachment, the discovery of what remains when everything else is gone. "In the beginning there are a great many battles," she said. Then, afterwards, ineffable joy. But she didn't elaborate on the joy, perhaps because, as she also taught, "just as one cannot build a ship unless one has some nails, so it is impossible to be saved without humility."

The church honored her without canonizing her in the Western sense – no formal cult, no feast day in the Roman calendar, though the Orthodox commemorate her on January 5. Her teaching influenced John Cassian's *Conferences*, which became foundational for Western monasticism. Through Cassian, her insights about the warfare of contemplative practice, the dangers of acedia (spiritual torpor), and the necessity of stability entered Benedictine tradition. Contemporary scholars like Laura Swan note how rarely early Christian women's voices survived the manuscript tradition. That Syncletica's did suggests her early communities found her indispensable.

She died, according to her *Life*, after three months of fever and gangrenous lung disease that left her unable to lie down, sitting upright as her body consumed itself. She was around eighty years old. To visitors who came to the tomb in her final days, she said: "You see these ravages which my body has suffered. If the inner self were also perishing, that would indeed be hard to bear. But this is no small cause for hope, that when the outer self is sick, the inner self is renewed." Then she asked them to leave. She had work to finish.
