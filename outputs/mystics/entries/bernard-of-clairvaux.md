# Bernard of Clairvaux

Bernard of Clairvaux (1090-1153) was a Cistercian abbot who became the most influential religious figure of 12th-century Europe - preaching the Second Crusade, deposing popes, corresponding with kings, all while insisting that his true work happened in silence, in his monastery, in prayer. He was a mystic who shaped politics, a contemplative who moved armies, a man who claimed to want nothing but God while exercising power few saints have matched.

## The Reluctant Power Broker

Born to Burgundian nobility, Bernard entered the monastery at Cîteaux in 1113 with thirty relatives in tow - he'd convinced them all to join him. By 1115, he was founding his own monastery at Clairvaux in a desolate valley. Within two decades, Clairvaux had sent out sixty-five daughter houses across Europe. Bernard had not sought this. He was chronically ill, exhausted, driven by something he experienced in prayer that would not let him rest.

The experience was erotic, embodied, overwhelming. His sermons on the Song of Songs - eighty-six of them, never completed - describe the soul's encounter with Christ as a lover's union. "Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth," the biblical text begins, and Bernard takes it literally. Not metaphorically - literally. The soul and God as bride and bridegroom, desire and consummation, presence and absence. He writes: "When the Word has visited me... I have felt that He was present; I remember later that He has been with me; I have sometimes even had a presentiment that He would come; but never did I feel His coming or His departure."

This is not theology - this is testimony. Bernard knew the difference between thinking about God and being invaded by God's presence. The presence came and went. He could not control it. What he could do was prepare, through lectio divina (divine reading), through the discipline of monastic life, through what he called "holy reading" that "lifts the veil" and lets Scripture become present experience rather than ancient text.

## The Honeyed Doctor

They called him "Doctor Mellifluus" - the honeyed doctor - for the sweetness of his prose. But the honey concealed steel. Bernard's mysticism fueled his certainty, and his certainty was absolute. He crushed the brilliant theologian Peter Abelard, whose rational approach to faith Bernard found cold and dangerous. He preached the Second Crusade with apocalyptic fervor, promising that fighting Muslims was an opportunity for salvation - when the crusade failed catastrophically, he blamed the sins of the crusaders. He was tender in his monastery, ruthless in the world.

His treatise *On Loving God* maps four stages of love: loving self for self's sake, loving God for self's sake, loving God for God's sake, and finally - rarely achieved - loving self for God's sake alone. This is the trajectory of mystical life as Bernard experienced it: progressive self-emptying until nothing remains but desire for God. "My merit is the mercy of God," he wrote. "I am not utterly without merit so long as He is not without mercy."

The darkness came too. Bernard knew desolation, the absence of the Beloved, the silence that felt like abandonment. His mysticism was not ecstasy alone - it was the full cycle of presence and loss, consolation and desolation, the "spiritual marriage" that includes nights when the bridegroom does not come.

## The Teacher of Desire

Bernard left no systematic method - he distrusted systems. But he taught through example and exhortation: seek God in Scripture, especially the Song of Songs. Submit to monastic discipline - not as punishment but as preparation. Wait in desire. The spiritual life is training the soul to want God more than comfort, more than visions, more than certainty. "We taste Thee, O Thou Living Bread, and we long for Thee," he prayed. The longing was the point.

His influence on Cistercian spirituality was total - the order became synonymous with a mysticism grounded in desire and embodied encounter. His theology of the "three kisses" (the kiss of the feet in penitence, the kiss of the hands in good works, the kiss of the mouth in contemplative union) shaped Christian contemplative practice for centuries. Teresa of Ávila knew Bernard. John of the Cross echoed him. The lineage is direct.

## The Saint Who Moved Armies

The medieval church canonized Bernard in 1174, just twenty-one years after his death. But historians wrestle with him. Caroline Walker Bynum notes how his violently dualistic language - body versus soul, flesh versus spirit - influenced medieval asceticism in troubling ways. Jean Leclercq's *The Love of Learning and the Desire for God* sees Bernard as exemplar of monastic theology, where study and prayer are inseparable. Etienne Gilson's *The Mystical Theology of Saint Bernard* argues he was primarily a mystic, not a theologian - his influence came from reported encounter, not coherent system.

Modern readers struggle with Bernard's politics - the crusade preaching, the persecution of Abelard, the certainty that could justify violence. But his mysticism remains undeniable: a lived testimony that the soul can experience union with God as intimately as lovers know each other, and that this experience is simultaneously gift and wound, presence and loss, honey and steel.

His last sermon on the Song of Songs ends mid-sentence. He died before completing it - fitting for a mystic who insisted the encounter never completes, the desire never ends, the Beloved is always approaching and always just beyond reach.
