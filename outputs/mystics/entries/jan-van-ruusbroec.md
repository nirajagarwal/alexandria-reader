# Jan van Ruusbroec

Jan van Ruusbroec (1293-1381) was a Flemish priest who spent the first half of his life serving a Brussels parish and the second half in a forest hermitage, writing prose so dense with mystical theology that it has confounded readers for six centuries. He's called "the Admirable" - a title that captures both reverence and bewilderment. His works describe stages of union with God in such intricate detail that they read like field reports from terrain few have entered. Meister Eckhart's condemnation was still fresh when Ruusbroec wrote; he knew the dangers of mystical language pushed too far, yet he pushed anyway.

Born in the village of Ruusbroec near Brussels, he was ordained at twenty-four and served for twenty-six years as a vicar at St. Gudule's Cathedral. In 1343, at age fifty, he and two companions withdrew to a hermitage in the forest of Soignes, seven miles from the city. The site became an Augustinian priory - Groenendaal, "Green Valley" - and Ruusbroec its prior. He spent the last thirty-eight years of his life there, writing, directing the community, and receiving visitors who came seeking his counsel. He died at eighty-eight, having produced twelve major works in Middle Dutch, all composed in that forest hermitage.

The experience Ruusbroec describes is not a single vision or moment of ecstasy but a structured ascent through degrees of intimacy with God. His masterwork, *The Spiritual Espousals*, maps three stages: the active life, the interior life, and the contemplative life. But it's the final stage - what he calls "the God-seeing life" - that pushes into territory so rarefied that translation barely helps. He writes of "essential union" where the soul transcends all images, all thoughts, all activity, and rests in bare unity with the divine ground. "We are all one," he insists, "one with God in essential being." This sounds like Eckhart's dangerous pantheism. But Ruusbroec immediately qualifies: we remain creatures; the distinction between Creator and created never dissolves completely. It's a both/and that Western theology struggles to hold.

His most controversial passage describes a state "above" even love - a naked encounter with the divine essence that precedes all distinctions, all relations, even the distinction between lover and beloved. "There," he writes, "the spirit is both active and at rest, for God is above activity and rest." The soul meets God "in the wild desert of the Godhead, where there is no distinction." Jean Gerson, Chancellor of the University of Paris, attacked this as heretical quietism in 1408. Gerson worried that Ruusbroec's language implied passivity, absorption, the loss of the soul's integrity. The controversy dogged Ruusbroec's reputation for centuries.

His path is not passivity but intense discipline. The active life requires ethical rigor - virtue, charity, obedience to commandments. The interior life demands prayer, meditation, abandonment of self-will. Only then, in rare souls and rarer moments, comes the contemplative breakthrough into essential union. Ruusbroec describes it as simultaneous rest and activity, a paradox that defies normal categories. The soul rests in God's ground while simultaneously flowing out in love and action. "Rest," he writes, "does not hinder work, and work does not hinder rest." It's mysticism without world-denial, union without quietism.

He was a practical man. His writings are mystical theology, not ecstatic poetry, constructed with scholastic precision. He quotes Scripture constantly, grounds his language in orthodox Trinitarian doctrine, and insists that true mystical experience manifests in renewed charity toward others. Visions, he warns, can mislead. Feelings of sweetness prove nothing. What matters is transformation - whether the soul grows in love, in humility, in self-forgetting service. "By their fruits you shall know them," he repeats. The mystic's life must show forth the union it claims.

Contemporary reception was mixed. His community revered him. Visitors came from across Europe - Gerard Groote, founder of the Brethren of the Common Life, spent months at Groenendaal absorbing Ruusbroec's teaching. Thomas à Kempis, author of *The Imitation of Christ*, studied Ruusbroec's works. But academic theologians were suspicious. After the Gerson controversy, Ruusbroec's works were copied but not widely distributed. He was beatified in 1908, but full canonization has never come - partly because his language remains so resistant to easy interpretation.

Modern scholars approach him with awe and caution. Paul Mommaers and Bernard McGinn have worked to situate him in the tradition - showing his debts to Hadewijch and to the Rhineland mystics, while marking his unique contribution. Where Eckhart speculated boldly and paid the price, Ruusbroec tried to describe the same depths with enough theological guardrails to avoid condemnation. Mostly he succeeded. His phenomenology of contemplative experience remains unmatched in Western mysticism for sheer detail and precision.

His legacy is the lineage that flows from Groenendaal into the Devotio Moderna movement, which shaped late medieval spirituality and influenced both Catholic reform and Protestant pietism. His insistence that mystical union must manifest in ethical action, not replace it, became normative for Catholic mysticism. And his vocabulary - "essential union," "the ground of the soul," "flowing out and flowing back" - provided language for mystics who came after to describe what cannot quite be described.

He remains difficult. *The Spiritual Espousals* requires multiple readings; sentences double back on themselves, qualifications pile up, paradoxes refuse to resolve. But for those willing to follow him into the forest, he offers something rare: not poetry about union, but cartography of it.
