# Bede Griffiths

An English Benedictine monk in orange robes, sitting cross-legged in an Indian ashram - this was Bede Griffiths in his final decades, a figure who tried to live at the meeting point of Christianity and Hinduism. Born in 1906, he died in 1993, having spent nearly forty years in India attempting what many considered impossible: the integration of Christian monasticism with Hindu contemplative practice. Whether he succeeded or merely created a beautiful syncretism remains contested.

Alan Richard Griffiths grew up in middle-class England, lost his Anglican faith as an adolescent, then recovered it dramatically at Oxford through a mystical experience in nature - "a sudden intuition of the living presence of the divine reality in nature." He converted to Catholicism, became a Benedictine monk, took the name Bede, and might have lived out his days in European monasteries if India had not called him. In 1955, at age forty-eight, he went to India to establish a Christian ashram, eventually settling at Shantivanam in Tamil Nadu, where he would remain for the rest of his life.

What Griffiths attempted was radical adoption. He didn't just study Hinduism - he lived it as fully as a Christian could. He wore the *kavi*, the orange robes of a *sannyasi*, a renunciant. He practiced Hindu forms of meditation, incorporated Sanskrit liturgy and bhajans into Christian worship, sat cross-legged for Mass, and read the *Upanishads* and *Bhagavad Gita* as deeply as the Gospels. He believed that Christianity needed what he called the "marriage of East and West" - that the rational, masculine, transcendent emphasis of Western Christianity required the intuitive, feminine, immanent wisdom of the East. His ashram became a place where Western seekers came to find a Christianity that breathed with Eastern contemplative practice.

His mysticism was theological rather than visionary. He didn't report dramatic experiences or dark nights, but rather worked toward what he called "cosmic consciousness" - an intuitive grasp of the divine presence in all things, beyond conceptual thought. In 1990, at age eighty-three, he suffered a severe stroke that left him temporarily paralyzed. He later described this as his deepest mystical breakthrough - a forced surrender that broke through his intellectual approach to God. "I suddenly felt this tremendous power of love," he wrote. "It was so powerful I burst into tears." His last years were marked by what he called a "descent into the feminine," a dissolution of the mental structures he had spent decades building.

His path was *sadhana* - Hindu spiritual practice adapted for Christian purposes. He practiced and taught forms of meditation that focused on going beyond thought to direct intuitive awareness. He advocated *jnana yoga*, the path of knowledge, integrated with *bhakti yoga*, the path of devotion, all in service of Christian contemplation. His Mass was celebrated with Sanskrit chants, Hindu mudras, and readings from both the Gospels and the Vedas. He taught that Christ is the universal presence found in all religions, that what Hindus call *Atman* or *Brahman* is what Christians call the indwelling Spirit.

The Catholic Church kept a watchful distance. Griffiths was never censured, but neither was he embraced. His work raised hard questions: Was he discovering the universal Christ in Hindu wisdom, or was he diluting Christianity into a vague perennialism? His defenders, including Thomas Merton (who corresponded with him) and Wayne Teasdale, saw him as a pioneer of deep interfaith dialogue. His critics - and there were many - saw syncretism, the loss of Christian distinctiveness, a man who had gone too far into Hindu thought to remain meaningfully Christian. The Orthodox theologian Kallistos Ware wrote appreciatively but carefully about Griffiths's "bold experiment," noting that such experiments require "spiritual discernment of a very high order."

What survives is a model of radical spiritual hospitality and a library of books attempting to articulate a Christian Vedanta. *The Marriage of East and West*, *Return to the Centre*, and *A New Vision of Reality* lay out his theological vision - that Christianity and Hinduism (and other traditions) are different paths up the same mountain, that contemplative experience reveals this underlying unity, that the next stage of Christian evolution requires this integration. Shantivanam still operates as an ashram-monastery. The category of "Christian Advaita" - Christian non-dualism - has become a recognized (if debated) contemplative path, and Griffiths is its most prominent Western exemplar.

His legacy is contested precisely because his project was radical. He pushed Christianity's boundaries harder than almost anyone in his century, and whether he enriched it or thinned it depends on your theological commitments. What's undeniable is that he lived with extraordinary integrity - forty years in India, adopting its forms, studying its texts, sitting in its silence, trying to find Christ there. Whether he found Christ or something else, he found it by going further than most Christians dared.
