# Catherine of Genoa

Catherine Fieschi was born in 1447 to one of Genoa's most powerful families - the Fieschi had produced two popes - but her adolescent desire for religious life was denied. At sixteen she was married off to Giuliano Adorno, a political alliance between rival clans. The marriage was a disaster. Giuliano was unfaithful, violent, a compulsive gambler who burned through his fortune. Catherine spent a decade in what she later called "a prison," attempting suicide, sinking into depression so profound she could barely leave her rooms.

Then in March 1473, while making confession at the urging of her sister (a nun), something broke open. Catherine experienced what she could only describe as a wound - her heart pierced by an overwhelming sense of divine love and simultaneous consciousness of her own wretchedness. "No more world, no more sins," she said afterward, though what she meant was not renunciation but transformation. She saw her whole life up to that moment as contaminated by self-love, and the contamination suddenly burned away. She spent the next four months in a state she called "aniente" - annihilation - barely able to eat or speak, flooded with what she experienced as God's pure love.

This was not a vision of something external. Catherine was precise about this. She encountered no images, heard no voices, saw no Virgin or Christ. "My Being is God," she would later write, "not by simple participation but by a true transformation of my Being." This was dangerous language - the same claim that got Marguerite Porete burned at the stake - but Catherine was describing an experience, not making a theological argument. She felt herself dissolved into God, the boundaries between self and divine erased. "My 'me' is God," she insisted, "nor do I know my selfhood except in Him."

The transformation was permanent but not peaceful. For the rest of her life - twenty-seven more years - Catherine lived in what she described as the presence of an interior fire, burning away everything that was not God. She called it "purgatory on earth," and meant it literally. The suffering was continuous, physical as well as spiritual. She would go for weeks eating nothing but the Eucharist, her body revolting against ordinary food. She trembled, wept, fell into trances in the street. But she refused to call any of this pathological. This was purgation, the necessary burning away of the false self.

Her practice was radical simplicity. She refused a confessor - unusual for any Catholic, unprecedented for a woman. "My director is within me," she said. She attended daily Mass, received communion, spent hours in what she called "remaining in the simple Being of God," but she developed no formal method, wrote no guide to prayer. When pressed, she would say only that the soul must be reduced to nothing so that God can be all. This was not quietism - she spent her days nursing plague victims at Genoa's Hospital of Pammatone, eventually becoming its director - but neither was it conventional piety. She lived simultaneously in the world and consumed by interior fire.

Her teaching emerges from two texts, neither written by her own hand. *Purgation and Purgatory* and *The Spiritual Dialogue* were compiled after her death from her oral teachings by friends and disciples, including Giuliano, who converted late in life and became her companion in running the hospital. The texts are strange - a dialogue between Body, Soul, Self-Love, and Spirit; an extended meditation on purgatory as a state of burning love rather than punishment. Her purgatory is desire itself - the soul seeing God clearly but still contaminated by self-love, unable to bear the unbearable joy of union until the contamination burns away.

"I see the divine glory without barrier," she wrote of the souls in purgatory, "but that glory which they see and desire and experience without attainment is their torment." The pain is the gap between vision and union, and the burning is love itself, consuming everything that maintains the gap. This purgatory is not imposed by God but generated by the soul's own growing capacity to see what it is not yet pure enough to fully receive.

The church didn't know quite what to make of her. She was beatified in 1675, canonized in 1737 as a model for nurses and the sick, but her writings were never celebrated the way Teresa's were, never widely distributed. Too strange, too unmediated, too insistent on the dissolution of self. Friedrich von Hügel's massive 1908 study, *The Mystical Element of Religion as Studied in Saint Catherine of Genoa and Her Friends*, brought her back into view for modern readers, analyzing her in psychological, historical, and theological dimensions while insisting on the authenticity of her experience.

Contemporary scholars remain divided. Some see in her accounts evidence of temporal lobe epilepsy, anorexia mirabilis, depression transmuted into mystical interpretation. Others argue that reducing her testimony to pathology misses what she was actually reporting - a specific form of consciousness, reproducible in certain contemplative traditions, in which the normal boundaries of self genuinely dissolve. She belongs in any account of Christian mysticism not because she was comfortable or comprehensible, but because she pushed to an extreme that few others reached and lived to describe: the experience of self as obstacle, and its burning away as the path to God.
