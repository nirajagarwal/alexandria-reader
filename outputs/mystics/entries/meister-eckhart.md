# Meister Eckhart

Eckhart von Hochheim, born around 1260 in Thuringia, joined the Dominican order as a teenager and rose to become one of medieval Christianity's most brilliant and dangerous thinkers. A trained scholastic theologian who administered entire Dominican provinces, he was also a preacher whose German sermons pushed mystical theology to edges that terrified church authorities. By the time he died around 1328, papal investigators were building a case that would condemn him posthumously for heresy—yet his influence on Christian mysticism proved impossible to extinguish.

What makes Eckhart dangerous is his insistence on a radical equivalence between the soul's ground and God's ground. "The eye with which I see God is the same eye with which God sees me," he wrote. In the *Breakthrough* (*Durchbruch*), the soul doesn't just encounter God—it discovers that its deepest essence has always been identical with the divine essence. "God's ground is my ground and my ground is God's ground." This wasn't metaphor. Eckhart meant it ontologically, and that's what got him into trouble.

His sermons describe a divine ground (*Grunt*) beyond all names, images, and concepts—beyond even "God" as an object of thought or worship. "I pray God to rid me of God," he said, meaning that the soul must let go of every conception of the divine, every spiritual consolation, every image of who or what God is. What remains when everything falls away isn't nothing. It's the *Godhead*, the silent abyss from which the Trinity itself emerges, and the soul's own uncreated essence.

The path to this realization requires what Eckhart called *Gelassenheit*—letting-be, releasement, a radical detachment from all creatures, all desires, even all virtuous intentions. "A man should be so poor that he is not and has not a place for God to act in. To reserve a place would be to maintain distinctions." The soul must become empty, pure receptivity, so thoroughly detached that even the desire for detachment disappears. In this nakedness, the soul discovers it has never been separate from God.

This is not a comfortable teaching. Eckhart's detachment includes detachment from the Virgin Mary, from Christ's humanity, from the sacraments, from virtue itself if virtue becomes something the ego grasps. "If anyone went to heaven in rapture like St. Paul and learned that a sick man needed him to give him a bit of soup, I think it would be best to come back from the rapture and serve the needy in greater love." True detachment finds God equally in every moment, not more in prayer than in serving soup.

His German sermons, preached to Dominican nuns and lay audiences in Cologne and Strasbourg, used vernacular language and paradox to shock listeners out of conventional piety. "God is not found in the soul by adding anything but by a process of subtraction." The soul gives birth to God. God is always being born in the soul. The just person doesn't just do just deeds—"the just person *is* justice." These claims sounded to authorities like pantheism, like the soul replacing God, like heresy.

The Inquisition investigated him in Cologne in 1326. Eckhart defended himself with precision—he was, after all, a trained scholastic—but also with defiance. He preached what he'd experienced and thought, and he wouldn't unsay it. Two years after his death, Pope John XXII condemned twenty-eight propositions extracted from his works. The condemnation stuck. For centuries, Catholic authorities treated Eckhart as heterodox at best, heretical at worst.

But his sermons survived in manuscript, copied by nuns, circulated underground. They influenced Johannes Tauler and Henry Suso in the fourteenth century, the Rhineland mystics who tried to make Eckhart safer without losing his depth. They surfaced in unexpected places—in Protestant mysticism, in German idealism, in Heidegger's philosophy. Bernard McGinn's scholarly work in the late twentieth century rehabilitated Eckhart's orthodoxy, showing how his paradoxes worked within Neoplatonic and Thomistic frameworks, even as they pushed those frameworks to breaking points.

What Eckhart left behind is a mystical theology that refuses consolation. No visions, no sweetness, no spiritual feelings that the ego can grasp and call its own. Just the ground of being, discovered in radical poverty. Matthew Fox championed him in the 1980s as a creation mystic, though Fox himself was eventually silenced by Rome. Reiner Schürmann read him philosophically as a thinker of "releasement." Contemporary practitioners of centering prayer recognize in Eckhart's apophatic intensity a Christian parallel to Zen's emptiness.

The church never fully reclaimed him. There's no St. Eckhart, no formal rehabilitation of his condemned propositions. But anyone who attempts to speak about mystical experience without idolatry, who insists that the soul's ground and God's ground might be one ground, who prays to be rid of God to find the Godhead—they're walking paths Eckhart cleared, whether they know his name or not.
