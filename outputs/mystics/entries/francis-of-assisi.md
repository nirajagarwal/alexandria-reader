# Francis of Assisi

Born Giovanni di Pietro di Bernardone around 1181 in Assisi, son of a wealthy cloth merchant who had married up, Francis spent his youth as the town's golden boy - leading drinking songs, financing parties, dreaming of knighthood. At twenty he went to war, was captured, spent a year as a prisoner, came home sick. Something shifted. At twenty-five, praying in the ruined chapel of San Damiano, he heard Christ speak from the crucifix: "Francis, repair my house, which is falling into ruin." He took it literally, started rebuilding chapels stone by stone. His father, watching the family fortune fund religious mania, sued him. Francis stripped naked in the public square, returned every stitch, and walked into the woods wearing a borrowed cloak.

## The Mystical Reversal

What Francis encountered was not visions of heaven but the presence of God precisely in what his world considered worthless. Lepers terrified him - until one day he kissed one, tasted the sweetness where he expected revulsion, and understood that Christ was actually there in the rotting flesh. "What had seemed bitter to me was turned into sweetness of soul and body," he wrote later in his Testament. This was his fundamental mysticism: a perceptual revolution where the despised revealed the divine.

He called it "perfect joy." In a famous teaching to Brother Leo, he described returning to their friary frozen and muddy, being turned away by the porter, beaten, left in the snow. "If we bear all these things patiently," he insisted, this was perfect joy - not because suffering was good, but because in utter poverty one encountered what could not be taken away. He was after something underneath every human transaction, every possession, every dignity. Not poverty as ascetic discipline but poverty as stripping down to the only thing that was real.

His relationship with creation extended the same logic. He preached to birds not because he was a naive nature mystic but because birds, owning nothing, anxious for nothing, revealed how creatures actually were - held in being by love, nothing else. "Brother Sun, Sister Moon" wasn't poetic personification; it was literal kinship. The Canticle he composed near the end, nearly blind and in constant pain, praised God for "Sister Death" with the same tenderness as for sunlight and water. Everything participated in being-toward-God; hierarchy dissolved.

## The Practice

Francis left no mystical theology. His form was imitation - becoming literally like Christ meant living without money, without status, without defense. When the first brothers gathered, they had one rule: the Gospel. Specifically Matthew 10, where Jesus sends the disciples out without purse, without bag, without shoes. Francis took it as instruction manual. They wandered, preached, begged, worked with their hands when they could, owned nothing individually or collectively.

Prayer for him was absorption. He would spend nights alone on mountainsides, not contemplating but communing. Brothers reported finding him levitating, engulfed in light, weeping. At La Verna in 1224, two years before his death, praying about how to love Christ more completely, he received the stigmata - bleeding wounds matching the crucifixion, which remained until he died. He tried to hide them. They were not for show; they were the place where the barrier between self and Christ failed.

He insisted on joy - not manufactured cheer but the hilarity of radical freedom. They called themselves *Joculatores Domini*, God's jugglers. He sang constantly, even when dying. The joy came from having nothing left to lose and discovering that God was there, had been there all along, was inescapable.

## The Church's Problem

Within ten years, Francis had thousands of followers and the church had a crisis. A movement based on absolute poverty, no property, no hierarchy, no theology - just imitation of Christ - was institutionally impossible. Pope Innocent III approved the brotherhood in 1210, but uneasily. Francis kept refusing modifications to the rule, kept insisting on literal poverty. By 1220, effective leadership had passed to others. By the time he died in 1226, the order was already splitting between those who wanted his full radicalism and those who needed rules, buildings, universities.

He was canonized in 1228, two years after death - remarkably fast. The church needed to contain him. The Franciscan order became one of the great intellectual and institutional powers of medieval Christianity, producing Bonaventure, Duns Scotus, William of Ockham. The "Spiritual Franciscans" who insisted on absolute poverty were condemned as heretics in 1323. Francis became safe - a saint who loved animals, patron of ecology, statue in the garden.

## The Lineage

But the original vision survives in witnesses. The *Fioretti* (Little Flowers), compiled in the next century, preserves stories that capture his strangeness. Dorothy Day and the Catholic Worker movement drew directly on his model. Contemporary scholars like André Vauchez and Donald Spoto have worked to recover the radical beneath the saint. Pope Francis took the name deliberately - the first pope to do so - as a program.

What Francis demonstrated was that mystical experience need not be esoteric. The encounter with divine presence was available in the most abject poverty, the most despised bodies, the most ordinary created things. His method was reversal: what the world called low, God called intimate. The practice was giving away every defense until the thing that remained was naked dependence - and discovering that's where love was all along.

He died at forty-four, damaged by asceticism and illness, singing Psalm 142 as the brothers wept. His last instruction was to be laid naked on the ground. Even at the end, he wanted nothing between himself and the earth that was his sister.
