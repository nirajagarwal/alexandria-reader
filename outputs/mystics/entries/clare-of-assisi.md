# Clare of Assisi

Born Chiara Offreduccio in 1194 to minor Umbrian nobility, she became the woman who made Francis of Assisi's vision of radical poverty livable for religious women - and in doing so, fought both her order and the papacy for thirty years to preserve it. She died in 1253, having never left the enclosure of San Damiano, the small church Francis had rebuilt with his own hands, where she and her sisters lived in absolute poverty, owning nothing, not even property in common. This was unprecedented. This was what she would not surrender.

Clare met Francis when she was eighteen. He was already a local scandal - the merchant's son who'd stripped naked before the bishop, renounced his inheritance, and was living in caves with a handful of companions. She heard him preach at San Giorgio during Lent of 1212. On Palm Sunday night, she escaped her family's palazzo through a side door and ran to the Portiuncula, the chapel where Francis and his brothers lived. He cut her hair and gave her a rough tunic. Her family came with armed men to drag her back. She clung to the altar cloths and would not be moved.

Within weeks, her younger sister Agnes joined her. Then other women - nobility, townswomen, eventually her own mother. They needed a place. Francis installed them at San Damiano, and Clare would live there for forty-one years, most of them as abbess, never once leaving the small compound. But what she built there was not withdrawal from the world. It was a demonstration of what Francis's vision looked like when women held it as fiercely as men.

The vision was simple and impossible: absolute poverty. No property, no guaranteed income, no endowments. Total dependence on providence and the work of their own hands. The first Rule Francis wrote for them in 1212 was radical: "No sister is permitted to send letters or to receive or give away anything outside the monastery without the permission of the Abbess." Everything shared, nothing owned, not even shoes. They ate what was given. They wore patched tunics. They went barefoot even in winter. They worked with their hands - spinning, weaving, nursing the sick who came to San Damiano. And they prayed without ceasing.

Clare called this "the privilege of poverty." It was what she fought for. The church could not tolerate it. Convents needed endowments to survive - how else could cloistered women eat? Pope Gregory IX, who had known Francis and loved Clare, repeatedly tried to give San Damiano property and steady income. She repeatedly refused. He offered to release her from her vow of poverty. She wrote back: "Holy Father, I crave for absolution from my sins, but not from the obligation of following Christ."

What she experienced in this poverty was something Francis had glimpsed but she lived more completely. She wrote of Christ as a mirror, and of gazing into that mirror until you became what you saw. Her letters to Agnes of Prague, a princess who'd left a royal marriage to follow Clare's example, are instructions in mystical transformation: "Gaze upon that mirror each day, O queen and spouse of Jesus Christ, and continually study your face within it, that you may adorn yourself within and without with beautiful robes... In that mirror, blessed poverty, holy humility, and ineffable charity shine forth."

She saw visions - once, bedridden on Christmas Eve, she heard the Mass being celebrated in the Basilica of San Francesco across town and saw the liturgy as if she were there. This made her, centuries later, the patron saint of television, which she might have found hilarious. But her mysticism was not primarily visionary. It was participatory. She insisted her sisters were not just imitating Christ but literally following him into poverty, into vulnerability, into absolute dependence on the Father.

This was also political. Women in her time had two options: marriage or the convent. Both required property. Clare created a third way - one that required nothing but consent and stripped everything else away. No dowry, no family money, no church endowments. Just bodies, prayer, and work. The institutional church resisted because it was ungovernable. How could the Pope protect women who owned nothing, not even the walls they lived behind?

She fought them brilliantly. When Gregory IX visited San Damiano and found them desperately poor, he tried again to grant them property. Clare set the table with bread - "Holy Father, bless this bread." He said that was a priest's job. She said, "Command me to do it." So he told her, as a kind of joke, to make the sign of the cross over the bread. She did. Witnesses reported that a clear cross appeared on every loaf. Gregory stopped trying to give her property.

Two days before she died, Pope Innocent IV finally approved her Rule - the first monastic rule written by a woman, for women, accepted by Rome. It preserved the "privilege of poverty" she'd defended for forty years: "Let them neither receive nor hold onto any possessions or property acquired through an intermediary, or even anything that might reasonably be called property, except for as much land as necessity requires for the integrity and proper seclusion of the monastery, and this land is not to be cultivated except as a garden for the needs of the sisters."

She was canonized in 1255, two years after her death. The Process of Canonization includes testimony from her sisters about her austerities - she wore a hair shirt, slept on vine branches, fasted to the point of illness. But they also reported that she was joyful, that she laughed easily, that when sisters came to her troubled, she sent them away comforted. She washed the sisters' feet. She served them at meals. She nursed them through illnesses, including caring for those with suppurating wounds that no one else would touch.

What she left was a network of Poor Clare monasteries that still exists, still living under versions of her Rule, still practicing enclosure and communal poverty. Not as radical as Clare's original vision - most now accept property and endowments - but carrying something of her insistence that poverty is not deprivation but privilege. And carrying her central insight: that following Christ is not metaphor. It's participation. You become what you gaze at. Stare long enough into the mirror of divine poverty, and you disappear into what you see.
