# Cynthia Bourgeault

An Episcopal priest and hermit who emerged in the late twentieth century as a primary carrier of the Centering Prayer lineage, Cynthia Bourgeault (b. 1947) has spent four decades making contemplative practice accessible while refusing to soften its radical edges. Trained as a medievalist before ordination, she encountered the teachings of Thomas Keating and the Christian contemplative tradition in the 1980s and recognized something her academic background had not prepared her for: a living transmission of practices that actually produced the transformation the mystics described. She became one of the few figures who could translate ancient contemplative wisdom into contemporary practice while maintaining theological rigor.

Bourgeault's mysticism is distinctly apophatic - oriented toward the via negativa, the way of unknowing, the deliberate release of conceptual frameworks in order to encounter the divine beyond thought. Her central practice, Centering Prayer, is not her invention but her inheritance. Developed in the 1970s by Trappist monks Thomas Keating, Basil Pennington, and William Meninger at St. Joseph's Abbey in Spencer, Massachusetts, Centering Prayer adapts the teachings of *The Cloud of Unknowing* and John of the Cross into a teachable method: twenty minutes of sitting in silence, gently releasing thoughts as they arise, returning to a sacred word that signals consent to the divine presence and action within. Bourgeault received this practice directly from Keating and became one of its most articulate teachers.

But she pushes beyond basic instruction into more challenging territory. In *Centering Prayer and Inner Awakening* (2004), she describes the practice not as relaxation or stress relief but as a "small death" - the voluntary dismantling of the ego's control systems. She writes: "The method is simple, but the work is hard. You are asking the mind to do something it finds deeply counterintuitive: to let go of the one thing it's been trained all its life to do - which is to think." The practice creates what she calls "the sacred wound" - a deliberate opening to divine action that feels more like surgery than comfort.

Her understanding of consciousness itself became more radical through her encounters with the Gurdjieff Work in the 1990s, particularly through her apprenticeship with Rafe, a Fourth Way teacher she lived and worked with for eleven years in solitude in the Colorado mountains. This period, described in *Lessons in Letting Go* (2019), pushed her contemplative practice into territory most Christian mystics don't explicitly map: the development of "second attention," the capacity to observe one's own consciousness while remaining present in ordinary life. She found in Gurdjieff's teaching about attention and presence a practical method that complemented and deepened her Christian contemplation. This integration makes some traditionalists uncomfortable - Gurdjieff was hardly orthodox - but Bourgeault insists the practices work on the same ground: the transformation of consciousness from identification with ego to what she calls "witnessing presence."

Her theological vision, most fully articulated in *The Wisdom Jesus* (2008) and *The Holy Trinity and the Law of Three* (2013), reimagines Christianity through a non-dual lens. Drawing on second-century Syrian Christianity and the Desert Fathers, she argues that Jesus taught a path of direct transformation, a "metanoia" that is not moral conversion but radical perceptual shift. In her reading, Christ's teaching points toward what she calls "unitive consciousness" - not merging into undifferentiated oneness but learning to see from "the mind of Christ," which perceives reality as fundamentally relational and whole. This is not pantheism but panentheism: God in all things, all things in God, while distinctions remain.

Her book *The Meaning of Mary Magdalene* (2010) generated controversy by proposing that Mary Magdalene was Jesus's primary teaching partner in the transmission of contemplative practice, the one who recognized him first after resurrection because she had mastered the interior capacity to see beyond form. Bourgeault reads the Gnostic gospels not as heresy but as alternative witnesses to Christianity's contemplative core. Conservative scholars dismissed this as speculation; others found in it permission to take seriously the tradition's marginalized texts and voices.

She lives now primarily in solitude in Maine, leading retreats sparingly and writing with increasing directness about what contemplative practice actually costs. In *Eye of the Heart* (2020), she addresses what she sees as the premature leap in contemporary spirituality to "nondual" language without the actual transformation of consciousness that term implies. Real non-dual awareness, she insists, comes only through the hard path of kenosis - self-emptying - the willingness to die to the ego's agenda repeatedly until something else emerges. She has little patience for spiritual bypassing or feel-good mysticism.

The reception is divided. She is revered in contemplative circles as a teacher who makes difficult practices accessible and who carries a direct lineage from Keating. Critics note her unorthodox synthesis - Gurdjieff, Mary Magdalene, Teilhard de Chardin, the Wisdom tradition - and question whether she has wandered outside Christian orthodoxy. Her defense is pragmatic: "These practices work. They produce the fruit Jesus described - love, joy, peace, patience, the capacity to see and serve. That's the only verification that matters."

Her legacy is the transmission of practice more than doctrine. Thousands of students have learned Centering Prayer through her teaching and continue sitting daily in the discipline she describes. She has trained teachers, established contemplative communities, and created a body of writing that will endure as a map of one contemporary mystic's path. Whether Christianity will claim her or sideline her remains to be seen. She seems unbothered either way.
