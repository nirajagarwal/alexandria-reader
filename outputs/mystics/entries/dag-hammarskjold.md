# Dag Hammarskjöld

Dag Hammarskjöld served as Secretary-General of the United Nations from 1953 until his death in a plane crash in 1961. A Swedish diplomat and economist, he navigated the Cold War's most dangerous crises with what colleagues described as an almost preternatural calm. No one knew, until the manuscript was found in his apartment after he died, that he had been keeping a spiritual journal for decades—a record of mystical experience hidden inside the life of international diplomacy. *Markings* revealed that one of the twentieth century's most effective men of action had been, all along, pursuing union with God.

The journal entries, written in Swedish and spanning from 1925 to 1961, chart a movement from intellectual searching to something darker and stranger. The young Hammarskjöld wrestled with meaning, purpose, the problem of the self. The mature diplomat recorded experiences of surrender, self-emptying, death, and resurrection. "I don't know Who—or what—put the question, I don't know when it was put. I don't even remember answering. But at some moment I did answer Yes to Someone—or Something—and from that hour I was certain that existence is meaningful and that, therefore, my life, in self-surrender, had a goal." That "Yes," undated but transformative, runs through *Markings* like an underground river beneath the public record of ceasefires and resolutions.

He read the mystics: Meister Eckhart, John of the Cross, Thomas à Kempis. He read medieval Swedish mystics unknown to most modern readers. His journal shows him working with their language, testing their claims against his own experience. "To be free, to be able to stand up and leave everything behind—without looking back. To say Yes." The mystical path he describes is one of radical self-negation, not as self-hatred but as liberation from the ego's tyranny. "The more faithfully you listen to the voice within you, the better you will hear what is sounding outside." Union with God required, for him, the complete dissolution of the separate self.

What practices led him there? Solitude, silence, and an almost monastic discipline embedded in diplomatic chaos. He would rise early for prayer and reading before the UN day began. He hiked alone in the Swedish mountains when he could. He practiced what he called "living deliberately"—a constant attention to the present moment, an unceasing examination of motive. The practice was mystical but the context was political. He wrote about God while negotiating with Khrushchev and Nasser, while organizing peacekeeping forces, while trying to prevent nuclear war. The contemplative life and the active life were not separate.

Hammarskjöld offered no systematic teaching—*Markings* is not a manual. But the journal records a path that resembles John of the Cross's dark night more than any gentler spirituality. "Pray that your loneliness may spur you into finding something to live for, great enough to die for." "Do not seek death. Death will find you. But seek the road which makes death a fulfillment." This is the language of someone who has walked through psychological annihilation and found, on the far side, not destruction but presence. "God does not die on the day when we cease to believe in a personal deity, but we die on the day when our lives cease to be illumined by the steady radiance, renewed daily, of a wonder, the source of which is beyond all reason."

When *Markings* was published posthumously in 1964, it stunned readers who knew Hammarskjöld only as the UN's pragmatic chief administrator. W.H. Auden, who wrote the foreword to the English translation, called it "a sort of White Book concerning his negotiations with himself—and with God." The book became a surprise bestseller, translated into dozens of languages. Here was proof that mystical experience was not limited to monastics or medieval saints—it could occur in the life of a modern secular diplomat, and might even, somehow, sustain the kind of clear-headed action the world desperately needed.

Some found the book deeply Christian; others noted how rarely Hammarskjöld used explicitly Christian language. His mysticism drew on Christian sources but read ecumenically, almost universally. He quoted the Bhagavad Gita, Japanese haiku, ancient Chinese texts. He attended Christian services but seemed to experience God more in mountain silence than liturgy. This borderlessness troubled some Christian readers and inspired others seeking a mysticism that could function in pluralist modernity.

Scholars still debate what to make of him. Psychologists note the depressive tendencies, the lifelong celibacy, the hints of suicidal ideation transmuted into spiritual surrender. Theologians argue whether his mysticism was genuinely Christian or a modern amalgam. Political historians wonder if the mystical practice genuinely informed his diplomatic work or simply ran parallel to it. But *Markings* remains stubbornly irreducible to any single interpretation. It is the record of someone who encountered something beyond language—and kept trying to find language for it anyway.

His legacy is double: the actual work at the UN, where he invented modern peacekeeping and expanded the Secretary-General's role; and the hidden work recorded in *Markings*, which demonstrated that contemplative depth and effective action need not be enemies. Meditation centers and diplomatic training programs both claim him. The book keeps finding new readers—activists exhausted by their work, seekers wary of traditional religion, anyone trying to reconcile the inner life with the world's demands. "In our era, the road to holiness necessarily passes through the world of action."
