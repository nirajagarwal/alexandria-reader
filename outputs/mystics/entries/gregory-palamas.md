# Gregory Palamas

Gregory Palamas lived through one of Byzantine Christianity's most vicious theological wars. Born around 1296 to an aristocratic Constantinople family, trained in theology and rhetoric, he abandoned a promising career at age twenty to become a monk on Mount Athos. There he learned hesychasm—the practice of continuous Jesus Prayer, controlled breathing, and interior stillness—from master to student, as it had been transmitted for centuries. When a Calabrian monk named Barlaam attacked hesychasm as primitive navel-gazing unworthy of educated Christians, Palamas spent the rest of his life defending what he'd discovered in prayer, developing a theological framework to explain how human beings could genuinely encounter God without either claiming to comprehend the divine essence or settling for mere symbolism.

The hesychast method he practiced was specific and demanding. The Jesus Prayer—"Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner"—repeated thousands of times daily while coordinating breath and attention. Sitting in specific postures, chin tucked toward chest, gaze directed toward the heart or navel. Cultivating hesychia, profound stillness, until prayer became continuous, woven into breathing itself. Practitioners reported light, warmth, an encounter with presence that transformed consciousness. Barlaam thought this was at best psychological manipulation, at worst demonic delusion masquerading as grace.

Palamas insisted they were encountering God—not in his unknowable essence, but in his energies. This distinction became the axis of everything that followed. God's essence remains utterly transcendent, beyond all knowing and participation. But God's energies—his creative, sustaining, illuminating activity in the world—are genuinely divine, uncreated, and accessible. The light the apostles saw on Mount Tabor at Christ's Transfiguration, the light hesychasts sometimes perceived in deep prayer—this was not created, not symbolic, not subjective. It was God's own energy, the same light that will blaze forth at the end of time, visible now to those whose inner eyes had been opened through ascetic practice and grace.

The theological implications were explosive. Palamas was claiming direct, experiential knowledge of God while maintaining divine transcendence. Not union with God's essence—that remained impossible—but real participation in divine energies. Not absorption into the One, but communion with the Trinity. The body was not an obstacle but participated in theosis, deification. That's why the physical methods of hesychasm mattered: the incarnation had vindicated matter, sanctified flesh, made possible a spirituality that was psychosomatic from the ground up.

The controversy consumed Byzantine intellectual life through the 1340s. Councils convened, anathemas flew, emperors took sides, Palamas was briefly imprisoned. But hesychasm won. Three councils between 1341 and 1351 endorsed Palamas's theology. Barlaam retreated to Italy. Palamas became Archbishop of Thessalonica in 1347, though he spent much of his tenure either traveling, in captivity to Turkish raiders, or battling both heretics and plague. He died in 1359. The Orthodox Church canonized him nine years later and declared his theology normative—the Palamite synthesis became the lens through which Eastern Christianity would understand mystical experience and the nature of God.

His legacy is the distinctive shape of Orthodox spirituality. The essence-energies distinction allowed Orthodox theology to hold together divine transcendence and real theosis in a way Western Christianity, lacking this framework, found harder to articulate. Hesychasm remained central to monastic practice, preserved on Athos and carried to Russia, Romania, Greece, the Balkans. The Jesus Prayer became the Orthodox equivalent of centering prayer or contemplation, a practice accessible to laypeople, woven into daily life. *The Philokalia*, the 18th-century collection of hesychast texts that sparked Orthodox renewal, includes Palamas's writings as authoritative guides.

Western scholars long dismissed Palamas as a late Byzantine curiosity, intellectually inferior to the Scholastics. That assessment is being reconsidered. Contemporary theologians—Orthodox and Western—see in Palamas a way past sterile debates about whether mystical experience is merely psychological or truly divine. He offers a third path: real encounter with the uncreated, but always mediated through energies, never presuming to comprehend essence. John Meyendorff's mid-20th-century work rehabilitated Palamas for Western readers. Today he's studied as a major theologian, not just an apologist for monks.

The practice continues. In Orthodox monasteries from Athos to Alaska, the Jesus Prayer is still repeated thousands of times daily, the same psychosomatic method Palamas defended. The light he wrote about—uncreated, divine, transformative—is still reported by hesychasts who've descended into stillness deep enough to find it. Whether that light is objective divine energy or something else altogether remains as contested now as in the 14th century. But the lineage is unbroken, and Palamas gave it a theology that has proved durable enough to carry it forward for seven centuries.
