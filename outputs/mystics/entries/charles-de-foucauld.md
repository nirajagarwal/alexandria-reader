# Charles de Foucauld

Charles de Foucauld was a French aristocrat turned hermit who died alone in the Sahara in 1916, having attracted exactly zero disciples during his sixteen years there. Today, thousands of people across six continents live by the rule he wrote for a community that never materialized in his lifetime. He is one of Christianity's great failures who succeeded posthumously.

Born in 1858 to wealth and military tradition, de Foucauld spent his twenties as a cavalry officer distinguished mainly for gluttony, sexual excess, and contempt for religion. He kept a mistress he dressed in men's clothing so she could accompany him on campaign. He was so obese his fellow officers called him "Piggy." When his commanding officer objected to the mistress, de Foucauld resigned his commission rather than send her away.

Then Morocco pulled him back. In 1883-84, he explored the country disguised as a Jewish merchant, producing geographic work that won him a gold medal from the Geographical Society of Paris. The journey did something else: exposure to Muslim prayer—five times daily, no matter what—haunted him. Here were people who took God seriously in a way his Catholic childhood had not. "Islam produced a profound disturbance in my soul," he wrote later. "The sight of this faith, of these souls living in the continual presence of God, made me glimpse something greater and truer than worldly occupations."

Back in Paris in 1886, not yet believing but unable to stop thinking about God, he began visiting the church of Saint-Augustin. He asked the priest, Abbé Huvelin, for religious instruction. Huvelin told him to kneel down and go to confession instead. De Foucauld converted on the spot—or rather, surrendered. What followed was not gradual spiritual development but immediate extremism.

He wanted to be as poor as Jesus had been poor. He tried the Trappists, the strictest order he could find, but they weren't poor enough—they owned their monasteries. He left for Nazareth and became a handyman for the Poor Clares, sleeping in a shed, doing menial work, seeking what he called "the last place"—the lowest position possible, because that's where Jesus had lived in the hidden years. "How can a servant be greater than his master?" he wrote. "Jesus took the last place so decisively that no one has been able to take it from him."

In 1901, he was ordained a priest. He promptly left for the Sahara—not to convert Muslims, which he knew was impossible and illegal, but to be a presence. He established himself at Béni Abbès, then deeper in the desert at Tamanrasset among the Tuareg people. He built a small hermitage, kept the Eucharist, learned Tuareg languages, compiled a Tuareg-French dictionary, translated poetry, offered hospitality to travelers, and prayed.

His prayer was constant. He developed the practice of repeating "My Father, I abandon myself to you" until the words dissolved into a state of trust that held through physical hardship, isolation, and the growing certainty that his mission was failing by any conventional metric. He wrote to a friend: "I want to cry the Gospel with my whole life." Not preach it—cry it, embody it, be it in a place where words about Christianity were unwelcome.

He envisioned founding a new religious congregation, the Little Brothers of Jesus, who would live among the poorest and most isolated people on earth. He wrote a rule for them. No one came. He wrote letters trying to recruit followers. No one came. He lived alone, prayed alone, died alone—killed in 1916 by bandits during a Tuareg uprising, probably shot by accident when a gun went off during a raid on his hermitage.

The Catholic Church wasn't sure what to make of him. His cause for canonization stalled for decades. He'd founded no order, converted no Muslims, produced no theological works of consequence, built no institutions. His prayer practice was extreme but unsystematic. His asceticism looked like masochism to some observers. Only his letters and his rule survived, circulating quietly.

Then, slowly, his vision caught fire. In 1933, five Little Sisters of Jesus formed in Algeria, following the life he'd imagined. After World War II, the Little Brothers finally came into existence. Today, the spiritual families inspired by de Foucauld include nineteen different congregations and fraternities—thousands of people living in small communities among the poor, in slums, prisons, and villages, practicing the presence rather than announcing the message. They are in Japan, Brazil, Madagascar, the Palestinian territories, Appalachia. They live with refugees, migrant workers, prisoners, people on the streets.

Contemporary scholars see in de Foucauld a particularly modern mysticism: radical identification with the marginalized, enacted not in a monastery but in the most exposed places. Historian Jean-François Six and biographer Marion Mill Preminger documented how his spirituality was inseparable from his location—he had to be where European Christianity wasn't, where he had nothing to offer but presence.

His mysticism was the mysticism of Nazareth, the hidden years, God present in the powerless and forgotten. "Jesus came to Nazareth where he was thought to be the son of a carpenter," he wrote, "and he remained there for thirty years." The incarnation's majority was spent invisible. De Foucauld wanted that invisibility, that poverty of influence, that utter dependence on God alone.

He was canonized in 2022, finally—not for miracles or martyrdom in the traditional sense, but for the radical poverty of his witness and the delayed bloom of his vision. His prayer of abandonment is prayed by people who've never heard his name: "Father, I abandon myself into your hands; do with me what you will."
