# Thomas Keating

Thomas Keating died in 2018 at age ninety-five, having spent seven decades as a Trappist monk and forty years teaching a method of silent prayer that millions now practice. A Cistercian abbot turned contemplative teacher, he took practices from the Christian mystical tradition - particularly The Cloud of Unknowing and John of the Cross - and reformulated them for late twentieth-century Americans who knew more about Zen meditation than their own contemplative heritage. The method he developed with William Meninger and Basil Pennington became known as Centering Prayer, and it sparked both a contemplative revival and persistent controversy about whether it was truly Christian or merely baptized Eastern meditation.

Keating entered the Trappist Abbey of Spencer in Massachusetts in 1944, was ordained in 1949, and became abbot of St. Joseph's Abbey in 1961 at age thirty-eight. He was abbot during Vatican II's upheavals, when religious orders were called to return to their founding charisms while engaging the modern world. Keating took this seriously. He opened the monastery to ecumenical and interfaith dialogue, hosted Zen teachers, and began asking why Christianity seemed to have no equivalent to the meditation practices attracting young seekers to Buddhism and Hinduism.

The answer, he realized, was that Christianity did have such practices - they'd simply been buried. Medieval texts like The Cloud of Unknowing described contemplative prayer as moving beyond words and images into simple presence to God. John of the Cross wrote of the "dark night" as the soul's passage from discursive meditation to contemplative union. The tradition called it "infused contemplation," "prayer of quiet," "prayer of union" - but it had largely disappeared from ordinary Catholic life, relegated to enclosed monasteries or treated as something God bestowed on saints, not something laypeople could practice.

In 1975, Keating and his fellow monks William Meninger and Basil Pennington began teaching a simple method drawn from this tradition. Sit in silence for twenty minutes. Choose a sacred word - "God," "Jesus," "peace," "love" - not as a mantra but as a symbol of consent to God's presence. When thoughts arise, return gently to the word. Do this twice daily. They called it Centering Prayer, borrowing the term from Thomas Merton, who had used "centered" to describe contemplative awareness.

What Keating emphasized was consent, not concentration. "Centering Prayer is not a technique for focusing or controlling the mind," he wrote in *Open Mind, Open Heart*. "It is not an effort to achieve a spiritual state. It is an effort to consent to God's presence and action within." The sacred word wasn't meant to suppress thoughts but to express intention - a willingness to let go of engagement with thoughts, feelings, and perceptions, and rest in God's presence beyond all content.

This was different from both traditional Christian "meditation" (discursive reflection on scripture) and from mantric practices in Eastern traditions. It drew particularly on The Cloud of Unknowing's "naked intent directed to God" and John of the Cross's teaching on the "dark night" as God's purifying work in contemplative prayer. Keating systematized these teachings into stages: relationship with God, transformation of consciousness, release of the "false self," emergence of contemplative awareness as one's stable ground.

His teaching attracted massive interest. Contemplative Outreach, founded in 1984, spread the practice worldwide. Keating wrote more than twenty books - *Open Mind, Open Heart*, *Invitation to Love*, *The Human Condition* - that combined classical mystical theology with contemporary psychology. He drew on John of the Cross and Teresa of Ávila but also Carl Jung and developmental psychology. He used phrases like "divine therapy" and "the false self system" that made sense to Americans raised on psychotherapy.

The controversy was immediate and ongoing. Critics argued that Centering Prayer was simply Christian-flavored Eastern meditation, that its emphasis on emptying the mind and passivity opened practitioners to spiritual danger, that it neglected Christ and scripture, that it reduced contemplation to technique. These objections came from both conservative Catholics suspicious of post-Vatican II experimentation and from contemplatives who felt Keating had simplified and commodified a tradition that warned repeatedly against seeking experiences or treating contemplation as achievable through method.

Keating responded carefully. He insisted Centering Prayer was not a replacement for liturgy, scripture, or active faith - it was one practice within the full Christian life. It was preparation for contemplation, not contemplation itself. The method created conditions for God to work; it didn't manufacture mystical states. He acknowledged that contemplative prayer involves risk - the "night" is real, the psychological material that surfaces can be difficult - but argued the tradition had always said this. As for the Eastern parallels, he saw them as evidence of universal human capacity for contemplative awareness, not as syncretism.

More sympathetic critics wondered if Keating's systematization missed something essential. The Cloud author and John of the Cross wrote for people already formed in Christian practice and theology, already committed to the way of self-emptying love. They offered guidance for advanced stages of prayer, not entry-level meditation. Could their teachings be extracted into a method and taught to anyone willing to sit silently for twenty minutes twice a day? Was consent to God's presence actually accessible through simple practice, or did it require the kind of radical conversion and sustained formation that medieval contemplatives assumed?

Contemporary scholars of mysticism assess Keating's work differently than his critics or advocates. Bernard McGinn sees him as part of a modern democratization of mysticism, making contemplative practice available beyond monasteries while inevitably transforming it. Cynthia Bourgeault, Keating's student and colleague, argues he successfully retrieved practices that had become unnecessarily obscure. Others note that Centering Prayer functions for many people exactly as Keating intended - as preparation for contemplation, as training in letting go, as embodied practice of consent to God.

Keating's later work explored Christian mysticism's relationship with other traditions through the Snowmass Interreligious Conference, annual gatherings of contemplatives from different faiths he helped convene. He remained clear that he spoke from within Christianity - his mysticism was Christocentric, even when he found resonances in Buddhism or Sufism - but he believed contemplatives across traditions recognized something in each other that doctrine couldn't capture.

What Keating made possible was the survival of Christian contemplative practice as a living tradition in the modern West. Whether one accepts his particular formulation or not, millions of people now know that Christianity has a contemplative dimension, that silent prayer is not alien to the tradition, that resting in God's presence is a practice not just a grace. The Cloud of Unknowing is in print and being read. John of the Cross's "dark night" is understood as part of contemplative transformation, not just poetic metaphor. Religious orders revived contemplative practice. Centering Prayer groups meet in parishes worldwide.

The unresolved question is whether what they're practicing is what Keating thought he was transmitting. Contemplative prayer in the Christian mystical tradition was never merely a method - it was response to grace, part of holistic transformation, inseparable from life of prayer and charity and surrender. Keating knew this and said it repeatedly. But in an age of techniques and practices, where people cross-train in meditation forms and construct spiritual lives eclectically, can traditional mysticism survive transmission through modern methods? Keating's gift and his gamble was believing it could.
