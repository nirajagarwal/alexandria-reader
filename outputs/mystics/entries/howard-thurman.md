# Howard Thurman

Howard Thurman (1899-1981) was a Black American mystic, theologian, and civil rights leader who found God in silence and carried that encounter into the struggle for justice. Born in Daytona Beach, Florida, grandson of a woman enslaved until her twenties, he became one of the most influential spiritual teachers of the twentieth century - the theologian Martin Luther King Jr. carried Thurman's *Jesus and the Disinherited* with him throughout the Montgomery campaign. But Thurman was not primarily an activist. He was a contemplative who insisted that the inward journey and the outward struggle were inseparable.

## The Encounter in Silence

Thurman's mysticism began in childhood, in the woods and along the water of Florida's coast. He returned throughout his life to what he called "the sound of the genuine" - a phrase from his grandmother, who told him to listen for that sound in himself and others. In solitude, particularly in nature, Thurman experienced what he described as the dissolution of all barriers. "I was with everything that was," he wrote of moments when the boundaries between self and world, self and God, fell away.

His practice was contemplative prayer - not petitionary, not even explicitly Christian in form, but a discipline of deep listening and receptivity. He would sit in silence for an hour each morning, sometimes longer. "In the stillness," he wrote, "the pools of consciousness are refilled." This was not escape from the world's pain but preparation for engagement with it. The God he encountered in silence was the God of the oppressed, intimate and sustaining.

## The Way of Inwardness

Thurman taught that genuine spirituality required going inward, beneath the social roles and wounds of racism, beneath even religious concepts, to what he called "the literal substance of the human spirit." This wasn't individualism - it was the work of finding what is most deeply shared. In silence, he believed, one could touch the ground of being that connects all persons.

His method combined Christian mysticism with influences he gathered widely. In 1935-36, he traveled to India, Ceylon, and Burma as part of a "Negro delegation" - the first significant African American pilgrimage to India. He met Gandhi, who asked him to sing spirituals and spoke of the "cosmic Christ" that transcended institutional Christianity. Thurman brought back a deepened conviction that contemplative experience could bridge traditions and that mysticism had political implications. The inward journey revealed unity; racism and violence were failures to see what silence made obvious.

He grounded this in Jesus, but not the Jesus of empire. In *Jesus and the Disinherited* (1949), he argued that Jesus spoke to and from the condition of the oppressed. The counsel to love enemies, to resist without violence, to maintain one's humanity under dehumanizing conditions - this came from someone who knew what it meant to live under occupation. Thurman's mysticism never floated free of history or embodiment. The Christ he encountered in contemplation was one who knew fear, who faced hatred, who refused the temptations of fear, deception, and hatred in return.

## The Teaching

Thurman founded Fellowship Church in San Francisco in 1944 - an intentionally interracial, intercultural, mystically-grounded community at a time when most American churches were segregated by law or custom. Services included long periods of silence, music from multiple traditions, and Thurman's own sermons - poetic, searching, grounded in experience rather than doctrine.

He taught at Howard University for years, mentoring King and others, then became the first Black dean of chapel at a predominantly white university (Boston University, 1953-1965). His influence on the civil rights movement was profound but indirect. He offered not tactics but spiritual foundation - the inner resources to resist without hatred, to maintain dignity under assault, to see the humanity in the oppressor.

His books - *Meditations of the Heart*, *The Inward Journey*, *Disciplines of the Spirit* - are not systematic theology but wisdom literature, guiding readers toward their own encounters with the sacred. "Don't ask what the world needs," he said in a sermon that became famous. "Ask what makes you come alive, and go do it. Because what the world needs is people who have come alive."

## Reception and Legacy

Thurman was both central and marginal. Central to the Black church's intellectual tradition, mentor to civil rights leaders, founder of institutions. Marginal because his mysticism made some wary - was it too interior, too universal, not Christian enough? The Black church has often been suspicious of contemplative individualism, rightly wary of anything that might distract from collective struggle. Thurman insisted the two were inseparable: without the inward journey, activism became brittle, reactive, vulnerable to the very hatred it opposed.

White mystics discovered him late. Thomas Merton corresponded with him, recognizing a kindred spirit. Contemporary contemplative movements - centering prayer, inter-spiritual dialogue, Christian meditation - claim him as a forerunner. But his legacy is most alive in communities that hold together contemplation and justice: the Center for Action and Contemplation (Richard Rohr), spiritual direction programs that center marginalized voices, churches trying to integrate silence into activism.

Scholars argue about his orthodoxy. Was his "religion of Jesus" too detached from the "religion about Jesus"? Did his universalism dilute the particular claims of Christianity? Luther Smith Jr.'s biography places him firmly in the Black church tradition while acknowledging his experimental edges. Alton Pollard III sees him as retrieving an older African American spiritual practice - the hush harbors where enslaved people encountered God in secret silence.

Thurman himself seems to have cared little for these debates. He reported what he found: that in the depths of silence, the false divisions fall away. That the sound of the genuine is the voice of God. That contemplation isn't escape but preparation. That the mystic's insight - all is one, all is connected - has revolutionary implications when taken seriously in a world structured by division.

He died in 1981, still teaching, still sitting in silence each morning. The Thurman Center at Boston University preserves his papers and continues his work. His books remain in print, quietly influential. And somewhere, people who've never heard his name sit in silence and discover what he knew: that in the depths, the barriers come down, and what remains is love - fierce, particular, and absolutely universal.
