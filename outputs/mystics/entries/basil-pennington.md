# Basil Pennington

Dom M. Basil Pennington (1931-2005) was a Trappist monk who helped translate an ancient Christian contemplative practice into a method that ordinary Americans could use. A scholar of monastic history who discovered that medieval monks had been doing something very simple - repeating a sacred word in silence - he and two fellow Cistercians packaged this as "Centering Prayer" in the 1970s. The result was arguably the most successful reintroduction of contemplative practice into mainstream Christian life since the Reformation.

Pennington entered the Abbey of St. Joseph in Spencer, Massachusetts in 1951, during the wave of monastic vocations inspired by Thomas Merton's *The Seven Storey Mountain*. He became a canonical lawyer, serving as secretary-general of the Synod of Cistercian Abbots, traveling to monasteries worldwide. This put him in contact with Zen masters, Hindu swamis, and Tibetan lamas during the interfaith dialogue boom of the 1960s. He watched non-Christians sit in silent meditation and wondered: where was the Christian equivalent?

The answer, he and fellow monks William Meninger and Thomas Keating discovered, was in *The Cloud of Unknowing*, that anonymous 14th-century English text that had told contemplatives to "take just a little word, of one syllable... and fasten this word to your heart, so that it never leaves you." The medieval author had described a practice startlingly similar to Eastern mantra meditation: choose a sacred word, return to it gently when thoughts arise, rest in God's presence beyond concepts and images.

In 1975, they began teaching what they called Centering Prayer at Spencer Abbey's guest house. Pennington later wrote the practice's defining manual, *Centered Living* (1986), followed by *Centering Prayer* (1980) and numerous other books. The method was deliberately simple: sit comfortably, choose a sacred word (God, Jesus, peace, silence), settle into God's presence, return gently to the word when thoughts intrude, sit for twenty minutes. Do this twice daily. That's it.

What Pennington and his collaborators had done was strip contemplative prayer down to its mechanics and make it teachable. No need to become a monk, master Latin, or spend years in spiritual direction. Here was a practice - a technology, almost - that a Presbyterian accountant or a Catholic schoolteacher could learn in twenty minutes and maintain for a lifetime.

The method spread rapidly through parishes, retreat centers, and ecumenical contemplative networks. Contemplative Outreach, the organization Keating founded to teach it, now operates in multiple countries. Critics, including some in Orthodox and traditional Catholic circles, have argued that what was lost in this translation was crucial: the theological context, the ascetic discipline, the sacramental and liturgical framework that made medieval contemplation Christian rather than simply generic meditation. Pennington was aware of this criticism. He insisted that Centering Prayer was meant to supplement, not replace, traditional liturgical prayer, Scripture reading, and sacramental life.

His own practice was embedded in Trappist monasticism - the Liturgy of the Hours prayed seven times daily, the Eucharist, the rhythm of lectio divina, work, and silence. He could afford the simplification because his life provided the context. The question was whether laypeople, practicing Centering Prayer in living rooms and lunch breaks, could maintain the Christian character of the experience without that surrounding structure.

Pennington himself showed little anxiety about this. He wrote prolifically about Trappist life, Thomas Merton, Mary, the Eucharist, and lectio divina. He dialogued openly with Buddhists - he'd practiced Zen meditation, met with the Dalai Lama, studied at ashrams. His approach was confidence rather than defensiveness: Christian contemplation could hold its own in the marketplace of meditation techniques because it led to the same depths the other traditions reached, and beyond.

He died suddenly in 2005, killed in a car accident while returning from giving a retreat. He was 74, still traveling, still teaching, still writing. By then Centering Prayer had been practiced by hundreds of thousands of Christians who had never heard of *The Cloud of Unknowing* but were sitting in silence twice a day, returning to their sacred word, doing what Christian contemplatives had been doing for centuries without knowing the lineage.

The scholarly assessment of Pennington's work is mixed. Some historians of Christian spirituality see him as a necessary popularizer who reintroduced the practice of contemplative prayer to a tradition that had nearly lost it. Others see a domesticated Buddhism in Christian vocabulary, stripped of the theological depth that made apophatic prayer truly Christian. What's undeniable is that he made something happen. Churches that had reduced prayer to petition and praise recovered a strand of their own tradition. People who might have become Buddhists instead became contemplative Christians.

The test of Centering Prayer will be whether it produces mystics - people who don't just practice a technique but undergo transformation, who move past method into actual encounter. The jury is still out. But Pennington bet that the method could lead to the mystery, that packaging the practice wouldn't kill what made it sacred. His legacy is a generation of Christians sitting in silence, repeating a word, waiting for God.
