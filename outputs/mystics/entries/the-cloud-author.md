# The Cloud Author

Sometime in the late 14th century, an English contemplative — probably a priest, probably in the Midlands, almost certainly alone — wrote a short treatise addressed to a single young person of twenty-four pursuing the contemplative life. *The Cloud of Unknowing* names neither author nor recipient. It survives in seventeen manuscript copies and has never been out of circulation since. The anonymity feels deliberate, even essential. The work describes a practice that strips away everything that can be known or imagined about God, leaving the practitioner in darkness reaching toward what cannot be grasped. Why would such a teacher need a name?

## The Darkness Beyond Knowledge

The Cloud teaches apophatic prayer — the way of unknowing. Where most Christian instruction approaches God through what can be affirmed (God is good, God is love, God is just), the Cloud insists on negation. All concepts of God, all images, all thoughts, all feelings — these must be pressed down beneath "a cloud of forgetting" while the practitioner reaches up toward God through "a cloud of unknowing." The practice is not meditation on Scripture or visualization of Christ's passion. It is simpler and stranger: a wordless reaching, a "naked intent toward God," sustained against the mind's constant effort to fill the darkness with something knowable.

The author quotes liberally from Pseudo-Dionysius, the 6th-century Syrian mystic whose *Mystical Theology* established the via negativa in Christian thought. But where Dionysius wrote in ornate Greek for a philosophical audience, the Cloud author writes in Middle English for a single, specific person attempting this work. The tone is intimate, practical, occasionally frustrated with how difficult it is to explain: "By love may he be gotten and holden, but by thought never."

## The Work of Unknowing

The practice is deceptively simple. Sit alone. Choose a single syllable — "God" or "love" — not as an object of meditation but as a tool to beat down thoughts as they arise. "And if any thought rise and will press continually above thee betwixt thee and that darkness, and ask thee saying, 'What seekest thou, and what wouldest thou have?' say thou that it is God that thou wouldest have." Everything else, all knowledge, all comfort, all feeling — even thoughts of holiness or Mary or the saints — is to be pushed down under that cloud of forgetting. What remains is unknowing, darkness, absence. And in that darkness, God may act.

The author is clear this is not for everyone. The work requires maturity, guidance, persistence. It produces no ecstasies, no visions, no certainties. The practitioner feels nothing, knows nothing, achieves nothing. This is contemplation in its most stripped-down form — not even imageless meditation, because images imply content. Just blind reaching.

## The Other Works

The same hand wrote at least five other treatises: *The Book of Privy Counseling*, *Dionise Hid Divinite* (a translation of Dionysius), *The Epistle of Prayer*, *The Epistle of Discretion in the Stirrings of the Soul*, and *A Treatise of Discernment of Spirits*. These expand and refine the teaching. *Privy Counseling* pushes even further into negation: "Nowhere bodily is everywhere ghostly." The discernment texts warn against spiritual delusion, the dangers of seeking experiences rather than God, the necessity of humility. The author had clearly seen spiritual ambition wreck people.

The texts suggest someone embedded in a tradition — probably a diocesan priest or a Carthusian, given the solitary emphasis — with access to a good library and years of practice. References to "exercises" and careful sequencing of instruction imply a tested method, not first-generation innovation. The writing is too sure-footed for a beginner, too practical for a pure theorist.

## Reception and Revival

The Cloud circulated quietly through the late medieval period, copied by hand in monastic scriptoria. The Protestant Reformation suppressed contemplative practice in England, but recusant Catholics preserved the text. Augustine Baker, a 17th-century Benedictine, used it to train nuns at Cambrai. But it wasn't until Evelyn Underhill's 1912 modernization that the Cloud found a wider audience. Underwell's edition shaped 20th-century Christian mysticism — Thomas Merton owned a copy, read it repeatedly, called it "central." The Trappist centering prayer movement, developed by Thomas Keating, William Meninger, and Basil Pennington in the 1970s, drew directly from the Cloud's method: a single word, repeated to beat down thoughts, opening to God in unknowing.

Contemporary scholars debate the author's identity (proposals include the Carthusian William Exmewe) and wonder about the recipient (a young hermit? a religious in formation?). Bernard McGinn places the Cloud in the broader European tradition of bridal mysticism and apophatic theology. Denys Turner analyzes its sophisticated epistemology. But the text resists academic capture as firmly as it resists naming God.

## What Remains

The Cloud's legacy is a practice that has survived six centuries because it describes something repeatable. Not a vision that must be believed, not a revelation that must be interpreted, but a method: sit, reach, unknow. The wordless prayer it teaches shows up in Quaker silence, in hesychast stillness, in centering prayer groups meeting in suburban church basements. The author would likely be baffled by the attention. The work matters; the name does not. The cloud matters; what you think you know does not. God may be reached in love, not in thought. And that reaching happens in the dark.
