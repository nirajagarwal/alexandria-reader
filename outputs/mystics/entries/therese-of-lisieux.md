# Thérèse of Lisieux

She died of tuberculosis at twenty-four in 1897, having spent nine years in a Carmelite convent in provincial France, never traveling more than a few miles from where she was born. Within twenty-eight years the Catholic Church declared her a saint and eventually a Doctor of the Church - one of only four women so honored. What Thérèse of Lisieux discovered in her brief, confined life was what she called the "little way," a path to sanctity available to anyone, requiring no visions, no mortifications, no extraordinary graces - only what she termed "confidence and love."

Thérèse Martin entered the Carmel at Lisieux at fifteen, younger than the order typically allowed, after personally petitioning Pope Leo XIII during a pilgrimage to Rome. She wanted the cloister because she wanted God, and she understood early that her path would not be marked by the dramatic mysticism of Teresa of Ávila or Catherine of Siena. She was, by her own assessment, too small, too weak, too ordinary. "I am a very little soul who can offer only very little things to our Lord," she wrote. This became her method.

The little way, as she articulated it in her autobiography *Story of a Soul*, was radical in its simplicity: do small things with great love. Pick up pins, smile at difficult sisters, accept humiliations without protest, turn every tiny action into an offering. She wrote: "My way is all confidence and love. I don't understand souls who fear a Friend so tender." This wasn't sentimentality - it was strategic theology. If sanctity required heroic virtue beyond ordinary capacity, then sanctity was for the few. Thérèse insisted it was for everyone, achievable through attention to the microscopic texture of daily life.

Her spiritual experience centered not on visions or consolations but on what she called "the science of love." She described herself as a "little flower" at God's feet, willing to be trampled or cherished, indifferent to the form love took. But this wasn't passive resignation. Her practice was active, disciplined attention: catching every thought of complaint, choosing kindness in every interaction, accepting praise and blame with equal serenity. She served as novice mistress while still young, and her guidance to other sisters was concrete: love shows itself in deeds, not feelings. "Jesus does not demand great actions from us but simply surrender and gratitude."

The last eighteen months of her life were marked by devastating spiritual darkness. The cheerful confidence of her early years vanished into what she called "the thickest darkness." She wrote: "When I sing of the happiness of heaven and of the eternal possession of God, I feel no joy in this, for I sing simply what I want to believe." She was assailed by doubts about immortality, about heaven, about whether anything existed beyond the grave. She compared her state to sitting at a table with atheists. This is rarely emphasized in popular accounts, but it dominated her final months - she died without the return of consolation, choosing faith as pure decision rather than experience.

Her death in 1897 might have ended the story there: another young religious dead of tuberculosis, mourned by her small community. But *Story of a Soul*, assembled from autobiographical fragments written under obedience to her prioress (who was also her sister), was published posthumously and became an immediate phenomenon. Translated into dozens of languages, it reached millions. Soldiers carried it in the trenches of World War I. Dorothy Day credited it with her conversion. Countless Catholics discovered in Thérèse's ordinariness a mysticism they could practice.

The scholarly reception has been more complex. Feminist theologians note the infantilizing language ("little" everything), the troubling father-fixation, the seemingly masochistic embrace of suffering. Historians point out that *Story of a Soul* was heavily edited by her sisters, particularly Pauline, who sentimentalized and doctrinally sanitized the original manuscripts. When the unedited texts were finally published in the 1950s, a more psychologically complex, theologically sophisticated Thérèse emerged - darker, funnier, more intellectually formidable than the sugary saint of popular devotion.

But the little way survives its packaging. Thomas Merton recognized in Thérèse a genuinely subversive figure, someone who dismantled the heroic mysticism that made sanctity the province of the extraordinary. Her insistence that holiness was possible in washing dishes, in silence, in hidden kindness, democratized the spiritual life. This was mysticism for the many, not the few. As she wrote: "Love proves itself by deeds, so how am I to show my love? I cannot do great things. The only way I can prove my love is by scattering flowers, and these flowers are every little sacrifice, every glance and word, and the doing of the least actions for love."

Her Doctors of the Church title, bestowed in 1997, recognized her as a teacher of the faith despite her youth, her lack of formal education, her brief life. What she taught was this: the mystical life is not elsewhere, in visions or raptures or extraordinary states. It is here, in the thousand unremarkable choices that constitute a day. God is encountered not by ascending to heights but by descending into smallness. "I prefer the monotony of obscure sacrifice to all ecstasies," she wrote. She meant it. She lived it. She died in darkness, still choosing it.
