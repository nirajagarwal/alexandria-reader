# Simone Weil

Simone Weil (1909-1943) was a French philosopher, political activist, and mystic who spent her entire adult life at the threshold of the church she could never quite enter. Born into a secular Jewish family in Paris, brilliant to the point of being unbearable, she taught philosophy, worked in factories, fought briefly in the Spanish Civil War, and wrote some of the most searing religious prose of the twentieth century—all while insisting she could not be baptized. She died of tuberculosis and self-starvation in England at thirty-four, leaving notebooks that read like transmissions from someone caught between worlds.

Her mystical experiences began without warning in 1937. While visiting a Portuguese fishing village, she watched a procession of fishermen's wives carrying candles and singing "ancient hymns of heart-rending sadness." Something broke open: "the conviction was suddenly borne in upon me that Christianity is pre-eminently the religion of slaves, that slaves cannot help belonging to it, and I among others." The next year, at the Benedictine abbey of Solesmes during Holy Week, suffering from splitting migraines, she discovered that intense attention to Gregorian chant could lift pain into something else. She wrote: "I was able by this exercise to rise above this wretched flesh, to leave it to suffer by itself, heaped up in a corner, and to find a pure and perfect joy in the unimaginable beauty of the chanting."

Then came the direct encounters. While reciting George Herbert's poem "Love" in English—"Love bade me welcome: yet my soul drew back"—she reported that Christ himself descended and possessed her. Not a vision, she insisted, but a presence: "In my arguments about the insolubility of the problem of God I had never foreseen the possibility of that, of a real contact, person to person, here below, between a human being and God." She experienced this presence repeatedly during prayer, a contact she described as more certain than any sensory experience. "God in his mercy had prevented me from reading the mystics," she wrote, "so that it should be evident to me that I had not invented this absolutely unexpected contact."

What practices led her there? Attention—a word she used with almost technical precision. Not concentration, not effort, but a quality of waiting presence she considered the substance of prayer. She spent hours in chapel without being Christian, learning to empty herself of what she called "the I." Manual labor became spiritual practice: her time working assembly lines wasn't radical chic but an attempt to understand affliction in the body. She prayed the Lord's Prayer in Greek every morning, word by word, as a practice of attention. "Absolutely unmixed attention is prayer," she wrote. Everything else she stripped away—food, warmth, human connection—as if trying to become pure capacity for reception.

Her teaching, scattered through notebooks and letters, centers on *decreation*—undoing the self to make space for God. Not the annihilation of self-hatred but something stranger: consciously consenting to the necessity that makes one just another object in God's creation, no more exempt from gravity than a falling stone. She called this "gravity and grace." We are subject to mechanical necessity, fallen creation, the relentless pull downward—but grace descends with more force than gravity pulls. The spiritual task is abandoning the delusional claim to be the center of the world. "We have to become nothing, we have to go down to the vegetative level in order to force grace to descend to that depth to look for us."

Affliction—*malheur*—was her term for the suffering that destroys the personality itself, physical pain plus social degradation plus the conviction that one is worthless. She insisted on clear eyes about human misery, refused consoling lies about suffering's nobility. Christ's cry of abandonment on the cross—*My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?*—was for Weil the truest theology. God must absent himself for creation to exist at all; love requires distance. The Incarnation was God's way of experiencing affliction from inside.

The church didn't know what to do with her. She attended Mass constantly but wouldn't be baptized. Her reasons shifted—solidarity with those outside the church, the church's historical crimes, her sense that she was called to remain at the threshold. Father Joseph-Marie Perrin, who became her closest spiritual friend, pleaded with her to enter. She refused: "I feel that it is necessary and ordained that I should be alone, a stranger and an exile in relation to every human circle without exception." Some scholars read this as psychological; others see theological radicalism. She may have been both unbaptizable and genuinely mystic.

Her legacy is impossible to contain. Simone de Beauvoir and Susan Sontag found her repellent—the self-starvation, the masochism, the apparent death-wish. Catholic intellectuals like T.S. Eliot and Flannery O'Connor were transfixed; Eliot compared her to the saints. Contemporary scholars argue over whether she was anorexic, whether her Judaism mattered more than she admitted, whether her politics and mysticism can be separated. Her notebooks—*Gravity and Grace*, *Waiting for God*, *The Need for Roots*—remain in print, still strange, still unassimilable. She founded no school, established no practice, converted to nothing. She simply reported what she found at the threshold and insisted on staying there, hungry, brilliant, impossible, utterly serious about contact with the God she could never quite let herself officially approach.
