# Evelyn Underhill

Evelyn Underhill (1875-1941) was a middle-class Englishwoman who became the first lay person to lecture on theology at Oxford, the first woman to give retreats to Anglican clergy, and the author of *Mysticism* (1911) - a 500-page survey that mapped mystical experience across traditions with unprecedented scope. She was not a mystic in the sense of Teresa of Ávila or John of the Cross. She was something stranger: a scholar who studied mystical theology until it seized her, then spent thirty years trying to live what she had written about.

She came to mysticism through literature, not prayer. Born into comfortable London society, educated but not at university (women couldn't take degrees), she read philosophy and poetry obsessively. Plotinus, Dante, the medieval mystics - she consumed them as aesthetic experiences. Marriage in 1907 to Hubert Stuart Moore, a barrister and yachtsman, gave her financial security and a partner utterly uninterested in her spiritual preoccupations. She worked in the British Museum. She began writing about mysticism as an intellectual pursuit.

Then the object of study became the subject doing the studying. Around 1907-1908, Underhill experienced what she later called "an overpowering vision of the Absolute." The details were private - she never described it fully - but it transformed everything. The mysticism she had analyzed now claimed her. For the next decade she struggled with what to do about it, swinging between Anglo-Catholicism and an almost-conversion to Rome (blocked by her husband's anti-Catholic politics), studying under Baron Friedrich von Hügel, who became her spiritual director and anchored her to institutional Christianity. "You need not be a mystic to be a saint," von Hügel told her, redirecting her focus from extraordinary states toward ordinary obedience.

*Mysticism* had already made her reputation before her own experiences deepened. The book is encyclopedic and systematic, dividing the mystical path into five stages: Awakening, Purgation, Illumination, the Dark Night, and Union. She drew on hundreds of sources - Christian, Sufi, Hindu - and argued that mystical experience, while culturally conditioned, pointed to something real. Bernard McGinn later noted that Underhill "did more than any other English writer to make mysticism accessible to the modern world," though academic scholars would find her categories too neat, her psychology dated, her comparative method too willing to collapse differences.

What makes Underhill's work endure is not her system but her descriptive precision. She knew how to write about inner states, how to make the abstract concrete:

> "The mystic is the person who attains to union with Reality. The artist is the person who makes some sort of contact with it. The artist and the mystic are brothers, making the same demand on life."

She understood mysticism as participatory knowledge, not speculative theory. Union was not merger or erasure but "a free and loving act of the soul." The via negativa was not rejection of created things but recognition of their transparency to the divine. Her chapter on "Voices and Visions" methodically distinguished authentic spiritual experience from pathological hallucination - a necessary distinction in an era when psychology was reducing mysticism to neurosis.

After von Hügel's death in 1925, Underhill became a retreat director and spiritual guide herself. She gave retreats to Anglican clergy across England - remarkable for a laywoman, impossible a generation earlier. She taught centering prayer before Keating formalized it, contemplative practices before they became trendy. Her retreat addresses, collected in books like *The Spiritual Life* (1937), are practical and grounded:

> "We mostly spend our lives conjugating three verbs: to Want, to Have, and to Do. Craving, clutching, and fussing... But we have nothing to do with the great triad of Heaven: to Be, to Know, and to Love."

She corresponded with hundreds of spiritual seekers, most of them ordinary believers struggling to pray. Her letters are patient, humorous, anti-romantic. To someone complaining about distractions in prayer: "God is much more interested in the intention than the attention." To someone seeking mystical experiences: "The real test of a genuine spiritual life is not ecstasy but character."

Underhill's pacifism, radicalized during World War I, put her at odds with Anglican establishment during the war years. She resigned positions, withdrew from public ministry, wrestled with what it meant to love God in a world bent on slaughter. Her book *The School of Charity* (1934) insisted that contemplation must lead to compassion, mysticism to social action - a connection that would become central to figures like Dorothy Day and Thomas Merton.

She died in 1941, during the Blitz, from cancer complicated by asthma. Her funeral was at St. Martin-in-the-Fields, the church where she had worshiped for years. No mystical visions, no ecstasies reported, no stigmata. Just decades of prayer, writing, guidance, and a persistent attempt to live what she had discovered: that the ordinary is charged with divine presence, that contemplation is not escape but engagement, that "the real mystic is not a spiritual aristocrat but a pioneer and a pilgrim."

Grace Adolphsen Brame's 2014 biography recovered Underhill from hagiography and noted the persistent tension between her marriage (Hubert never shared her faith), her vocation (never ordained, always lay), and her temperament (introverted, melancholic, driven). Dana Greene's earlier biographical work emphasized how Underhill translated mystical theology for modernity without reducing it to psychology or sentimentality.

She is read now less for her systematic theology - that dates - than for her insistence that mystical experience is not reserved for saints or solitaries. It's available to anyone willing to pay attention, to practice, to persist. "We are all called," she wrote, "to be mystics in a measure." The measure varies. The call remains.
