# Walter Hilton

An Augustinian canon in England who died around 1396, Walter Hilton wrote what may be the most systematic guide to contemplative prayer in the medieval English mystical tradition. Where Julian of Norwich received visions and the Cloud author wrote tersely about unknowing, Hilton produced *The Scale of Perfection* - a detailed roadmap for the journey from self-knowledge to union with God. He was writing for enclosed religious, particularly women, who had chosen the contemplative life but needed practical instruction on what actually happens in sustained prayer and how to navigate it.

Hilton's mysticism is psychological and progressive. *The Scale* describes a path in two books: the first devoted to the purgative life, stripping away sin and attachment; the second to the illuminative and unitive stages, where the soul is gradually reformed in God's image. This is not dramatic visionary mysticism but patient interior work. Hilton describes the "dark night" before the Cloud author's text was likely known, speaks of a "restful travail" in prayer, and insists that genuine contemplation comes only after long discipline. He is suspicious of visions, locutions, and bodily feelings - they might be real, but they're not the goal. The goal is a transformation so thorough that the soul begins to love what God loves, see what God sees, want only what God wants.

He wrote with the precision of someone who had watched souls at work. On the obstacle of ordinary thoughts in prayer: "Thy heart is not yet purified, and therefore thou canst not feel the warm love of Jesus Christ." On the desire for spiritual consolations: they will come and go, don't chase them. On the role of darkness: "This darkness is naught else but a lacking of knowing and a lack of ghostly feeling of God." What looks like absence is preparation - the soul learning to operate without the supports of sense experience or conceptual knowledge.

Hilton's method is grounded in Augustine's understanding of the *imago Dei* - the soul made in God's image but deformed by sin. Contemplation is reformation, the restoration of that image through grace. The path requires self-knowledge ("know thyself first"), then knowledge of God through Christ. He is relentlessly Christocentric: Jesus is not left behind as the soul advances but becomes more central. The highest contemplation is not abstract union but intimate participation in Christ's own knowledge of the Father.

He also wrote *The Mixed Life*, a letter to a lay nobleman advising how to balance active duties with contemplative practice - rare recognition that most people couldn't enter religious life but still needed guidance. His other works include treatises on the psalms and several Latin writings, but *The Scale* is his monument. It circulated widely in manuscript, was printed in 1494, influenced English Catholic spirituality through the Reformation, and was rediscovered by 20th-century scholars looking for an indigenous English contemplative tradition.

Hilton is less dramatic than Julian, less paradoxical than the Cloud author, more systematic than either. He offers something like a field manual for the interior life - detailed, practical, psychologically acute. Bernard McGinn calls him "the most comprehensive English mystic" of his period. Where Julian emphasized divine love's constancy and the Cloud author insisted on radical unknowing, Hilton mapped the territory between them: how the soul actually moves from ordinary prayer to something more naked, sustained, transformative. He assumed this was possible for anyone willing to do the work, though few would reach the heights. The contemplative life was not about extraordinary experiences but ordinary people slowly becoming transparent to grace.

The English mystical tradition produced four major texts in fifty years - *The Cloud*, Julian's *Revelations*, Richard Rolle's writings, and Hilton's *Scale*. Hilton's stands out for its sobriety and system. He died when the tradition was at its peak, just before the chaos of the 15th century and the rupture of the Reformation would scatter it. *The Scale* survived in manuscript through centuries of suppression, was eventually translated and printed, and now sits on the shelves of monasteries and retreat houses as a guide to prayer that still works. You can follow his instructions and something happens.
