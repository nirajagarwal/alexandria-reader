# Angela of Foligno

In 1285, a well-to-do married woman in the Umbrian town of Foligno began praying for the death of her family. She was around 37 years old, recently converted from what she later called a life of vanity and sin, and she wanted to pursue absolute poverty in imitation of Francis of Assisi. Her mother, husband, and children stood in the way. Within a year, all of them were dead. Angela took this as divine permission and gave away everything she owned.

What followed was a descent—or ascent, depending on perspective—into one of the most extreme mystical experiences recorded in Christian history. For the next two decades until her death in 1309, Angela lived in voluntary poverty as a Franciscan tertiary and experienced visions of such intensity that she would scream in public, collapse in churches, and require others to physically restrain her. Her spiritual autobiography, dictated to her confessor Brother Arnaldo and known as the *Memorial*, remains one of the most psychologically complex and theologically dangerous texts of medieval mysticism.

Angela reported encountering Christ with overwhelming sensory immediacy. She saw him crucified, tasted his blood, thrust her hands into the wound in his side. "I felt such fire within me," she told Arnaldo, "that I stood and leaned against the wall so as not to fall, and I uncovered my throat so that the cold might temper some of the fire I felt, but I could not." Her visions were often violent and erotic. She experienced mystical union with Christ as a kind of annihilation of self—a destruction she actively sought. "I want to die," she wrote, "but not to flee suffering. Rather I want to die of love."

The path to these experiences was radical poverty combined with intense contemplation of Christ's passion. Angela spent hours meditating on specific moments of the crucifixion, particularly Christ's words of dereliction: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" She sought to participate in that abandonment. She drank water used to wash lepers' sores. She begged for her food. She described eighteen distinct "steps" of spiritual transformation in the *Memorial*, moving from initial contrition through progressive stripping away of attachment, security, and finally selfhood itself.

Her teaching, such as it was, came through these dictated accounts to Arnaldo rather than systematic instruction. She gathered a small community of followers, both male and female, and her cell became a pilgrimage site for those seeking her counsel. But Angela was primarily reporting what she found, not prescribing a method. Her central insight was that union with God required entering into the darkness of God's own self-emptying on the cross. "In this most deep darkness," she said, "I found the fullness of God."

The church's reception of Angela was cautious but ultimately positive. Arnaldo, clearly disturbed by what he was transcribing, repeatedly insisted on her orthodoxy and had the *Memorial* examined by theologians. They found nothing heretical, though the text skates close to quietism and pantheism at points. Angela speaks of a state beyond virtue and vice, beyond even the humanity of Christ, where the soul rests in the divine darkness. She was never canonized during the Middle Ages, but her writings influenced later mystics including John of the Cross. Pope Francis beatified her in 2013, seven centuries after her death.

Modern scholars remain divided on how to read Angela. Caroline Walker Bynum sees her as exemplifying medieval women's somatic mysticism, where devotion expressed itself through the body in extreme ways. Bernard McGinn treats her as a legitimate voice in the apophatic tradition, pursuing the God beyond images. Psychological readings note possible symptoms of trauma—those dead family members she prayed away—and the self-punishing quality of her asceticism. The eroticism of her visions troubles some readers; others see it as honest acknowledgment of the body's role in spiritual experience.

What's undeniable is the rawness of her testimony. Angela does not theologize her experiences into comfort. She reports encountering a God who is both ravishing love and annihilating darkness, whose presence brings such pleasure that it's indistinguishable from pain. Her legacy is permission for mystical experience to be messy, frightening, and physically overwhelming—not the serene contemplation of devotional art but something closer to what she actually wrote: "I screamed with a loud voice, and I cried out without any shame, because the agony was so very great and intense that I could not hold back."
