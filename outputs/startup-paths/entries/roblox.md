# Roblox

***The platform that became a universe***

> **Years:** 2004-present

David Baszucki and Erik Cassel started with a physics simulator. In 1989, Baszucki had founded Knowledge Revolution, making educational software that let students experiment with virtual physics. Teachers used it. Students played with it — often ignoring the lesson to just build and crash things together. When Baszucki sold that company to MSC Software in 1998, he took note of what had captivated users: not learning physics, but creating worlds.

By 2004, broadband was spreading into homes. Flash games were everywhere, but they were disposable — play once, forget. MMORPGs had millions hooked, but they were prescriptive: follow the quest, level up, repeat the content designers created. Baszucki and Cassel saw a gap. What if the players were the designers? What if the game was whatever kids decided to make?

They built it in Cassel's garage in Menlo Park. The technology was ambitious for the time: a physics engine that let objects interact realistically, a scripting language simple enough for beginners, and servers that could host thousands of user-generated worlds simultaneously. They called it DynaBlocks initially, then pivoted to Roblox — a portmanteau of "robots" and "blocks." The aesthetic was deliberately crude: blocky avatars, simple textures, cartoonish physics. It ran on cheap computers. That mattered.

They launched in 2006. Early growth was organic and weird. Kids found it, built obstacle courses and role-playing towns, invited friends. Parents didn't understand it. The gaming press mostly ignored it — too young, too crude, not a "real" game. That dismissal was protective. While journalists chased the next Halo, Roblox quietly accumulated millions of children who logged in after school to hang out in worlds their friends had made.

The monetization model was unusual. Users could build for free. Playing was free. But Roblox sold "Robux," a virtual currency, and let creators charge for access to their games or for virtual items within them. Initially, Roblox kept most of the revenue. But in 2013, they introduced the Developer Exchange program: creators could convert Robux back into real dollars. It was a slow payoff — you needed to accumulate substantial Robux, and Roblox's cut was steep — but it worked. Teenagers who had started building for fun began treating it like a job. Some made thousands of dollars. A few made millions.

This creator economy became the engine. By 2015, some of the most popular experiences on Roblox were built by teenagers or college students, not the company. *Adopt Me*, a pet simulation game created by two developers from the UK, would eventually reach billions of visits. *Jailbreak*, a cops-and-robbers game, was built by a college student. Roblox didn't need to make hit games. It needed to make the platform where hit games emerged.

But there were growing pains. Moderation was a nightmare. Kids built inappropriate content. Predators tried to use the chat. Roblox's moderation system was part algorithmic, part human, and perpetually overwhelmed. The company added filters, restricted chat for younger users, hired thousands of moderators. Critics argued it was never enough. Lawsuits cropped up. Regulation loomed. Roblox responded with layers of controls but never fully solved the problem — the cost of a platform where millions of kids interact daily.

Funding came slowly, then all at once. Roblox raised modest rounds in the early years, then $92 million in 2017, $150 million in 2020. By then, the pandemic had arrived. Lockdowns put kids at home with nothing to do. Roblox became their social life. Daily active users surged from 19 million in early 2020 to over 30 million by year's end. Hours played skyrocketed. Revenue doubled, then doubled again.

In March 2021, Roblox went public via direct listing. No IPO roadshow, no underwriters setting a price — they just let the market decide. It debuted at a valuation of $45 billion. Baszucki, who had spent fifteen years building a thing journalists called a kids' game, was suddenly a billionaire many times over. Wall Street analysts scrambled to understand what Roblox was. A gaming platform? A social network? A metaverse? Roblox called itself a "human co-experience platform." The market didn't care about the label. It cared about the numbers: tens of millions of daily users, billions in revenue, and a creator economy that was mostly self-sustaining.

The post-pandemic years tested the thesis. As kids went back to school, engagement plateaued. Revenue growth slowed. The stock fell from its peak. Critics argued Roblox had been a pandemic beneficiary, not a structural shift. But the users didn't leave. They aged up. Roblox began courting older audiences, partnerships with brands like Gucci and Nike, concerts by Lil Nas X. It leaned into the metaverse narrative just as Facebook rebranded to Meta. The terminology was trendy, but the substance was the same: Roblox was still a place where people built worlds and hung out.

By 2024, Roblox had over 70 million daily active users. The top creators were earning millions. The platform had paid out billions to developers. Baszucki talked about a future where Roblox was agnostic to device, reality, or age — a persistent space where human experience happened. Whether that vision materializes or whether Roblox becomes another platform kids outgrow remains to be seen. But the path is clear: from a physics simulator teachers used, to a game kids made, to a universe they inhabit.
