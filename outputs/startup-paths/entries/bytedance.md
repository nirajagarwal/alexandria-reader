# ByteDance

***The algorithm that ate the world***

> **Years:** 2012-present

Zhang Yiming founded ByteDance in Beijing in 2012 with a thesis that ran counter to conventional wisdom in Chinese tech. While Tencent and Sina Weibo built social graphs — connecting people to people — Zhang believed the future belonged to content graphs. Connect people to content they'd actually want to see, regardless of who created it. Let machine learning, not social networks, determine what surfaces.

Zhang had studied microelectronics at Nankai University but gravitated to software. He worked at Microsoft briefly, then several Chinese startups. At travel site Kuxun, he watched recommendation engines work. At 99fang, a real estate platform, he saw how personalization drove engagement. By his late twenties, he understood something: the algorithms were getting good enough to replace human curation and social filtering entirely.

ByteDance's first product, Toutiao, launched in August 2012 as a news aggregator. No editors. No social graph. Pure algorithmic recommendation. It scraped content from across the Chinese internet and used machine learning to serve each user a personalized feed. Traditional media companies sued for copyright infringement. Regulators investigated. Zhang paid settlements and kept building.

Toutiao grew explosively. Within three years it had 100 million daily active users. The algorithm worked. People spent hours scrolling content selected specifically for them. ByteDance raised money — first from SIG, then from Sequoia China. By 2016, the company was valued at over $10 billion without having expanded beyond China or beyond text and images.

Then came video. Zhang noticed that short-form video was exploding in China. Musical.ly, founded by Chinese entrepreneurs but targeted at Western teens, proved that 15-second clips could be addictive. Kuaishou was growing fast in China with user-generated short videos. Zhang saw the pattern: video was the next frontier, and ByteDance's recommendation engine would dominate it.

In September 2016, ByteDance launched Douyin, a short-form video app for the Chinese market. Fifteen-second videos, easy editing tools, music integration. But the core innovation was the feed. Unlike YouTube or even early Kuaishou, Douyin's feed wasn't based on subscriptions or social connections. You opened the app and immediately started watching an algorithmically-generated stream of videos. Swipe up for the next one. The algorithm learned from every interaction — watch time, replays, shares, skips.

Douyin's growth was meteoric. Within a year it had 100 million users. ByteDance's engineering culture obsessed over retention metrics and feed optimization. Teams ran thousands of A/B tests. The algorithm improved daily. Douyin became the most addictive app in China.

In late 2017, ByteDance launched TikTok, the international version of Douyin. The app was nearly identical but operated separately to comply with Chinese regulations. Initial growth outside China was slow. Musical.ly, despite its Chinese founders, had better traction with Western teenagers.

In November 2017, ByteDance acquired Musical.ly for approximately $1 billion. In August 2018, it merged Musical.ly into TikTok, migrating Musical.ly's users to the TikTok platform. The combined app kept the TikTok name and ByteDance's superior algorithm.

The effect was explosive. TikTok's growth in the US, Europe, and India accelerated dramatically. The algorithm proved even more powerful with Musical.ly's existing content and users as training data. By early 2019, TikTok was the most downloaded app in the US. Teenagers and then adults disappeared into infinite scroll sessions. The "For You Page" — TikTok's algorithmic feed — became cultural shorthand for addictive personalization.

ByteDance's valuation soared. By 2020, it was worth $140 billion, making it the world's most valuable startup. Zhang ran the company with an engineer's mentality — data-driven, execution-focused, relatively apolitical. But TikTok's success created geopolitical problems.

The Trump administration declared TikTok a national security threat in 2020, claiming the app could share American user data with the Chinese government. Trump issued executive orders attempting to ban TikTok unless ByteDance sold it to a US company. Oracle and Walmart emerged as potential buyers. The deal was structured, then stalled, then became moot when Biden took office and shifted to a review process rather than a forced sale.

But the pressure didn't end. India banned TikTok in 2020, eliminating ByteDance's largest market by user count overnight. The US continued investigating. Europe raised questions. Zhang, facing mounting scrutiny and apparently exhausted, stepped down as CEO in 2021, though he remained chairman.

ByteDance navigated the geopolitical storm by compartmentalizing. TikTok operated with US-based leadership, stored American data on Oracle servers, and maintained separation from ByteDance's Chinese operations. Douyin remained massive in China. ByteDance continued building other products — Lark (workplace software), Resso (music streaming), a suite of productivity tools.

By 2024, TikTok had over 150 million users in the US and more than a billion worldwide. It had reshaped how content spreads, how culture forms, how attention flows. Its influence on social media was absolute — Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, and countless other TikTok clones testified to that. ByteDance remained private, valued north of $200 billion, still controlled by Zhang and early investors.

The company proved Zhang's original thesis: the algorithm mattered more than the social graph. Connection to content, optimized by machine learning and measured by engagement, beat connection to people. ByteDance built the most powerful recommendation engine in the world, then wrapped it in the most addictive format — short-form video delivered in infinite scroll. The result was a new kind of media company, one that knew what you wanted to see before you did, and kept you watching long after you meant to stop.
