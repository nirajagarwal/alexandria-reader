# ByteDance/TikTok

***The algorithm that conquered attention***

> **Years:** 2012-present

Zhang Yiming graduated from Nankai University in 2005 with a degree in software engineering and spent the next seven years building and selling internet companies in Beijing. By 2012, he had studied the mechanics of viral content distribution across multiple attempts. He understood something specific: recommendation algorithms could predict what people wanted to watch better than social graphs could. Facebook showed you content from friends. Zhang wanted to show you content from strangers that an algorithm knew you would love.

He founded ByteDance in March 2012 with four other engineers in a small Beijing apartment. The name came from the idea of bytes dancing on the screen. The first product, Toutiao, was a news aggregation app that used machine learning to recommend articles. No editors. No following required. Just an algorithm watching what you read and predicting what you'd read next. Within a year, Toutiao had ten million users. Within three years, it had one hundred million.

The insight was simple but profound: engagement metrics revealed preference better than stated preference. If someone claimed to love serious journalism but consistently clicked celebrity gossip, show them gossip. The algorithm was amoral about content quality. It cared only about time spent, videos finished, shares completed. This made ByteDance very good at giving people what they actually wanted rather than what they said they wanted.

By 2016, ByteDance was valued at over $10 billion on the strength of Toutiao alone. But Zhang saw the next turn: short-form video. Chinese users were creating fifteen-second clips on apps like Kuaishou. The format was raw, unpolished, amateur — and magnetic. In September 2016, ByteDance launched Douyin, a short-form video app with the same recommendation engine that powered Toutiao, but applied to video. Full-screen, sound-on, vertical video. Swipe up for the next one. The algorithm learned what you liked within minutes.

Douyin exploded in China. Within a year, it had one hundred million daily active users. The algorithm was ruthless in its effectiveness. It didn't matter how many followers you had. A video from someone with zero followers could go viral if the algorithm predicted it would hold attention. This was democratic in theory, addictive in practice. Users spent hours swiping through an infinite feed of strangers dancing, lip-syncing, joking, teaching. The average session length was extraordinary.

Zhang understood that algorithms don't stop at borders. In November 2017, ByteDance paid nearly $1 billion to acquire Musical.ly, a short-form video app popular with American teenagers. Musical.ly had the users. ByteDance had the algorithm. In August 2018, ByteDance merged Musical.ly into a new app for international markets: TikTok. Same interface as Douyin. Same algorithmic core. Different content library.

TikTok grew faster than any social media platform in history. By early 2019, it was the most downloaded app in the U.S. The For You Page — the algorithmic feed — was unnervingly good at prediction. Users described it as mind-reading. The algorithm tracked watch time, rewatches, shares, comments, and hundreds of other signals. It could identify micro-genres: sad girl autumn, cottage core, finance bro comedy. It served you content you didn't know you wanted until you couldn't stop watching.

Traditional social media companies scrambled. Facebook had built an empire on the social graph. TikTok proved you didn't need friends to be social. You needed an algorithm that understood desire. Instagram launched Reels in 2020 as a direct TikTok clone. YouTube launched Shorts. Every platform pivoted toward algorithmic feeds and short-form video. They were all copying ByteDance.

But TikTok's success created exposure. By 2020, U.S. lawmakers worried about data flowing to China. The app had 100 million American users sharing location data, biometric information, viewing habits. President Trump issued executive orders to ban TikTok unless ByteDance sold it to a U.S. company. Oracle and Walmart negotiated a deal. The Trump administration left office. The Biden administration continued the pressure but through regulatory channels rather than outright bans. India banned TikTok entirely in 2020, erasing ByteDance's largest potential market overnight.

ByteDance responded by building data centers in the U.S., hiring American executives, and proposing governance structures that would isolate American user data. But the core tension remained: TikTok's algorithm was its value, and the algorithm lived in Beijing. Any real separation would destroy what made TikTok work. Meanwhile, the app kept growing. By 2023, TikTok had over 150 million American users and was the most visited website in the world, surpassing Google.

The geopolitical pressure intensified. In March 2024, the U.S. House passed a bill requiring ByteDance to divest TikTok or face a ban. ByteDance said the algorithm wasn't for sale. A forced sale without the algorithm would be selling a shell. The company was valued at over $200 billion, making it one of the most valuable private companies in the world. But the value rested on technology that might be banned in its largest growth market.

Zhang stepped down as CEO in 2021, citing a preference for long-term technology development over daily operations. ByteDance continued expanding into gaming, education technology, and enterprise software. But TikTok remained the crown jewel and the existential risk. The company had proven that attention could be conquered with sufficiently advanced prediction. It had also proven that conquering attention at global scale made you a national security concern.

The outcome remains unresolved. TikTok operates in dozens of countries, prints money through advertising, and shapes culture in ways Facebook once did. It faces bans, restrictions, and competitors with infinite resources trying to copy its algorithm. ByteDance keeps the algorithm in Beijing. The algorithm keeps getting better at knowing what you want to watch next.
