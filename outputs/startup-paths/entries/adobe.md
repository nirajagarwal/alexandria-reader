# Adobe

***From desktop publishing revolution to creative cloud empire***

> **Years:** 1982-present

In December 1982, John Warnock and Charles Geschke left Xerox PARC with a technology their former employer had no interest in commercializing. They had developed PostScript, a programming language that could describe the precise layout of text and graphics on a printed page. Xerox saw it as a solution without a market. Warnock and Geschke saw it as the foundation of a new industry.

They named their company Adobe, after the creek that ran behind Warnock's house. The first challenge was survival. PostScript was elegant technology, but in 1982, personal computers couldn't do much with documents beyond displaying green text on black screens. The founders needed someone to build hardware that could bring PostScript to life.

Steve Jobs provided the lifeline. Apple was developing the LaserWriter, a printer that would cost $7,000 but produce output that looked professionally typeset. Jobs licensed PostScript for the LaserWriter, giving Adobe $2.5 million in advance payments and a per-unit royalty. When the LaserWriter shipped in 1985 alongside the Macintosh and Aldus PageMaker, desktop publishing was born. What had required $100,000 in typesetting equipment could now be done with $10,000 in computer hardware.

Adobe went public in 1986. Revenue came almost entirely from PostScript licensing deals with printer manufacturers. It was a good business, but Warnock and Geschke understood the trap: they were dependent on hardware companies who might eventually decide to build their own page description languages. They needed to control more of the value chain.

The answer was software. In 1987, Adobe released Illustrator, a drawing program for the Macintosh. It let designers create the kind of graphics that PostScript could describe. Then came Photoshop, acquired from brothers Thomas and John Knoll in 1988 for what would prove to be an absurdly low price. Adobe didn't invent Photoshop—Thomas Knoll wrote the first version as a graduate student project—but Adobe saw what it could become.

Through the 1990s, Adobe assembled what would become the Creative Suite: Premiere for video editing, After Effects for motion graphics, InDesign to finally kill QuarkXPress's dominance in page layout. Each acquisition or development extended Adobe's reach into creative workflows. The strategy was ecosystem lock-in through workflow integration. A designer who learned Photoshop would want Illustrator. A design shop using Illustrator would want InDesign. The tools talked to each other in ways that competitors' products didn't.

But the business model was showing cracks. Adobe sold perpetual licenses—pay once, own forever. Customers would skip versions, sometimes several in a row, waiting for features that mattered to them. Revenue was cyclical, spiking with major releases and slumping between. Wall Street hated the unpredictability. Piracy was rampant; Creative Suite cost thousands of dollars, putting it out of reach for students and small studios who simply copied it instead.

In 2011, Adobe announced a subscription model. Creative Suite would become Creative Cloud—$50 per month for access to all Adobe's creative tools, always updated, cloud storage included. The reaction was volcanic. Petitions gathered hundreds of thousands of signatures. Designers felt betrayed. They didn't want to rent software. They wanted to own it.

Adobe didn't blink. In 2013, they stopped selling perpetual licenses entirely. Creative Cloud or nothing.

The financial transformation was dramatic. By 2015, Adobe had more Creative Cloud subscribers than they'd ever had Creative Suite customers. Revenue became predictable, recurring, growing. The subscription model did something perpetual licenses never could: it made Adobe indispensable rather than just useful. Stop paying, lose access to years of work files. The switching cost became infinite.

The pivot to cloud did something else—it democratized access. At $50 per month, students and small studios could actually afford legitimate Adobe software. The piracy problem evaporated. A generation of designers who might have used pirated copies or switched to cheaper alternatives became paying customers instead.

But Adobe's dominance created complacency. Feature development slowed. Bugs persisted across versions. Competitors emerged—Sketch for interface design, Figma for collaborative design, Affinity for photo editing—all faster, cheaper, or more innovative. Adobe responded not with better products but with acquisition attempts. In 2022, they announced a $20 billion deal to buy Figma, the collaborative design tool that had emerged as the first genuine threat to Adobe's design monopoly in decades.

Regulators in the U.S. and EU saw a monopolist trying to buy its way out of competition. By 2024, facing certain regulatory rejection, Adobe abandoned the acquisition and paid Figma a $1 billion termination fee.

Today Adobe is worth over $200 billion. Creative Cloud has 32 million subscribers generating $20 billion in annual revenue. The tools that desktop publishers used in 1985—PostScript, Illustrator, Photoshop—remain industry standards forty years later. But the company faces an uncomfortable reality: it succeeded by making itself essential, then locked customers in with subscriptions. Now it must innovate to keep them, and innovation was never what Adobe did best. What Adobe did best was consolidate.

The path from PostScript to Creative Cloud wasn't inevitable. It required seeing that the real business wasn't printers or software licenses but creative workflows themselves. Adobe built the tools, then built the trap, then made the trap so comfortable that customers stayed willingly. Until they don't.
