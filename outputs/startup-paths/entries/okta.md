# Okta

***The identity company that rode the cloud wave and became infrastructure***

> **Years:** 2009-present

Todd McKinnon had been at Salesforce for seven years, rising to head of engineering, when he walked away in 2009. His co-founder, Frederic Kerrest, had been at Salesforce too — in product and sales. They left not because they saw Salesforce failing, but because they saw what it represented: a future where companies would run on cloud software, not servers in closets.

The insight was simple but profound. Every SaaS application — Salesforce, Workday, Google Apps — required its own login. Every employee needed to remember passwords for a dozen services. IT departments were losing control of who had access to what. The old model — Microsoft Active Directory managing access to software behind the corporate firewall — didn't work when the software lived in someone else's cloud.

McKinnon and Kerrest founded Okta to be the identity layer for cloud software. Not a password manager. Not single sign-on bolted onto an old directory system. A new system, built from the ground up for a world where companies would use hundreds of cloud applications.

The market didn't yet know it needed this. In 2009, "the cloud" was still a suspicious buzzword. Enterprises ran software on their own servers. Salesforce was the radical exception. McKinnon and Kerrest were betting that within a decade, most enterprise software would be SaaS — and that when it was, someone would need to be the universal identity provider.

They raised $2 million from Khosla Ventures. Ben Horowitz at Andreessen Horowitz came in soon after. Horowitz understood infrastructure bets. Okta was a pick-and-shovel play on the cloud gold rush.

The early product was narrowly focused: single sign-on for cloud applications. You logged into Okta once, and it handled authentication for everything else. Simple value proposition. Hard technical problem. Active Directory was decades of accumulated edge cases. Building a new directory that enterprises would trust was not trivial.

The first customers were small companies and tech startups already living in the cloud. Okta became part of the stack alongside Salesforce and Google Apps. But McKinnon wanted enterprises. The real money was in selling to IT departments managing thousands of employees across hundreds of applications.

The problem was timing. In 2010, enterprises weren't ready. CIOs didn't believe the cloud was secure enough. They didn't trust a startup with their identity infrastructure. Okta spent years selling to companies that weren't yet convinced they needed what Okta offered.

What saved them was patience and the market catching up. By 2013, the cloud was inevitable. Salesforce had proven the model. Microsoft itself was moving Office to the cloud. Every enterprise software vendor was building a SaaS version. And every single one needed identity management.

Okta's bet was paying off. They hadn't just built single sign-on. They'd built a platform. Other companies could integrate with Okta to handle authentication. The more applications supported Okta, the more valuable Okta became to enterprises. It was a network effect in infrastructure.

They raised more money. Series C in 2015: $75 million at a $600 million valuation. The business was growing, but not yet profitable. Enterprise sales cycles are long. Implementations take months. Revenue compounds slowly, then suddenly.

By 2017, Okta had 4,000 customers and $160 million in revenue. They went public. The IPO priced at $17, valuing the company at $1.5 billion. It was not a spectacular debut, but it was validation. A company selling identity infrastructure to enterprises could be a public company. The cloud had matured enough to support a pure-play identity layer.

The years after IPO were the growth phase McKinnon had been building toward. Every company was buying SaaS. Every SaaS purchase created more complexity. Okta's platform expanded — multi-factor authentication, lifecycle management, API access control. They acquired companies to fill gaps. In 2021, they bought Auth0 for $6.5 billion, absorbing a competitor that had focused on developer-centric identity tools.

Revenue grew past $1 billion. The stock peaked above $270 in 2021, giving Okta a market cap near $40 billion. They'd won. Identity for the cloud era was a massive market, and Okta had become the default.

Then the cycle turned. Interest rates rose. SaaS multiples collapsed. Okta's growth slowed as enterprises cut software spending. A breach at a third-party vendor exposed customer data. The stock fell to $60 — an 80% drop from the peak. Not a death spiral, but a reckoning. The company was profitable, growing, essential infrastructure — but the era of limitless growth expectations was over.

Okta today is what McKinnon bet it would become: the identity layer for enterprise cloud software. Tens of thousands of companies use it. It's boring infrastructure that works. The stock price drama is noise. The business is what matters — and the business won because McKinnon and Kerrest saw the cloud coming before most enterprises believed it would arrive. They built for a future that took longer than venture timelines expected but arrived exactly as they predicted. Timing is luck, but preparation is strategy. Okta had both.
