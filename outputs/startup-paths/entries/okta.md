# Okta

***The identity layer that became infrastructure***

> **Years:** 2009-present

In 2009, Todd McKinnon was vice president of engineering at Salesforce, watching the company grow into a cloud giant. He had helped build the platform that was replacing on-premise software, but he kept noticing the same problem: every company adopting cloud services faced a nightmare of managing user identities and access across dozens of disconnected systems. IT departments were drowning in password resets, security teams couldn't enforce policies consistently, and employees wasted hours logging into different applications. The old solution—Microsoft Active Directory—worked beautifully for on-premise software but was never designed for a world where everything lived in the cloud.

McKinnon saw the pattern clearly. As more companies moved to cloud services, identity would become critical infrastructure. Someone needed to build the identity layer for the cloud era. He pitched the idea to Frederic Kerrest, a former colleague, and in January 2009 they left Salesforce to start Okta. The timing seemed terrible. The financial crisis had just cratered the economy, venture capital had dried up, and enterprise software was supposed to be dead. Everyone said the cloud was for consumers, not businesses.

They raised a seed round anyway—$750,000 from Floodgate—and built the first version in Kerrest's apartment. The concept was straightforward: a cloud-based service that would manage employee identities and control access to all their applications through single sign-on. One login, one password, access to everything. Behind the scenes, Okta would handle authentication, enforce security policies, and integrate with both cloud apps and on-premise systems. They called it "identity as a service."

The first challenge was the chicken-and-egg problem. Enterprises wouldn't adopt Okta without integrations to the apps they used. App vendors wouldn't prioritize Okta integrations without a large customer base. McKinnon and Kerrest spent the first year building integrations manually, one app at a time. Salesforce, Google Apps, Box, Workday. They created a network of pre-built connections that made Okta immediately useful. By the time they launched publicly in 2010, they had a hundred integrations ready.

Early customers were mostly tech companies already comfortable with cloud services—Salesforce customers who understood the problem Okta solved. The sales cycle was brutal. Selling security software to enterprises meant navigating IT bureaucracies, proving reliability, and overcoming deep skepticism about trusting a startup with something as critical as identity. But every successful deployment became a reference account. Every integration they built made the next sale easier.

The product market fit came faster than expected. By 2011, they had real revenue and momentum. Companies weren't just buying Okta for convenience—they were buying it because cloud adoption was accelerating and they had no other solution. The alternative was cobbling together VPNs, legacy directory services, and manual processes. Okta worked. It scaled. It didn't break.

They raised a Series A in 2011, then a Series B in 2012. McKinnon made a crucial strategic decision: focus exclusively on the enterprise market. No consumer products, no distractions. While competitors tried to be everything to everyone, Okta would be the best identity platform for businesses. They built features enterprises demanded—multi-factor authentication, lifecycle management, detailed audit logs, compliance certifications. They hired enterprise sales teams who understood how to sell to CIOs and security officers.

The competitive landscape was treacherous. Microsoft, with its dominant Active Directory, was the obvious threat. But Microsoft was slow to adapt Active Directory for the cloud, giving Okta years of runway. Smaller competitors like OneLogin and Ping Identity competed directly, but Okta outexecuted them on integrations, user experience, and enterprise features. The key advantage was the network effect—every new integration made Okta more valuable, and every new customer created incentive for more integrations.

By 2015, Okta had grown into a substantial business with thousands of customers and hundreds of millions in revenue. They had become infrastructure—companies depended on Okta to run. The valuation climbed with each funding round. They raised a Series E at a $1.2 billion valuation, joining the unicorn club. McKinnon resisted pressure to sell. He believed Okta could be an independent, enduring company.

The IPO came in April 2017. They priced at $17 per share, raising $187 million and valuing the company at $1.6 billion. The stock jumped 38% on the first day. Wall Street finally understood what McKinnon had seen in 2009: identity was fundamental infrastructure for the cloud era, and Okta had won the race to build it.

Post-IPO, Okta expanded aggressively. They acquired Stormpath in 2017 to add developer tools, then Auth0 in 2021 for $6.5 billion—a massive bet on customer identity management beyond the enterprise. The Auth0 acquisition was controversial. Some saw it as Okta dominating the entire identity market. Others worried the company was overpaying and straying from its core.

The business kept growing. By 2024, Okta served over 18,000 customers including half the Fortune 500. The product had expanded far beyond single sign-on into a comprehensive identity platform managing billions of authentication events daily. The market cap fluctuated with tech valuations but held above $10 billion even through downturns.

What McKinnon and Kerrest built wasn't just a product but a category. "Identity as a service" became standard nomenclature. Every cloud-first company now assumes identity will be managed by a specialized platform, not homegrown tools. Okta made that assumption possible. The company that started in an apartment during a financial crisis became the infrastructure layer enabling modern work—invisible, essential, ubiquitous.
