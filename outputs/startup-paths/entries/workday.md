# Workday

***The enterprise software insurgency that refused to compromise***

> **Years:** 2005-present

In 2005, Dave Duffield had already built and sold PeopleSoft, a human resources and financial software company, to Oracle for $10.3 billion. He was 64 years old. He could have retired. Instead, Oracle's Larry Ellison made a critical miscalculation: he called Duffield's life's work "junk" and promised to stop developing PeopleSoft products. Duffield had been forced to sell in a hostile takeover. Now Ellison was insulting what he'd built. Duffield decided to do it again—and do it differently.

He partnered with Aneel Bhusri, PeopleSoft's former head of product strategy, who was 38 and working in venture capital. They saw an opening. The enterprise software model was broken. Companies like Oracle and SAP sold expensive licenses for software installed on-premises. Implementation took years. Upgrades were nightmares. Customers were locked in and miserable.

The cloud was emerging, but enterprise buyers didn't trust it for their most sensitive data—payroll, HR records, financial systems. Salesforce had proven cloud delivery could work for CRM, but those were sales records, not the company's core operational systems. HR and finance executives were conservative. They wanted proven technology, not experiments.

Duffield and Bhusri bet they could build cloud-native enterprise software that was actually good. Not just cheaper or more convenient—genuinely superior. They raised $15 million from Greylock Partners and started building. The name "Workday" came from their vision: software you could use every workday without hating it.

Their first target was human capital management—HR systems. They knew this space intimately from PeopleSoft. They understood what customers wanted and what the legacy systems couldn't deliver. But instead of replicating the old architecture in the cloud, they built something new. A single database for all HR data. An interface that didn't require a training manual. Automatic updates that wouldn't break customizations.

The early days were a grind. They launched in 2006 with no customers and a product that needed years of development before it could handle the complexity of large enterprises. They sold to mid-sized companies first—big enough to have real HR complexity, small enough to take a risk on an unproven vendor. Their first customer went live in 2008.

Then 2008 happened. The financial crisis froze enterprise software spending. Companies that might have considered a new system stopped signing contracts. Workday's pipeline evaporated. They had built for two years and were just beginning to gain traction when the market collapsed.

But the crisis also helped them. Oracle and SAP were so expensive, so slow to implement, that when budgets recovered, companies looked for alternatives. Workday's cloud model meant no hardware to buy, no data center to build. Faster deployment. Lower upfront costs. For companies emerging from the crisis looking to do more with less, it was compelling.

They started winning major accounts. First dozens of employees, then hundreds, then thousands. Chiquita Brands in 2009. Flextronics in 2010. They weren't just winning mid-market deals anymore—they were taking Fortune 500 accounts away from Oracle and SAP.

In 2012, they expanded into financial management—the other half of the enterprise software equation. This was riskier. HR systems touch everyone in a company, but finance is the nervous system. CFOs don't experiment. But Workday had proven they could execute. They built financial management with the same architecture: cloud-native, unified database, consumer-grade interface. And they sold it bundled with HR, creating a platform that could run core operations for an entire enterprise.

They went public in October 2012 at $28 per share, raising $637 million in the largest software IPO since VMware. The stock jumped 75% on the first day. Investors believed. By then, Workday had over 400 customers and revenue approaching $250 million annually.

The growth continued. They passed $1 billion in revenue in 2016. $3 billion in 2019. $5 billion in 2022. They kept winning deals against Oracle and SAP—not by being cheaper, but by being better. Faster to implement. Easier to use. More flexible. And because everything was in the cloud, they could update the software twice a year. Every customer got the improvements automatically. No more painful, expensive upgrades.

Oracle and SAP tried to respond. They built cloud versions of their products, but they were retrofits—legacy systems awkwardly adapted to the cloud. Workday's advantage was architectural. They were cloud-native from day one. The difference showed in win rates.

By 2024, Workday serves over 10,000 customers, including more than half the Fortune 500. Revenue exceeds $7 billion annually. The company is worth over $60 billion. Duffield, now in his eighties, remains chairman.

The path was improbable: a 64-year-old founder, starting over in a category dominated by entrenched giants, selling to conservative enterprise buyers, launching just before a global financial crisis. But Workday succeeded because they refused to compromise. They didn't build a cheaper version of the old model. They rebuilt enterprise software from scratch, cloud-native, at a time when that bet was far from obvious. They sold it to skeptical buyers who eventually realized the cloud wasn't just adequate—it was superior. And they executed relentlessly for nearly two decades, outlasting competitors who thought age and experience would be liabilities. Duffield proved otherwise.
