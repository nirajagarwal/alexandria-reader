# DoorDash

***The third mover that won by refusing to quit***

> **Years:** 2013-present

In January 2013, Tony Xu, Andy Fang, Evan Moore, and Stanley Tang were Stanford students working on a class project about small business problems. They walked University Avenue in Palo Alto asking shop owners what made their lives difficult. A macaroon shop owner said she was too busy to hire delivery drivers but customers kept asking for delivery. The students built a simple website that weekend. It got orders immediately.

This was not a novel insight. Grubhub had launched in 2004. Seamless in 1999. By 2013, food delivery was a solved problem in major cities. The conventional wisdom was clear: restaurants in dense urban areas would pay for access to office workers ordering lunch. The market was mature.

Xu saw something different. The macaroon shop wasn't in Manhattan. It was in Palo Alto — a suburb. The entire delivery model assumed density. Xu thought the model could work anywhere if the unit economics worked. He convinced his co-founders to defer their job offers and try it for a year.

They called it Palo Alto Delivery. They delivered themselves, driving orders in their own cars. Xu insisted they deliver even when it seemed inefficient. He wanted to understand the physics of the problem — traffic patterns, parking, restaurant pickup times, customer expectations. While competitors optimized software, DoorDash optimized operations. Xu spent months as a Dasher, the term they coined for delivery drivers.

The first insight was that restaurants outside major cities were desperate for delivery. They had customers but no infrastructure. Grubhub and Seamless served restaurants that already did delivery. DoorDash served restaurants that didn't. That expanded the market.

The second insight was that consumers in suburbs had cars and didn't think they needed delivery. But once they tried it — especially families with young children — they ordered repeatedly. Convenience was more valuable than anyone measured.

In 2014, they raised $2.4 million from Khosla Ventures and rebranded as DoorDash. They launched in San Francisco but also in places like Redwood City and San Jose. Competitors thought this was foolish. These markets had lower order density. The unit economics seemed worse.

But DoorDash had learned something about driver supply. In suburbs, people with cars had downtime between activities — after dropping kids at school, between shifts, on weekends. They would Dash for two hours. In cities, delivery workers were professionals working full shifts. DoorDash's model attracted casual labor. That gave them flexible supply that could match variable demand.

By 2015, the food delivery wars had begun. Uber launched UberEats. Postmates expanded. Amazon tried Amazon Restaurants. Venture capital poured in. The narrative was that Uber would win because of its existing driver network and brand. DoorDash was third in market share and seemed vulnerable.

Xu made two decisions that defined the company. First, he focused on suburbs and mid-sized cities where competitors weren't. While others fought over Manhattan, DoorDash built density in Columbus, Phoenix, and Sacramento. They learned to operate profitably at lower order volumes.

Second, he obsessed over restaurants. Competitors saw restaurants as inventory. Xu saw them as customers. DoorDash built tools to help restaurants manage orders, analyze sales, and market to customers. They gave restaurants more control over pricing and presentation. They became the platform restaurants preferred, which became a moat as multi-tenanting created operational headaches.

The company nearly died anyway. In 2016 and 2017, they burned cash building driver supply. Investors questioned whether delivery would ever be profitable. Some board members pushed for a sale. Xu refused. He believed the market was winner-take-most and that surrender meant defeat.

The breakthrough came from data. DoorDash's suburban strategy generated information about how delivery worked in different geographies. They could predict demand better, route more efficiently, and price more precisely. By 2018, these small advantages compounded. Their gross margins improved while competitors' stagnated.

In 2019, DoorDash passed Grubhub to become the market leader. Uber was distracted by its IPO and scandals. Postmates was acquired by Uber. Grubhub sold to Just Eat Takeaway. DoorDash had won by outlasting.

Then came COVID-19. Restaurants closed for dining. Delivery became essential infrastructure. DoorDash's order volume tripled. The company that had seemed marginal became critical. They went public in December 2020 at a $38 billion valuation.

The post-pandemic question was whether delivery habits would persist. They did. Remote work, families with young children, and older consumers had normalized delivery. The market hadn't contracted to pre-pandemic levels. DoorDash expanded into grocery, convenience, and alcohol delivery. They acquired Wolt to expand internationally.

The path DoorDash took was improbable. They entered a mature market third. They focused on geographies competitors ignored. They survived years of losses while better-funded rivals failed. Xu's insight was that food delivery wasn't one market but thousands of local markets, and that winning required understanding operations more than technology.

The company succeeded not because they were first or best-funded, but because they refused to accept that late entry meant defeat. They found underserved markets, built better tools, and persisted through the valley where most companies die. The outcome reveals something about stamina: sometimes the last company standing is simply the one that refused to quit.
