# DoorDash

***The late mover who won by not moving at all***

> **Years:** 2013-present

In January 2013, four Stanford students built a website to solve a problem no one else cared about: restaurants in Palo Alto didn't deliver. Tony Xu, Stanley Tang, Andy Fang, and Evan Moore called it Palo Alto Delivery. The market seemed unpromising. Delivery had been tried. Kozmo.com burned through $280 million in the dot-com crash. Webvan spent $800 million on infrastructure before collapsing. Food delivery specifically looked even worse — low margins, logistics nightmares, reluctant restaurants.

But something had changed. Smartphones put GPS in everyone's pocket. The gig economy was emerging. And crucially, Xu and his co-founders weren't trying to build Webvan. They weren't warehousing inventory or employing drivers. They were connecting three parties: restaurants that didn't deliver, drivers who wanted flexible work, and customers who wanted food. The model was Uber, not FedEx.

They started by doing it manually. Xu would take orders on a phone, call restaurants, then drive the food himself. The first night, they made $240. Within months, they'd expanded to a few dozen restaurants in Palo Alto. They renamed the company DoorDash and raised $2.4 million from Khosla Ventures and others. The pitch was simple: delivery as infrastructure. Restaurants got a new revenue stream without hiring drivers. Customers got access to restaurants that never delivered before.

The problem was they weren't first, second, or even third. GrubHub had been around since 2004 and was about to go public. Postmates launched in 2011. Uber was preparing to launch UberEats. Instacart was raising huge rounds. The food delivery wars were heating up, and DoorDash was late to a crowded field with better-funded competitors.

Their edge was counterintuitive: they focused on suburbs. While Seamless and GrubHub dominated dense urban cores like Manhattan and San Francisco, and while Postmates and Uber chased the same territory, DoorDash went to San Jose, Fresno, and Portland suburbs. They targeted families in sprawling neighborhoods with chain restaurants and strip malls. The unit economics were worse — longer distances, fewer orders per square mile. But competition was lighter. They could sign up restaurants more easily. They could learn and iterate without fighting for every meal.

Xu, who became CEO, approached it like a logistics problem. He obsessed over delivery times. The team built software to optimize routing, predict demand, and manage driver supply. They studied McDonald's drive-through efficiency and FedEx hub operations. While competitors focused on user experience and branding, DoorDash optimized the middle layer — the actual delivery. Xu spent months as a driver himself, testing the system, finding friction points.

By 2015, they had critical mass in second-tier cities. They raised $40 million. The strategy was working, but slowly. Growth was steady, not explosive. Then something unexpected happened: their competitors made mistakes. GrubHub, post-IPO, raised fees on restaurants and stopped innovating. Postmates struggled with unit economics and kept raising prices. Uber launched UberEats but treated it as a side project, using the same drivers as rideshare without optimizing for food delivery.

DoorDash pressed the advantage. They kept suburban focus but started expanding into urban markets competitors had neglected. They offered better terms to restaurants than GrubHub. They built DashPass, a subscription model, before competitors. Most importantly, they didn't chase profitability early. They accepted losses to gain market share, betting that scale would eventually make the model work.

The turning point came around 2018. DoorDash hit 50% market share in many mid-sized cities. They used that leverage to expand into places like Los Angeles and Chicago. The pattern repeated: enter a market, subsidize heavily, sign up restaurants aggressively, optimize operations obsessively, outlast competitors. They raised $535 million at a $1.4 billion valuation, then another $400 million at $1.4 billion again, then $600 million at $4 billion.

The business fundamentals remained questionable. Take rates were thin. Customer acquisition costs were high. Driver churn was constant. They lost money on most orders. Restaurants complained about fees. Cities started regulating commission caps. The model seemed structurally unprofitable.

But they kept growing. By 2019, DoorDash had more U.S. market share than GrubHub and Uber Eats combined. They'd become the default delivery app in most of America. Then COVID-19 hit. Overnight, delivery shifted from convenience to necessity. Order volume exploded. DoorDash's market share surged past 50%. The company that had been burning cash suddenly had more demand than it could handle.

They went public in December 2020 at a $39 billion valuation, more than double their last private round. The stock opened at $182, valuing the company at $60 billion. Xu's stake was worth over $3 billion. The late mover had won.

The path reveals something subtle about timing and positioning. DoorDash didn't have better technology than Uber or better brand than GrubHub. They won by avoiding direct competition long enough to build operational excellence, then using that excellence to win when competitors stumbled. They succeeded not by being first or best, but by being relentless in markets others ignored, then capitalizing when circumstances shifted.

The company remains controversial. Drivers protest working conditions. Restaurants resent the fees. Profitability is inconsistent. But the path from Palo Alto website to public company worth tens of billions reveals a pattern: sometimes the winner is the one who finds empty space, masters execution, and waits for the world to come to them.
