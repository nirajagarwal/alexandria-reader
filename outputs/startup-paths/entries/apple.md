# Apple

***From garage to empire: The improbable resurrection that redefined consumer technology***

> **Years:** 1976-present

In 1976, Steve Wozniak built a computer for himself. He showed it to his friend Steve Jobs at the Homebrew Computer Club in Palo Alto. Jobs saw something Wozniak didn't: people would pay for this. Not hobbyists soldering their own machines — regular people who wanted a computer that just worked. They sold Wozniak's VW van and Jobs's calculator to raise $1,300, built fifty units in Jobs's parents' garage, and created Apple Computer Company.

The Apple I was a circuit board. The Apple II, released in 1977, was the insight made real: a complete computer in a plastic case with a keyboard, color graphics, and eight expansion slots. It came assembled. You plugged it in. For the first time, non-engineers could own computers. Schools bought them. Families bought them. VisiCalc, the first spreadsheet program, ran on it and turned the Apple II into a business tool. By 1980, Apple was printing money.

Then the world shifted. IBM entered the personal computer market in 1981 with an open architecture that let other manufacturers build compatible machines. Microsoft licensed its operating system to anyone. Suddenly there were dozens of PC makers, all competing on price. Apple kept its hardware and software closed, controlling everything, charging premium prices. The market chose abundance and affordability over elegance and control.

Jobs had already moved on to the Macintosh, convinced that the future was graphical interfaces and mice, not command lines. He was right about the destination but wrong about the path. The Mac launched in 1984 with a legendary Super Bowl ad positioning Apple as the rebel against IBM's conformity. The computer itself was beautiful, revolutionary, and fatally constrained — underpowered, unexpandable, overpriced. Developers stayed with the PC. Businesses stayed with the PC. The Mac became a niche product for designers and educators.

Jobs's intensity had created Apple's early success. Now it was destroying the company. He fought with CEO John Sculley, whom he'd recruited from Pepsi with the line, "Do you want to sell sugar water for the rest of your life, or do you want to come with me and change the world?" In 1985, the board sided with Sculley. Jobs was stripped of power, then left entirely.

Apple spent the next eleven years searching for an identity. It licensed Mac OS to other manufacturers, then reversed course. It tried portable devices, digital cameras, gaming consoles — products that presaged the future but arrived too early or executed too poorly. Microsoft Windows improved with each version, borrowing the Mac's graphical interface ideas while running on cheaper hardware. Apple's market share cratered. By 1997, the company was ninety days from bankruptcy.

Microsoft, ironically, needed Apple alive. A monopoly investigation loomed, and Microsoft could point to Apple as proof of competition. Bill Gates invested $150 million in Apple in exchange for patent cross-licensing and a promise that Microsoft Office would continue on the Mac. It was humiliating and necessary.

Apple needed a new operating system. Its own efforts had failed. The company agreed to buy NeXT, the computer company Jobs had founded after leaving Apple. The acquisition was as much about Jobs as the technology. He returned as advisor, then interim CEO, then CEO.

Jobs cut immediately. He killed dozens of products, reducing Apple's lines to four: consumer and professional, desktop and portable. He convinced Jony Ive, a designer ready to quit, to stay. Together they created the iMac — a translucent, colorful all-in-one computer that looked like nothing else in the beige world of PCs. It was unapologetically different. It sold. Apple stopped bleeding.

But selling Macs to loyalists wouldn't build a company. Jobs saw something others missed: people wanted their music everywhere, and the MP3 players on the market were terrible. In 2001, Apple released the iPod. It held 1,000 songs, synced with iTunes on your Mac, and fit in your pocket. "1,000 songs in your pocket" was the entire pitch. Within two years, the iPod was selling faster than Macs.

Then Jobs broke iPod's platform. In 2003, Apple launched iTunes for Windows. Mac purists revolted, but Jobs understood: iPod's market wasn't Mac owners, it was everyone. Letting Windows users buy iPods and music from iTunes turned Apple into a music company that happened to make computers. By 2006, iPod was half of Apple's revenue.

Jobs took the insight further. The iPod was a phone without the phone. What if you added the phone? Carriers controlled mobile phones, dictating features and design. Jobs convinced AT&T to let Apple control the entire experience in exchange for exclusivity. The iPhone launched in 2007: a glass slab with no keyboard, no stylus, just a touchscreen that responded to fingers. It was iPod, phone, and internet device in one. Developers initially had no access. When Apple opened the App Store in 2008, tens of thousands of apps appeared in months. The iPhone became the computer in everyone's pocket.

The iPad followed in 2010. Critics called it a big iPhone. Jobs called it the future of computing. Both were right. Apple Watch, AirPods, services — each extended the ecosystem. Every product integrated with every other product, creating switching costs and reinforcing loyalty. The closed system that nearly killed Apple in the 1980s became its moat in the 2000s.

Jobs died in 2011. Tim Cook, his successor, lacked Jobs's product vision but excelled at operations and scale. Apple became the first trillion-dollar company, then the first two-trillion-dollar company. It prints money from services — App Store fees, subscriptions, advertising. It has more cash than most countries' GDP.

The company that almost died became the most valuable company on Earth by doing what Jobs insisted on from the start: controlling the entire experience, charging premium prices, and making products so good that people accept the constraints. The difference between 1985 and 2025 isn't the strategy. It's that the market finally valued what Apple was selling.
