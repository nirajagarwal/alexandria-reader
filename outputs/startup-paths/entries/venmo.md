# Venmo

***The social payments app that built a brand so strong its acquirer had to let it live***

> **Years:** 2009-present

In 2009, Andrew Kortina and Iqram Magdon-Ismail were roommates at the University of Pennsylvania who had stayed friends after graduation. During a weekend visit, Kortina forgot his wallet and Magdon-Ismail spotted him cash. Writing a check to pay him back felt absurd — a relic from another century. The friction of settling small debts between friends seemed like a problem worth solving.

They weren't the first to notice. PayPal had existed for a decade. Banks offered person-to-person transfers. But these solutions felt corporate, transactional, designed for commerce rather than friendship. Kortina and Magdon-Ismail saw something different: payments as a social act, something that happened between people who knew each other, with context and commentary and inside jokes.

The first version of Venmo wasn't even an app. It was an SMS service. You texted a command and it moved money. The interface was minimal because it had to be — texts could only hold so much. But that constraint became a feature. Payments were quick, casual, almost conversational. You could split a dinner check while still at the table.

Early traction came from the founders' own network — Penn students and recent graduates living in cities, splitting rent and bar tabs and concert tickets. These weren't business transactions. They were the financial exhaust of friendship. And Venmo's interface, with its social feed showing who paid whom (with optional notes), turned that exhaust into content. You could see your friends' lives through their payments. The pizza emoji. The inside joke. The late-night food delivery. It was inadvertently brilliant — payments as social media.

The growth was organic but slow. Getting both sides of a transaction onto a new platform is always hard. But the social feed created a discovery mechanism. You saw your friends using it and joined to be part of the club. By 2012, they were processing $20 million per month, almost entirely among millennials in urban areas.

That same year, Braintree — a payment processing company — acquired Venmo for $26.2 million. It seemed like a logical exit. Venmo had a product people loved but no clear path to profitability. Braintree had enterprise relationships and processing infrastructure. Kortina and Magdon-Ismail could focus on product while Braintree figured out the business model.

Then in 2013, PayPal acquired Braintree for $800 million, and Venmo came along as part of the deal. This should have been the end of the story — a small acquisition inside a larger acquisition, destined to be absorbed into the parent company's product line. PayPal already did person-to-person payments. Why run two brands?

But something unexpected happened. Venmo kept growing. By 2014, it was processing $2.4 billion in annual volume. The users were fanatically loyal. Among people under 30, "Venmo" had become a verb. "Just Venmo me" meant something PayPal never had — cultural currency.

PayPal faced a choice. Integrate Venmo and risk killing what made it special, or let it run independently and risk cannibalizing PayPal's own P2P efforts. They chose independence, but the monetization problem remained. Venmo made money on interchange fees when users paid with credit cards, but most transactions used bank accounts because Venmo made that the default. The company was popular, growing, and barely profitable.

Competitors emerged. Square launched Cash App in 2013 with a similar interface but less social emphasis. Zelle, backed by major banks, launched in 2017 with massive distribution but zero cultural resonance. Apple Pay Cash arrived the same year. Facebook added payments. Google tried multiple times. Every big tech company wanted to own person-to-person payments.

Venmo's advantage was its brand, but brands don't pay the bills. PayPal began experimenting with business models. They added a "Pay with Venmo" button for online merchants. They introduced a Venmo debit card. They tested in-app purchasing and cryptocurrency trading. Each addition risked diluting the core experience — the simple, social, friend-to-friend payment tool that users loved.

The pandemic accelerated everything. Person-to-person payments surged as people avoided cash and split delivery orders. Venmo's payment volume hit $159 billion in 2021. But Cash App, with its investing features and direct deposit capability, was growing faster and monetizing better. Zelle, boring and bank-integrated, processed even higher volume.

By 2024, Venmo had won culturally but struggled financially. It was the brand everyone knew, the verb everyone used, the app that defined social payments. But Cash App made more money per user. Zelle had more volume. And PayPal, Venmo's owner, was itself under pressure from activist investors questioning whether it should exist as an independent company.

The acquisition that was supposed to give Venmo resources and scale had instead left it in limbo. Too valuable to shut down, too hard to monetize, too risky to mess with. Kortina and Magdon-Ismail had built something genuinely new — payments that felt social rather than transactional. But "social" turned out to be better for brands than for business models.

Venmo proved that cultural adoption doesn't automatically translate to economic value. It demonstrated that being a verb matters less than being profitable. And it showed that sometimes the hardest part of innovation isn't creating something people love — it's figuring out how to make money from it before someone else does.
