# Venmo

***The social payment app that became the verb***

> **Years:** 2009-present

In 2009, Andrew Kortina and Iqram Magdon-Ismail were roommates at the University of Pennsylvania, working on a music startup that wasn't going anywhere. During a weekend visit to New York, Kortina forgot his wallet. Magdon-Ismail covered dinner. Later, the awkwardness of settling up — writing a check, finding an ATM, doing math on scraps of paper — felt absurd. They were building products for smartphones. Why did paying a friend still work like 1987?

They pivoted. The music startup died. Venmo was born as a way to text money to friends. The first version was literally SMS-based — you texted a dollar amount and recipient to a phone number, and Venmo handled the rest. It was clunky. Banks didn't make it easy. But the core insight was sound: payments between friends should feel like conversation, not commerce.

The early product had almost no users. Kortina and Magdon-Ismail seeded it themselves, splitting dinner tabs through the app and posting the transactions publicly. This wasn't just dogfooding — it was the product. Venmo's defining feature, the social feed showing who paid whom with emoji-laden captions, started as a way to make the product feel alive when almost nobody was using it. "Josh paid Sarah 🍕❤️" was content. It was proof of life.

The college demographic found it first. Splitting rent, bar tabs, Ubers before Uber had in-app splitting. Venmo spread through group houses and friend networks. The social feed became performative — inside jokes, emoji strings, deliberate vagueness. Paying someone back wasn't just a transaction; it was a minor publication. The feed was weird, but it worked. People wanted to be on the platform their friends were on. Network effects locked in fast.

Venmo's growth had one problem: it was burning money. Literally. Every transaction cost them more than they made. They charged nothing to send money between bank accounts and ate the interchange fees when users funded payments with credit cards. The business model was "get big, figure it out later." By 2012, they needed an exit or they'd die.

Braintree, a payment processor, bought Venmo for $26.2 million. It wasn't a victory lap. Braintree wanted the user base and the mobile-first DNA. For Kortina and Magdon-Ismail, it was survival. A year later, PayPal acquired Braintree for $800 million, largely because Venmo came with it. PayPal had missed mobile. Venmo was the way back in.

Under PayPal, Venmo exploded. The parent company had infrastructure, compliance expertise, and patience for losses while the platform scaled. By 2017, Venmo was processing $17.6 billion in payment volume annually. It became a verb. "Venmo me" entered the lexicon. PayPal finally monetized it — merchant acceptance, instant transfer fees, a debit card. The social feed remained, a fossil of the early insight that payments could be content.

But Venmo never fully escaped its original limitation: it only worked well between friends. Attempts to expand into commerce felt forced. Merchants didn't need another payment rail. Venmo's brand was casual, social, emoji-laden — not the gravitas of "real" transactions. Competitors like Zelle, launched by a consortium of banks, offered instant transfers without leaving your bank app. Zelle had no social feed, no brand cachet, and processed more volume than Venmo by 2018. Turns out most people just wanted to move money, not perform it.

Venmo's path reveals a recurring startup trap: building something people love that doesn't obviously make money. The social feed was brilliant product design and terrible business strategy. It created stickiness but not revenue. The peer-to-peer model had brutal unit economics. Venmo won by being delightful and then getting acquired by someone who could absorb the losses long enough to extract value.

Today, Venmo is profitable, massive, and culturally entrenched. PayPal reports it separately in earnings. It generated over $1 billion in revenue in 2023. But it's a feature of the PayPal empire, not an independent company. The founders left years ago. The outcome wasn't failure — it was subsumption. Venmo succeeded by becoming indispensable, then becoming owned.

The company that made splitting a pizza feel like a social act became a utility. The feed still exists, but nobody checks it anymore. The insight was right: payments should feel effortless. The business was right: scale first, monetize later. The path was right: get acquired before you run out of money. What Venmo couldn't do was become PayPal itself. It became the thing PayPal bought to stay relevant. That's a kind of victory, even if it's not the one the founders imagined in 2009, writing checks for dinner and thinking there had to be a better way.
