# GeoCities

***The city that taught a generation to build on the web***

> **Years:** 1994-2009

In 1994, the web was a void. Most people couldn't imagine what to do with it. David Bohnett and John Rezner saw something different: not a library of documents, but a place where ordinary people could stake a claim. They called it Beverly Hills Internet, then quickly renamed it GeoCities — digital neighborhoods where anyone could build a home.

The metaphor was literal. Users didn't just get web hosting. They joined themed "neighborhoods." Technology enthusiasts went to SiliconValley. Artists claimed SoHo. Personal pages filled Heartland. Each neighborhood had its own aesthetic, its own culture. The taxonomy was crude but powerful — it gave structure to chaos, community to isolation.

The product was radical in its simplicity. You didn't need to know HTML, though the tool would teach you if you wanted to learn. You didn't need money — the basic service was free, supported by banner ads. You just needed something to say. GeoCities provided 2MB of server space and a WYSIWYG editor. Millions of people, most of whom had never considered themselves publishers, suddenly were.

What they built was glorious and terrible. Flashing text. Tiled backgrounds. Guestbooks. Hit counters. MIDI files playing unbidden. GIF animations of flames, stars, dancing babies. Every rule of design violated simultaneously. Every page a different experiment in what the web could be.

It looked like chaos. It was chaos. But it was generative chaos. GeoCities pages were where people learned HTML by viewing source and copying code. Where teenagers built shrines to their favorite bands. Where hobbyists documented their obsessions. Where small businesses got their first online presence. The barrier to publishing had been a printing press, a broadcast license, a record deal. Now it was nothing.

The growth was exponential. By 1997, GeoCities was the fifth most visited site on the web. By 1998, it had 3.5 million users — "homesteaders" — publishing 2 million pages. Venture capital arrived. The company went public in August 1998, in the first flush of dot-com mania. The IPO raised $66 million. The stock price tripled on the first day.

Then came the first crisis. In June 1999, GeoCities changed its terms of service. The new language claimed ownership over everything users posted — text, photos, code. The reaction was instant and furious. Homesteaders organized. They built protest sites on GeoCities' own servers. They coordinated boycotts. GeoCities backed down within days, apologizing and reverting the terms. But the damage was done. Trust, once broken, doesn't fully repair.

A month later, Yahoo came calling. The deal was $3.57 billion in stock — the third-largest acquisition in internet history at that point. Yahoo saw GeoCities as a content engine, a community play, a traffic generator. They had 19 million users posting content that brought in visitors that saw ads. The math seemed obvious.

But Yahoo didn't understand what they'd bought. GeoCities wasn't a product — it was a culture. The neighborhoods mattered. The creative anarchy mattered. Yahoo saw inefficiency and ugliness. They saw wasted server space on pages with twelve visitors. They saw brand risk in user-generated chaos.

Slowly, Yahoo began to optimize. They changed the URLs, breaking millions of links across the web. They imposed stricter rules. They pushed users toward Yahoo-branded templates. They turned neighborhoods into directories. The homesteaders began to leave. Not in a flood — it was too slow for that — but in a steady, quiet exodus. They went to Blogger, to LiveJournal, to self-hosted WordPress blogs. Anywhere that felt less corporate, less controlled.

By the mid-2000s, GeoCities was a ghost town. The pages remained, but few were updated. New users stopped coming. Yahoo kept the lights on, but without investment or attention. The web had moved on to Web 2.0 — to platforms with real-time updates, social graphs, algorithmic feeds. Static HTML pages in themed neighborhoods felt ancient.

In April 2009, Yahoo announced they would shut down GeoCities. The closure came in stages — Japan's version survived until 2019 — but for most of the world, it ended on October 26, 2009. Preservationists raced to archive what they could. The Archive Team pulled down terabytes of data. Individual users scrambled to save their pages. Much was lost anyway.

The numbers were staggering. At its peak, GeoCities hosted 38 million pages. It was the third most-visited site on the web. And then it vanished, taking with it a vast, messy archive of late-90s internet culture — the first draft of what people thought the web could be.

GeoCities failed because Yahoo treated it as a product when it was a platform, as content when it was expression, as real estate when it was community. But it succeeded first, magnificently, because it gave millions of people their first experience of building something on the internet. It was training wheels for a generation of web creators.

The aesthetic died. Flashing text and tiled backgrounds became punchlines. But the idea survived, mutated, spread. GeoCities taught people that the web wasn't something you consumed — it was something you made. That lesson outlived the platform by decades.
