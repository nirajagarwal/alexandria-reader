# Nubank

***The card that conquered Latin America by saying no to everyone else***

> **Years:** 2013-present

David Vélez arrived in São Paulo in 2013 with a problem that wasn't his own. The Colombian entrepreneur had spent years at Sequoia Capital watching Latin American startups struggle with payments and banking infrastructure. But the real revelation came when he tried to open a bank account in Brazil. The process took forty-five days. When his credit card application was denied — despite Stanford degrees and a track record in finance — he understood the market's fundamental flaw. Brazil's banking system wasn't just bad at serving customers. It was designed to exclude them.

Brazil's banking oligopoly was extractive by structure. Five banks controlled 85% of assets. They charged the highest interest rates and fees in the world, not despite having 55 million unbanked Brazilians, but because of it. Credit cards carried annual fees of $50 in a country where millions earned less than $400 per month. Opening an account required proof of income, proof of residence, and the patience to wait in bank branches that seemed engineered to make you leave. The banks could operate this way because they had no competition and no incentive to change.

Vélez saw what incumbents saw as a feature — exclusion — as a product opportunity. With co-founders Cristina Junqueira and Edward Wible, he launched Nubank around a single product: a credit card with no annual fee, managed entirely through a smartphone app. No branches. No bureaucracy. Apply in minutes, approved in hours, card delivered in days. The signature purple card arrived in an envelope that said "Bye bye, bureaucracy."

The product was simple. The distribution model was radical. Nubank launched with a waiting list. In a market where banks begged people to take credit cards, Nubank made you wait for an invitation. Scarcity as strategy. Early adopters became evangelists. They posted unboxing videos of purple cards like they were Apple products. They invited friends. Within months, the waiting list hit 100,000.

The banks noticed but didn't react. A credit card company with no branches, run by foreigners, targeting customers the banks had already decided weren't worth serving? Let them learn the hard way. Brazil's banking regulations were labyrinthine, designed by and for incumbents. The credit risk models assumed everyone without formal employment was high-risk. Nubank would lose money on defaults and discover why banking in Brazil required scale, lobbying power, and acceptance of how things worked.

Nubank did discover losses. But they also discovered something the banks had never bothered to learn: millions of Brazilians weren't high-risk because they were poor. They were excluded because the banks' underwriting models didn't know how to evaluate them. Nubank built new models using mobile data, payment patterns, and social signals. They underwrote risk the way the customer lived — digitally, informally, but predictably. Default rates came in below projections. The customer base exploded.

By 2015, Nubank had a million customers. By 2017, five million. The banks finally noticed. They launched their own digital offerings, slashed fees, improved apps. Too late. Nubank's advantage wasn't the technology — it was trust. Customers who'd been rejected, nickeled-and-dimed, and humiliated by banks for decades weren't coming back for a better app. They wanted revenge. Switching to Nubank wasn't just financial. It was emotional.

Vélez understood this. Customer service wasn't a cost center — it was the product. Support staff were called "Xpeers." Response times were measured in minutes, not days. When customers complained on social media, Nubank replied publicly, often with humor. When a customer got married and wanted matching purple cards for the couple, Nubank made it happen. The brand became a movement.

The expansion was methodical. Credit cards led to savings accounts, personal loans, insurance, investments. Each product launched with the same principle: take what banks made complicated and hostile, make it simple and human. By 2020, Nubank had 25 million customers in Brazil and had expanded to Mexico and Colombia. The company Vélez started in a co-working space was worth $10 billion.

The December 2021 IPO on the New York Stock Exchange valued Nubank at $41 billion, the largest Latin American offering ever. Vélez rang the bell wearing a purple hoodie. The company had 48 million customers, more than the population of Argentina. In Brazil, Nubank had become the country's fourth-largest financial institution by customer count, despite having no branches. One in three adult Brazilians used Nubank.

The legacy banks still don't understand what happened. They point to Nubank's slim margins, note it took years to reach profitability, observe that serving the poor is hard. They're right on the facts but wrong on the lesson. Nubank didn't win by being more efficient at banking. They won by understanding that in markets where incumbents optimize for extraction, customers will flock to anyone who treats them like humans. The product wasn't the card. It was respect.

Today, Nubank operates across five countries with over 100 million customers. The purple card has become so ubiquitous in Brazil that it's featured in soap operas and funk songs. The company that started by saying "Bye bye, bureaucracy" demonstrated something Silicon Valley often forgets: the biggest opportunities aren't always in inventing new technology. Sometimes they're in taking technology that already exists and using it to serve people everyone else decided weren't worth serving.
