# Nubank

***The credit card that became a bank — by refusing to act like one***

> **Years:** 2013-present

In 2013, Brazil had one of the world's most profitable and least loved banking systems. Five major banks controlled 80% of the market. The average credit card interest rate hovered around 200% per year. Opening a bank account required hours of paperwork and a branch visit. Customer service meant waiting on hold, then waiting in line, then being told to come back tomorrow.

David Vélez, a Colombian venture capitalist at Sequoia, kept hearing the same complaint from entrepreneurs across Latin America: dealing with banks was impossible. Not difficult — impossible. Starting a business meant navigating bureaucracy designed to say no. He decided someone should fix it. When he couldn't find that someone, he became them.

Vélez recruited Cristina Junqueira, a former Itaú executive who understood the industry's dysfunction from inside, and Edward Wible, an engineer who had built backend systems at Amazon. They raised $2 million and set out to build a bank. Except they couldn't, because Brazil didn't issue new banking licenses. So they started with a credit card.

The first version of Nubank was radically simple: a purple Mastercard managed entirely through an app. No annual fee. No branch visits. No phone menu labyrinth. Interest rates still high by global standards but transparent and lower than incumbents. Customer service through in-app chat that actually responded. The card arrived by mail in a sleek purple package that felt more like receiving an iPhone than a financial product.

Launch was by invitation only. Not as a marketing tactic — they literally couldn't handle volume. Brazilian financial regulation required every single credit application to be reviewed by a human at a traditional bank partner. Nubank's team sat with stacks of applications, manually entering data into archaic banking systems. They processed hundreds of applications per day when they needed thousands. The waitlist grew to 100,000.

The incumbents barely noticed. A credit card for people with good credit who wanted better service? That was the profitable segment everyone already served. The real breakthrough came when Nubank started approving people the big banks rejected — not the unbanked, but the underbanked. People with informal income. People without extensive credit histories. People in neighborhoods where branches didn't bother opening.

Nubank's data science team built approval models from scratch, incorporating signals traditional banks ignored: phone payment history, utility bills, even how someone filled out the application. They weren't being charitable. They realized incumbent banks were leaving money on the table because their models optimized for avoiding defaults rather than identifying good risks everyone else missed.

By 2016, Nubank had 1.5 million customers and raised $150 million led by Sequoia and Kaszek. They finally secured a banking license — not by waiting for regulators to grant one, but by acquiring a small licensed institution. Suddenly the credit card company could offer checking accounts, savings, personal loans, investments. Each product stripped to its essence, delivered through the app, explained in plain Portuguese instead of banking jargon.

The growth curve became vertical. 5 million customers in 2017. 12 million in 2018. 20 million in 2019. Nubank wasn't just growing — it was becoming a category. In Portuguese, people stopped saying "I have a Nubank credit card." They said "I have a Nubank." The product name became the verb. The purple card became ubiquitous in São Paulo and Rio, then spread to cities the big banks considered secondary markets.

Traditional banks finally responded. They launched apps. They reduced fees. They tried to match Nubank's service. But they couldn't match the culture. Nubank's app kept improving weekly. Their customer service remained responsive. Most importantly, they kept saying yes when others said no. By the time incumbents started competing, Nubank had trained millions of Brazilians to expect better.

The pandemic accelerated everything. With branches closed, digital banking became necessity. Nubank's advantage — built entirely for mobile from day one — compounded. They crossed 40 million customers in 2020. In 2021, they expanded to Mexico and Colombia, applying the same playbook to similarly concentrated banking markets.

The December 2021 IPO on the New York Stock Exchange valued Nubank at $41 billion, making it the most valuable bank in Latin America despite having launched just eight years earlier. Sequoia's early bet became one of venture capital's most successful returns. Vélez, Junqueira, and Wible became billionaires. More than 70 Nubank employees became millionaires.

But the story isn't just growth metrics. By 2024, with over 100 million customers across three countries, Nubank had fundamentally altered what people expected from financial services in Latin America. Competitors couldn't ignore mobile anymore. They couldn't ignore customer service. They couldn't ignore the millions of people traditional models excluded.

The purple card succeeded because it solved the real problem. Everyone thought Latin American banking was bad because the region was poor. Nubank proved the region was underserved because the banks were bad. They made money by treating customers better, charging less, and approving more people. The insight wasn't technological. It was human. People would switch banks if someone made it easy and treated them with respect.

The company that started because getting a credit card was too hard became the bank for a generation that inherited dysfunction and chose to leave it behind.
