# Slack

***The accidental product that became the fastest-growing business application in history***

> **Years:** 2013–2021

Stewart Butterfield had already failed once at making a successful game company. Glitch, his second attempt at building a massively multiplayer online game, shut down in November 2012 after two years of development and $17 million in funding. The game itself was quirky and beautiful — players collaborated to build a whimsical world — but it never found enough players to sustain itself. Butterfield and his team had to lay off most of their employees and return money to investors.

But during Glitch's development, the team had built something else: an internal communication tool to coordinate their remote and distributed workforce. Instead of relying on email and IRC, they created a system that organized conversations into channels, integrated with other tools, and made everything searchable. After shutting down the game, Butterfield and three co-founders — Eric Costello, Cal Henderson, and Serguei Mourachov — looked at what they had built for themselves and saw a different opportunity.

This was Butterfield's second time extracting a product from a failed game. His previous startup, Ludicorp, had been building an online game called Game Neverending when the team realized their photo-sharing feature was more valuable than the game itself. That feature became Flickr, which Yahoo acquired in 2005 for $35 million. Now he was doing it again.

In February 2013, the team incorporated Tiny Speck — the company behind Glitch — as Slack Technologies. The name was a backronym: "Searchable Log of All Conversation and Knowledge." They spent most of 2013 refining the product, using it themselves, and testing it with a small group of friendly companies. The core insight was simple but profound: email was designed for correspondence between individuals, not for the way modern teams actually worked. Slack organized communication around topics, projects, and teams rather than chronological threads in an inbox.

The product launched publicly in February 2014. Growth was immediate and viral. Companies discovered Slack through word-of-mouth, adopted it team-by-team, and spread it throughout their organizations. Within 24 hours of launch, Slack had 8,000 sign-ups. After three months, they had 60,000 daily active users. By the end of 2014, they had raised $180 million in funding at a $1.12 billion valuation — less than two years after shutting down Glitch.

The timing was perfect. Remote work was increasing, distributed teams were becoming common, and the consumerization of enterprise software meant employees expected their work tools to be as polished as their personal apps. Slack delivered on all of this. The interface was clean, the emoji reactions were delightful, the integrations were powerful, and the loading messages were charming. It felt like a consumer product that happened to be for work.

Microsoft noticed. In 2016, the company launched Microsoft Teams, a direct competitor to Slack built into Office 365. Butterfield responded by taking out a full-page ad in The New York Times welcoming Teams to the market — a cheeky move that generated enormous publicity but underestimated the threat. Microsoft had distribution advantages Slack couldn't match: Teams came bundled with Office subscriptions, IT departments already had Microsoft relationships, and enterprise buyers trusted the established vendor.

Slack continued growing rapidly through 2017 and 2018. By March 2018, they had 8 million daily active users, with 3 million paying customers. The company went public in June 2019 through a direct listing — no IPO, no roadshow, just letting existing shares trade on the New York Stock Exchange. The stock opened at $38.50, valuing the company at $23 billion.

But the Microsoft competition was grinding. Teams was growing faster, particularly in large enterprises. By July 2019, just a month after Slack's direct listing, Teams had 13 million daily active users compared to Slack's 10 million. The bundling strategy was working. Enterprises that already paid for Office 365 saw Teams as essentially free, even if Slack was technically superior.

Slack filed an antitrust complaint against Microsoft with the European Commission in July 2020, arguing that bundling Teams with Office was anti-competitive. The complaint echoed arguments from the browser wars of the 1990s. Microsoft was using its dominance in one market to crush competition in another.

The complaint changed nothing about the competitive dynamics. By April 2020, Teams had 75 million daily active users. COVID-19 had accelerated the shift to remote work, and Microsoft's enterprise relationships meant companies defaulted to Teams for their newly distributed workforces. Slack had 12.5 million daily active users at the time. The gap was widening.

In December 2020, Salesforce announced it would acquire Slack for $27.7 billion — the largest acquisition in Salesforce's history. For Butterfield and his co-founders, the sale represented both validation and pragmatism. They had built the fastest-growing business application ever, reaching a billion-dollar valuation faster than any enterprise software company before them. But they couldn't outrun Microsoft's distribution advantages alone. Salesforce offered a path to continue growing within a larger ecosystem that could match Microsoft's enterprise presence.

The acquisition closed in July 2021. Butterfield became CEO of Slack within Salesforce, reporting to Marc Benioff. The product that emerged from a failed game had become a $27 billion asset, but its independence ended where it began: extracting value from the ruins of one vision to build something else.

Slack succeeded by being early to see how workplace communication needed to change, executing brilliantly on that vision, and riding the wave of remote work's normalization. It failed to become an independent giant because bundling still matters in enterprise software, and Microsoft owned the bundle. The path from gaming failure to communication success to corporate acquisition reveals both the power of pivoting and the limits of even the fastest-growing startups when facing entrenched platform advantages.
