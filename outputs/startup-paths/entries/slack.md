# Slack

***The accidental pivot that ate email***

> **Years:** 2013-2020 (acquired)

Stewart Butterfield had done this before. In 2004, his online game *Game Neverending* had failed, but while building it, his team created a photo-sharing tool they needed internally. That tool became Flickr, which Yahoo acquired for roughly $25 million in 2005. Butterfield stayed at Yahoo for a few years, then left to try again.

In 2009, he founded Tiny Speck to build *Glitch*, a whimsical multiplayer game set in a surreal world. The game had devoted fans but never found mainstream appeal. By 2012, the money was running out. Butterfield faced the same question he'd faced eight years earlier: what do we salvage from the wreckage?

The answer, again, was the internal tool. While building *Glitch*, the distributed team had created a communications system they actually liked using. It organized conversations into channels, integrated with other tools, made history searchable, and felt fast. Traditional chat felt like shouting into a void. Email was an overflowing inbox. This felt like something else — ambient awareness without chaos.

In August 2012, Tiny Speck shut down *Glitch* and refunded remaining subscription fees. Butterfield told the team they were pivoting to enterprise software. The gaming veterans — artists, designers, game engineers — would now build a product for workplace communication. Several people quit. The rest rewrote the pitch deck.

Butterfield understood that successful enterprise tools spread bottom-up, not top-down. Salesforce had sold to executives. Dropbox had spread through individual users. Butterfield wanted Slack to spread like Dropbox but monetize like Salesforce. The product had to be so obviously better that people would install it without permission, but valuable enough that companies would eventually pay.

The name "Slack" came from "Searchable Log of All Conversation and Knowledge." The team spent months refining not just features but feel. Messages appeared instantly. Emoji reactions added lightweight expression. Integrations let people work inside Slack instead of switching between tools. The design was playful without being childish — a hard balance for enterprise software.

They launched in August 2013 with a preview release. No press coverage. Just invitations to friends and a few companies. Word spread fast. Tech companies, especially distributed teams, signed up immediately. The growth curve was steeper than anything Butterfield had seen at Flickr.

Six months after launch, Slack announced an $11 million Series A led by Andreessen Horowitz and a concurrent $31 million Series B led by Accel and Spark. The company had 15,000 daily active users. By October 2014, it had 285,000. By April 2015, it crossed one million.

The metrics that mattered weren't just signups but engagement. Teams that adopted Slack replaced email for internal communication within weeks. Daily active users represented 93% of registered users — retention numbers more typical of consumer social networks than enterprise software. People checked Slack compulsively. Some worried it was *too* addictive.

Competition emerged from every direction. Microsoft had Skype and Lync. Facebook launched Workplace. Google built Hangouts Chat. Atlassian had HipChat, then bought Stride. All were credible companies with distribution advantages. None gained traction against Slack.

The core problem was that Slack wasn't one feature. It was a thousand details that worked together. Fast load times. Good search. Thoughtful notifications. Keyboard shortcuts that made sense. An API that third-party developers actually wanted to use. Competitors could copy features but not the accumulated feel of rightness.

Microsoft was different. In 2017, they launched Teams, bundled free with Office 365. Slack took out a full-page ad in *The New York Times* congratulating Microsoft on the launch and offering advice: "Empathy, craftsmanship and design aren't just buzzwords for us." The ad went viral. It also revealed anxiety.

Teams grew slowly at first, then faster. Microsoft's distribution was overwhelming — every enterprise already paid for Office, so Teams was "free." IT departments preferred fewer vendors. Slack's growth continued, but the competitive dynamic had shifted. They were no longer building market share against weak substitutes. They were fighting a war of attrition against infinite capital.

Slack went public through a direct listing in June 2019, valuing the company at roughly $20 billion. Wall Street focused immediately on Microsoft. Could Slack grow fast enough to justify the valuation while Teams was bundled with a product everyone already bought? The financials were strong — growth, retention, expansion revenue — but the narrative was "Slack versus Microsoft." Microsoft was enormous and patient.

Behind the growth numbers, another question loomed: what *was* Slack? A messaging app? A collaboration platform? An operating system for work? The company added video calling, screen sharing, workflow automation. Each addition made sense individually but created confusion about identity. Email had been simple. Slack was becoming everything.

In December 2020, Salesforce announced it would acquire Slack for $27.7 billion — the largest software acquisition ever at the time. The deal made strategic sense. Salesforce owned customer data. Slack owned employee communication. Together, they could own more of the enterprise. But the price revealed something else: independent Slack couldn't outspend Microsoft indefinitely.

Butterfield stayed to run Slack inside Salesforce. The product continued growing, now bundled with Salesforce's offerings. The startup that had accidentally spawned from a failed game, that had grown faster than almost any enterprise company in history, that had genuinely changed how millions of people worked, had found its exit.

The irony was perfect. Butterfield had built two companies from the ruins of games. Flickr had sold for $25 million and slowly withered inside Yahoo. Slack sold for a thousand times more and became part of something larger. Both times, he had created something people loved by trying to build something else.

The pivot had worked. The question was whether the ending was triumph or escape.
