# Enron

***The accounting fraud that became synonymous with corporate collapse***

> **Years:** 1985–2001

In 1985, Kenneth Lay merged two natural gas pipeline companies to form Enron. The timing seemed perfect: deregulation was sweeping through American energy markets, and Lay, a former federal energy regulator, knew how to work the system. The company would transport natural gas through thousands of miles of pipeline. Steady, predictable, boring—the kind of infrastructure business that pays reliable dividends and puts executives to sleep.

Jeffrey Skilling had other ideas. A McKinsey consultant who studied Enron's business, Skilling saw pipelines as primitive. The real opportunity was creating markets. In 1990, Lay brought him in to launch Enron Finance Corp, which would trade natural gas like commodities traders moved pork bellies and wheat. Instead of owning assets, Enron would be an intermediary, matching buyers and sellers, taking spreads, creating liquidity where none existed.

Skilling convinced Lay to adopt mark-to-market accounting—a technique banks used where you book the projected future profits of a deal immediately, rather than recognizing revenue as it actually comes in. Sign a twenty-year gas supply contract, run your model, and record the net present value of all those future profits today. For a company built on long-term contracts, it transformed financial statements overnight. Revenues exploded. Wall Street loved it. Skilling became president and COO.

The business seemed brilliant. Enron would create markets in anything: electricity, bandwidth, weather derivatives, advertising time. You had surplus capacity in anything? Enron would build a trading platform. They launched Enron Online in 1999, an electronic marketplace that became one of the world's largest e-commerce sites. At its peak, Enron traded $2.5 billion in commodities daily.

The culture reflected Skilling's philosophy. He instituted rank-and-yank: employees rated on a forced curve, bottom 15% fired annually. Traders made fortunes. The Houston headquarters became a monument to ambition—traders on trading floors, glass and steel architecture, contemporary art in the lobbies. Fortune named Enron "America's Most Innovative Company" six years straight.

But mark-to-market created a perverse incentive: deals mattered more than their actual performance. Book the revenue now, worry about delivery later. Traders learned to game their own models. Assume high future prices. Assume low future costs. Assume the deal performs perfectly for twenty years. The accounting department became a profit center.

Andrew Fastow, the CFO, took it further. He created hundreds of special purpose entities—off-balance-sheet partnerships that existed in accounting limbo. These partnerships would buy Enron assets, often at inflated prices, removing debt from Enron's balance sheet while booking gains. Fastow named them after Star Wars characters: Chewbacca, Jedi, Raptor. He personally managed some, earning millions in management fees while employed as Enron's CFO—an obvious conflict the board approved.

The SPEs became a shell game. Enron would transfer underperforming assets to them, booking profits. The partnerships needed equity to remain off-balance-sheet, so Enron guaranteed them with its own stock. As long as Enron's stock price rose, the scheme held. Fastow created partnerships to hedge other partnerships. The whole structure was collateralized by Enron stock and faith in Enron's future.

The California energy crisis of 2000–2001 revealed the culture's rot. Enron traders manipulated electricity markets, creating artificial shortages, driving up prices. Internal memos later showed them joking about "stealing money from California grandmothers." They created strategies with names like "Fat Boy" and "Death Star." Rolling blackouts hit the state while Enron posted record profits.

In early 2001, Skilling became CEO. Lay moved to chairman. But cracks appeared. An executive named Sherron Watkins sent Lay an anonymous memo warning that Enron would "implode in a wave of accounting scandals." Analysts started asking questions about the SPEs on quarterly calls. Skilling grew defensive, famously calling one "an asshole" on a conference call.

In August 2001, Skilling abruptly resigned, citing personal reasons. The stock, which had hit $90, was trading in the $40s. Lay reassured investors, bought stock himself. But in October, Enron disclosed a $638 million third-quarter loss and a $1.2 billion reduction in shareholder equity related to the partnerships. The SEC opened an inquiry.

The credit rating agencies, suddenly attentive, began downgrading Enron's debt. This triggered clauses in the SPE agreements requiring Enron to post more collateral or issue more stock. Which lowered the stock price. Which triggered more collateral requirements. The death spiral accelerated.

Banks stopped lending. Trading partners stopped trading—Enron's business depended on counterparty confidence. In November, Dynegy agreed to acquire Enron for $8 billion, then backed out after examining the books. On December 2, 2001, Enron filed for bankruptcy—the largest in American history at the time. The stock, once $90, was pennies.

Twenty thousand employees lost jobs. Many had retirement accounts heavily invested in Enron stock, encouraged by management even as executives sold their own shares. Arthur Andersen, Enron's accounting firm, was convicted of obstruction for shredding documents and effectively dissolved. Congress passed Sarbanes-Oxley, reshaping corporate governance and accounting oversight.

Skilling got twenty-four years (later reduced). Fastow got six. Lay was convicted but died before sentencing. The company that revolutionized energy trading became a case study in fraud.

What killed Enron wasn't bad luck or market forces. It was a culture that rewarded appearance over substance, that confused projections with reality, that turned accounting into storytelling. Mark-to-market let them book future profits. The SPEs let them hide present losses. As long as the stock price rose, reality could be deferred. When it fell, there was nothing underneath.
