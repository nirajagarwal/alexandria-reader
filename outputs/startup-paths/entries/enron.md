# Enron

***The fraud that redefined corporate America***

> **Years:** 1985-2001

In 1985, Kenneth Lay merged two natural gas pipeline companies to create Enron. It was a defensive move—deregulation was upending the energy business, and scale mattered. The new company owned thousands of miles of pipeline infrastructure across America. It was solid, boring, predictable. Within fifteen years, it would be neither solid nor boring, and its collapse would send shockwaves through every boardroom in the country.

The transformation began with Jeffrey Skilling, a McKinsey consultant who proposed that Enron could do more than move gas through pipes. It could create markets. Deregulation meant energy was becoming a commodity that could be traded like pork bellies or Treasury bonds. Skilling convinced Lay that Enron should become an energy trader—buying and selling gas, electricity, even bandwidth, capturing spreads and arbitraging price differences across markets.

Lay hired Skilling in 1990. Within a year, Enron had created a Gas Bank, essentially becoming a financial intermediary for energy. Producers could lock in prices. Buyers could hedge risk. Enron sat in the middle, collecting fees and taking positions. The business required sophisticated modeling and aggressive trading. It also required creative accounting.

That's where Andrew Fastow came in. Hired as CFO in 1998, Fastow was a deal-maker who understood how to structure transactions that maximized reported earnings while minimizing reported debt. The key was mark-to-market accounting. Unlike traditional utilities that recognized revenue as they delivered energy, Enron could book the entire projected profit from a long-term contract the moment it was signed. A twenty-year power deal worth $50 million in projected profits? Book $50 million today.

The problem: those projections were often wildly optimistic. The bigger problem: Enron started creating projections specifically to justify the numbers it wanted to report.

Meanwhile, the company was expanding beyond energy trading into everything—water, broadband, weather derivatives. Each new venture required capital. Each venture needed to show profits to justify the stock price. And the stock price was everything. Enron's compensation system was built on stock options. The company's ability to borrow hinged on its credit rating, which hinged on its reported earnings and debt levels.

Fastow engineered a solution: special purpose entities. These were separate companies—with names like Chewco, Jedi, and Raptors—that Enron could use to move debt off its balance sheet and park underperforming assets. The entities would buy Enron assets at inflated prices, booking immediate gains for Enron. They were supposedly independent, but Fastow himself secretly controlled many of them and was making millions in fees.

By 2000, Enron was trading at sixty times earnings. Analysts didn't understand the business model—the company's financial statements were Byzantine—but Wall Street loved the growth. Revenue had exploded from $13 billion in 1996 to over $100 billion in 2000. The company was hailed as the model for the New Economy. Fortune named it "America's Most Innovative Company" six years running.

Inside, executives knew. Traders were manipulating California's newly deregulated electricity market, creating artificial shortages that caused rolling blackouts. Accountants were booking revenue on deals that existed only on paper. Fastow's entities were propped up with Enron stock—a circular arrangement that would collapse if the stock price fell. Executives were selling shares while publicly proclaiming confidence.

In August 2001, Skilling abruptly resigned. He claimed personal reasons. The stock price began falling. That same month, an Enron vice president named Sherron Watkins sent Lay an anonymous memo warning that the company might "implode in a wave of accounting scandals."

In October, Enron announced a $638 million loss and a $1.2 billion reduction in shareholder equity related to Fastow's partnerships. The SEC launched an investigation. Fastow was fired. Credit agencies downgraded Enron's debt to junk status. The company's trading partners demanded cash collateral Enron didn't have.

On December 2, 2001, Enron filed for bankruptcy. It was the largest corporate bankruptcy in American history. Twenty thousand employees lost their jobs and much of their retirement savings, which had been heavily invested in Enron stock. Shareholders lost $74 billion. Arthur Andersen, Enron's accounting firm, collapsed after being convicted of obstruction of justice for shredding documents.

The scandal revealed how completely the checks had failed. Auditors had approved fraudulent accounting. Board members had waived conflict-of-interest rules to allow Fastow's schemes. Wall Street analysts had maintained buy ratings while privately expressing doubts. Credit agencies had rated junk as investment-grade.

Skilling and Lay were convicted of fraud and conspiracy. Lay died before sentencing. Skilling served twelve years. Fastow cooperated with prosecutors and served six. But the real sentence was collective—Congress passed the Sarbanes-Oxley Act, imposing new controls on corporate governance and auditing. The era of trust-me accounting was over.

What made Enron different from ordinary fraud was the scale and sophistication. This wasn't a rogue trader or a desperate CEO cooking the books. It was systematic, elaborate, and sustained—a decade-long construction of a phantom company that looked real enough to fool everyone who was supposed to be watching. The lesson wasn't just that fraud happens. It was that institutions designed to prevent fraud had become complicit in enabling it.
