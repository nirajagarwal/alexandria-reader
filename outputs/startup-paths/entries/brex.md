# Brex

***Two Stanford dropouts couldn't get a credit card — so they built a bank for startups***

> **Years:** 2017-present

Henrique Dubugras and Pedro Franceschi were nineteen years old when they sold their first company, a Brazilian payments processor called Pagar.me, for an undisclosed sum in 2016. They moved to Stanford to start computer science degrees they had no intention of finishing. Within months, they were building their next company. The problem was absurdly personal: as international students with no U.S. credit history, they couldn't get a credit card. Neither could any of their friends starting companies. The traditional banking system looked at a startup with venture capital in the bank and saw nothing — no income history, no assets to collateralize, just risk.

Corporate credit cards in 2017 still operated on logic from the 1970s. Banks evaluated businesses the way they evaluated individuals: through personal guarantees, credit scores, and years of financial history. For a founder who'd just raised a seed round, this meant either putting the card in their own name — exposing personal credit to company risk — or spending weeks on paperwork that often ended in rejection. The entire model assumed businesses grew slowly and predictably. Venture-backed startups did neither.

Dubugras and Franceschi saw the disconnect immediately. A startup with two million dollars from Sequoia had demonstrably more ability to pay a credit card bill than a twenty-two-year-old with thirty thousand in student loans. But the banking system couldn't see it. Traditional underwriting looked backward at history. Startups were about forward momentum. There was a business to be built in that gap.

They dropped out of Stanford in 2017 — Dubugras had attended for one quarter, Franceschi for two — and started Brex. The insight was simple: underwrite based on cash in the bank, not credit history. If a company had raised venture capital, that money was visible. Brex could assess risk by looking at burn rate and runway rather than personal credit scores. They could issue cards instantly to founders who'd been rejected everywhere else.

The technical challenge was everything else. They needed a bank charter or a banking partner. They needed compliance infrastructure. They needed card processing relationships. Most critically, they needed to convince VCs to trust two teenagers who'd never worked in financial services. What they had was Y Combinator. Paul Graham's network opened doors that would have stayed closed to unknown Brazilian founders, no matter how impressive their first exit.

Brex launched in June 2018 with a product aimed at the YC portfolio. The value proposition was almost too good: instant approval, higher limits than traditional cards, no personal guarantee required. For startups, it solved an immediate pain point. For Brex, it created a customer base with unusual characteristics. These companies were growing fast, spending aggressively on software and ads, and often failing spectacularly. Traditional banking logic said this was toxic. Brex bet it could manage the risk through data.

The moat wasn't the underwriting model — that was replicable. It was distribution and product velocity. Brex integrated with startup infrastructure: accounting software, expense management tools, banking services. They added rewards programs optimized for startup spending patterns — points on AWS, not on gas stations. They built a product that felt like it was designed by founders for founders, because it was. Within a year, thousands of startups had switched.

The growth was extraordinary. By 2019, Brex had raised over $300 million at a $2.6 billion valuation. They expanded beyond startups to e-commerce companies, then to life sciences, then to small businesses more broadly. The pandemic accelerated everything. With remote work exploding, software spending soared. Brex's customer base was growing revenue faster than almost any other segment of the economy.

Then the market turned. In 2022, as venture funding dried up and startups extended runways, Brex faced a problem inherent to its model: its customers were burning less cash. Lower spend meant lower interchange revenue. Simultaneously, Brex had expanded aggressively into small business, where unit economics were fundamentally different. A corner pizzeria generated a fraction of the revenue of a venture-backed startup, with similar servicing costs.

In June 2023, Brex announced it was exiting the SMB market entirely. Thousands of small business customers received thirty-day notices that their cards would be cancelled. The backlash was immediate. Small business owners felt abandoned after being courted. The narrative flipped from scrappy disruptor to callous tech company. Brex argued the move was necessary to focus on its core market — venture-backed startups and scaling enterprises. The subtext was clear: the unit economics didn't work.

The pullback revealed something fundamental about Brex's model. The company had grown by serving a customer base with unusual characteristics: high spend, high growth, high tolerance for new financial products, and access to essentially free capital. When that capital got expensive, the customers changed behavior. When Brex tried to expand beyond that base, it discovered that most businesses weren't like startups. They spent less, grew slower, and expected products that Brex wasn't built to provide.

By 2024, Brex had stabilized around its original insight. The company was valued at over $12 billion and served thousands of high-growth companies. It had expanded into expense management, business accounts, and bill pay — building a financial operating system for startups rather than just a card. The corporate card was now the entry point to a broader relationship.

The outcome remains open. Brex proved you could build a multi-billion dollar financial services company by ignoring traditional banking wisdom and underwriting venture-backed risk. Whether that model survives sustained periods of tight venture capital remains to be seen. The company exists in symbiosis with the startup ecosystem. When startups thrive, Brex thrives. When they contract, so does Brex. Two Brazilian teenagers couldn't get a credit card, so they built a bank. The bank works brilliantly — as long as venture capital keeps flowing.
