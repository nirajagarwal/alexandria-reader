# Brex

***Two Stanford dropouts without credit histories built the corporate card for companies without credit histories***

> **Years:** 2017-present

Henrique Dubugras and Pedro Franceschi had already sold one company before they could legally drink in the United States. They founded Pagar.me, a Brazilian payments processor, as teenagers and sold it for eight figures before moving to California to attend Stanford. The plan was to get degrees and perhaps start something new. They lasted one semester.

The insight came from frustration. As international students building a VR company — their first attempt in the Valley — they couldn't get corporate credit cards. No credit history meant no cards, even though their bank accounts showed they had money. Every founder they talked to had the same problem. Startups flush with venture capital couldn't access basic financial infrastructure because they lacked the traditional metrics banks used to assess risk.

The obvious solution was to build a credit card for startups. The less obvious part was how. Traditional card issuers underwrote based on personal credit scores and years of financial history. Startups had neither. But they had something else: venture funding. Money sitting in bank accounts. Burn rates that were trackable. Investors who had already performed due diligence.

Dubugras and Franceschi spent months meeting with banks, trying to convince them to issue cards against this new model. Every bank said no. The regulatory burden was too high, the risk too uncertain, the market too small. Then they found a partner willing to take the bet: a small bank in Utah that would handle the regulatory infrastructure while Brex built the product and underwrote the risk.

They launched in 2018 with a card that approved companies in minutes based on their cash in the bank and venture funding, rather than personal credit scores. No personal guarantee required. Higher limits than traditional cards. Rewards structured for startup spending — points for rideshares and SaaS subscriptions instead of airline miles. Integration with accounting software so expenses flowed directly into ledgers.

The timing was perfect. Venture funding was flooding into startups. The companies receiving that funding were run by founders who had grown up with smartphone interfaces and expected instant gratification. Applying for a corporate card shouldn't take weeks of paperwork and credit checks. Brex's application took ten minutes.

The viral loop was built into the product. VCs recommended Brex to their portfolio companies. Those companies' employees got cards and later started their own companies and chose Brex. The addressable market expanded as venture funding grew. By the end of 2018, Brex had thousands of customers and had issued hundreds of millions in credit.

But the card was just the entry point. Every time a company swiped a Brex card, Brex learned about their spending patterns, their growth, their financial health. That data became the foundation for everything else: cash management accounts, bill pay, expense management software. Brex wasn't just issuing credit. It was building the financial operating system for startups.

The challenge was the same one every neobank faced: how to make money. Interchange fees — the percentage merchants pay on card transactions — provided revenue, but margins were thin. The real money was in interest on deposits and lending. But in 2019, interest rates were near zero, and lending to startups was exactly the risk traditional banks avoided.

Brex expanded beyond startups. In 2020, they launched products for e-commerce companies and later for life sciences and mid-market businesses. Each vertical required different underwriting models and different product features. E-commerce companies needed higher limits for inventory purchases. Life sciences companies needed longer payment terms for equipment.

The pandemic was simultaneously crisis and opportunity. Startups cut spending, which meant lower interchange revenue. But remote work accelerated digital banking adoption. Companies that might have stuck with legacy banks for inertia were suddenly reconsidering all their vendors. Brex raised money at a $7.4 billion valuation in 2021, at the peak of fintech enthusiasm.

Then the market turned. Venture funding dried up. Startups cut spending or died. The very volatility that made startups underserved by traditional banks also made them risky customers in a downturn. In 2023, Brex laid off 20% of its staff and announced it would no longer serve small startups, focusing instead on companies spending at least $400,000 annually.

The pivot was controversial. The companies Brex was abandoning were the ones it had been founded to serve. But the unit economics didn't work. Small startups generated little interchange revenue and required significant support. Mid-market companies were more stable, spent more, and needed sophisticated treasury management tools that commanded higher margins.

By 2024, Brex had navigated the transition. Revenue was growing. The product had evolved into a comprehensive financial platform — corporate cards, expense management, bill pay, travel booking, reimbursements — all integrated. The company served thousands of businesses, processing billions in annual spending.

What Brex proved was that credit history wasn't the only signal of creditworthiness. Cash in the bank, VC backing, and spending patterns could underwrite risk just as effectively. They didn't invent corporate cards, but they rebuilt them for a generation of companies that banks didn't understand and wouldn't serve.

The company founded by two Stanford dropouts without credit histories had become the bank for companies without credit histories. Until it decided those companies weren't profitable enough.
