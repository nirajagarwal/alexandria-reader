# Better Place

***The battery-swap dream that burned a billion dollars***

> **Years:** 2007-2013

In 2007, Shai Agassi walked away from SAP, where he'd been the youngest executive board member and heir apparent to the CEO position. He had a vision that made his corporate career look small: he would solve global warming by replacing every gasoline car with an electric one. Not just in one country, but everywhere. He had a model. He had governments ready to partner. He had nearly a billion dollars in funding. What he didn't have was physics on his side.

Agassi's insight was that electric cars had failed because of anxiety about range and charging time. Batteries were expensive, so cars cost too much. Charging took hours, making long trips impractical. His solution was elegant: don't make people own batteries. Make them swap them. Pull into a Better Place station, drive onto a platform, and in less time than filling a gas tank, robots would swap your depleted battery for a charged one. Batteries would stay at stations, always charging, always ready. The car would be cheap because you didn't buy the battery. You'd subscribe to the network, like a cell phone plan. Miles instead of minutes.

It was Agassi's charisma as much as the idea that opened doors. He convinced Israel to become the pilot country — small, densely populated, politically motivated to reduce oil dependence. He convinced Renault-Nissan to build a car specifically for battery swapping, the Fluence Z.E. He convinced investors including Israel Corp, HSBC, and Morgan Stanley to pour in $850 million, one of the largest venture rounds ever for a clean energy company. He convinced Denmark to be the second market. By 2009, before a single station was built, Better Place was valued at over $1 billion.

The problems started with scale. Battery swap stations weren't gas stations. They were complex automated facilities requiring robotics, inventory management, and space for dozens of batteries continuously charging. Each station cost millions. Building enough to cover a country required hundreds of millions. Better Place had committed to infrastructure before proving demand.

Then came the dependency trap. The Fluence Z.E. was the only car that worked with Better Place stations. Renault produced them slowly and priced them high. Customers saw an expensive car that could only refuel at Better Place stations — stations that didn't exist yet in most places they wanted to drive. Better Place needed customers to justify building stations. Customers needed stations to justify buying cars. Neither side moved first.

The subscription model, which looked brilliant in presentations, collapsed under scrutiny. Heavy drivers wanted unlimited miles. Light drivers didn't want to pay for a network they rarely used. The pricing structure that made sense for Better Place made sense for almost no actual customer. The company kept changing plans, confusing the market further.

Agassi ran Better Place like he was still pitching it. Offices in multiple countries, a staff that peaked around 400, partnerships that consumed resources without generating revenue. He traveled constantly, giving keynotes, meeting with governments, selling the vision. The company was spending roughly $12 million a month. By late 2011, it had installed 40 swap stations in Israel, 19 in Denmark. It had sold approximately 1,000 cars. It had burned through more than half a billion dollars.

The board pushed Agassi out in October 2012. His replacement, Evan Thornley, arrived to discover the company had weeks of cash remaining and no path to profitability visible on any timeline. He cut staff, closed Denmark, tried to focus on Israel. But the fundamental model didn't work. Swap stations served too few customers at too high a cost. The cars were too expensive and too limited. The technology never failed — the robots worked beautifully — but the business never worked.

Better Place declared bankruptcy in May 2013. Total losses exceeded $850 million. The swap stations were dismantled. The Fluence Z.E. was discontinued. A few hundred Better Place cars remained on Israeli roads, suddenly orphaned from their network, worth almost nothing.

What happened was less a failure of technology than a failure of sequencing. Agassi had tried to build the destination before proving the path. He needed a network to sell cars, but needed cars to justify the network. He needed scale to make economics work, but couldn't reach scale without economics that already worked. Tesla, which Agassi had dismissed as a toy for rich people, was proving that customers would accept slower charging if the car was desirable enough. Better Place proved that brilliant infrastructure meant nothing if the user experience didn't justify the switching cost.

The pattern is familiar: a charismatic founder, a transformative vision, capital that mistakes ambition for progress, and a fundamental unit economics problem disguised by the grandeur of the mission. Better Place wasn't ahead of its time. It was solving the wrong problem. The barrier to electric cars wasn't charging time. It was cost, performance, and charging availability. Tesla solved those. Better Place solved a problem that turned out not to matter.
