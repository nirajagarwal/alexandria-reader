# Better Place

***The billion-dollar bet on swappable batteries that couldn't outrun the charging curve***

> **Years:** 2007-2013

Shai Agassi had the kind of résumé that made investors listen. By forty, he'd risen to the top ranks of SAP, in line to become CEO of one of Europe's largest software companies. But in 2007, he walked away to pursue an idea that seemed both audacious and inevitable: he would solve electric vehicles by solving the battery problem.

The insight was elegant. Electric cars weren't taking off because batteries were expensive, had limited range, and took hours to charge. What if you didn't own the battery? What if you could swap it out in minutes, like exchanging a propane tank? Build a network of battery-swapping stations, sell the car cheap, charge subscription fees for the battery service. The model worked for mobile phones — why not vehicles?

Agassi had charisma, connections, and a pitch that landed with the force of revelation. He raised $200 million before Better Place had built anything. Investors included HSBC, Morgan Stanley, Israel Corporation, and VantagePoint Capital Partners. Shimon Peres, Israel's president, became a vocal supporter. By 2009, Better Place had raised nearly $750 million, one of the largest cleantech venture rounds in history.

Israel became the test market. Small country, short distances, a government eager to reduce oil dependence. Better Place announced partnerships with Renault-Nissan, which would build the cars. The Fluence Z.E. sedan would be the vehicle, modified to accommodate Better Place's swappable battery system. Denmark signed on as a second market.

The technology was real. Better Place built battery-swap stations that could exchange a depleted battery for a charged one in under five minutes. Robotic arms lifted the car, removed the battery from beneath, and installed a fresh one. It worked. Videos of the swap in action looked like science fiction made manifest.

But the model required density to function. A driver needed confidence that a swap station would be nearby. That meant building hundreds of stations before selling many cars. Better Place began construction across Israel. Each station cost over $500,000. The company also installed thousands of public charging points as backup. The infrastructure came first, burning cash with no revenue to offset it.

Then the car arrived. The Fluence Z.E. wasn't what Agassi had promised. It was a converted sedan, not a vehicle designed from the ground up for electric propulsion. It was expensive even with the battery subscription model. It was, frankly, boring. Early adopters wanted Teslas, not rebranded Renaults.

Tesla, meanwhile, was proving a different thesis. Elon Musk bet that battery technology would improve fast enough to make swapping unnecessary. The Model S, launched in 2012, offered over 200 miles of range and access to a growing Supercharger network. Charging times were dropping. The technology curve was steeper than Agassi had anticipated.

Better Place had locked itself into Renault. No other manufacturer wanted to redesign vehicles around Better Place's proprietary swapping system. That meant customers had one car option. Tesla owners could choose from a growing array of electric vehicles. Better Place offered a single model few people wanted.

The economics never closed. Better Place needed scale to spread infrastructure costs, but couldn't achieve scale without more cars, and couldn't offer more cars without more manufacturers, and couldn't attract manufacturers without scale. The subscription model assumed high utilization, but customers in Israel didn't drive enough miles to generate sufficient revenue. Batteries degraded slower than projected, meaning fewer swaps.

Internal tensions mounted. Agassi was a visionary CEO, not an operational one. Executives clashed over strategy. Should they expand to new markets or fix Israel first? Should they pivot to charging instead of swapping? Agassi resisted changes that would invalidate the original vision. The board forced him out in October 2012.

His replacement, Evan Thornley, inherited a company burning through $10 million a month with no path to breakeven. Better Place had sold fewer than 1,400 cars in Israel. Denmark had failed to gain traction. The company went back to investors for more capital. No one wanted in. By early 2013, Better Place had burned through nearly $850 million.

In May 2013, Better Place filed for bankruptcy. The assets sold for $12 million to a scrap dealer. The battery-swap stations were dismantled. Customers who'd bought Fluence Z.E. vehicles were left with cars dependent on an infrastructure that no longer existed.

Agassi's fundamental error was betting against the technology curve. Battery swapping made sense if batteries remained expensive, low-range, and slow to charge. But all three constraints were loosening faster than Better Place could build network effects. By the time Better Place collapsed, Tesla was selling thousands of Model S sedans that charged faster, drove farther, and didn't require proprietary infrastructure.

The model assumed the battery problem was permanent. It wasn't. It was temporary, and Better Place built a billion-dollar solution to a problem that was already disappearing. The charging curve caught them.
