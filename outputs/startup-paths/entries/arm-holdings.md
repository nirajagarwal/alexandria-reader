# Arm Holdings

***The architecture that conquered mobile by giving up control***

> **Years:** 1990-present

In 1990, twelve engineers sat in a barn in Cambridge, England, with a problem that seemed trivial compared to the computing giants of the era. Apple needed a chip for its Newton handheld device. Acorn Computers, a British firm that had built respectable machines for schools, spun out a joint venture with Apple and VLSI Technology to design it. They called it Advanced RISC Machines — ARM.

The twelve engineers had no fabrication plants. No sales force worth mentioning. What they had was a processor architecture called RISC — Reduced Instruction Set Computing — that used fewer, simpler instructions than the complex chips Intel was building. RISC wasn't new. Academic researchers had explored it for years. But Acorn's implementation was unusually elegant. More importantly, it was unusually power-efficient.

The Newton failed. Apple's handheld was too expensive, too early, too clumsy. But the ARM chip inside it was good. Really good. It sipped power while competitors guzzled it. For devices that ran on batteries, this mattered enormously. The twelve engineers in the barn had accidentally built the architecture the mobile future would need.

In 1998, ARM made a decision that would define everything that followed: they would not manufacture chips. Ever. Intel made chips. Motorola made chips. Every serious semiconductor company made chips. ARM would design processor architectures and license them. Companies could license the design, modify it, manufacture it themselves, and ARM would take a royalty on every chip sold.

The decision came partly from necessity — fabrication plants cost billions — but it transformed necessity into strategy. By licensing rather than manufacturing, ARM eliminated the conflict that kept Intel from serving everyone. Intel competed with potential customers. ARM didn't. A company could license ARM's design and not worry that ARM would undercut them next quarter with a better chip.

The model seemed questionable. Royalties were small — pennies per chip. Volume would have to be massive. In the late 1990s, the PC was king, Intel was dominant, and mobile phones were simple devices making calls. Where would the volume come from?

It came from phones. Nokia licensed ARM. Then Ericsson. Then everyone. As phones became smarter, they needed more processing power but couldn't sacrifice battery life. ARM's architecture delivered both. By the early 2000s, ARM processors were in nearly every mobile phone made. Not ARM chips — ARM designs, manufactured by dozens of companies under license.

When the iPhone launched in 2007, it ran on an ARM processor. So did Android phones. So did iPads and Kindles and fitness trackers and smart thermostats. The more devices became mobile or embedded, the more they needed ARM. The company that didn't manufacture anything became the foundation of mobile computing.

The licensing model created a flywheel. Every company that licensed ARM became invested in the ecosystem. Software written for ARM-based devices worked across platforms. Developers learned ARM. Tools improved. The architecture became standard not through monopoly but through ubiquity. Companies could compete fiercely on chip implementation while sharing the underlying architecture.

ARM went public in 1998, grew quietly through the 2000s, then exploded as smartphones devoured the world. By 2015, ARM-based chips were shipping at a rate of 15 billion per year. The company still didn't manufacture a single one.

Then came the acquisitions. In 2016, SoftBank bought ARM for $32 billion — a massive bet that ARM would power not just phones but the Internet of Things, autonomous vehicles, data centers. SoftBank's vision was intoxicating but vague. ARM continued growing, but the pressure to justify the price intensified. The licensing model remained, but strategic questions multiplied. Should ARM push into servers where Intel dominated? Should it hold tighter control over designs? SoftBank needed returns.

In 2020, Nvidia announced plans to acquire ARM for $40 billion. The deal promised to unite the dominant chip designer with the dominant GPU maker. It also threatened the open licensing model that made ARM valuable. Customers revolted. Regulators balked. China, the U.S., and Europe all raised competition concerns. In 2022, Nvidia abandoned the acquisition.

ARM went public again in 2023, this time on Nasdaq, raising $5 billion in the largest IPO of the year. The company SoftBank had bought for $32 billion was now valued at $54 billion. But the triumphant return masked deeper tensions. Apple, once ARM's partner, was designing its own ARM-based chips with less reliance on ARM's latest designs. Qualcomm faced legal battles with ARM over licensing terms. China's semiconductor ambitions complicated everything.

The fundamental model hasn't changed. ARM still doesn't manufacture chips. It still licenses designs. But the world it created is more complicated now. Data centers are experimenting with ARM-based servers, threatening Intel's last stronghold. RISC-V, an open-source architecture, offers an alternative that costs nothing to license. The geopolitical fracturing of the semiconductor industry means ARM's position as neutral Switzerland is harder to maintain.

The company that conquered mobile by refusing to control manufacturing now faces questions it could once ignore. As chips become more strategic, more geopolitical, more contested, can a licensor remain above the fray? The model that seemed weak in 1990 became dominant by 2010. Whether it remains dominant through 2030 depends on whether the forces that made it powerful — openness, neutrality, ubiquity — can survive forces that want chips to be weapons, platforms, moats.

Arm Holdings proved you could win by designing what others build. The question now is whether you can keep winning that way.
