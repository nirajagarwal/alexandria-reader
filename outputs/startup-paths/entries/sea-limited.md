# Sea Limited

***From a gaming clone to Southeast Asia's everything app***

> **Years:** 2009-present

In 2009, Forrest Li left his job at Motorola and founded Garena, a game publishing company in Singapore. The idea was straightforward: bring popular games to Southeast Asia, a fragmented market of hundreds of millions of people with no dominant digital platform. Li licensed games from Western and Chinese developers—most notably League of Legends from Riot Games—and operated them across the region. It was not innovative. It was execution in a market others overlooked.

The model worked. Southeast Asia had rising internet penetration, growing middle classes, and young populations obsessed with gaming. Garena became the region's leading game publisher by being present, localized, and competent. By 2014, it was profitable and generating hundreds of millions in revenue. Li could have stayed there: a solid regional business, generating cash, operating in the shadow of global giants.

Instead, he looked at what Tencent had done in China. Tencent began with games, then expanded into payments, e-commerce, and digital services. Li saw the same opportunity in Southeast Asia. The region had no Amazon, no Alibaba, no dominant e-commerce player. Lazada, backed by Rocket Internet, was the leader but struggled with execution and unit economics. Li believed Garena could be the foundation for something larger.

In 2015, Sea launched Shopee, an e-commerce marketplace targeting mobile-first consumers in Southeast Asia. The timing was precarious. E-commerce in the region was notoriously difficult: fragmented logistics, low credit card penetration, poor infrastructure, diverse languages and payment systems. Alibaba had just invested a billion dollars into Lazada. Amazon was circling. Shopee entered late, undercapitalized, and fighting established players.

Li's advantage was capital from Garena's gaming profits and a willingness to lose money. Shopee launched with aggressive subsidies: free shipping, seller incentives, buyer promotions. It was modeled directly on Taobao—chat-based shopping, gamified discovery, live-streaming. The app felt like social media. Competitors felt like catalogs.

The subsidies worked. Shopee gained share in Indonesia, the region's largest market. Lazada had focused on premium buyers. Shopee went after everyone, particularly lower-income consumers making their first online purchases. It was not elegant. It was a land grab.

Then Li made a second move. In 2014, Sea had launched SeaMoney, a digital wallet. By 2017, it was clear that payments were the key to locking users into the ecosystem. Shopee integrated SeaMoney into checkout. Gaming users were pushed toward the wallet. E-commerce users were offered rewards for paying with it. Slowly, Sea was building a closed loop: play games, shop online, pay digitally, all within Sea's universe.

In 2017, Sea went public on the New York Stock Exchange, raising over a billion dollars. The IPO was controversial. The company was losing hundreds of millions annually. Revenue was growing, but profitability was distant. Skeptics called it a Tencent proxy—Sea's largest shareholder was Tencent, which had taken a stake in 2010 and increased it over time. But the IPO succeeded because Li sold a story: Southeast Asia was the last major internet market up for grabs, and Sea was best positioned to own it.

The years after IPO were a controlled burn. Shopee expanded into Taiwan, Brazil, and later Latin America. It launched Shopee Pay, its rebranded wallet, across the region. It invested heavily in logistics, building fulfillment centers and last-mile delivery networks. Losses widened. In 2019, Sea lost $1.5 billion. In 2020, $1.6 billion. Investors tolerated it because growth was explosive.

The pandemic accelerated everything. E-commerce orders in Southeast Asia doubled. Shopee became the most-downloaded shopping app globally in 2020. Sea's stock soared from $40 in early 2020 to over $360 by late 2021. The company was worth more than $200 billion, making it one of the most valuable in Southeast Asia.

Then the reversal. In 2022, as interest rates rose and growth investors fled, Sea's stock collapsed. It fell 80% from its peak. The company laid off staff, shut down Shopee operations in India and Europe, and slowed expansion. The market demanded profitability. Li pivoted. He cut marketing spend, reduced subsidies, and focused on core markets. By late 2023, Shopee was profitable in Southeast Asia. SeaMoney was approaching breakeven. Garena, the original cash engine, was shrinking as pandemic gaming habits normalized, but it no longer mattered. The e-commerce business could stand on its own.

Sea's path reveals a particular kind of startup journey: the conglomerate builder in an emerging market. Li did not invent e-commerce or digital payments. He cloned what worked elsewhere and executed it in a region where infrastructure and competition were weak enough to allow a multi-vertical play. The key was patience and capital. Sea lost billions before it made money. Most startups cannot survive that. Sea survived because gaming profits funded early losses, Tencent provided credibility and capital, and public markets tolerated the burn as long as growth continued.

The outcome is unfinished. Sea is profitable but fragile. Competition from TikTok Shop, Lazada, and Tokopedia remains intense. The company has a platform across gaming, e-commerce, and payments, but it has never achieved the dominance in any single vertical that Alibaba or Amazon have. It is large, not inevitable. It won by being first and willing to lose. Whether that is enough depends on whether Southeast Asia consolidates around a single platform or fragments further. Sea bet on consolidation. The market is still deciding.
