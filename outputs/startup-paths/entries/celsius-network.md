# Celsius Network

***The crypto lender that promised yields and delivered ruin***

> **Years:** 2017-2022

In 2017, as Bitcoin surged past $10,000 for the first time, Alex Mashinsky saw an opportunity in the gap between what banks paid depositors and what crypto holders wanted. A serial entrepreneur who'd founded seven companies and claimed to have invented VoIP, Mashinsky launched Celsius Network with a revolutionary pitch: deposit your crypto, earn weekly interest payments up to 18% APY, and borrow against your holdings at rates as low as 1%. No fees. "Unbank yourself," the company proclaimed.

The model seemed elegant. Celsius would take customer deposits, lend them to institutional borrowers and DeFi protocols at higher rates, and pass most of the yield back to depositors. It was banking rebuilt for crypto — with customers as stakeholders, not products. Mashinsky positioned himself as a populist crusader against Wall Street, frequently comparing Celsius to a credit union while attacking traditional banks on his weekly YouTube AMAs.

The timing was perfect. As crypto markets exploded in 2020 and 2021, retail investors flooded into Celsius seeking returns that made traditional savings accounts look like poverty. The company grew from 50,000 users in mid-2019 to 1.7 million by early 2022. Assets under management reached $25 billion. Celsius raised over $750 million from venture capitalists including Caisse de dépôt et placement du Québec, one of Canada's largest pension funds, at a $3 billion valuation.

Behind the yields, the reality was more precarious than Celsius disclosed. The company made increasingly risky bets to generate returns. It became a major player in DeFi "yield farming," deploying customer funds across protocols with complex interdependencies. It made large uncollateralized loans to crypto hedge funds. Most dangerously, it used customer Bitcoin to mine its own CEL token, trying to prop up the price while using CEL as collateral for loans — a circular structure that worked only as long as CEL held value.

Celsius also operated under minimal regulatory oversight. While Mashinsky called it a "platform" rather than a bank, it functioned like an uninsured bank with no capital requirements, no reserve mandates, and no depositor protections. State securities regulators in New Jersey, Texas, and Alabama ordered Celsius to stop offering interest accounts, but the company continued operating in other jurisdictions.

The warnings were there. Crypto researcher Dirty Bubble Media published detailed analyses in late 2021 showing that Celsius's reported yields were mathematically impossible from legitimate lending alone. Former employees told reporters about chaotic internal operations and risky investment decisions made by a small inner circle. Short-seller reports questioned the company's solvency. Celsius dismissed all criticism as coordinated FUD — fear, uncertainty, and doubt — spread by competitors and haters.

The collapse began in May 2022 as the broader crypto market crashed. When Terra/Luna imploded, wiping out $40 billion in value, Celsius had exposure through its DeFi positions. As Bitcoin and Ethereum prices fell, the collateral backing Celsius's own loans lost value, triggering margin calls. The company faced a crisis: it couldn't meet withdrawal requests without selling assets at terrible losses, but restricting withdrawals would trigger a bank run.

On June 12, 2022, Celsius froze all customer withdrawals, citing "extreme market conditions." The announcement came via blog post on a Sunday night. Some 1.7 million users suddenly couldn't access their funds. Many had their entire life savings in Celsius. The company promised this was temporary — "a pause to stabilize liquidity."

It was over. As bankruptcy attorneys examined Celsius's books, the scale of the hole became clear: the company owed customers $4.7 billion but had only $1.8 billion in liquid assets. The rest was locked in illiquid investments, already liquidated positions, or simply gone. Celsius had a $1.2 billion deficit. Customer deposits had been used to pay earlier customer withdrawals and to maintain the illusion of solvency — classic Ponzi mechanics, though whether by design or desperation remains disputed.

Celsius filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in July 2022. The filing revealed that Mashinsky had withdrawn $10 million of his own funds from Celsius in the weeks before the freeze — transactions he later claimed were for tax payments. As customers realized their money was likely gone, Mashinsky continued his YouTube AMAs, offering vague reassurances until his resignation in September 2022.

In July 2023, Mashinsky was arrested and charged with securities fraud, commodities fraud, and wire fraud. Prosecutors alleged he had misled customers about Celsius's financial health and manipulated the price of CEL tokens. The indictment painted a picture of a company that was insolvent for much of its existence, using new deposits to fund withdrawals while Mashinsky publicly claimed the company was "operationally profitable."

Bankruptcy proceedings revealed the familiar tragedy of crypto winter: customers who thought they were getting safe yield learned they were unsecured creditors in a failed financial experiment. Some will recover pennies on the dollar through asset liquidation. Most will get nothing.

Celsius demonstrated that crypto's innovation wasn't new financial instruments — it was regulatory arbitrage. By calling deposits something else and operating outside banking law, Celsius could offer returns that traditional banks couldn't and take risks that traditional banks weren't allowed. The yields were real until they weren't. The platform worked until it didn't. And when it failed, customers discovered that "unbanking yourself" meant losing the protections that banking regulation exists to provide.
