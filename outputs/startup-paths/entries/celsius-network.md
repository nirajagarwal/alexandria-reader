# Celsius Network

***The crypto bank that promised safety and delivered catastrophe***

> **Years:** 2017-2022

Alex Mashinsky had sold companies before. VoIP pioneer, serial entrepreneur, veteran of multiple exits. When he turned to cryptocurrency in 2017, he brought the confidence of someone who had played the game and won. His pitch for Celsius Network was seductive in its simplicity: a bank for the crypto age, where you deposited your Bitcoin or Ethereum and earned yields that made traditional savings accounts look like jokes. Eight percent, ten percent, sometimes higher. No fees. Weekly payouts. Your crypto working for you.

The tagline said it all: "Unbank yourself."

Mashinsky positioned Celsius as the antithesis of traditional finance. Banks paid you nothing and charged for everything. Celsius would flip the model — 80% of revenue to depositors, 20% to the company. It was populist, anti-establishment, perfectly pitched for the crypto true believers who saw themselves as revolutionaries dismantling the old order. Mashinsky became a fixture on Twitter and YouTube, preaching the gospel of decentralized finance while building a centralized institution that controlled billions in customer assets.

The model appeared elegant. Customers deposited crypto. Celsius lent it out to institutional borrowers at higher rates, pocketed the spread, and passed most of it back to depositors. Basic banking, dressed in blockchain rhetoric. By 2021, Celsius held over $20 billion in customer assets. Mashinsky's personal wealth soared. The company became one of crypto's most visible brands.

What customers didn't see was the risk accumulating behind the curtain. Celsius wasn't just lending crypto to creditworthy institutions. It was gambling. The company deployed customer funds into DeFi protocols — decentralized finance platforms that promised high returns through algorithmic lending and yield farming. These protocols were experimental, often audited poorly or not at all, and carried risks that traditional finance would never accept. Celsius also made enormous bets on specific tokens, held vast amounts of its own CEL token as collateral, and operated with minimal transparency about where customer funds actually went.

The warning signs emerged early. Customers trying to withdraw large amounts faced delays. Executives quietly departed. Short-sellers and crypto researchers began publishing reports questioning Celsius's solvency, pointing to mismatches between assets and liabilities, to risky bets on illiquid tokens, to the circular logic of using CEL tokens as collateral for loans. Mashinsky dismissed critics as fear-mongers and haters. He appeared on podcasts defending the model, insisting Celsius was rock-solid.

Then came May 2022. Terra, a supposedly stable algorithmic stablecoin ecosystem, collapsed in a death spiral that wiped out $40 billion in value within days. Celsius had exposure. Not catastrophic on its own, but enough to spook depositors. Withdrawal requests surged. Celsius paid them — at first. But the company's assets weren't as liquid as promised. Much of what Celsius held was locked in DeFi protocols, or consisted of tokens that couldn't be sold without crashing their price, or was tied up in the company's own CEL token, which had no real external value.

On June 12, 2022, Celsius froze all withdrawals. Customers woke to find their funds trapped. No warning, no gradual unwinding. One day you could access your crypto, the next you couldn't. The company claimed it needed to "stabilize liquidity" and promised resumption soon. It never came.

The unraveling was swift and brutal. Within weeks, Celsius filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. Court filings revealed a $1.2 billion hole in the balance sheet. Customer funds had been used to plug losses, to manipulate the price of CEL tokens, to pay earlier withdrawals in classic Ponzi fashion. Celsius had lent hundreds of millions to Mashinsky himself. The institutional loans that were supposed to be low-risk turned out to be catastrophically bad bets. The DeFi protocols that promised transparency were black boxes that lost everything.

Over 600,000 customers lost access to their funds. Some had life savings in Celsius. Retirees, immigrants who trusted Mashinsky's pitch that this was safer than banks, people who believed the yields were real and the risk was minimal. Bankruptcy proceedings would recover pennies on the dollar. Mashinsky, who had withdrawn millions before the collapse, faced fraud charges from the SEC, CFTC, and Department of Justice.

Celsius was sold as the future of banking: transparent, fair, customer-first. It operated like the worst of banking's past: opaque, reckless, extractive. Mashinsky had promised to unbank his customers. In the end, he simply robbed them.

The company that styled itself as revolution was just a bank run waiting to happen, and when it happened, there was no FDIC, no bailout, no safety net. Just locked accounts and empty promises.
