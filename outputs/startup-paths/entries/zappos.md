# Zappos

***The service obsession that sold shoes — and itself***

> **Years:** 1999-2020 (acquired 2009, brand phased out 2024)

In 1999, Nick Swinmurn walked through a San Francisco mall looking for a specific pair of Airwalks. One store had the right style in the wrong size. Another had the right size in the wrong color. He left empty-handed and went home to search online. The selection was terrible. That frustration became a company.

Swinmurn's insight wasn't complicated: shoes were a $40 billion market in the U.S., and almost none of it happened online. Department stores carried limited inventory. Returns were a hassle. But people bought shoes constantly. If someone could solve the inventory and friction problems, the market was enormous.

The original name was ShoeSite.com. The original model was drop-shipping — no inventory, just a middleman between customer and store. Swinmurn photographed shoes at local retailers, listed them online, bought them retail when orders came in, and shipped them out. Margins were razor-thin, but it proved demand existed. Tony Hsieh, who had sold his first company LinkExchange to Microsoft for $265 million, invested. He joined as co-CEO in 2000. The company rebranded to Zappos — a variation on zapatos, Spanish for shoes.

The early strategy was simple: carry everything. While other retailers might stock fifty styles, Zappos would stock five hundred. Selection would be the moat. But drop-shipping couldn't scale. Too many out-of-stocks, too many delays, too little control. In 2003, Zappos made the expensive decision to hold inventory. They leased a warehouse in Kentucky. Margins got thinner. Cash got tighter. But now they controlled the experience.

That control became the obsession. Zappos didn't just ship shoes — it delivered surprise. Customers ordered standard shipping and received overnight. They returned shoes months later without questions. Call center reps weren't scripted or timed. One famously spent ten hours on a single call. Another sent flowers to a customer who mentioned a death in the family. These weren't PR stunts — they were policy. The metric wasn't cost per call. It was repeat customer rate.

Hsieh believed culture was strategy. He articulated ten core values — things like "Create Fun and A Little Weirdness" and "Be Humble." New hires went through weeks of training regardless of role. At the end, Zappos offered them $3,000 to quit. The idea: anyone who took the money didn't belong. Most stayed. The company became famous not for shoes but for service. Business schools taught Zappos cases. Call centers sent people to tour the warehouse.

By 2008, Zappos was doing over $1 billion in gross merchandise sales. But it was still losing money. The business required constant inventory investment. Customer acquisition was expensive. Free shipping and free returns were expensive. The 2008 financial crisis made capital scarce. In 2009, Amazon came with an offer: $1.2 billion, mostly in stock. Hsieh negotiated unusual terms. Zappos would remain independent. The board would stay. The culture would stay. Amazon agreed.

For years, the deal looked vindicated. Zappos kept its brand, its campus, its weirdness. Amazon gave it capital and logistics infrastructure. Revenue grew. The service reputation held. Hsieh became a spokesperson for conscious capitalism, moved Zappos headquarters to downtown Las Vegas as part of an urban revitalization project, wrote a bestselling book called Delivering Happiness.

But the autonomy was always conditional. In 2013, Hsieh eliminated managers and imposed holacracy — a self-management system with roles instead of titles, governance meetings instead of hierarchy. It was radical even for Zappos. Hundreds of employees left. The ones who stayed struggled. Productivity suffered. Amazon watched but didn't interfere — yet.

By 2020, the terms had changed. Amazon retired the Zappos board. Leadership positions consolidated. The Las Vegas headquarters closed. Hsieh stepped down as CEO. Then in November 2020, he died in a house fire, aged 46, amid reports of erratic behavior and struggles with addiction. The company continued, but the animating spirit was gone.

In 2024, Amazon absorbed Zappos fully. The brand name disappeared from the website. It redirected to Amazon Fashion. The independent domain went dark. The customer service philosophy, the core values, the cultural experiment — all folded into the larger machine. Zappos no longer existed as a distinct entity. It was inventory and logistics, optimized for the parent.

What remains is the pattern. Zappos proved people would buy shoes online if the experience was frictionless. It proved service could be a product. It proved culture could scale, at least for a time. But it also proved that independence, once sold, is borrowed. The acquirer's priorities eventually dominate. What made Zappos Zappos — the inefficient obsession with delight over metrics — couldn't survive inside a company built on cost and speed.

The company that sold happiness sold itself. And in the end, what it built was inseparable from the man who built it. When he was gone, the rest was just infrastructure.
