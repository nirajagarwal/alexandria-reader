# Pebble

***The smartwatch before anyone wanted smartwatches***

> **Years:** 2012-2016

Eric Migicovsky had been trying to make a smartwatch since 2008, back when that meant hacking together a BlackBerry dev kit and strapping it to your wrist. He was a student at the University of Waterloo, cycling through the Canadian winter, irritated that checking his phone meant taking off his gloves. By 2011, his company Allerta had built InPulse, a watch that showed BlackBerry notifications. It sold maybe a thousand units. BlackBerry declined to acquire them. The company was nearly dead.

Then the iPhone happened. Not the launch—the dominance. By 2012, BlackBerry was collapsing and iOS was ascendant. Migicovsky saw the opening: rebuild the watch for iPhone users. But there was no money. Y Combinator rejected them twice. Traditional VCs wouldn't touch hardware. In April 2012, Migicovsky uploaded a Kickstarter campaign with a goal of $100,000. He expected it might fail.

It raised $10.3 million in 37 days—the most successful Kickstarter campaign ever at that point. Sixty-nine thousand people pre-ordered a watch that didn't exist yet. The pitch was simple: your phone stays in your pocket, notifications appear on your wrist, the battery lasts a week. The demo video showed Migicovsky on his bike, glancing at his wrist. It felt like the future, delivered.

Manufacturing nearly killed them. Pebble had been five people building prototypes in a Palo Alto garage. Now they owed sixty-nine thousand watches to strangers on the internet. They found a factory in Taiwan, flew engineers back and forth, discovered that injection molding is unforgiving and supply chains are brutal. The first batch had screen tearing issues. The second batch shipped six months late. Backers grew furious. Migicovsky kept posting updates, kept apologizing, kept shipping. By September 2013, they had delivered.

And people loved it. The hardware was chunky, the screen was e-paper, the interface was primitive. But it worked. Cyclists, runners, people in meetings—anyone who wanted to triage notifications without pulling out their phone. App developers started building for it. Pebble released an SDK. Thousands of watch faces appeared. The community felt like early iPhone, scrappy and inventive.

In 2013, Pebble raised $15 million in Series A funding. The narrative was irresistible: Kickstarter success story scales into real company. They hired, expanded, built Pebble Steel—a sleeker version in metal. Then rumors started circulating that Apple was building a watch.

When Apple announced the Apple Watch in September 2014, Pebble's path forked. They could try to compete directly—color screens, touch interfaces, apps—or double down on what made them different: week-long battery, always-on display, simplicity. They chose both. Pebble Time launched on Kickstarter in February 2015, raising $20.3 million, breaking their own record. Color e-paper screen, thinner design, new timeline interface. It looked like validation.

But the Apple Watch launched in April 2015, and the oxygen left the room. Pebble Time shipped to backers in May—a month later than planned. Reviews were kind but tepid. The Apple Watch was slow, expensive, needed daily charging, but it was an Apple product. Retail chains cleared shelf space. Best Buy, Target, AT&T stores—all carried Apple. Pebble had online sales and scattered boutique retailers.

Sales cratered. Pebble had grown to 180 employees expecting millions in revenue. Instead, they sold hundreds of thousands. Migicovsky cut 25% of staff in March 2016. They launched Pebble 2 and Pebble Time 2 on Kickstarter again—raising $12.8 million—but now Kickstarter felt like a lifeline, not a launchpad. The campaigns succeeded; the business was failing.

By fall 2016, Pebble was negotiating with investors for a down round just to keep the lights on. Intel had been in acquisition talks earlier—offering $70 million—but Pebble wanted more. Intel walked. Now there were no buyers. In November, Fitbit offered to acquire Pebble's intellectual property and hire some engineers, but not assume debts or continue the products. It was a fire sale. Pebble accepted $23 million.

In December 2016, Pebble announced it was shutting down. Backers who had pre-ordered Pebble 2 and Time 2 would not receive their watches. Kickstarter refunds were partial. The anger was immediate. Migicovsky posted a long, earnest explanation about running out of runway. It didn't help.

Pebble proved smartwatches could work—four years before Apple proved they could sell. They built a loyal community, pioneered e-paper displays on wrists, created the template for notification-first wearables. Every smartwatch since has borrowed from Pebble's design language. But proving a category exists is different from capturing it. Apple had infinite capital, brand power, retail presence, and ecosystem lock-in. Pebble had Kickstarter campaigns and a prayer.

The question isn't why Pebble failed. It's whether they ever had a chance. They moved too early, with too little capital, into a market where the biggest company in the world decided to compete. Timing is everything in startups. Pebble had the right product at the right time, except the right time was three years later and required being Apple. They were the smartwatch before anyone wanted smartwatches. By the time people wanted them, Apple was there, and Pebble was history.
