# Pebble

***The smartwatch that proved the market and lost it***

> **Years:** 2012-2016

Eric Migicovsky had been building smartwatches since 2008. His first attempt, inPulse, connected BlackBerry phones to a wrist display. It found a small audience — developers, early adopters — but BlackBerry's decline killed any hope of mass market. By 2011, Migicovsky and his small team were running out of money. They pitched venture capitalists. No one believed people wanted computers on their wrists.

In April 2012, they turned to Kickstarter. The goal was $100,000. They raised $10.3 million from 68,929 backers. It was the most funded Kickstarter project in history at the time. The market had spoken. People wanted this.

Pebble shipped the first watches in January 2013. E-paper display. Week-long battery life. Notifications from your iPhone or Android. A growing app ecosystem. The device wasn't beautiful — it was plastic and utilitarian — but it worked. It did what smartwatches were supposed to do: extend your phone to your wrist without requiring constant charging. Early reviews were mixed but curious. The Watch app store launched with games, fitness trackers, watch faces. Developers liked it. The platform was open. The tools were accessible.

By the end of 2013, Pebble had shipped 400,000 units. They raised $15 million in Series A funding. The company grew to over 100 employees. They moved into larger offices in Palo Alto. The product improved. Pebble Steel launched in early 2014 — same internals, better design. Metal body. Gorilla Glass. It looked like a watch people might actually want to wear.

Then the landscape shifted.

In September 2014, Apple announced the Apple Watch. Samsung had already released several Galaxy Gear models. Google announced Android Wear. The tech giants were entering. Pebble's investors started asking what the exit strategy was. How do you compete with Apple? With Google? With Samsung's marketing budget?

Migicovsky believed in differentiation. Apple Watch would need daily charging. Pebble lasted a week. Apple Watch was a closed ecosystem. Pebble worked with both iOS and Android. Apple Watch was expensive. Pebble was affordable. They weren't competing directly — they were serving different users with different priorities.

In February 2015, Pebble returned to Kickstarter. Pebble Time introduced a color e-paper display and a new timeline interface. They raised $20.3 million from 78,471 backers. Another record. The message seemed clear: there was still a market for what Pebble was building.

But the market was fragmenting. Apple Watch launched in April 2015 and sold millions of units in the first quarter. Not because it was better — reviews noted the short battery life, the unclear use case, the sluggish performance. But because it was Apple. Because it integrated seamlessly with iPhone. Because millions of iPhone users were willing to try it.

Pebble shipped Pebble Time in May 2015, followed by Pebble Time Round in November — the first genuinely attractive Pebble watch. Thin, circular, elegant. But the battery life was only two days. They'd compromised their core advantage for aesthetics. Sales were disappointing.

By early 2016, Pebble was in trouble. They'd raised over $25 million on Kickstarter and about $26 million in venture funding, but manufacturing costs were high and sales were declining. Apple Watch was dominating the high end. Fitbit dominated fitness. Pebble occupied an increasingly narrow middle ground — people who wanted smartphone notifications on their wrist but didn't want the Apple ecosystem or the daily charging requirement.

They tried to raise more funding. Investors passed. The narrative had shifted. Smartwatches were now an Apple market or a fitness tracker market. Pebble's differentiation — the battery life, the open platform, the cross-platform support — didn't matter enough to enough people.

In March 2016, they launched one more Kickstarter. Pebble 2, Pebble Time 2, and Pebble Core (a screenless device for runners). They raised $12.8 million. But it wasn't enough. Manufacturing delays. Cash flow problems. Layoffs.

Intel expressed interest in acquiring them. Talks progressed. Then Intel killed its wearables division. The deal evaporated.

In December 2016, Fitbit acquired Pebble for roughly $23 million — less than the company had raised on Kickstarter alone. It was an acqui-hire. Fitbit wanted the software talent and the intellectual property. They shut down Pebble's servers. Stopped manufacturing. Ended support. The watches became expensive paperweights.

Migicovsky and key team members joined Fitbit. Most left within a year.

Pebble proved the market for smartwatches. They showed that people would pay for wrist-worn computers that extended smartphone functionality. They built a loyal community. They demonstrated that crowdfunding could finance hardware at scale. But proving a market and winning it are different problems. Apple didn't invent the smartwatch. They simply had the ecosystem, the capital, the retail presence, and the brand to make it mass market. Pebble built the trail. Apple built the highway.

The Pebble faithful still wear their watches, kept alive by community-developed software. A product that outlived its company. A proof of concept that someone else executed.
