# YouTube

***The video platform that won by losing money at scale***

> **Years:** 2005-present

In February 2005, three former PayPal employees registered the domain youtube.com. Chad Hurley, Steve Chen, and Jawed Karim had witnessed the infrastructure revolution at PayPal — how to move money at internet scale — and they saw another infrastructure problem waiting to be solved. The internet was choking on video.

The founding story has multiple versions. One involves a dinner party where guests couldn't easily share videos. Another centers on Janet Jackson's Super Bowl halftime show and the impossibility of finding clips online. The truth is simpler: video was broken on the web. Files were too large. Formats were incompatible. Hosting was expensive. Embedding was impossible. If you wanted to share a video, you attached it to an email and hoped.

The technical challenge was immense. Video requires bandwidth that would bankrupt most startups. Storage costs scale linearly with content. Transcoding — converting uploaded videos into formats that browsers could play — required serious computational power. Most sensible people would have concluded the unit economics made a video platform impossible.

YouTube's insight was that the unit economics didn't matter if the platform became essential. They would lose money on every video view and make it up in volume — or rather, in becoming the default infrastructure for internet video. They built for ease: one-click uploading, automatic transcoding, simple embedding. The technical complexity disappeared behind a red upload button.

The first video, "Me at the zoo," uploaded April 23, 2005, showed Jawed Karim at the San Diego Zoo talking about elephants. Eighteen seconds. Unremarkable. But it worked. You clicked, it played. No plugins, no downloads, no format confusion.

Growth came faster than anyone expected. By July 2006, users were uploading 65,000 videos daily. By October, 100 million videos viewed per day. The traffic was staggering. The bandwidth bills were terrifying. Sequoia Capital led a $3.5 million Series A in November 2005, followed by an $8 million Series B in April 2006. The money burned fast. Every viral video was a financial hit.

But virality was the product. A lonelygirl15 video would get a million views. An SNL sketch would spread before the network could issue a takedown. User-generated content exploded — bedroom musicians, makeup tutorials, gaming commentary, amateur comedy. YouTube wasn't hosting professional content; it was creating a new category of creator.

The copyright situation was legally catastrophic. Users uploaded TV shows, movies, music videos. Viacom sued for $1 billion. The major labels threatened. The DMCA's safe harbor provisions protected YouTube — barely — as long as they responded to takedown notices. They built ContentID, a system for identifying copyrighted material automatically. It was innovative and insufficient. The lawsuits piled up.

Google was watching. They had launched Google Video in 2005 and it was dying. YouTube was winning because it was simpler, faster, more viral. Google tried to compete, then realized they should acquire. In October 2006, eighteen months after YouTube's launch, Google paid $1.65 billion.

The tech press called it insane. YouTube had virtually no revenue. The copyright liability was enormous. The bandwidth costs were unsustainable. But Google saw infrastructure. They had the data centers, the bandwidth, the ad technology. YouTube had the network effects and the cultural dominance. Google could absorb the losses while building the monetization.

It took years. YouTube lost money consistently through 2010. Ad rates on user-generated content were terrible. Premium advertisers didn't want their brands next to amateur videos. The platform experimented — partnerships with professional content creators, YouTube TV, YouTube Premium, Super Chat for live streams. They built an entire creator economy: ad revenue sharing, sponsorships, merchandise integration.

The breakthrough was scale and patience. As broadband spread and mobile video exploded, YouTube became default infrastructure. Every phone had the app. Every website embedded YouTube videos. Musicians launched careers from bedroom studios. Brands built channels. Politicians campaigned. The presidential debates streamed live.

By 2020, YouTube generated $19.8 billion in ad revenue. The creator economy they spawned was worth tens of billions more. MrBeast built a production empire on YouTube revenue. Educational channels reached millions. The platform had become essential cultural infrastructure — the world's second-largest search engine, processing more queries than Bing, Yahoo, and Ask combined.

The unit economics eventually worked, but only at a scale that would have killed any normal startup. Google's willingness to lose billions while the platform matured was the hidden subsidy that made YouTube possible. No VC-backed startup could have survived long enough to reach profitability. The infrastructure play only worked because Google could treat it as infrastructure.

YouTube didn't solve the copyright problem; they outlasted it. ContentID processed hundreds of millions of claims. Rights holders learned they made more money monetizing viral clips than issuing takedowns. The system was imperfect but sustainable — controversial enough to anger creators and copyright holders in equal measure, but functional enough to keep operating.

The company the founders built was a bet that infrastructure would matter more than economics, that dominance would eventually generate revenue, that being essential was more valuable than being profitable. They were right, but only because Google could afford to be patient. The path from three former PayPal employees to the world's video platform required both vision and the kind of institutional patience that only a search monopoly's balance sheet could provide.
