# Zoom

***The overnight success that took nine years of obscurity***

> **Years:** 2011-present

Eric Yuan had spent fourteen years at Cisco, where he became one of the founding engineers of WebEx after Cisco acquired it in 2007 for $3.2 billion. He watched as the product he'd helped build grew bloated with enterprise features, complicated interfaces, and reliability issues. Customer satisfaction scores declined. Engineering became about maintaining legacy architecture rather than reimagining what video collaboration could be.

Yuan proposed fixing it. He pitched Cisco leadership repeatedly: rebuild WebEx from the ground up for the mobile era, prioritize ease of use, make it work reliably every time. The answer was always no. Too risky. Too expensive. The existing product generated too much revenue to justify the disruption. In 2011, after his final proposal was rejected, Yuan quit. Forty engineers quit with him.

They incorporated Zoom Video Communications with a vision that seemed almost naive in its simplicity: video conferencing that just worked. No downloads before joining. No complicated setup. No dropped calls. The insight wasn't technological—WebEx and its competitors had the basic technology. The insight was that enterprise video conferencing had optimized for IT departments and feature checklists instead of for the experience of people actually trying to talk to each other.

The early years were hard. Yuan had a reputation from WebEx, which helped with initial enterprise sales, but Zoom entered a crowded market. Skype dominated consumer video. Cisco WebEx, Google Hangouts, Microsoft Lync (later Teams), and GoToMeeting owned different slices of enterprise. VCs questioned whether there was room for another player. Yuan raised a modest $3 million seed round in 2011 and a $6 million Series A in 2013—small numbers for a company competing in enterprise software.

Zoom's advantage was focus. While competitors added features, Zoom obsessed over reliability and simplicity. Yuan instituted a rule: if Zoom's quality scores dropped below a certain threshold, engineers stopped building new features and fixed quality issues. The product was fast. It worked on bad connections. It worked on phones, tablets, laptops, conference rooms. Joining a meeting took two clicks.

Enterprise customers noticed. Initially IT departments resisted—Zoom didn't have all the features of entrenched competitors. But employees started using it anyway. A classic bottom-up adoption pattern: someone would use Zoom for one meeting because it was easier, then another, then suddenly departments were asking IT to get Zoom licenses. The product was so simple that it didn't require training. The network effects were natural: if your client used Zoom, you used Zoom.

Revenue grew steadily through the mid-2010s. Zoom reached $60 million in revenue by early 2017, then doubled to $151 million by early 2018. It was profitable—unusual for a SaaS company at that stage. Yuan had kept costs low, avoided the spending sprees typical of venture-backed startups. In January 2017, Zoom raised $100 million at a $1 billion valuation. By the Series D in January 2019, the valuation had reached $1 billion. Three months later, Zoom went public.

The IPO in April 2019 priced at $36 per share, valuing the company at $9.2 billion. It was a successful offering but not spectacular. Zoom was a good business—growing, profitable, well-run—but it was still just enterprise video conferencing. The stock climbed steadily through 2019 into early 2020, reaching around $107 per share by February 2020.

Then COVID-19 hit.

In March 2020, as the world locked down, Zoom became the infrastructure of quarantine. Schools used it for classes. Companies used it for everything. Doctors used it for appointments. Families used it for birthdays and holidays and funerals. Zoom's daily meeting participants went from 10 million in December 2019 to 300 million by April 2020.

The company had to scale faster than any enterprise software company in history. Zoom added server capacity, expanded data centers, made emergency deals with cloud providers. The free tier—which Yuan insisted on keeping free even as costs exploded—brought in hundreds of millions of new users. The infrastructure held. Mostly. Security issues emerged: "Zoombombing," encryption concerns, data routing through China. Zoom fixed them in weeks, not months.

The stock went parabolic. By October 2020, Zoom traded above $550 per share—a market cap exceeding $150 billion. It had become worth more than the seven largest airlines combined. Yuan's stake made him a billionaire many times over.

The boom couldn't last. As vaccines rolled out and the world reopened, usage patterns normalized. The stock declined through 2021 and 2022. By late 2022, it traded around $70 per share, still double the IPO price but a shadow of the peak. Competition intensified. Microsoft Teams, bundled with Office 365, became the default for many enterprises. Google Meet improved. The market began treating Zoom as a pandemic winner facing structural decline.

Yet Zoom remained profitable and growing, if more slowly. Revenue for fiscal 2023 hit $4.4 billion, up from $2.7 billion the year before. The company expanded into Zoom Phone, Zoom Rooms, Zoom Events—trying to become a broader communications platform rather than just video meetings. Whether that works remains to be determined.

The story of Zoom is the story of building something excellent for nine years in relative obscurity, then having the world suddenly need exactly what you built. It's also the story of every overnight success: the part everyone remembers is brief, accidental, and impossible to replicate. The part that mattered was the boring middle—the years of obsessing over quality scores and making video calls reliable when nobody was paying attention.
