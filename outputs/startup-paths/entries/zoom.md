# Zoom

***The pandemic's unlikely hero, built on enterprise software no one wanted to use***

> **Years:** 2011–present

Eric Yuan spent fourteen years at Cisco, most of them working on WebEx after Cisco acquired it in 2007. WebEx was the market leader in video conferencing, dominant in enterprise, profitable, entrenched. Yuan led the engineering team. He kept proposing that they rebuild the product from the ground up — make it cloud-native, make it actually work, make it something people would want to use rather than tolerate. Cisco said no. The architecture was legacy, but it worked well enough. Customers were locked in. Why take the risk?

So in 2011, Yuan left to build what Cisco wouldn't. He took forty engineers with him. They started from scratch. No legacy code, no technical debt, no compromises with old architectural decisions. Cloud-native from day one. The product would be called Zoom.

The market looked terrible. Video conferencing was crowded and dominated. Cisco's WebEx had the enterprise. Microsoft had Skype and would soon bundle video into Office. Google was building Hangouts. Startups died trying to compete with bundled, free-with-enterprise-suite video tools. Why would anyone pay for standalone video conferencing?

Yuan's answer: because all the existing products were terrible. They crashed constantly. Audio cut out. Screen sharing didn't work. You needed to download plugins and restart your browser. Join codes didn't work. The experience was so reliably bad that "can you hear me?" became the standard opening of every call. People used video conferencing because they had to, not because they wanted to.

Zoom's entire design philosophy was obsessive focus on reliability and ease of use. It had to work on the first try, every time. One click to join. No plugins required. Bandwidth adaptation so calls didn't drop when networks got congested. Screen sharing that actually worked. Gallery view that made group calls feel like you were in a room together. Small things, executed ruthlessly well.

They launched in 2013. Early traction came from sales teams — people who lived on video calls and noticed immediately when something worked better. Yuan knew enterprise sales from his Cisco years. Zoom used a freemium model: free for calls under forty minutes, paid for longer calls and additional features. The free tier was generous enough that people could try it risk-free. Once they did, they upgraded, because it worked.

Growth was steady but not explosive. By 2017, Zoom had ten million users and was profitable. The company IPO'd in April 2019 at a $9.2 billion valuation. Investors were skeptical. Why would Zoom sustain that valuation when Microsoft was bundling Teams with Office 365? Yuan owned 22% of the company. The market gave Zoom credit for execution, but it looked like a product that would eventually get squeezed by free alternatives.

Then the pandemic hit.

In January 2020, Zoom had about ten million daily meeting participants. By April, it had 300 million. The entire world suddenly needed video conferencing. Schools, doctors' appointments, family dinners, yoga classes, AA meetings, courts, therapy sessions — everything went remote. Zoom became the verb. "Let's Zoom" replaced "let's have a call."

The spike broke every assumption. Zoom's infrastructure scaled in ways even Yuan hadn't anticipated it would need to. Server capacity went from comfortable to inadequate in weeks. The engineering team worked around the clock to add capacity. It held. Barely, but it held.

Other problems emerged. Zoom wasn't built for a world where elementary school classes happened on video. Security issues surfaced — "Zoombombing," where uninvited participants crashed calls, became a headline problem. Privacy advocates raised concerns about encryption. Zoom scrambled to fix it: better default security settings, end-to-end encryption, waiting rooms, host controls.

The company's revenue went from $622 million in 2019 to $2.65 billion in 2021. The stock price peaked at $559 in October 2020, a market cap of $159 billion. Eric Yuan, who left Cisco because they wouldn't rebuild WebEx, now ran a company worth more than Cisco.

But the pandemic also revealed Zoom's fragility. The growth was usage-driven, not just user-driven. When people returned to offices, they Zoomed less. Revenue growth slowed. The stock fell. By late 2022, shares traded at $70. Investors realized Zoom had benefited from a once-in-a-century event that wouldn't repeat. The question now was whether it could hold onto the users it gained — or whether Teams, Google Meet, and other bundled alternatives would slowly win back the enterprise.

Zoom responded by expanding: Zoom Phone for telephony, Zoom Rooms for conference room hardware, Zoom Events for virtual conferences. The strategy was to become a full communications platform, not just video. But this put them in direct competition with Microsoft's entire Office suite and Google Workspace. They no longer competed just on product quality. They competed on ecosystem lock-in, bundling, and price.

The company remains profitable and growing, but the narrative has shifted. During the pandemic, Zoom was the essential tool. Now it's a successful enterprise software company defending market share. Yuan's bet — that a relentless focus on product quality could beat bundled mediocrity — worked. But it worked because everyone had to use video, and for a moment, everyone chose Zoom. The long-term question is whether quality alone can hold a market when the alternatives are free with tools customers already pay for.

Zoom's path reveals an uncomfortable truth: sometimes the difference between a solid business and a giant is an event no one could predict. Yuan made every right decision. He built a better product, scaled it brilliantly, survived the sudden spike. But without the pandemic, Zoom might have IPO'd, grown steadily, and eventually been acquired. Instead, it became a verb and a giant — and now has to figure out if it can stay one.
