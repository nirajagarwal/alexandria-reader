# Grab

***From ride-hailing clone to Southeast Asian super-app empire***

> **Years:** 2012–present

In 2012, Anthony Tan was a second-generation Malaysian businessman studying at Harvard Business School when he noticed something odd about his family's automotive distribution empire back home. His relatives owned cars, but getting around Kuala Lumpur remained a nightmare. Taxis were unreliable, often refused short trips, and operated like cartels. Meanwhile, halfway around the world, a startup called Uber was beginning to reimagine urban transportation.

Tan's Harvard thesis became a business plan. He pitched the idea of "MyTeksi" — essentially Uber for Malaysia — to his classmates, his professors, and anyone who would listen. Most were skeptical. Southeast Asia seemed too fragmented, too poor, too cash-dependent for a smartphone-based ride-hailing service. But Tan had an advantage: his family's automotive connections and his co-founder Tan Hooi Ling, a Harvard classmate who understood technology. In June 2012, they launched MyTeksi in Malaysia with money from Tan's family and the conviction that what worked in San Francisco could work in Kuala Lumpur.

The early bet paid off faster than anyone expected. Drivers loved having a reliable way to find passengers. Passengers loved knowing a car would actually show up. Within months, MyTeksi was processing thousands of rides daily. Tan and Hooi Ling expanded to Singapore, the Philippines, Thailand. They weren't inventing the model — they were adapting it to terrain Uber didn't yet understand. They integrated cash payments when Uber insisted on credit cards. They recruited motorcycle taxis when cars made no sense in Bangkok traffic. They negotiated with regulators while Uber picked fights.

In 2014, venture capital arrived. SoftBank, Didi Chuxing, and other investors poured money into the renamed "GrabTaxi," valuing the company at hundreds of millions. The message was clear: Southeast Asia's ride-hailing market was up for grabs, and whoever dominated it would control urban mobility across 600 million people. Uber took notice and launched aggressively across the region. For three years, Grab and Uber burned cash in a brutal subsidy war, paying drivers and discounting rides to win market share city by city.

Grab had advantages Uber couldn't replicate. The founders spoke local languages, understood local regulations, and knew which officials mattered in Jakarta versus Manila. They hired local teams who knew that Indonesia required cash, that Thailand required motorcycle taxis, that Singapore required premium service. Uber optimized globally; Grab adapted locally. By 2016, Grab was winning on every metric that mattered: rides, drivers, user growth.

But the subsidy war was unsustainable. Both companies were losing hundreds of millions annually. Uber was simultaneously fighting Didi in China, Ola in India, and established taxi lobbies everywhere. In 2017, Uber sold its China operations to Didi in exchange for equity. The message to Southeast Asia was coming: Uber wanted out of expensive battles it couldn't win.

In March 2018, Uber surrendered. It sold its Southeast Asian operations to Grab for 27.5% equity in the combined company. Overnight, Grab went from battling for market share to owning it — dominant in ride-hailing across eight countries, with minimal competition. The victory was total. Tan had taken a Harvard thesis and turned it into a regional monopoly in six years.

But Grab's ambitions extended beyond rides. Even before acquiring Uber's operations, the company was transforming into a super-app — the WeChat or Alipay of Southeast Asia. GrabPay became a digital wallet. GrabFood delivered meals. GrabExpress couriered packages. The logic was elegant: once you controlled urban mobility, you controlled the daily transaction layer of hundreds of millions of lives. Drivers became delivery workers. Cars became distribution networks. The app became infrastructure.

The super-app strategy unlocked new revenue streams but created new challenges. Grab entered businesses — food delivery, payments, lending — where established players fought back. Regulators grew concerned about monopoly power. The company faced antitrust investigations in multiple countries. Profitability remained elusive as expansion costs mounted. SoftBank kept funding, but each round came with higher expectations and more pressure to justify the valuations.

In 2021, Grab went public via SPAC at a $40 billion valuation — the largest-ever SPAC deal at the time. The stock soared briefly, then collapsed as investors realized the company was nowhere near profitable and the post-pandemic mobility market looked different than projected. By 2022, shares had fallen 75% from their peak. The super-app was real, used by millions daily, but the path to profitability remained unclear.

Today, Grab operates across 500 cities in eight countries. It processes billions in transactions annually across ride-hailing, food delivery, payments, and financial services. The founders succeeded beyond their Harvard thesis: they built the closest thing Southeast Asia has to a digital infrastructure layer. But the outcome remains unfinished. The company that won the ride-hailing war now fights different battles — against unprofitability, against super-app competitors like GoTo in Indonesia, against the gravitational pull of Western investors who expected faster returns.

What looked like total victory in 2018 turned out to be the beginning of a longer, harder path. Grab proved that cloning Silicon Valley's best ideas and adapting them to Southeast Asia could build an empire. Whether that empire ever justifies its cost is still an open question.
