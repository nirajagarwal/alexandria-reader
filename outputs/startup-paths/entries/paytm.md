# Paytm

***The super-app that rode a nation's digital leap, then stumbled at the gates of public markets***

> **Years:** 2010-present

In 2010, Vijay Shekhar Sharma was running a mobile recharge platform called One97 Communications, a modest business serving India's prepaid telecom users. The insight wasn't revolutionary — people needed to top up their phones — but the execution was solid enough to build a small company. Then came the opportunity to transform it entirely.

The company launched Paytm (Pay Through Mobile) as a digital wallet. Initially, it was just another way to recharge phones without visiting a shop. But Sharma saw something larger: India's vast unbanked population, its smartphone adoption curve, and the friction in every payment. In a country where cash was king and credit cards rare, a mobile wallet could become infrastructure.

The early growth was steady but unremarkable. Indians were wary of digital payments. Trust was scarce. Then in November 2016, Prime Minister Narendra Modi announced demonetization — overnight, 86% of India's currency by value became invalid. The nation needed to exchange old notes or go digital immediately. Paytm was ready.

Sharma ran full-page newspaper ads with a giant QR code and the line "Paytm Karo" (Do Paytm). The company scaled from 125 million users to 200 million in eight weeks. Merchants adopted it because customers demanded it. Customers used it because merchants accepted it. The network effects compounded daily. What had been a gradual climb became a vertical ascent.

Flush with attention and usage, Paytm attracted capital. Alibaba and Ant Financial invested. SoftBank poured in money. Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway took a stake. The valuation soared past $16 billion. Sharma, who had once taken loans against his mother's jewelry to keep One97 alive, was now a billionaire founder of India's most visible fintech.

But visibility and viability are different. Paytm expanded aggressively: payments, e-commerce, ticketing, financial services, insurance, mutual funds, gold purchases. The strategy was to become India's super-app — the WeChat of South Asia. Everything under one interface. Revenue would follow usage.

The problem was that revenue didn't follow fast enough. Payment wallets in India had thin margins, and competitors multiplied. Google Pay and PhonePe offered the same utility with cleaner interfaces and no platform ambitions to fund. They focused on Unified Payments Interface (UPI), the government's open digital payments system, while Paytm tried to balance its closed wallet with UPI integration. The incentives misaligned.

Paytm's e-commerce venture burned cash competing with Amazon and Flipkart. Its financial services arm required regulatory capital and time to mature. The company was growing revenue but losing money at scale. In fiscal year 2020, it lost $570 million on revenue of $550 million. The path to profitability was a distant narrative, not an imminent plan.

Still, in 2021, Paytm filed for India's largest-ever initial public offering: $2.5 billion at a $20 billion valuation. The roadshow pitched the super-app vision, the first-mover advantage, the integration of payments and commerce and finance. Institutional investors were cautious. Retail investors, swept up in India's IPO frenzy, subscribed eagerly.

The stock debuted on November 18, 2021, and immediately fell 27% — the worst first-day performance for a major Indian IPO in years. It kept falling. Within months, it had lost more than 70% of its value. The market had looked past the narrative and seen the unit economics, the competition, the regulatory uncertainty, and the path to profitability that required too many assumptions.

What followed was a painful correction. Paytm laid off employees, shut unprofitable verticals, and focused on its core payments and lending businesses. Sharma, who had once said "we are not in a race to profitability," revised the message. The company began reporting smaller losses, then in 2023, its first quarterly profit. But the stock price didn't recover.

Then in January 2024, the Reserve Bank of India delivered a blow: it barred Paytm Payments Bank, the company's banking arm, from onboarding new customers and ordered it to wind down operations. The regulator cited persistent non-compliance and supervisory concerns. Paytm had to migrate millions of users and merchants to other banks. The disruption was immediate.

Sharma began exploring options. In early 2025, reports emerged that he was in talks to sell his stake. The likely buyers were other fintech players or private equity firms looking for distressed assets in India's digital economy. The super-app vision had contracted to a survival strategy.

Paytm's story is not one of outright failure — it remains operational, it has paying customers, it helped digitize a nation. But it is a story of a company that mistook a moment for a mandate. Demonetization handed Paytm a windfall of attention and users. The assumption was that attention would convert to market power, and market power to profit. Instead, it converted to competition. Google and PhonePe offered the same service without the overhead of super-app ambitions. UPI, the government's open standard, ensured no single player could monopolize payments.

Paytm rode India's digital wave. But waves carry many boats, and being first in the water doesn't guarantee staying afloat longest. The public markets, at least, wanted to see the waterline.
