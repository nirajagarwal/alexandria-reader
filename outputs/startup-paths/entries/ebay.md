# eBay

***The marketplace that turned collectibles into commerce***

> **Years:** 1995-present

On Labor Day weekend 1995, Pierre Omidyar sat at his computer in San Jose writing code for what he thought would be an experiment. He was a software engineer at General Magic, working on the side to build internet tools. One of them was a simple marketplace where people could list items for auction. He called it AuctionWeb, hosted on his personal site alongside pages about the Ebola virus. The legend says his girlfriend wanted to trade Pez dispensers. The truth is simpler: Omidyar believed in the efficient market, in letting buyers and sellers find their own prices.

The first item sold was a broken laser pointer. Omidyar contacted the winning bidder to make sure they understood it didn't work. The buyer replied: "I'm a collector of broken laser pointers." That's when Omidyar realized he'd stumbled onto something real. People wanted to buy things that had no traditional retail market. Strange things. Used things. Things nobody knew how to price.

Traffic grew. By February 1996, the site was generating enough revenue from listing fees — ten cents to twenty-five cents per auction — that Omidyar had to upgrade his ISP account. By June, he'd quit his job. AuctionWeb was pulling in $10,000 a month. Omidyar recruited his friend Jeff Skoll, a Stanford MBA, to write the business plan. They renamed it eBay — Omidyar's intended name for his consulting business, Echo Bay, shortened because the domain was taken.

The site had no inventory, no warehouses, no employees handling goods. It was pure infrastructure. Buyers and sellers met, transacted, and left feedback. The feedback system — Omidyar's innovation — solved the trust problem that should have killed an anonymous marketplace. Reputation became currency. Power sellers emerged, people running full-time businesses out of their garages. Collectibles were the engine: Beanie Babies, trading cards, memorabilia. Entire secondary markets materialized.

But eBay was chaotic. The site crashed constantly. Customer service was Omidyar and Skoll answering emails. They needed management. In 1997, they hired Meg Whitman, a Harvard MBA who'd worked at Hasbro and Disney. She was their fifth choice. The first four turned them down. Whitman thought the site was ugly, the business bizarre. But she saw the numbers: millions in revenue, almost pure profit, exponential growth. She took the job.

Whitman professionalized the operation. She brought in engineers to stabilize the platform, built a customer service team, introduced category managers. She also made a crucial strategic choice: eBay would grow through acquisition and international expansion, not by forcing a single business model. When collectors started selling cars, eBay bought Auto Trader. When people wanted fixed-price options, eBay added "Buy It Now." When competitors emerged abroad, eBay bought them or launched local sites.

The IPO in September 1998 was a phenomenon. The stock opened at $18, shot to $47, and closed at $54. Omidyar and Skoll became billionaires. Whitman became the face of the company. eBay was proof that the internet could generate real profits, not just promise. The market capitalization hit $20 billion by 1999.

But the auction model had limits. eBay worked for unique items — collectibles, used goods, one-offs. It worked less well for commodity goods where price discovery mattered less than convenience. Amazon was building a different model: fixed prices, fast shipping, predictable experience. eBay's growth began to slow. The platform attracted fraud. Sellers gamed feedback systems. Buyers complained about quality. The site's reputation shifted from quirky treasure hunt to sketchy flea market.

Whitman tried to evolve. eBay bought PayPal in 2002 for $1.5 billion, solving the payment problem and gaining a fintech subsidiary. It bought Skype in 2005 for $2.6 billion, hoping to integrate communication into commerce. That acquisition failed spectacularly. Skype didn't fit. eBay sold it four years later at a loss. The company also expanded into China, competing with Alibaba. That failed too. Alibaba understood local dynamics — seller relationships, payment systems, logistics — in ways eBay didn't.

By 2008, the core business was stagnant. Amazon had become the default for online shopping. Craigslist had captured local classified ads. Etsy was winning handmade goods. eBay was stuck between markets. Whitman stepped down. Her successor, John Donahoe, tried to fix the platform — simplifying the interface, lowering fees for high-volume sellers, pushing fixed-price listings. But the marketplace that had thrived on chaos couldn't easily become streamlined. Sellers who'd built businesses around the old system revolted.

eBay spun off PayPal in 2015, splitting the company into two. PayPal was the growth business; eBay was the legacy. The core auction platform limped along, still profitable, still massive, but no longer defining the future. Amazon was logistics and speed. Etsy was craft and community. Craigslist was local and free. eBay was the place you went when you didn't know where else to go.

What eBay proved: you could build a massive business by connecting buyers and sellers without owning anything. The platform model worked. But being first didn't mean staying dominant. eBay created a market, then watched others build better versions. The auction format — its original insight — became a limitation. Markets want efficiency, but they also want certainty. eBay offered price discovery when no one knew what things were worth. Once they did, auctions felt like friction.

The company still exists, still processes billions in transactions annually. But it occupies a strange space: too successful to die, too constrained to reinvent itself. It created the online marketplace and then became a monument to what marketplaces used to be.
