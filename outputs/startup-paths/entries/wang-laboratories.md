# Wang Laboratories

***The word processor king who couldn't see the PC revolution***

> **Years:** 1951-1992

An Wang arrived in America from China in 1945 with forty dollars and a scholarship to Harvard. By 1948, he had invented magnetic core memory — the technology that would make modern computing possible. IBM paid him half a million dollars for the patent. In 1951, at age thirty-one, Wang opened a small laboratory in Boston to build specialized calculators. He had no grand vision of computing empires. He simply wanted independence.

For two decades, Wang Laboratories grew steadily in the shadows of the mainframe giants. Wang made desktop calculators, then moved into word processing in the early 1970s. The timing was perfect. Secretaries typed on IBM Selectrics. Corrections meant retyping entire pages. Wang's dedicated word processors — terminals connected to minicomputers — let users edit on screen before printing. Law firms, government offices, and corporations bought thousands of units. The machines were expensive, proprietary, and incredibly profitable.

By 1978, Wang Laboratories was doing $200 million in revenue. An Wang had built a vertically integrated kingdom — his own processors, his own operating system, his own sales force, his own service network. The company went public that year. The stock soared. Wang became a billionaire, and Boston became a tech hub rivaling Silicon Valley. He bought the Boston Garden and put his name on it.

The early 1980s were the peak. Wang's word processing systems dominated offices across America. The company expanded into minicomputers, competing directly with Digital Equipment Corporation and Data General. Revenue hit $3 billion by 1988. Wang employed 33,000 people. An Wang appeared on magazine covers as a visionary immigrant entrepreneur. The company built a massive headquarters complex in Lowell, Massachusetts — twin towers that symbolized arrival.

But the PC was already eating the foundation. Apple II and then IBM PC with WordPerfect and Lotus 1-2-3 could do word processing for a fraction of Wang's price. The machines were open, modular, upgradeable. Wang's systems were closed, proprietary, and increasingly obsolete. Companies that had spent millions on Wang infrastructure faced a choice: keep paying Wang's premium prices or rip everything out and start over with PCs.

An Wang saw the threat but couldn't respond. His entire business model depended on proprietary lock-in. Moving to open standards meant cannibalizing his own revenue. His word processors were still profitable. The sales force resisted change — their commissions came from big system sales, not cheap PCs. Wang tried introducing PC-compatible products, but half-heartedly. The company culture was built around An Wang's engineering philosophy: integrated systems under central control. PCs represented the opposite.

The financial structure made pivoting impossible. Wang had gone public but maintained control through dual-class shares. An Wang made all major decisions. He promoted his son Fred to president in 1986 despite limited experience and internal opposition. The move demoralized senior executives. Several left. Fred Wang struggled with the scope of the crisis. An Wang, now in his sixties and in declining health, couldn't let go.

The collapse came fast. Revenue peaked in 1988 at $3 billion, then fell off a cliff. Customers abandoned Wang systems en masse. The minicomputer business evaporated as PC networks took over. By 1989, the company was losing money. By 1990, it was hemorrhaging cash. An Wang finally removed Fred and brought in outside management, but it was too late. The debt load from the boom years was crushing.

An Wang died of cancer in March 1990, at age seventy. He had built one of America's great technology companies and watched it disintegrate in his final years. The company limped forward under new management, trying to salvage something from the wreckage. They sold the towers. They laid off tens of thousands. They tried to reposition as a software and services company.

In August 1992, Wang Laboratories filed for bankruptcy. The company emerged from bankruptcy in 1993 as a shadow of itself, then was eventually acquired and absorbed. The brand disappeared. The Lowell towers stood empty for years before being converted to condos.

Wang's story is the story of disruptive innovation in miniature. An Wang built a phenomenally successful business solving a real problem — editing documents before printing. He built deep expertise and infrastructure. He created a self-reinforcing system that was highly profitable precisely because it was closed and proprietary. When a new technology made that system obsolete, he couldn't pivot. The very things that made Wang Laboratories successful — vertical integration, proprietary control, premium pricing — became fatal liabilities.

The company's death wasn't inevitable in 1980 or even 1985. But by 1988, when management finally acknowledged the crisis, the window had closed. The installed base was already switching. The debt was already unsustainable. An Wang's refusal to cede control — and his decision to promote his son — poisoned any chance of transformation.

What survives is An Wang's early innovation. Magnetic core memory made the computer age possible. That contribution outlived the company by decades. But Wang Laboratories itself became a cautionary tale: the giant that couldn't adapt, the founder who held on too long, the company that mistook a temporary advantage for permanent dominance.
