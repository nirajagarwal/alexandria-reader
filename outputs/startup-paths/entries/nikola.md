# Nikola

***The electric truck mirage***

> **Years:** 2014-2024

In 2014, Trevor Milton founded Nikola Motor Company with a vision that seemed audacious but timely: Tesla had proven electric vehicles could be premium products, but Elon Musk was focused on cars. Milton saw an opening in heavy trucks. The name itself—Nikola Tesla's first name—announced the ambition. This would be Tesla for the commercial vehicle world.

Milton was a serial entrepreneur from Utah, a salesman by temperament. He'd started an alarm company in his twenties and sold it. He understood pitching, momentum, narrative. He saw that the trucking industry was ripe for disruption: diesel was expensive, emissions regulations were tightening, and fleet operators would pay premiums for lower operating costs. The total addressable market was enormous.

The founding idea was elegant: electric semi-trucks for short and medium hauls, hydrogen fuel cell trucks for long hauls. Batteries for predictable routes, hydrogen for flexibility. Nikola would build the vehicles and the fueling infrastructure. Vertically integrated, end-to-end solution. The pitch was compelling enough that by 2016, Nikola claimed over $2.3 billion in pre-order reservations.

But there was a problem at the core. Nikola wasn't building trucks—not really, not yet. It was building hype. The company excelled at computer renderings, concept vehicles, and promotional videos. In December 2016, Nikola unveiled the Nikola One, a hydrogen-electric truck, at a dramatic event in Salt Lake City. The truck looked real. The specifications sounded real. Reservations poured in.

What fleet operators didn't know: the truck on stage had no functioning drivetrain. It was a shell. The promotional video showing the Nikola One cruising down a highway? The truck had been towed to the top of a hill and rolled down. No engine, no fuel cell, no deception disclosed.

This pattern—stunning visuals masking hollow progress—would define Nikola. Milton was exceptional at raising money and generating media coverage. Partnerships were announced with Bosch, Ryder, Anheuser-Busch. Each announcement moved the stock. By 2020, Nikola went public through a SPAC merger with VectoIQ at a $3.3 billion valuation.

The market, drunk on EV mania and Tesla's success, sent Nikola's stock soaring. At its peak in June 2020, Nikola's market cap briefly exceeded Ford's—$29 billion for a company that had never manufactured or sold a single truck. Milton became a billionaire on paper. He posted constantly on social media, attacking critics, promising revolution, selling the dream.

Then the façade cracked. In September 2020, Hindenburg Research published a report titled "Nikola: How to Parlay an Ocean of Lies into a Partnership with the Largest Auto OEM in America." The report was devastating: the rolled-down-hill truck, faked dashboard displays, hydrogen production claims that were impossible with existing technology, a "natural gas" that was actually sourced conventionally. The evidence was damning, methodical, public.

Milton attacked Hindenburg, called the report a hit job by short sellers. But within days, General Motors—which had just agreed to take an 11% stake and manufacture Nikola's electric truck—revised its deal significantly. Within two weeks, Milton resigned as executive chairman. The stock collapsed.

Federal investigations followed. In July 2021, Milton was indicted on three counts of fraud. Prosecutors argued he'd lied to investors about "nearly all aspects of the business." The trial in 2022 revealed the extent of the deception: Milton had claimed Nikola produced hydrogen at below-market rates when it didn't produce any; he'd implied the company had built working trucks when it hadn't; he'd overstated partnerships and capabilities at every turn.

In October 2022, Milton was convicted on three of four counts. He was sentenced to four years in prison in December 2023. The judge noted that Milton "lied to the company's investors and deceived them," causing losses in the hundreds of millions.

Meanwhile, Nikola the company tried to survive its founder. It delivered its first production trucks—battery-electric Tre models, manufactured by Iveco in partnership—in 2021. The hydrogen truck remained vaporware. The company burned cash, diluted shareholders repeatedly, and struggled to scale. In 2023, it delivered fewer than 100 trucks. The infrastructure promises—hydrogen fueling stations across North America—remained mostly unfulfilled.

By 2024, Nikola's stock traded for under a dollar, down from its 2020 peak of $93. The market cap was less than $200 million. Production continued in small numbers, but the company was a penny stock zombie, kept alive by sunk costs and faint hopes.

The Nikola story is a cautionary tale about hype, timing, and fraud. Milton understood that in 2020's frothy market, narrative could temporarily substitute for product. He wasn't wrong about the opportunity—electrification of trucking is real, hydrogen may play a role. But he mistook salesmanship for engineering and believed his own pitch. The market, desperate for the next Tesla, suspended disbelief until it couldn't.

What remains is wreckage: investors who lost billions, a founder in prison, a company limping toward irrelevance, and a reminder that eventually, someone asks to see the truck actually drive.
