# Nikola

***The hydrogen hype that fooled Wall Street***

> **Years:** 2014-2023

Trevor Milton grew up in Utah, dropped out of college, and bounced through sales jobs before founding his first company — dHybrid Systems — which sold natural gas storage systems for trucks. He sold it in 2014 and walked away with enough money and confidence to try something bigger. Much bigger. He wanted to build the Tesla of heavy trucks, except better than Tesla, cleaner than Tesla, using hydrogen instead of batteries.

In 2014, he founded Nikola Motor Company, named after Nikola Tesla, the inventor Elon Musk's company was also named after. The symmetry was intentional. Milton positioned Nikola as the anti-Tesla — a company that would dominate the one market Tesla couldn't crack: long-haul trucking. Battery-electric semis, Milton argued, would never work for cross-country freight. Too heavy, too slow to charge, too limited in range. Hydrogen fuel cells were the answer. Fill up in minutes, drive 500 miles, zero emissions. He promised a future where Nikola would build both the trucks and the fueling infrastructure — a vertically integrated hydrogen ecosystem.

The first truck design, the Nikola One, was unveiled in 2016 at a Salt Lake City event that felt more like a Steve Jobs keynote than a trucking conference. Sleek renders, dramatic lighting, Milton on stage in jeans and a black shirt, promising a revolution. He showed a prototype. It looked real. Fleet operators took reservations. The company claimed billions in pre-orders. Milton's pitch was magnetic: early Tesla investor without the Tesla valuation, first-mover in hydrogen, savior of the climate and the trucking industry.

But Nikola had no revenue. It had no production facility. It had barely tested the technology. What it had was Milton's charisma, slick marketing, and a vision ambitious enough to suspend disbelief.

The company went public in June 2020 via a SPAC merger — the hot financial instrument of that moment — and the market went wild. Nikola's valuation hit $28 billion within days, briefly surpassing Ford. It had delivered zero trucks. Milton became a billionaire on paper. Institutional investors piled in. General Motors announced a $2 billion partnership. Nikola seemed to be the next Tesla, the next SpaceX, the next Musk-scale disruption.

Then the short-seller report dropped.

In September 2020, Hindenburg Research published "Nikola: How to Parlay an Ocean of Lies into a Partnership with the Largest Auto OEM in America." It was devastating. Hindenburg accused Milton of lying about nearly everything: the technology, the partnerships, the pre-orders, even basic demonstrations. The most damning claim involved a 2018 promotional video showing the Nikola One driving down a highway. Hindenburg alleged the truck wasn't driving — it was rolling downhill. Nikola had towed it to the top of a slope and filmed it coasting.

Nikola issued a rebuttal that didn't help. The company admitted the truck in the video was not self-propelled but insisted it had never claimed otherwise. "In motion" was not the same as "under its own power," they argued. Investors weren't convinced. The stock collapsed. GM renegotiated its partnership into something much smaller. Within two weeks, Milton resigned as executive chairman. He denied all fraud allegations.

Federal prosecutors disagreed. In July 2021, Milton was indicted on three counts of fraud. The indictment painted a picture of systematic deception: false claims about production timelines, fake orders from customers, fabricated demonstrations, misleading statements to investors. The case went to trial in 2022. Prosecutors argued Milton had knowingly lied to inflate the stock price and enrich himself. Milton's defense claimed he was an optimistic entrepreneur, not a criminal — that startup founders exaggerate, and that's not fraud.

In October 2022, a jury convicted him on three counts. He was acquitted on one. In December 2023, he was sentenced to four years in prison.

Meanwhile, Nikola the company continued without him, though barely. It pivoted away from hydrogen and toward battery-electric trucks — the very technology Milton had dismissed. It began delivering small numbers of trucks to customers, real trucks this time, but the company burned cash and struggled to scale. The grand vision of a hydrogen network never materialized. By 2023, Nikola was a penny stock, valued at a fraction of its peak, still not profitable, still not clear it would survive.

The Nikola story became a parable. It had everything: a charismatic founder, a climate-friendly narrative, buzzy technology, institutional validation, SPAC-era exuberance, and then the unraveling. It revealed how much the market wanted to believe in the next Tesla, how powerful storytelling could substitute for substance, and how long a company could survive on narrative alone — until it couldn't.

Milton went to prison. Nikola limped on. The hydrogen revolution in trucking never came. The path that seemed to lead toward Tesla-scale dominance led instead to disgrace, indictment, and collapse — a reminder that hype and hope are not a business model.
