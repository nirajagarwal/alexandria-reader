# Fitbit

***The wearable pioneer that won early and lost the endgame***

> **Years:** 2007-2021

James Park and Eric Friedman started Fitbit in 2007 with a conviction that people would wear computers if the computers were small enough and served a clear purpose. Park had worked at CNET and several startups. Friedman had done stints at Epinions and other Valley companies. Neither came from hardware. Neither came from fitness. They came from software, from the web, from data. They looked at the nascent smartphone sensor revolution — accelerometers becoming cheap, wireless protocols maturing — and saw an opportunity to make the invisible visible: movement, activity, the body's daily rhythms translated into data.

The first Fitbit launched in 2009. It was a clip, not a wristband. You attached it to your pocket or bra. It counted steps. It tracked sleep if you wore it with a wristband at night. It synced wirelessly to a web dashboard. The industrial design was minimal. The value proposition was clear: know how much you move. The price was $99. Pre-orders came in before the product shipped. The quantified self movement was emerging — small, passionate, eager for tools. Fitbit found its early adopters there.

The timing was exquisite. The iPhone had launched two years earlier. Apps were exploding. The idea of personal data, of tracking yourself, was shifting from weird to aspirational. Nike had the FuelBand. Jawbone had the UP. But Fitbit shipped first, iterated faster, and understood something crucial: most people don't want to be athletes. They want to move a little more. Ten thousand steps became the goal. The metric was arbitrary but perfect. It was achievable. It was gamifiable. It was social.

The company raised a Series A in 2008, shipped product in 2009, and by 2010 was doing real revenue. They expanded the product line. The Fitbit Ultra added an altimeter to count floors climbed. The Zip was cheaper. The One was sleeker. But the real breakthrough came in 2013 with the Fitbit Flex — a wristband, not a clip. It looked like a bracelet. You could wear it all day without thinking about it. It didn't look like medical equipment. It looked like jewelry, or almost like jewelry. Sales exploded.

By 2015, Fitbit owned the wearable fitness market. They had 85% market share. They went public that June. The IPO priced at $20. It opened at $30.40. By the end of the day, the company was worth $6.5 billion. Park and Friedman had built a hardware business in the smartphone era, a fitness business in the software era, a consumer electronics company without Apple's resources or Garmin's experience. They had done it by being early, focused, and relentless about distribution — selling through Best Buy, Target, Costco, everywhere people shopped.

The peak was brief. Apple launched the Apple Watch in April 2015, two months before Fitbit's IPO. The first version was slow, expensive, confused. It didn't seem like a threat. But Apple iterated. The watch got faster, cheaper, clearer in purpose. Fitbit had pioneered the market. Apple had the ecosystem. By 2016, Apple was outselling Fitbit. By 2017, it wasn't close.

Fitbit tried to evolve. They acquired Pebble's assets in 2016 for $23 million — a smartwatch pioneer brought low by the same forces threatening Fitbit. They launched their own smartwatch, the Ionic, in 2017. It flopped. The reviews were brutal: too slow, too buggy, too little app support. They tried a lighter version, the Versa, in 2018. It sold better but couldn't reverse the slide. The Apple Watch had become the watch. Samsung had Galaxy Watch. Garmin dominated the serious athlete market. Fitbit was squeezed.

The company pivoted toward health. They launched Fitbit Premium, a subscription service for insights and coaching. They pursued partnerships with health insurers and employers. They pitched Fitbit as a clinical tool, not just a consumer gadget. It made strategic sense — wearables were generating medically useful data, and health systems were interested. But it was a slow build. Consumer revenue was collapsing faster than health revenue could grow.

The stock, which had touched $50 in 2015, traded below $3 by 2019. Park remained CEO through it all. The company cut staff, consolidated product lines, tried to stabilize. They had brand recognition. They had tens of millions of users. They had years of health data. But the consumer market had moved. Wearables were either smartwatches or niche athletic tools. The middle ground — Fitbit's ground — was disappearing.

Google announced the acquisition in November 2019 for $7.35 a share, roughly $2.1 billion. Less than a third of the IPO valuation. The deal closed in January 2021 after regulatory reviews. Google wanted the health data platform, the user base, the hardware expertise for its own wearable ambitions. Park stayed on briefly, then left. The Fitbit brand remained, for now, but as a Google product line, not an independent company.

Fitbit had pioneered the category, educated the market, and built a real business. They made wearables mainstream. They made step-counting a cultural habit. They sold over 100 million devices. But they couldn't defend against platform players with infinite resources and vertical integration. They were too big to stay niche, too small to fight Apple. They won the early game and lost the endgame. The path that created the market wasn't the path that could hold it.
