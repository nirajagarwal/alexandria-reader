# Netscape

***The browser that won the web and lost the war***

> **Years:** 1994-2003

In early 1994, Marc Andreessen was twenty-two years old and recently arrived in Silicon Valley. He had spent the previous year at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications at the University of Illinois, where he and a small team built Mosaic, the first web browser that made the internet visual and accessible. Mosaic spread like wildfire through universities and research labs, but Andreessen saw nothing from it—NCSA owned the code, and he'd moved on.

Jim Clark saw something different. Clark had founded Silicon Graphics and made a fortune, but he'd been pushed out by his own board. He was looking for the next big thing, and when he met Andreessen, he found it. The web was exploding. Mosaic had proven people wanted graphical browsers. But Mosaic was slow, buggy, and controlled by a university. Clark proposed they build a better browser and a company around it. They would move fast, hire the best engineers, and capture the internet before anyone else realized it was worth capturing.

They called it Mosaic Communications, then changed the name to Netscape after NCSA threatened to sue. Andreessen recruited most of his old Mosaic team. They worked in a cramped office in Mountain View, writing code around the clock. The goal was simple: build the best browser in the world and give it away for free. The business model would come later.

Netscape Navigator 1.0 launched in December 1994. It was faster than Mosaic, more stable, and infinitely more polished. Within weeks, it dominated. By early 1995, Netscape had 75% of the browser market. The company wasn't selling the browser—it was free for individuals, nominally paid for corporations—but the usage created a platform. Netscape sold server software to companies that wanted to build websites. The browser was the distribution channel. The servers were the revenue.

In August 1995, Netscape went public. It was one of the most anticipated IPOs in history. The company had been profitable for exactly one quarter. It was sixteen months old. On the first day of trading, the stock opened at $28, shot to $75, and closed at $58. Netscape's market cap hit $2.9 billion. Andreessen, still twenty-three, was worth $58 million. The IPO didn't just make Netscape rich—it ignited the dot-com boom. Suddenly, the internet wasn't a curiosity. It was a gold rush.

But Microsoft was watching. Bill Gates had dismissed the internet as a fad, but Netscape's success changed his mind. In December 1995, Gates issued his "Internet Tidal Wave" memo, redirecting the entire company toward the web. Microsoft would bundle a browser with Windows. They would give it away for free—actually free, not Netscape's honor-system free. They would crush Netscape.

Internet Explorer 1.0 was terrible. IE 2.0 was mediocre. But Microsoft had infinite resources and Windows on 90% of computers. With IE 3.0 in 1996, they closed the gap. Netscape's market share started to erode. The browser wars had begun.

Netscape fought back by betting on the future. If the browser was going to be free, the money had to come from somewhere else. They pivoted to enterprise software—web servers, intranet tools, commerce platforms. They tried to turn the browser into a platform itself, with Java and JavaScript making the web an application environment. The vision was right, but the execution was scattered. Netscape was trying to be ten companies at once.

Meanwhile, Microsoft ground them down. IE 4.0 launched in 1997, integrated into Windows so deeply that uninstalling it could break the operating system. PC manufacturers couldn't ship Netscape even if they wanted to—Microsoft wouldn't allow it. Netscape's market share dropped from 80% to 50% to 30%. Revenue growth stalled. The stock collapsed.

By 1998, Netscape was dying. The enterprise pivot hadn't worked. The browser market was lost. The company had no path to profitability. In November, AOL acquired Netscape for $4.2 billion in stock—a fortune, but a fraction of what it might have been. The acquisition was defensive. AOL wanted the engineers and the brand, but mostly it wanted to keep Netscape out of Microsoft's hands. Netscape's browser became AOL's browser. The enterprise products were folded into AOL's portfolio. The company dissolved.

Before it ended, Netscape made one final move that would outlive everything else. In January 1998, the company open-sourced the Netscape browser code, releasing it as the Mozilla project. It was a Hail Mary—if they couldn't win commercially, maybe they could build a community. The Mozilla code was a mess, and the project nearly died, but it survived. Years later, it became Firefox, the only browser that could challenge Microsoft's monopoly. Netscape lost, but it planted the seed for the next fight.

The Department of Justice sued Microsoft in 1998 for antitrust violations, citing the Netscape war as evidence. Microsoft lost. They were ordered to break up the company, though the ruling was overturned on appeal. But the damage was done. Microsoft's dominance was checked. The web stayed open.

AOL shut down Netscape's browser division in 2003. By then, IE had 95% market share. Netscape was a memory. But the company had already changed everything. It proved the internet could be commercial. It triggered the dot-com boom. It forced Microsoft to fight for the web instead of owning it. It turned the browser into the operating system of the future. Netscape lost the war, but it won the web.
