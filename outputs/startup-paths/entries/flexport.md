# Flexport

***The logistics startup that promised to modernize freight forwarding, scaled rapidly on venture capital, then hit the limits of software-first thinking in a hardware-heavy industry***

> **Years:** 2013-present

Ryan Petersen had spent years in the import-export business, moving goods from China to the United States. He knew the pain intimately: the phone calls, the faxes, the emails asking "where is my shipment?" The opacity of global freight forwarding drove him crazy. Customs brokers and freight forwarders operated like relics, gatekeepers to information that should be readily available. In 2013, he founded Flexport with a vision: bring software-era transparency and efficiency to the $2 trillion global logistics industry.

The timing seemed right. Amazon had trained consumers to expect real-time tracking. Manufacturers and retailers were globalizing supply chains. Yet the systems connecting them remained pre-internet — paper documents, phone calls, disconnected databases. Petersen saw an industry ripe for disruption, where even basic visibility could be revolutionary.

Flexport built a dashboard where clients could see their shipments in real time, manage documents digitally, and get instant quotes. For small and medium businesses importing from Asia, this was transformative. They'd been locked out by minimum volumes or forced to accept whatever service traditional forwarders offered. Flexport gave them Amazon-like experience for ocean freight.

The company raised money easily. Founders Fund led the Series A in 2015. The pitch was elegant: freight forwarding is a massive, fragmented industry with terrible user experience and fat margins. Apply software, aggregate demand, and capture huge value. Investors from Silicon Valley saw parallels to other industries software had eaten. Peter Thiel became a vocal supporter.

Revenue grew fast. Flexport signed thousands of small importers, then landed bigger fish — manufacturers, consumer brands, retailers seeking supply chain modernization. The company expanded beyond ocean freight into air, trucking, customs brokerage, warehousing. By 2019, Flexport had raised over $1 billion and reached a $3.2 billion valuation. It employed thousands. Petersen spoke at conferences about revolutionizing global trade.

But beneath the growth, tensions emerged. Freight forwarding, it turned out, was less software problem than it appeared. The hard parts weren't building dashboards — they were managing relationships with carriers, negotiating rates, handling exceptions, dealing with customs in dozens of countries, coordinating physical movement of containers. Software improved the customer interface, but the underlying work remained labor-intensive and low-margin.

Traditional forwarders had fat margins partly because they were inefficient, but also because they handled complexity clients didn't see. When a container got stuck in port, when documentation was wrong, when a trucker didn't show up — human expertise and relationships solved problems. Flexport hired armies of operations staff. The headcount scaled almost as fast as revenue. The margin profile started looking less like software, more like logistics.

Then COVID hit. In early 2020, as global trade seized up, Flexport seemed vulnerable. But the pandemic created chaos that actually favored Flexport's model. Traditional forwarders couldn't handle the volume swings and disruptions. Clients desperate for visibility flocked to Flexport's platform. The company thrived through 2020 and 2021 as supply chains convulsed. Revenue soared. The valuation hit $8 billion.

Petersen leaned in. Flexport raised $935 million in February 2022 at an $8 billion valuation and started hiring aggressively — thousands of new employees to capture the moment. The company acquired Shopify's logistics business for $2.1 billion, bringing fulfillment centers and millions of small merchants into the fold.

The bet was that the chaos was the new normal, that supply chains had permanently changed. It was wrong. By late 2022, freight rates were collapsing. The shipping boom reversed into a shipping recession. Container prices fell 80%. Demand for logistics services cratered. Flexport had built for a world that no longer existed.

The company burned through cash. The Shopify acquisition looked mistaken — fulfillment was yet another low-margin, operationally complex business far from Flexport's core. Layoffs started in 2022 and accelerated through 2023. Flexport cut 20% of staff, then more. The valuation plummeted in secondary markets.

In September 2023, the board made a dramatic move: Petersen stepped down as CEO. Dave Clark, the former Amazon executive who'd been brought in as COO months earlier, took over. It was a Silicon Valley rarity — a founder pushed aside while the company was still private. Clark immediately announced more cuts and a return to core business.

Three months later, Clark was out too. The board brought Petersen back as CEO in February 2024. The whiplash was extraordinary. Petersen returned to a smaller, chastened company. Flexport sold the Shopify logistics business back to Shopify for a fraction of what it paid. The focus narrowed: freight forwarding, stripped of the grander ambitions.

Flexport still exists, still serves thousands of clients, still brings useful technology to a legacy industry. But the story reveals limits. Software can improve freight forwarding — it can't transform it into a software business. The industry's complexity, its reliance on human relationships and physical operations, its low margins and cyclical nature — these proved more fundamental than the user interface problems Flexport solved.

The company raised over $2.3 billion. It showed that venture-scale capital could build a modern freight forwarder quickly. What it couldn't show was whether that forwarder could generate venture-scale returns. The jury remains out. Flexport continues to operate, smaller and more focused. Whether it finds a path to the outcome its investors expected, or whether it becomes a modest success in an industry resistant to disruption, depends on whether modern logistics is ultimately a software problem or a logistics problem that software can only improve at the margins.
