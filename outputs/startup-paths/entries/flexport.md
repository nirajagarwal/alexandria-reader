# Flexport

***The freight forwarder that raised billions to modernize logistics, then discovered the industry resists disruption***

> **Years:** 2013-present

Ryan Petersen spent years working in international trade before founding Flexport. He had run an import-export business, dealt with customs brokers, navigated the bureaucracy of moving goods across oceans. He knew the friction intimately: the phone calls and faxes, the opaque pricing, the days waiting to learn where a shipment actually was. The global logistics industry moved $4 trillion in goods annually using technology that felt decades old. Petersen saw an opening.

In 2013, he launched Flexport as a tech-enabled freight forwarder. The pitch was elegant: traditional freight forwarders were middlemen who coordinated shipping but provided little visibility or control. Flexport would build software that gave customers real-time tracking, transparent pricing, and data analytics on their supply chains. It would compete not by owning ships or planes — the capital requirements were prohibitive — but by being the interface layer between shippers and carriers. Technology would compress weeks of back-and-forth into minutes of clicks.

The timing looked perfect. E-commerce was exploding. Companies increasingly needed complex global supply chains but lacked the expertise to manage them. Amazon had proven that logistics could be a competitive advantage. Venture capitalists, flush with capital and hungry for the next massive unsexy industry to disrupt, paid attention.

Flexport raised $3 million in seed funding in 2014. By 2015, it had closed a $20 million Series A led by Founders Fund. Peter Thiel himself joined the board. The narrative was irresistible: software eating the world, now coming for an industry that still relied on spreadsheets and handshakes. Flexport signed up customers — initially small and mid-sized companies that lacked the volume to command attention from traditional forwarders. The software worked. Customers could see their containers moving across the Pacific in real time. They loved it.

Revenue grew quickly. Flexport expanded from ocean freight to air freight, customs brokerage, trucking. It opened offices in Hong Kong, Amsterdam, Shanghai. In 2017, it raised $110 million at a $900 million valuation. The company was becoming a full-service logistics provider, but with software at the core. Petersen spoke about building the "operating system for global trade." Investors believed him. By 2019, Flexport had raised over $1 billion and reached a $3.2 billion valuation. SoftBank's Vision Fund led a $1 billion round.

The company hired aggressively. Engineers built more features. Salespeople chased enterprise customers. Flexport acquired companies to fill gaps in its service offering. It purchased a customs brokerage, a trucking company, warehouse space. The strategy was vertical integration with a tech veneer — own enough of the stack to control the customer experience, differentiate through software.

Then 2020 arrived. The pandemic turned global supply chains into chaos. Shipping rates exploded. Container availability collapsed. Port congestion became catastrophic. For Flexport, this was both opportunity and crisis. Revenue surged as customers desperate for capacity turned to anyone who could move freight. But the chaos exposed the limits of Flexport's model. When capacity disappeared, software couldn't conjure ships. When ports backed up, dashboards couldn't speed unloading. Flexport was still fundamentally a freight forwarder, subject to the same constraints as every other forwarder. The technology provided visibility into the disaster, not immunity from it.

Flexport handled the surge poorly. Customer service deteriorated under the volume. The software struggled with edge cases the pandemic created. Some customers, expecting tech-company responsiveness, encountered the same delays they faced with traditional forwarders. The narrative began to crack. Flexport wasn't revolutionizing logistics — it was digitizing it. Not nothing, but not everything.

In 2022, as supply chains normalized and freight rates collapsed, Flexport's growth stalled. The company had hired for a boom that ended abruptly. Revenue growth slowed dramatically. Losses mounted — Flexport had been burning cash for years, betting on growth to cover fixed costs. In October 2022, it laid off 20% of staff. Petersen acknowledged the company had over-hired and over-expanded.

Shopify, the e-commerce platform, offered a lifeline. In 2023, Shopify acquired Flexport's logistics business for a stake in the combined entity, and Shopify's head of product, Tobi Lütke, joined Flexport's board. Petersen stepped down as CEO. Dave Clark, a former Amazon executive who had briefly led Flexport, also departed after a tumultuous tenure. Petersen eventually returned as CEO in 2024, but the company had shrunk. More layoffs followed. The valuation, once over $8 billion at its 2022 peak, had cratered.

Flexport today operates as a scaled-back logistics company with good software. It still has customers who value the platform. It still moves billions in goods. But the vision of transforming global trade through technology looks more distant. The logistics industry, it turns out, is hard to disrupt. Margins are thin. Relationships matter. Volatility is endemic. Software helps but doesn't overcome the fundamental physics of moving containers across oceans or the entrenched power of shipping lines and port operators.

Flexport raised over $2.3 billion on the premise that logistics was ripe for a tech revolution. It proved logistics could be made more transparent and user-friendly. What it couldn't prove was that this alone justified the valuations, the burn rate, or the belief that a software layer could fundamentally reshape an industry defined by physical assets, regulatory complexity, and cyclical economics. The company persists, smaller and soberer, still seeking a path to profitability. The story isn't over, but the unicorn narrative is.
