# Ring

***The doorbell camera that survived death, a pivot, and Amazon***

> **Years:** 2013-2018

Jamie Siminoff stood in his garage in 2011, frustrated. He was building a WiFi-enabled doorbell because he kept missing package deliveries while working. The device would send video to his phone when someone rang. Simple enough. What he didn't know was that this frustration would lead him through a very public rejection, a complete rebrand, and ultimately a sale that would make him a symbol of vindication.

The original product was called DoorBot. Siminoff, a serial entrepreneur who'd sold his previous company, launched it in 2013 after raising money on Christie Street, a crowdfunding platform he himself had co-founded. The irony wasn't lost on him. DoorBot worked — a camera, a speaker, motion detection, cloud recording — but it looked like what it was: a first-generation hardware product built by a software entrepreneur. Bulky. Utilitarian. The kind of thing early adopters forgive but mainstream consumers ignore.

In late 2013, Siminoff did what founders of promising-but-struggling hardware startups do: he applied for Shark Tank. The appearance aired in November 2013. He asked for $700,000 for 10% equity. The Sharks circled. Kevin O'Leary called it "a worthless product." The segment ended with all five Sharks declining to invest. The clip would later be replayed millions of times as a lesson in how wrong investors can be.

But in the moment, it was just rejection on national television. Except for one thing: the exposure worked. DoorBot's website crashed from traffic. Orders flooded in. The product was getting attention even if the Sharks didn't bite. Siminoff realized he had something, but he also realized DoorBot as a name and brand wasn't going to carry it far enough. The product was good. The packaging was wrong.

In 2014, he rebranded. DoorBot became Ring. Same product, better name, cleaner design. The shift was more than cosmetic. Ring positioned itself not as a doorbell replacement but as the first line of defense for your home. Security, not convenience. The market was larger, the value proposition sharper, the customer's willingness to pay higher. This wasn't about missed packages anymore. It was about knowing who's at your door, recording evidence, deterring theft.

The rebrand worked. Ring began growing quickly. Siminoff raised a Series A from DFJ and other investors. Then more rounds followed. Ring expanded the product line: spotlight cameras, floodlight cameras, stick-up cameras, alarm systems. Each product integrated with the Ring app and the growing network of Ring users. The company wasn't just selling cameras; it was building an ecosystem.

The ecosystem had network effects. Ring launched the Neighbors app, which let users share videos of suspicious activity in their area. A theft caught on one Ring camera could alert an entire neighborhood. Police departments began partnering with Ring, requesting footage for investigations. Privacy advocates raised alarms, but the value proposition for homeowners was clear: your camera protects you, but the network protects everyone.

By 2016, Ring had raised over $200 million. Revenue was accelerating. The company was on track to hit $100 million in sales. But hardware is brutal. Margins are thin. Customer acquisition is expensive. Siminoff was burning cash to grow, betting that scale would eventually deliver profitability. The math wasn't guaranteed.

Amazon noticed. In early 2018, Amazon acquired Ring for a reported $1 billion. The deal made sense for both sides. Ring got Amazon's resources, distribution, and integration with Alexa. Amazon got a foothold in home security, a category it didn't dominate, and access to a network of devices and data that fit its ambient computing vision. Siminoff stayed on to run Ring as a subsidiary.

The acquisition made Siminoff a symbol. The Shark Tank rejection became retroactive proof of investor blindness. The clip was re-edited, shared, memed. Siminoff appeared on shows to talk about perseverance. The narrative was irresistible: rejected on TV, vindicated by billion-dollar exit. But the real story was less about vindication and more about iteration. DoorBot failed because it was positioned wrong. Ring succeeded because Siminoff listened, rebranded, expanded, and found the bigger market.

After the acquisition, Ring's growth accelerated further. Amazon integrated Ring devices across its ecosystem. By 2020, Ring was estimated to control more than 40% of the smart doorbell market. Police partnerships expanded. Privacy concerns grew louder. Ring became a case study in surveillance capitalism, neighborhood watch culture, and the tradeoffs between security and privacy.

For Siminoff, the path from garage frustration to billion-dollar exit took seven years. It required a public failure, a rebrand, a pivot from convenience to security, and the luck of timing — entering a market just as home automation was becoming mainstream and privacy concerns hadn't yet hardened into regulation. The doorbell camera wasn't a new idea, but Ring found the frame that made it essential. That's the path that worked.
