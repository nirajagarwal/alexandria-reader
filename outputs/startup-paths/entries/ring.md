# Ring

***The doorbell that became a fortress, then a fortress that became a liability***

> **Years:** 2013–present

Jamie Siminoff stood in his garage in 2011, staring at the problem that would define the next decade of his life. He'd missed a delivery. Again. Not because he wasn't home — because he couldn't see who was at the door until it was too late. The solution seemed obvious: put a camera in the doorbell. Connect it to his phone. Answer the door from anywhere.

He called it DoorBot. The technology was straightforward enough — video streaming, Wi-Fi, motion detection. But the execution was harder than it looked. Doorbells need to work in rain, snow, extreme heat. They need to last years on a battery, or wire into ancient electrical systems that vary by house. They need to connect to phones reliably, instantly, because nobody waits for a doorbell to buffer.

Siminoff had sold a previous company and sunk his own money into DoorBot. He launched on Christie Street in 2013, raising about $364,000 from crowdfunding. The product worked, barely. Early adopters tolerated the glitches because the promise was so clear: never miss a delivery, see who's at your door, talk to visitors without opening it.

In 2013, he did what desperate founders do — he went on *Shark Tank*. Asked for $700,000 for 10% of the company. Demonstrated the product. Kevin O'Leary called it "stupid." The others passed. No deal. The segment aired, and Siminoff went home empty-handed but with something more valuable than a Shark Tank investment: millions of people had just seen his product work on television.

Orders flooded in. The name DoorBot was generic, easily confused. Siminoff rebranded to Ring — short, memorable, exactly what a doorbell does. He raised a proper Series A. Then another round. The product got better. Battery life improved. Video quality sharpened. The app became reliable. But Ring was still a doorbell company, competing for attention in the invisible infrastructure of the home.

The pivot came from watching what customers did with the product. They weren't just answering the door remotely. They were watching. Monitoring. Recording. Catching package thieves. Identifying suspicious activity. Ring wasn't a convenience product — it was a security product. Siminoff leaned in. He added spotlight cams, floodlight cams, stick-up cams. Ring became a whole-home security ecosystem, with the video doorbell as the entry point.

The business model shifted too. Hardware at near cost, then $3-10/month subscriptions for video storage and smart alerts. Razor and blades for the surveillance age. The subscriptions turned Ring from a one-time purchase into a recurring revenue stream. Investors noticed. So did Amazon.

By 2018, Ring was in over a million homes. Revenue was reportedly over $400 million. But scaling hardware is capital-intensive, especially hardware that needs to work flawlessly in every climate, on every electrical system, for years. Siminoff was raising larger and larger rounds — a reported $28 million Series B, then $109 million Series C. The company was growing, but it was burning cash to build manufacturing, inventory, and a customer support operation that could handle millions of doorbells ringing at once.

Amazon offered over $1 billion. Closed in February 2018. For Siminoff, it was validation after the Shark Tank rejection — a narrative too perfect for journalists to resist. For Amazon, it was a beachhead into the home. Alexa integration. Access to Ring's computer vision technology. A network of cameras that could eventually become infrastructure for delivery verification, logistics, maybe more.

Under Amazon, Ring exploded. Prime Day promotions. Integration with Amazon Key for in-home delivery. Partnerships with police departments — over 2,000 of them by 2021 — where Ring gave law enforcement access to a portal for requesting footage from Ring users during investigations. The Ring Neighbors app became a social network for crime and safety, where users shared clips of suspicious activity, package thieves, prowlers.

Then the problems started showing. Security researchers found vulnerabilities — weak default passwords, unencrypted video streams. Reports emerged of Ring employees watching customer video feeds. The police partnerships drew scrutiny — civil liberties groups called it surveillance infrastructure built by private enterprise, with minimal oversight. Ring's terms allowed it to respond to emergency requests from police without user consent or warrants.

The narrative fractured. What started as a product to see who's at the door became, for critics, a privatized panopticon. A network of cameras pointed at sidewalks, streets, neighbors, creating a database of video searchable by law enforcement. For supporters, it was crime reduction, community safety, evidence when it mattered. For opponents, it was surveillance creep, eroding privacy norms one doorbell at a time.

Siminoff stayed on through 2023, then stepped back. Ring remains a massive business within Amazon — estimates suggest it's in over 20 million homes. The video doorbell category it created now includes competitors from Google, Arlo, Eufy, dozens of others. The product works seamlessly. The subscription revenue compounds. The controversy persists.

Ring succeeded as a business — a billion-dollar exit, a dominant market position, a product category it invented and still leads. But it also became a symbol of the surveillance questions embedded in connected home devices. The doorbell that started as a solution to missed packages became infrastructure for watching, recording, and sharing what happens outside millions of front doors. Whether that's safety or surveillance depends on who you ask.
