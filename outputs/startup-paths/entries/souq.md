# Souq

***The Amazon of the Middle East — until it became Amazon***

> **Years:** 2005-2017

In 2005, Ronaldo Mouchawar and Samih Toukan were trying to solve a problem that barely existed yet in the Arab world: online shopping. The internet was still dial-up in most of the region. Credit card penetration was abysmal. Logistics infrastructure was decades behind the West. E-commerce seemed like an idea imported from a different planet.

They started anyway. Mouchawar, a Syrian who'd worked at Yahoo, and Toukan, a Jordanian entrepreneur who'd already sold one company, launched Souq.com as an auction site — a Middle Eastern eBay. The model made sense: let sellers come to you, avoid the inventory problem, build the marketplace first. For the first few years, it limped along. Auctions never caught fire. The trust wasn't there. Neither was the payment infrastructure.

In 2009, they pivoted. Souq became a retailer. They bought inventory, warehoused it, sold it direct. The shift was existential — from marketplace operator to full-stack e-commerce company. It meant capital requirements. It meant logistics. It meant betting that the infrastructure they needed would materialize around them as they built.

The bet paid off slowly, then suddenly. Smartphones arrived in the Middle East like a flood. By 2011, mobile internet leapfrogged desktop in adoption. A generation skipped PCs entirely and went straight to apps. Souq was there. They launched a mobile app early. They built payment systems around cash-on-delivery because that's what customers trusted. When parcels arrived at doors, drivers collected crumpled bills. It was analog and expensive, but it worked.

The logistics problem was harder. Addresses in Dubai or Riyadh weren't like addresses in New York. Street names were suggestions. Building numbers were theoretical. Souq drivers learned neighborhoods like taxi drivers, navigating by landmarks and phone calls. "I'm at the building with the green awning near the mosque." The company built a delivery network from scratch because no one else would.

By 2013, Souq was the largest e-commerce platform in the Arab world. They operated across six countries — UAE, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman. Each market had its own quirks. Saudi Arabia, the biggest prize, required careful navigation. Cultural norms shaped product selection. Gender segregation affected logistics. But the market was enormous and oil-rich and internet-hungry.

Competitors appeared. Noon, backed by Emirati billionaire Mohamed Alabbar, launched with huge capital. Amazon was always rumored to be coming. Alibaba circled. But Souq had the advantage of incumbency and local knowledge. They understood Ramadan shopping spikes. They knew which payment methods worked in which neighborhoods. They had relationships with customs authorities in a dozen ports.

The company raised capital in waves. Tiger Global, Naspers, IFC all invested. By 2016, Souq was valued at over $1 billion. They had 45 million visitors a month. The business was growing 80% year over year. They employed 3,000 people. The path seemed clear: keep scaling, dominate the region, eventually IPO.

Then Amazon called. Talks began in late 2016. At first, it seemed like a standard acquisition dance — due diligence, valuation debates, structure negotiations. But for Mouchawar and his investors, the calculus was complex. Souq was winning. The market was growing faster than almost anywhere on earth. Mobile-first consumers were easier to acquire than desktop-trained Westerners. Why sell now?

The answer was competition. Amazon was coming to the Middle East either with Souq or without it. If without, they would bring infinite capital and a global brand. Noon was already spending lavishly. The market could afford one dominant player. Maybe two. Not three. And Amazon offered something no local investor could: integration into a global system. Souq's merchants could access Amazon's worldwide marketplace. The technology stack would leap forward overnight.

In March 2017, Amazon announced the acquisition for $580 million — later reported as closer to $650 million with earnouts. It was Amazon's largest-ever acquisition outside the United States. For Mouchawar, it was validation and surrender in one. He'd built the thing that proved the market existed. Now the giant would take it from here.

The transition was bumpy. Amazon rebranded Souq slowly, testing what to keep and what to change. Cash-on-delivery stayed. The name lasted until 2019, when Souq.com became Amazon.ae and Amazon.sa. Mouchawar stayed on to run the Middle East operation. He became a vice president in Seattle's empire, a regional governor reporting to distant powers.

By 2024, the transformation was complete. The Souq brand was memory. Amazon's Middle East operation was growing but not dominating as expected. Noon held its ground. The market fractured more than anticipated. The inevitable conquest looked messier in execution.

Souq proved e-commerce could work in the Middle East. They built payment rails and logistics networks in markets everyone said weren't ready. They showed a path from zero to billion-dollar valuation. And then they showed something else: that even winners get absorbed. The founder who builds the category often doesn't own its maturity. Mouchawar created Amazon's entry. Whether that was the best exit or the last exit depends on what he wanted the ending to be.
