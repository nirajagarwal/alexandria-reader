# WorldCom

***The accounting fraud that became a $100 billion funeral pyre***

> **Years:** 1983-2002

In 1983, Bernard Ebbers was running a string of motels in Mississippi when he and a group of investors saw an opportunity in the breakup of AT&T. The deregulation of long-distance telephone service opened the market to competitors, and they launched Long Distance Discount Services (LDDS) with $650,000 in capital. Ebbers, who had no telecommunications experience but possessed an aggressive business instinct, became CEO in 1985.

The company grew through acquisition. Between 1991 and 1997, LDDS completed over sixty mergers, buying competitors and network capacity at a furious pace. In 1995, the company acquired Williams Telecommunications Group and adopted the WorldCom name. Ebbers operated on a simple premise: buy companies, cut costs, scale operations, and use the rising stock price to fund the next acquisition. It worked. The stock climbed from pennies to over sixty dollars per share.

The crown jewel came in 1998 when WorldCom acquired MCI Communications for $37 billion — at the time, the largest corporate merger in history. Overnight, WorldCom became the second-largest long-distance carrier in America. Wall Street celebrated. Ebbers appeared on magazine covers. The company's aggressive expansion seemed visionary as the internet boom accelerated demand for data transmission.

But the acquisition engine masked fundamental problems. WorldCom's organic growth was slowing. The long-distance business was becoming commoditized, with prices falling and competition intensifying. Sprint, AT&T, and new fiber-optic carriers were all fighting for the same enterprise customers. WorldCom's revenue growth depended on buying revenue, not earning it.

In 1999, Ebbers attempted to acquire Sprint for $129 billion, which would have created a telecommunications behemoth controlling much of America's voice and data traffic. The Department of Justice blocked the deal on antitrust grounds. It was the beginning of the end.

Without Sprint, WorldCom couldn't maintain its growth narrative. The stock began to fall. Ebbers, who had borrowed hundreds of millions of dollars against his WorldCom shares to fund other investments (including a timber company and a yacht-building business), faced margin calls. The board extended him over $400 million in corporate loans to prevent him from selling shares and cratering the stock further. He kept borrowing. The board kept lending.

By 2000, the telecommunications sector was collapsing. The dot-com bubble burst, taking demand for network capacity with it. Companies that had spent billions building fiber-optic networks found themselves with massive overcapacity. Bandwidth prices plummeted. WorldCom's revenue growth stalled, then reversed.

Inside the company, CFO Scott Sullivan and his team began manipulating the numbers. They reclassified ordinary operating expenses as capital expenditures, spreading costs over years instead of recognizing them immediately. This inflated earnings and hid losses. They also created fictitious revenue entries to meet analyst expectations. The fraud started small but accelerated as the company's real performance deteriorated. Quarter after quarter, WorldCom reported profits while burning cash.

In 2001, Ebbers assured investors that WorldCom remained strong. He told analysts the company would weather the downturn better than competitors. Behind the scenes, internal auditor Cynthia Cooper and her team were finding irregularies in the books. When they brought concerns to Sullivan, he told them to stop looking. They didn't.

In April 2002, the board forced Ebbers to resign as the stock fell below a dollar. In June, the internal audit team presented their findings: $3.8 billion in fraudulent accounting entries. Sullivan was fired. The number kept growing as investigators dug deeper. The eventual total reached $11 billion in accounting fraud — the largest in American history.

WorldCom filed for bankruptcy in July 2002 with $103 billion in assets, making it the largest bankruptcy filing ever at the time. Seventeen thousand employees lost their jobs. Shareholders lost virtually everything. The company's bonds became worthless. Pension funds and mutual funds that had invested in what seemed like a stable telecommunications giant watched billions evaporate.

The fraud had been surprisingly simple: moving numbers between columns in the accounting system. No sophisticated offshore schemes, no complex derivatives. Just reclassifying line costs as capital expenditures and inventing revenue that never existed. The auditors at Arthur Andersen — already reeling from their role in the Enron scandal — had failed to catch it.

Ebbers was convicted of fraud, conspiracy, and filing false documents. He received twenty-five years in federal prison. Sullivan pleaded guilty and testified against Ebbers in exchange for a reduced sentence. Other executives went to prison. The company's outside auditors faced civil lawsuits. The SEC implemented new rules requiring CEOs and CFOs to personally certify their financial statements.

WorldCom emerged from bankruptcy in 2004 as MCI Inc., shedding the toxic brand. Verizon acquired it in 2006 for $7.6 billion — a fraction of what WorldCom had paid for MCI eight years earlier. The name disappeared into Verizon's enterprise services division.

The WorldCom story is often grouped with Enron as a parable of corporate greed and accounting fraud. But the dynamics were different. Enron was arcane, complex, built on financial engineering. WorldCom was straightforward: a company that stopped growing, couldn't admit it, and lied. The fraud extended for years because everyone — executives, auditors, analysts, investors — wanted to believe the growth story. By the time anyone looked hard at the numbers, the fraud had become too large to hide and too expensive to unwind.
