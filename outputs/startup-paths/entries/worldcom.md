# WorldCom

***The fraud that became too big to hide***

> **Years:** 1983-2002

In 1983, Bernie Ebbers was a motel manager in Mississippi. He had no background in telecommunications, no technical expertise, no venture capital connections. What he had was timing and appetite. When AT&T was broken up that year, the long-distance market opened to competition. Ebbers and a group of small investors pooled $650,000 to start Long Distance Discount Services, later renamed WorldCom.

The strategy was simple: buy capacity wholesale from AT&T's network, resell it at a discount, undercut the big carriers on price. It worked. Small businesses that had paid inflated rates to AT&T switched eagerly. Within two years, WorldCom was profitable. Within five, it was growing fast enough to go public.

But Ebbers didn't want to build a company. He wanted to build an empire.

The telecommunications industry in the 1990s was consolidating rapidly. Ebbers saw that the winners would be those who controlled the most fiber, the most network capacity, the most customers. So he started acquiring. Between 1991 and 1997, WorldCom bought more than sixty companies. Some were small regional carriers. Others were significant players. The deals were financed through stock swaps and debt — WorldCom's rising share price made it currency for expansion.

The largest deal came in 1997: MFS Communications, which owned UUNET, the backbone provider carrying much of the internet's traffic. At $14 billion, it was the largest merger in tech history to that point. Suddenly WorldCom wasn't just a discount long-distance reseller. It owned critical internet infrastructure. Wall Street loved the story. The stock soared.

Then came the deal that made WorldCom a giant: MCI, the second-largest long-distance carrier in America. MCI had been negotiating with British Telecom when Ebbers swooped in with a $37 billion bid. It was hostile, audacious, and successful. Overnight, WorldCom became the second-largest telecommunications company in the world, carrying half of all internet traffic and serving millions of business customers.

Ebbers was celebrated as a visionary. He appeared on magazine covers. Analysts praised his strategy of "roll-up growth" — buying competitors, cutting costs, capturing economies of scale. The company's headquarters remained in Clinton, Mississippi, a small town where Ebbers still lived. He drove a pickup truck. He owned a ranch. He seemed like the anti-corporate CEO, building an empire without losing touch.

But the growth concealed a problem. The acquisitions were expensive. The debt was mounting. And the telecom market was changing. The dot-com boom had led to massive overinvestment in fiber optic capacity. By 2000, there was far more bandwidth than demand. Prices for long-distance and data services collapsed. Every major carrier saw revenues stagnate or decline.

WorldCom had always grown by acquiring revenue. Now revenue was flat, and the stock price was falling. Wall Street expected growth. Ebbers needed to show growth. So he began manufacturing it.

The fraud started small. In 1999, under pressure to meet earnings targets, WorldCom's CFO Scott Sullivan began reclassifying operating expenses as capital expenditures. The distinction is simple: operating expenses reduce profits immediately; capital expenditures are spread over time. By moving costs from one category to the other, WorldCom could report higher profits without any actual improvement in performance.

At first, the amounts were modest — millions, not billions. But as the telecom market worsened, the gap between reality and expectations widened. So the fraud escalated. By 2001, WorldCom was booking $3.8 billion in false profits. Quarterly earnings reports showed steady growth while the underlying business hemorrhaged cash.

Ebbers didn't invent the fraud, but he created the pressure that made it necessary. He borrowed personally against his WorldCom stock to finance other ventures — timber, hotels, a yacht-building company. As the stock fell, margin calls forced him to borrow more. The board extended him over $400 million in loans. He needed the stock price to recover. Sullivan needed to make the numbers work. Auditors asked questions, but Sullivan had answers. The fraud continued.

In March 2002, the SEC began investigating WorldCom's accounting. Ebbers resigned the next month, though he maintained ignorance of any wrongdoing. Internal auditors, suspicious of irregularities, began their own investigation. On June 25, 2002, Cynthia Cooper, WorldCom's head of internal audit, informed the board that the company had fraudulently inflated profits by $3.8 billion.

The number would eventually reach $11 billion — the largest accounting fraud in U.S. history to that point.

WorldCom filed for bankruptcy on July 21, 2002. It was the largest bankruptcy in American history, displacing Enron, which had collapsed just seven months earlier. Seventeen thousand employees lost their jobs. Investors lost $180 billion in market value. Pension funds that had trusted WorldCom's blue-chip status were devastated.

Ebbers was convicted of fraud, conspiracy, and filing false statements in 2005. He was sentenced to twenty-five years in prison. Sullivan received five years after cooperating with prosecutors. The company that Ebbers built from $650,000 in a Mississippi motel room had become too big to survive its own lies.

WorldCom emerged from bankruptcy in 2004 as MCI Inc., a smaller, cleaner company. Verizon acquired it in 2006. The brand disappeared entirely. What remained was a cautionary tale: growth at any cost becomes cost at any price.
