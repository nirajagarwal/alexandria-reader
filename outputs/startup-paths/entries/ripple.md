# Ripple

***The cryptocurrency that defied death by becoming something else entirely***

> **Years:** 2012-present

In 2011, Jed McCaleb had already built one internet phenomenon. Mt. Gox, his bitcoin exchange, was handling the majority of the world's bitcoin trading volume when he sold it to Mark Karpeles. But McCaleb saw bitcoin's fundamental flaw: it was slow, wasteful, and couldn't scale to handle real payment volumes. He started designing something better.

The idea wasn't entirely new. Ryan Fugger had created a system called RipplePay in 2004, envisioning a peer-to-peer network for IOUs between trusted parties. McCaleb saw how to merge Fugger's vision with cryptocurrency technology — but without bitcoin's energy-hungry mining. He recruited Chris Larsen, who had co-founded the online lender E-LOAN and then Prosper, bringing financial industry credibility and operational experience. In 2012, they launched OpenCoin, later renamed Ripple Labs, with Fugger's blessing to build on his concept.

The Ripple protocol they designed was technically elegant. Instead of proof-of-work mining, transactions would be validated by a consensus of trusted nodes. Settlement would take seconds, not minutes or hours. The network could handle 1,500 transactions per second versus bitcoin's seven. And crucially, it was designed to bridge currencies — dollars to euros to bitcoin to anything else — making it a protocol for the financial system rather than a replacement for it.

They pre-mined 100 billion XRP tokens at inception. Ripple Labs would hold the majority, using them to incentivize adoption and fund operations. This decision would haunt them for years. To bitcoin purists, pre-mining was sacrilege. Cryptocurrency was supposed to be decentralized, mined by anyone with computing power. Ripple looked like a company playing at crypto while maintaining corporate control.

McCaleb departed in 2013 amid disagreements about direction, later founding Stellar as a more decentralized alternative. But Ripple pressed forward with a bet that seemed counterintuitive in cryptocurrency circles: they would court the very banks that crypto was supposedly disrupting.

The pitch to financial institutions was compelling. Cross-border payments were a nightmare of correspondent banking relationships, SWIFT messages, and settlement delays. A transaction from the US to Thailand might pass through five intermediary banks, taking days and losing 6-7% to fees. Ripple offered near-instant settlement at a fraction of the cost. Banks could hold XRP as a bridge currency or use Ripple's technology without the token at all.

Initial traction came from smaller banks and remittance companies willing to experiment. Spain's Santander became an early adopter. American Express partnered with Ripple for business payments to the UK. MoneyGram, the remittance giant, signed on. Each partnership was trumpeted as validation. But adoption remained limited. Banks were interested in the technology, skeptical of the token.

The regulatory hammer fell slowly, then all at once. In December 2020, the Securities and Exchange Commission filed suit against Ripple Labs, Larsen, and current CEO Brad Garlinghouse. The charge: conducting an unregistered securities offering by selling XRP. If XRP was a security, not a currency, then Ripple had violated decades of securities law.

The lawsuit exposed Ripple's central contradiction. They claimed XRP was a decentralized cryptocurrency like bitcoin. But the SEC pointed out that Ripple Labs controlled the vast majority of tokens, funding operations by selling them. They marketed XRP's price appreciation. They were, the SEC argued, selling shares in a common enterprise — the textbook definition of a security.

The case should have killed Ripple. Bitcoin and Ethereum were arguably decentralized enough to avoid securities classification. But projects with corporate parents, pre-mined tokens, and ongoing sales? The SEC seemed to be drawing a line, and Ripple was on the wrong side.

XRP's price crashed. Exchanges delisted the token. Banking partners quietly distanced themselves. The company had raised hundreds of millions but now faced existential legal risk. Larsen and Garlinghouse faced potential personal liability. The smart play was settlement, accepting restrictions, moving on.

Instead, Ripple fought. They spent over $200 million on legal fees, hired former government officials, and mounted an aggressive defense. The bet was that proving XRP wasn't a security would unlock massive value — both for the company's holdings and for the broader crypto industry watching nervously.

In July 2023, Judge Analisa Torres delivered a split decision that felt like victory. She ruled that XRP sales on public exchanges were not securities offerings because purchasers had no reasonable expectation of profit from Ripple's efforts. But institutional sales directly from Ripple to sophisticated investors were securities. It was a nuanced ruling in a space used to binary outcomes.

Ripple declared victory. XRP's price doubled. But the ruling solved less than it seemed. Institutional sales were still restricted. The SEC appealed. Banking adoption remained limited. The company had survived but not yet thrived.

By 2024, Ripple had evolved into something unexpected: a blockchain infrastructure company that happened to hold enormous amounts of a cryptocurrency. They offered enterprise blockchain solutions. They invested in crypto startups. They lobbied aggressively for favorable regulation. XRP existed, sometimes useful as a bridge asset, often just there — a legacy of the original vision, neither dead nor fully alive.

The company endures not because it achieved its original vision of revolutionizing cross-border payments through XRP, but because it had the resources to outlast a regulatory assault and the flexibility to become something else. Ripple proved that a well-funded crypto project could survive by adaptation, political engagement, and sheer attrition. Whether that survival leads to eventual triumph or just延长的 irrelevance remains the question XRP holders keep asking.
