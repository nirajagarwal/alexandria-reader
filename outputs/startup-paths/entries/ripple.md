# Ripple

***The blockchain company that fought the government and survived***

> **Years:** 2012-present

In 2004, a Canadian web developer named Ryan Fugger created RipplePay, a decentralized monetary system meant to let communities create their own currencies. It went nowhere. But the name caught the attention of Jed McCaleb, who had founded eDonkey (a file-sharing network that Metallica helped sue into oblivion) and Mt. Gox (the Bitcoin exchange he sold before it spectacularly collapsed). McCaleb saw something different: a way to make banks move money faster using blockchain technology.

In 2012, McCaleb recruited Chris Larsen, a veteran entrepreneur who had built E-LOAN and Prosper, two lending platforms that had taken on banks. Together they reimagined Ripple as OpenCoin, building a new protocol from scratch. They convinced Fugger to hand over the name. Their pitch was simple: Bitcoin was too slow and energy-intensive to handle real payment volumes. Ripple would be the grown-up version — fast, cheap, and built for institutions.

The technology was clever. Instead of mining like Bitcoin, Ripple used a consensus protocol. Transactions settled in seconds, not minutes. The system could handle thousands of transactions per second, versus Bitcoin's handful. And crucially, it didn't require every participant to download the entire blockchain. Banks could actually use this.

But there was a catch. The protocol came with a native token called XRP. Ripple Labs (as OpenCoin renamed itself) created 100 billion XRP at genesis and kept the majority. The company claimed XRP was necessary as a bridge currency — a way to move value between different fiat currencies without pre-funded accounts everywhere. Critics saw something else: a company printing its own money, calling it a utility token, and selling it to fund operations while claiming it wasn't a security.

McCaleb left in 2013, eventually founding Stellar, a competing protocol. He took billions of XRP with him, which he was contractually obligated to sell slowly. This created a permanent overhang — one of the largest holders was a disgruntled ex-founder who might dump tokens at any moment.

Ripple began signing up banks. In 2016, they announced partnerships with Santander, UBS, UniCredit. The headlines were impressive. The reality was murkier. Banks were testing the technology, but few were using XRP. They liked the messaging protocol — a way to send payment instructions securely — but saw no reason to touch the token. Ripple had built two products: xCurrent (the messaging layer banks wanted) and xRapid (which required XRP and banks didn't). Revenue came from selling XRP, not software licenses.

The crypto boom of 2017 changed everything. XRP's price exploded from under a cent to over $3. At peak, the outstanding XRP was worth more than $130 billion, making Ripple's founders paper billionaires. Larsen briefly appeared on the Forbes billionaires list. The company raised $55 million at a $10 billion valuation. But the rise attracted attention.

In December 2020, the Securities and Exchange Commission filed a lawsuit. The charge: Ripple had been conducting an unregistered securities offering since 2013, raising over $1.3 billion by selling XRP. The SEC argued that XRP holders were investing in Ripple's efforts, expecting profits from the company's work — the classic definition of a security under the Howey Test.

The lawsuit was an existential threat. Coinbase and other exchanges immediately delisted XRP. The token's price collapsed. Banks that had been testing Ripple grew cautious. The company had sold hundreds of millions in XRP quarterly to fund operations. That revenue stream vanished. Brad Garlinghouse, who had taken over as CEO in 2015, called it an attack on the entire crypto industry. The SEC was trying to regulate by enforcement, he argued, rather than providing clear rules.

Most companies would have settled. Ripple fought. They hired top lawyers and framed the case as defending not just themselves but the principle that tokens could be commodities, not securities. The crypto industry rallied behind them. Coinbase, facing its own regulatory pressure, filed an amicus brief. So did the Blockchain Association and the Chamber of Digital Commerce.

The case dragged through discovery and motions for three years. In July 2023, Judge Analisa Torres issued a split ruling. XRP sold to institutional investors was a security, she ruled. But XRP sold on exchanges to retail buyers was not — those buyers weren't investing in Ripple's efforts, they were trading a commodity. It was a partial victory that both sides claimed as validation.

The SEC appealed. Ripple counter-appealed. But the company had survived. XRP relisted on exchanges. The price recovered. Banks like SBI Holdings in Japan and financial institutions in the Middle East expanded their use of Ripple's technology. The company had found revenue beyond token sales — software licenses, transaction fees, consulting. They opened an office in Dubai and talked about an IPO once the legal situation clarified.

By 2024, Ripple had become the test case for how crypto companies could navigate US regulation. They had the resources to fight, the conviction to endure, and just enough legal victory to claim vindication. Whether XRP would ever see widespread bank adoption remained unclear. But Ripple had proven a company could bet its existence on a courtroom and emerge intact.

The path revealed something: in an industry built on decentralization, the companies that survived longest were those willing to engage with centralized power. The paradox of Ripple is that its success came not from disrupting banks but from convincing them it was safe, and not from avoiding regulators but from outlasting them in court. They built a blockchain for the establishment and nearly died for it, only to discover that fighting back was the most establishment move of all.
