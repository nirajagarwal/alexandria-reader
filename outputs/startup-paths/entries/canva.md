# Canva

***The design tool that made graphic design a commodity — by making it feel like play***

> **Years:** 2012-present

In 2011, Melanie Perkins was teaching students how to use desktop publishing software at the University of Western Australia. She watched them struggle with programs built for professionals — PhotoShop, InDesign, QuarkXPress — to create simple yearbooks and newsletters. The learning curve was vertical. The frustration was palpable. Most gave up before they finished.

Perkins saw the disconnect. Design software was built for experts who spent years mastering arcane toolsets. But the internet had created millions of people who needed to create graphics: social media managers, small business owners, teachers, bloggers, marketers. They didn't need industrial-strength tools. They needed something fast, intuitive, and good enough.

She started with a narrow wedge: Fusion Books, an online tool for creating school yearbooks. Simple templates. Drag-and-drop interface. It worked. Students who couldn't navigate Photoshop could build yearbooks in an afternoon. By 2012, the tool was used in hundreds of Australian schools. But Perkins knew the real opportunity was bigger. Every person with an internet connection occasionally needed to create something visual. The market wasn't graphic designers. It was everyone else.

She teamed up with her partner Cliff Obrecht and tech co-founder Cameron Adams. They pitched the vision as "democratizing design" — making professional-looking graphics accessible to anyone. Investors were skeptical. Design software was dominated by Adobe, a $30 billion incumbent with a decades-long moat of professional users and industry-standard formats. Convincing people to switch from Photoshop would require not just a better product but a different mental model. And the team was in Perth, Australia — about as far from Silicon Valley gravity as you could get while still speaking English.

They raised a small seed round from Australian investors in 2012 and launched the beta in 2013. The core insight was templates. Instead of starting with a blank canvas, users started with pre-designed templates for specific use cases: Facebook covers, Instagram posts, flyers, presentations, business cards. The templates were beautiful enough to use as-is and flexible enough to customize. You could create something passable in minutes instead of hours.

The interface was designed to feel like rearranging furniture, not manipulating pixels. Drag this text box. Drop in this photo. Change this color. No layers, no masks, no blend modes. The simplicity was a feature, not a bug. Power users complained that Canva couldn't do what Photoshop could. Perkins didn't care. Canva wasn't competing with Photoshop. It was competing with not making anything at all.

Growth was viral and bottom-up. A teacher made a classroom poster and shared it with colleagues. A social media manager at a small startup created branded Instagram posts and showed the founder. A real estate agent designed a flyer and told their office. Each user became a distribution node. The free tier was generous enough to be genuinely useful, which kept users around long enough to need premium features: more templates, stock photos, the ability to resize designs for different platforms with one click.

By 2014, Canva had a million users. By 2016, ten million. The growth curve looked like a social network, not enterprise software. But the business model was SaaS: a freemium product with individual subscriptions ($12.95/month for Pro) and team plans. Revenue scaled with users, and users scaled virally. Investors who initially passed started paying attention. In 2015, Canva raised $15 million in a round led by Vayner Capital and Felicis Ventures at a $165 million valuation. By 2018, another round at $1 billion. Unicorn status reached with a product that felt more like a toy than enterprise software.

The competition tried to respond. Adobe launched Adobe Spark, a simplified design tool aimed at Canva's market. Microsoft added design features to PowerPoint and Office. Google launched a similar tool within Workspace. None caught fire. Canva had the network effects of templates — millions of user-created designs became new templates, creating a constantly expanding library. It had the muscle memory of an interface people had already learned. And it had the distribution of users bringing it into organizations rather than IT departments buying licenses.

Perkins kept expanding the surface area. Canva added video editing, website building, presentation tools, document design, print-on-demand services. Each feature pulled in a new use case. Each use case brought new users. The strategy was horizontal: become the default tool for all non-professional visual creation. By 2021, Canva had 60 million monthly active users and was valued at $40 billion after a funding round that made it one of the most valuable private tech companies in the world.

The design community bristled. Canva was making design "too easy," flooding the world with mediocre graphics based on templates. It was the McDonald's of design — fast, cheap, ubiquitous, and offensive to anyone who cared about craft. Perkins shrugged. The alternative wasn't that everyone would hire a designer or learn Photoshop. The alternative was worse design or no design. Canva democratized mediocrity, which was a massive step up from nothing.

The company's path avoided many classic startup failure modes. It didn't pivot away from the original insight. It didn't get acquired early by a desperate incumbent. It didn't scale too fast and implode. It didn't chase enterprise contracts that would have required redesigning the simplicity that made it work. Perkins stayed CEO through hypergrowth, unusual for a first-time founder building to this scale.

By 2024, Canva had over 170 million users in 190 countries. It was valued north of $40 billion. Perkins and Obrecht, still running the company from Sydney, were among Australia's wealthiest people. The product had become infrastructure: the default tool for creating anything visual that didn't require a professional designer. It had made graphic design a commodity by making it feel like play.

The outcome reveals the power of collapsing friction. Canva didn't invent new design capabilities. It made existing capabilities accessible by removing the need to learn. In a world where everyone needs to create, the tool that feels like no tool at all wins.
