# Canva

***From PowerPoint killer to design empire — the slow-build triumph of simplicity***

> **Years:** 2012–present

In 2007, Melanie Perkins was teaching design software at the University of Western Australia. She watched students struggle with basic tasks in Photoshop and InDesign — professional tools built for professionals, dense with features most people would never need. The gap was obvious: the world needed design, but design tools assumed expertise that most people didn't have and wouldn't acquire. Perkins saw the future differently: a web-based platform where anyone could create presentations, posters, invitations. Not design for designers. Design for everyone else.

She convinced her boyfriend Cliff Obrecht to join her. Together with Cameron Adams, a designer she met at a tech conference, they started small. Very small. Their first product wasn't Canva — it was Fusion Books, an online tool for creating school yearbooks. This was strategic. Yearbooks were perfect: a real use case, paying customers (schools), and a narrow enough problem to solve without infinite resources. Fusion Books launched in 2008. It worked. Schools bought it. By 2012, it was Australia's largest yearbook provider.

But Perkins wasn't building a yearbook company. She was learning how to build the thing she'd imagined in that classroom: a design platform for the entire internet.

The pitch for Canva was ambitious — and nobody wanted to fund it. Perkins and Obrecht spent three years trying to raise a seed round. Investors in Australia didn't understand the vision. Silicon Valley investors didn't want to invest in an Australian company with no US footprint. The product didn't exist yet, and the founding team had no technical co-founder who could build it. Perkins cold-emailed hundreds of investors. She got rejected by everyone.

Then she got an introduction to Bill Tai, a Valley investor, through Guy Kawasaki, Apple's former chief evangelist. Tai saw something. Kawasaki saw something. In 2012, Canva raised a small seed round. Perkins moved to San Francisco. She pitched relentlessly. Lars Rasmussen, co-founder of Google Maps, joined as technical advisor. Cameron Adams came on full-time as the technical co-founder they'd been missing.

Canva launched publicly in August 2013. The product was simple: drag-and-drop design in a browser. Templates for social media posts, flyers, presentations. Free tier with basic features. Paid tier with premium images and templates. No installation. No training. You could make something decent-looking in minutes.

The growth was not explosive. It was steady. Teachers used it for classroom materials. Small business owners used it for Facebook ads. Nonprofits used it for event posters. Canva wasn't competing with Adobe — it was serving people Adobe had never designed for. The market was vast and underserved.

Perkins was patient. While competitors raised massive rounds and burned fast, Canva grew deliberately. The company stayed in Sydney — not San Francisco — which kept costs lower and culture intact. They focused on product, not hype. They added features incrementally: photo editing, video tools, presentation mode, print fulfillment. Each addition expanded the use cases without breaking the simplicity.

By 2015, Canva had five million users. By 2017, ten million. Revenue grew alongside users because the business model worked: freemium with clear value in the paid tier. The paid product wasn't a trick. It was genuinely better, and people upgraded willingly.

In 2018, Canva raised $40 million at a $1 billion valuation. Unicorn status. But the company barely changed its rhythm. Perkins stayed CEO. The team stayed in Sydney. The product stayed simple. They kept adding capabilities — websites, whiteboards, video editing — but always behind the same interface, always approachable.

The pandemic accelerated everything. Remote work meant more presentations. Social distancing meant more digital content. Teachers moved online and needed materials fast. Small businesses needed to market digitally. Canva's user base exploded. By 2021, sixty million users. By 2023, over 150 million.

Canva's 2021 funding round valued the company at $40 billion — one of the most valuable private software companies in the world. Perkins became one of the world's youngest female billionaires. Still CEO. Still in Sydney. Still running the same company she'd imagined in that classroom in 2007.

The competition came, inevitably. Adobe launched Adobe Express, a simplified design tool aimed directly at Canva's market. Microsoft integrated design features into Office. Google beefed up Slides. But Canva had the advantage of focus. Adobe was a professional tool trying to be simple. Canva was a simple tool becoming more capable. The difference mattered.

Canva wasn't just simpler — it was faster, more collaborative, and built for the workflows of non-designers. It had millions of templates created by its community. It had integrations with the tools people already used. It had a brand that meant "easy design" in the way Photoshop meant "image editing."

By 2024, Canva was generating over $2 billion in annual revenue. Profitable. Global. Still private. Perkins showed no interest in an IPO. The company didn't need the capital, and going public would mean quarterly pressures that might distort the long-term vision.

Canva's path reveals a pattern: the power of serving the underserved, the advantage of patience, the compounding returns of simplicity. Perkins didn't disrupt Adobe by beating them at their own game. She built a different game. She identified a massive market — everyone who needed design but wasn't a designer — and built precisely for them. She stayed focused when focus was unfashionable. She grew steadily when steady growth looked slow.

The company that almost no one would fund became one of the most valuable software companies on earth. Not because it was the most technically sophisticated. Because it made something hard into something easy, and easy turned out to be worth billions.
