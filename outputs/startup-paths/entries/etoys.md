# eToys

***The $8 billion IPO that couldn't survive Christmas***

> **Years:** 1997-2001

In 1997, Toby Lenk saw what everyone saw but few believed: parents would buy toys online. Disney veteran Lenk had watched retail transform through catalog shopping and understood the pain of toy stores — limited selection, seasonal madness, stressed parents dragging children through aisles of temptation. The internet promised something better: browse at midnight, ship to your door, avoid the crowds.

He wasn't alone in seeing it. Amazon had launched the year before. But Lenk believed toys deserved a specialist. Parents wanted expertise, gift recommendations, age-appropriate suggestions. Amazon sold everything. eToys would be the toy authority online.

The timing seemed perfect. Internet penetration was accelerating. E-commerce infrastructure was maturing. The first generation of children raised on computers were becoming parents themselves. And venture capital was flooding into anything with ".com" after its name.

Sequoia Capital led a $20 million Series A in 1998. By then, eToys had built an impressive warehouse operation in California, stocked deep inventory, and created a website that actually worked — no small feat in 1998. The company shipped fast, wrapped gifts, included batteries. Parents loved it. Revenue grew from $700,000 in 1997 to $30 million in 1998.

Then came the IPO. In May 1999, at the peak of dot-com euphoria, eToys went public at $20 per share. It opened at $78. By day's end, the company was valued at $8 billion — more than Toys "R" Us, a century-old retailer with 1,500 stores and $11 billion in revenue. eToys had $30 million in revenue and had never posted a profit.

The valuation was absurd, but eToys wasn't alone. In 1999, investors valued growth over profit, vision over fundamentals, potential over reality. eToys raised $164 million in the IPO. It had capital to dominate.

The company spent aggressively. A $20 million marketing deal with Warner Bros. A Super Bowl ad costing $2.5 million. Partnerships with AOL and Yahoo. The purple logo plastered across billboards and buses. Brand awareness soared. So did costs.

The business model had a problem, though it wasn't obvious during the boom. Toys are seasonal. Sixty percent of sales happened in Q4. eToys needed massive warehouse capacity for Christmas but that capacity sat mostly empty the rest of the year. Shipping was expensive — toys are bulky, low-margin items. The company was selling $30 toys and spending $15 to ship them, often with free shipping promotions to win customers.

Customer acquisition costs were brutal. In the land-grab mentality of 1999, everyone believed scale mattered more than unit economics. Get big fast, dominate the category, profitability would follow. eToys spent $100 to acquire customers buying $40 worth of toys.

Revenue grew to $151 million in 1999. Losses grew faster: $190 million. For every dollar of revenue, eToys lost $1.26.

The company knew it needed to reach $750 million in revenue to break even at scale. The path was clear: spend more, grow faster, reach profitability through volume. In dot-com logic, this made sense.

Then the market turned. In March 2000, the NASDAQ peaked and began its collapse. Capital that had flowed freely dried up. Investors who had valued growth above all suddenly wanted profitable businesses. The IPO window slammed shut. Companies burning cash faced a reckoning.

eToys stock, which had touched $86, fell below $1 by December 2000. The company still had $30 million in cash, but it wasn't enough for another year of losses. Christmas 2000 would make or break the company.

They went all in. Massive inventory purchases. Aggressive marketing. The warehouse was full, the website ready, the customer service staffed. Then the orders came — too many orders. The system was overwhelmed. Shipments were late. Packages were wrong. Customer service collapsed under the volume. The very success eToys had spent millions pursuing destroyed its ability to deliver.

Parents who had ordered toys in November received them in January, after Christmas. After the moment that mattered. The angry calls and emails were relentless. The brand, which eToys had spent $100 million building, was damaged in weeks.

Revenue for 2000 hit $237 million, up from $151 million. But losses were $244 million. The company had $18 million in cash and $274 million in debt. In February 2001, after failing to secure additional funding, eToys filed for bankruptcy.

KB Toys bought the eToys brand and customer list for $5.4 million — a company once valued at $8 billion sold for the price of a modest house. The 500,000-square-foot warehouse was liquidated. The purple logo disappeared from the internet.

Toby Lenk had been right about the market. People would buy toys online. Amazon's toy section thrives. Target and Walmart dominate online toy sales. The category exists and is massive.

But eToys' path required perfection: flawless execution at massive scale, sustained capital access, profitable unit economics, and time to reach break-even volume. They got the growth. They missed everything else. In the land grab, they grabbed land they couldn't hold.
