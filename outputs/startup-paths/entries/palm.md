# Palm

***The research lab that built the future of mobile computing, sold it too early, and watched others reap the reward***

> **Years:** 1992-2010

In 1992, Jeff Hawkins was convinced that personal computers would eventually fit in your pocket. This was not a widely shared belief. Laptops weighed ten pounds. The idea of computing power shrinking to palm-size seemed like science fiction, or at best, a niche market for tech enthusiasts with too much money.

Hawkins had been designing portable electronics at GRiD Systems, where he'd watched the company's early pen-based computers fail to find a market. The technology was ahead of the interface. Users didn't need computers that tried to replace desktops. They needed something different: a device that did a few things exceptionally well. Hawkins spent weekends at a diner with a block of wood carved to the size he imagined a pocket computer should be, testing whether it felt right to carry, to hold, to use.

He founded Palm Computing with Donna Dubinsky and Ed Colligan in 1992. Their first product, the Zoomer, launched in partnership with Tandy and Casio, was a disaster. It tried to do everything and succeeded at nothing. The handwriting recognition didn't work. The interface was cluttered. It was too expensive. The company nearly died.

Hawkins went back to basics. He invented Graffiti, a simplified handwriting system that required users to learn new letter forms but worked reliably. He designed the interface around four core functions: calendar, contacts, tasks, and notes. Everything else was stripped away. The result was the Pilot 1000, launched in 1996.

It was a sensation. The Pilot cost $299, synced with desktop computers via a cradle, and did exactly what busy professionals needed without trying to replace their PCs. Palm sold more units in eighteen months than the entire pen computing industry had sold in its history. The company couldn't manufacture them fast enough. Computing magazines called it the most successful consumer electronics launch ever.

But Palm's corporate structure was a mess. The company had sold itself to U.S. Robotics in 1995 for survival capital. When 3Com acquired U.S. Robotics in 1997, Palm became a subsidiary of a subsidiary, buried inside a networking hardware company that didn't understand consumer electronics. Hawkins and Dubinsky chafed under 3Com's management. Decision-making slowed. Product development languished.

In 1998, Hawkins and Dubinsky left to found Handspring, licensing the Palm OS to compete with their former company. They launched the Visor, a Palm-compatible device with an expansion slot that let users add capabilities. It worked. Handspring captured market share while Palm, still inside 3Com, struggled to innovate.

Meanwhile, Microsoft saw the market Palm had created and wanted it. They launched Windows CE devices, spending hundreds of millions to evangelize their platform. The PocketPC grabbed headlines and developer attention. Palm's advantage was its installed base and simplicity, but the OS was aging. Written in the early 1990s, it couldn't handle modern demands: color screens, multimedia, wireless networking, multitasking.

3Com spun Palm out in an IPO in March 2000, at the peak of the dot-com bubble. The stock opened at $38 and closed the first day at $95, valuing Palm at $53 billion—more than the parent company that still owned most of it. It was absurd. Palm had revenue but unclear path to sustaining growth against Microsoft's assault.

The split left Palm divided. The hardware division remained Palm Inc. The software platform became PalmSource. This separation weakened both. Hardware couldn't control its destiny without owning the OS. Software couldn't ensure its platform evolved to serve users without controlling hardware innovation.

Palm tried to modernize. They acquired Handspring in 2003, reuniting the founders. They launched the Treo, a smartphone that combined the Palm OS with phone capabilities and a keyboard. It found an audience among business users—lawyers, doctors, executives who needed email on the go. But the Treo was expensive, and the OS underneath still couldn't compete with what was coming.

In 2007, Apple launched the iPhone. It wasn't just better than Palm; it was a different category. The touchscreen was responsive. The interface was intuitive. The App Store created an ecosystem. Palm's advantage—simplicity and installed base—evaporated. Suddenly, Palm devices felt ancient.

Palm tried one last reinvention. They hired Jon Rubinstein, who had led hardware development for the iPod. He greenlit Palm Pre, a new device with a new OS called webOS. It was beautiful. The interface was gesture-based, smooth, years ahead in multitasking design. It launched in June 2009 to rapturous reviews.

It failed. Sprint was the exclusive carrier, limiting reach. The app ecosystem was tiny compared to iPhone and the surging Android. Hardware quality was inconsistent—the Pre's slider mechanism broke. Most importantly, the market had moved. Consumers didn't want another platform. They wanted apps, and apps were being built for iOS and Android.

Palm burned through cash trying to compete. In April 2010, HP acquired Palm for $1.2 billion, mostly for the webOS intellectual property. HP launched one webOS tablet, the TouchPad, then killed the entire division six weeks later. WebOS was sold to LG, where it powers smart TVs—a footnote.

Jeff Hawkins had been right in 1992. Computers would fit in pockets. Palm proved the market existed and showed how it should work. They created the category, defined its interface conventions, and demonstrated its commercial viability. Then they watched Microsoft, Apple, and Google harvest what they'd planted. The company that invented the smartphone died just as smartphones conquered the world.
