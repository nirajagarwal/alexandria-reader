# Gojek

***The app that turned Jakarta's motorcycle taxis into a $10 billion platform — and taught Southeast Asia how to build for chaos***

> **Years:** 2010–present

In 2010, Nadiem Makarim stood on a Jakarta street corner, frustrated. He needed to get across the city fast, but traffic was gridlocked. Around him, motorcycle taxis — ojeks — weaved through the congestion, their drivers calling out destinations. They'd been part of Jakarta's informal transit system for decades, unregulated and cash-only, clustering at street corners waiting for fares. Makarim, a Harvard Business School graduate who'd worked at McKinsey, saw infrastructure hidden in plain sight.

The insight wasn't just "Uber but for motorcycles." It was understanding that Indonesia's chaos was a feature, not a bug. Jakarta had 30 million people, crushing traffic, and an economy where 60% of workers were informal. The ojek drivers were entrepreneurs already — they just lacked coordination, pricing transparency, and access to the customers beyond their corner.

Makarim co-founded Gojek with Michaelangelo Moran, initially as a call center. Customers would phone in, and dispatchers would radio drivers. It was clunky, but it proved demand existed. In 2015, they launched the smartphone app. Timing mattered: Indonesia's mobile internet penetration was exploding, and Uber had validated the ride-hailing model globally, making it easier to explain.

But Gojek wasn't just copying Uber's playbook. Uber assumed infrastructure — credit cards, street addresses, smartphone literacy. Indonesia had none of that reliably. Most people used cash. Addresses were vague descriptions ("the blue house near the warung"). Drivers might not have data plans. Gojek built for these constraints. They partnered with drivers to provide phones and data. They made cash the default payment method. They added features like "pick me up from wherever I am right now" because street addresses were fiction.

The real genius was expansion beyond rides. By 2016, Gojek had added GoFood (food delivery), GoSend (package delivery), and GoPay (mobile wallet). The logic was elegant: they had drivers idle between rides, customers who needed more than transport, and a payments problem to solve. Each service reinforced the others. GoFood generated more trips. More trips meant more GoPay adoption. GoPay made transactions faster and created data.

The super-app model became Gojek's defining innovation. Where Western tech companies specialized, Gojek bundled. One app for transport, food, payments, massages, cleaning, groceries, logistics. It made sense in a market where smartphone storage was limited and consumers wanted simplicity. The app became infrastructure — the layer between urban Indonesians and everything they needed.

Growth was ferocious. By 2017, Gojek was in 50 Indonesian cities. Funding poured in — Google, Tencent, JD.com, Sequoia. The valuation hit $10 billion by 2019, making it Indonesia's first decacorn. Makarim became a national figure, the entrepreneur who'd legitimized the informal economy and given hundreds of thousands of drivers stable income and insurance.

But success attracted war. Grab, the Singapore-based super-app, was expanding aggressively. Both companies burned cash on driver incentives and customer discounts, competing for dominance across Southeast Asia. It was a subsidization race with no clear endgame. The fight got brutal in shared markets like Vietnam and Thailand. Unlike the U.S., where Uber and Lyft found an uneasy coexistence, Southeast Asia was winner-take-most. Network effects and consumer habit meant the leading app would dominate.

Gojek expanded regionally — Vietnam, Thailand, Singapore — but struggled outside Indonesia. Grab had a head start in those markets and deeper local knowledge. The international push drained resources while the core Indonesian market required constant defense. By 2020, the pandemic hit. Ride volume collapsed. Food delivery surged but at lower margins. The path to profitability, always distant, receded further.

In 2021, Makarim left to become Indonesia's Education Minister — a move that signaled both his stature and perhaps the company's maturation past the founder stage. That same year, Gojek merged with Tokopedia, Indonesia's leading e-commerce platform, forming GoTo Group. The deal was framed as creating a national champion, but it was also a recognition that standalone unit economics weren't working. Scale needed to be combined, losses consolidated, and the public market offering prepared.

GoTo went public in April 2022 with Indonesia's largest-ever IPO, raising $1.1 billion. The stock immediately fell. The market was souring on unprofitable tech companies. By late 2022, GoTo's value had dropped 75% from its peak. Layoffs followed. The super-app that had seemed unstoppable looked bloated and over-extended.

Yet Gojek's core achievement remains undeniable: it formalized the informal economy at scale. Hundreds of thousands of drivers gained steady work. Millions of small merchants accessed digital customers. GoPay became one of Indonesia's top digital wallets, pushing financial inclusion. The company proved that emerging markets didn't need to copy Silicon Valley's infrastructure-dependent models — they could build around the infrastructure they had.

The path from street corner frustration to national institution took a decade. The question now is whether the institution can become profitable, or whether building for chaos means永久 subsidizing it. Gojek didn't just ride Indonesia's growth — it reorganized how millions of people moved, ate, and transacted. Whether that reorganization generates sustainable returns or remains a venture-backed public service is the final chapter still being written.
