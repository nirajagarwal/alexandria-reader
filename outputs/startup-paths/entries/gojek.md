# Gojek

***The motorcycle taxi app that became Southeast Asia's everything company***

> **Years:** 2010-present

Nadiem Makarim grew up privileged in Jakarta, son of a cabinet minister, educated at Brown and Harvard Business School. He worked at McKinsey. He understood Indonesia's infrastructure problem not as an outsider diagnosing it, but as someone who lived it daily. Jakarta's traffic was legendary — a city of ten million where a five-mile trip could take two hours. The wealthy had drivers. Everyone else had ojek: motorcycle taxis that wove through gridlock, unregulated, cash-only, clustered at street corners waiting for fares.

In 2010, Makarim started Gojek as a call center. Customers phoned in, operators dispatched riders. Twenty drivers. Basic coordination. The insight wasn't technology — it was trust. Ojek drivers had terrible reputations. They overcharged tourists, drove recklessly, sometimes robbed passengers. Makarim recruited carefully, required helmets, set fixed prices. He was building quality control in a market that had none.

For four years, it grew slowly. A few hundred drivers. Modest revenue. Then in 2014, Makarim saw Uber and Grab gaining traction in Southeast Asia. He rebuilt Gojek as a smartphone app. Timing was perfect: smartphone adoption was exploding across Indonesia's emerging middle class. The app launched in January 2015.

Within months, Gojek had tens of thousands of drivers. The model worked because Indonesian labor was cheap and motorcycles were everywhere. A driver could earn more with Gojek than most formal sector jobs, and passengers paid less than traditional taxis. The unit economics were sustainable in ways they never quite worked in Western markets.

But Makarim saw something competitors missed. Uber was building a global product. Gojek could build an Indonesian one. Traffic in Jakarta wasn't just bad — it was existential. People needed everything delivered. Food spoiled waiting in traffic. Medicine arrived too late. Gojek had a fleet of motorcycles and an app. They could move anything.

In late 2015, Gojek launched GoFood for restaurant delivery, GoSend for packages, GoMart for convenience store items. Then GoMassage, GoCleaning, GoGlam for beauty services. The app became a platform. Each service used the same driver network, the same payment system, the same trust infrastructure Makarim had spent five years building.

The pivot to super-app happened fast. By 2016, Gojek wasn't a ride-hailing company that delivered food. It was a logistics platform that happened to move people. The frequency was extraordinary — users opened the app multiple times per day. The data was gold: Gojek knew where people lived, worked, ate, shopped. They knew traffic patterns better than the government.

Funding poured in. Sequoia and DST invested. Then KKR and Warburg Pincus. By 2017, Gojek had raised over a billion dollars. Valuation hit $3 billion. The competition was Grab, which had started in Malaysia and was better funded, more polished, expanding aggressively. The battle for Southeast Asia became a two-horse race.

Gojek's advantage was Indonesia. They understood local needs. They launched GoPoints, a loyalty program that worked in a market where most people didn't have credit cards. They integrated with local banks for digital payments. They hired drivers who spoke Javanese, Sundanese, dozens of local languages. Grab was regional. Gojek was Indonesian.

But being Indonesian was also limiting. Grab operated in eight countries. Gojek struggled to expand beyond Indonesia, entering Vietnam and Thailand but never gaining significant traction. The super-app model that worked in Jakarta didn't translate. In Singapore, people didn't need motorcycle deliveries. In Vietnam, local competitors were entrenched. Gojek's strength — deep local knowledge — was also its constraint.

By 2018, the burn rate was unsustainable. Both Gojek and Grab were losing hundreds of millions subsidizing rides and deliveries. Google, Tencent, and Temasek invested in Gojek. SoftBank funded Grab. The war escalated. Neither side could afford to lose Indonesia — it had more people than every other Southeast Asian market combined.

Makarim knew the endgame wasn't exit — it was dominance or death. Gojek launched GoPlay for streaming video, GoPay for digital payments, GoInvestasi for mutual funds. The super-app expanded until it was infrastructure. Four million drivers depended on it for income. Millions of small businesses relied on it for delivery. The Indonesian government started treating Gojek as critical national infrastructure.

In 2019, Makarim left to become Minister of Education. The founder departing for government was unusual but logical — Gojek had become too important to be just a company. Kevin Aluwi and Andre Soelistyo, co-founders who had been there since the call center days, took over as co-CEOs.

The pandemic should have killed them. Instead, it validated everything. With lockdowns, GoFood became essential. Gojek processed millions of deliveries daily. The driver fleet, which seemed like a liability when rides collapsed, was the asset that mattered. Revenue from food delivery and logistics exceeded rides for the first time.

In 2021, Gojek merged with Tokopedia, Indonesia's largest e-commerce platform, forming GoTo Group. The combined entity went public on the Indonesian stock exchange in 2022, the largest IPO in Indonesian history. Valuation at IPO: $27 billion.

But the public markets were brutal. The stock dropped 50% in the first year. Profitability remained elusive. The super-app model that created winner-take-all dominance in Indonesia produced impressive revenue but thin margins. Grab, its eternal rival, faced the same problem: immense scale, unclear path to profit.

Gojek today is infrastructure. It moves people, food, goods, and money across Indonesia. It employs millions indirectly. It changed how cities function. Whether it becomes sustainably profitable — whether the super-app model can generate returns that justify the capital invested — remains uncertain. But the path from twenty motorcycle drivers taking phone calls to a publicly traded company worth tens of billions, integral to the daily life of hundreds of millions of people, is already complete.
