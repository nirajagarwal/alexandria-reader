# Twitch

***The accidental empire built on watching strangers play video games***

> **Years:** 2011-present

In 2007, Justin Kan and Emmett Shear were running Justin.tv, a live streaming platform where anyone could broadcast anything. The site attracted a chaotic mix of content: people streaming their daily lives, pirated sports, random webcam feeds, and increasingly, gamers streaming themselves playing video games. The business wasn't working. Advertisers didn't want to be associated with the unpredictable content. Revenue was weak. The company was burning through capital with no clear path forward.

But one category kept growing. The gaming section of Justin.tv had become unusually sticky. Viewers would watch for hours. They'd come back daily. They'd participate in chat, forming communities around individual streamers. In 2011, Shear and his team did something that seemed insane to outside observers: they spun off the gaming content into a separate site called Twitch and bet the company's future on people wanting to watch other people play video games.

The traditional media world dismissed it. Why would anyone watch someone else play a game instead of playing themselves? The question missed what was actually happening. Twitch wasn't competing with playing games. It was competing with television, with YouTube, with hanging out. Watching a skilled player navigate a difficult game was entertaining. Watching a charismatic personality react to horror games was hilarious. Watching professional esports competitions was thrilling. And the live chat created a sense of presence, of being part of something happening right now, that pre-recorded content couldn't match.

The timing was perfect in ways the founders couldn't have fully planned. Gaming was exploding as a mainstream entertainment category. YouTube had trained a generation to watch gaming content but struggled with live streaming. Broadband speeds had finally reached the point where high-quality live video was viable for both streamers and viewers. Console makers were building streaming capability directly into PlayStation and Xbox. And a new generation of gamers had grown up watching Let's Play videos and streams as naturally as previous generations watched MTV.

Twitch grew explosively. By 2013, it was the fourth-largest source of internet traffic in the United States during peak hours, behind only Netflix, Google, and Apple. The business model emerged organically. Viewers could subscribe to their favorite streamers for $5 a month, with Twitch taking a cut. Viewers could buy virtual currency to "tip" streamers during broadcasts. And advertisers, seeing the engaged young male demographic that was increasingly unreachable through traditional TV, began to spend.

The company raised $35 million in 2013. By mid-2014, Twitch had 55 million unique visitors per month watching 15 billion minutes of content. Google came calling, reportedly offering over $1 billion. But Amazon swooped in with a $970 million all-cash offer that closed in August 2014. For a company that had spun out of a failing startup just three years earlier, it was a staggering outcome.

Under Amazon, Twitch continued to grow but also began to face the challenges of success. YouTube launched YouTube Gaming as a direct competitor. Facebook and Microsoft entered the live streaming space. Content moderation became increasingly complex as the platform grew — dealing with harassment in chat, copyright issues with game music, hate speech, and the behavior of top streamers who could say or do things that damaged the platform's reputation.

The economics of streaming created new problems. Top streamers became celebrities with enormous leverage. When Ninja, Twitch's biggest star with millions of followers, signed an exclusive deal with Microsoft's Mixer in 2019, it shocked the industry. Microsoft paid tens of millions for the exclusive. When Mixer shut down a year later, these streamers found themselves in limbo, demonstrating the volatility of the creator economy Twitch had pioneered.

The platform expanded beyond gaming. The "Just Chatting" category became one of the most-watched, with streamers simply talking to their audience. Music, art, cooking, and even politics found audiences. This created an identity crisis. Was Twitch a gaming platform or a general live streaming platform? The company's policies and culture were built around gaming, but its growth increasingly came from elsewhere.

The pandemic turbocharged Twitch's growth. With people locked at home and traditional entertainment shut down, live streaming exploded. Hours watched increased by 99% in 2020. But the growth also exposed infrastructure weaknesses and content moderation failures. "Hate raids" where coordinated groups would flood Black and LGBTQ streamers with abuse became a crisis. Twitch's response was slow and inadequate, leading to a day-long boycott by streamers.

By 2024, Twitch remained dominant but no longer invincible. TikTok had trained a generation on short-form video. YouTube's streaming service had steadily gained ground. Discord and other platforms offered streamers ways to build direct relationships with fans without platform intermediaries. The cost of running the service — the bandwidth alone was staggering — made profitability elusive despite the massive audience.

What Twitch proved was that the line between entertainment and participation had dissolved. Viewers weren't passive consumers. They were participants in a live event, members of a community, supporters of creators they felt they knew personally. The parasocial relationships between streamers and viewers created an intensity of engagement that traditional media couldn't match. Whether that model could survive the evolution toward AI-generated content, virtual streamers, and fragmenting attention remained an open question.

Twitch had succeeded by seeing what others missed: that the future of entertainment wasn't about better content, but about better connection. The company that almost died as Justin.tv became a cultural force by betting everything on the simple idea that sometimes, watching is more fun than playing.
