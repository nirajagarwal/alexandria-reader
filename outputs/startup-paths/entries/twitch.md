# Twitch

***The accidental platform that turned streaming into spectacle***

> **Years:** 2011-present

In 2007, Justin Kan and Emmett Shear launched Justin.tv, a site where anyone could broadcast live video. Kan wore a camera on his head 24/7, streaming his life in San Francisco. The stunt worked as marketing — people watched, then started broadcasting themselves. Within a year, thousands of channels existed. Most were mundane: someone's webcam pointed at a room, a college student doing homework, amateur DJs in bedrooms.

But one category exploded: gaming. People broadcasting themselves playing video games. Not highlights, not edited montages — raw gameplay with live commentary. By 2010, gaming was the largest category on Justin.tv by an order of magnitude. Shear and his team watched the traffic graphs. The pattern was undeniable. They had built a general live video platform. The market wanted something else.

In June 2011, they spun out the gaming section as a separate site: Twitch. The name came from twitch gameplay — the split-second reflexes required in first-person shooters. The rebrand was clinical: different site, different brand, same technology. They kept Justin.tv running alongside it. The bet was that focus would accelerate what was already working.

It did. Gaming was entering a cultural shift. E-sports tournaments were drawing arena crowds. Games like League of Legends and StarCraft II had competitive scenes with professional players. YouTube had gaming content, but it was edited, packaged, delayed. Twitch was live. Viewers could watch skilled players in real-time, ask questions in chat, feel present at the performance. The chat became part of the experience — thousands of viewers reacting simultaneously, creating an impromptu community.

Early growth came from e-sports. Tournament organizers needed a way to broadcast competitions online. Twitch provided the infrastructure for free, took a cut of ad revenue, and let organizers keep subscription fees. Major tournaments like The International — Dota 2's championship with million-dollar prize pools — streamed on Twitch. Hundreds of thousands watched. The platform became the default venue for competitive gaming.

But the bigger insight was that people would watch others play games even when nothing competitive was happening. Streamers built audiences not through skill but through personality. They were entertainers. Some were funny, some were skilled, some created narrative drama. The most successful were all three. Viewers came for gameplay, stayed for parasocial relationships. Streamers who went live daily, acknowledged regulars in chat, and made viewers feel like insiders built loyal followings.

The business model emerged organically. Viewers could subscribe to individual channels for $4.99/month, gaining perks like custom emotes and ad-free viewing. Twitch split the revenue with streamers. Top streamers made six figures. The platform added Bits — a virtual currency viewers could buy and spend in chat to show support. Streamers received a penny per Bit. Donations flowed directly through third-party services. The top 1% of streamers earned more than traditional media personalities.

Amazon noticed. In August 2014, they acquired Twitch for $970 million. Commentators questioned the price. YouTube existed. Facebook had live video. What justified near-billion-dollar valuation for a site where people watched other people play video games?

What they missed: Twitch wasn't competing with YouTube. It was a different medium. YouTube was edited content, algorithmically surfaced, consumed passively. Twitch was live performance, community-driven, participatory. Viewers spent hours in a stream, not minutes. They returned daily. Amazon saw engagement metrics that rivaled traditional television among young demographics. They saw a platform positioned at the intersection of gaming, entertainment, and community — three markets each worth tens of billions.

Post-acquisition, Twitch expanded beyond gaming. Creative streams — artists drawing, musicians performing, people coding — emerged. IRL streams — broadcasters with mobile rigs walking through cities, attending events, living life — became a category. Twitch Plays Pokémon — a 2014 experiment where chat commands controlled a shared game — drew 1.16 million viewers. The platform became infrastructure for any live experience that benefited from real-time audience interaction.

But the core remained gaming. By 2020, Twitch streamed 10 billion hours of content annually. The top streamers — Ninja, Pokimane, xQc — were household names among Gen Z. Deals worth millions lured talent between platforms as YouTube Gaming and Facebook Gaming competed. Ninja's move to Microsoft's Mixer in 2019 reportedly cost $30 million. When Mixer shut down a year later, streamers returned to Twitch. The network effects — audiences, community, discoverability — were too strong.

The model created complications. Streamers performed for hours daily, maintaining constant engagement, managing chat, walking the line between entertaining and controversial. Burnout was common. Drama was frequent. The platform's moderation struggled to keep pace with millions of hours of live content. Hate raids — coordinated attacks where bots flooded streams with slurs — became a crisis in 2021. Twitch's response was slow. Streamers staged a day-long boycott.

Revenue remained challenging. Subscription splits favored Twitch 50-50, later 70-30 for top partners. But most streamers struggled to monetize. The top 1% captured most revenue. The platform subsidized small streamers' bandwidth costs without return. Ad loads increased, frustrating viewers. Amazon pushed integration with Prime — subscribers got one free channel subscription monthly — but conversion remained a puzzle.

Yet the fundamental product held. Twitch became synonymous with live streaming the way Google became synonymous with search. When COVID-19 lockdowns began in 2020, viewership surged. Twitch hours watched increased 67% year-over-year. Musicians, DJs, fitness instructors, and chefs went live. The accidental platform built for people broadcasting Halo matches had become infrastructure for live human performance.

Justin.tv shut down in 2014, its technology and team fully absorbed into Twitch. The general video platform couldn't compete with YouTube. The focused gaming platform became a category of one.
