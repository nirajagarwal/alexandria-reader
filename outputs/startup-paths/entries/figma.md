# Figma

***The design tool that won by building for multiplayer***

> **Years:** 2012–2024 (acquired by Adobe)

In 2012, Dylan Field dropped out of Brown University with an idea that sounded absurd: build professional design software that runs entirely in a web browser. He was twenty years old. His co-founder, Evan Wallace, had been working on graphics rendering technology. Together they believed the future of creative tools was collaborative and cloud-native, even though every serious designer still relied on desktop applications that treated files like physical objects.

The timing seemed terrible. Adobe dominated design software. Sketch had just launched and was winning over interface designers frustrated with Photoshop's bloat. The browser itself was considered too slow, too limited for real creative work. WebGL, the technology they'd need for hardware-accelerated graphics, was barely supported. Professional designers worked alone, handing off static files. Collaboration meant emailing PSDs back and forth or gathering around a single screen.

Field and Wallace spent the first four years building infrastructure. Not features, not marketing — just the rendering engine and operational transform algorithms that would make real-time multiplayer editing possible. They raised a seed round from Greylock, but progress was invisible to outsiders. Other startups launched and gained users. Figma remained in private beta, refining the engine. Wallace rewrote Chrome's rendering pipeline to handle vector graphics efficiently. They built their own font rendering system. The technical work was extraordinary. The product barely existed.

When Figma finally opened to the public in 2016, it looked like just another vector design tool. But two things were different. First, it was fast in the browser — genuinely fast, not "fast for a web app." Second, it was multiplayer by default. Multiple people could edit the same file simultaneously, with cursors and selections visible in real time. You could watch someone design. You could jump in and help. Design became less like writing a novel and more like playing music together.

The multiplayer feature seemed like a nice-to-have. It turned out to be structural. Design had always been collaborative in theory but isolated in practice. Designers worked alone, then presented to stakeholders who suggested changes without understanding constraints. Developers received static mockups and translated them incorrectly. Product managers requested tweaks without seeing the broader system. Figma collapsed these handoffs. The designer, the PM, the engineer, and the executive could all be in the same file, seeing changes as they happened, leaving comments attached to specific elements. The collaboration wasn't just faster — it was a different kind of work.

Adoption started with small teams. A few designers at a startup would try it. The free tier was generous. They'd invite their PM. The PM would invite engineering. Soon the whole product team was in Figma. The browser meant no installation barriers, no license seats gathering dust. A contractor could jump in. A stakeholder could preview without needing an account. The friction of using Figma was lower than the friction of explaining why you weren't using it.

Sketch, the incumbent everyone expected Figma to battle, had chosen the opposite architecture. Native Mac app, excellent performance, robust plugin ecosystem. But single-player. Sketch added cloud syncing and commenting later, but bolted onto a single-user foundation. Figma's multiplayer was fundamental. Every feature was built assuming multiple people might be present. The version history, the component system, the auto-layout — all designed for teams, not individuals who occasionally collaborated.

Adobe noticed too late. By 2020, Figma had become the default tool for interface design at major tech companies. Design systems, the practice of building reusable component libraries, worked naturally in Figma. Entire organizations could maintain a single source of truth for their visual language. Adobe launched XD, their cloud-based competitor, but it felt like catch-up. The incumbency advantage had flipped. Teams already in Figma had no reason to leave. Teams still on Sketch or Photoshop had reasons to switch.

The business model was elegant. Free for individuals and small teams. Paid for organizations that needed advanced features and admin controls. The free tier wasn't a loss leader — it was how Figma spread. Students learned on it. Side projects started with it. When those people joined companies, they brought Figma with them. The bottoms-up adoption was unstoppable because the product was sticky not through lock-in but through habit and network effects. Your team was in Figma. Your components were in Figma. The cost of leaving was cultural, not financial.

By 2022, Figma was valued at $20 billion and controlled design at most technology companies. Adobe, facing an existential threat to its creative software franchise, announced it would acquire Figma for $20 billion — one of the largest software acquisitions in history. The deal faced regulatory scrutiny. Adobe's dominance in creative software plus Figma's dominance in interface design meant fewer alternatives for designers. The merger would consolidate the market.

The acquisition cleared in 2024 after divestitures and commitments. Adobe got Figma. Designers worried about what would happen to the product's momentum under a company known for slow-moving enterprise software. Field and Wallace had built something rare: a tool that changed how creative work happens, not by adding features to an existing category but by reimagining the fundamental architecture. They made design multiplayer when everyone thought creative work was inherently solitary. They proved the browser could handle professional workloads when native apps seemed inevitable.

The path was technical depth first, product second. Four years of invisible work on infrastructure before features designers would notice. A bet that collaboration was more valuable than any single feature. A business model that treated free users as the growth engine rather than a cost center. And timing that looked wrong — too early for browser performance, too late to compete with Sketch — but turned out to be exactly right.
