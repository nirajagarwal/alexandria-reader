# Figma

***The collaborative design tool that beat Adobe at its own game***

> **Years:** 2012-present (acquired 2022, deal later abandoned)

Dylan Field dropped out of Brown University in 2012 with an idea that sounded almost naively ambitious: build Photoshop in a web browser. Not a simplified version. Not a toy. The real thing. Professional-grade design software that ran entirely online, where multiple people could work simultaneously on the same file.

He wasn't a designer. His co-founder Evan Wallace wasn't either. They were engineers who'd met at Brown, both frustrated by how designers worked — emailing files back and forth, maintaining version control manually, jumping between applications to turn static mockups into working prototypes. The entire workflow seemed stuck in the desktop era while the rest of software development had moved to cloud-based collaboration.

The technical challenge was immense. Professional design tools demanded performance that web browsers weren't built to deliver. Vector graphics rendering, real-time collaboration, file handling for complex documents — all of it needed to work smoothly in Chrome. Most experienced engineers would have called it impossible. Field and Wallace spent their first two years just building the rendering engine.

They launched in 2015 with something that barely worked. Early Figma was slow, crashed frequently, and lacked basic features that Sketch — then the ascendant design tool — had perfected. The design community was skeptical. Adobe owned the professional market. Sketch owned the insurgent startup market. Who needed another design tool, especially one hampered by running in a browser?

But Field had made one crucial architectural decision: multiplayer from the ground up. Other tools added collaboration as a feature. Figma built everything around the assumption that design was collaborative. When you opened a Figma file, you saw everyone else's cursor in real time. No "check out" and "check in." No merge conflicts. Just instant, simultaneous work.

The first believers were design teams at startups. Not the designers themselves initially — they had muscle memory in other tools. But product managers and engineers who suddenly could jump into design files, leave comments, even make simple edits themselves. Figma turned design from a handoff into a conversation. The whole product development cycle compressed.

The adoption pattern was distinctive. Individual designers might resist, but once one team at a company adopted Figma, it spread. Because collaboration was the product, every new user made Figma more valuable. The network effects weren't about connecting with strangers like social media. They were about working together across functional boundaries that had previously required translation layers.

Field kept the product free for individual users and small teams. Revenue came from larger organizations. This "freemium" approach let Figma spread organically through companies from the bottom up, bypassing procurement processes that favored established vendors. By the time executives noticed their teams had standardized on Figma, the decision was already made.

Performance improved steadily. The rendering engine Wallace had built kept getting faster. Features that seemed impossible in a browser — advanced vector editing, sophisticated typography, complex effects — arrived one by one. Designers who'd dismissed Figma as a lightweight tool found themselves migrating anyway. The technical constraints that once limited it became invisible.

The pandemic accelerated everything. When design teams went remote in 2020, tools built for co-located work collapsed. Figma's core value proposition — seamless remote collaboration — became existential. Companies that had been "Sketch shops" converted almost overnight. Design education shifted online, and students learned Figma by default because it worked the same whether you were in a classroom or your bedroom.

By 2022, Figma had become infrastructure. The company claimed three million users. It dominated product design at tech companies. Adobe's Creative Cloud suite still owned print and marketing, but in digital product design — the growth category, where software was eating the world — Figma had won.

In September 2022, Adobe announced it would acquire Figma for $20 billion. Twenty billion for a ten-year-old company that had never been profitable. The price shocked observers, but Adobe saw clearly: Figma was eating its future. If web design replaced desktop design, and collaborative tools replaced individual tools, Figma owned the new paradigm.

But the deal faced immediate regulatory scrutiny. The U.K.'s Competition and Markets Authority and the European Commission both investigated, concerned that Adobe acquiring its most significant competitor would entrench monopoly power. In December 2023, Adobe and Figma mutually abandoned the acquisition. Adobe paid Figma a $1 billion termination fee.

Figma remains independent. The company Field started by rejecting desktop software architecture had forced Adobe — the company that defined desktop creative software — to acknowledge it was competing on Figma's terms. Whether Figma eventually goes public, gets acquired by someone else, or remains private, it has already reshaped how digital products are designed.

The path wasn't about better features. It was about seeing that the real problem wasn't the tools designers used, but the gaps between how designers, developers, and product managers worked. Field built a product where collaboration was the foundation, then waited for the world to realize that's what it needed.
