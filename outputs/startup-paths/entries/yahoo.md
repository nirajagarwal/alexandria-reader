# Yahoo

***The search engine that won the early web, then lost its way***

> **Years:** 1994-2017

In early 1994, two Stanford electrical engineering graduate students named Jerry Yang and David Filo were doing what graduate students do: procrastinating on their dissertations. They began compiling lists of interesting websites, organizing them into categories on a page they called "Jerry and David's Guide to the World Wide Web." The web was still so small you could theoretically catalog it by hand. Their guide became addictive to early web users desperate for any form of navigation in the chaos of cyberspace.

By fall, their hobby had consumed their workstations, their trailer, and any pretense of academic work. They renamed it Yahoo — an acronym for "Yet Another Hierarchically Organized Oracle," though the cowboy exclamation was the real appeal. Traffic exploded. By early 1995, Yahoo was serving a million page views per day on Stanford's network. The university politely suggested they take it elsewhere.

They incorporated in March 1995. Sequoia Capital, which had passed on many internet ideas, saw something different here: not technology risk but pure distribution play. Mike Moritz led a $2 million round. The bet was that organizing the web mattered more than searching it. Yahoo's human-curated directory felt trustworthy in ways algorithmic search didn't. You browsed Yahoo the way you browsed a library.

Yahoo went public in April 1996 at $13 per share. On day one it closed at $43. The company was barely two years old. Revenue was minimal. No one cared. Yahoo was the front door to the internet, and everyone wanted in.

What Yahoo understood before others was that being a portal mattered more than being a search engine. In 1997 and 1998, they bought everything: email, calendaring, shopping, news, sports, entertainment. If people did it online, Yahoo wanted to be where they did it. The strategy worked brilliantly. By 1999, Yahoo was the most visited website on earth, worth $120 billion at the peak. The directory that two students built to avoid dissertation work had become one of the most valuable companies in the world.

But Yahoo had a problem it couldn't see. Google launched in 1998 with a genuinely better search algorithm. Yahoo dismissed it as technology for technology's sake. Why would users want algorithmic search when human curation worked fine? In 2000, Yahoo even licensed Google's search technology to power its own results, not seeing the existential threat.

The dotcom crash hammered Yahoo like everyone else. The stock fell from $118 to $8. But Yahoo had real revenue from advertising, real traffic, real business. They survived when hundreds of others vaporized. By 2002, Yang and Filo stepped back from operations, bringing in Hollywood executive Terry Semel as CEO. The company needed adult supervision to become a real media business.

Semel pursued media deals aggressively. Yahoo bought Inktomi and Overture, getting its own search technology. But the company's focus remained scattered across dozens of properties. Google, meanwhile, did one thing superbly: search that worked better every day, monetized through advertising that was both more targeted and less intrusive than Yahoo's banner approach.

In 2006, Yahoo had a chance to change everything. Facebook, barely two years old, was available for $1 billion. Yahoo agreed, then lowered the offer to $850 million as its own stock price fell. Mark Zuckerberg walked. Yahoo also rejected a chance to buy Google in 2002 for $5 billion. The company kept choosing to build rather than buy the future.

By 2007, Yahoo's position was deteriorating. Google dominated search. Facebook was swallowing social. Yang returned as CEO but seemed paralyzed by sentimentality for what Yahoo had been. Microsoft offered $44.6 billion in 2008 — roughly $33 per share for a company trading at $19. The board rejected it. Yahoo's stock would never see that price again.

A parade of CEOs followed: Carol Bartz, fired over the phone by the board; Scott Thompson, gone after four months over a resume scandal; Ross Levinsohn, interim; Marissa Mayer, hired away from Google with great fanfare in 2012. Mayer tried everything: mobile focus, content acquisitions, the $1.1 billion purchase of Tumblr. Nothing arrested the decline. Yahoo had become a portfolio of assets slowly losing relevance, held together by momentum and a stake in Alibaba worth more than Yahoo itself.

The final chapter was fragmentation. In 2017, Verizon bought Yahoo's core business for $4.5 billion — less than a tenth of the Microsoft offer and a fraction of what Yahoo had once been worth. The Alibaba stake and Yahoo Japan were spun off separately. The company that had defined the early internet ended as spare parts.

Yahoo's failure wasn't singular or dramatic. It was death by a thousand missed turns. They had capital, talent, traffic, and time. What they lacked was focus and the ability to see that being everything to everyone on the internet was a strategy for the web's first decade, not its second. They won the portal era so decisively they couldn't imagine it would end.
