# Standard Oil

***The monopoly that rewrote the rules of capitalism***

> **Years:** 1870-1911

In 1870, John D. Rockefeller founded Standard Oil in Cleveland with a simple insight: the money wasn't in finding oil, it was in refining it. The Pennsylvania oil fields were chaos — wildcatters drilling everywhere, boom-and-bust cycles, refineries popping up and going bankrupt. Rockefeller saw waste. He saw opportunity in consolidation.

He was twenty-eight years old, a bookkeeper by training, methodical to the point of obsession. He'd made money in commodities during the Civil War and moved into oil refining in 1863. By 1870, his refinery was already the largest in Cleveland. But that wasn't enough. The industry was fragmented, inefficient, prone to price wars that benefited no one. Rockefeller wanted order.

His weapon was the railroad rebate. He approached the major railroads and made them an offer: guarantee them large, steady shipments of oil, and in return, get lower rates than competitors. The railroads agreed. Suddenly, Standard Oil could undercut everyone. When competitors couldn't compete on price, Rockefeller bought them. Sometimes he offered fair terms. Sometimes he didn't need to. By 1872, Standard controlled about 25% of American refining capacity. By 1880, it was 90%.

The method was ruthless in its efficiency. Standard would enter a market, drop prices below cost until local refiners went bankrupt, then buy their facilities for pennies. It would pressure railroads to deny shipments to competitors. It would cut off suppliers who did business with rivals. The company didn't just win — it eliminated the possibility of competition.

But there was another side. Rockefeller wasn't just a predator; he was a builder of systems. He integrated vertically, owning pipelines, tank cars, storage facilities, distribution networks. He invested in research, finding uses for petroleum byproducts that other refiners threw away. He standardized processes across refineries, reducing costs through operational excellence. Under Standard Oil, kerosene prices fell from 26 cents per gallon in 1870 to 8 cents by 1880. The company made oil affordable to ordinary Americans.

In 1882, Rockefeller faced a legal problem. Standard Oil had grown across state lines, but no corporate structure existed for a multi-state company. His lawyers invented one: the trust. Standard Oil of Ohio became the core, with stockholders transferring their shares to a board of trustees who controlled the entire network. It was an innovation in corporate structure, the prototype for the modern corporation. It was also a way to obscure ownership and evade state regulations.

By the 1890s, Standard Oil was the largest, most powerful company in America. It controlled not just refining but the entire supply chain from oil field to consumer. It had amassed a fortune that made Rockefeller the richest man in the world. But the very size that made it dominant made it a target.

Journalists began investigating. Ida Tarbell, whose father had been ruined by Standard's practices, published a devastating series in McClure's Magazine starting in 1902. She documented the railroad rebates, the predatory pricing, the secret deals. Public opinion turned. Standard Oil became the symbol of everything wrong with industrial capitalism — the octopus strangling competition, the monopoly that answered to no one.

State governments tried to break it up. In 1892, the Ohio Supreme Court dissolved the trust structure. Standard reorganized as a holding company in New Jersey, which had more permissive laws. But the pressure kept building. In 1906, the federal government filed suit under the Sherman Antitrust Act. The case took five years. In 1911, the Supreme Court ruled unanimously that Standard Oil must be dissolved.

The breakup created thirty-four separate companies. Standard Oil of New Jersey became Exxon. Standard Oil of New York became Mobil. Standard Oil of California became Chevron. Standard Oil of Indiana became Amoco. Standard Oil of Ohio became Sohio, later absorbed by BP. The pieces of the empire became empires themselves.

Rockefeller, by then retired and focused on philanthropy, watched from a distance. The breakup made him even richer — the combined market value of the separated companies exceeded what the unified Standard had been worth. The monopoly was gone, but the infrastructure, the expertise, the accumulated capital remained.

The story of Standard Oil is the story of a man who saw inefficiency and destroyed it, who built systems that transformed an industry and made a product affordable, who accumulated power so completely that democracy had to intervene. It set the template for antitrust law, for corporate regulation, for the uneasy relationship between capitalism and the state. The companies that emerged from its breakup dominated global energy for the next century.

Rockefeller had wanted order. He got it, built it, and held it for forty years. Then the order he created became intolerable to everyone else, and they tore it apart. The pieces still rule the world.
