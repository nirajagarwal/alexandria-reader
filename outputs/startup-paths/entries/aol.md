# AOL

***The dial-up giant that conquered the internet, then lost it***

> **Years:** 1985-present

Steve Case wasn't supposed to build an internet empire. In 1983, he was a marketing executive at Pizza Hut, testing whether people would order pizza through their television sets. The experiment failed, but Case saw something: people wanted to connect through screens. When he joined a struggling online service called Quantum Computer Services in 1985, most Americans had never heard of email. By 2000, his company — renamed America Online — would be worth more than Time Warner, Disney, and News Corp combined.

Quantum started as a service for Commodore 64 owners. The idea was simple: dial into a computer network and access news, weather, games. But the execution was clunky, and the market was tiny. Case became CEO in 1991 and made a bet that would define the company: AOL wouldn't be for computer enthusiasts. It would be for everyone.

This was radical. In the early 1990s, getting online meant knowing Unix commands and navigating text-based interfaces. CompuServe and Prodigy served educated professionals. Case wanted to reach middle America — people who had never touched a computer. AOL's interface used buttons, icons, and the friendly greeting "You've Got Mail." It was the Fisher-Price version of the internet, and critics mocked it. Tech-savvy users called it "America On Hold" and dismissed its chat rooms as vapid. Case didn't care. He wasn't building for them.

The growth strategy was as aggressive as the product was simple. AOL carpet-bombed America with free trial CDs. They came in cereal boxes, magazine inserts, shrink-wrapped with electronics. AOL sent out 250 million CDs in one year alone. The marginal cost was nothing; the customer lifetime value was enormous. People tried it, got hooked on email and chat rooms, and stayed. By 1996, AOL had 5 million subscribers. By 1998, 15 million. By 2000, 25 million — more than half of all Americans with internet access.

The business model was beautiful. Subscribers paid $19.95 a month for unlimited access. AOL didn't just provide the connection — it *was* the internet for most users. People went online by opening AOL. They read news through AOL. They shopped through AOL's partnerships with Amazon and Barnes & Noble. AOL collected a cut of every transaction. The company became a gateway, a platform, and a tollbooth all at once.

But the unlimited access model nearly destroyed them. In 1996, AOL switched from hourly billing to unlimited monthly plans. Usage exploded. The network couldn't handle it. Subscribers got busy signals for hours. Lawsuits piled up. State attorneys general investigated. Case's bet almost killed the company, but AOL upgraded its infrastructure frantically, and the crisis passed. By 1997, they were back on track — and heading toward the deal that would define them.

The Time Warner merger, announced in January 2000, was the largest in history. AOL, a 15-year-old company, would acquire Time Warner, a media conglomerate with HBO, CNN, Warner Bros, and Time Magazine. The price: $165 billion. Steve Case sold it as the future: AOL's internet platform combined with Time Warner's content empire. "We're in the business of building brands," Case said. Wall Street believed him. Time Warner shareholders approved.

It was a disaster from the start. The cultures clashed immediately. AOL's executives were brash dot-com cowboys who wore khakis and thought in quarters. Time Warner's were old-media executives who wore suits and thought in decades. AOL's ad revenue — built on banner ads during the bubble — collapsed after 2000. The promised synergies never materialized. Time Warner executives discovered that AOL had been inflating revenue through questionable ad deals. By 2002, the company posted a $99 billion loss — the largest in corporate history.

The internet itself moved past AOL. Broadband replaced dial-up, and broadband users didn't need AOL's walled garden. Google became the gateway to the web. Facebook became the place for connection. AOL's subscriber base peaked in 2002 and declined every quarter after. The company that had defined the internet for 20 million households became obsolete within five years.

Time Warner spun off AOL in 2009, valuing it at $3.4 billion — 98% less than the merger price. AOL stumbled through various reinventions: a content farm strategy, an advertising technology play, acquisitions of TechCrunch and The Huffington Post. None of it worked. Verizon bought AOL in 2015 for $4.4 billion, mostly for its ad tech. In 2021, Verizon sold it to Apollo Global Management for $5 billion. Today AOL exists as a portfolio of declining media properties and legacy dial-up subscribers — yes, hundreds of thousands still pay for AOL dial-up.

Steve Case's original insight was correct: the internet would be for everyone, and the company that made it easy would win. AOL won that battle completely. But winning the battle for dial-up meant nothing when the war moved to broadband, search, and social. The company that brought America online became the symbol of what the internet left behind.
