# ServiceNow

***The enterprise software giant that emerged from one founder's rage against terrible IT tools***

> **Years:** 2004-present

Fred Luddy had spent two decades building enterprise software when he founded ServiceNow in 2004. He'd worked at various software companies, seen countless IT departments struggle with clunky, expensive systems, and grown increasingly frustrated with how bad the tools were. At Peregrine Systems, he'd helped build asset management software. At Remedy, he'd seen help desk systems that required armies of consultants just to configure. The enterprise software industry had become a racket — vendors sold complex products that took years to implement, required constant customization, and locked customers into expensive maintenance contracts.

Luddy wanted to build something different: help desk software that ran entirely in a web browser, required no installation, and could be configured without an army of consultants. The model was simple — monthly subscriptions, automatic updates, and clean design. He called it Glidesoft initially, later renaming it ServiceNow.

The timing was terrible. In 2004, the dotcom crash was still fresh. Enterprise buyers were skeptical of hosted software. The dominant model was still on-premise installations. Salesforce had pioneered cloud CRM but remained controversial — many IT departments considered cloud applications insecure and unserious. The idea of running critical IT infrastructure through a web browser seemed reckless.

Luddy funded the first two years himself, working from a small office in San Diego. He built the initial product alone, writing code and talking to potential customers. The pitch was straightforward: your IT help desk doesn't need to be a nightmare of servers and consultants. It can be a web application that just works. Early customers were small IT departments willing to take a chance on something unproven because their existing tools were so painful.

The product solved a real problem. IT help desks at most companies ran on systems built in the 1990s — Remedy, Peregrine, HP Service Center. These required dedicated servers, complex installations, and specialized administrators. Making simple changes meant hiring consultants. The total cost of ownership was astronomical. ServiceNow offered the same functionality through a browser, with updates that happened automatically and configuration anyone could learn. The value proposition was obvious to anyone who'd suffered through traditional enterprise IT tools.

But selling to enterprise IT departments in 2006 meant overcoming deep skepticism. IT leaders had been burned by software vendors repeatedly. They'd invested millions in systems that under-delivered. They'd been through painful implementations. They didn't trust cloud applications with their critical workflows. ServiceNow's sales cycle was long and grinding.

The company raised $5 million in 2006 from JMI Equity. The funding allowed Luddy to hire a sales team and build out the product. By 2007, ServiceNow had gained traction among mid-sized companies. The recession of 2008-2009 could have killed them, but instead it helped. Companies desperate to cut IT costs looked at ServiceNow's subscription model and saw an escape from expensive maintenance contracts and consultant fees. The value proposition became impossible to ignore.

In 2009, ServiceNow raised $41.5 million in a Series C led by Greylock Partners. The company brought in Frank Slootman as CEO in 2011. Slootman had led Data Domain to a $2.4 billion acquisition by EMC and had a reputation for aggressive growth. Luddy, who preferred building product to running a large organization, stepped back to focus on technology as chief product officer.

Under Slootman, ServiceNow accelerated. The company had initially focused just on IT help desk software — tracking tickets, managing incidents, handling service requests. But Luddy had built the platform to be flexible. ServiceNow expanded into IT operations management, asset management, configuration management. The same platform that tracked help desk tickets could manage any workflow. That insight transformed the business.

ServiceNow went public in 2012 at $18 per share, raising $210 million. The market valued it at $2.4 billion — remarkable for a company with $150 million in revenue. Wall Street was betting on the platform play. If ServiceNow could become the system of record for IT operations, it could expand into other enterprise workflows.

That's exactly what happened. ServiceNow moved into HR service delivery, facilities management, security operations, customer service management. Every large organization had workflows that needed automation, tracking, and management. ServiceNow's platform could handle all of them. The value to customers was consolidation — instead of buying point solutions from dozens of vendors, they could build workflows on a single platform.

The growth was relentless. Revenue grew from $267 million in 2012 to over $7 billion by 2023. The market cap exceeded $140 billion. ServiceNow became one of the most valuable enterprise software companies in the world, competing with Oracle and SAP but with a modern cloud architecture they couldn't match.

The success came from Luddy's original insight: enterprise software didn't have to be painful. It could be elegant, cloud-native, and user-friendly. But it also came from recognizing that IT service management was just the entry point. The real opportunity was becoming the platform for all enterprise workflows. ServiceNow didn't just replace Remedy. It replaced dozens of enterprise applications by providing a flexible platform for any process that needed automation.

By 2024, ServiceNow served over 85% of the Fortune 500. The company Luddy started alone in San Diego had become enterprise infrastructure — the system that large organizations used to run themselves. The rage against terrible IT tools had produced one of the defining enterprise platforms of the cloud era.
