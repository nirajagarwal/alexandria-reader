# Anduril

***Defense technology built by Silicon Valley outsiders who refused to stay out***

> **Years:** 2017-present

In the summer of 2017, Palmer Luckey was out. Facebook had paid $2 billion for his virtual reality company Oculus three years earlier, making him a legend at twenty-one. Then came the controversy — political donations, alleged support for inflammatory causes, internal tensions at Facebook. By March 2017, he was gone, officially to "pursue other interests." He was twenty-four years old with money, notoriety, and no clear next act.

What he did next surprised almost everyone: he started a defense company.

The founding team was deliberate. Luckey recruited Trae Stephens, a Palantir veteran who had spent years watching Silicon Valley's relationship with the Pentagon grow increasingly strained. Matt Grimm came from Palantir too, bringing deep experience in systems integration. They added Joseph Chen, a former Palantir engineer, and Brian Schimpf, an Oculus colleague. This was not a team of defense industry veterans trying to modernize. This was Silicon Valley talent deliberately crossing a line most of their peers refused to approach.

The thesis was straightforward: the U.S. military was losing its technological edge. China was investing heavily in AI, autonomous systems, and advanced sensors. American defense contractors were building weapons systems designed in the 1980s, iterating slowly, prioritizing relationships over innovation. Meanwhile, the best engineers in America — concentrated in San Francisco, Seattle, and Austin — largely refused to work on defense projects. Google employees had revolted over Project Maven, an AI contract with the Pentagon. The cultural gap between Silicon Valley and the Department of Defense had become a chasm.

Anduril would bridge it. Not by asking permission, but by building products the military didn't know it needed yet and proving they worked.

The first product was Lattice, a software platform that ingested data from multiple sensors — radar, cameras, drones — and fused it into a single operational picture. Built on it was a network of autonomous surveillance towers for border security. Not theoretical. Actual hardware deployed in actual terrain. They called the towers Sentry. The first installations went up along the southern U.S. border in 2018, using autonomous systems and AI to detect movement without requiring constant human monitoring.

This was the strategy: build real products, deploy them quickly, prove value, scale. No decade-long development cycles. No requirements documents the size of phone books. Build, test, iterate — the Silicon Valley model applied to defense.

The Pentagon noticed. So did Customs and Border Protection. Anduril won contracts. Not the massive, multi-billion dollar programs that dominated traditional defense budgets, but enough to prove the model worked. By 2019, they had raised $200 million in venture funding at a valuation exceeding $1 billion. Andreessen Horowitz led. Founders Fund, where Stephens was a partner, invested heavily.

Then they expanded. Autonomous underwater vehicles for submarine detection. Ghost, a drone designed for reconnaissance and surveillance. Dive, an autonomous underwater vehicle. Roadrunner, a reusable interceptor drone. Each product followed the same pattern: autonomous, AI-enabled, designed to be manufactured at scale with modern techniques rather than artisanal defense contractor methods.

The Ukraine war in 2022 validated the thesis brutally. Autonomous drones, cheap and numerous, proved decisive. Traditional armored vehicles became vulnerable. The conflict became a testing ground for exactly the kind of technology Anduril was building. The Pentagon's urgency increased. So did Anduril's contract pipeline.

By 2024, Anduril was valued at $14 billion. They had major contracts with the U.S. military, allied nations, and a manufacturing facility in Ohio designed to mass-produce autonomous systems. They were hiring aggressively — engineers who might have gone to Google or Meta were choosing defense work, motivated by geopolitics, technological challenge, or both.

The cultural battle continued. Protests erupted at some universities when Anduril recruited on campus. Employee activism remained a risk. But the landscape had shifted. The threat from China felt more tangible. The war in Ukraine had made autonomous systems less theoretical. A new generation of engineers seemed more willing to work on defense technology than their predecessors had been five years earlier.

Anduril's approach was working, but it remained unproven at the scale that mattered most. They had not yet won a multi-billion dollar program of record — the kind of contract that sustains defense companies across decades. Their systems had been deployed, but not tested in peer conflict against a sophisticated adversary. The manufacturing challenge of producing thousands of autonomous systems remained largely ahead of them, not behind.

They were racing against time and incumbents. Traditional defense contractors were watching, some attempting to copy the model, others lobbying to preserve existing procurement processes. Anduril's advantage was speed and technology. The question was whether they could maintain both while scaling to meet the military's actual requirements — not dozens of systems, but thousands.

Luckey had bet that the defense industry was ready for disruption, that Silicon Valley talent could be redirected toward national security, and that the Pentagon would embrace rapid iteration over glacial procurement. Seven years in, the bet looked prescient. Whether it would be vindicated or just early remained an open question. The path ahead required not just building better technology, but transforming how the world's largest bureaucracy bought weapons. One was hard. Both might be impossible.
