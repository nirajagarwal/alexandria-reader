# Atlassian

***The bootstrap that scaled without venture capital***

> **Years:** 2002-present

In 2002, two Australian university friends, Mike Cannon-Brookes and Scott Farquhar, graduated with computer science degrees and faced a choice: take graduate jobs at the usual consulting firms or try something different. They chose different, but not because they had a revolutionary vision. They wanted to replicate the comfortable lifestyle of their older peers who'd started tech companies during the dot-com boom. Their goal was modest: each make $48,500 a year — matching the graduate salary at the firms they were avoiding.

They had no groundbreaking idea. What they had was experience watching software teams struggle with basic collaboration. During university internships, they'd seen developers and project managers using email, spreadsheets, and wikis to track bugs and manage projects. It was chaos. Enterprise software existed for this — from companies like IBM and Mercury Interactive — but it cost hundreds of thousands of dollars and took months to deploy. Small teams couldn't afford it. Large teams hated it.

Cannon-Brookes and Farquhar decided to build simpler tools. Not revolutionary products, but practical ones: bug tracking and issue tracking software that teams could actually use. They called the company Atlassian, a name chosen because it would appear early in alphabetical listings. They borrowed $10,000 on credit cards to cover initial expenses and started coding.

The first product, Jira, launched in 2002. It was bug tracking software, but designed with a crucial insight: make it good enough that developers would want to use it, and cheap enough that a team lead could buy it without procurement approval. They priced it at $1,200 for small teams — a fraction of enterprise alternatives. No salespeople. Customers downloaded a trial, and if they liked it, they bought a license online. This wasn't a strategy borrowed from textbooks. It was necessity. They couldn't afford sales teams.

The model worked. Development teams, tired of enterprise bloatware, found Jira through Google searches and word of mouth. By 2004, Atlassian was profitable. By 2006, they had thousands of customers across sixty countries and had never spent money on traditional sales or marketing. The products sold themselves because they solved real problems simply.

In 2004, they launched Confluence, wiki software for team collaboration. Same model: easy to try, easy to buy, cheap enough to avoid procurement hell. The products integrated with each other, creating an ecosystem. Teams that used Jira for bug tracking adopted Confluence for documentation. The land-and-expand model happened organically.

What distinguished Atlassian wasn't just product quality — it was how they distributed and sold. While competitors hired expensive sales teams to close six-figure deals, Atlassian perfected "bottom-up" adoption. Individual teams bought licenses with departmental budgets. As usage spread, small licenses grew into large ones. A $1,200 purchase by one team became a $100,000 enterprise deployment as other teams followed. By the time procurement noticed, Atlassian was already embedded.

They also made an unusual choice: no venture capital. While Silicon Valley competitors raised millions and burned through cash building sales teams, Atlassian grew on revenue. This wasn't ideology. Cannon-Brookes and Farquhar were in Sydney, far from Sand Hill Road, and their business didn't need outside money. Profitability from year two meant they controlled their destiny. They could build for the long term without pressure to hit arbitrary growth targets or prepare for acquisition.

The strategy had consequences. Growth was steady, not explosive. They didn't dominate headlines. But they also didn't face down-rounds, layoffs, or founder dilution. By 2010, Atlassian had 300 employees and $100 million in revenue — grown entirely on customer money.

In 2010, they finally took outside investment: $60 million from Accel Partners at a $3.3 billion valuation. Notably, it wasn't to fund operations — it was to give early employees liquidity. The company didn't need capital; it needed to reward the team that had helped build it. Most of the money went to buying shares from employees and founders, not into the company treasury.

They continued expanding the product line: Bitbucket for code repositories, HipChat for team messaging, Bamboo for continuous integration. Some acquisitions succeeded; others didn't. HipChat eventually lost to Slack. But the core products — Jira and Confluence — kept growing. By 2015, they had 50,000 customers and $320 million in revenue.

In December 2015, Atlassian went public on NASDAQ at a $4.37 billion valuation. The IPO was unusual: they skipped the roadshow. No presentations to investors, no analyst meetings. They filed an F-1, set a price, and listed. Their argument: the business spoke for itself. Revenue was growing, margins were healthy, customers were loyal. After thirteen years profitable, they didn't need to convince anyone.

The market agreed. The stock opened at $27.50 and climbed. By 2021, Atlassian's market cap exceeded $100 billion. The two founders, who'd borrowed $10,000 to avoid graduate jobs, were each worth over $10 billion. They'd built one of Australia's most valuable companies without venture capital, without a sales team, and without moving to Silicon Valley.

The company faced challenges. Competition from Microsoft, Slack, and newer collaboration tools pressured margins. The shift from perpetual licenses to subscription pricing required operational changes. Cultural issues emerged as the company scaled past 5,000 employees. But the fundamentals remained: products that solved real problems, a distribution model that let customers discover and buy easily, and discipline around profitability.

Atlassian proved a path that contradicted Silicon Valley orthodoxy. You didn't need venture capital to scale. You didn't need sales teams to reach enterprise customers. You didn't need to be in the Bay Area. What you needed was product that users wanted, distribution that made buying easy, and patience to grow sustainably. The story wasn't dramatic — no pivots, no near-death moments, no founder drama. Just steady execution, year after year, until suddenly you were a giant.
