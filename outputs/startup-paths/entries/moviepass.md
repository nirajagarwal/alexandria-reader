# MoviePass

***The race to zero***

> **Years:** 2011–2019

In 2011, two entrepreneurs launched a subscription service for moviegoers. Stacy Spikes and Hamet Watt believed theaters were leaving money on the table. Frequent moviegoers paid full price every time. Casual viewers stayed home. A monthly subscription could convert the latter into the former while capturing recurring revenue from both.

The model was simple: pay a monthly fee, see unlimited movies in theaters. MoviePass would buy tickets at full price from theaters, then eat the difference. The bet was that most subscribers wouldn't see enough movies to make the company lose money on each user. They'd behave like gym members — paying monthly, attending occasionally.

Early MoviePass charged $30-50 per month depending on the market. It worked, barely. The company signed up a few thousand early adopters, mostly in major cities. Theaters were skeptical but willing to take the full-price ticket sales. Investors saw potential but not the exponential growth venture capital demands. For six years, MoviePass existed in this liminal state — a working business model that couldn't scale fast enough to justify the capital it needed.

Then, in 2017, analytics firm Helios and Matheson bought a majority stake. They had a different theory. MoviePass wasn't a subscription business. It was a data play. With millions of subscribers, they'd know everything about moviegoing behavior — who sees what, when, where, with whom. That data could be sold to studios, theater chains, advertisers. The subscription was just customer acquisition. Losses didn't matter if you were building the most valuable database in entertainment.

To acquire those millions of subscribers, they needed a price that was impossible to refuse. In August 2017, MoviePass dropped its price to $9.95 per month. For the cost of one ticket, you could see a movie every day. The response was immediate and overwhelming. Subscribers surged from 20,000 to over 3 million within a year. The service crashed repeatedly under the load. Headlines declared it the best deal in entertainment.

The economics were catastrophic. The average MoviePass subscriber saw movies that cost the company $21.80 per month. The company was losing over $20 million monthly by mid-2018. Helios and Matheson funded the losses by issuing more stock, diluting existing shareholders into oblivion. The stock price, which had peaked near $9,000 per share (pre-split), collapsed below a dollar.

The promised data business never materialized. Theaters refused to cooperate with a company trying to disrupt their pricing. Studios didn't need MoviePass's data — they had their own. Advertisers weren't interested in targeting people who'd already decided to see a movie. The vague plan to monetize user data ran into the hard reality that no one wanted to pay for it.

As cash dwindled, MoviePass began changing the rules. First, they limited new subscribers to three movies per month. Then four. Then they blocked major releases on opening weekend. They introduced surge pricing. They made subscribers photograph their ticket stubs to prove they'd actually attended. They borrowed tactics from their own worst critics, the practices that had made cable companies and airlines hated.

Subscribers revolted. The service that had been lauded as too good to be true became infamous for restrictions that made it nearly unusable. People who'd signed up for unlimited movies found themselves unable to check in, blocked from seeing popular films, or discovering that no screenings were "available" in their city. The company issued debit cards to pay for tickets, then those cards would mysteriously stop working.

By summer 2019, MoviePass was functionally dead. It still had nominal subscribers, but the service barely worked. Helios and Matheson was delisted from NASDAQ. Spikes, the original co-founder who'd been pushed out during the growth phase, watched from the outside as his creation imploded.

The autopsy was straightforward. MoviePass lost money on every transaction and had no path to profitability. The data business was fantasy. The pivot to restrictions destroyed the value proposition. Theaters never needed MoviePass — they launched their own subscription services, keeping 100% of the economics. AMC's A-List, priced at $19.95-24.95 depending on market, offered a sustainable version of the same idea: three movies per week, built on a margin structure where the theater paid itself wholesale prices, not retail.

MoviePass proved that a business model could be simultaneously popular and impossible. Millions of people wanted the service. It genuinely changed behavior, driving people back to theaters. But desire and economic reality are different things. You can't subsidize every transaction and call it customer acquisition if you never stop subsidizing. The company spent three years demonstrating that venture capital can fund negative unit economics long enough to build a massive user base, but that user base becomes worthless the moment you stop losing money on each one.

What killed MoviePass wasn't lack of demand. It was arithmetic. They needed customers to see fewer movies than they paid for. Customers who signed up saw more. The company tried to solve this with restrictions, destroying the product in the process. They'd built a business on the assumption that people wouldn't use what they paid for. Instead, they'd given movie lovers exactly what they wanted: permission to see everything. The customers won. The company died.
