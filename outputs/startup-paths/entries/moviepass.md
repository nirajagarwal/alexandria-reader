# MoviePass

***The subscription model that promised infinite movies — and burned infinite cash***

> **Years:** 2011-2019

MoviePass launched in 2011 with a simple pitch: pay a monthly fee, see unlimited movies in theaters. It wasn't a new idea — entrepreneurs had tried flat-rate cinema subscriptions before, and theater chains themselves had flirted with it. But co-founders Stacy Spikes and Hamet Watt believed the model could work if they cracked the unit economics. They built partnerships with theaters, charged $50 per month, and aimed for the sweet spot where subscription revenue exceeded the cost of tickets.

For years, MoviePass was a niche product. A few thousand film enthusiasts paid $30-50 monthly to see multiple movies. The company negotiated deals with independent theaters and some regional chains, eating the difference between what subscribers paid and what tickets cost. It was sustainable at small scale — barely. The founders raised modest venture funding and kept iterating, but growth was slow. Theater chains were wary. The big question always lingered: could this ever be profitable?

In 2016, Mitch Lowe joined as CEO. Lowe had credentials — a Netflix co-founder, former Redbox president — and brought Silicon Valley growth thinking to what had been a careful, measured operation. Around the same time, data analytics firm Helios and Matheson became interested in MoviePass not for the subscription revenue, but for the data. If you knew what millions of people watched, where they went, what they bought, you could monetize that information. You could become not a ticket reseller but a consumer insights platform.

In August 2017, Helios and Matheson acquired majority control of MoviePass and dropped the monthly price to $9.95. Unlimited movies, any theater, for less than the cost of a single ticket in most cities.

The response was instantaneous. Subscribers surged from 20,000 to 400,000 in weeks. Within months, MoviePass had over two million users. The press called it the next Netflix, a disruptor upending an industry. Lowe spoke confidently about leverage: with millions of subscribers, they could force theaters to share revenue, negotiate bulk ticket discounts, sell data to studios, drive traffic to restaurants and bars near theaters. The model wasn't about ticket arbitrage — it was about building a platform.

The problem was that none of the hypothetical revenue streams materialized fast enough. Theater chains refused to negotiate. AMC, the largest chain, openly called MoviePass's model unsustainable and launched its own competing subscription. Studios weren't buying data insights at scale. And every new subscriber cost MoviePass money — real money, immediately. At $9.95 per month, a user who saw just two movies cost the company more than they paid. Heavy users — and MoviePass attracted heavy users — could cost $50, $80, even $100 per month in ticket purchases.

Helios and Matheson kept funding the losses, betting that scale would unlock the business model. But scale accelerated the bleeding. The company burned through $20 million a month, then $40 million. By mid-2018, MoviePass had three million subscribers and was hemorrhaging cash with no path to profitability in sight.

Desperation followed. MoviePass began restricting which movies subscribers could see, blocking major releases, limiting showtimes. They implemented surge pricing. They temporarily ran out of cash and couldn't pay for tickets, leaving subscribers stranded at theater kiosks with declined cards. They changed the terms of service repeatedly, switching from unlimited to three movies per month, then adding new restrictions every few weeks. Users who had signed up for unlimited movies felt betrayed. Subscriber growth reversed. Cancellations accelerated.

Behind the scenes, the situation was darker. Helios and Matheson's stock, once buoyed by MoviePass hype, collapsed. They had no more cash to inject. MoviePass tried to raise emergency funding and failed. They tried to sell, but who would buy a company losing tens of millions monthly with no viable model? In September 2019, MoviePass shut down service entirely. By January 2020, Helios and Matheson filed for bankruptcy.

Stacy Spikes, the original co-founder who had been pushed out during the Helios acquisition, bought back the company's assets in bankruptcy. He relaunched MoviePass in 2022 with a fundamentally different model: variable pricing, credits instead of unlimited access, tiers based on usage. It was the model they probably should have had all along — sustainable, boring, unphotogenic to venture capital.

The MoviePass story is a case study in mistaking customer acquisition for product-market fit. Millions of people will sign up if you sell dollars for dimes. That proves demand for free money, not demand for your business. The company became a rocket ship by lighting cash on fire, and once the cash ran out, there was nothing underneath. The data play never materialized. The theater leverage never materialized. The advertising revenue never materialized. It was always just arbitrage, and arbitrage without a path to positive unit economics is just a very expensive hobby.

What MoviePass proved was that consumers would love a cheap movie subscription. What it failed to prove was that anyone could make money offering one at that price. AMC and other chains learned the lesson: they launched their own subscriptions, but priced them higher, restricted them to their own theaters, and built them as loyalty programs, not standalone businesses. They worked because the theater kept the concession revenue and controlled the whole experience.

MoviePass's path was the classic venture-fueled unsustainable growth story, but compressed and vivid. Most such companies die slowly, pivoting and shrinking over years. MoviePass went from obscurity to ubiquity to catastrophe in less than two years. It was a cautionary tale delivered at 10x speed.
