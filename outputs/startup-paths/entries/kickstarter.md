# Kickstarter

***The platform that changed how things get made — then struggled with what it had become***

> **Years:** 2009-present

In 2002, Perry Chen stood outside a New Orleans jazz club, frustrated. He wanted to bring a musician to the city for a show, but he couldn't afford it unless he knew others would buy tickets. The idea lodged: what if you could fund something before it existed, pooling commitments from strangers who wanted it to be real?

Seven years later, Chen teamed with Yancey Strickler and Charles Adler in a small New York apartment. They'd all worked at the edges of the creative economy — music, arts, technology — and shared a conviction that the traditional funding model was broken. Artists had to beg institutions. Inventors had to convince manufacturers. The internet made global audiences possible, but no infrastructure existed to turn scattered enthusiasm into actual money.

Kickstarter launched in April 2009 with 28 projects. The mechanism was elegant: all-or-nothing funding. A creator sets a goal and deadline. Backers pledge money. If the goal is met, the creator gets funded and must deliver. If not, nobody pays. This wasn't pre-order. It wasn't investment. It was something new: patronage at scale, with accountability built in.

The timing was accidental brilliance. The 2008 financial crisis had frozen traditional capital. But it had also demolished faith in institutions. People wanted to fund things directly — to see their money create something tangible. Kickstarter offered that. The early projects were small: an indie album, a short film, a design object. But the platform revealed something nobody quite expected: the intensity of demand for things the market said didn't exist.

In 2010, a playing card company called Theory11 raised $250,000 — twenty times their goal. Not because playing cards were revolutionary, but because they'd found their people. This became the pattern. Kickstarter didn't just fund projects. It proved markets existed. It showed creators they weren't alone.

The breakthrough came in 2012 with Pebble. A smartwatch, before Apple made smartwatches. Pebble asked for $100,000. They raised $10.3 million from 69,000 backers, shattering every record. Suddenly Kickstarter wasn't just for artists. It was a product-launch platform. Hardware companies used it to validate demand before manufacturing. Game studios bypassed publishers. The platform became infrastructure for an entire mode of production: make the thing your audience actually wants, not what a gatekeeper thinks will sell.

By 2013, Kickstarter had funded $1 billion in projects. Oculus raised $2.4 million there, then sold to Facebook for $2 billion — causing fury among backers who'd helped birth it, only to see it absorbed by exactly the kind of corporation Kickstarter was supposed to circumvent. This tension — between empowering creators and feeding the traditional system — would never resolve.

The platform's problems multiplied with its success. Fraud became endemic. Projects raised millions, then delivered nothing or something terrible. Backers had no recourse. They weren't customers with consumer protection. They weren't investors with equity. They were patrons left holding the bag. The all-or-nothing model prevented obvious scams but couldn't stop incompetence or dishonesty after funding.

Worse, success warped incentives. Kickstarter became a marketing engine. Companies with capital used it to generate buzz and validate concepts, not because they needed the money. The platform designed for underdogs became a launch pad for the well-connected. Algorithms favored projects already succeeding, creating winner-take-all dynamics. The democracy of funding became an oligarchy of visibility.

Internally, Kickstarter struggled with its identity. In 2015, they became a Public Benefit Corporation — legally obligating themselves to balance profit with public good. In 2017, staff tried to unionize; management resisted, then accepted, becoming the first major tech company with a union. In 2020, they laid off workers amid the union's first contract negotiation. The company preaching creator empowerment was fighting its own staff over power.

The blockchain era brought fresh crisis. In 2021, Kickstarter announced it would rebuild on blockchain technology. The backlash was immediate and fierce. Creators and backers saw it as capitulation to crypto's extractive logic. After months of protest, the company quietly downplayed the initiative. It proceeded, but without conviction.

Meanwhile, the market Kickstarter created had been colonized. Indiegogo was already a competitor. Then came equity crowdfunding platforms like Seedrs and Crowdcube. Patreon took the creator-support model and made it recurring. GoFundMe turned crowdfunding into charity infrastructure. YouTube, Twitch, and OnlyFans integrated direct fan support into their platforms. Kickstarter's innovation — connecting creators with supporters — became table stakes.

The company never became a giant. It never IPO'd. It remained profitable but constrained by its principles: the 5% fee, the no-equity model, the commitment to not exploit users for maximum growth. These principles prevented Kickstarter from becoming extractive. They also prevented it from becoming dominant.

Today, Kickstarter occupies a strange position. It still funds thousands of projects annually. Graphic novels, tabletop games, and design objects thrive there. But the platform's cultural centrality has faded. It succeeded so thoroughly at proving the model that others could copy it, improve parts, and capture specific niches. Kickstarter showed that direct creator-supporter relationships could work at scale. Then scale fractured into a thousand specialized platforms.

The company built the infrastructure for a more direct economy. Then discovered that infrastructure, once built, belongs to everyone.
