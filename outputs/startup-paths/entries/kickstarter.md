# Kickstarter

***The platform that made patronage scalable, then struggled with what it had created***

> **Years:** 2009-present

In 2009, Perry Chen was still thinking about a concert he'd wanted to produce in New Orleans five years earlier. He'd needed money upfront but couldn't guarantee ticket sales. The traditional funding model — investors taking equity, or loans requiring repayment — made no sense for a one-night event. What he needed was a way for people to commit money contingent on the show happening. If enough people said yes, everyone would be charged. If not, nobody paid anything. The show never happened, but the idea wouldn't leave him alone.

Chen met Yancey Strickler, a music journalist, and Charles Adler, a designer, in New York. They shared a frustration with how creative projects got funded — or more often, didn't. Record labels, publishers, and galleries controlled access to capital, which meant they controlled which art got made. The internet had democratized distribution; anyone could put music or writing online. But it hadn't democratized funding. Making things still required money upfront, and most creators couldn't get it.

They built Kickstarter as a platform for all-or-nothing crowdfunding. A creator set a funding goal and a deadline. Backers pledged money, but their cards weren't charged unless the project hit its goal. If it fell short, everyone walked away. This solved two problems: creators knew they had enough money to deliver before committing, and backers weren't left funding doomed projects. The company would take 5% of successful campaigns.

The early projects were small and idiosyncratic. An album recording. A documentary. A performance piece. Kickstarter was explicitly not for equity investment or loans. It was patronage, structured for the internet age. Backers got rewards — early copies, credits, experiences — but not ownership. This kept it outside securities regulation and preserved something important: the relationship was between creator and audience, not entrepreneur and investor.

The model worked. By 2012, Kickstarter had enabled over 25,000 projects and moved over $300 million to creators. Then Pebble happened. A smartwatch project raised $10.3 million from 68,000 backers, making it one of the most-funded projects in crowdfunding history. Suddenly Kickstarter wasn't just for artists scraping together $5,000. It was a product launch platform, a market research tool, a manufacturing pre-order system for hardware startups.

This was both triumph and complication. The Pebble campaign proved Kickstarter could change not just creative funding but how consumer products reached market. Hardware startups could validate demand and fund initial production without venture capital. But it also shifted the platform's center of gravity. The artists who'd been the core community found themselves competing for attention with gadgets and games. More importantly, the nature of the backer relationship changed. Backing an album meant supporting an artist. Backing a smartwatch meant buying a product, or thinking you were.

The fundamental tension was this: Kickstarter had built infrastructure for patronage but discovered it was also infrastructure for pre-orders. The company insisted it wasn't a store, but backers increasingly treated it like one. When projects failed to deliver — and many did, especially complex hardware projects — backers felt defrauded. They'd given money expecting a product, and the product didn't come. Kickstarter's position was that backers were supporting an attempt, not buying a guarantee, but this framing satisfied neither creators drowning in angry comments nor backers who'd waited years for vaporware.

The company made choices that revealed what it wanted to be. In 2015, it became a Public Benefit Corporation, legally committing to balance profit with public good. It remained independent, resisting acquisition offers and venture pressure to maximize growth. In 2017, Strickler, by then CEO, announced they were exploring blockchain technology, a decision that provoked internal revolt and was quietly abandoned. The company understood its community was its moat but kept misjudging what that community wanted.

By 2020, Kickstarter had facilitated over $5 billion in pledges across more than 200,000 projects. It had launched careers, funded films that won Oscars, and spawned a cultural model where creators went directly to audiences. But it had also become a graveyard of broken promises. The most successful projects were often the most troubled — ambitious hardware that proved harder to manufacture than anticipated, video games that ballooned in scope, creators who discovered that managing 50,000 backers was a job in itself.

The platform never solved the core contradiction. It wanted to be infrastructure for creative risk-taking, but users — both creators and backers — wanted it to be a reliable marketplace. It insisted projects could fail, but its business model required enough successes to keep backers coming back. It positioned itself as an alternative to traditional gatekeepers, but over time it accumulated its own gatekeeping function through opaque moderation and featured projects that shaped what succeeded.

Kickstarter proved you could build a billion-dollar company on patronage, but it couldn't decide whether patronage meant supporting attempts or buying results. The ambiguity was profitable — it attracted both communities — but corrosive. The company that made crowdfunding mainstream never quite figured out what crowdfunding was for, and spent fifteen years managing the tension between two incompatible answers.
