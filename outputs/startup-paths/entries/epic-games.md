# Epic Games

***The engine that became an empire***

> **Years:** 1991-present

Tim Sweeney taught himself programming on an Apple II in his parents' basement in Potomac, Maryland. In 1991, at age twenty, he founded Potomac Computer Systems to sell shareware games he'd written. The company's first release, ZZT, was a text-mode adventure game built with its own level editor. The editor mattered more than anyone realized at the time. Players could create their own levels and share them. Sweeney had stumbled onto something: tools were more valuable than content.

He renamed the company Epic MegaGames in 1992, publishing shareware titles like Jill of the Jungle and Jazz Jackrabbit through the mid-nineties. These were competent platform games in an era dominated by Sonic and Mario, but they paid the bills and taught Sweeney's small team how to build. More importantly, Sweeney was building something else in parallel: a game engine. He called it the Unreal Engine, and it would power Epic's 1998 first-person shooter, Unreal.

Unreal arrived into a market shaped by id Software's Quake II. It needed to compete on graphics, performance, and moddability. Sweeney's engine delivered all three. But the real insight came after launch: other developers wanted to license it. Digital Extremes, Legend Entertainment, Ion Storm — studios building their own games wanted Epic's technology. Sweeney had built tools for himself; he discovered he'd built a business licensing tools to everyone else.

The Unreal Engine business grew alongside Epic's game development. Unreal Tournament in 1999 became a phenomenon in competitive multiplayer gaming. The company dropped "Mega" from its name, becoming simply Epic Games. But the tension was already apparent: was Epic a game studio that licensed technology, or a technology company that made games to prove its engine?

In 2006, Epic released Gears of War as an Xbox 360 exclusive. The game showcased Unreal Engine 3's capabilities and became a massive hit, selling millions and spawning a franchise. Microsoft paid for exclusivity; Epic collected both publishing fees and engine royalties from the wave of studios adopting UE3 for their own projects. By the late 2000s, Unreal Engine powered a substantial portion of the industry's AAA titles. Sweeney had positioned Epic at the center of game development infrastructure.

Then the ground shifted. Mobile gaming exploded. Free-to-play games replaced premium titles. Digital distribution undermined retail. Unity Technologies emerged with a cheaper, more accessible engine that dominated mobile. Epic's strength in high-end console and PC graphics suddenly looked like expensive overkill for the growth segments of the market. Between 2008 and 2012, Tencent acquired a 40% stake in Epic for $330 million, providing capital but also validation that Epic needed an injection of resources to compete in changing markets.

Epic's response came in 2017 with Fortnite. The game began as a cooperative zombie survival shooter, a modest project that had languished in development. Then Epic's team saw PlayerUnknown's Battlegrounds exploding and made a decision: pivot the game to battle royale mode. Fortnite Battle Royale launched in September 2017, two months after they started building it. Free-to-play, cartoonish, accessible across platforms, constantly updated with new content and events. Within a year, it had 125 million players.

The money was staggering. Fortnite generated $5.4 billion in 2018 alone, mostly from cosmetic microtransactions. Players paid for character skins, emotes, and battle passes in a game they got for free. Epic had built the infrastructure to update the game constantly, host live events inside it, and process billions in transactions. Fortnite wasn't just a game; it was a platform, a social space, a cultural phenomenon among teenagers worldwide.

With Fortnite profits, Sweeney moved aggressively into platform wars. In 2018, Epic launched the Epic Games Store to challenge Steam's dominance in PC game distribution. Where Valve took a 30% cut, Epic took 12%. They paid for exclusives, gave away free games weekly, and burned hundreds of millions subsidizing their assault on Steam's market position. Developers liked the economics; many players hated the fragmentation and the forced launcher.

In 2020, Epic deliberately violated Apple's App Store rules by implementing direct payments in Fortnite's iOS version, bypassing Apple's 30% fee. Apple removed Fortnite from the App Store. Epic immediately sued, arguing Apple's control over iOS app distribution was an illegal monopoly. The case was calculated confrontation: Epic had prepared the lawsuit in advance, complete with an Orwell-parody video mocking Apple's famous 1984 ad. After a trial in 2021, Epic largely lost. The judge ruled Apple could keep its App Store monopoly but required Apple to allow developers to link to outside payment systems. Both sides appealed.

Throughout this period, Unreal Engine continued evolving. By the time Unreal Engine 5 launched in 2022, it powered not just games but film production, architectural visualization, and virtual production for television. The Mandalorian used UE for its groundbreaking LED volume stages. Epic had positioned its engine as infrastructure for any real-time 3D content, not just games. The company employed thousands, had made Sweeney a billionaire multiple times over, and wielded influence across entertainment and technology.

Epic's path reveals a pattern: tools outlast content. Sweeney's games succeeded, but his engines built empires. The company that started selling shareware in the early nineties became critical infrastructure for an industry, then leveraged one massive hit to fund an assault on platform economics themselves. They went from making the picks and shovels to mining gold to challenging who controls the mine. Fortnite gave them the resources to fight Apple and build a competing store; the engine gave them staying power beyond any single game's lifecycle. The basement programmer who taught himself to code built something larger than he initially imagined, then kept building, tool after tool, battle after battle.
