# Epic Games

***From game engine to platform power — and a fight with Apple that redefined the war for digital distribution***

> **Years:** 1991–present

Tim Sweeney started Epic Games in his parents' basement in Potomac, Maryland, in 1991, teaching himself to code by reading assembly language manuals. His first game, ZZT, was a shareware success — simple ASCII graphics, but it came with an editor that let players create their own worlds. That editor was the signal of everything to come. Sweeney wasn't just building games. He was building tools.

Through the nineties, Epic released a string of successful titles — Jazz Jackrabbit, Unreal. But Unreal was different. The game itself was a solid first-person shooter, but the real innovation was the Unreal Engine, the technology powering it. Sweeney had built a modular, licensable game engine that other developers could use. While id Software kept its tech proprietary, Epic sold access. By 1998, studios were licensing Unreal Engine to build their own games. Epic had found a business model that didn't depend on hit after hit. It could profit from everyone else's hits.

The engine business scaled. Unreal Engine 2 powered hits like Tom Clancy's Splinter Cell and BioShock. Unreal Engine 3 became the industry standard for the Xbox 360 and PlayStation 3 era. Epic wasn't the biggest game company, but it was the infrastructure. Developers paid upfront licensing fees plus royalties on sales. The incentives aligned: Epic succeeded when its customers succeeded.

In 2006, Epic released Gears of War, a franchise that became a tentpole for Microsoft's Xbox. The company was profitable, independent, growing. Sweeney maintained majority control — rare for a company of Epic's scale. Tencent invested $330 million in 2012 for a 40% stake, valuing Epic at over $800 million, but Sweeney kept decision-making power. The capital gave Epic room to take risks without answering to a board demanding quarterly growth.

The first big risk was Fortnite. Originally conceived as a cooperative survival game, Fortnite languished in development for years. It launched in July 2017 to decent but not exceptional reception. Then PUBG exploded. The battle royale format — 100 players, last one standing — was suddenly everywhere. Epic saw the opening. In two months, a small team inside Epic built Fortnite Battle Royale. It launched in September 2017 as a free-to-play mode.

It was a cultural phenomenon. By mid-2018, Fortnite had 125 million players. It wasn't just big — it was ubiquitous. Kids played it, celebrities played it, athletes did Fortnite dances in end zones. Epic's monetization was brilliant: the game was free, but players paid for cosmetic items — skins, emotes, pickaxes. No pay-to-win, just digital fashion. Fortnite generated $5.4 billion in 2018 alone.

The Fortnite windfall gave Epic leverage to make a move on distribution. For years, Valve's Steam had been the dominant PC game store, taking a 30% cut of every sale. Epic launched the Epic Games Store in December 2018, offering developers an 88/12 split. They paid tens of millions for timed exclusives, undercutting Steam with better terms and guaranteed revenue for developers. It was a direct assault on Valve's monopoly.

Then Epic went after mobile. Apple's App Store and Google's Play Store both took 30% of in-app purchases. For years, developers grumbled but complied — there was no alternative. In August 2020, Epic pushed an update to Fortnite on iOS and Android that let players buy V-Bucks (in-game currency) directly from Epic at a discount, bypassing Apple and Google's payment systems. Within hours, both companies removed Fortnite from their stores.

Epic immediately filed lawsuits and released a video parodying Apple's famous 1984 ad, casting Apple as Big Brother. This wasn't an accident. Epic had planned the confrontation, built a coalition of developers frustrated with app store fees, and framed the fight as a battle for the open internet. Sweeney positioned Epic as the underdog fighting monopolistic gatekeepers, even though Epic was worth over $15 billion.

The legal battle dragged on. In 2021, a judge ruled that Apple didn't have an illegal monopoly on mobile gaming but ordered Apple to allow developers to link to external payment methods. Both sides appealed. Epic lost on most counts but won a symbolic crack in Apple's armor. More importantly, the case put regulatory pressure on Apple and Google worldwide. South Korea passed a law banning app store payment monopolies. The EU's Digital Markets Act forced Apple to allow third-party app stores.

Epic didn't win the lawsuit outright, but it moved the window. The fight made app store fees a political issue, not just a business gripe. Sweeney was willing to burn billions in Fortnite revenue to wage the war. Fortnite remained banned on iOS — the most lucrative mobile platform — for years. Epic absorbed the loss.

By 2023, Epic was valued at $31.5 billion. Unreal Engine 5 had become the standard for next-gen games and was expanding into film, architecture, and virtual production. Fortnite remained a cash machine despite the iOS ban. The Epic Games Store was still unprofitable but growing, sustained by Fortnite money. Epic had acquired Bandcamp, Sketchfab, and ArtStation — infrastructure for creators. The strategy was clear: build the tools, control the platforms, own the distribution.

Epic's path wasn't about hitting a single jackpot. It was a decades-long accumulation of leverage: game success funded engine development, engine revenue funded platform experiments, Fortnite's windfall funded a war on app stores. Sweeney never cashed out, never went public, never let investors dilute his control. He built a company that could afford to fight multi-year legal battles because it owned the infrastructure everyone else depended on. Epic didn't just make games. It made the battlefield and then chose its wars.
