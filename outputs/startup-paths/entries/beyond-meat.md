# Beyond Meat

***The mission-driven IPO that discovered Wall Street doesn't care about mission***

> **Years:** 2009-present

Ethan Brown was not a vegan when he decided to build a company that would replace meat. He was a businessman who had spent years in clean energy and fuel cells, convinced that the biggest climate problem wasn't energy—it was livestock. Animals raised for food generated more greenhouse gases than all transportation combined. But telling people to eat less meat had failed for decades. Brown's insight was simpler: make plant-based meat that carnivores would actually choose.

He founded Beyond Meat in 2009 with backing from Kleiner Perkins and the Obvious Corporation, the investment vehicle of Twitter's founders. Early investors included Bill Gates, who had become obsessed with agricultural emissions. The pitch was compelling—not "eat vegetables" but "eat meat made from vegetables." The technology came from Fu-Hung Hsieh, a University of Missouri food scientist who had figured out how to align plant proteins to mimic muscle fiber. Brown licensed the patents and started building.

The first products were terrible. Beyond Chicken strips launched in 2012 at Whole Foods, marketed as a meat substitute for health-conscious shoppers. They tasted like health food. Sales were modest. The company burned cash developing better formulations while competitors like Impossible Foods raised more money and generated more buzz. For years, Beyond Meat was the also-ran in the fake meat race, the company investors tolerated because of Brown's relentlessness and the mission's appeal.

Everything changed with the Beyond Burger in 2016. Instead of mimicking processed chicken, Brown's team reverse-engineered ground beef—the texture, the browning, the way it cooked. They used beet juice to make it "bleed." And crucially, they sold it in the meat aisle, not the vegetarian section. The product was designed for meat-eaters, positioned as meat, priced like premium meat. Whole Foods put it in 280 stores. It sold out immediately.

The Beyond Burger created a category. Sales went from $16 million in 2016 to $88 million in 2017 to $88 million in just the first quarter of 2019. Restaurant chains started calling. TGI Friday's, Carl's Jr., A&W in Canada—mainstream brands wanted plant-based options, and Beyond Meat had the product that didn't taste like compromise. The company signed a deal with Dunkin' for breakfast sausage. Another with KFC for plant-based chicken. Distribution expanded to Costco, Target, Kroger.

Brown took the company public in May 2019, one of the year's most anticipated IPOs. Beyond Meat priced at $25, a valuation that made venture investors nervous—too high, too early, too much hype. The stock opened at $46 and hit $239 by July, making it the best-performing IPO of the year. The company was worth $14 billion despite annual revenue of just $88 million. Wall Street had decided plant-based meat was the future, and Beyond Meat was going to own it.

Then reality arrived. The stock's peak came before the company could deliver the growth investors had priced in. Competition intensified—Impossible Foods expanded retail distribution, legacy food companies launched their own plant-based lines, and restaurant chains played suppliers against each other for better terms. The pandemic initially boosted Beyond Meat as consumers stocked home kitchens, but restaurant sales collapsed. Revenue grew but not fast enough to justify the valuation.

By 2022, the cracks were obvious. Revenue growth slowed to single digits. The company had expanded internationally and into new products before proving profitability in core markets. Operating losses widened. Retailers cut shelf space as demand softened and competition crowded freezers. The stock that hit $239 fell below $10. The market cap dropped from $14 billion to under $600 million.

The problem wasn't the product—the Beyond Burger worked. The problem was the assumption that creating a good meat alternative would automatically generate a massive, loyal customer base. Beyond Meat discovered that most consumers were curious triers, not committed converts. People would buy plant-based meat occasionally, when convenient or on sale, but rarely as a full replacement. The die-hard vegans and vegetarians were a small base. The flexitarians were fickle. The meat-eaters mostly kept eating meat.

Brown's mission hadn't changed, but the market had stopped caring about mission. Investors wanted profits, not impact metrics about carbon saved or animals spared. The company cut costs, laid off staff, and consolidated product lines. By 2024, Beyond Meat was profitable in some quarters but shrinking overall. It remained the category leader in a category that turned out to be smaller than the hype suggested.

Beyond Meat proved you could build great plant-based meat and still struggle to build a great business. The path from innovation to IPO had worked. The path from IPO to sustainable giant hadn't. It became a case study in what happens when a mission-driven company goes public at a mission-driven valuation and then has to answer to shareholders who just want returns.
