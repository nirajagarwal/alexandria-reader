# Tesla

***The bet that electric cars could be aspirational, not compromises***

> **Years:** 2003-present

In 2003, Martin Eberhard and Marc Tarpenning founded Tesla Motors with a contrarian thesis: electric cars had failed not because the technology was impossible, but because carmakers had built them as penalty boxes for environmentalists. Toyota's RAV4 EV had shown the technology worked. The EV1, which GM famously crushed, had inspired a cult following. The problem wasn't the motor. It was that no one wanted to drive an electric golf cart.

Eberhard and Tarpenning were serial entrepreneurs, not car guys. They started with a plan to use lithium-ion laptop batteries — thousands of them, packaged into a single battery pack. The technology existed. The question was whether you could make something people actually wanted to buy.

The first insight: don't compete with the Prius on price. Build a car so desirable that being electric becomes an advantage, not a compromise. They licensed a chassis from Lotus and aimed for a high-end sports car. The Roadster would be fast, expensive, and electric — in that order.

Elon Musk entered in 2004, leading the Series A and joining the board. He had just sold PayPal and was spending his fortune on risky bets. He believed in the mission but also saw himself as the visionary. Within two years, the relationship with Eberhard deteriorated. Musk pushed for more control. By 2007, Eberhard was out. Musk became CEO in 2008, just as the financial crisis hit and the company was running out of cash.

The Roadster shipped in 2008 — two years late, over budget, and to a company on the edge of bankruptcy. But it worked. It proved the concept. Celebrities bought them. The Tesla brand meant performance, not penance.

The next bet was existential: could they build a real car company? The Model S was the answer — a luxury sedan designed in-house, manufactured at scale. They bought a shuttered GM-Toyota factory in Fremont for pennies on the dollar. They raised money, burned through it, raised more. The Model S launched in 2012 to rave reviews. Motor Trend named it Car of the Year. Tesla had credibility.

But credibility didn't mean profitability. Production was a nightmare. The Model X, an SUV with absurdly complex falcon-wing doors, became a manufacturing disaster. Musk had insisted on features that made production nearly impossible. The company bled cash. Short sellers circled. Every quarter, analysts predicted collapse.

The Model 3 was supposed to be the mass-market car that made Tesla sustainable. Announced in 2016, orders flooded in — 400,000 reservations within weeks. But actually building it nearly destroyed the company. Musk called it "production hell." Robots couldn't assemble the car. The factory couldn't hit targets. Musk slept on the factory floor, personally debugging the line. By mid-2018, Tesla was burning $7,000 per minute. Bankruptcy seemed inevitable.

Then they hit the production target. 5,000 Model 3s per week by July 2018. It was ragged and desperate, but it worked. The company turned profitable — barely, briefly, but enough. The short thesis broke. Tesla survived.

What followed was validation beyond what even believers expected. Tesla became the most valuable carmaker on Earth, worth more than the next ten combined. The stock became a meme, a movement, a battleground. Musk's behavior grew erratic — tweeting threats to take the company private, smoking weed on podcasts, fighting with regulators. The SEC forced him to step down as chairman. He stayed as CEO.

By 2020, Tesla was printing cash. The Model 3 was the best-selling luxury car in America. The Model Y, a crossover SUV, launched to instant demand. The company built Gigafactories in Shanghai, Berlin, and Texas. Every traditional carmaker scrambled to build electric vehicles, validating Tesla's thesis but also threatening its lead.

The company's valuation made no conventional sense. It sold a fraction of the cars that Toyota or Volkswagen did, yet was worth multiples more. Bulls saw the future — a company that would dominate not just EVs but autonomous driving, energy storage, and AI. Bears saw a bubble inflated by hype and Musk's cult of personality.

Tesla had done what skeptics said was impossible. It built an American car company from scratch — the first to succeed since Chrysler in 1925. It made electric cars aspirational. It forced an entire industry to change. But it remained inseparable from Musk — his vision, his chaos, his persona. The company's value was tied not just to its cars but to belief in his ability to will impossible things into existence.

By 2024, the competitive landscape had shifted. Every major carmaker was launching EVs. Chinese manufacturers like BYD were building cheaper, competitive alternatives. Tesla's growth slowed. The Cybertruck, a polarizing stainless steel wedge, arrived years late to mixed reviews. Musk's attention was divided — running SpaceX, acquiring Twitter, pursuing AGI ambitions.

Tesla had proven electric cars could win. Whether Tesla itself would remain the winner was a different question. The company had survived every crisis by defying expectations. The final test was whether it could thrive not as a scrappy insurgent, but as an incumbent defending its lead.
