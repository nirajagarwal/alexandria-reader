# Peloton

***The pandemic windfall that became a cautionary tale***

> **Years:** 2012-present

John Foley wasn't trying to reinvent fitness when he founded Peloton in 2012. He was trying to solve a problem he had: as a father with a demanding job, he couldn't get to boutique spin classes anymore. The classes cost $34 each in New York City, required advance booking, and demanded an hour of travel time he didn't have. What if the experience could come to him?

The idea was straightforward—put a high-end stationary bike in people's homes, stream live classes, and charge a subscription. But the execution required threading several needles at once. Peloton needed to be a hardware company, building a premium bike with a tablet attached. A content company, producing dozens of live classes daily with charismatic instructors. A software company, creating apps for streaming and community features. And a logistics company, delivering 140-pound bikes into living rooms and setting them up.

Each of those businesses is hard. Doing all of them simultaneously, as a startup with no revenue, was harder. Foley and his co-founders—Tom Cortese, Graham Stanton, Hisao Kushi, and Yony Feng—spent their first year just building a prototype. They launched a Kickstarter in 2013 that raised $307,000, validating that people would pay $1,500 for a connected bike. That was enough to raise a Series A.

The business model was unusual. Peloton lost money on every bike sold initially—the $2,000 price barely covered manufacturing and delivery. The bet was on subscription revenue: $39 per month, recurring, from people who used the bike regularly. This meant Peloton needed retention. If customers bought the bike and stopped using it within a few months, the unit economics collapsed.

The retention bet paid off. Peloton's classes weren't generic workout videos. They were live, with leaderboards showing your rank against thousands of other riders in real time. Instructors shouted out usernames during class. There were themed rides—45 minutes of Beyoncé, or a holiday ride, or a Pride celebration. Users developed parasocial relationships with instructors like Robin Arzón and Cody Rigsby. The bikes sat in living rooms, always visible, always available, with classes starting every 15 minutes. Retention rates were extraordinary—over 90% of buyers still used the bike after a year.

The company grew fast but not smoothly. Demand consistently outpaced supply. In 2016, Peloton ran out of bikes and had to pause marketing. In 2017, they couldn't deliver bikes fast enough and faced eight-week backlogs. Every growth surge exposed new breaking points—instructor capacity, studio space, delivery logistics, customer service. Foley kept raising capital to build ahead of demand: $325 million in 2017, $550 million in 2018. By the time Peloton went public in September 2019 at a $8 billion valuation, it had raised $1.2 billion.

The IPO was rough. Peloton priced at $29 and promptly fell to $20. Investors questioned whether the market was big enough. How many people would pay $2,000 for a bike plus $39 a month? The company was still unprofitable, burning $150 million a quarter, and heavily dependent on U.S. urban markets. Then came the holiday ad—a husband gifts his already-thin wife a Peloton bike, and she documents her "fitness journey" in what viewers read as a dystopian portrait of marital pressure. The ad went viral for all the wrong reasons. The stock fell below $25.

Then came March 2020. Gyms closed. Boutique fitness studios shut down. SoulCycle stopped operating. Suddenly millions of people were stuck at home, unable to access the classes they used to take. Peloton became essential. Demand exploded. The company went back to multi-month delivery delays. The stock climbed to $30, then $50, then $80. By December 2020, it hit $160—a $50 billion valuation. Revenue in fiscal 2021 would reach $4 billion, up from $1.8 billion the year before.

Peloton responded by spending. They bought Precor, a commercial fitness equipment maker, for $420 million to secure manufacturing capacity. They opened a U.S. factory in Ohio. They hired thousands of employees—going from 2,500 to 8,600 in a year. They expanded into new categories, launching a $4,000 treadmill. They invested heavily in international markets. Every decision assumed the pandemic demand was permanent.

It wasn't. By mid-2021, gyms were reopening. Vaccines were widespread. People who had bought bikes during lockdown were using them less. Worse, Peloton had flooded the market—most people who wanted a Peloton and could afford one had already bought one. New customer acquisition costs were rising. The company cut the bike price from $2,245 to $1,495, destroying margins. Revenue growth stalled. The stock fell from $160 to $80 to $40.

The treadmill became a crisis. In 2021, Peloton's Tread+ was linked to the death of a child and dozens of injuries. The company initially resisted a recall, then reversed course and recalled both treadmill models. Sales of the Tread were suspended for months. The safety issue crystallized a broader question: was Peloton's operational competence matching its ambition?

By February 2022, the stock was at $24—down 85% from its peak. Peloton announced layoffs of 2,800 employees. Foley stepped down as CEO, replaced by Barry McCarthy, the former CFO of Spotify and Netflix. McCarthy's mandate was clear: stop the bleeding. He cut jobs again, closed the Ohio factory before it fully opened, outsourced manufacturing, and explored strategic alternatives including a potential sale.

No buyer emerged at a price the board would accept. Instead, McCarthy focused on wringing profitability from the existing base. He experimented with rental models, corporate wellness deals, and app-only subscriptions for people who didn't own Peloton hardware. He cut content production costs. By 2023, the company was no longer hemorrhaging cash, but growth was elusive. The subscriber base stabilized around 3 million but stopped expanding. The stock hovered in the $5-$10 range.

Peloton had built something real—a loyal community, a brand people loved, retention metrics most consumer companies envy. But it had scaled for a future that didn't arrive. The pandemic wasn't a new normal; it was a temporary anomaly. The company spent two years building for a market that evaporated in six months. What was left was a smaller, profitable business trapped in the cost structure of a giant.
