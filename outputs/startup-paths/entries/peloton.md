# Peloton

***The pandemic darling that proved temporary demand is not permanent growth***

> **Years:** 2012-present

John Foley was not a cyclist. He was a frustrated executive who hated his commute to the gym. In 2011, working at Barnes & Noble's digital division, he paid $2,000 for a spinning class package in Manhattan and found himself skipping sessions because getting there took an hour. The classes themselves were electric — the instructor, the music, the room full of people pushing themselves together. What he wanted was that experience without the schlep.

The insight wasn't that people would pay for home fitness equipment. That market was old and saturated with dust-gathering treadmills. The insight was that what made boutique fitness worth $30 per session wasn't the bike — it was the performance. The instructor calling you out by name. The leaderboard showing you beating yesterday's you. The community of strangers becoming a tribe.

Foley assembled a team including Tom Cortese, a former executive from evite and TRX, and Yony Feng, an engineer. They spent a year building a prototype. The hardware was straightforward — a stationary bike with a tablet. The hard part was the content operation. They needed to replicate the energy of a live class through a screen, stream it reliably, make the experience feel intimate despite being broadcast to thousands. They needed instructors who could command a room they couldn't see.

They launched on Kickstarter in 2013, asking $1,200 for early units of a bike that would retail for $1,500. They raised $307,000. It validated demand but revealed the challenge: hardware margins are thin, shipping bikes is expensive, and customer acquisition costs are brutal. This wasn't software. Every sale required manufacturing, logistics, installation, and customer service for a physical object that could break.

The model only worked if people stayed. The bike was the razor; the $39 monthly subscription was the blade. But unlike razors, people could stop using the bike and forget to cancel. Engagement became everything. Peloton obsessed over it. They tracked output per ride, frequency of use, social follows, milestone achievements. They hired instructors who were part drill sergeant, part therapist, part influencer. Robin Arzón, Cody Rigsby, Ally Love — they became celebrities to the devoted.

The community formed organically and intensely. Facebook groups. Hashtags. Real-life meetups. The leaderboard — once just a feature — became a social network. People competed with strangers across the country, then followed them on social media. They scheduled their days around live classes. They bought Peloton apparel to wear when not on the bike. The brand became identity.

Foley raised venture capital aggressively: $10.5 million in 2014, $30 million in 2015, $325 million in 2017. The company was burning cash on customer acquisition, content production, and inventory. But the unit economics worked if customers stayed, and customers were staying. By 2017, Peloton claimed 90% of bikes were still in use after a year. The company was growing 150% annually.

They expanded. Added a treadmill. Opened retail showrooms in high-traffic neighborhoods — modern, airy spaces where people could try the bike and feel the brand. Launched an app-only tier for people who didn't want the hardware. The addressable market kept growing.

They went public in September 2019 at $29 per share, raising $1.16 billion. The market was skeptical. The stock dropped below $20 within months. Analysts questioned whether this was a sustainable business or an expensive fad. Peloton was still unprofitable. The COVID doubters multiplied. Some said the market was saturated — how many people would pay $2,500 for a bike? Short sellers piled on.

Then March 2020 arrived.

Gyms closed. Boutique fitness studios shut down overnight. People were trapped at home, anxious, needing outlet and routine. Peloton couldn't manufacture bikes fast enough. Wait times stretched from weeks to months. The stock rocketed from $27 to $170. Revenue doubled, then doubled again. In fiscal 2021, Peloton did $4 billion in revenue, up from $1.8 billion the year before. They had 2.3 million subscribers.

The company acted like the surge was permanent. They invested $420 million in a factory in Ohio. They bought Precor, a commercial fitness equipment maker, for $420 million. They expanded the product line. They hired thousands. Foley went on CNBC and projected 15 million subscribers.

The pandemic ended. Gyms reopened. People wanted to be around other people again. They wanted to leave their homes. The waitlist evaporated. Then it reversed — warehouses filled with unsold inventory. Peloton cut prices. Offered financing. The $39 subscription hadn't changed, but growth stalled. The people who wanted Pelotons had bought them. The people who bought them during lockdown stopped using them.

In 2021, the company faced a crisis it never anticipated: a child died in an accident involving the Tread+ treadmill. Then another. Peloton initially fought a recall, then relented. The brand took damage. The safety issue became a symbol of a company that had scaled too fast.

The stock collapsed from its pandemic highs. By January 2022, it was below $25, down 85% from peak. The board pushed Foley out as CEO and replaced him with Barry McCarthy, former CFO of Spotify and Netflix. McCarthy made brutal cuts: 4,000 layoffs, outsourced manufacturing, closed retail stores, paused content spending.

The turnaround narrative didn't catch. Revenue kept declining. Subscribers weren't leaving at alarming rates, but they weren't growing. The company that had been valued at $50 billion now scraped $2 billion. McCarthy tried pivoting to services, partnering with Lululemon, selling used bikes. Nothing reignited growth.

In May 2024, McCarthy resigned. The company announced more layoffs — another 15% of staff. Rumors circulated about private equity buyers, strategic acquirers, maybe bankruptcy. The bikes still work. The instructors still teach. The subscribers who stayed remain devoted. But the company built for exponential growth discovered it had captured temporary demand and mistaken it for a permanent shift in behavior.

Peloton's path wasn't failure of execution or product. It was the oldest mistake in venture-backed startups: confusing a moment for a movement.
