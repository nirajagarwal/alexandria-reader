# 3Com

***The networking pioneer that won too early and lost the next war***

> **Years:** 1979-2010

In 1979, Robert Metcalfe had a problem. He'd invented Ethernet at Xerox PARC — a way to connect computers over local networks using coaxial cable — and watched Xerox fumble the commercialization. The personal computer was arriving. Every company would need networks. And the inventor of the core technology was sitting on the sidelines.

Metcalfe left Xerox and founded 3Com with Howard Charney and others. The name stood for "Computer Communication Compatibility." The pitch was simple: we will connect all these computers that are suddenly appearing on desks. We will make Ethernet the standard. We will build the networking industry.

The timing was perfect. IBM had just launched the PC. Companies were buying them by the dozen. But PCs sitting alone on desks were just expensive typewriters. Connected together, sharing printers and files, they became infrastructure. 3Com sold Ethernet adapter cards that slotted into PCs. By 1984, they were public. By the late 1980s, Ethernet had defeated Token Ring and other competing standards. 3Com had won.

The victory came from strategic patience. Metcalfe made Ethernet an open standard — anyone could build Ethernet products. This seemed counterintuitive. Why not lock it up? But openness meant widespread adoption. It meant Ethernet became the default. And 3Com, with its head start and expertise, could sell premium products into a growing market. They didn't need to own the standard. They needed the standard to win.

Through the 1980s, 3Com expanded. They acquired companies. They built modems, routers, switches — the full stack of networking gear. When companies needed to connect their offices, they called 3Com. The company became synonymous with networking itself.

Then came the hubs. In the early 1990s, 3Com bought several companies making network hubs and switches. These were the devices that sat in server rooms and connected dozens or hundreds of computers. Higher margins than adapter cards. Stickier relationships with IT departments. 3Com's revenue climbed past a billion dollars. They seemed unstoppable.

But they were fighting the last war. A new kind of networking was emerging: enterprise-scale switching and routing, the infrastructure that connected entire corporate campuses and, soon, the internet itself. This required different technology — faster, more sophisticated, more expensive. It required selling to a different buyer: not the PC user or small business, but the CIO of a Fortune 500 company.

Cisco saw this future. They built routers for this new scale. They hired enterprise sales teams. They positioned themselves not as a PC networking company but as the infrastructure provider for the internet age. By the mid-1990s, Cisco was eating 3Com's lunch in the high end.

3Com tried to respond. In 1995, they spun out Palm Computing, the handheld device maker they'd acquired. Palm became a phenomenon — the PalmPilot defined the PDA category. For a moment, 3Com looked visionary. They'd moved beyond networking into computing itself. The stock soared.

But Palm was a distraction from the core problem. 3Com's networking business was being commoditized from below and out-innovated from above. Cheap Asian manufacturers flooded the market with Ethernet cards and hubs. Cisco owned the high-end routing and switching market. 3Com was squeezed in the middle.

In 2000, 3Com spun out Palm to shareholders. It was a tacit admission: the future wasn't in 3Com's networking business. The split left 3Com as a pure networking play just as the dot-com bubble burst. Revenue collapsed. The company that had defined networking in the 1980s was now struggling to matter in the 2000s.

They tried everything. They pivoted to wireless. They tried to build enterprise switches to compete with Cisco. They acquired companies and divested divisions. Nothing stuck. The problem was fundamental: 3Com had won the first networking war so decisively that they couldn't see the second war was different. They kept optimizing for adapter cards and small business hubs while Cisco built the infrastructure of the internet.

There was a brief resurgence. In 2003, 3Com formed a joint venture with Huawei, the Chinese telecom giant. The partnership gave 3Com access to low-cost manufacturing and new products. For a few years, it worked. Revenue stabilized. The company looked like it might survive.

But by 2007, it was clear 3Com was a shell. The brand still had value — nostalgia, installed base, enterprise relationships. But the technology was increasingly irrelevant. Hewlett-Packard tried to buy 3Com for $2.7 billion but the deal collapsed over regulatory concerns about the Huawei connection. In 2009, Huawei sold its stake. A few months later, HP tried again. This time, the deal closed for $2.7 billion — a fraction of 3Com's peak market cap.

HP wanted the enterprise networking assets to compete with Cisco. They rebranded 3Com's products under the HP name. By 2010, the 3Com brand was retired. The company that invented Ethernet, that won the first networking war, that seemed destined to own computer communication for decades — it simply ceased to exist.

What killed 3Com wasn't failure. It was success. They won so completely in the 1980s that they couldn't adapt when the rules changed in the 1990s. They kept selling to small businesses when the real money moved to enterprises. They kept optimizing products when the game became platforms. They kept thinking of networking as a PC problem when it became an internet problem.

Cisco didn't beat 3Com with better technology. They beat them by understanding that the market had shifted. The companies that survived weren't the ones who won the early battles. They were the ones who recognized when the war had changed.
