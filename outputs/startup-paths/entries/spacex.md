# SpaceX

***The improbable bet that private industry could become a spacefaring civilization***

> **Years:** 2002-present

In June 2002, Elon Musk sat in a Moscow hotel room, freshly insulted by Russian rocket engineers who had laughed at his offer to buy refurbished ICBMs. He'd just sold PayPal for $1.5 billion and wanted to send a greenhouse to Mars as a publicity stunt to reignite interest in space exploration. The Russians wanted $8 million per rocket. Musk thought he could build them cheaper. On the flight home, he sketched out the numbers. His spreadsheet said a rocket could cost a tenth of what established players charged. He had no aerospace experience. The entire commercial space industry had failed for fifty years. He decided to start a rocket company anyway.

Space Exploration Technologies Corporation began with an idea that seemed absurd to everyone who understood rockets: make them like software—iterate fast, fail often, get cheaper with each version. The aerospace industry worked the opposite way. Cost-plus contracts, decade-long development cycles, components machined to perfection. Boeing and Lockheed Martin split the military launch market through a joint venture called United Launch Alliance. Each launch cost $400 million. NASA paid Russia $70 million per seat to ferry astronauts to the International Space Station after the Space Shuttle retired. Nobody thought competition would help. Rockets were strategic infrastructure, not products.

Musk hired Tom Mueller, a rocket engineer who'd been building engines in his garage. They set up shop in a warehouse in El Segundo, California, near LAX. Mueller designed the Merlin engine—simpler than the Russian designs, but using the same basic principles. The team was small, mostly young engineers willing to work hundred-hour weeks for equity and the chance to work on spaceships. They moved fast. Big aerospace companies took years to test components; SpaceX tested Merlin engines weekly, blowing up test stands regularly. Every failure taught them something. The culture was pure Silicon Valley—rapid iteration, vertical integration, questioning every inherited assumption.

The first three Falcon 1 launches failed. The first reached 30 seconds before a fuel leak caused a fire. The second made it to space but tumbled during stage separation. The third failed when stages collided. Each failure cost millions Musk couldn't spare. He'd sunk $100 million of his own money. In 2008, SpaceX was nearly bankrupt. Tesla, which Musk also ran, was simultaneously imploding. He had enough money to save one company, not both. Then NASA announced awards for Commercial Orbital Transportation Services—contracts to replace Space Shuttle cargo capability. SpaceX needed the Falcon 1 to work to even compete.

The fourth launch happened in September 2008. Everything held. The rocket reached orbit. SpaceX became the first private company to launch a liquid-fueled rocket to orbit. Two months later, NASA awarded SpaceX a $1.6 billion contract for twelve cargo flights to the ISS. The company survived.

What happened next rewrote the economics of spaceflight. SpaceX introduced the Falcon 9 in 2010—a heavy-lift rocket priced at $60 million per launch versus ULA's $400 million. The price wasn't just aggressive; it was predatory. ULA couldn't compete without restructuring their entire operation. SpaceX won commercial satellite contracts, military launches, NASA missions. By 2015, they had manifests booked years out.

But Musk's real obsession was reusability. Rockets were thrown away after one use—like building a 747, flying it once, then scrapping it. If rockets could land and fly again, launch costs could drop by orders of magnitude. The entire aerospace industry said it was impossible. The mass penalty of landing legs and fuel reserves made the economics implausible. SpaceX tried anyway.

They failed for years. Boosters smashed into drone ships in the Atlantic. They exploded on landing pads. They tipped over. Each failure played out on livestream—spectacular fireballs, then Musk tweeting deadpan assessments of what broke. The attempts seemed reckless to traditional aerospace. Then in December 2015, a Falcon 9 first stage flew to space, separated, flipped around, reignited its engines, and landed vertically at Cape Canaveral. SpaceX proved reusability possible. Within two years, they were routinely landing boosters and reflying them.

The company expanded into internet satellites with Starlink—a constellation of thousands of small satellites providing global broadband. It seemed like distraction from Mars. But Starlink became SpaceX's cash engine. By 2023, it generated billions in revenue, funding Starship development. Meanwhile, SpaceX launched more mass to orbit than every other company and country combined. They undercut competitors so thoroughly that European and Russian launch providers required government subsidies to survive.

Starship—a fully reusable super-heavy lift vehicle—began testing in 2023. Early attempts exploded spectacularly. The FAA delayed launches over environmental reviews. But SpaceX kept building. By late 2024, Starship was reaching orbit. The vehicle could carry 100 tons to low Earth orbit at a fraction of current costs. If it worked, Mars became economically plausible.

SpaceX's success reshaped what was possible. Before SpaceX, private spaceflight meant billionaire tourists on Russian rockets. After SpaceX, private industry launched astronauts, built space stations, planned moon bases. The company proved that commercial incentives could outperform government contractors, that vertical integration beat supply chains, that reusability wasn't impossible.

But the path was contingent. If the fourth Falcon 1 had failed, SpaceX would have died. If NASA hadn't awarded the COTS contract, no amount of vision would have mattered. The company succeeded because Musk bet everything at moments when rational actors would have quit, because the team moved fast enough to learn from failures before money ran out, because they entered a market where incumbents had stopped innovating.

SpaceX remains private, valued at over $200 billion in late 2024. It hasn't reached Mars yet. But it made space accessible. That was the improbable part—not the destination, but the proof that getting there didn't require nations, just obsession, capital, and a willingness to watch rockets explode until they didn't.
