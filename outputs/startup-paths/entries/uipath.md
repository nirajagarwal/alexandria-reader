# UiPath

***The automation empire built from Bucharest***

> **Years:** 2005-present

In 2005, three Romanian engineers founded a company called DeskOver in Bucharest. Daniel Dines, Marius Tîrcă, and a small team began building automation tools for desktop applications — the kind of unsexy enterprise software that processes forms, moves data between systems, and handles repetitive digital tasks. They weren't thinking about venture capital or Silicon Valley. They were building software services for hire, the kind of work that kept the lights on in Eastern European tech hubs.

For years, they operated in obscurity. DeskOver built automation libraries and SDKs that other companies could license. The revenue was modest but steady. The team stayed lean. They sold to enterprises that needed to reduce the tedium of digital work — insurance companies processing claims, banks moving data between legacy systems, healthcare organizations managing patient records. The market existed, but it was fragmented, technical, and unsexy. No one was calling it a revolution.

By 2013, Dines recognized a pattern. Enterprises were asking for the same capabilities over and over: something that could watch what humans did on computers and replicate it. Not just APIs or scripts, but software that could interact with interfaces the way humans did — clicking buttons, reading screens, filling forms. He saw that what they'd been selling piecemeal could become a platform. He renamed the company UiPath and pivoted from services to product.

The timing was perfect, though it wasn't obvious yet. Enterprises were drowning in digital processes that couldn't be fully automated because they crossed too many systems. An insurance claim might touch five different applications, each with its own interface, each requiring human eyes and hands. Traditional enterprise software required expensive integration projects. UiPath offered something different: software robots that could be trained by watching employees work, then deployed to handle the repetitive parts.

They called it Robotic Process Automation — RPA. The term made it sound like industrial robotics for knowledge work, which was exactly the point. UiPath built a visual development environment where business analysts, not just programmers, could design automation workflows. The barrier to entry dropped. The sales cycle shortened. Enterprises that would spend months evaluating enterprise software could deploy a UiPath bot in weeks.

The company stayed in Bucharest, leveraging lower costs and a deep talent pool in a city that had quietly become one of Europe's engineering centers. But Dines knew the customers were elsewhere. In 2015, UiPath opened offices in London and New York. They began selling into the Fortune 500 in earnest.

Revenue grew fast. In 2017, they closed a $30 million Series A, their first institutional funding after twelve years of bootstrapping. Six months later, they raised $153 million. The venture world suddenly noticed. Here was a capital-efficient company with massive revenue growth, strong unit economics, and a market that seemed enormous. Every large enterprise had processes begging for automation.

By 2018, UiPath was the fastest-growing enterprise software company in history by some metrics. ARR went from $1 million to $100 million in under three years. Competitors emerged — Automation Anywhere, Blue Prism — and the RPA category became one of the hottest in enterprise tech. Analysts proclaimed it a revolution. UiPath raised $568 million at a $7 billion valuation, becoming Romania's first decacorn.

The growth came with challenges. The product was powerful but required skill to implement well. Enterprises would deploy dozens or hundreds of bots, then struggle to manage them. Some bots broke when underlying applications changed. The promise of business users building their own automation collided with the reality that complex workflows still required technical expertise. UiPath invested heavily in making the platform more robust, building governance tools, adding AI capabilities for reading documents and understanding unstructured data.

The company also faced the inherent tension in its business model. RPA was often a stepping stone. Enterprises used it to automate processes across legacy systems they couldn't easily replace. But over time, those legacy systems would be replaced or integrated properly. What happened to the RPA layer then? UiPath's answer was to become more than just screen-scraping robots — to add machine learning, process mining, and orchestration, to become the intelligent automation platform that coordinated work across an enterprise, not just mimicked human clicks.

In April 2021, UiPath went public at a $35 billion valuation, one of the largest software IPOs in years. Dines rang the opening bell at the NYSE. The company that had bootstrapped for over a decade, that had started in Bucharest far from the usual startup centers, had become one of the most valuable software companies in the world.

But the public markets were less forgiving than private ones. Revenue growth, while still strong, was slowing. The pandemic had accelerated digital transformation, but it had also pulled forward demand. The competitive landscape was shifting — Microsoft was building automation tools into its own platforms. The question of whether RPA was a permanent layer or a temporary bridge remained unanswered. The stock dropped 40% in the first year, then stabilized as the company demonstrated it could grow sustainably and expand beyond pure RPA.

By 2024, UiPath was still growing, still profitable, still headquartered in New York but with deep roots in Bucharest. The outcome wasn't a fairy tale exit or a spectacular crash. It was something more interesting: proof that you could build a massive enterprise software company far from traditional centers, that bootstrapping discipline could coexist with venture scale, and that the most valuable automation wasn't always the most visible.
