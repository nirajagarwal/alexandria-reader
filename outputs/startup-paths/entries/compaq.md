# Compaq

***The clone that became the original***

> **Years:** 1982-2002

In 1981, three senior managers from Texas Instruments met for lunch at a Houston pie shop. Rod Canion, Jim Harris, and Bill Murto weren't looking to start a revolution. They were looking for a business opportunity in the sudden gold rush around the IBM PC, announced that August. By the time coffee arrived, Canion had sketched a portable computer on a paper placemat. The sketch looked like a sewing machine with a handle. It would become the fastest company to reach $1 billion in sales.

The timing was everything. IBM had done something unprecedented: published the technical specifications of the PC. They assumed the money was in mainframes. The PC was almost an experiment. That openness created a window, and Canion's team saw it immediately. They weren't going to make a cheaper IBM PC clone — dozens of companies would try that and fail. They would make something IBM couldn't: a portable PC that ran all the same software. Take your work home. Take it on a plane. The value proposition was obvious to anyone who'd ever worked late.

They needed a name. Someone suggested "Gateway," but a company already used it. They tried "Compaq" — short for compatibility and compact. It sounded technical. It sounded serious. More importantly, it was available.

The first meeting with venture capitalists in January 1982 was supposed to be a preliminary conversation. Canion brought the placemat sketch and a business plan. Ben Rosen, a former analyst who'd become one of tech's most respected VCs, stopped him midway through the pitch. "How much do you need?" 

Canion had planned to ask for $1.5 million in a first round, maybe $3 million total. Rosen offered $2.5 million immediately. By the end of the week, they had commitments for $30 million. The portable PC market didn't exist yet, but Rosen understood what IBM's openness meant. The question wasn't whether PC clones would succeed, but which ones would win.

Compaq shipped its first portable in March 1983. The machine weighed 28 pounds — "portable" was generous — but it worked. More importantly, it ran every piece of IBM PC software perfectly. This was harder than it looked. IBM's BIOS — the basic input/output system that let software talk to hardware — was copyrighted. Compaq couldn't just copy it. They used "clean room" development: one team analyzed IBM's BIOS and wrote specifications, another team wrote new code from those specs, never seeing the original. The result was legally distinct but functionally identical.

The Compaq Portable sold for $2,995, more than an IBM PC. It didn't matter. First-year sales hit $111 million. The following year: $329 million. The year after: $504 million. Fastest company to reach $100 million. Fastest to $200 million. Fastest to $500 million. They went public in 1983. The stock soared.

But the portable market was a beachhead, not a destination. By 1985, Compaq was making desktop PCs that competed directly with IBM — and winning. They were faster. They used newer Intel chips before IBM did. They had better engineering. When Intel released the 386 chip in 1986, IBM waited. Compaq shipped a 386 machine in 1986. For the first time, a clone beat IBM to market with IBM-compatible technology. The student had become the teacher.

The shift was tectonic. For decades, IBM set standards and others followed. Now Compaq led and IBM scrambled to catch up. The PC market fractured. Dozens of clone makers competed on price, but Compaq competed on innovation. They were more expensive than generic clones but better than IBM. It was a viable middle position — for a while.

By the early 1990s, the market had changed. Dell was selling directly to customers, cutting out distributors and retailers, passing savings to buyers. Compaq was stuck in the traditional retail channel, with traditional margins. Dell's prices kept dropping. Compaq's sales slowed. The board pushed out Rod Canion in 1991. His replacement, Eckhard Pfeiffer, launched a price war. If Dell wanted to compete on cost, Compaq would compete on cost and scale. They bought Tandem Computers in 1997 for $3 billion. They bought Digital Equipment Corporation in 1998 for $9.6 billion.

The acquisitions were supposed to move Compaq beyond PCs into enterprise computing — servers, services, consulting. Instead, they created an unwieldy giant. Too many product lines. Too many overlapping technologies. Too much complexity. The PC business was commoditizing. The enterprise business was getting hammered by Sun, HP, and IBM. Pfeiffer was forced out in 1999.

The dot-com crash devastated demand. Dell kept winning on price. HP, under Carly Fiorina, was hungry for scale. In 2001, HP proposed a merger. The deal was controversial — Walter Hewlett, son of HP co-founder Bill Hewlett, led a public battle against it — but it closed in 2002. Compaq ceased to exist as an independent company. The brand lingered on some HP products for a few years, then disappeared.

Twenty years from paper placemat to Fortune 500 to gone. Compaq proved that speed and execution could beat incumbent giants. Then it proved that beating IBM didn't mean beating the market. The PC became a commodity. The companies that thrived sold on price or pivoted to services. Compaq tried both too late. The same openness that created the opportunity — IBM's published specs — made differentiation impossible. Eventually, every PC was the same. Compaq had been the best at making something that, in the end, couldn't be best at anything.
