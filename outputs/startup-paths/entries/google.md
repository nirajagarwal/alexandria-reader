# Google

***The search engine that became the infrastructure of the internet***

> **Years:** 1998-present

In September 1998, Larry Page and Sergey Brin incorporated Google Inc. in a friend's garage in Menlo Park. They were both PhD students at Stanford, supposed to be writing dissertations. Instead, they were obsessed with a problem: web search was broken.

The web was exploding, and existing search engines — AltaVista, Excite, Yahoo — returned results by counting how many times keywords appeared on a page. You could game it easily. Spam sites stuffed invisible keywords into their code. Search results were increasingly useless. Page and Brin saw this as a graph theory problem. The web was a network of linked pages. Links were endorsements. A page linked to by many important pages was probably important itself. They called their algorithm PageRank, partly after Larry's last name, partly because it ranked pages.

They built a prototype called BackRub on Stanford's servers. It crawled so much of the web it crashed the university's internet connection. Their advisors told them to finish their PhDs. They didn't listen.

What set Google apart initially wasn't just the algorithm. It was the interface. While Yahoo's homepage was cluttered with links and news and ads, Google's was almost empty: a logo, a search box, two buttons. This was intentional design philosophy, but it was also practical — they didn't have enough servers to build a complicated portal. The simplicity became the brand.

They needed money to buy servers. In August 1998, they pitched Andy Bechtolsheim, co-founder of Sun Microsystems, at 8 AM on a Stanford porch. He saw a demo, got back in his car, and wrote them a check for $100,000 on the spot, made out to "Google Inc." — a company that didn't exist yet. They incorporated two weeks later so they could deposit the check.

Early decisions shaped everything that followed. They could have sold ads based on keywords, like Overture did, accepting money from anyone willing to pay to appear in search results. Instead, they separated paid ads from search results, labeled them clearly, and ranked ads partly on relevance, not just payment. Users noticed the difference. Traffic grew exponentially through word of mouth.

By 2000, they were handling 100 million searches per day. The dot-com bubble was collapsing around them, but Google's usage kept climbing. They made money, quietly, through AdWords — the auction system for text ads that would eventually become one of the most profitable business models ever created. Advertisers bid on keywords. Google displayed ads next to search results. Users clicked. Advertisers paid per click, not per impression. The more relevant the ad, the more clicks, the more money. The system incentivized useful ads.

The IPO in 2004 was unconventional. They used a Dutch auction instead of letting investment banks allocate shares to preferred clients. They published a founder's letter that opened: "Google is not a conventional company. We do not intend to become one." They created a dual-class share structure giving Page and Brin voting control. Wall Street was skeptical. The stock opened at $85 and more than doubled within a year.

What followed was expansion into adjacent territory. Gmail in 2004, with a gigabyte of storage when competitors offered megabytes. Google Maps in 2005, which made digital cartography suddenly useful. YouTube, acquired in 2006 for $1.65 billion — expensive at the time, prescient in retrospect. Android, acquired in 2005, released as an open-source mobile OS in 2008, became the world's dominant smartphone platform by giving it away free to manufacturers.

The advertising engine funded everything. By 2007, Google's market cap exceeded $200 billion. By 2010, search was so dominant that "google" became a verb in the Oxford English Dictionary. The company processed over a trillion searches per year.

But dominance created new problems. Antitrust scrutiny began in earnest around 2010. European regulators argued Google favored its own services in search results. The company paid billions in fines and made small adjustments but maintained its core model. Privacy advocates criticized the surveillance business model — free services in exchange for user data and attention. Google's response was incremental change, not transformation.

Internally, the founders tried to preserve startup culture at scale. The "20% time" policy — employees could spend one day a week on side projects — produced Gmail and AdSense but became harder to sustain as headcount reached tens of thousands. Hiring standards remained notoriously rigorous: multiple interviews, algorithmic puzzles, committee review. The result was extraordinary technical talent and a certain cultural insularity.

In 2015, Page and Brin restructured the company under a holding company called Alphabet. Google became a subsidiary focused on core products — search, ads, Android, YouTube. Speculative projects — self-driving cars, life extension research, internet balloons — became separate Alphabet companies. The move made financial sense, clarifying where money was made versus spent, but also signaled the founders' restlessness with being merely a search company.

By 2020, Google handled over 90% of global search queries. It had 135,000 employees. YouTube was the second-largest search engine. Gmail had 1.8 billion users. Android ran on 71% of smartphones. Chrome was the dominant browser. Google Cloud was third in market share behind Amazon and Microsoft but growing. The company generated $182 billion in revenue, mostly from advertising.

The path from garage to dominance took twenty-two years, but the core insight — that link structure reveals authority — emerged in a Stanford dorm room before the company existed. Everything since has been execution, expansion, and the gravitational pull of network effects. The search engine became infrastructure. The advertising model became the economic foundation of the free internet. The company that organized the world's information became one of the most powerful institutions in the world, public or private.
