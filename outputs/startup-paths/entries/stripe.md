# Stripe

***The infrastructure that made internet commerce feel obvious***

> **Years:** 2010–present

In 2010, accepting payments online was still unnecessarily hard. Developers who could build complex web applications found themselves wrestling with decades-old banking systems, filling out paperwork, and integrating clunky APIs that seemed designed to make simple things difficult. Patrick and John Collison, brothers from rural Ireland who had already sold a startup, saw this as absurd. They believed that accepting money on the internet should be as easy as any other API call.

The insight was deceptively simple: make it seven lines of code to accept a payment. No merchant accounts. No navigating procurement processes at banks. No PDF forms. Just drop in Stripe's JavaScript library and you could charge credit cards. The brothers built the first version at a startup incubator in 2010, then got into Y Combinator despite having already started the company. Paul Graham saw what they saw—this was infrastructure that the entire internet economy would eventually need.

The early strategy was surgical. Stripe didn't try to compete with PayPal for eBay sellers or chase big enterprise contracts. They targeted developers building new things on the internet—SaaS companies, marketplaces, apps with subscription models. These were businesses that either didn't exist yet or were just finding product-market fit. Stripe would grow with them. The first users were YC companies and developers in the brothers' network. The product spread through word of mouth among engineers who had suffered through older payment systems.

What looked like a simple API masked enormous complexity beneath. The Collisons weren't just wrapping existing payment processors—they were rebuilding how money moved through the internet from the ground up. They had to navigate banking regulations in multiple countries, manage fraud detection, handle disputes, reconcile transactions, and deal with the arcane rules of card networks. They made supporting new payment methods and expanding to new countries feel trivial to their customers while doing immensely hard work behind the scenes.

The company's timing was nearly perfect. They launched just as the shift to software-as-a-service was accelerating, as mobile apps were exploding, and as a new generation of internet businesses was being born. These companies needed payment infrastructure that could scale with them, that worked globally, that could handle recurring billing and marketplaces and split payments. Stripe built all of it. By making payments invisible, they enabled entirely new business models.

Growth came in waves. First the startups. Then the scale-ups. Then the established companies that rebuilt their payment infrastructure on Stripe's platform. Shopify adopted Stripe. Lyft and Instacart ran on it. Salesforce integrated it. The company expanded from simple payments to the full financial stack—billing, fraud prevention, corporate cards, banking services. Each new product solved some complexity that slowed down internet businesses.

The regulatory gauntlet never ended. Expanding to each new country meant obtaining licenses, partnering with local banks, and navigating different rules about money movement. Stripe moved carefully, launching in countries where they could do it right rather than rushing to claim territory. They became a payments company that thought like a technology company and a technology company that took financial regulation seriously.

By 2020, Stripe was processing hundreds of billions in payments annually. The COVID pandemic accelerated everything—physical retail moved online overnight, and Stripe was the infrastructure powering much of that shift. Companies that had resisted online sales suddenly needed to accept payments on the internet, and Stripe made it possible in days rather than months. The company's valuation reached $95 billion in 2021, making it the most valuable private fintech company in America.

But scale brought scrutiny. Stripe had avoided the reputational disasters that plagued many payment processors, but they weren't immune to the tensions inherent in controlling financial infrastructure. They faced pressure over which businesses they would serve, how they handled disputes, what counted as fraud. Every decision about acceptable use was a political choice dressed as a technical one. The company maintained unusual discipline—they stayed private, avoided the growth-at-all-costs mentality that consumed competitors, and kept investing in infrastructure rather than rushing to IPO.

The competition never stopped arriving. Square moved up from small businesses. PayPal rebuilt its developer tools. Banks finally started trying to modernize. Technology giants launched payment features. Every fintech startup needed a position on whether to compete with Stripe, integrate with them, or avoid payments altogether. Most ended up as Stripe customers. The company had become infrastructure in the same way that AWS was infrastructure—you could build around them, but it was usually easier to build on them.

What the Collisons built wasn't just a successful company—it was a demonstration that deeply technical founders could tackle regulated industries if they were willing to do the unglamorous work. Stripe succeeded because they made the complex simple for their users while handling the complexity themselves. They understood that infrastructure doesn't win by being flashy. It wins by being reliable, well-documented, and so obvious in retrospect that people forget what came before.

The company remains private as of 2024, still controlled by its founders, still expanding into new countries and new products. Whether it stays independent or goes public, whether its valuation holds or adjusts, the work is already done. Stripe changed what was possible to build on the internet by making payments boring. In infrastructure, boring is the highest praise.
