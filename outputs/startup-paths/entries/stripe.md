# Stripe

***The payments infrastructure that made the internet economy possible***

> **Years:** 2010-present

In 2010, the internet was already reshaping commerce, but online payments remained surprisingly broken. Every entrepreneur who wanted to accept credit cards online faced the same kafkaesque ritual: weeks of paperwork, integration with clunky legacy systems, and APIs designed for banks rather than developers. Patrick and John Collison, brothers from rural Ireland who had already sold their first startup, saw this friction as an architectural problem. The internet economy was being bottlenecked by 1990s payment infrastructure.

They started with a radical premise: what if accepting payments online could be as simple as adding seven lines of code? Not a gradual improvement over existing systems, but a complete rethinking of how payments should work for developers building internet businesses. Patrick was 22, John was 20. They had no background in financial services. This turned out to be an advantage.

The first version of Stripe launched in private beta in 2011. The Collisons knew they needed proof that developers actually wanted this, so they did something unusual: they personally integrated Stripe for early users. They would sit with founders, sometimes taking over their keyboards, and implement payment processing for them on the spot. This hands-on approach served two purposes. It demonstrated that their API actually was as simple as promised. And it gave them direct feedback on what developers needed.

The early traction came from a specific type of company: startups building new kinds of internet businesses. These weren't traditional e-commerce sites trying to replicate offline retail. They were marketplaces, SaaS companies, on-demand services — business models that existed only because the internet made them possible. Stripe understood these companies because the Collisons were part of the same world. They knew that a marketplace needed to split payments between buyers and sellers. They knew that subscription businesses needed to handle recurring billing, failed payments, and upgrades. They built for these use cases from the start.

The technical implementation mattered, but the business model mattered more. Traditional payment processors made money from setup fees, monthly minimums, and complex pricing structures that obscured the real cost. Stripe charged a simple percentage: 2.9% plus 30 cents per transaction. No setup fees, no monthly minimums, no gotchas. This aligned their incentives with their customers' growth. When a startup processed more payments, Stripe made more money. The relationship was collaborative rather than extractive.

By 2012, Stripe had raised $20 million from Peter Thiel, Sequoia, and Andreessen Horowitz. The funding validated the thesis, but it also created expectations. Payment processing is a scale business with thin margins. To justify a venture-scale valuation, Stripe needed to become infrastructure for a significant portion of internet commerce. They expanded methodically: adding support for more countries, more currencies, more payment methods. Each expansion required navigating different regulatory regimes, different banking relationships, different local payment preferences.

The competition emerged on two fronts. Incumbent processors like PayPal recognized the threat and tried to modernize their developer offerings. New startups copied Stripe's model — simple APIs, transparent pricing, developer-focused positioning. Stripe's response was to build deeper. They launched Stripe Connect to handle the complexity of marketplace payments. They introduced Stripe Radar for fraud detection. They built billing tools for subscription businesses. Each product addressed a real problem their customers faced as they scaled.

The strategy was infrastructure maximalism. Rather than just processing payments, Stripe aimed to handle every financial operation an internet business might need. They launched Stripe Atlas to help founders incorporate companies and open bank accounts. They built tools for revenue recognition, tax compliance, and financial reporting. They started Stripe Capital to advance money to businesses based on their transaction history. Each new product deepened the integration and increased the switching costs.

By 2020, Stripe was processing hundreds of billions of dollars annually. They powered payments for Amazon, Google, Shopify, and millions of smaller companies. The valuation had reached $95 billion, making it one of the most valuable private companies in the world. The Collisons had remained CEO and President, unusual for founders running a company at this scale. They insisted on maintaining a technical culture even as headcount grew past 7,000 employees.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated everything. As physical retail shut down, businesses scrambled to move online. Stripe's infrastructure became essential. They processed over $640 billion in 2021. The valuation briefly touched $95 billion before contracting to $50 billion in 2023 as venture markets corrected. But the business fundamentals remained strong: revenue growing, losses narrowing, optionality increasing.

The path Stripe took required patience unusual in venture-backed startups. They spent years building capabilities that wouldn't generate immediate revenue. They prioritized infrastructure quality over rapid feature shipping. They expanded internationally at a measured pace, ensuring they understood each market before entering. This patience was possible because the Collisons maintained control and because early investors believed in the long-term thesis.

What made Stripe possible was timing. They arrived at the moment when internet businesses were growing fast enough to justify dedicated infrastructure, but existing solutions were still designed for the previous generation of e-commerce. They made bets that seemed obvious in retrospect but were non-consensus at the time: that developers would be the primary buyers, that emerging business models would matter more than established ones, that financial infrastructure could be built as software rather than inherited from banks.

The company continues to expand its definition of what internet economic infrastructure means. The original seven lines of code that let you accept payments online became the foundation for dozens of products spanning the entire financial stack. Whether Stripe becomes the complete financial operating system for internet businesses or remains primarily a payments company at scale depends on execution over the next decade. But the core insight — that making complex things simple for developers creates enormous value — has already proven out. The internet economy they helped enable is now larger than most national economies.
