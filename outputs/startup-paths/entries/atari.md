# Atari

***The pioneer that invented the industry and then lost it***

> **Years:** 1972-1996 (as independent entity)

---

Nolan Bushnell and Ted Dabney started Atari in 1972 with $500, a name from the Japanese board game Go, and a simple conviction: video games could be more than novelties in university labs. They had already built Computer Space, the first commercial arcade game, for another company. It flopped — too complicated for bar patrons. The lesson stuck. Their next game would be simple enough for anyone.

Pong was that game. A white dot bouncing between two paddles on a black screen. No instructions needed. Bushnell installed the first prototype in a Sunnyvale bar called Andy Capp's Tavern in September 1972. Within days, the machine stopped working. The problem: it was so popular the coin box had overflowed, jamming the mechanism. By 1973, Atari was shipping Pong machines as fast as they could build them. Competitors flooded in with clones, but Atari had won the race to define what an arcade game was. They had created an industry.

The home console followed inevitably. If people paid quarters to play in bars, they would pay hundreds to play at home. Atari released the home Pong console for Christmas 1975 through a Sears exclusive. It sold 150,000 units. The company was growing so fast Bushnell could barely manage it. Money flowed in, but also out — on R&D, manufacturing, ambitious bets. In 1976, needing capital and structure, Bushnell sold Atari to Warner Communications for $28 million.

The sale gave Atari resources to attempt something audacious: a programmable home console. The Atari 2600, released in 1977, didn't ship with a fixed set of games. It played cartridges. New games meant new sales, forever. The business model was brilliant. The execution was troubled. The 2600 launched slow. Games were scarce, graphics crude, price high. For two years it looked like an expensive mistake.

Then Space Invaders arrived. Atari licensed the arcade hit for the 2600 in 1980. Sales exploded. Suddenly every household wanted one. The 2600 became the center of living rooms. Parents who had never touched a joystick found themselves playing. By 1982, Atari was generating $2 billion in revenue, more than the entire film industry's box office. It was the fastest-growing company in American history.

But the foundation was cracking. Warner treated Atari like a content business, greenlighting everything. Quality collapsed under volume. The company released hundreds of games, most forgettable or broken. Retailers stocked shelves with trash alongside treasures. Consumers lost the ability to distinguish. Third-party developers flooded in, churned out shovelware, and eroded trust in the platform. Atari's own games grew sloppy. They rushed E.T. The Extra-Terrestrial to market in five weeks for Christmas 1982. It was almost unplayable. They manufactured more cartridges than consoles in existence. Most ended up in landfills.

Meanwhile, competitors arrived with superior hardware. Coleco and Mattel released consoles that made the 2600 look ancient. Personal computers — Commodore, Apple — offered more power and flexibility. Atari's next-generation machines, the 5200 and later the 7800, launched poorly and late. The 5200's controllers broke constantly. The 7800 sat in warehouses for two years during corporate turmoil.

The crash came in 1983. Retailers, burned by overstock and returns, slashed orders. Atari's revenue fell from $2 billion to $338 million in a year. Warner took a $536 million loss. The company laid off thousands. The press declared video games a fad that had ended. Parents agreed. The unsold inventory — pallets of E.T. cartridges and other failures — was buried in a New Mexico landfill, a literal grave for the industry Atari had created.

Warner split Atari in two in 1984. The home console and computer division was sold to Jack Tramiel, the hard-edged founder of Commodore. The arcade division stayed with Warner briefly before also being sold. Tramiel ran Atari as a lean, almost vindictive operation, slashing costs and pushing new computers — the ST line — that were excellent machines sold into a market that no longer trusted the name. Atari limped through the late 1980s, releasing the Lynx handheld and the Jaguar console, both technically impressive and commercial disasters. By then, Nintendo and Sega owned what Atari had built.

The Jaguar, released in 1993 as the first 64-bit console, was Atari's last stand. It launched with a handful of games and a controller with more buttons than sense. Developers ignored it. Consumers didn't care. In 1996, Atari Corporation merged with JTS, a disk drive manufacturer. The video game business was over. The name would be bought and sold for years by companies hoping nostalgia had value. It did, but only as a memory.

Atari invented the video game industry, proved it could be bigger than Hollywood, and then disintegrated through mismanagement, greed, and failure to adapt. They had built the market and the platform, but treated both as if they would last forever without care. By the time they realized quality mattered, trust was gone. Nintendo learned the lesson — strict licensing, quality control, first-party excellence — and built an empire on Atari's grave.
