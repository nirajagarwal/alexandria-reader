# Spotify

***The pirates who became the music industry***

> **Years:** 2006–present

In 2006, the music industry was at war with the internet. Napster had been crushed. LimeWire and BitTorrent flourished in the shadows. Record labels sued teenagers. Steve Jobs had convinced them to sell songs for 99 cents on iTunes, but piracy still dominated. The industry's strategy was legal threats and technological barriers. It wasn't working.

Daniel Ek saw something else. A Swedish programmer who'd made millions building advertising technology, Ek had grown up pirating music. He understood the appeal: instant access to everything. But he also saw piracy's friction — the hunt for files, the quality issues, the malware. He believed you could beat piracy not by making it illegal, but by making legal music better than pirated music. The insight seemed obvious. Convincing anyone it could work was another matter.

Ek partnered with Martin Lorentzon, another Swedish tech entrepreneur. They started Spotify in Stockholm, far from Silicon Valley and the music industry's power centers. That distance proved useful. The major labels were embattled and desperate. Ek offered them a deal: let him stream their entire catalogs, and he'd pay them a share of revenue — both from ads and subscriptions. The labels had rejected similar proposals before. But Ek had leverage. Piracy in Sweden was so rampant that any revenue seemed better than nothing. In 2008, Spotify launched in Sweden with unprecedented access: virtually all recorded music, instantly, legally.

The product was remarkable. No waiting for downloads. No managing files. Search for any song and it played within seconds. The experience felt magical because it was technically brutal. Spotify deployed peer-to-peer technology — the same infrastructure that powered piracy — to reduce server costs and latency. Users didn't just consume music; their devices helped distribute it. The irony was intentional.

Growth in Sweden was explosive. Within months, piracy dropped measurably. Ek had proven the concept: make legal easier than illegal, and people switch. But Sweden was small. The real prize was global expansion, and the labels weren't ready to give him that. They'd been burned before. They wanted to see sustained revenue. They wanted control. They wanted to hedge their bets by licensing to multiple services. Ek spent years negotiating country-by-country launches. It was slow, frustrating, and necessary. By 2011, Spotify was in seven European countries and finally, the United States.

The US launch came with complications. Free streaming enraged some artists. Thom Yorke called it "the last desperate fart of a dying corpse." Taylor Swift pulled her catalog. The criticism stung, but the economics were real: per-stream payouts were fractions of a cent. Spotify's defense was existential — without streaming, piracy would dominate and artists would get nothing. The labels, now dependent on Spotify's revenue, mostly agreed. The company kept growing.

The growth strategy was relentless user acquisition, funded by venture capital. Spotify raised hundreds of millions while remaining private far longer than typical tech unicorns. The delay was strategic. Going public meant transparency about the uncomfortable truth: Spotify paid roughly 70% of revenue to labels. The margins were terrible. Profitability seemed distant. But the user growth was undeniable. By 2015, Spotify had 75 million users, 20 million paying. By 2018, when it finally went public via direct listing, it had 170 million users, 75 million paying.

The public markets revealed what insiders knew: Spotify was a scale business with structural problems. The labels held leverage because they owned the content. Spotify couldn't differentiate on catalog — everyone had the same songs. It competed on experience, recommendations, and playlists. The algorithmic playlists — Discover Weekly, Release Radar — became cultural phenomena. They worked because Spotify had data no one else had: what millions of people listened to, when, and in what sequence. The company wasn't just streaming music; it was learning taste.

But learning taste didn't solve the margin problem. Spotify tried various strategies. It paid labels less per stream if users found music through Spotify playlists rather than artist pages — a move that infuriated musicians but improved economics. It invested heavily in podcasts, acquiring Gimlet, Anchor, and Parcast, then signing exclusive deals with Joe Rogan and others. Podcasts had better margins because Spotify could sell ads directly and own the content. The strategy was obvious: become less dependent on music labels.

The label problem persisted anyway. Even as Spotify's revenue grew into billions, profitability remained elusive. The company reported quarterly profits occasionally, then losses again. Investors tolerated it because the user growth continued and the vision remained compelling: become the audio platform for everything. Music, podcasts, audiobooks. Global reach, personalized delivery, data-driven curation.

By 2024, Spotify had over 600 million users, more than 200 million paying. It dominated music streaming globally. It had transformed how people consumed music, resurrected the industry's revenue, and made Ek a billionaire. But it still paid most of its revenue to labels. It still fought for margins. The company had won the format war but remained trapped in the economics it had accepted to launch.

The story of Spotify is the story of revolutionary user experience built on unrevolutionary business terms. Ek saw the future correctly: streaming would replace ownership, access would beat piracy, and personalization would define the experience. But seeing the future and controlling it are different things. Spotify needed the labels to exist. That need became permanent. The pirates became the industry, but the industry still owned the treasure.
