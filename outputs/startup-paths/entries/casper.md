# Casper

***The mattress company that made sleep a brand — then discovered that growth and profitability rarely share a bed***

> **Years:** 2014-2023

In early 2014, five friends — Philip Krim, Neil Parikh, T. Luke Sherwin, Jeff Chapin, and Gabe Flateman — sat around a table discussing a problem that seemed both mundane and massive: buying a mattress was terrible. The showroom experience felt predatory. Sales tactics were aggressive. Prices were opaque. The products themselves — with their proprietary names and incomprehensible specifications — seemed designed to prevent comparison shopping. The mattress industry, dominated by manufacturers like Sealy and Tempur-Pedic and retailers like Mattress Firm, had operated the same way for decades.

The friends had different backgrounds — advertising, finance, design — but they shared a conviction that the rise of direct-to-consumer e-commerce could disrupt this industry. The model was already proving itself in other categories. Warby Parker had done it for glasses. Dollar Shave Club for razors. The pattern seemed clear: take a product with inflated retail margins, sell it online, tell a better story, and offer a better price.

Casper launched with a single product: a foam mattress that came compressed in a box small enough to fit in an elevator. The mattress itself was good enough — memory foam over latex over support foam — but the innovation was elsewhere. They offered free shipping, free returns, and a 100-night trial period. They charged $500 for a twin, $950 for a king — far less than comparable mattresses in stores. The risk was entirely on Casper: if customers didn't like it, they could get a full refund and Casper would arrange pickup and disposal.

The first mattresses sold out in 28 minutes.

The press loved the story. A boxed mattress delivered to your door felt like the future. Tech blogs covered Casper like a software startup. The founders appeared on morning shows unboxing mattresses in real-time, watching them expand from compressed bricks to full-size beds. The ritual became a social media phenomenon — customers posted unboxing videos, celebrities mentioned their Casper mattresses, podcasts accepted sponsorship deals.

Casper raised $1.85 million in seed funding in June 2014. By October, they raised $13.1 million more. By 2015, they projected $100 million in revenue. The company was growing faster than Warby Parker or Dollar Shave Club had at similar stages.

They expanded beyond the original mattress, adding pillows, sheets, bed frames, dog beds. They opened physical retail stores — "showrooms," they called them, trying to distance from the mattress stores they'd positioned against — in major cities. They partnered with Target to sell a budget line. They spent heavily on marketing, particularly podcast ads and subway posters featuring playful, sleep-themed copy.

But the economics of mattresses differed from glasses or razors in crucial ways. A mattress is expensive to ship. It's expensive to take back. Unlike razors, there's no recurring revenue — a customer buys once every eight to ten years. Customer acquisition costs were high and rising. Paid social media ads, once cheap, became expensive as Facebook and Instagram matured. Every competitor — and there were suddenly many competitors — was bidding on the same keywords and audiences.

Purple, Leesa, Tuft & Needle, Nectar, Saatva — a dozen direct-to-consumer mattress companies emerged, each with similar products, similar prices, similar marketing, similar trial periods. The ease of entry that made Casper's rise possible enabled a dozen imitators. The category became known as "bed-in-a-box," and the companies became nearly indistinguishable to consumers.

Casper kept raising money. $55 million in 2015. $75 million in 2016. $170 million in 2017. $100 million in 2019. Each round valued the company higher. Target, the retail giant Casper had partnered with, invested. So did Ashton Kutcher, Leonardo DiCaprio, and Tobey Maguire.

Revenue grew — $200 million in 2016, reportedly $600 million by 2019 — but profits didn't follow. The company's losses widened. In 2019, they lost $92 million on $439 million in revenue. The fundamentals were difficult: high shipping costs, high return rates, high marketing costs, low repeat purchase rates, and increasing competition. The mattress industry's fat margins, it turned out, had covered real costs. When you cut prices, delivered for free, and accepted returns at your own expense, the math became brutal.

In February 2020, Casper went public. The company had been preparing for an IPO since 2019, initially hoping for a $1 billion valuation. By filing time, that had dropped to $750 million. On IPO day, the stock opened at $12 per share, valuing the company at $476 million — less than half what they'd raised in private funding.

The market was skeptical. Casper's prospectus revealed mounting losses and a business model that seemed structurally challenged. The company had spent $423 million on advertising in the previous three years. Customer acquisition costs were rising. The average customer was worth less than it cost to acquire them.

Then the pandemic hit. Initially, Casper benefited — stuck at home, consumers invested in their bedrooms. Revenue grew. But the stock didn't recover. By 2021, shares traded below $5. The company laid off staff, closed retail stores, cut marketing spend.

In November 2021, a private equity firm, Durational Capital Management, offered to buy Casper for $6.90 per share — roughly $286 million, barely a third of the IPO valuation, and less than a quarter of the capital raised. The board recommended acceptance. Shareholders approved. In February 2022, Casper went private again.

The company still exists. It still sells mattresses online. It still ships them in boxes. But the story of Casper isn't really about mattresses. It's about the limits of the direct-to-consumer model when applied to products without repeat purchase, when copied by competitors, when venture capital pressures growth over sustainability. Casper convinced investors and consumers that it was a technology company selling mattresses. It was, in the end, a mattress company using technology to sell. That distinction mattered more than anyone wanted to admit.
