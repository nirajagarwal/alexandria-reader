# Casper

***The mattress-in-a-box that rode the DTC wave to a $1.1 billion valuation, then discovered that disruption doesn't guarantee profits***

> **Years:** 2014–present

In 2013, five friends sat in a New York apartment discussing a shared frustration: buying a mattress was terrible. The showrooms felt sleazy, the prices seemed arbitrary, the whole experience designed to confuse rather than inform. Philip Krim, Neil Parikh, T. Luke Sherwin, Jeff Chapin, and Gabe Flateman weren't mattress industry veterans. That was precisely the point.

They launched Casper in April 2014 with a single product: a $950 memory foam mattress that arrived compressed in a box. The pitch was elegant in its simplicity. One mattress, universal comfort, 100-night trial, free returns. No showrooms, no commissioned salespeople, no twelve models with meaningless names. Order online, sleep on it, decide.

The timing proved impeccable. Direct-to-consumer brands were having a moment. Warby Parker had shown that you could disrupt sleepy industries by cutting out middlemen and selling online. Venture capital was flooding into companies with similar playbooks. Casper's founders had studied these models carefully. They knew the formula: take a product that hadn't changed in decades, wrap it in millennial-friendly branding, compress the supply chain, and ride Facebook ads to growth.

The mattress-in-a-box concept wasn't entirely new — smaller companies had tried it — but Casper executed with unprecedented polish. The unboxing became an event. Customers filmed themselves cutting open the vacuum-sealed package, watching the mattress expand like a magic trick. Videos went viral. The company encouraged this, understanding that every shared unboxing was free advertising.

Revenue hit $100 million in less than two years. By 2015, Casper had raised $70 million from investors including Leonardo DiCaprio and Tobey Maguire. The Wall Street Journal called it "the Warby Parker of mattresses." The comparison seemed apt: both sold something traditionally purchased in physical stores, both promised better products at lower prices, both built brands that felt less like corporations and more like lifestyle choices.

But the mattress industry noticed. And unlike eyewear, mattresses had low barriers to entry. Within months, competitors emerged: Leesa, Tuft & Needle, Purple, Nectar, dozens more. Each promised the same basic value proposition — comfort, convenience, fair prices. Casper had created a category, but categories are hard to own.

The company responded by expanding. They added pillows, sheets, dog beds, a $1,200 mattress, a $3,500 mattress. They opened retail stores — the very thing they'd claimed to disrupt. In 2017, they launched a sleep-tracking device. The strategy seemed to be: become a sleep company, not just a mattress company. Own the entire nighttime routine.

Customer acquisition costs, meanwhile, climbed steadily. In the early days, Facebook ads were cheap and effective. By 2018, every DTC brand was competing for the same audience. Casper spent $300 million on marketing that year — nearly as much as its revenue. They were buying growth, and the unit economics were brutal.

The venture capital kept flowing anyway. In 2019, Casper raised $100 million at a $1.1 billion valuation. They filed to go public the following February, and the S-1 revealed the reality behind the growth story. The company had never turned a profit. It had lost $73 million in the first nine months of 2019 alone. Revenue growth was slowing. Customer acquisition costs were rising faster than revenue.

The IPO priced at $12 per share, well below the expected range, valuing the company at $476 million — less than half the previous private valuation. On the first day of trading, shares fell further. Investors weren't buying the story anymore. The math didn't work.

Then COVID-19 hit. In a twist no one predicted, lockdowns boosted mattress sales. People stuck at home decided to upgrade their sleep experience. Casper's revenue grew. But so did every competitor's. The pandemic revealed that Casper wasn't winning because of superior execution or sustainable advantages. It was riding category-wide trends.

Target acquired a minority stake in 2020 and began selling Casper products in stores. This was presented as expansion, but it looked like desperation — giving away margin to a retailer after spending years claiming retail was the problem. By 2021, the stock traded around $4 per share.

In November 2021, a private equity firm acquired Casper for $286 million — roughly a quarter of its peak valuation. The founders exited. Early employees who'd joined for equity found their shares worth a fraction of what they'd been promised. Late-stage investors took significant losses.

The company still exists, still sells mattresses, still has the clean website and the clever branding. But the story Casper told about itself — that you could build a massive, profitable business by disrupting distribution and branding — proved incomplete. They disrupted the buying experience. They built a recognizable brand. They grew fast. But they never solved the fundamental problem: mattresses are commodities, and in a commodity business, sustainable profits require either massive scale or meaningful differentiation. Casper achieved neither.

What they did achieve was proof of concept. They showed that people would buy mattresses online, that the category could be reimagined, that the old players were vulnerable. Every competitor that flooded in after them owed their existence to Casper's validation. They built the road everyone else drove on. That may matter more, in the end, than whether Casper itself survived the journey profitably.
