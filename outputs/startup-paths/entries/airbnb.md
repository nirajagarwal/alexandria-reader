# Airbnb

***Three air mattresses, eight years of near-death, and the reinvention of hospitality***

> **Years:** 2007-present

In October 2007, Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia couldn't make rent on their San Francisco apartment. A design conference was coming to town, hotels were sold out, and they had an idea: inflate three air mattresses in their living room and offer attendees a place to crash with breakfast included. They called it "Air Bed & Breakfast." Three people paid $80 each to sleep on their floor.

It was a stunt born of desperation, not vision. But when they tried it again for the 2008 South by Southwest conference in Austin, something clicked. Not enough to build a business — they got two bookings — but enough to suggest people might actually do this. Chesky brought in his former roommate Nathan Blecharczyk to build a proper website. They launched officially in August 2008, timing their debut to the Democratic National Convention in Denver when hotels were overbooked.

The timing was catastrophic. Within weeks, Lehman Brothers collapsed. The economy cratered. Nobody wanted to travel, much less stay in a stranger's home. By late 2008, Airbnb was generating $200 in weekly revenue. They had credit card debt and no investors. The idea seemed ridiculous: convince people to let strangers sleep in their homes, and convince strangers to do it. Both sides of that equation violated basic instincts about safety and privacy.

They tried everything. Chesky and Gebbia designed collectible cereal boxes — "Obama O's" and "Cap'n McCain's" — and sold them for $40 each during the Democratic and Republican conventions. They made $30,000, enough to stay alive. Paul Graham heard about the cereal stunt and accepted them into Y Combinator not because the idea seemed promising but because they were "cockroaches that would not die." Graham told them what nobody wanted to hear: go to New York, photograph the apartments yourself, meet your users.

In early 2009, they flew to New York with a professional camera. They went door to door, photographing listings. The listings with professional photos got two to three times the bookings. But more importantly, they met their hosts. They learned that hosts weren't running businesses — they were regular people trying to make rent, meet interesting travelers, or monetize empty space. The insight shifted everything. This wasn't a hotel alternative. It was a new economy.

Still, growth was slow. Investors passed. The idea was too weird, the market too small, the trust problem too fundamental. They raised a small seed round in April 2009 at a $1.3 million valuation. By mid-2010, they had expanded internationally but remained tiny — a rounding error in the travel industry. Then they started growing in unexpected markets: SXSW worked, but so did far-flung locations without hotel infrastructure. They weren't just cheaper — they were offering something hotels couldn't: authentic local experience, unique spaces, human connection.

The trust problem they obsessed over. They built verification systems, reviews, insurance policies. In 2011, disaster struck: a host's apartment was ransacked by a guest. The story went viral. Airbnb's early response was clumsy, prioritizing PR over support. Chesky took personal control, issued a public apology, and announced a $50,000 host guarantee (later raised to $1 million). The crisis forced them to professionalize their trust and safety systems years earlier than planned.

They raised a $112 million Series B in July 2011 at a $1.3 billion valuation. Growth accelerated. By 2012, they had surpassed 10 million nights booked. The network effects kicked in: more hosts meant more inventory, which meant more guests, which meant more hosts. They expanded beyond apartments into unique properties — castles, treehouses, yurts — creating inventory hotels could never match.

But growth brought conflict. Cities accused them of enabling illegal hotels and driving up rents. New York, San Francisco, and Barcelona passed laws restricting short-term rentals. Airbnb fought back, sometimes clumsily. They funded opposition campaigns, framed themselves as champions of the middle class, and argued that most hosts were regular people renting out their own homes — even as professional operators accumulated multiple properties on the platform.

The company culture was intensely mission-driven, sometimes cult-like. Chesky studied Disney, Pixar, and Apple. He rewrote the company values multiple times. Employees were expected to embody "belonging" — the idea that Airbnb wasn't a booking platform but a movement to make anyone feel at home anywhere. The idealism was genuine but could clash with the brutal math of a hypergrowth startup.

By 2017, Airbnb was generating over $2.6 billion in revenue and was valued at $31 billion. They had expanded into "Experiences" — locally hosted activities — trying to own more of the travel stack. They built tools for professional property managers while insisting they were a community marketplace. The contradictions multiplied but so did the business.

Then COVID-19 hit. Travel collapsed overnight. Bookings fell 80%. Airbnb laid off 25% of staff — 1,900 people — in May 2020. Chesky's memo was widely praised for its empathy and transparency, but the company was in crisis. The IPO they'd been preparing for seemed dead.

Except the business adapted faster than hotels. As lockdowns eased, people wanted to travel but avoid crowds. They drove to nearby rural properties instead of flying to cities. They stayed longer, working remotely from cabins and beach houses. Airbnb's network included exactly the inventory people suddenly wanted. By Q3 2020, the business had recovered to pre-pandemic levels.

They went public in December 2020 at a $47 billion valuation. The stock doubled on the first day, valuing the company at over $100 billion. The three founders who couldn't make rent in 2007 were each worth over $10 billion.

As of 2024, Airbnb facilitates over 6 million active listings across 100,000 cities. It has normalized what once seemed crazy: sleeping in strangers' homes, letting strangers into yours. It reshaped urban housing markets, forced cities to rewrite regulations, and changed how people travel. The air mattresses are gone. The contingency remains. If Chesky and Gebbia had made rent in October 2007, none of this would exist.
