# Hudson's Bay Company

***The fur trade monopoly that became a department store empire***

> **Years:** 1670-present

In 1668, two French traders arrived in London with a proposal. Pierre-Esprit Radisson and Médard des Groseilliers had been rebuffed by French authorities in Quebec, so they brought their plan to the English: bypass the St. Lawrence River entirely, sail into Hudson Bay, and trade directly with Indigenous peoples for furs. Prince Rupert, cousin to King Charles II, saw the opportunity. Two years later, the King granted a charter to "The Governor and Company of Adventurers of England trading into Hudson's Bay." The company received not just a monopoly on trade, but sovereignty over an area comprising roughly one-third of modern Canada — 1.5 million square miles called Rupert's Land.

The business model was elegant. Ships sailed from England each summer, anchored in Hudson Bay, and established trading posts along the coast. Indigenous trappers brought beaver pelts and other furs to these posts. The company traded manufactured goods — guns, metal tools, blankets, alcohol — for furs. Ships returned to London before winter ice made the bay impassable. The furs fed European fashion's insatiable demand for felt hats made from beaver fur.

For a century and a half, Hudson's Bay Company barely moved inland. Why take the risk? Indigenous middlemen brought furs to the coastal posts. The company operated with skeleton crews — a handful of English factors overwintering at each post. Overhead was minimal. Returns were steady. The company paid dividends in most years and became a reliable, if unspectacular, investment for English gentry.

Competition changed everything. The North West Company, formed by Scottish and French-Canadian traders in Montreal, refused to wait at the coast. They pushed inland, building posts directly in trapping territories. They intercepted furs before Indigenous traders could reach Hudson Bay. By 1800, the rivalry had turned violent. The companies armed their men. Skirmishes broke out. Both sides recruited Indigenous allies. Costs spiraled as they built competing posts mere miles apart, each trying to outbid the other for furs.

The violence peaked at Seven Oaks in 1816. Twenty-one settlers and Hudson's Bay Company employees died in a confrontation with North West Company traders and their Métis allies. The British government finally intervened. In 1821, the companies merged under the Hudson's Bay Company name, creating a monopoly even more powerful than before. The combined entity controlled fur trading across virtually all of British North America.

This is when Hudson's Bay Company became something stranger than a mere trading firm — it was a proto-state. Company officers enforced law, conducted diplomacy with Indigenous nations, explored and mapped the territory, and governed a domain larger than most European countries. George Simpson, appointed governor in 1826, ran the operation like an autocrat, personally inspecting posts from Labrador to the Pacific, cutting costs, and maximizing extraction.

But the fur trade was dying. By the 1850s, beaver populations had crashed from overtrapping. Silk replaced beaver felt in European fashion. The company's profit center was evaporating. Meanwhile, British Columbia and Canadian politicians looked covetously at Rupert's Land. Settlers wanted farmland. Railways needed routes west.

In 1869, Hudson's Bay Company sold Rupert's Land to the new Dominion of Canada for £300,000 and one-twentieth of the fertile land in the territory. It was a staggering transaction — a private company selling sovereignty over one-third of a continent. The company retained its trading posts and pivoted. If settlers were coming west, they would need supplies.

Through the late 1800s and early 1900s, Hudson's Bay Company transformed its trading posts into retail stores. The first department store opened in Winnipeg in 1881. More followed in Vancouver, Victoria, Edmonton, Calgary. The company that once traded blankets for beaver pelts now sold those blankets to middle-class shoppers. It became a department store chain, eventually Canada's largest.

The department store era brought new competition. Eaton's, Simpson's, and eventually American chains like Sears entered Canadian retail. Hudson's Bay Company competed on heritage and real estate — it owned prime downtown properties in major cities. But by the 1970s, department stores themselves were becoming obsolete. Suburban malls and big-box retailers ate market share. The company diversified desperately: oil and gas, office supplies, even a failed venture into home improvement retail.

Ownership churned. In 2006, American businessman Jerry Zucker bought the company for $1.1 billion. After his death, it went to NRDC Equity Partners in 2008. The company went public again in 2012 under Richard Baker, who saw value in the real estate portfolio. Hudson's Bay acquired Saks Fifth Avenue, Lord & Taylor, and attempted a revival through luxury positioning.

It didn't work. Online retail gutted department stores. The company closed Lord & Taylor. It took Hudson's Bay private again in 2020, as the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the retail apocalypse. Stores closed. The company restructured debt. By 2023, it operated fewer than 100 stores, a shadow of its former footprint, surviving on residual brand equity and valuable urban real estate.

The Hudson's Bay Company still exists. It is 354 years old, one of the oldest continuously operating companies in North America. But the company that once governed a third of a continent now manages the decline of department stores in an e-commerce age. The furs are gone. The monopoly is gone. What remains is a brand, some real estate, and the inertia of an institution that outlived the world that created it.
