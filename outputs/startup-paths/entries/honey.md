# Honey

***The coupon that became a data empire***

> **Years:** 2012-2020

In 2012, Ryan Hudson was sitting in a coffee shop in Los Angeles when he noticed something peculiar about how people shopped online. They would find something they wanted to buy, then open a new tab and search "promo code" plus the retailer's name. Sometimes they'd find a working code. Usually they wouldn't. Either way, they'd waste five minutes hunting through forums and sketchy coupon sites, most of which existed solely to harvest clicks for affiliate commissions.

Hudson had sold his previous company, Flood.com, to AOL. He had money, time, and a programmer's instinct for automating tedious tasks. The idea was absurdly simple: a browser extension that automatically tried every known coupon code at checkout. Users wouldn't have to hunt. They'd just click a button and watch Honey test codes until it found one that worked.

His co-founder, George Ruan, built the first version in a few weeks. They launched with basic functionality: when you reached a checkout page, Honey's button would appear. Click it, and the extension would cycle through available codes, applying the best one automatically. If no codes worked, you lost nothing but a few seconds.

The early growth came from college students on Reddit. Someone would post "holy shit, this thing actually saved me $20" and thousands would install it. The product was too useful not to spread. Within months, Honey had hundreds of thousands of users. Within a year, millions.

The business model was equally simple: affiliate commissions. Retailers pay a percentage when someone buys through an affiliate link. Honey collected that commission whenever a user made a purchase with a code Honey found. Users saved money. Retailers got sales they might have lost to abandoned carts. Honey took a cut of the transaction.

But Hudson saw something bigger than coupon arbitrage. Every time someone used Honey, the extension learned. It learned which codes worked, when they worked, which retailers had codes at all. It learned shopping patterns—what people bought, when they bought, how much they spent, where they abandoned carts. This data was extraordinarily valuable, and Honey was collecting it from millions of transactions across thousands of retailers.

By 2015, Honey had raised real venture capital and was adding features. The extension didn't just find codes anymore. It tracked prices across the web and alerted users when prices dropped. It created wishlists. It offered Honey Gold, a rewards program that gave users points for shopping through Honey, redeemable for gift cards. Each feature deepened engagement and generated more data.

The affiliate model was working, but the real value was in what Honey knew about e-commerce. They had visibility into shopping behavior that even Amazon didn't have—because Honey sat on top of the entire web. They knew when Target's codes performed better than Walmart's. They knew which products people comparison-shopped for and which they bought impulsively. They knew price sensitivity across categories.

Retailers who initially viewed Honey as a minor affiliate partner began to see it as essential infrastructure. Honey could tell them things about their own customers they didn't know. The relationship shifted. Honey wasn't just driving incremental sales. It was providing market intelligence.

By 2018, Honey had 10 million users and had saved them a collective $1 billion in discounts. The press loved the story: scrappy extension helps people save money, nothing sinister, everyone wins. But inside the industry, everyone knew Honey was sitting on a gold mine of transaction data. The question wasn't whether Honey would be acquired, but by whom and for how much.

PayPal made the move in 2019. They paid $4 billion—the largest acquisition in PayPal's history. To outsiders, it seemed like an absurd price for a coupon extension. Four billion dollars for software that finds promo codes?

But PayPal wasn't buying a coupon finder. They were buying the largest dataset of shopping intent outside of Amazon and Google. Honey had 17 million users by then, and those users were revealing their purchase intentions before they bought. PayPal could integrate Honey's data with its payment network and offer retailers predictive analytics about shopping behavior, price optimization, and conversion patterns that nobody else could match.

The acquisition also solved a strategic problem for PayPal. They had tried and failed to build meaningful consumer loyalty. People used PayPal to check out, but they didn't love PayPal. Honey was beloved. Users trusted it. PayPal could use Honey to rebuild consumer relationships outside the sterile confines of payment processing.

Hudson and Ruan became extraordinarily wealthy. Early employees made millions. The VC firms that bet on a coupon extension at a time when coupons seemed like a dying relic of the pre-internet age looked prescient.

What Honey understood—what made it worth $4 billion—was that the value wasn't in the coupons. Coupons were the bait. The value was in being present at the moment of purchase decision across the entire internet, for millions of people, every single day. That presence generated data. The data revealed patterns. The patterns were worth more than any individual transaction.

Honey's path reveals a recurring pattern in consumer internet businesses: the product users see is often not the product that gets acquired. Users thought they were using a coupon finder. PayPal bought a data collection engine with unprecedented visibility into e-commerce behavior. Both things were true. The genius was building the second while appearing to build only the first.
