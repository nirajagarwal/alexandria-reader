# Nokia

***The giant that invented the smartphone and then forgot how***

> **Years:** 1865-2013 (as independent mobile phone company)

Nokia began as a paper mill in Finland in 1865, became a rubber boot manufacturer, then a cable maker, and by the 1990s had transformed itself into the world's dominant mobile phone company. This ability to reinvent — to see when one business was dying and pivot to the next — had sustained Nokia for over a century. Then, suddenly, it couldn't do it anymore.

In 1996, Nokia became the world's largest mobile phone manufacturer. By 2000, it held 40% of global market share. The Nokia 3310, released that year, became iconic — indestructible, reliable, everywhere. Nokia phones were status symbols in Finland, workhorses in India, fashion statements in Europe. The company printed money. Its market capitalization peaked at $303 billion in 2000. For a brief period, Nokia represented more than half of the Helsinki stock exchange's total value.

Nokia understood that phones would become computers. In 1996, they released the Nokia 9000 Communicator — a phone with a full keyboard and email. In 2001, they partnered with Psion and others to create Symbian, an operating system for smartphones. By 2007, Symbian powered the majority of the world's smartphones. Nokia had arrived at the future first.

Then Steve Jobs walked onto a stage in January 2007 and held up an iPhone.

Nokia executives watched and weren't particularly worried. The iPhone was expensive, worked only on AT&T, and had no physical keyboard. Nokia's research showed customers wanted keyboards. Nokia had been building phones for twenty years; Apple had built exactly zero. The iPhone was a beautiful toy for rich Americans. Nokia's N95, released the same year, had more features — better camera, GPS, removable battery, expandable storage. In the markets that mattered — Europe, Asia, the developing world — Nokia was untouchable.

But something had shifted. The iPhone wasn't just a phone with more features. It was a different understanding of what a phone should be. Apple had rebuilt the entire experience from the ground up, starting with multitouch. Nokia had accumulated two decades of assumptions about how phones worked. Apple had none of those assumptions.

Inside Nokia, the problem was visible earlier than the market realized. Symbian had become a labyrinth. Multiple teams worked on multiple versions. The codebase was massive, tangled, nearly impossible to modify quickly. Building a simple feature took months. Testing took longer. Symbian had been designed when phones had tiny screens and limited memory; retrofitting it for the touchscreen era was like rebuilding a ship while at sea.

In 2008, Google released Android. Now Nokia faced threats from both sides — Apple at the high end, Android manufacturers at every other price point. Nokia's smartphone share began eroding. The company tried to respond. They released touchscreen Symbian phones. They started work on MeeGo, a new Linux-based operating system developed with Intel. They acquired mapping company Navteq for $8.1 billion. They reorganized. They reorganized again.

In September 2010, Stephen Elop became CEO — the first non-Finn to lead Nokia. He came from Microsoft, where he had run the Business Division. Three months in, he sent a memo to all Nokia employees. It became known as the "burning platform" memo. Nokia was standing on a burning oil platform, he wrote. The seas below were cold and dark, but jumping was the only way to survive. Symbian was the burning platform. The company had to jump.

In February 2011, Nokia announced a partnership with Microsoft. Nokia would adopt Windows Phone as its primary smartphone platform. Symbian would be phased out. MeeGo would be abandoned after shipping one device. Nokia was betting everything on an operating system that had less than 2% market share and came from a company with no mobile credibility.

The announcement devastated employee morale. Nokia had spent years building Symbian, and many believed MeeGo showed promise. Now both were dead. Sales of Symbian phones collapsed immediately — who would buy a phone the manufacturer had declared obsolete? The first Windows Phone wouldn't ship until November. Nokia had created a nine-month gap with no competitive product.

When the Lumia 800 finally launched, it was beautiful. Windows Phone's interface was genuinely innovative — live tiles, fluid animations, excellent typography. Reviews were positive. But the app ecosystem was barren. Developers were building for iOS and Android. Every popular app arrived on Windows Phone late, or never. Consumers didn't care how elegant the interface was if Instagram wasn't there.

Nokia kept trying. They built excellent hardware. They added exclusive features — better cameras, wireless charging. They spent enormous sums on marketing. Nothing changed the trajectory. By 2013, Nokia's smartphone share had fallen to 3%. The company was burning through cash. The stock traded at a fraction of its peak value.

In September 2013, Microsoft bought Nokia's phone business for $7.2 billion — about 2% of Nokia's market value at its peak. The deal closed in April 2014. Microsoft laid off thousands of Nokia employees, wrote off most of the acquisition's value within two years, and exited the phone business in 2016. The Nokia that had dominated mobile phones for fifteen years was gone.

The company that remained — Nokia Networks, the infrastructure business — survived and eventually thrived. But the path from world dominance to irrelevance took just six years. Nokia had invented the smartphone era and somehow missed it. They had been early and then, fatally, late. They had the talent, the resources, the distribution, the brand. They had everything except the ability to see that their advantages had become anchors.
