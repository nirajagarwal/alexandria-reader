# Shopify

***The snowboard shop that became the infrastructure of commerce***

> **Years:** 2004-present

In 2004, Tobias Lütke wanted to sell snowboards online. He was a programmer from Germany living in Ottawa, frustrated by how terrible the available e-commerce software was. The existing platforms—clunky, expensive, designed for enterprises—made simple tasks complicated. Lütke decided to build his own. The snowboard shop, Snowdevil, never became much of a business. The software he built to run it became Shopify.

Lütke's co-founders were Scott Lake, a college friend, and Daniel Weinand, who had worked with Lütke in Germany. They weren't trying to build a platform. They were trying to solve a problem they had themselves. The software was written in Ruby on Rails—a framework so new it barely had documentation. This choice would matter later. Rails made rapid development possible. It also attracted a certain kind of developer, the kind who valued craft and disliked bloat.

They launched Shopify in 2006 as a hosted platform. You paid a monthly fee, set up your store, and started selling. No servers to manage, no software to install. This was obvious in retrospect but radical at the time. E-commerce platforms were complicated beasts that required IT departments. Shopify was for people who just wanted to sell things.

The early growth was slow and organic. Small merchants, artists, makers—people who couldn't afford Magento or didn't want to deal with osCommerce. The platform was simple but extensible. Developers could build apps and themes. This wasn't planned as a strategic moat. It emerged from Lütke's engineering philosophy: build the core well, let others extend it.

The first real money came from venture capital in 2010—a $7 million Series A. By then, Shopify had tens of thousands of merchants. The business model was elegant: subscription revenue plus a cut of payment processing. The more merchants sold, the more Shopify made. Incentives aligned.

The competition in the early years was fragmented. BigCommerce, Volusion, Magento—each had a niche. Amazon was the giant, but Amazon didn't let you own your brand or your customer. Shopify did. This distinction—merchant empowerment versus platform dominance—became the core of Shopify's identity. They positioned themselves as the anti-Amazon, the infrastructure for independent commerce.

The real inflection came around 2013-2014. Mobile commerce was exploding. Shopify had invested heavily in mobile checkout, making it smooth when most platforms treated mobile as an afterthought. They also launched the Shopify App Store, turning the platform into an ecosystem. Developers built tools for email marketing, inventory management, shipping, analytics. Merchants could compose their own stack. Shopify was becoming infrastructure.

The IPO in 2015 raised $131 million at a $1.3 billion valuation. Lütke remained CEO—unusual for a founder at that scale, but Lütke was unusual. He was technical, product-focused, uninterested in the performance of being a tech CEO. He wrote code. He thought in systems. Shopify's culture reflected this: engineering-driven, deliberate, allergic to bullshit.

The platform kept expanding. Shopify Plus launched for enterprise merchants. Shopify Capital offered loans to merchants. Shopify Shipping negotiated rates with carriers. Shopify POS brought the platform into physical retail. Each move deepened the moat. Switching costs grew not through lock-in but through integration. A merchant using ten Shopify services had no reason to leave.

The pandemic was an accelerant. Physical retail collapsed overnight. Every business needed to sell online immediately. Shopify's sign-ups surged. Revenue doubled. The company's market cap briefly exceeded $150 billion, making it more valuable than any traditional retailer. Lütke became one of the richest people in Canada.

But the pandemic boom was also a trap. Shopify hired aggressively—headcount ballooned. They expanded into logistics, building fulfillment centers to compete directly with Amazon. This was a departure from their core model. Shopify had always been software. Fulfillment was physical, capital-intensive, a different business entirely.

The pullback was brutal. In 2022, as e-commerce growth normalized, Shopify laid off 10% of its workforce. Lütke took responsibility in a memo that was uncommonly direct: they had bet the pandemic surge would continue and been wrong. In 2023, they sold the logistics business to Flexport, admitting the mistake. The market cap had fallen by more than half from its peak.

Yet the core business remained. By 2024, Shopify powered over two million merchants, processing hundreds of billions in gross merchandise volume. It was the infrastructure of independent commerce—not the biggest platform, but the one that let anyone compete. The snowboard shop had become the picks and shovels of the internet economy.

The path Shopify took was unusual. They started from a real need, built software that solved it, and expanded concentrically. They avoided the temptation to become Amazon. When they tried—logistics, fulfillment—they retreated. The discipline to stay close to their core was rare. So was the founder who stayed technical, stayed deliberate, stayed CEO.

The outcome is still being written, but the pattern is visible: infrastructure wins by enabling others. Shopify didn't need to be the biggest merchant. They needed to be essential to all the merchants. That distinction—between being the store and being the system that powers stores—was the entire strategy.
