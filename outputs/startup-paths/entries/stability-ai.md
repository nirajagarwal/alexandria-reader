# Stability AI

***The open-source insurgent that couldn't stay stable***

> **Years:** 2019-present

Emad Mostaque didn't look like a typical AI founder when he took control of Stability AI in 2020. He was a hedge fund analyst turned entrepreneur with a philosophy degree from Oxford, more interested in democratizing technology than hoarding it behind API gates. The company had been founded in 2019 as a collective working on open-source machine learning, but Mostaque saw something bigger: a chance to challenge the emerging AI oligarchy before it consolidated power.

His thesis was simple and radical. While OpenAI, Google, and others were building increasingly closed systems, Stability would release powerful AI models freely. Anyone could download them, modify them, run them locally. No usage fees, no content filters controlled by distant corporations, no gatekeepers deciding which use cases were acceptable. In a world racing toward AI as a service, Stability would offer AI as a tool.

The vehicle for this vision arrived in August 2022: Stable Diffusion. The image generation model wasn't the first of its kind — DALL-E 2 and Midjourney existed — but it was the first anyone could truly own. Within weeks of release, millions had downloaded it. Artists used it. Researchers built on it. Pornographers and propagandists exploited it. This was precisely Mostaque's point: technology shouldn't come with a corporate morality layer.

The market responded enthusiastically to the vision if not yet the business model. Stability raised $101 million in October 2022 at a $1 billion valuation. Coatue and Lightspeed led. The pitch was compelling: become the Red Hat of AI, offering open models with commercial support and services around them. Let others charge per token while Stability built the infrastructure layer everyone needed.

But creating revolutionary technology and building a sustainable company are different problems. Stable Diffusion was generating more controversy than revenue. Artists claimed it was trained on their work without permission, filing class-action lawsuits. Getty Images sued for copyright infringement over its training data. Meanwhile, the model was free — thousands ran it on their own hardware, paying Stability nothing.

The financial reality was brutal. Training large models cost tens of millions in compute. Stability was burning roughly $8 million monthly by mid-2023 while generating minimal revenue. The team had grown to over 200 people across multiple continents, an unfocused sprawl working on audio models, video generation, language models, and various other projects. There was innovation but no coherence.

Mostaque's leadership style accelerated the chaos. He was a visionary who inspired loyalty but struggled with execution. He made grand promises about capabilities and timelines that engineering teams couldn't meet. He positioned himself as the face of open-source AI in countless interviews and podcasts while competitors quietly built businesses. Former employees would later describe a company where strategy shifted constantly, where resources were scattered across too many initiatives, where the CEO's priorities seemed more philosophical than practical.

By early 2024, the finances were catastrophic. Stability had burned through its funding and needed more. But the fundamentals looked terrible to investors. Despite Stable Diffusion's massive adoption, monetization remained elusive. Competition had intensified — Meta released its own open-source models, Midjourney dominated the commercial image generation market, and well-funded players were entering every category Stability touched.

In March 2024, Mostaque resigned as CEO. The stated reason was to pursue decentralized AI. The reality was investor pressure and a board that had lost confidence. He'd failed to build a business commensurate with the technology's impact. Stable Diffusion had been downloaded over 100 million times, influencing an entire generation of AI development, yet the company was nearly insolvent.

The new leadership, including former Weta Digital CTO Prem Akkaraju as CEO, pivoted toward enterprise deals and commercial licensing. They cut staff, focused resources, and tried to build actual revenue streams around the technology. But the window might have closed. Open-source alternatives existed for nearly everything Stability offered. The company that had democratized AI generation now had to compete in markets it had helped create but never dominated commercially.

The lawsuits continued. In July 2024, a consortium of music publishers sued over alleged copyright infringement in training data for audio models. The legal bills mounted while revenue stayed modest. By late 2024, Stability had raised additional funding but on terms suggesting desperation rather than strength. Valuation details remained undisclosed, generally a bad sign.

Stability AI had changed the AI landscape. By proving that powerful generative models could be open-sourced, it forced every major player to reconsider their strategies. Meta's Llama models exist partly because Stability demonstrated the viability of open distribution. The creative tools landscape was entirely reshaped by what Stability released.

But impact isn't sustainability. The company that championed accessible AI couldn't make the model accessible and profitable simultaneously. Mostaque's vision was correct about where technology should go and wrong about how companies get there. You can build in public or build a business, but doing both simultaneously requires execution discipline Stability never achieved. The revolution they sparked would be monetized by others — some who kept their models closed, others who open-sourced with clearer business models.

By 2025, Stability existed in an awkward middle space: too important to ignore, too troubled to bet on, too chaotic to stabilize. The name had become unintentionally ironic — a company built on the idea of democratizing AI, destabilized by the economics of doing so.
