# DeepMind

***The lab that taught machines to think, then sold itself to survive***

> **Years:** 2010-present

In 2010, Demis Hassabis returned to an old obsession. He had been a chess prodigy at four, a video game designer at seventeen, and a neuroscientist by his late twenties. Now he wanted to solve intelligence itself — not human intelligence, but the general problem of learning and reasoning. He called the company DeepMind.

Hassabis recruited Shane Legg, who had studied machine learning and artificial general intelligence, and Mustafa Suleyman, a friend from university who had founded a telephone counseling service for young people. The founding team was unusual: a neuroscientist-entrepreneur, a theorist, and a social entrepreneur. They shared a conviction that artificial intelligence could be built not through hand-coded rules but through learning — algorithms that improved themselves through experience.

The pitch was audacious. They would build artificial general intelligence. Not narrow systems that played chess or recognized faces, but something closer to the flexibility of human cognition. Investors thought they were insane or visionary, sometimes both. But Hassabis had credibility. He understood both neuroscience and machine learning. He could articulate a path from narrow systems to something broader.

Peter Thiel's Founders Fund invested first. Then Elon Musk, who had not yet soured on AI research. Horizon Ventures, the investment arm of Hong Kong billionaire Li Ka-shing, put in money. By 2012, DeepMind had raised enough to hire researchers and rent servers. They were not building a product. They were building a research lab that happened to be a company.

The early work focused on reinforcement learning — teaching algorithms to play Atari games by trial and error, with no knowledge of the rules. The system learned to maximize score through repeated attempts. It learned Breakout, Space Invaders, Pong. By 2013, it was beating human experts on dozens of games. The approach was general. The same algorithm learned different games without modification. That generality was the point.

In 2014, Facebook tried to buy DeepMind. Google entered the bidding. The auction became intense. DeepMind was not profitable. It had no customers. But it had talent and momentum, and both Facebook and Google knew that AI would define the next phase of computing. Google won with an offer of $500 million — later reported as closer to $650 million. At the time, it was one of the largest AI acquisitions ever.

The sale was not inevitable. Hassabis had raised enough capital to remain independent. But staying independent meant becoming a product company or raising larger and larger rounds. Building AGI required compute — massive amounts of it — and access to data. Google had both. The acquisition gave DeepMind the resources to pursue long-term research without quarterly revenue pressures. But it also meant surrendering autonomy.

Google gave DeepMind unusual freedom. It remained a separate entity, based in London, with its own culture and governance. Hassabis stayed in charge. The researchers kept publishing openly, unusual for a corporate lab. DeepMind established an ethics board to oversee how its work would be deployed. Google agreed not to use DeepMind's technology for weapons or surveillance without consent.

In 2016, DeepMind unveiled AlphaGo, a system that played the ancient board game Go. Go was considered beyond the reach of AI — too complex for brute-force search, too intuitive for pure calculation. AlphaGo combined deep learning with tree search. In March 2016, it defeated Lee Sedol, one of the world's best players, four games to one. The match was broadcast globally. Hundreds of millions watched. Move 37 in Game Two — a strange, beautiful move no human would play — became famous. The machine had intuition.

AlphaGo was not commercial. It did not generate revenue. But it demonstrated that DeepMind's approach worked on problems previously thought intractable. The research continued. AlphaGo Zero learned Go entirely from self-play, without human games. AlphaZero extended the approach to chess and shogi. The algorithms became more general, more efficient, more autonomous.

Meanwhile, the costs escalated. DeepMind burned through hundreds of millions annually — salaries for elite researchers, compute for massive experiments, the overhead of a London lab. By 2020, accumulated losses exceeded $1 billion. Google absorbed the costs, but tensions emerged. DeepMind's healthcare unit, which had partnered with the UK's National Health Service, was folded into Google Health. The ethics board dissolved. The autonomy Hassabis had negotiated eroded as Google sought returns on its investment.

Then came AlphaFold. In 2020, DeepMind's system for predicting protein structures achieved unprecedented accuracy. Proteins fold into complex three-dimensional shapes, and those shapes determine function. Predicting structure from amino acid sequences had been a grand challenge in biology for fifty years. AlphaFold solved it. The impact was immediate. Researchers could now predict structures in hours instead of years. DeepMind released the database of predictions for free. It was the first project that justified the acquisition to anyone outside AI circles.

By 2023, DeepMind had merged more fully into Google's AI operations, combining forces with Google Brain to form Google DeepMind. The independence was gone. The founders had left or stepped back. Suleyman departed to start Inflection AI. Hassabis remained, now an executive within Google's broader AI organization.

The path reveals the problem of long-term research inside capitalism. DeepMind wanted to solve intelligence. Google wanted applications. For years, Google's patience and resources allowed DeepMind to pursue the former. AlphaFold justified that patience. But the logic of the corporation asserts itself eventually. The lab that taught machines to think could not remain a lab forever. It needed an owner wealthy enough to fund the work and patient enough to wait for returns. Google was that owner. The price was autonomy. The outcome was survival, but on someone else's terms.
