# Pets.com

***The perfect storm of venture capital, television advertising, and timing***

> **Years:** 1998-2000

In the summer of 1998, Greg McLemore looked at the pet supply market and saw inefficiency begging for disruption. Americans spent $23 billion annually on pet supplies, most of it at physical retail stores with limited selection and inconsistent pricing. McLemore had worked at venture-backed startups before. He knew the playbook: attack fragmented retail with e-commerce, use venture capital to buy market share, achieve scale, dominate.

He wasn't alone in seeing this opportunity. By late 1998, at least a dozen pet supply e-commerce sites had launched. But McLemore had connections. He brought in Julie Wainwright, a seasoned executive, as CEO. More importantly, he secured funding from Hummer Winblad Venture Partners and then from Amazon, which invested $50 million for a 50% stake in February 1999. Amazon wanted a foothold in pet supplies without building the business itself. Pets.com would handle operations and brand. Amazon would handle logistics and fulfillment. It seemed like a perfect partnership.

The funding unlocked ambition. Pets.com didn't just want to sell pet supplies online — it wanted to become the brand people associated with pet care. In the late 1990s, that meant television advertising. Lots of it. The company hired the San Francisco agency TBWA\Chiat\Day, the same firm that had created Apple's "Think Different" campaign. They needed a mascot.

The sock puppet appeared almost by accident. A designer at the agency stitched together a simple hand puppet from a sock, added mismatched button eyes and a microphone. The character had deadpan delivery and an endearing cheapness. "Because pets can't drive" became the tagline. The puppet appeared on Good Morning America, was interviewed by People magazine, had a balloon in the Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade. It became more famous than the company.

The advertising worked in the narrowest sense — brand awareness soared. By early 2000, Pets.com had 80% unaided brand recognition in its target demographic. But brand awareness doesn't pay bills, and the economics of pet supply e-commerce were brutal. Dog food is heavy and cheap. Shipping costs more than margin. Customers might order $20 of product. Pets.com would spend $5 on shipping, another $15 on customer acquisition, and generate negative contribution margin on nearly every order. The plan was to make it up in volume and repeat purchases, but volume was expensive to acquire and repeat rates disappointed.

Amazon's logistics partnership helped, but not enough. The fundamentals were wrong. Pets.com was selling commodity products with thin margins and high shipping costs to customers with low online purchasing frequency. The company needed vast scale to achieve unit economics. But achieving scale required capital to burn on advertising. And the capital markets were about to close.

The IPO happened in February 2000, right as the NASDAQ peaked. Pets.com raised $82.5 million at $11 per share. The stock opened at $11 and closed the first day at $11. No pop. Bad sign. Within weeks, the broader market began to crack. By April, the dot-com bubble was deflating rapidly. Public market investors, who months earlier had rewarded growth-at-any-cost, suddenly cared about profitability. Pets.com had none and no clear path to it.

The company tried to adjust. They raised prices, cut marketing spend, renegotiated supplier contracts. Too late. The business model had been predicated on cheap capital allowing years of losses while building scale. That capital was gone. By the summer of 2000, Pets.com was burning through its remaining cash. The stock, which had briefly touched $14, fell below $1.

In October 2000, just nine months after the IPO, Pets.com announced it would shut down. The company had burned through $300 million. It liquidated inventory, laid off all 320 employees, and shut down operations in November. The sock puppet, which had cost the company tens of millions in advertising spend, was sold to a car loan company for use in their commercials. They paid $125,000.

The failure was spectacular but not unique. Webvan, eToys, Kozmo — all followed similar paths. Venture-backed e-commerce companies selling low-margin physical goods, burning capital on customer acquisition faster than they could achieve sustainable unit economics, all running out of runway when the capital markets closed.

But Pets.com became the symbol. The sock puppet appeared on the cover of magazines about the bubble. The company was cited in congressional testimony about venture capital excess. Part of this was the puppet itself — such a perfect embodiment of style over substance. Part was the speed: from Amazon investment to liquidation in 22 months. And part was the obviousness, in retrospect, of the flawed economics. Heavy, cheap products with thin margins do not ship profitably. This was knowable in 1999. People just weren't asking.

The lesson wasn't that pet supplies can't be sold online. Chewy, founded in 2011, reached $3.5 billion in revenue by 2018 doing exactly that. The difference was timing and discipline. By 2011, online shopping frequency was higher, shipping costs had improved, and venture capitalists had learned to insist on better unit economics. Chewy spent years grinding toward profitability before going public. It succeeded on the same terrain where Pets.com died, but walked a different path.
