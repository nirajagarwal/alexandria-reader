# Fairchild Semiconductor

***The company that invented Silicon Valley***

> **Years:** 1957-1987 (as independent entity)

In 1957, eight young engineers walked away from one of the most prestigious jobs in American technology. They left William Shockley, Nobel laureate and co-inventor of the transistor, not because they lacked opportunity but because working for a genius had become impossible. Shockley was brilliant and paranoid, inspirational and cruel. His management style oscillated between micromanagement and chaos. His technical direction had shifted from transistors to a four-layer diode that none of them believed would work.

The eight — Julius Blank, Victor Grinich, Jean Hoerni, Eugene Kleiner, Jay Last, Gordon Moore, Robert Noyce, and Sheldon Roberts — wanted to build things. They were young, most in their late twenties or early thirties, and they shared a conviction that silicon transistors represented the future. Shockley was chasing germanium. They were right. He was wrong. But none of them had money, and in 1957, engineers didn't start companies.

They found Sherman Fairchild, heir to an IBM fortune and an inventor himself. Fairchild offered an unusual deal: he would fund them as a division of Fairchild Camera and Instrument, with an option for the team to buy independence if they succeeded. They moved into a small building in Mountain View, named themselves Fairchild Semiconductor, and went to work.

The early days were heroic in the specific way that technical problems allow. They needed to manufacture silicon transistors reliably and cheaply. The technology existed in theory, but manufacturing at scale was unsolved. Jean Hoerni developed the planar process, a method of building transistors by depositing layers on a flat silicon surface. It was elegant, repeatable, and revolutionary. Robert Noyce, the group's de facto leader — charming, strategic, visionary — saw what planar process enabled: you could build multiple transistors on a single chip of silicon.

Noyce and Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments independently invented the integrated circuit in 1958 and 1959. The legal battle over patents would drag on for years, eventually settling in a cross-licensing agreement. But Fairchild had the manufacturing advantage. Planar process made integrated circuits practical. By 1960, they were selling them.

The market exploded. NASA needed them for the Apollo program — space demanded reliability, and integrated circuits eliminated the thousands of hand-soldered connections that caused failures. The military needed them for missiles. Computer makers needed them to shrink machines from room-sized to desk-sized. Fairchild's revenues grew from $500,000 in 1958 to $130 million by 1967.

But the structure that had enabled their founding contained the seeds of their dissolution. The eight founders were employees of a subsidiary. They had salaries and small stock options in the parent company, not equity in the thing they had built. Sherman Fairchild and the East Coast executives at Fairchild Camera treated the semiconductor division as a cash cow, siphoning profits back to the camera business rather than reinvesting in silicon. The founders watched their invention make other people rich.

They began to leave. In 1961, Jay Last and Jean Hoerni co-founded Amelco. Others followed to different ventures. The pattern was set. Fairchild engineers, trained in the most advanced manufacturing in the world, funded by venture capitalists who had learned from Sherman Fairchild's model, started their own companies. Intersil. Signetics. National Semiconductor. Advanced Micro Devices. Intel.

Intel was the rupture that mattered most. In 1968, Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore left to start a company focused on semiconductor memory. They took Andy Grove, Fairchild's director of operations, with them. Noyce was the visionary, Moore the technologist, Grove the executor — the perfect founding team. Arthur Rock, the investor who had originally connected the eight engineers with Sherman Fairchild, funded them. Intel became the template: engineer-founders, venture capital, stock options for employees, and a relentless focus on the next technology rather than milking the current one.

Fairchild tried to adapt. They hired new management. They invested in new processes. But they were competing against their own alumni, people who had learned everything at Fairchild and were now unburdened by a parent company's demands. The semiconductor industry moved in generations — from transistors to integrated circuits to microprocessors to memory chips — and each generation favored new entrants over incumbents. Fairchild had created the industry. Now the industry's logic worked against them.

By the 1970s, Fairchild was in decline. The parent company's mismanagement continued. The semiconductor division cycled through CEOs. The best engineers kept leaving to start competitors. In 1979, Fairchild Camera sold the semiconductor division to Schlumberger, the French oil services company, for $425 million. Schlumberger tried to turn it around and failed. In 1987, they sold it to National Semiconductor for $200 million less than they had paid.

The mathematics of Fairchild's influence are almost absurd. By one count, more than 70 companies trace their lineage directly to Fairchild alumni. Intel, AMD, and dozens of others. The venture capital model that funds Silicon Valley emerged from the Fairchild deal. The idea that engineers could found companies, that stock options could align incentives, that turnover could be creative destruction rather than simply destructive — all of that came from watching Fairchild succeed brilliantly and fail to keep its people.

Silicon Valley is named for the material Fairchild mastered. But it exists because of the pattern Fairchild established: brilliant engineers leave to start the next thing, funded by investors who learn from the last thing, in a region where this cycle repeats until it becomes an economy. Fairchild didn't just build semiconductors. It built the system that builds everything else.
