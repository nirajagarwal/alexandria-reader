# Wise

***The peer-to-peer vision that became a regulated empire***

> **Years:** 2011-present

Two Estonian immigrants in London had the same problem. Taavet Hinrikus, Skype's first employee, earned in euros but needed pounds. Kristo Käärmann earned in pounds but had a mortgage in euros. Every month, both lost money to bank fees and exchange rate markups that felt like theft.

Their solution was elegant: they didn't actually transfer money across borders. Hinrikus put euros into Käärmann's Estonian account. Käärmann put pounds into Hinrikus's UK account. No international wire. No conversion fees. No bullshit. They posted the deal on their blog, expecting maybe their friends would join. Within a month, strangers were emailing asking to use their system.

This was January 2011. The financial crisis had ended, but trust in banks had not recovered. The founders built a website where people could match currency needs peer-to-peer. It felt radical: banks charged 4-5% for international transfers through a combination of hidden fees and rigged exchange rates. TransferWise charged 0.5% and showed you exactly what you were paying. Transparency as product.

The early growth came from word of mouth in immigrant communities — Poles in the UK, Germans in Spain, people who sent money home and felt robbed every time. The peer-to-peer model worked until it didn't. As volume grew, matching became harder. You needed people who wanted to swap the exact amounts in the exact currencies at the exact time. The founders realized they would need to hold money themselves, become a money transmitter, get regulated.

This was 2013. Most fintech startups avoided regulation, built fast, asked forgiveness. Käärmann and Hinrikus went the opposite direction. They hired compliance officers before marketing managers. They got licenses in every market they entered. It was slow, expensive, the opposite of "move fast and break things." But they had seen what happened to startups that played cute with financial regulations. They vanished.

The licensing opened a new model. Instead of peer-to-peer matching, TransferWise held multi-currency accounts. Your dollars went into their US account, their UK account paid out pounds, and they charged you the mid-market rate plus their transparent fee. They weren't moving your specific money across borders — they were using local money to settle local transfers. The innovation wasn't technology. It was regulatory arbitrage and honest pricing.

Banks noticed when the volume hit billions. HSBC threatened to close TransferWise's UK account. The message was clear: you're disrupting our most profitable product. The founders went public with the threat. "Banks are trying to kill us," they told the Financial Times. UK regulators intervened. The account stayed open. The publicity drove more users.

By 2015, TransferWise was processing over £1 billion monthly. They expanded beyond person-to-person transfers into business accounts. The pitch to small businesses was the same as to immigrants: banks are ripping you off, we're not. Shopify merchants, freelancers, import-export companies signed up. The product became a multi-currency bank account you could hold in 50+ currencies and spend with a debit card. The name "TransferWise" suddenly felt too narrow.

The growth created odd tensions. The founders had positioned themselves as the anti-bank, the scrappy disruptor fighting gouging incumbents. But as they scaled, they became what they had opposed: a regulated financial institution with compliance departments, risk assessments, and accounts they sometimes froze. Users who had joined for the "stick it to the banks" vibe found themselves dealing with the same bureaucracy they had fled.

The company's response was to double down on transparency. In 2017, they started publishing the speed of their transfers versus competitors in real-time on their website. In 2019, they showed exactly how much profit they made on each transaction. It was performance art as corporate strategy. Look, we're different because we show you everything.

In 2021, they changed the name to Wise. "We're not just transfers anymore," Käärmann said. The rebrand coincided with a direct listing on the London Stock Exchange at a £9 billion valuation. The founders chose direct listing over traditional IPO to avoid paying banks the very fees they had built their company to eliminate. Poetic consistency or ideological purity, depending on your view.

The model had proven out. By 2024, Wise moved over £100 billion annually for 16 million customers. Revenue crossed £1 billion. They were profitable. The peer-to-peer dream was dead — less than 10% of transfers still matched that way. But the founding insight held: banks charged too much, transparency built trust, and being regulated wasn't a burden if you built for it from day one.

What Wise revealed was that disrupting financial services didn't require blockchain or AI or complex technology. It required better unit economics, regulatory patience, and the discipline to show customers exactly what they were paying for. The peer-to-peer origin story was marketing by the end. The real product was a multinational money transmitter that acted less like a bank and made sure everyone knew it.
