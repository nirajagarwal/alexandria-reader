# Wise

***The money transfer startup that made banks look ridiculous***

> **Years:** 2011-present

In 2008, two Estonians living in London had a problem that millions shared but nobody had bothered to solve properly. Kristo Käärmann needed to send money from his London salary back to Estonia for his mortgage. Taavet Hinrikus was getting paid in euros by Skype but needed pounds to live in London. The banks charged them both absurd fees and offered exchange rates that bore no relation to reality.

So they made a deal. Taavet would transfer euros to Kristo's Estonian account at the real exchange rate. Kristo would transfer pounds to Taavet's UK account. No money crossed borders. No banks took their cut. It was elegant, obvious, and completely contrary to how the financial system worked.

They did this manually for a year. Then they realized: if two people have this problem, millions do. What if they could build a platform that matched people going opposite directions? Not a bank. Not even really a money transfer service. A matching engine wrapped in a consumer product.

They called it TransferWise. The pitch was simple: the real exchange rate, a small transparent fee, and none of the bullshit that banks buried in the fine print. They launched in January 2011 with £10,000 of their own money and a website that looked like it cost about that much.

The product worked because the economics worked. Traditional banks and money transfer services made money on the spread — quoting you an exchange rate worse than the real one and pocketing the difference. A customer might think they were paying a £5 fee when the bank was actually taking £50 on the spread. TransferWise reversed it: show the real rate, charge an explicit percentage fee, make it all transparent. They could undercut everyone because they weren't moving money internationally at all — just matching flows.

The early growth was founder-driven and scrappy. Taavet, who had been employee #1 at Skype and knew how to build products people loved, focused on making the experience delightful. Kristo handled operations and the unglamorous work of getting banking licenses in multiple countries. They both did customer support. When someone complained, they'd fix it personally.

The breakthrough came from transparency as marketing. They created a calculator showing exactly how much every competitor would charge versus TransferWise. Banks charged 4-8%. MoneyGram and Western Union charged double digits. TransferWise charged 0.5-1%. They took out billboards showing the difference. "Same day transfer. No hidden fees. Unlike your bank." The banks couldn't respond because everything TransferWise said was true.

Expansion meant licenses. Lots of licenses. Sending money internationally requires regulatory approval in every country you operate in. It required holding accounts in multiple currencies. It required building compliance systems that could satisfy dozens of regulators simultaneously. This was not the sexy part of fintech, but it was the moat. Once you built it, nobody else wanted to.

By 2014, they were processing £1 billion annually. By 2016, £1 billion monthly. The model scaled because liquidity begets liquidity. The more customers they had, the more likely they could match flows without money crossing borders. When they couldn't match, they'd move money in bulk through wholesale markets at rates consumers could never access. Every new customer made the service better for existing customers.

The competition tried to respond. Banks launched "low-fee" international transfer products that still buried spreads in the exchange rate. PayPal and Venmo moved internationally but kept their fees high. Nobody could match TransferWise's combination of price and transparency because nobody else had built their business model around elimination of spreads.

The company went after adjacent problems. If people needed to hold money in multiple currencies, they'd offer multi-currency accounts. If businesses needed to pay contractors internationally, they'd build business accounts. If people wanted debit cards that worked everywhere at the real exchange rate, they'd issue cards. Each expansion used the same underlying infrastructure: hold balances in local currencies, move money locally, match international flows.

By 2020, TransferWise had 10 million customers and was profitable. They'd moved £5 billion monthly. Kristo and Taavet still owned significant stakes but had brought in professional management. The company had 2,500 employees and banking licenses in dozens of countries. They'd built what the banks should have built but couldn't because their business models depended on the inefficiency.

In 2021, they dropped the "Transfer" and became just Wise. The rebrand reflected reality: they were no longer just about transfers. They were building a global account that worked everywhere at the real exchange rate. A bank for people who lived internationally.

The company went public through a direct listing in July 2021, valued at £9 billion. No IPO, no underwriters taking their cut. It was fitting — even in going public, they found a way to eliminate the middleman's fee.

Wise never disrupted banking by becoming a bank. They disrupted it by showing that most of what banks did was artificial complexity designed to hide fees. They built a better product by building a simpler one. The innovation wasn't technology. It was transparency plus liquidity plus relentless focus on the real cost of moving money.

The big strategic question was always: how do banks respond when someone makes their business model look predatory? The answer turned out to be: slowly, defensively, and never quite enough. Because Wise wasn't wrong about the fees.
