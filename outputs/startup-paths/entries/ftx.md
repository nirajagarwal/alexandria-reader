# FTX

***The crypto empire built on customer money***

> **Years:** 2019-2022

Sam Bankman-Fried started his career as a trader at Jane Street Capital, where he learned arbitrage and risk management in traditional markets. In 2017, he left to start Alameda Research, a quantitative crypto trading firm. The insight was simple: crypto markets were inefficient. Bitcoin traded at different prices on different exchanges. Sometimes the spread was 10%, 20%, even 30%. You could buy on one exchange, sell on another, and pocket the difference.

Alameda printed money. By 2019, Bankman-Fried — known universally as SBF — had identified a different opportunity. Crypto traders needed better infrastructure. The existing exchanges were slow, crashed during volatile periods, and lacked sophisticated products. He recruited Gary Wang, an MIT classmate and Google engineer, and Nishad Singh, a Facebook engineer. Together they built FTX: a cryptocurrency exchange designed by traders for traders.

FTX launched in May 2019 with a focus on derivatives — futures, options, leveraged tokens. The interface was clean. The matching engine was fast. It offered products professional traders wanted but couldn't find elsewhere. Within months, FTX was processing billions in daily volume. The growth was extraordinary even by crypto standards.

SBF had a story investors loved. He was an effective altruist who planned to earn billions and give it all away. He lived simply, drove a Corolla, slept on a beanbag. He spoke the language of rationalism and expected value maximization. In 2021, FTX raised $900 million at an $18 billion valuation. Six months later, another $400 million at $32 billion. The cap table included Sequoia, SoftBank, BlackRock, Tiger Global.

FTX moved fast. It acquired smaller exchanges and trading platforms. It bought naming rights to the Miami Heat arena for $135 million. It hired Tom Brady and Gisele Bündchen as brand ambassadors. It ran Super Bowl ads. SBF became the face of crypto regulation, testifying before Congress, meeting with regulators, arguing that clear rules would legitimize the industry.

Behind the growth was a structure that would prove fatal. Alameda Research and FTX were separate companies in theory. In practice, they were intertwined. Alameda was FTX's largest trading client. It had special privileges on the platform — exemptions from liquidation protocols, the ability to maintain a negative balance. This was justified as market-making necessity. Major trading firms need special arrangements. Nothing unusual.

What was unusual: Alameda was borrowing customer funds from FTX. Not as formal loans. The customer deposits sat in accounts controlled by Alameda. When Alameda needed capital for trading, it used FTX customer money. Billions of dollars of customer assets were lent to a trading firm that was supposed to be independent. The separation was fiction.

For a while, it worked. Crypto was booming. Assets were appreciating. Alameda's bets were winning more than losing. FTX kept growing. The system seemed to function. But the structure was inherently unstable. It depended on continued growth, continued confidence, continued market conditions that made the borrowing invisible.

In 2022, the crypto market collapsed. Bitcoin fell from $69,000 to under $20,000. Ethereum, Solana, and everything else fell harder. Alameda had enormous positions that were suddenly deeply underwater. The collateral securing its borrowing lost value. FTX faced a math problem: customer deposits that should have been available weren't. They were locked in Alameda's losing positions.

In November 2022, CoinDesk published a report on Alameda's balance sheet. It showed that most of Alameda's assets were FTT — FTX's own token. This meant Alameda was backed by value FTX itself had created, a circular arrangement. Binance's CEO, Changpeng Zhao — known as CZ — announced he would sell Binance's FTT holdings. FTT price dropped. Customers grew nervous.

They began withdrawing. On November 6, FTX saw $5 billion in withdrawal requests. It couldn't meet them. The money wasn't there. SBF scrambled to raise emergency funding. He convinced CZ to consider acquiring FTX. CZ did initial due diligence and backed out within 24 hours. The hole was too large.

On November 11, FTX filed for bankruptcy. The company's assets were missing. Somewhere between $8 billion and $10 billion in customer funds had disappeared — lent to Alameda, lost in bad trades, or in some cases simply stolen. SBF had transferred hundreds of millions to personal accounts. FTX's new CEO, brought in to manage the bankruptcy, called it the worst failure of corporate controls he had seen in his career.

The unraveling was fast. SBF was arrested in the Bahamas in December 2022 and extradited to the United States. At trial in 2023, Caroline Ellison, who ran Alameda and had been SBF's girlfriend, testified that he directed her to use FTX customer funds. Gary Wang and Nishad Singh, the co-founders who built the technology, also pleaded guilty and testified. The defense was that SBF was disorganized, not fraudulent — that he made mistakes but didn't intend to steal. The jury deliberated for four hours. Guilty on all seven counts.

FTX had seemed like the future — fast, sophisticated, well-funded, politically connected. It collapsed in a week. The story revealed that the gap between appearing successful and being legitimate can persist until the moment it can't. In markets built on confidence, that moment comes fast.
