# FTX

***The exchange that burned brightest and fastest***

> **Years:** 2019-2022

Sam Bankman-Fried graduated from MIT in 2014 and went to work at Jane Street Capital, a quantitative trading firm where he learned arbitrage and market microstructure. By 2017, he'd left to start Alameda Research, a cryptocurrency trading firm that exploited inefficiencies in the nascent crypto markets. The best opportunity was simple: Bitcoin traded at different prices on different exchanges. Alameda could buy on one, sell on another, and pocket the spread. At its peak, this "kimchi premium" — the price difference between Korean and American exchanges — reached 30%.

But arbitrage opportunities decay as markets mature. By 2019, Bankman-Fried identified a new problem: the cryptocurrency exchanges themselves. They crashed during volatility. Their interfaces confused traders. They lacked sophisticated derivatives products. He could build a better exchange. In April 2019, he launched FTX — Futures Exchange — targeting professional traders with features they actually wanted: partial liquidations, reliable infrastructure, and exotic products like tokenized stocks and prediction markets.

FTX grew in the shadow of Binance, already the world's largest crypto exchange. But FTX carved a niche: it felt professional. Traders who'd grown frustrated with Binance's outages and opaque operations switched to FTX. By July 2019, three months after launch, FTX was processing $300 million in daily volume. Binance noticed. Instead of crushing the competitor, Changpeng Zhao invested — Binance took a small stake in FTX.

The COVID-19 pandemic turbocharged cryptocurrency speculation. Stimulus checks met lockdown boredom met fear of inflation. Everyone became a trader. FTX's daily volume hit $10 billion by May 2020. Bankman-Fried raised $900 million at an $18 billion valuation in July 2021. By January 2022, FTX was valued at $32 billion. He bought naming rights to the Miami Heat's arena for $135 million. He hired Tom Brady and Gisele Bündchen as brand ambassadors. He testified before Congress about crypto regulation, positioning himself as the responsible adult in an industry known for libertarian maximalists and obvious frauds.

The persona Bankman-Fried cultivated was distinctive: he wore cargo shorts to meetings, claimed to sleep on a beanbag in his office, and talked about effective altruism — the philosophical movement focused on using wealth to do maximum good. He pledged to give away his fortune. He donated to pandemic prevention, Democratic politicians, and animal welfare causes. He lived, he said, on $200,000 a year despite being worth $26 billion. The scruffiness seemed to authenticate his seriousness about changing the world rather than merely getting rich.

The structure beneath this image was different. FTX and Alameda Research were legally separate entities, but practically intertwined. Bankman-Fried owned both. Caroline Ellison, his sometimes-girlfriend, ran Alameda. Gary Wang, his MIT roommate, was FTX's chief technology officer. Nishad Singh, another close friend, was director of engineering. The companies shared an office in the Bahamas, where FTX had relocated to evade regulatory scrutiny. They shared a penthouse where the leadership lived together. The boundaries between the companies were social, not structural.

This blurred line became catastrophic because of one decision: FTX allowed Alameda to borrow against customer deposits. Not explicitly — customer funds were supposed to remain segregated — but through a special exemption in the code. Alameda could go negative, borrowing billions from the exchange. The justification was market making: Alameda needed liquidity to provide depth on FTX. But Alameda used these borrowed funds to make venture investments, buy real estate, and cover losses from bad trades. By mid-2022, Alameda owed FTX $8 billion.

This worked fine when asset prices rose. Alameda's collateral — much of it FTT, FTX's own exchange token — increased in value. But in May 2022, the Terra-Luna stablecoin collapsed, triggering a cascade of liquidations across crypto. Prices crashed. Alameda's collateral evaporated. The firm lost billions. Rather than liquidate Alameda, FTX sent more customer funds to cover the losses. The hole grew deeper.

In November 2022, CoinDesk published Alameda's balance sheet, leaked by unknown sources. It showed that most of Alameda's $14.6 billion in assets was FTT tokens — assets they essentially printed themselves. Changpeng Zhao read the article and announced Binance would sell its FTT holdings. FTT's price collapsed. Customers panicked and tried to withdraw funds from FTX. On November 6, customers attempted to withdraw $5 billion. FTX didn't have it. The exchange halted withdrawals. Bankman-Fried negotiated an emergency acquisition by Binance. Binance's due diligence team spent one day examining FTX's books and walked away. The company was insolvent by approximately $8 billion.

On November 11, 2022, FTX filed for bankruptcy. A million creditors had claims totaling billions. Bankman-Fried resigned as CEO. The new CEO, a restructuring specialist who'd managed Enron's bankruptcy, said in a filing that FTX had "old-fashioned embezzlement" and the failure of control systems was "unprecedented." There were no board meetings, no independent oversight, no separation of duties. Alameda had a secret backdoor in FTX's accounting system that allowed unlimited borrowing. FTX's internal Slack messages described loans to executives as "loans and whatnot."

On December 12, 2022, Bahamian authorities arrested Bankman-Fried at the request of the U.S. government. He was extradited, charged with wire fraud, securities fraud, and money laundering. At trial in November 2023, former colleagues testified against him. Caroline Ellison described him instructing her to use FTX customer funds. Gary Wang explained the backdoor he'd coded at Bankman-Fried's direction. Bankman-Fried took the stand and claimed he didn't know about Alameda's borrowing, that the lawyers had approved everything, that this was all a terrible misunderstanding. The jury deliberated for four hours. Guilty on all seven counts. In March 2024, he was sentenced to 25 years in federal prison.

The bankruptcy estate ultimately recovered enough assets — including Anthropic shares that Alameda had bought for $500 million and were now worth billions — to repay customers. But the repayment was in dollars, calculated at November 2022 prices. Customers who'd held Bitcoin got back dollars, missing the subsequent price increase. Many lost fortunes not from theft, but from timing.

FTX collapsed not from external competition or market forces, but from the gap between what Bankman-Fried said and what he did. The effective altruist who lived on a beanbag had stolen $8 billion. The sophisticated risk engine had a backdoor for his trading firm. The adult in the room was the same as every exchange operator who'd absconded with customer funds, just better at PowerPoint. The path from MIT to federal prison took five years.
