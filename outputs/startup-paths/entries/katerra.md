# Katerra

***The unicorn that tried to reinvent construction and collapsed under its own ambition***

> **Years:** 2015-2021

In 2015, a group of Silicon Valley veterans looked at the construction industry and saw what tech founders always see in old industries: waste, inefficiency, and opportunity. Michael Marks, former CEO of Flextronics, and Fritz Wolff, his longtime colleague, believed they could do to construction what manufacturing had done to electronics — vertically integrate, automate, and optimize the entire supply chain. They called it Katerra.

The pitch was elegant. Construction was fragmented: architects, general contractors, subcontractors, suppliers, each taking a margin, each a source of delay and error. Buildings cost too much and took too long because nobody controlled the whole process. Katerra would own it all — design, manufacturing, materials, assembly. They would prefabricate components in factories, ship them to sites, and assemble buildings like IKEA furniture. Faster, cheaper, better.

It was catnip for investors. SoftBank's Vision Fund, flush with capital and hungry for moonshots, led a $865 million round in 2018. By 2019, Katerra had raised over $2 billion. The valuation hit $4 billion. It was the largest construction technology startup in the world.

The company expanded at breathtaking speed. They acquired firms across the value chain: an architecture practice, an engineering firm, a door manufacturer, a lumber company. They built factories in Phoenix and Spokane to manufacture cross-laminated timber and modular building components. They hired thousands of employees. They signed contracts for apartment buildings, hotels, corporate campuses. They promised to revolutionize how America builds.

The cracks appeared early, but momentum obscured them. Factories opened before manufacturing processes were debugged. Projects launched before designs were finalized. The company took on complex custom buildings when their model worked best for repetitive designs. They bid aggressively to win contracts, often underbidding to gain market share, assuming they would achieve the efficiency gains that would make the numbers work.

Those gains never materialized. Prefabrication sounds simple in theory but is viciously hard in practice. Buildings are not phones. Every site has different soil, different codes, different inspectors. Components manufactured in Phoenix had to work in Seattle and Dallas and Sacramento, each with different regulations. Designs that looked good in CAD software failed when they met reality. Tolerances that worked in a factory didn't survive shipping and field conditions.

Projects fell behind schedule. Costs overran budgets. Clients complained. Some sued. Katerra's reputation among developers — the customers they needed most — began to sour. The vertically integrated model that was supposed to eliminate margins instead multiplied risk. When one piece failed, everything downstream failed with it.

The financial model was brutal. Construction operates on thin margins even when executed well. Katerra was burning cash on factories, acquisitions, and overhead while underpricing projects and struggling to deliver. They needed perfect execution to break even. They were getting chaos.

By 2020, the company was restructuring. Executive turnover accelerated. Projects were cancelled or handed off. The lumber mill shut down. Then COVID hit, disrupting supply chains and halting construction sites. But COVID was catalyst, not cause. Katerra was already in trouble.

SoftBank, dealing with its own crisis after WeWork's collapse and a brutal portfolio writedown, stopped funding Katerra's losses. In early 2021, the company desperately sought a buyer or new investor. None materialized. In June 2021, Katerra shut down. Thousands lost their jobs. Projects were abandoned mid-construction. Suppliers went unpaid. Lawsuits piled up.

The failure revealed a fundamental misunderstanding. Katerra's founders believed construction was inefficient because it lacked vertical integration and technology. The truth was more complex. Construction is fragmented because buildings are heterogeneous, contexts are variable, and risk is easier to manage when distributed. The industry's structure is not an accident of history but an adaptation to reality.

Vertical integration works brilliantly for standardized products at scale. It can work for construction when focused narrowly — modular hotels, cookie-cutter apartments, repeatable designs. But Katerra tried to be everything: custom and standardized, luxury and affordable, residential and commercial. They built the infrastructure for mass production while taking on bespoke projects. They optimized for a future that required discipline they didn't have and a market that didn't yet exist.

The technology was real. The factories could produce quality components. The software worked. But technology alone couldn't overcome the fundamentals: undisciplined expansion, cash burn that required perfection, a business model that multiplied rather than mitigated construction's inherent risks.

Katerra's collapse became a case study in the limits of "tech thinking" applied to physical industries. Not every industry can be disrupted by treating it as a software problem. Not every inefficiency is an opportunity. Sometimes the old way is old because it works, and the new way is just expensive failure with better branding.
