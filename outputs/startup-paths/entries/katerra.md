# Katerra

***The startup that tried to fix construction and broke itself***

> **Years:** 2015-2021

In 2015, Fritz Wolff and Michael Marks looked at the construction industry and saw inefficiency everywhere. Buildings took too long to build, cost too much, and the process involved dozens of fragmented players—architects, contractors, suppliers, subcontractors—each adding margin and delay. Software had transformed industries from retail to transportation. Why not construction?

Their answer was Katerra: a company that would vertically integrate the entire construction process. It would design buildings, manufacture the components in its own factories, and handle construction—all under one roof. By controlling the supply chain and standardizing designs, Katerra would build faster and cheaper than traditional methods. The pitch resonated. SoftBank led a $865 million investment in 2018, valuing Katerra at $3 billion before it had proven the model at scale.

Wolff and Marks weren't naive newcomers. Marks had been CEO of Flextronics, a contract manufacturing giant. They understood supply chains and industrial production. They believed construction's problems were solvable through manufacturing discipline and vertical integration. They were right about the problems. They were wrong about almost everything else.

Katerra moved fast. It acquired lumber mills, started a drywall factory, bought design firms, opened fabrication facilities across the country. It hired thousands of people. It took on projects—apartment buildings, schools, offices—often bidding aggressively to win business and prove the model. The vision was compelling: imagine ordering a building like you order a car, with predictable costs and timelines, built from components manufactured to precise specifications.

But construction isn't manufacturing. Every building site is different. Local building codes vary. Labor markets are regional. Weather matters. A factory can optimize for one product made millions of times. A construction company faces unique conditions on every project. Katerra tried to standardize the unstandardizable.

The company's projects began running into trouble. A major apartment complex in Seattle faced delays and cost overruns. Subcontractors filed liens claiming unpaid bills. Buildings designed for Katerra's manufactured components encountered problems when those components didn't arrive on time or didn't fit as planned. The factories, built to supply Katerra's projects, weren't running at capacity. The company was burning through capital on multiple fronts: building factories, acquiring companies, covering losses on construction projects.

In 2019, Katerra's COO left. Then its CFO. Michael Marks stepped down as CEO. The executive turnover signaled internal chaos. Projects were losing money. The factories were expensive to operate and underutilized. The company had built infrastructure for a scale it hadn't reached and might never reach. Traditional construction companies could lose money on a project and make it up elsewhere. Katerra was losing money on projects while also carrying the fixed costs of factories and a sprawling organization.

SoftBank kept investing. Katerra raised over $2 billion total, with SoftBank providing the vast majority. The funding sustained the vision but also enabled the expansion that made the problems worse. More projects meant more complexity. More factories meant more overhead. The company needed volume to justify the infrastructure, but the volume wasn't materializing—or when it did, the projects were unprofitable.

COVID-19 hit in 2020. Construction sites shut down. Supply chains seized up. Projects delayed. Katerra's finances, already strained, became critical. The company laid off workers, closed facilities, tried to right-size. But the fundamental problem remained: the business model required enormous scale to work, and even at the scale Katerra had reached, it wasn't working.

In early 2021, SoftBank stopped funding. Katerra had no path to profitability without continued capital infusions. It filed for bankruptcy in June 2021. Projects were left incomplete. Subcontractors and suppliers were left unpaid. Employees lost jobs. The factories closed or were sold.

What killed Katerra wasn't lack of ambition or capital. It was the gap between theory and reality. The theory was elegant: vertical integration would eliminate inefficiency. The reality was brutal: construction's inefficiencies exist because building is inherently complex, local, and variable. Every project is a prototype. Standardization helps at the margins but can't transform the underlying economics.

Katerra also fell victim to its own speed. It expanded before proving unit economics. It built infrastructure before securing demand. It took on projects at prices that assumed future efficiencies it never achieved. SoftBank's capital allowed Katerra to grow faster than its learning curve, accumulating obligations before understanding if the model worked.

The construction industry remains inefficient. Buildings still take too long and cost too much. But Katerra's failure suggests the problem isn't solvable by attacking it head-on with vertical integration and capital. The companies that have succeeded in construction technology—project management software, scheduling tools, marketplace platforms—work within the industry's structure rather than trying to replace it entirely.

Katerra tried to be Tesla for buildings. But buildings aren't cars. The attempt cost over $2 billion and left incomplete projects scattered across the country. The path from vision to bankruptcy took six years.
