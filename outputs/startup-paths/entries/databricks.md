# Databricks

***The data platform that turned academic Spark into a $43 billion empire***

> **Years:** 2013-present

In 2013, Ali Ghodsi, Matei Zaharia, Ion Stoica, and four other computer science researchers from UC Berkeley faced a decision that separated them from thousands of other academics: whether to commercialize their research or watch others do it badly. They had built Apache Spark, an open-source data processing engine that was already replacing Hadoop across the industry. Companies were deploying it, but struggling. The gap between research and production was wide, and the founders knew the terrain better than anyone.

They called the company Databricks—a name that sounded like infrastructure, which it was, and like building blocks, which captured the modular vision. The founding insight was not that Spark needed to exist—it already did—but that the world needed a unified platform where data scientists and engineers could work together without drowning in infrastructure complexity. AWS existed. Hadoop existed. But the tooling was fragmented, the learning curve steep, the production deployments brittle.

The early pitch was simple: Spark-as-a-service. But the ambition was larger. The founders envisioned a collaborative workspace where data teams could go from raw data to machine learning models without switching contexts, without deploying clusters manually, without the friction that made data science feel like fighting the tools instead of solving problems. They raised $14 million in Series A from Andreessen Horowitz in 2013, before the product was fully built. The credibility came from Spark adoption—already powering data pipelines at Apple, Netflix, and dozens of major companies—and from the technical depth of the team.

The first version launched in 2014 as a managed Spark service with notebooks—an interface borrowed from scientific computing that let users write code, see results, and share work in one place. It was not revolutionary interface design. It was the right interface for the moment. Data scientists were already using Jupyter notebooks locally; Databricks brought that experience to production scale. The platform handled cluster management, job scheduling, version control. Customers could focus on analysis instead of infrastructure.

But managed Spark was not enough to build a category-defining company. By 2015, cloud providers were offering their own Spark services. The moat was not the engine—it was open source—but the integrated experience. Databricks invested heavily in performance optimizations to Spark itself, contributing improvements back to the open-source project while building proprietary features on top. They introduced Delta Lake in 2019, a storage layer that brought reliability and performance to data lakes, addressing the chaos of files-in-S3 architectures that dominated the era. Delta Lake became open source but was tightly integrated with Databricks, creating a flywheel: better open-source tools drove adoption, adoption drove platform lock-in.

The growth path was enterprise sales, not bottom-up viral adoption. Data platform decisions are made by VPs of Engineering and Chief Data Officers, not individual developers expensing a credit card. Databricks built a sales organization that could navigate six-month procurement cycles and million-dollar contracts. By 2017, they had raised over $250 million and were expanding internationally. The customer list read like the Fortune 500: Shell, Comcast, HP, SAP.

The competitive landscape was brutal. AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud all had native Spark offerings and infinite capital. Snowflake was becoming the data warehouse king with a competing vision—structured data, SQL-first, no Spark required. Databricks positioned itself as the lakehouse: a unified platform for structured and unstructured data, for SQL and machine learning, for analysts and data scientists. It was a bigger tent than Snowflake's warehouse, a more opinionated product than AWS Glue.

The Snowflake comparison became inevitable. Both were cloud data platforms, both were growing explosively, both were raising at escalating valuations. When Snowflake went public in 2020 at a $33 billion valuation on its first day, the question became: when would Databricks follow? The answer was: not yet. They raised a $1 billion Series G in 2021 at a $28 billion valuation, then another $1.6 billion in 2023 at $43 billion. Staying private allowed them to invest in long-term bets without quarterly earnings pressure.

The long-term bet was AI. As machine learning moved from research labs to production, Databricks positioned itself as the infrastructure layer. They acquired MosaicML in 2023 for $1.3 billion, bringing in capabilities for training and deploying large language models. The pitch evolved: not just a data platform, but the platform for the entire AI lifecycle—data preparation, model training, inference, governance.

The founder dynamic was unusual for a company of this scale. Matei Zaharia, the original creator of Spark, remained deeply technical, continuing to publish research while serving as Chief Technologist. Ali Ghodsi, as CEO, became the public face—charismatic, strategic, able to translate technical depth into market vision. Ion Stoica co-led research initiatives while serving on the board. The academic roots never fully disappeared; Databricks still published papers, still contributed to open source, still hired from the research community.

By 2024, Databricks had over 10,000 customers and billions in annual recurring revenue. They were profitable on a unit basis but investing heavily in growth. The IPO question loomed but remained unanswered. The market for their offering was enormous—every company with data, which was every company—but so was the competition. AWS could bundle, Snowflake could simplify, startups could niche.

The path from Spark to Databricks was not inevitable. Open-source infrastructure rarely translates to venture-scale outcomes. The academics could have stayed in academia, licensed the technology, or sold early to a cloud provider. Instead, they built a platform company on the foundation of an open-source project, navigating the tension between community contribution and proprietary value. They expanded from data processing to machine learning to AI, always staying close to where enterprises were spending money at scale.

The outcome is still being written. Databricks stands as one of the most valuable private software companies, a rare example of deep technical founders building both the research and the business. Whether the $43 billion valuation represents triumph or overshoot depends on the next chapter—the IPO, the competition, the AI market's evolution. But the path is clear: from Berkeley lab to production infrastructure, from open-source project to platform empire, from seven researchers to a company redefining how the world builds with data.
