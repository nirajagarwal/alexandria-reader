# Ford Motor Company

***The assembly line that remade the world — and the founder who couldn't let go***

> **Years:** 1903-present

In June 1903, twelve investors put up $28,000 to fund Henry Ford's third attempt at building a car company. His first two had failed — the Detroit Automobile Company collapsed after eighteen months, and the Henry Ford Company ended when Ford clashed with his investors over his obsession with racing cars instead of making production vehicles. He was forty years old. In Detroit's small manufacturing world, he was known as talented but difficult, a brilliant mechanic whose companies never quite worked.

The new Ford Motor Company started in a converted wagon factory. Ford and his team — including machinist C. Harold Wills and coal dealer Alexander Malcomson, the main backer — built cars in batches, letter-coded. The Model A sold for $850. It was simple, reliable, and worked. Within fifteen months, the company had sold 1,700 cars and turned a profit. But Ford was already thinking beyond incremental success.

The automobile market in 1903 was luxury goods. Cars cost what a house cost. They broke constantly. Only the wealthy could afford them, and only the mechanically inclined could keep them running. Ford saw something different: a vehicle so cheap and reliable that ordinary people — farmers, workers, the middle class — could own one. Not as a luxury. As a tool.

Between 1903 and 1908, Ford cycled through Models B, C, F, K, N, R, and S. Each iteration taught him something about simplification, durability, and cost. In 1908, he introduced the Model T. It was ugly, basic, high-clearance for rough rural roads, and sold for $825 — already cheaper than competitors. More importantly, it was designed for mass production. Ford had learned from bicycle manufacturers and meat packing plants: continuous flow, interchangeable parts, division of labor.

The Model T was an instant success. Demand overwhelmed supply. But Ford's real breakthrough came in 1913 at the Highland Park plant. His team implemented the moving assembly line — inspired by the "disassembly" lines of Chicago slaughterhouses. Workers stayed in place; the chassis moved. Assembly time for a Model T dropped from twelve hours to ninety-three minutes. Production costs collapsed. Ford dropped the price to $575. Then $440. Then $360.

In 1914, Ford announced that his workers would be paid $5 per day — roughly double the prevailing wage. Business leaders called him a socialist. His investors thought he was insane. Ford understood what they didn't: his workers were his customers. High wages meant low turnover, higher productivity, and a growing market for his cars. It was the first hint of the mass-consumption economy that would define the twentieth century.

By 1918, half of all cars in America were Model Ts. Ford stopped making anything else. You could have any color you wanted, as long as it was black — black paint dried fastest. The company printed money. Ford bought out his minority shareholders in 1919 for $105 million, giving him complete control. He built River Rouge, the largest integrated factory in the world — raw materials went in one end, finished cars came out the other. At its peak, Ford employed more than 100,000 workers.

But Henry Ford couldn't evolve. As the market matured in the 1920s, customers wanted style, comfort, options. General Motors, under Alfred Sloan, offered multiple brands at multiple price points, annual model changes, installment financing. Ford refused. The Model T was perfect. Why change it? By 1927, even Ford couldn't ignore that GM was winning. He shut down production for five months to retool. The Model A was a hit, but Ford had lost the lead forever.

Ford's rigidity extended to people. He built a private security force under Harry Bennett that intimidated workers and spied on executives. When the UAW tried to organize Ford plants in the 1930s, Bennett's men beat organizers bloody. Ford became an anti-Semitic crank, publishing conspiracy theories in his newspaper, The Dearborn Independent, accepting a medal from Nazi Germany in 1938. His son Edsel, groomed as successor, was undermined and humiliated at every turn. Talented executives left. Those who stayed learned to tell Ford what he wanted to hear.

World War II saved the company. Ford produced B-24 bombers, Jeeps, and engines, regaining technical credibility. But Edsel died in 1943, broken by his father's cruelty. Henry, eighty and declining, briefly reassumed control. The company bled talent and money. In 1945, Edsel's widow threatened to sell her shares unless Henry II — her son, just back from the Navy — took over. Henry Ford grudgingly agreed. He died in 1947.

Henry Ford II fired Harry Bennett immediately and brought in the "Whiz Kids" — former Air Force officers led by Robert McNamara — to professionalize management. Ford survived, thrived for decades, stumbled repeatedly, required government loans in 2009, and remains family-controlled in the twenty-first century.

Henry Ford invented modern manufacturing and helped create the middle class. He also built a company culture of paranoia and control that nearly destroyed what he created. The assembly line was genius. The inability to let go, to adapt, to trust others — that was the other path, the one that almost ended it all.
