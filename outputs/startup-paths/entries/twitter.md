# Twitter/X

***The town square that almost died, survived, and became a political weapon***

> **Years:** 2006–present

In March 2006, Jack Dorsey sent the first tweet: "just setting up my twttr." The platform emerged from Odeo, a failing podcasting startup whose founders—Dorsey, Evan Williams, and Biz Stone—needed a new idea after iTunes added podcasting support. They built a service where you could broadcast short status updates to your friends via SMS. The 140-character limit came from the constraints of text messaging. They called it obvious, simple, almost trivial.

For two years, Twitter was a curiosity. Tech insiders used it. Mainstream culture ignored it. The company had no business model, chronic technical failures, and a product that was difficult to explain. The "fail whale"—the error page showing a whale being lifted by birds—became Twitter's unofficial mascot because users saw it so often. The servers couldn't handle the traffic. The team couldn't decide what Twitter was for.

Then, in 2007, they set up screens at South by Southwest. Tweets from the conference scrolled past in real-time. Conference-goers started tweeting obsessively. Twitter usage tripled. The team won the festival's web award. More importantly, they discovered what Twitter was: a global conversation layer, a way to follow events as they happened through the eyes of people experiencing them.

The Iranian election protests of 2009 proved it. When the government blocked traditional media, protesters used Twitter to organize and broadcast to the world. The State Department asked Twitter to delay scheduled maintenance to keep the service running. Suddenly Twitter wasn't just a tech platform—it was infrastructure for global communication, for revolution, for collective attention.

The growth was explosive and chaotic. Celebrities joined. Media organizations built their strategies around it. The Arab Spring happened on Twitter. By 2013, the platform had 200 million active users and filed for an IPO. The company was valued at $31 billion on its first day of trading.

But Twitter never figured out how to make money at the scale its influence suggested it should. It sold ads, but users hated them and the targeting was weak. It tried promoted tweets, but engagement was low. Facebook printed money by knowing everything about its users. Twitter knew what you said publicly but couldn't monetize that into reliable revenue. Wall Street grew impatient. The stock price declined. User growth stalled around 300 million—massive but tiny compared to Facebook's billions.

Worse, the platform became toxic. Harassment campaigns proliferated. Coordinated abuse targeted women and minorities. Bots spread disinformation. State actors used Twitter to manipulate elections. The company's moderation was inconsistent and widely criticized from all sides. Twitter was simultaneously too censorious and not censorious enough, depending on who you asked. The global town square had become a shouting match and a weapon.

Leadership churned. Dorsey was ousted as CEO in 2008, returned in 2015. Dick Costolo led through the IPO years. Each regime promised to fix growth, fix revenue, fix toxicity. None succeeded completely. By 2020, activist investors were pushing for Dorsey's removal again. The board resisted, then didn't. In 2021, Dorsey stepped down and Parag Agrawal, the CTO, took over.

Four months later, Elon Musk bought 9% of the company. Then he offered to buy it all for $44 billion. Then he tried to back out, claiming Twitter had lied about the number of bots on the platform. Twitter sued to force the deal through. In October 2022, Musk completed the purchase, firing Agrawal and most of the executive team immediately.

What followed was unlike any acquisition in tech history. Musk fired roughly 80% of Twitter's employees, sometimes via late-night email. He reinstated banned accounts, including Donald Trump's. He charged for verification, destroying the system that distinguished real accounts from impersonators. He rebranded Twitter to "X," claiming it would become an "everything app." Advertisers fled. Revenue collapsed. The platform's reliability degraded. Musk tweeted conspiracy theories and attacked critics. Major brands left. Competitors like Threads and Bluesky emerged, founded by former Twitter employees.

Yet Twitter—X—persisted. Musk's changes drove some users away but activated others. The platform became more explicitly political, more chaotic, more central to certain communities and less relevant to others. Musk claimed X was worth less than half what he paid for it, but he also integrated it with his other companies and used it as a political weapon, openly supporting candidates and causes.

By 2024, X was unrecognizable from the Twitter of 2006, or even 2020. It had survived countless near-deaths: technical failures, revenue crises, leadership chaos, and an acquisition that would have destroyed most companies. It remained one of the most influential platforms in the world, shaping political discourse and breaking news, even as its cultural dominance fragmented and its business model remained unsolved.

The path Twitter took—from SMS experiment to revolution platform to cautionary tale to oligarch's megaphone—revealed something fundamental about social networks: they are not just products but public infrastructure, yet they are owned and controlled by private interests. Twitter's influence always exceeded its business success. Its outcome remains unwritten.
