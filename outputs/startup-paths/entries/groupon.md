# Groupon

***The fastest rocket ship that forgot where it was going***

> **Years:** 2008-present

Andrew Mason started The Point in 2008 with a lofty goal: collective action. The platform would let people organize around causes—campaigns would only execute if enough people committed. Save a business from closing. Boycott a company until it changed. Harness the power of numbers for social good.

Nobody used it.

Eric Lefkofsky, Mason's billionaire backer and former boss, wasn't funding philosophy. He wanted traction. In October 2008, as the financial system melted down, they noticed one thing worked: group buying for discounts. People would commit to a deal—half-price pizza, discounted spa treatments—if enough others did too. Get 100 people, trigger the deal, everyone wins.

They built it in a weekend. Called it Groupon—a portmanteau so obvious it hurt. One deal per day, per city. The merchant got guaranteed customers. Consumers got 50% off or more. Groupon took half the revenue. Simple.

It was a rocket ship.

Chicago loved it. Then Boston, then New York. Every city they entered exploded. The model was perfect for 2009: consumers desperate to save money, businesses desperate for customers, both willing to try anything. The daily email became a ritual. Office workers debated whether to pull the trigger before the timer ran out.

Mason, quirky and self-deprecating, became the anti-CEO. He wrote deal descriptions himself—rambling, weird, funny. "The Andes are a series of mountains in South America that have absolutely nothing to do with today's deal." People forwarded the emails just for the copy. The product worked because it was entertaining.

The unit economics seemed magical. No inventory, no logistics. Sales teams signed up merchants. Engineers built city-specific sites. Marketing was word-of-mouth and the deals themselves. Revenue scaled linearly with cities. They raised $135 million in 2010. Then $950 million. Then turned down a $6 billion acquisition offer from Google.

That was December 2010. Groupon was two years old and suddenly worth more than most companies ever reach. They had conquered 35 countries. Revenue was projected to hit $1 billion faster than any company in history.

The IPO came in November 2011. The company was now worth $12.7 billion. Mason rang the opening bell in a t-shirt. Groupon had 10,000 employees. The model was being copied everywhere, but they had the brand, the scale, the sales force. They were going to own local commerce.

The problems were already visible to anyone looking closely.

Merchants hated them. The deals attracted bargain hunters who never returned. Businesses lost money on every transaction—half to Groupon, the rest barely covering cost. Restaurants got slammed by Groupon customers who tipped on the discounted price, not the full value. Repeat business was a myth. Within a year, most merchants refused to run a second deal.

Customer acquisition costs were insane. Groupon spent more on sales and marketing than it made in gross profit. They were buying growth, not building a sustainable business. The company was profitable on paper only because of accounting tricks that classified marketing as an investment. When forced to restate, the losses were staggering.

The international expansion was chaos. They'd copied the blitzscaling playbook without the infrastructure to support it. Country managers operated independently, sometimes fraudulently. Quality control was nonexistent. Deals went live for businesses that didn't exist. Customer service couldn't keep up.

Competitors swarmed. LivingSocial raised $650 million and launched everywhere Groupon was. Dozens of clones—dozens—appeared overnight. The only differentiation was who could spend more to acquire customers. The market became a race to the bottom.

Mason tried to evolve. They acquired companies, launched Groupon Goods (physical products at a discount), built a reservation system, tested subscription models. Nothing worked. The core business was deteriorating, and the pivots felt desperate.

The board fired Mason in February 2013, less than 18 months after the IPO. His farewell memo was quintessentially him: "After four and a half intense and wonderful years as CEO of Groupon, I've decided that I'd like to spend more time with my family. Just kidding—I was fired today."

The stock, which had peaked at $26, was now at $4. It would eventually fall below $2.

What followed was a slow dissolution. New CEOs tried to fix the model. They couldn't. The fundamental problem was unfixable: the product destroyed value for merchants while training consumers to never pay full price. Groupon was a tax on desperation, and desperation isn't a sustainable market segment.

The company survived, but barely. By 2020, the pandemic—which should have made deals more relevant—nearly killed them. Merchants weren't open. Consumers weren't going out. The stock traded under $1. The company was delisted from major indices.

Today Groupon exists, shrunken and mostly forgotten. The daily deal is still there, still structured the same way, still offering 50% off things nobody needs. The market cap hovers around $500 million, down from $12 billion at its peak.

The story wasn't about the model being too early. It was too hollow. Groupon scaled before proving it created value for anyone but itself. They grew faster than any company in history by borrowing from the future—paying for customers today who would never come back tomorrow. When the bill came due, there was nothing underneath.

The fastest rocket ship in history, it turned out, was just falling with style.
