# Rappi

***The company that turned Latin America's chaos into a delivery empire***

> **Years:** 2015-present

Simón Borrero, Sebastián Mejía, and Felipe Villamarín started Rappi in 2015 in Bogotá, Colombia, with a simple premise: in Latin America, everything takes too long and nothing works reliably. Banks are bureaucratic, formal retail is limited, and logistics infrastructure is decades behind developed markets. What looked like disadvantages to most investors looked like opportunity to them.

The initial idea was narrow: deliver anything in under an hour. Not restaurant food. Not groceries specifically. Anything. A bottle of wine, a phone charger, cigarettes, cash from an ATM, a birthday cake at midnight. They called their couriers "Rappitenderos" and gave them a mandate that bordered on absurd: if a customer requests it and it's legal, figure out how to deliver it.

This wasn't Uber Eats or Instacart with a Latin American skin. Those services assumed existing retail infrastructure — restaurants with digital ordering systems, grocery stores with inventory management, addresses that map software could parse. Rappi assumed none of that existed. They built backward from chaos.

The founders recruited their first Rappitenderos from motorcycle taxi drivers, a gray-market workforce that already knew how to navigate Bogotá's anarchic traffic. They launched with no restaurant partnerships and no retail agreements. A customer would order. A Rappitendero would buy it with a Rappi credit card, photograph the receipt, and deliver it. The unit economics made no sense. The logistics were manual. But it worked.

Within months, they realized something: Latin America's informal economy was massive, unbanked, and hungry for digital tools. Street vendors, small restaurants, corner stores — they all wanted access to customers but had no way to reach them. Rappi became the interface between informal commerce and smartphone users. They weren't just a delivery app. They were infrastructure.

In 2016, they raised $2 million and expanded to Mexico. The playbook was the same: hire local couriers, enable cash payment, deliver anything. By 2017, they had raised $100 million from Andreessen Horowitz and DST Global. Not because the metrics were pretty — they were burning cash at a staggering rate — but because the vision was clear. Rappi was building the operating system for commerce in a region where one didn't exist.

They expanded vertically. RappiBank offered credit and payment services to the unbanked. RappiTravel booked buses and flights. RappiCash let users withdraw money from ATMs via courier. RappiPay became a digital wallet. Every product attacked a different friction point in daily life. Critics called it unfocused. Borrero called it inevitable. In markets where infrastructure is broken, you have to build everything.

The growth was exponential but messy. City launches were chaotic. Couriers sometimes fought with restaurant staff. Fraud was rampant — both from users and Rappitenderos. But Rappi's advantage was speed. They iterated faster than competitors, absorbed losses incumbents couldn't stomach, and treated regulation as a problem to negotiate around rather than a constraint to respect.

By 2018, they were in nine countries and raising a $200 million Series D led by DST Global. Valuation hit $1 billion. Rappi was Latin America's first delivery unicorn, and the narrative shifted. No longer a scrappy Colombian startup, they were the champion that might actually compete with global giants like Uber and Deliveroo expanding into the region.

But the competitive landscape intensified. Uber Eats poured money into Mexico and Brazil. iFood, backed by Naspers, dominated Brazil. DiDi Food entered with Chinese capital and Chinese playbooks. Every market became a subsidy war. Discounts spiraled. Driver wages rose. Margins, already negative, cratered further.

Rappi's response was to go bigger. In 2019, they raised $1 billion in a Series E led by SoftBank's Vision Fund, hitting a $3.5 billion valuation. The capital was a weapon. They could outlast competitors in the burn race. They expanded into convenience stores, dark stores, and ghost kitchens. They launched Turbo, a subscription service for unlimited free delivery. They added pharmacies, pet supplies, and electronics.

The pandemic was a paradox. Demand exploded — delivery became essential overnight. But so did costs. Every competitor saw the same surge. Labor became scarce. Regulations tightened. Governments suddenly cared about gig worker conditions. The gold rush was crowded, and margins stayed brutal.

SoftBank's blessing became a curse when the Vision Fund imploded. Growth-at-all-costs fell out of fashion. Profitability became the metric that mattered. Rappi, built to expand infinitely on cheap capital, had to become a real business. They cut unprofitable cities, reduced discounts, raised prices, and laid off staff.

In 2021, they raised $500 million more at a $5.25 billion valuation — up, but not the hypergrowth trajectory SoftBank deals implied. The message was clear: they were too big to fail but too messy to thrive. By 2022, layoffs hit 6% of corporate staff. Borrero stepped back from day-to-day operations.

The path to exit narrowed. An IPO seemed distant — public markets had soured on unprofitable delivery companies. Acquisition was complicated — potential buyers like Uber or Amazon faced antitrust scrutiny. A slow grind toward profitability while fending off competitors became the likely future.

Today, Rappi operates across nine countries, facilitates billions in transactions, and remains the largest delivery platform in Latin America outside Brazil. It has not exited. It has not failed. It has become the thing it set out to disrupt: essential infrastructure, too embedded to remove, too subscale to dominate, locked in a permanent war of attrition with rivals who won't surrender.

The story of Rappi is the story of seeing chaos as canvas. They built when others waited for infrastructure to arrive. But infrastructure isn't profitable. It's necessary. And necessity doesn't always make you rich.
