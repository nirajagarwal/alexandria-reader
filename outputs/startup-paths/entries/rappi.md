# Rappi

***The Latin American super-app that conquered chaos through chaos***

> **Years:** 2015-present

In 2015, three Colombians—Simón Borrero, Sebastián Mejía, and Felipe Villamarín—sat in a Bogotá apartment wrestling with a problem that plagued their daily lives. They wanted to order groceries, pick up cash from an ATM, pay bills, buy movie tickets. Each task required a different app, a different intermediary, a different hassle. The city's infrastructure made simple errands complex. Traffic was a nightmare. Banks had limited hours. Retail was fragmented across thousands of small shops.

What if one app could do everything? What if a fleet of motorcycle couriers—rappitenderos—could become your personal assistant for literally anything you needed in a city?

The idea was audacious. Most successful startups focus obsessively. Rappi would do the opposite. It would be deliberately unfocused—a super-app for urban Latin America that could fulfill any request within an hour. Need groceries? Done. Medicine at 2am? On the way. Cash delivered to your door? Why not. The breadth was the point.

They launched in Bogotá with a simple promise: order anything, get it in minutes. The first rappitenderos were university students on motorcycles, many of whom had never done delivery work. The app was buggy. Logistics were improvised. But in a city where infrastructure constantly failed you, where getting anything done required navigating traffic and bureaucracy and closed shops, Rappi was magic.

The model worked because it embraced Latin America's informal economy rather than fighting it. While American delivery companies tried to optimize route algorithms and warehouse inventory, Rappi weaponized chaos. Rappitenderos became local problem-solvers. Customer wants something not in the app? The rappitendero calls the restaurant directly, negotiates, figures it out. Store is closed? They know another store. This wasn't inefficiency—it was competitive advantage.

Word spread virally. Young professionals in Bogotá downloaded the app and discovered they could eliminate hours of daily frustration. Order breakfast while showering. Get documents notarized without leaving the office. Have someone wait in line at the bank. In cities where time was eaten by infrastructure failure, Rappi sold time back.

By 2016, they had expanded to other Colombian cities and raised $2 million. But Borrero had bigger ambitions. He saw that Latin America's dysfunction created opportunity. The region's retail was fragmented, its logistics primitive, its digital payments adoption low. A super-app could leapfrog decades of infrastructure development. While developed markets had specialized apps for everything, Latin America could skip straight to the everything-app.

In 2017, Rappi raised $50 million from Andreessen Horowitz. Marc Andreessen called it one of the most impressive teams he'd encountered—founders who understood their market's unique dynamics in ways Silicon Valley couldn't replicate. The funding fueled aggressive expansion across nine countries: Mexico, Argentina, Chile, Peru, Ecuador, Uruguay, Brazil, Costa Rica, and Colombia.

Each market was different. Mexico City's middle class wanted restaurant delivery. São Paulo's elite wanted groceries. Santiago wanted pharmacies. Rappi's chaotic, do-anything model adapted to each. They opened dark stores—micro-warehouses stocked with high-demand items. They partnered with thousands of restaurants and shops. They offered financial services—credit, bill payment, phone recharges. The super-app vision was becoming real.

But expansion was brutally expensive. Rappitenderos earned commissions, not salaries, but the company subsidized heavily to keep prices low and volumes high. Delivery was loss-making. Rappi was spending to buy market share, betting it could achieve network effects before running out of capital. Restaurants needed couriers; couriers needed orders; users needed selection. Get all three spinning fast enough, and the economics would eventually work.

Competitors emerged. Uber Eats, iFood, and PedidosYa fought for the same customers. Price wars erupted. Rappi responded by going broader—adding more services, more verticals, more reasons to open the app. They launched RappiCredit, lending to users. RappiFavor, for personal errands. RappiCash, for ATM withdrawals. The strategy was to become indispensable through ubiquity.

In 2018, they raised $200 million at a $1 billion valuation. In 2019, another $1 billion led by SoftBank's Vision Fund at a $3.5 billion valuation. Rappi was Latin America's fastest-growing startup, delivering 10 million orders monthly across 250 cities.

Then COVID-19 hit. Lockdowns transformed Rappi from convenience to necessity. Order volumes exploded. Groceries, medicine, essential goods—suddenly everyone needed delivery. Revenue surged 300%. But so did costs. Demand overwhelmed logistics. Rappitenderos worked grueling hours. The app crashed under load. The chaos that had been charming became critical infrastructure under stress.

The pandemic also exposed deeper tensions. Rappitenderos protested working conditions—no benefits, no job security, all risk. Governments in Colombia, Chile, and Mexico began pushing for gig worker protections. Rappi's unit economics, already shaky, faced regulatory pressure that could mandate employee classification.

SoftBank, hemorrhaging from other Vision Fund disasters, turned skeptical. The 2021 funding round came at a $5.25 billion valuation—growth, but slower than expected. Rappi started cutting. They pulled out of Argentina. Consolidated operations. Focused on profitability over expansion.

By 2023, the company faced a crossroads. The super-app vision had worked—Rappi had 30 million users, partnerships with 350,000 merchants, delivery in 300 cities. But it still wasn't profitable. Competitors had deep pockets. Regulations tightened. Capital markets punished growth-at-all-costs.

The founders doubled down on financial services. RappiCard became a full banking product—credit cards, savings accounts, investments. The bet was that in Latin America's underbanked markets, Rappi could become a fintech platform that happened to do delivery, rather than a delivery company that happened to offer financial services. The margins were better. The lock-in was stronger.

Today, Rappi remains private and unprofitable but dominant in Colombia and growing in Mexico and Brazil. It processes billions in transactions annually. It has become infrastructure—the way millions of Latin Americans interact with their cities, buy groceries, pay bills, get cash.

The outcome is still being written. Rappi could become the WeChat of Latin America—the app that does everything, the platform that owns the customer relationship. Or it could be outspent by better-capitalized competitors, crushed by regulations, or suffocated by its own complexity. The chaos that enabled its rise might ultimately consume it.

What's certain is that Rappi saw something others missed: in places where infrastructure is broken, the company that embraces the chaos—rather than imposing order—can win. The question is whether chaos scales.
