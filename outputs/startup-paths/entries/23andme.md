# 23andMe

***The privacy promise that became a data trap***

> **Years:** 2006-present

In 2006, Anne Wojcicki saw a market inefficiency so obvious it seemed criminal. Healthcare was a trillion-dollar industry built on reactive treatment, yet the human genome — sequenced just three years earlier — offered the possibility of predictive, personalized medicine. The data existed. The technology was plummeting in cost. But patients couldn't access their own genetic information without a doctor's permission, and doctors had little incentive to order tests for healthy people.

Wojcicki wasn't a scientist. She was a Wall Street analyst who'd spent years studying healthcare companies and concluded the entire industry was designed to keep patients in the dark. Her co-founders brought the technical credibility: Linda Avey had worked in genomics, and Paul Cusenza in bioinformatics. They raised $3.9 million from venture firms and launched with a vision that merged consumer empowerment with scientific ambition: sell genetic tests directly to customers, build the world's largest genetic database, and use that data to revolutionize drug discovery.

The early product was expensive — $999 for a spit kit that told you about ancestry, disease risk, and traits like earwax type. But it hit the cultural moment perfectly. This was peak Web 2.0, when "democratizing information" was gospel and users happily traded data for services. Time Magazine named 23andMe's kit "Invention of the Year" in 2008. Wojcicki's marriage to Google co-founder Sergey Brin didn't hurt the buzz. Google Ventures invested $3.9 million in 2007.

The business model was elegant in theory. Phase one: sell tests, build database. Phase two: monetize the database through pharmaceutical partnerships. Customers would get cheap genetic insights. Drug companies would get unprecedented research data. 23andMe would sit in the middle, collecting revenue from both sides.

The FDA had other ideas. In 2013, after years of informal warnings, the agency ordered 23andMe to stop marketing health-related genetic tests. The company had been providing information about disease risk — including for serious conditions like breast cancer and Alzheimer's — without proving the tests were accurate or that customers could interpret them correctly. 23andMe kept selling ancestry tests but had to gut the health reports that justified the $99 price point.

The ban lasted four years. During that time, 23andMe made a strategic choice that would define its future: it pivoted hard into the pharmaceutical partnership model. In 2015, it launched a therapeutics division. By 2018, it had partnered with GlaxoSmithKline in a $300 million deal giving GSK access to 23andMe's genetic database — now containing data from over 5 million customers who'd consented to research participation.

The FDA gradually relented. Between 2017 and 2019, 23andMe won approval to offer certain health reports again. The company went public via SPAC in 2021 at a $3.5 billion valuation. Wojcicki framed the listing as vindication: they'd survived the regulatory gauntlet and built exactly what they'd promised — a genetic database powering drug discovery.

But the math had shifted underneath them. The cost of genetic sequencing kept falling, which meant tests kept getting cheaper — good for customers, bad for margins. Competition intensified from Ancestry.com and others. By 2021, most people who wanted a consumer genetics test had already bought one. The market was saturated.

Worse, the pharmaceutical partnerships weren't producing blockbuster returns. Drug discovery is slow and failure-prone even with perfect data. 23andMe's database, while large, had sampling problems: customers were overwhelmingly white, female, and affluent. The therapeutics division burned cash without bringing products to market.

Then came the part Wojcicki probably didn't anticipate in 2006: customers got scared. Initially, people had trusted 23andMe with their genetic data because the company promised it would never be shared without consent. But after the GSK deal, privacy advocates started asking harder questions. What happens to your data if 23andMe gets acquired? Goes bankrupt? Gets hacked? In 2023, a data breach exposed information on 6.9 million users — not their raw genetic data, but enough to spook the market.

The stock collapsed. By late 2024, shares that traded at $17.65 on SPAC debut were under $5. The company announced layoffs and shut down its therapeutics division. The board resigned en masse over disagreements about strategy. Wojcicki took the company private in a desperate attempt to avoid delisting.

The irony is sharp. 23andMe succeeded at its original mission: it built a massive genetic database and proved consumer genomics could work. Over 14 million people have used its service. The company generated real scientific insights about disease and human variation. But success at the mission didn't translate to a sustainable business.

The problem wasn't the science or the vision. It was the model itself. Consumer genetics turned out to be a one-time purchase in a market that reached saturation within a decade. The database — the supposed moat — was only valuable if customers trusted you with it, and trust is fragile when your business model requires monetizing their most intimate data. Drug companies would pay for access, but not enough to justify a multi-billion dollar valuation.

Wojcicki had promised to democratize genetic information. She did. But what she built was worth far less than what it cost to build. The data is still there, sitting on servers, representing millions of people who thought they were buying personalized health insights. What they actually bought was a seat on a platform whose business model required turning their DNA into someone else's product.
