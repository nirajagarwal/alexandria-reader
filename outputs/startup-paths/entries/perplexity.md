# Perplexity

***The search engine that thought it could skip Google's decades***

> **Years:** 2022-present

In August 2022, when Aravind Srinivas and three co-founders launched Perplexity, the graveyard of Google competitors stretched back two decades. Cuil, Powerset, Blekko, Neeva — each had tried to dethrone search and failed. The pattern was clear: better technology wasn't enough. Google had the index, the infrastructure, the habit, and the ad machinery that printed money. You couldn't beat them by being incrementally better. You had to make search feel obsolete.

Srinivas had been at OpenAI, watching GPT-3 reshape how people interacted with information. His co-founders — Denis Yarats, Johnny Ho, and Andy Konwinski — came from DeepMind, Meta, and Databricks. They'd seen the AI wave building. They saw something Google couldn't see from inside its own castle: the search box was dying. Not because people stopped asking questions, but because they wanted answers, not ten blue links. They wanted conversation, not keyword matching. They wanted synthesis, not a scavenger hunt.

The first version of Perplexity was deceptively simple. You typed a question. It gave you an answer with citations. That was it. No ads. No SEO spam. No "People also ask" boxes filled with tangentially related garbage. Just the answer, sourced, clean. It felt like the future because it was what search should have been all along.

The timing was perfect and terrible. Perfect because ChatGPT launched three months later and suddenly everyone understood what conversational AI could do. Terrible because ChatGPT had OpenAI's brand, Microsoft's billions, and a product that felt magical even when it hallucinated. Perplexity had to compete in a space that went from empty to crowded overnight.

But Perplexity had something ChatGPT didn't: citations. Every answer was grounded in sources. You could verify. You could trace. This mattered to people who wanted truth, not plausible-sounding fiction. While ChatGPT users discovered their AI confidently made things up, Perplexity users got answers they could trust — or at least check.

The growth was explosive by normal startup standards, glacial by AI-hype standards. By early 2023, they had millions of queries per month. By late 2023, tens of millions. The product felt fast, accurate, useful. Power users — researchers, journalists, developers — started switching. Not abandoning Google entirely, but adding Perplexity to their workflow for the questions that mattered.

The money followed. NEA led a $26 million Series A in March 2023. By January 2024, IVP and NEA put in $73.6 million at a $520 million valuation. By April 2024, another $63 million at over $1 billion. By summer, whispers of $250 million more at $3 billion. In eighteen months, Perplexity went from zero to unicorn. The investors weren't betting on revenue — they were betting on displacement. If AI search could take even 10% of Google's market, it was worth tens of billions.

Then came the complications. In June 2024, Forbes and Wired accused Perplexity of plagiarism. The AI was scraping their content, summarizing it, and serving answers that kept users from ever visiting the original sites. Perplexity's defense — that it cited sources — rang hollow to publishers who saw their traffic evaporate. By October, News Corp sent a cease-and-desist. The New York Times was reportedly preparing legal action. Perplexity launched a "Publishers Program" offering revenue share, but most major outlets refused. They'd seen this movie before with Google News. They weren't interested in crumbs.

The deeper problem was structural. Perplexity's value proposition — get the answer without leaving — was existential threat to the web's economic model. Publishers made money from ads and subscriptions on their sites. If AI gave you the answer directly, why click through? Perplexity could cite sources all day, but if citations didn't drive traffic, they were just fig leaves. The open web had survived Google because Google still sent traffic. AI search threatened to break that bargain.

Meanwhile, Google wasn't standing still. In May 2024, they launched AI Overviews — answers at the top of search results, powered by Gemini. It was clunky at first, telling users to put glue on pizza and eat rocks. But Google had infinite resources to improve it. They also had the index, the distribution, the Chrome default, the Android integration. Perplexity was fast and clean, but Google was everywhere.

By late 2024, Perplexity had 100 million weekly queries and 15 million monthly active users. Impressive for a two-year-old startup. A rounding error for Google. They launched Perplexity Pages, letting users create AI-generated research reports. They added voice search, image search, shopping. They were expanding fast, chasing product-market fit in multiple directions at once.

The question wasn't whether Perplexity worked. It did. The question was whether working was enough. Search is a natural monopoly. The best search engine gets more users, more data, better results, more users. Google spent twenty years building that flywheel. Perplexity thought AI could reset the game, create a new kind of search where the old rules didn't apply.

But the old rules still applied to publishers. They still applied to regulation — the FTC was circling. They still applied to unit economics — AI inference was expensive, search needed scale. And they still applied to distribution. Perplexity could be better, but Google was default.

Srinivas talks about building "the world's best answer engine." It's a telling phrase. Not search engine — answer engine. The bet is that the future isn't about finding information, it's about synthesizing it. That Google's core product — the ten blue links — is already dead, people just don't know it yet.

Maybe he's right. Maybe in five years, search means conversation and synthesis, and Google's index is just raw material for AI. Maybe Perplexity captures that future. Or maybe search is harder to disrupt than it looks. Maybe the entrenched player adapts fast enough. Maybe the legal and economic problems are insurmountable. Maybe another startup with more funding and better distribution takes the space.

Perplexity is live ammunition in the battle to reinvent search. Whether it becomes the weapon that wins the war or just another casualty depends on questions without easy answers: Can you kill Google without killing the web? Can you scale AI inference to Google's query volume? Can you become a habit when the competition is already muscle memory? The company is two years old, worth billions on paper, and still figuring out what it wants to be when it grows up. The only certainty is that staying still means death.
