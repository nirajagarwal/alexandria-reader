# Perplexity

***The search engine that answered by reading the whole internet***

> **Years:** 2022–present

In December 2022, when ChatGPT was barely a month old and the world was still figuring out what to do with suddenly-capable AI, four former researchers launched a product that seemed almost too simple: a search engine that answered questions by actually reading sources and citing them. Aravind Srinivas, Denis Yarats, Johnny Ho, and Andy Konwinski built Perplexity with a straightforward thesis—people don't want ten blue links, they want answers with receipts.

Srinivas had been at OpenAI during the GPT-3 era, watching the technology develop. He'd seen researchers use large language models to summarize and synthesize information, but the outputs were often confident and wrong—hallucinations dressed as facts. The insight wasn't that AI could search better than Google. It was that AI could read search results better than humans could, if you forced it to show its work.

The first version of Perplexity was almost deliberately minimal. You asked a question. It searched the internet, read the top results, synthesized an answer, and showed you exactly which sources it used for each claim. No algorithmic feed, no ads between you and the answer, no SEO-poisoned content farms gaming their way to the top. Just the question, the answer, and the citations.

It launched into a market that looked impossible to crack. Google had 90% search share and two decades of infrastructure. Bing had Microsoft's resources and had just integrated ChatGPT. Dozens of AI startups were building question-answering systems. But Perplexity had noticed something: Google's business model made it structurally unable to give direct answers. Every answer needed to preserve the click to the website, preserve the ad impression, preserve the ecosystem that generated $200 billion in annual revenue.

The early growth was word-of-mouth and demo-driven. Developers and researchers started using it because it was faster than reading papers themselves. Students discovered it gave better explanations than ChatGPT because it pulled from current sources. Journalists found it useful for quick research. Within three months, Perplexity had a million users. Within six months, ten million.

The product evolved quickly. They added "Copilot" mode, which asked clarifying questions before searching. They launched mobile apps that made it faster to ask questions than to type into Google. They added the ability to search specific domains—academic papers, Reddit discussions, YouTube transcripts. They introduced "Spaces" for collaborative research. Each feature pushed against the same constraint: make getting the right answer easier than getting a list of links.

The money came fast. Jeff Bezos invested in the seed round. Bessemer led a Series A in March 2023 at a $150 million valuation. By January 2024, they raised a Series B at $520 million. By April, they'd done another round at over $1 billion, with Bezos doubling down and Daniel Gross, Nvidia, and others joining. The startup that launched fifteen months earlier was now valued like a late-stage company.

The revenue model was subscription, not advertising. Perplexity Pro gave you more queries, access to newer models, and the ability to upload documents for analysis. It was the inverse of Google's model—charge users for a better product rather than selling their attention to advertisers. Within a year, they had paying subscribers and a clear path to profitability, even as they kept the free tier generous enough to grow the user base.

Then came the lawsuits. In October 2024, News Corp sued Perplexity for copyright infringement, claiming the AI was reproducing paywalled content without permission. Dow Jones and the New York Post joined. The complaints argued that Perplexity was building a business on content it didn't license, using AI to launder copyright violation through summarization. In December, the New York Times filed suit as well, lumping Perplexity in with OpenAI and Microsoft as companies training on journalism without compensation.

Perplexity's defense was that it was no different from Google showing snippets, that it cited sources and drove traffic, that fair use protected transformative synthesis. But the publishers saw it differently—Google showed snippets to get you to click through; Perplexity showed enough that you never needed to. The business model that made Perplexity better for users made it existentially threatening to publishers.

The company kept growing through the controversy. By late 2024, they'd hit 100 million queries per week. They'd partnered with SoundHound to power voice assistants and with telecommunications companies to integrate into their platforms. Srinivas became a visible figure in AI—articulate about the technology, unapologetic about disrupting search, willing to argue publicly with critics.

The question hanging over Perplexity isn't whether it works—it clearly does—but whether the path it's on is sustainable. The copyright fights aren't nuisance suits; they're challenges to the core business. If publishers win, Perplexity either licenses content at prohibitive cost or stops being able to answer questions about news and current events. If publishers lose, the precedent could accelerate the collapse of the ad-supported content ecosystem that still funds most journalism and creative work on the internet.

Perplexity found a genuine opening—the gap between what search engines could do and what users wanted them to do. They built a product people loved by making AI do the work of reading and synthesizing that humans didn't want to do themselves. Whether that work can be done without breaking the economics of the content it depends on is the problem they're solving now. The answers they're looking for aren't in their training data.
