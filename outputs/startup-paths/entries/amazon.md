# Amazon

***From online bookstore to everything store***

> **Years:** 1994-present

Jeff Bezos was a senior vice president at D.E. Shaw, a quantitative hedge fund in New York, when he noticed a number that changed his life: the internet was growing at 2,300 percent per year. It was 1994. He was thirty years old. He made a list of twenty products that could be sold online, ranked them by feasibility, and concluded that books made the most sense. The catalog was enormous — millions of titles in print — yet no physical bookstore could stock more than a fraction. An online store could offer selection that physical retail could never match. He called his wife MacKenzie, quit his job, and they drove west to Seattle while he typed a business plan on a laptop.

He named the company after the world's largest river. The domain Cadabra.com briefly existed but was abandoned when someone misheard it as "cadaver." Amazon.com launched from a garage in Bellevue in July 1995. Bezos held meetings at a Barnes & Noble cafe, using the competition's space to plan its disruption. The first employee was Shel Kaphan, a programmer who built the site. Early orders came from all fifty states and forty-five countries in the first thirty days. They rang a bell each time an order came in. Within weeks, the bell never stopped, so they retired it.

The site was crude but functional. It had reviews — user-generated content before that was a concept people named. It had recommendations based on what other customers bought. It had selection that physical stores couldn't touch. The warehouse was chaotic. Everyone packed books on hands and knees until someone suggested buying packing tables. Bezos installed a neon sign reading "Amazon.com" visible from the highway, annoying neighbors but announcing arrival.

Funding came from family, then angels, then Kleiner Perkins in 1996 — eight million dollars at a valuation that made early angels very rich. The company went public in May 1997 at $18 per share, raising $54 million. Skeptics abounded. Barron's ran a cover: "Amazon.bomb." The thesis: books were low-margin, shipping was expensive, Barnes & Noble would crush them online, the valuation was absurd. Bezos wrote an annual shareholder letter explaining that Amazon would focus on long-term growth over short-term profits, would make bold investments, and would fail often. It was a warning and a manifesto. Most ignored it.

The expansion began immediately. Music, then videos, then toys, then electronics. Critics called it unfocused. Bezos called it inevitable. The vision was never books. It was everything. Infrastructure investments were massive — warehouses, logistics, technology. Losses mounted. The stock collapsed during the dot-com crash, falling from $106 to $6 by 2001. Layoffs came. The press wrote obituaries. Bezos kept building.

Two insights saved and transformed the company. First: the infrastructure built to sell products could be rented to others. Amazon Web Services launched in 2006, offering cloud computing to startups and enterprises. It became more profitable than retail. Second: the platform could host third-party sellers, turning Amazon from a retailer into a marketplace. By 2023, third-party sales accounted for sixty percent of units sold. Amazon took a cut, provided logistics, and avoided inventory risk. The flywheel spun faster.

The Kindle arrived in 2007, sold out in six hours, and redefined book publishing. Amazon Prime launched in 2005 — unlimited two-day shipping for $79 annually — and seemed insane until it wasn't. Prime created lock-in. Members spent more, shopped more often, tolerated price increases. Video and music were added to justify the rising fee. By 2024, Prime had over 200 million members worldwide.

Whole Foods was acquired in 2017 for $13.7 billion, bringing physical retail and groceries into the empire. Amazon entered pharmacy, healthcare, logistics for third parties, advertising, satellite internet. Each move leveraged existing infrastructure. Each expansion triggered regulatory scrutiny and competitor panic.

The culture was notoriously intense. The "leadership principles" were real. Frugality was doctrine — door desks as symbol. Bezos read every customer complaint email forwarded with a "?" and expected answers. Meetings began with silent reading of six-page memos. PowerPoint was banned. Data ruled decisions. Employees burned out or thrived. Turnover was high. Performance was Darwinian.

Bezos stepped down as CEO in 2021, becoming executive chairman. Andy Jassy, who built AWS, took over. Bezos turned attention to Blue Origin, his space company, and The Washington Post, which he bought in 2013. By then Amazon employed 1.5 million people, generated over $500 billion in annual revenue, and held a market cap above $1.5 trillion.

The company faced antitrust investigations in the U.S. and Europe, accused of anti-competitive practices — favoring its own products, using seller data against them, squeezing suppliers. Unions organized in warehouses, citing brutal conditions and surveillance. The outcomes remained unresolved, but the scrutiny was constant.

Amazon's path was never one thing. It was books, then everything, then infrastructure, then marketplace, then media, then logistics provider. Each layer built on the last. Each expansion seemed like distraction until it became dominance. The original insight — infinite selection, customer obsession — never changed. The execution compounded. The critics who predicted failure in 2000 were correct about the risks and wrong about the will. Bezos believed scale would eventually overcome losses. He was patient when capital was cheap and ruthless when it mattered. The everything store became everything because it refused to stay a bookstore.
