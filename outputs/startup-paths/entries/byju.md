# Byju's

***The unicorn that devoured itself***

> **Years:** 2011-present

In 2011, Byju Raveendran was teaching entrance exam prep classes in stadiums across India. A former engineer and teacher, he'd developed a reputation for making complex math problems comprehensible to thousands of students at once. His sessions filled cricket stadiums. Students traveled across states to attend. Parents begged for recordings. The insight was simple but profound: in India's hyper-competitive education system, where entrance exams determined futures, great teaching at scale was worth more than gold.

Byju's Think & Learn was founded to capture that magic on tablets. The early product was video lessons — Raveendran himself on screen, working through problems with the charisma that had filled those stadiums. The company targeted students preparing for IIT-JEE and other engineering entrance exams, the gatekeepers to middle-class success in India. The model was freemium: try the lessons, then pay ₹20,000-40,000 ($250-500) for full access. In a country where parents would mortgage houses for their children's education, this wasn't expensive. It was essential.

The product worked. Students loved it. Completion rates were high. Test scores improved. By 2015, Byju's had hundreds of thousands of paying users and real revenue. Then came the pivot that would define everything: expansion into K-12 education. Not just exam prep anymore, but core schooling for grades 4-12. The addressable market exploded from millions to hundreds of millions.

Investors noticed. Chan Zuckerberg Initiative led a $50 million round in 2016. Sequoia, Lightspeed, and Tencent followed. The valuations climbed: $800 million in 2017, $3.6 billion in 2018, $5.7 billion in 2019. Byju's became India's most valuable startup, the edtech darling of emerging markets. The narrative was irresistible: democratizing education in a country desperate for it, technology enabling social mobility, and a charismatic founder-teacher who understood pedagogy and business.

But the growth engine that powered those valuations wasn't the product — it was sales. Byju's built an aggressive field sales force that operated like a swarm. Sales teams would visit homes, demonstrate the app to parents, and push for immediate purchases. The tactics grew increasingly aggressive: high-pressure closes, loan financing arranged on the spot, contracts signed before parents fully understood the terms. Families took on debt to buy courses their children sometimes never completed. The sales force wasn't selling education; they were moving product. Revenue exploded, but so did customer complaints.

The pandemic seemed like vindication. Schools closed. Online education became mandatory overnight. Byju's raised $1.2 billion in 2020-2021. The valuation hit $16.5 billion, then $22 billion. With capital flooding in, the company went on an acquisition spree: Aakash Educational Services for $950 million, Epic for $500 million, Great Learning for $600 million, Tynker, Toppr, Scholar, HashLearn. Fifteen acquisitions in two years. Each promised synergy, market expansion, or geographic reach. Each added complexity, overhead, and integration challenges.

Then Byju's made its boldest bet: Whitehat Jr, purchased for $300 million in 2019. Whitehat taught coding to kids, promising six-year-olds they could build apps and make money. The marketing was spectacular and misleading — ads showing children as young as six becoming app developers and earning money, claims of job opportunities for child coders. When critics called out the deceptive advertising, Byju's responded with legal threats rather than accountability. The brand began to crack.

By 2022, the wheels were coming off. Revenue growth slowed. The delayed FY21 financials, when finally released, showed ₹4,588 crore in losses on ₹2,280 crore in revenue — losing twice what they earned. Auditor Deloitte resigned, citing concerns about financial reporting. Investors who'd pumped in billions started asking hard questions. Three board members resigned. The company laid off thousands while Raveendran continued insisting growth was just ahead.

The 2023 collapse was slow-motion and spectacular. Byju's failed to file FY22 financials on time. Lenders accused the company of concealing money and demanded immediate repayment of a $1.2 billion term loan. The company defaulted. Investors marked down their stakes to near zero. Prosus wrote off its entire $578 million investment. BlackRock valued its shares at less than $1. The company, once valued at $22 billion, was essentially worthless on paper.

But Byju's didn't die cleanly. It thrashed. Raveendran fought the creditors, refused to step down, claimed persecution and conspiracy. The company sued lenders. Lenders sued the company. The board fired Raveendran; he claimed they had no authority. Employees went unpaid for months. Partners sued for unpaid dues. The courts froze assets. The spectacular unicorn became a spectacular mess — bankruptcy proceedings, legal battles, and recriminations playing out across international courts.

What killed Byju's wasn't the product. The core teaching content still worked. What killed it was mistaking venture capital for validation, growth for health, and sales aggression for product-market fit. The company raised $6 billion and spent it building an empire rather than a sustainable business. The acquisitions never integrated. The sales force created resentment. The accounting was opaque. The governance was weak. And when the music stopped, there was nothing underneath but debt and broken promises.

Raveendran had built something real in those stadium sessions — genuine teaching that helped students. But somewhere between the stadiums and the $22 billion valuation, the mission got lost. The company optimized for growth metrics that pleased investors rather than learning outcomes that helped students. It became a case study in how easily "changing the world" becomes "raising the next round," and how the path from unicorn to cautionary tale can be faster than anyone imagines.
