# Byju's

***From tutoring prodigy to India's most spectacular startup collapse***

> **Years:** 2011-2024

Byju Raveendran was an engineer who never planned to teach. In 2003, he was working on a ship, far from India's cramped coaching centers where millions of students prepared for entrance exams. But friends kept asking him for help with the CAT, India's MBA entrance test. He started teaching a few people. Then a few more. By 2008, he was filling stadiums — literally, sports stadiums — with students paying to hear him explain quantitative reasoning. He had something rare: the ability to make math feel intuitive, even exciting.

The question was whether this magic could scale beyond his physical presence. In 2011, Raveendran founded Think & Learn, which would become Byju's. The early product was offline — recorded lectures on USB drives and tablets shipped to students. India's middle class was exploding, and parents would pay almost anything for educational advantage. The model worked. By 2015, Byju's had revenue and real users. Then they made the app.

The Byju's app launched in 2015 as freemium. Students could access some content free, then parents bought subscriptions for the full library. The interface was slick by Indian edtech standards. The content was Raveendran's magic, packaged as videos and games. But the real innovation was sales. Byju's built an army of door-to-door salespeople who visited homes, demonstrated the app on tablets, and closed parents on multi-year subscriptions — often ₹50,000 to ₹100,000 ($600-1,200), a significant sum in India. Parents signed. The combination of aspiration, guilt, and hard-sell tactics worked brutally well.

Venture capital noticed. In 2016, Mark Zuckerberg visited Byju's office. In 2018, Tencent invested. By 2019, Byju's was valued at over $5 billion, India's first edtech unicorn. Raveendran was a celebrity founder, profiled as the teacher who built an empire. Revenue grew to hundreds of millions. The pitch was simple: India had 260 million school-age children, rising incomes, obsessive parental focus on education, and digital infrastructure finally reaching beyond cities. Byju's would become the learning layer for the entire country.

Then came 2020. COVID-19 closed schools globally. Physical coaching centers shut. Parents panicked. Byju's downloads exploded — from 40 million users to over 100 million in months. Investors saw vindication. SoftBank invested. Byju's raised money at higher and higher valuations: $8 billion, $12 billion, $16.5 billion, finally $22 billion in 2022. It was India's most valuable startup, more than a decacorn.

With capital, Byju's went on an acquisition spree. It bought Aakash Educational Services, a chain of physical coaching centers, for nearly $1 billion. It bought Epic, a U.S. digital reading platform, for $500 million. It bought Tynker, Great Learning, Toppr — companies spanning kids coding, professional upskilling, test prep. It spent $300 million to sponsor the Indian cricket team. It hired Lionel Messi as brand ambassador. The vision expanded: Byju's would be the global learning company, serving students from kindergarten through professional education, across continents.

The cracks appeared almost immediately. Byju's business model had always depended on aggressive sales tactics, but as it scaled, the tactics got worse. Salespeople allegedly mis-sold products, pushed predatory loans on lower-income families, made refunds nearly impossible. Customer complaints multiplied. Regulators noticed. Meanwhile, revenue recognition became murky. Byju's booked multi-year subscription payments upfront, inflating revenue numbers. Auditor Deloitte resigned in 2022, citing concerns. Then KPMG resigned. So did multiple board members.

The financial reality unraveled. Despite $22 billion valuation, Byju's was burning cash at extraordinary rates — reportedly over $1 billion a year. The acquisitions weren't integrating. Revenue growth, stripped of accounting gymnastics, was slowing. Schools had reopened; the COVID bump reversed. Investors who had poured in money at peak valuation started demanding answers. In 2022, Byju's tried to raise a down round and failed. By 2023, it was trying to raise at 80% down from peak and still struggling.

Then the disputes turned legal. Byju's took a $1.2 billion term loan from international lenders in 2021. When the company missed financial reporting deadlines and allegedly diverted funds to obscure entities, lenders moved to accelerate the loan. Byju's sued. Lenders countersued. The company defaulted. Meanwhile, India's Enforcement Directorate began investigating foreign exchange violations. The Board of Control for Cricket in India sued for unpaid sponsorship fees. Vendors sued for unpaid bills. Employees went unpaid. Layoffs came in waves — thousands of people.

Raveendran fought every battle publicly. He blamed investors, lenders, auditors, journalists. He claimed vendetta and conspiracy. He tried desperate measures: a rights issue to existing investors that most refused. A proposed sale of Epic back to its founders that collapsed. Attempts to restructure that went nowhere. In 2024, creditors filed for insolvency. Raveendran was ousted from the board of his own company. Byju's, once worth $22 billion, entered insolvency proceedings owing over $1 billion to creditors, with valuation effectively zero.

What killed Byju's was not the market or the product. Parents still pay for education. The app still works. What killed it was a founder who confused growth with success, capital with validation, and ambition with strategy. Raveendran could teach quantitative reasoning but could not manage a complex business at scale. The sales-driven model that worked for subscriptions became predatory. The acquisitions made for headlines, not integration. The financial engineering hid problems instead of solving them.

By the end, Byju's owed money to everyone: lenders, vendors, employees, cricket boards. The insolvency process would take years. Investors who had marked paper returns in the billions would write it all down. Students who had paid for multi-year subscriptions found content disappearing. The man who filled stadiums ended his startup running from creditors, his name synonymous not with education but with excess and failure.
