# Jet.com

***The price war that became an exit***

> **Years:** 2014-2018

Marc Lore had already sold one company to Amazon. In 2010, Quidsi — the parent company of Diapers.com — went to Jeff Bezos for $545 million after Amazon had waged a brutal price war against them. Lore stayed through the earnout, watched Amazon's machine up close, then left. He spent his non-compete period thinking about what he'd learned. The conclusion: you could beat Amazon, but only by being more Amazon than Amazon.

Jet.com launched in July 2015 with the most audacious retail proposition in years. The pitch was simple: real-time dynamic pricing that dropped with every item you added to your cart. Buying more things together? Lower prices. Waiving free returns? Lower prices. Paying with debit instead of credit? Lower prices. The algorithm would show you exactly how much you'd save by making different choices. It was Amazon's efficiency engine turned into a customer-facing game.

The model made sense if you squinted. Amazon had trained customers to expect low prices and free shipping, but those conveniences cost money. Jet would expose the economics and let customers optimize. Buy a case instead of a single unit. Let the system route from a closer warehouse. Accept a slightly longer delivery window. Each choice shaved pennies, and pennies accumulated.

Lore raised $80 million before launch — then another $140 million four months later. The investors included Bain Capital, Accel, and Goldman Sachs. The story was compelling: Lore knew Amazon's playbook, had already built a billion-dollar e-commerce company, and was attacking the giant's core business with better unit economics. Jet claimed it could be 10-15% cheaper than Amazon on identical baskets while maintaining healthier margins.

The launch was massive. Jet spent heavily on customer acquisition — $20 million in the first few months alone. The company offered a $50 membership fee (later dropped) and discounts deep enough that early adopters called it "too good to be true." They were right to be skeptical. Jet was losing money on every order, burning through venture capital to buy market share. But Lore had seen this playbook work. Quidsi had done the same thing. So had Amazon itself in the early days.

The problem was scale and speed. Amazon had fifteen years and infinite capital. Jet needed to reach critical mass before the money ran out. By early 2016, Jet had raised $500 million total — a staggering sum — but was burning $20-25 million per month. The company had maybe eighteen months of runway, and Amazon was watching. More worryingly, the "smart cart" that was supposed to drive Jet's advantage wasn't creating the behavior change the model required. Customers liked the discounts but didn't optimize their carts the way Jet needed them to. They wanted Amazon-level convenience at Jet-level prices, which was economically impossible.

Walmart was also watching. Doug McMillon had become CEO in 2014 with a mandate to fix Walmart's catastrophic digital strategy. Walmart.com was growing but still a rounding error against Amazon. The company needed to move faster, buy better technology, and convince Silicon Valley that Walmart was serious about e-commerce. Jet looked like a solution: proven leadership, modern tech stack, and a direct assault on Amazon's model.

In August 2016, Walmart acquired Jet for $3.3 billion in cash and stock. It was Walmart's largest-ever acquisition and the biggest e-commerce deal in years. Lore would run Walmart's entire U.S. e-commerce operation, and Jet would become the laboratory for Walmart's digital ambitions. The price seemed insane — Jet was barely a year old, unprofitable, and hadn't proven its model worked. But Walmart wasn't buying revenue; it was buying talent, technology, and speed.

The deal made Lore the most successful repeat founder in e-commerce. He'd now sold two companies for nearly $4 billion combined, both to giants trying to compete with Amazon. The investors who'd bet on him eighteen months earlier had just made spectacular returns. For a moment, Jet looked like vindication of the blitzscaling model: raise huge rounds, grow impossibly fast, and exit before unit economics matter.

But Jet's path after acquisition revealed the problem with the strategy. Walmart tried to scale Jet as a standalone brand targeting urban millennials — the demographic Walmart struggled to reach. The dynamic pricing remained, but it never drove the behavior change required. Customers used Jet for deals but didn't change how they shopped. By 2018, Walmart quietly began integrating Jet into Walmart.com. By 2020, Jet.com was effectively shut down, its technology and team absorbed into the parent company.

Lore stayed until 2021, overseeing Walmart's successful e-commerce transformation. Walmart's online sales grew from $13 billion when he joined to over $40 billion when he left. He built the infrastructure, bought the companies (Bonobos, ModCloth, Eloquii), and created a credible challenger to Amazon's retail dominance. But Jet itself — the brand, the model, the original vision — disappeared.

The company existed for six years and as an independent brand for barely two. It never proved its unit economics worked at scale. It never became the Amazon-killer Lore envisioned. But it achieved something more pragmatic: it was the key that unlocked Walmart's digital transformation and made Lore and his investors very rich. Whether that counts as success depends on which story you're telling.
