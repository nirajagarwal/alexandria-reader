# Jet.com

***The audacious gamble that became a $3 billion lesson in retail economics***

> **Years:** 2014-2020

Marc Lore had already sold one company to Amazon. In 2010, his first startup Quidsi — which ran Diapers.com and Soap.com — went to Jeff Bezos for $545 million after Amazon had tried to crush it with predatory pricing. Lore stayed through his earn-out, watching from inside as Amazon operated. He left in 2013 with two convictions: e-commerce margins were broken, and he knew how to fix them.

The insight was elegant. Amazon's prices included the cost of Prime's free two-day shipping, spread across all products. But not every customer needed speed, and not every product needed individual packaging. If you could separate price from delivery speed, pass savings directly to customers, and aggregate orders for efficiency, you could undercut Amazon on price while still making money. It was arbitrage built on customer choice and logistics optimization.

Lore raised $80 million before Jet.com launched in July 2015 — one of the largest seed rounds in e-commerce history. The funding came easy. Investors knew Lore could execute, and the Amazon threat felt real enough that disrupting it seemed worth the bet. Jet promised prices 10-15% below Amazon through a membership model: $50 annually for access to wholesale-style savings. The site would dynamically adjust prices based on what else customers bought, how far items shipped, and whether they waived free returns. Every choice that saved Jet money saved the customer money.

The launch was massive. Jet spent heavily on customer acquisition — $100 million in the first year alone. Within six months, they had 2.4 million customers and were processing tens of thousands of orders daily. The interface was clean, the savings were real, and the value proposition was clear. Early customers loved it. The press called it an Amazon killer.

But the economics were brutal from day one. The membership model wasn't working. Customers joined for deals but didn't shop frequently enough to justify the acquisition cost. Jet pivoted three months after launch, dropping the membership fee and relying purely on volume and vendor partnerships to sustain margins. This made customer acquisition easier but made profitability even more distant. Every order lost money. The dynamic pricing algorithm was sophisticated, but sophistication doesn't matter if the unit economics don't close.

Lore kept raising. Another $140 million in October 2015. Then $350 million more in early 2016, at a $1.5 billion valuation. The pitch remained compelling: scale would solve it. Get big enough, and the logistics efficiency would kick in. Build the customer base, and the vendors would give better terms. Meanwhile, Amazon's dominance was growing, and retail giants were panicking. Walmart watched Amazon eat into groceries and general merchandise while their own e-commerce operation floundered. They needed a different approach.

In August 2016, barely a year after launch, Walmart acquired Jet for $3.3 billion — the largest e-commerce acquisition in history at the time. Jet had never turned a profit. Revenue was under $1 billion. The company was burning through $20 million monthly. But Doug McMillon, Walmart's CEO, wasn't buying Jet's technology or customer base. He was buying Marc Lore, and Lore's vision for how to fight Amazon.

The deal made Lore head of Walmart's entire U.S. e-commerce operation. Jet became the innovation lab, the brand for urban millennials, the testing ground for ideas that could scale across Walmart's empire. Walmart poured resources in. They bought Bonobos, ModCloth, and Moosejaw — digitally-native brands that Jet could integrate. They expanded Jet's product catalog. They pushed the brand into new markets. For a moment, it looked like the gamble might work.

But the fundamental tension never resolved. Jet's model required scale and efficiency that only made sense at Amazon's size. Walmart had scale, but Walmart's logistics were built for stores, not e-commerce. The cost to retrofit was enormous. Meanwhile, Jet's urban millennial brand didn't align with Walmart's core customer. The overlap was minimal. Marketing to one diluted the other.

By 2019, the narrative had shifted. Walmart stopped breaking out Jet's numbers. The separate brand acquisitions were folded into Walmart.com. Jet's competitive advantage — the dynamic pricing algorithm, the savings through aggregation — had been absorbed into Walmart's broader platform. The distinct identity was fading.

In May 2020, Walmart quietly shut down Jet.com. The domain redirected to Walmart.com. The brand disappeared. Walmart said Jet had accomplished its purpose: accelerating Walmart's e-commerce capabilities and bringing Lore's expertise in-house. Walmart's online sales had indeed grown dramatically, from $14 billion when they bought Jet to over $40 billion by 2020. But none of that growth came from Jet itself.

What died wasn't just a website. It was the thesis that a new entrant could beat Amazon at its own game through better algorithms and pricing transparency. Jet proved you could attract customers and raise billions on that promise. But it also proved that e-commerce at scale is a game of infrastructure, not just innovation — and that infrastructure takes decades and unimaginable capital to build. Amazon had both. Jet had neither.

Lore left Walmart in 2021, his earn-out complete. He started another company. The cycle continues.
