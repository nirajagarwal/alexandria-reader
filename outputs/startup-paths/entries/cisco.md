# Cisco

***The acquirers who built an empire by buying the future***

> **Years:** 1984-present

In December 1984, a husband and wife who worked at Stanford University wanted to send each other emails across campus. The problem was that Stanford's computer networks — like most networks everywhere — couldn't talk to each other. Leonard Bosack ran the computer science department's network. Sandy Lerner ran the business school's. They were separated by a few hundred yards and an insurmountable technical barrier.

They solved it in Bosack's living room. Using technology Bosack had helped develop at Stanford, they built a device that could route data packets between incompatible networks. It was called a router, and it made the internet possible. They called their company Cisco, after San Francisco, and funded it with credit cards.

Stanford wanted the technology. Bosack and Lerner wanted to commercialize it. The university's technology licensing office moved slowly. The couple moved fast. They began selling routers from their living room to other universities and research institutions that had the same problem. By 1986, they were doing $250,000 a month in sales. Stanford eventually licensed them the technology, taking an equity stake and royalties.

The timing was perfect in a way the founders couldn't have fully anticipated. In the late 1980s, the internet was transitioning from a research project to infrastructure. Organizations needed to connect networks. Cisco's routers were the only products that worked reliably at scale. The company grew 100% year-over-year. By 1990, they went public with $70 million in revenue.

Then the founders lost control. In 1990, the board — led by Sequoia Capital's Don Valentine, who had invested in 1987 — forced out Sandy Lerner over management style conflicts. Bosack, furious at his wife's firing, quit in protest. They sold their shares for $170 million and left. Valentine brought in John Morgridge as CEO. Morgridge brought in John Chambers from Wang Laboratories in 1991. In 1995, Chambers became CEO.

What happened next defined Cisco more than the router did. Chambers understood that the internet was going to explode and that Cisco couldn't build everything fast enough. So Cisco would buy everything. Between 1993 and 2000, Cisco acquired 71 companies. Not for their revenue or customer bases, but for their technology and engineering talent.

The acquisition machine had a method. Cisco targeted companies with 50-100 employees building complementary technologies — switching, security, wireless, optical networking. They paid premiums but moved fast. They kept founders and engineers, integrated them rapidly, and maintained velocity. The model worked because Cisco had the distribution. Small companies built innovation; Cisco gave them scale instantly.

The strategy matched the market perfectly. The internet was growing exponentially. Demand for networking equipment was insatiable. Companies needed routers, switches, security appliances — everything required to build the infrastructure of the digital economy. Cisco became the arms dealer of the dot-com boom. By 2000, Cisco was the most valuable company in the world, worth $555 billion.

Then the crash came. The dot-com bubble burst. The companies buying Cisco's equipment vanished overnight. Revenue fell 30%. The stock fell 86% from its peak. The acquisition spree stopped. Cisco laid off 18,000 employees.

But Cisco didn't die. The infrastructure it built remained essential. The internet kept growing, just more slowly. Chambers refocused on enterprise customers — banks, hospitals, corporations building internal networks. Cisco maintained gross margins above 60%, an almost unheard of level for hardware. The routers were expensive, but switching costs were enormous. Once a company built its network on Cisco equipment, replacing it meant replacing everything.

Cisco resumed acquiring, but differently. Larger deals now. In 2003, Linksys for home networking. In 2006, Scientific-Atlanta for video. In 2012, NDS for video software. Some worked; many didn't. The model that worked brilliantly in the 1990s — buying 50-person startups building point technologies — worked less well when Cisco tried to enter new markets entirely.

The 2010s brought new challenges. The cloud meant customers needed different architectures. Software-defined networking threatened to commoditize hardware. Competitors like Arista built faster, cheaper switches. Amazon, Google, and Microsoft built their own networking equipment. The internet Cisco built was being rebuilt without Cisco at the center.

Chambers stepped down in 2015. His successor, Chuck Robbins, pivoted toward software and subscriptions. More acquisitions: AppDynamics for $3.7 billion in 2017. Duo Security for $2.35 billion in 2018. Splunk for $28 billion in 2024 — Cisco's largest acquisition ever, a bet that security and observability are the future of networking.

Today, Cisco is worth $240 billion. Still massive, still profitable, still essential to how the internet works. But no longer the inevitable winner. The story of the next decade is whether Cisco can transform fast enough — from hardware to software, from products to platforms, from selling boxes to selling outcomes.

The company that made the internet possible by routing packets between incompatible networks must now figure out how to route itself between incompatible eras. The living room startup that became the most valuable company in the world did it by acquiring the future faster than anyone else. The question now is whether the future can still be bought.
