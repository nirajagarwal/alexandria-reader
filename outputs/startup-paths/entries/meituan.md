# Meituan

***From thousand-ticket war to super-app dominance through survival, merger, and relentless execution***

> **Years:** 2010-present

Wang Xing had already failed twice. Xiaonei, his Facebook clone, had been sold out from under him. Fanfou, his Twitter equivalent, got shut down by Chinese authorities. In 2010, at twenty-nine, he founded Meituan with four co-founders in Beijing. The name meant "beautiful together" — a group-buying site modeled on Grouping, which had just launched in America and was growing explosively.

The timing looked perfect. China's internet population had crossed 400 million. Smartphones were beginning to penetrate. Urban middle-class Chinese wanted deals on restaurants, movies, massages, everything. Groupon's model — aggregate demand, negotiate bulk discounts with merchants, take a cut — seemed infinitely replicable.

Wang raised $12 million from Sequoia China and Alibert Capital. Meituan launched in March 2010 with a simple interface: one deal per city per day. A restaurant meal for half price. A spa package at 60% off. Buy now, redeem later. The merchants got customers. Meituan got 5-10% commission. Users got savings.

Within months, the model exploded. Not just Meituan — everyone. By mid-2011, China had over 5,000 group-buying sites. They called it the "Thousand Groupon War." Venture capital poured in. Rivals launched with massive marketing budgets, flooding subways and elevators with ads. Lashou raised $166 million. Groupon itself entered China through a joint venture. The competition was subsidy-driven and savage.

Most competitors focused on growth at any cost — signing merchants with minimal vetting, offering deals below merchant cost, subsidizing users to switch platforms. Wang took a different path. Meituan emphasized merchant quality and operational efficiency. While rivals burned through capital on television ads, Meituan focused on ground game: sales teams in every city, relationship-building with merchants, customer service that actually worked. The interface stayed clean. The deals stayed real.

By 2012, the battlefield was littered with corpses. Thousands of group-buying sites had collapsed. Funding dried up. The survivors consolidated. Meituan had quietly become profitable in several cities while competitors bled cash. Wang's discipline was paying off.

But profitability wasn't enough. The group-buying model itself was evolving. Smartphones were everywhere now, and users wanted more than daily deals. They wanted food delivery, movie tickets, hotel bookings, everything local services could offer, on-demand. The model shifted from "one deal per day" to "horizontal platform for local services." Whoever won food delivery would win the war.

Meituan entered food delivery in 2013, late to a market already contested by Ele.me and others. Delivery was brutal economics: low margins, high logistics costs, endless merchant and driver coordination problems. But it was also the highest-frequency use case — users ordering lunch, dinner, snacks, multiple times per week. Whoever owned food delivery could cross-sell everything else.

Wang bet the company on delivery. Meituan built its own logistics network instead of relying entirely on third parties. Sales teams signed up restaurants block by block. The app became a super-app: food delivery, but also restaurant reviews, movie tickets, hotel bookings, salon appointments, everything local. The subsidies were enormous — burning cash to win customers and drivers. But Meituan's operational discipline kept waste lower than rivals.

The real inflection came in 2015. Meituan and Dianping, its closest competitor in local services, merged. Dianping had started as a restaurant review site — China's Yelp — and expanded into group-buying and delivery. The merger created a combined entity with 250 million users and dominant market share in most cities. Wang became CEO. The new company, Meituan-Dianping, was valued at $15 billion.

But the delivery war wasn't over. Ele.me, backed by Alibaba, was still fighting hard. The subsidies continued. Then in 2018, Alibaba acquired Ele.me entirely for $9.5 billion, turning food delivery into a proxy war between Meituan and China's e-commerce giant. The battle intensified. Meituan's losses mounted — $1.1 billion in 2018 alone.

Wang's response was expansion. Meituan moved into hotel bookings, challenging Ctrip. Into ride-hailing, challenging Didi. Into grocery delivery, bike-sharing, everything adjacent to local services. The strategy was sprawl: use food delivery's frequency to cross-sell higher-margin services. Lose money on delivery, make it back on hotels and travel.

It worked. By 2019, Meituan's hotel bookings exceeded Ctrip's in room nights. The super-app had 450 million users. The company went public in Hong Kong in 2018 at a $50 billion valuation despite ongoing losses. Investors bet on long-term dominance.

Then COVID-19 hit. Restaurants closed. Delivery demand surged but margins stayed brutal. Meituan's diversification saved it — grocery delivery, medicine delivery, essential services kept revenue flowing. The company's logistics network, built over years, became critical infrastructure. By 2021, Meituan finally turned a full-year profit.

But new problems emerged. Chinese regulators cracked down on internet platforms, imposing fines and restrictions on monopolistic practices. Meituan was fined $530 million in 2021 for anti-competitive behavior. The political environment for tech giants had shifted. Growth slowed. The stock fell from peaks.

Today, Meituan operates in a consolidated market. It has 680 million users and dominates local services in China. Food delivery is enormous but low-margin. Hotels and travel are profitable. The company is valued around $100 billion, down from a peak of $245 billion. Wang Xing remains CEO, still executing, still expanding cautiously into new categories.

The path from group-buying site to super-app took fourteen years and required survival through a thousand-competitor bloodbath, a major merger, subsidy wars with Alibaba, regulatory crackdowns, and a pandemic. Meituan won not through brilliance of original idea but through operational discipline, relentless execution, and strategic patience. It outlasted rivals, outbuilt infrastructure, and diversified revenue when growth slowed. The outcome was dominance — not exit, but endurance as one of China's essential platforms.
