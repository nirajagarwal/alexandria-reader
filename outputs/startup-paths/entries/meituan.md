# Meituan

***The super-app that conquered Chinese local services through relentless subsidy wars and vertical integration***

> **Years:** 2010-present

Wang Xing had already failed twice. Xiaonei, his Facebook clone, had been sold from under him. Fanfou, his Twitter equivalent, had been shut down by censors. When he started thinking about group buying in late 2009, he was thirty years old and running out of chances. But he had learned something from watching companies die: in China's internet market, whoever moved fastest and spent hardest usually won.

Groupon had just launched in the United States and the model was elegantly simple. Merchants offered steep discounts if enough customers committed to buy. The platform took a cut. It required no inventory, no logistics, just sales. Wang saw it could work even better in China, where millions were just getting smartphones and discovering online commerce. He called his new company Meituan — "beautiful group."

The timing created a gold rush. Within months of Meituan's March 2010 launch, China had over a thousand group-buying sites. They called it the "thousand groupon war." Venture capitalists threw money at anyone with a website. Lashou raised $150 million. Dianping, already dominant in restaurant reviews, added group buying. American companies tried to parachute in. The market was chaos.

Meituan's advantage was discipline. While competitors bought Super Bowl ads and subway station takeovers, Wang focused on merchant relationships and unit economics. He hired thousands of sales agents to visit restaurants personally, explaining the platform, helping with photography, training staff. Other sites offered 70% discounts to attract users. Meituan held at 50-60%, knowing merchants couldn't sustain deeper cuts. When rivals burned through their funding, Meituan was still standing.

By 2011, the thousand competitors had collapsed to dozens. By 2012, the war was essentially between Meituan and Dianping. Both raised huge rounds. Both subsidized heavily. Neither could knock the other out. The fight dragged on for years, draining capital, because group-buying alone wasn't enough. The frequency was too low, the margins too thin, the defensibility too weak.

Wang began building something bigger. The insight was simple: users came to Meituan to find restaurants, but they also needed food delivered, taxis called, hotels booked, movie tickets bought. Every local service transaction could flow through one platform. In 2013, Meituan added movie ticketing. In 2013, they launched food delivery. In 2015, they tested ride-hailing. Each vertical started the same way — subsidize until users formed habits, then gradually raise prices.

The food delivery war became brutal. Ele.me, backed by Alibaba, was already established. Baidu threw money behind Baidu Waimai. Meituan entered late but spent without limit. Delivery drivers earned ¥100 per day in subsidies alone. Restaurants paid nothing to list. Users got meals below cost. The daily burn rate across the industry reached hundreds of millions of yuan. Drivers played the platforms against each other, keeping multiple apps open, cherry-picking the best orders.

Meituan was losing enormous sums but Wang understood the game. This wasn't about profitability. It was about becoming infrastructure. Once users defaulted to Meituan for lunch, for dinner, for everything local, the platform became unassailable. The subsidy war was buying lock-in.

In 2015, Meituan and Dianping merged, ending their five-year war. Together they were worth $15 billion. But the battles weren't over. Ele.me, backed by Alibaba's infinite capital, intensified its assault. The delivery wars escalated. By 2017, Meituan was losing ¥5 billion annually on food delivery alone. But orders were growing 300% yearly. Market share was expanding. Wang kept spending.

Then came the pivot that defined Meituan's future. In 2017, they moved seriously into ride-hailing, challenging Didi directly. Didi dominated Chinese transportation, but Meituan saw an opening. Users already used Meituan for restaurant reservations. Why not add the ride home? They launched in Shanghai with aggressive driver subsidies. Didi retaliated by expanding into food delivery. The two super-apps went to war across each other's territory.

The ride-hailing fight nearly broke Meituan. Driver subsidies and user discounts cost billions. Didi had deeper pockets and total market dominance. After a year of combat, Meituan pulled back, ceding ride-hailing while defending food delivery. But the message was sent: Meituan wouldn't stay in its lane. Every local service was fair game.

By 2018, when Meituan IPO'd in Hong Kong, the company had never made an annual profit. It had burned tens of billions building its empire. But it had 400 million users, 5.5 million merchants, and 600,000 delivery drivers. It processed 6.4 billion transactions annually. It was China's everything-local platform.

The subsequent years brought the final consolidation. Alibaba acquired Ele.me fully and poured resources into the fight. Meituan and Ele.me settled into duopoly, still subsidizing, still competing, but no longer trying to eliminate each other. Both were too big to die. In 2020, Meituan finally posted its first annual profit. In 2021, it reached $200 billion in market capitalization, briefly surpassing Alibaba.

Then came regulatory pressure. Beijing cracked down on platform monopolies, worker exploitation, and merchant fees. Meituan was fined $530 million for antitrust violations. The stock crashed. Wang had to recalibrate, raising driver pay, cutting merchant commissions, slowing expansion.

Today Meituan stands as China's dominant local services platform — food delivery, hotel booking, restaurant discovery, movie tickets, grocery delivery, bike sharing, all in one app. It reached profitability not through unit economics but through scale and scope. The model was never sustainable at small size. But at sufficient scale, even thin margins on enormous volumes generate billions.

Wang Xing's lesson, learned through two failures and one brutal victory: in China's internet wars, you win by outlasting everyone else's capital, then extracting rents from the infrastructure you've become.
