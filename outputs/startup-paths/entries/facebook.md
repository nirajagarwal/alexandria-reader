# Facebook/Meta

***The dorm room network that rewired human connection — and paid the price of its own power***

> **Years:** 2004-present

Mark Zuckerberg launched "Thefacebook" from his Harvard dorm room in February 2004. He was nineteen. The site let Harvard students create profiles, add friends, and browse the network — digital versions of the printed facebooks distributed to incoming freshmen. Within 24 hours, over a thousand students had signed up. Within a month, more than half of Harvard's undergraduate population had profiles.

The insight wasn't profound. Zuckerberg had simply noticed that people were desperate to connect online, to present themselves, to see and be seen by their peers. Earlier attempts existed — Friendster, MySpace — but they were open to everyone, cluttered, customizable to the point of chaos. Thefacebook was clean, exclusive, and real-name-only. You needed a .edu email address. This constraint was the entire value proposition.

Zuckerberg expanded to other elite colleges — Yale, Columbia, Stanford — one at a time. Each launch created a feeding frenzy. Students waited for their school to be added. The exclusivity amplified desire. By December 2004, the site had a million users.

Early investors saw what Zuckerberg was building. PayPal cofounder Peter Thiel wrote a $500,000 check for 10.2% of the company in the summer of 2004. Accel Partners invested $12.7 million at a $98 million valuation in 2005. Zuckerberg dropped out of Harvard. Facebook — the "The" was dropped in 2005 — moved to Palo Alto.

The expansion strategy was methodical. College by college, then high schools, then corporations, then finally, in September 2006, anyone over thirteen with an email address. Each expansion triggered howls from existing users who feared dilution of the network's value. They were wrong. Network effects compounded. More users meant more reasons to join. By the end of 2006, Facebook had twelve million users.

The real breakthrough came in 2006 with the News Feed. Before this, Facebook was a static directory. You visited someone's profile to see updates. The News Feed algorithmically surfaced stories from your network in a constantly updating stream. Users could see everything — breakups, party photos, status changes — without actively searching. The initial reaction was outrage. Users felt surveilled, exposed. Within days, they were addicted. Time on site exploded.

In 2007, Facebook opened its platform to outside developers. Suddenly, third-party apps could integrate with Facebook's social graph. Games, quizzes, and utilities spread virally through the network. Zynga's FarmVille became a phenomenon. Facebook became not just a social network but an operating system for social interaction. Monthly active users hit 100 million in August 2008.

Revenue lagged growth. Facebook had users but struggled to monetize without alienating them. Early ads were ineffective. The breakthrough came with targeted advertising — using the data users voluntarily provided to serve relevant ads. Facebook knew your age, location, interests, relationship status, and who your friends were. This data made ads vastly more valuable than traditional display advertising. Revenue scaled.

Microsoft invested $240 million in 2007 at a $15 billion valuation, buying 1.6% of the company. The valuation seemed absurd. It proved conservative. By 2010, Facebook was profitable. By 2012, it had a billion users.

The IPO in May 2012 was a disaster of execution and a triumph of outcome. Technical glitches marred the NASDAQ debut. The stock, priced at $38, fell to $17.55 within months. Critics declared Facebook overvalued, a fad, finished. They were wrong. Mobile was the future, and Facebook had nearly missed it. The site's early mobile experience was clunky, an afterthought. Zuckerberg bet the company on mobile-first redesign. By 2013, mobile advertising revenue surpassed desktop. The stock recovered and soared.

Facebook entered a phase of aggressive acquisition and imitation. It bought Instagram for $1 billion in 2012 — seen as absurd overpayment — and WhatsApp for $19 billion in 2014. When Snapchat rejected acquisition offers, Facebook copied its features and integrated them into Instagram. The strategy worked. Instagram became a juggernaut. Facebook now controlled the three largest social platforms outside China.

Dominance bred scrutiny. Cambridge Analytica in 2018 revealed that a researcher had harvested data from 87 million Facebook users without consent, then sold it to political consultants. The scandal ignited a firestorm. Zuckerberg testified before Congress. #DeleteFacebook trended. Regulatory pressure intensified. But usage barely dipped. Facebook had become infrastructure.

The 2020s brought existential questions. Whistleblower Frances Haugen leaked internal documents showing Facebook knew its algorithms amplified divisive content, harmed teen mental health, and prioritized engagement over safety. Congressional hearings, antitrust lawsuits, and regulatory scrutiny intensified. Zuckerberg rebranded the company to Meta in October 2021, pivoting toward the metaverse — immersive virtual reality environments. The bet was ambitious and derided. Reality Labs, Meta's VR division, lost $13.7 billion in 2022 alone.

Meanwhile, TikTok ate Facebook's lunch with younger users. The algorithm-driven short video format proved more engaging than the News Feed. Meta responded with Reels, copying TikTok as it had copied Snapchat. It helped, but TikTok kept growing.

As of 2024, Meta has nearly four billion users across its platforms. It prints money — $134 billion in revenue in 2023, mostly from advertising. The stock has rebounded from its 2022 lows. But the company faces structural challenges: regulatory threat, platform fatigue, competition from TikTok, and Zuckerberg's expensive metaverse gamble.

Facebook started as a way to connect college students. It became the infrastructure of global communication, reshaped politics and media, made Zuckerberg one of the world's richest people, and triggered a reckoning about the power of platforms. The path from dorm room to dominance was not inevitable. It required execution, ruthlessness, luck, and a willingness to move fast and break things — including, eventually, the trust that made it possible.
