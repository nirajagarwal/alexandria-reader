# Snowflake

***The data warehouse that made the cloud inevitable***

> **Years:** 2012-present

In 2012, three data infrastructure veterans looked at the cloud and saw what the database giants were missing. Benoit Dageville and Thierry Cruanes had spent years at Oracle building the engine that powered enterprise data warehouses. Marcin Zukowski had cofounded Vectorwise, a high-performance analytics database. They understood the old world intimately — on-premise servers, rigid capacity planning, databases that required armies of administrators to keep running. And they saw that the cloud wasn't just a new place to run old software. It was a different substrate entirely.

The incumbents were trying to lift and shift. Oracle and Teradata were porting their on-premise products to AWS and Azure, charging the same premiums, offering the same complexity. The cloud-native startups were building operational databases — fast reads and writes for applications — but not analytics. Nobody was rebuilding the data warehouse from first principles for cloud architecture. Dageville, Cruanes, and Zukowski started Snowflake to do exactly that.

The technical insight was separation of storage and compute. Traditional data warehouses bundled them together — you paid for servers that did both, and scaling meant buying more of everything. Snowflake decoupled them. Storage lived in cheap cloud object stores like S3. Compute spun up and down on demand. You could run a hundred queries simultaneously by provisioning compute for an hour, then shut it all down. You paid for what you used. More importantly, you didn't need database administrators to manage clusters, tune performance, or plan capacity. The system handled it.

This wasn't an obvious win in 2012. The cloud was still suspect in the enterprise. Data gravity — the idea that data is too big to move — favored incumbents. Early cloud data tools were toys compared to what Oracle could do. Snowflake launched in 2014 after two years of building in private beta. The first customers were startups and digital-native companies — the ones already all-in on cloud infrastructure. They understood immediately. Load your data into S3, point Snowflake at it, and run SQL. No installation, no tuning, no operations.

The growth engine was elegant. Snowflake's pricing model — consumption-based, billed by the second — aligned perfectly with customer incentives. You started small, proved value, and scaled up naturally. The product was fast enough that customers ran queries they wouldn't have bothered with on the old systems. More usage meant more queries, which meant more value, which justified more budget. And because compute was elastic, sudden spikes in usage didn't cause outages — just bigger bills that customers happily paid because the alternative was their analytics grinding to a halt.

By 2017, Snowflake was growing triple digits year over year. The enterprise was capitulating to the cloud. AWS had proven it wouldn't disappear. Azure was gaining ground. Google Cloud was emerging. And the data warehouse, the last holdout of on-premise computing, was migrating. Snowflake rode that wave harder than anyone. The company was multi-cloud from the start — running on AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud — which mattered enormously as enterprises hedged their bets.

The competition noticed. AWS launched Redshift, a cloud data warehouse, in 2012 — the same year Snowflake started. But Redshift was fundamentally on-premise architecture in the cloud. It required capacity planning, cluster management, and operational overhead. Google launched BigQuery, which was genuinely cloud-native but tied to Google's ecosystem. Snowflake's multi-cloud stance and operational simplicity became a moat. When customers ran benchmarks, Snowflake was faster and easier. When they ran proofs of concept, Snowflake just worked.

The company raised aggressively. $26 million in 2014. $105 million in 2017. $450 million in 2018 at a $3.9 billion valuation. The last private round valued it at $12.4 billion. Investors saw the margins — gross margins above 60%, once infrastructure costs were factored in — and the growth rate, which wasn't slowing. In September 2020, Snowflake went public in the largest software IPO in history. It opened at $245, more than double the IPO price of $120, valuing the company at $70 billion. Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway bought in, a near-unprecedented move for Buffett in a tech IPO.

The valuation was stratospheric by traditional metrics — over 100 times revenue. The market was pricing in total displacement of the legacy data warehouse market and expansion beyond it. Snowflake was adding features relentlessly: data sharing, data marketplaces, support for unstructured data, machine learning integration. The pitch was no longer just "cloud data warehouse" but "data cloud" — a platform where all enterprise data lived and flowed.

Post-IPO, Snowflake acquired companies to fill gaps. Streamlit, a Python app framework, to let data scientists build interfaces. Others for better search, metadata management, and governance. Revenue grew from $592 million in fiscal 2021 to $2.8 billion in fiscal 2024. The customer base expanded from tech startups to finance, healthcare, retail — every industry drowning in data and desperate to make sense of it.

By 2024, Snowflake's market cap hovered around $60 billion, down from its peak but still massive. The company faces real competition now. Databricks, with its lakehouse architecture, appeals to data engineering teams. Google's BigQuery has gotten better. AWS keeps iterating on Redshift. But Snowflake established the template: cloud-native, consumption-based, operationally simple. The data warehouse that required a priesthood of DBAs became a product you sign up for with a credit card.

What Snowflake revealed was that cloud wasn't just cheaper or more flexible. It was a different economic model. When you remove the operational burden, when you align pricing with usage, when you make the hard parts invisible, customers will migrate even when they swore they never would. The founders saw the shift before the market did, built the right architecture, and rode the wave all the way to the public markets. The path was neither obvious nor easy, but in hindsight, it looks inevitable.
