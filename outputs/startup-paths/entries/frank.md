# Frank

***The pivot that became a neobank — before neobanks were cool***

> **Years:** 2017-present

Charlie Lee was 27 when he founded Frank in 2017, but he'd already lived the college financing nightmare. At Wharton, he'd watched classmates struggle with the FAFSA — the Free Application for Federal Student Aid — a 108-question labyrinth that determined access to billions in financial aid. The form was so confusing that families paid hundreds of dollars to services that would fill it out for them. Lee saw the absurdity: a free form that required paid help to complete.

Frank's original pitch was simple: make FAFSA easy. The product used AI to auto-populate fields, translated jargon into plain language, and walked students through the process. It took twenty minutes instead of hours. Students loved it. Parents loved it. Lee raised a seed round from Reach Capital and other education-focused VCs. By 2019, Frank claimed 300,000 users.

But there was a problem buried in the model. Frank was free to students. Revenue came from partnerships with colleges that paid to reach Frank's users — students actively seeking financial aid, a valuable audience. The business worked, but it was a marketing channel play dressed up as fintech. Lee was building a lead generation engine, not a financial services company.

The pivot came in 2020. Frank launched banking products — a no-fee checking account, a debit card, savings tools. The thesis: students who trusted Frank with their FAFSA would trust it with their money. The FAFSA tool became the acquisition funnel for a neobank. Lee began talking about Frank as "a financial platform for young people," positioning against Chime and Current but with an education-first brand.

The pivot attracted new capital. In 2021, Frank raised $25 million in a Series B led by Contour Venture Partners. The round valued the company at over $100 million. Lee told the press Frank had five million users. The number appeared in pitch decks, articles, and investor presentations. It was the foundation of Frank's valuation: millions of young customers, high engagement, a path to monetization.

JPMorgan Chase noticed. The bank had been trying to reach younger customers for years, launching digital products that never gained traction. Frank offered a shortcut: a beloved brand, millions of Gen Z users, a founder who spoke their language. In September 2021, JPMorgan announced it would acquire Frank for $175 million.

The deal closed in early 2022. Lee joined JPMorgan as a managing director. Frank's team integrated into the consumer bank. The plan was to use Frank's brand and user base to accelerate JPMorgan's student banking strategy. For Lee, it was vindication — a 27-year-old founder selling to America's largest bank.

But something was wrong with the numbers. After the acquisition closed, JPMorgan began digging into Frank's user data. The claimed five million users — the figure that justified a nine-figure acquisition — didn't match reality. According to JPMorgan's later court filings, fewer than 300,000 customers had opened accounts. The inflated numbers appeared to have been used to secure partnerships, attract investors, and ultimately sell the company.

In 2022, JPMorgan shut down Frank. In January 2023, they sued Charlie Lee and Frank's Chief Growth Officer for fraud, seeking $175 million in damages. The complaint alleged Lee had purchased data on millions of students to create fake accounts, making Frank appear far larger than it was. JPMorgan claimed they'd been deceived at every stage — pitch meetings, due diligence, final negotiations.

Lee denied the allegations. His legal team argued JPMorgan was deflecting from its own failed integration, that the bank hadn't properly understood Frank's metrics, that "users" had been defined broadly to include anyone who'd interacted with Frank's free tools, not just account holders. The case is ongoing.

What remains is a story about the compression of startup lifecycle. Frank went from idea to exit in under five years. It pivoted from fintech-adjacent to actual fintech. It claimed hockey-stick growth in a market desperate for youth engagement. And it sold to an acquirer that wanted the story — the brand, the founder, the user base — without fully interrogating whether the story was true.

The Frank case has become a cautionary tale cited in M&A negotiations and due diligence processes. For founders, it's a reminder that inflated metrics eventually collide with reality. For acquirers, it's evidence that even sophisticated buyers can be blinded by narrative. For the startup ecosystem, it's a data point on what happens when growth-at-all-costs culture meets the actual costs.

The legal battle will determine Lee's culpability. But the path is already clear: a legitimate problem (FAFSA complexity), a reasonable solution (simplification software), a logical pivot (banking for students), inflated metrics, a rushed acquisition, and a spectacular unraveling. The story Frank told worked right up until someone checked if it was true.
