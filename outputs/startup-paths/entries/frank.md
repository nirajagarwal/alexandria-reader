# Frank

***The pivot that almost worked — until the market caught up***

> **Years:** 2015-2021

Frank began with a simple premise: student loans were confusing, and someone should make them less so. Charlie Javice founded the company in 2015 after watching classmates at the University of Pennsylvania struggle with financial aid forms. The FAFSA — the federal form required for student aid — was notoriously complex, running over 100 questions and requiring tax documents most eighteen-year-olds had never seen. Javice, already a serial entrepreneur at twenty-three, saw an obvious opportunity: build software that made the form easier.

The product worked. Frank's interface guided students through the FAFSA step-by-step, pre-filled data where possible, and promised completion in minutes instead of hours. Students loved it. But there was a fundamental problem: Frank couldn't charge students. The form was free to file. The benefit was convenience, not access. And teenagers applying to college don't have money.

So Frank pivoted to a model familiar in education technology: monetize the data. If Frank couldn't charge students, it could charge the institutions that wanted to reach them. Colleges would pay to advertise to students who fit their profile. Financial services companies would pay to offer loan refinancing or credit cards. Frank became a lead generation engine with a helpful tool as the front door.

The business transformed. Frank raised $5 million in 2017, positioning itself not as a FAFSA tool but as a "financial aid platform" connecting students with opportunities. Investors included Founders Fund and Gingerbread Capital. Javice was profiled as a Forbes 30 Under 30 honoree. The narrative was clean: help students navigate aid, build a massive user base, monetize through partnerships.

By 2021, Frank claimed 5 million users — a staggering number that would make it one of the most-used financial aid tools in the country. The company employed around thirty people and had raised roughly $20 million total. Then JPMorgan Chase came calling.

The bank was building a digital banking platform called Chase for Students and wanted to acquire companies that could accelerate growth. Frank seemed perfect: millions of young users, a trusted brand in financial aid, expertise in a demographic Chase wanted to own. In September 2021, JPMorgan agreed to acquire Frank for $175 million.

It should have been a triumph. A six-year-old startup, acquired by one of the largest banks in the world, at a price that made Javice and early employees wealthy. The press release emphasized Frank's reach and Chase's commitment to student financial wellness. Integration began immediately.

But within months, JPMorgan grew suspicious. Frank's user numbers didn't match internal engagement data. Marketing campaigns weren't generating the response rates a 5 million-user base should produce. The bank launched an internal investigation. What they found, according to a lawsuit filed in late 2022, was fraud.

JPMorgan alleged that Frank had fabricated the majority of its user base. Instead of 5 million users, the bank claimed Frank had fewer than 300,000 real customers. To cover the gap, Javice and her team had allegedly purchased data sets of student names and created fake accounts to inflate the numbers. When JPMorgan asked for evidence during due diligence, Frank allegedly provided files full of synthetic users.

The bank sued for fraud. Javice countersued, claiming JPMorgan had buyer's remorse and was scapegoating her. The case turned ugly fast. Emails surfaced showing Javice had asked a data scientist to create "realistic" user profiles. JPMorgan produced testimony from Frank employees who claimed they were told to fabricate data. Javice's legal team argued the bank had done inadequate diligence and was now embarrassed.

In April 2023, federal prosecutors charged Javice with wire fraud, bank fraud, and securities fraud. The indictment laid out a detailed case: false user numbers in fundraising materials, fabricated data provided to JPMorgan, fake email accounts created to simulate engagement. If convicted, she faced decades in prison.

Frank was shut down. The domain redirected to Chase's homepage. The tool that had helped hundreds of thousands of students navigate financial aid disappeared. JPMorgan took a write-down on the acquisition. The 30 Under 30 profile was updated with a note about the charges.

The story wasn't just about fraud. It was about a business model that never quite worked. Frank's early product was real and useful, but there was no monetization path that didn't depend on scale. Without millions of users, the lead generation model collapsed. Investors wanted growth. Acquirers wanted reach. And Javice, facing the gap between what Frank was and what it needed to be, allegedly chose fabrication.

The case is still unfolding. Javice has pleaded not guilty. Her lawyers argue the user count was based on "defensible methodologies." But the outcome is already clear: Frank went from a celebrated exit to a cautionary tale in under two years. It showed how easy it is to fake scale in a world that rewards growth above all else. And it revealed the danger of building a company where the product worked but the business never did.
