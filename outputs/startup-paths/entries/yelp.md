# Yelp

***The review platform that survived its own community***

> **Years:** 2004-present

In the summer of 2004, Jeremy Stoppelman and Russel Simmons were working on an email-based referral network. Friends would email each other questions — "Know a good plumber?" — and get recommendations back. The idea came from Stoppelman's own frustration trying to find a decent doctor in San Francisco. But email was too slow. The product felt dead on arrival.

Their investor, Max Levchin from PayPal, suggested they pivot or shut down. Instead of killing it, they opened up the platform. Let anyone write a review of any business. Make it public. Make it searchable. The Real Yellow Pages, one early tagline went, though that undersold what was actually happening. They were building a database of opinions, and opinions turned out to be more valuable than listings.

The early adopters were almost all in San Francisco. Young, tech-adjacent, opinionated. They wrote long, clever reviews. They competed for funny votes. They formed a community. Yelp threw parties — "Yelp Elite" events at local businesses — and the reviewers showed up. They weren't users. They were contributors, and they liked the status.

The model was elegant. Businesses wanted to be listed because customers were looking. Customers kept returning because the reviews were authentic — or felt authentic, which was the same thing for growth. Yelp made money by selling ads to the businesses, particularly the ones with bad reviews who wanted to be seen next to their better-rated competitors. 

But the community that built Yelp also threatened to destroy it.

By 2006, businesses were crying foul. They claimed Yelp's sales team was extorting them: pay for ads or watch your good reviews disappear. Yelp denied it. They pointed to their filtering algorithm, designed to surface trustworthy reviews and suppress fake ones. But the algorithm was a black box, and perception mattered more than process. A business owner who paid and saw a bad review vanish — or didn't pay and watched a good review get filtered — had no way to know if the system was working as designed or working as feared.

The controversy never quite went away. Investigations by the FTC, studies by academics, class-action lawsuits. Yelp's position remained consistent: the algorithm was neutral, the sales team had no control over reviews, and the filtering was meant to protect users from manipulation. But the business model created perverse incentives whether Yelp acted on them or not. A company that makes money from businesses worried about their reviews will always face suspicion.

The product kept growing anyway. Yelp expanded city by city, building critical mass in each before moving to the next. They opened internationally. They added features: check-ins, reservations, food delivery. The reviews remained the core, and the community remained engaged, even as the early intimacy faded into scale.

Then the mobile shift arrived, and Yelp was ready. Searching for a restaurant while standing on the street corner was the perfect use case. Location-based reviews exploded. By 2012, Yelp went public at a $1.3 billion valuation. The stock popped on day one, and Stoppelman rang the opening bell at the New York Stock Exchange.

But going public also exposed the limits. Google launched local reviews and buried Yelp in search rankings, sending its own users to Google reviews instead. Facebook added recommendations. TripAdvisor dominated travel. Amazon owned product reviews. Every platform wanted to own opinions because opinions drove traffic and traffic drove ads.

Yelp's growth slowed. The stock oscillated. The company cut costs, bought competitors like Eat24, then sold them when synergies didn't materialize. Activist investors pushed for a sale. Stoppelman resisted. He believed Yelp was worth more independent, but the market disagreed. Potential acquirers circled — Google, Yelp's own competitor, supposedly offered $4 billion in 2009 and Stoppelman passed — but no deal ever closed.

The extortion narrative resurfaced periodically, now joined by complaints about toxic reviewers who weaponized one-star ratings over minor grievances, and business owners who gamed the system with fake five-stars. Yelp became a symbol of internet reviewing culture at its best and worst: democratic and corrupt, useful and petty, indispensable and resented.

Still, the company survived. Revenue grew steadily if not explosively, mostly from advertising and partnerships. The reviews kept coming. Hundreds of millions of them. The community kept participating, even if the parties got smaller and the Elite status mattered less. People still searched Yelp before trying a new restaurant, even if they also checked Google and Instagram.

By 2020, the pandemic nearly killed what litigation couldn't. Restaurants closed. Reviews dropped. Ad spending collapsed. Yelp laid off staff and Stoppelman took no salary. But as cities reopened, the searches came back. People wanted to know which businesses survived, which were safe, which were worth supporting.

The company that started as an email referral network and pivoted into public reviews outlasted countless competitors and threats. It never became the giant some expected. It never solved the trust problems inherent to its model. But it built something durable: a database of opinions that enough people found useful enough of the time to keep returning. In the attention economy, that was enough to survive, even if it wasn't enough to dominate.

Yelp remains independent, public, and profitable — smaller than it might have been, more controversial than it wanted to be, and more resilient than its critics predicted.
