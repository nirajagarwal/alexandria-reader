# Chime

***Banking for people the banks forgot***

> **Years:** 2013-present

Chris Britt and Ryan King met at Salesforce in San Francisco. Britt had worked at Visa. King had been at Green Dot, a pioneer in prepaid cards. Both had spent enough time in financial services to notice something: the people who needed banks most were the ones banks served worst.

The math was brutal. Fifty-three million Americans were either unbanked or underbanked in 2013. They paid an average of $2,412 per year in fees — overdrafts, minimum balances, check cashing, wire transfers. Banks made money from people who didn't have money. The digital wave of fintech — Simple, Moven, GoBank — was stylish but aimed at the same middle-class customers traditional banks already served well. No one was building for the hourly worker living paycheck to paycheck.

Britt and King started with a question: what if you built a bank account that couldn't charge fees? Not "low fees" or "fee-free with conditions." Just no fees. Ever.

They launched Chime in 2014 as a mobile-first bank account with no monthly fees, no overdraft fees, no minimum balance. The value proposition was clarity itself: we will not take money from you for banking. They partnered with Bancorp Bank to hold deposits — Chime wasn't technically a bank, but a financial technology company layering services on top of banking infrastructure. This kept regulatory overhead manageable while they proved the model.

The first product was simple. Direct deposit, a Visa debit card, automatic savings. The app had spending notifications and balance updates that actually helped people avoid overdrafts instead of profiting from them. They added SpotMe in 2019 — a feature that let users overdraw their account by small amounts (starting at $20, eventually up to $200) without fees. It was overdraft protection that worked like a friend covering you until payday, not a loan shark charging $35 per infraction.

Growth was immediate but not explosive. In 2015, they raised $13 million from Cathay Innovation and Aspect Ventures. By 2017, they had 500,000 accounts. Respectable. But in 2018, something shifted. They crossed one million accounts. Then two million. By 2019, they had 5 million. In 2020, 8 million. By 2021, 13 million. The curve bent vertical.

What happened? Partially, timing. The 2018-2020 period saw explosive growth in direct-to-consumer fintech adoption. Partially, product. SpotMe and early direct deposit (getting your paycheck up to two days early) hit genuine pain points. But mostly, market. Chime had built for the 53 million. When word spread — and it spread peer-to-peer, not through Super Bowl ads initially — those users found what they'd been looking for. A bank account that didn't punish them.

The revenue model was elegant. Chime made money from interchange fees — the small cut merchants pay when someone swipes a debit card. Average revenue per user was lower than a traditional bank, but customer acquisition cost was also lower, and retention was higher. No fees meant no reason to leave out of resentment. And unlike neobanks targeting affluent millennials, Chime's users used their accounts as their primary bank. High transaction volume. High engagement.

By 2020, Chime was the most valuable U.S. neobank. A $14.5 billion valuation. More than 80% of digital bank downloads in America. They employed 1,000 people. Revenue in 2020 hit $600 million, up from $200 million the year before. The path to profitability was visible.

Then friction. In 2021, the CFPB started investigating Chime over how it handled customer disputes and account closures. Some users reported frozen accounts and inaccessible funds. Chime maintained it was following standard anti-fraud procedures, but the public complaints accumulated. The tension was real: serving people with thin credit histories and irregular income meant higher fraud risk. Chime had to balance accessibility with security, and sometimes got it wrong.

The IPO, long rumored, kept not happening. In 2021, valuations for unprofitable fintech companies soared, and Chime was already profitable or nearly so. But by 2022, the market had soured. Chime raised at a $25 billion valuation in mid-2021, then reportedly struggled to go public as fintech valuations collapsed. By 2023, the company was said to be valued closer to $15 billion in secondary markets. Still enormous. But no longer the unstoppable rocket.

As of 2024, Chime has over 14 million accounts and generates more than $1 billion in revenue. It remains private. The company has added credit-building products, a secured credit card, and lending features. It's expanding into what a full-service bank looks like for its users — but always without monthly fees.

The outcome is not yet written. Chime could go public, be acquired by a traditional bank desperate for digital relevance, or remain independent indefinitely. What's clear is this: Britt and King identified a market everyone else dismissed as unprofitable, built a product those users needed, and proved the economics worked. The most valuable U.S. neobank isn't chasing the affluent. It's serving the 53 million.
