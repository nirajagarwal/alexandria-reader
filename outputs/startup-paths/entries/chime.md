# Chime

***The neobank that made banking boring — and beloved***

> **Years:** 2013-present

Chris Britt and Ryan King met at Kabbage, the small business lending startup, where they watched traditional banks fail their customers in slow motion. Britt had spent years in fintech, watching banks charge fees that seemed designed to punish people for being poor. Overdraft fees. Minimum balance fees. Fees for checking your balance. The math was perverse: the less money you had, the more the bank took from you.

In 2013, they founded Chime with a thesis that felt obvious once stated: millions of Americans would switch banks if someone offered them basic services without the predatory fee structure. Not innovation for its own sake. Just fairness. No monthly fees. No overdraft fees. No minimum balance requirements. Get your paycheck two days early. Simple mobile banking that worked.

The insight was deeper than it appeared. They weren't targeting the unbanked — people with no bank account at all. They were targeting the underbanked — people with accounts at traditional banks who got crushed by fee structures designed in an earlier era. This was a huge market. Nearly half of Americans had less than $400 in emergency savings. For them, a $35 overdraft fee wasn't an inconvenience. It was a crisis that triggered more crises.

Chime launched as a mobile-first bank, though technically it wasn't a bank at all. They partnered with The Bancorp Bank and later Stride Bank to hold deposits, sidestepping the years-long process of getting a banking charter. This made them a fintech company, not a bank — a distinction that would matter later. They could move fast. They could iterate. They could market aggressively without the compliance burden that made traditional banks so slow.

The product was deliberately simple. A checking account. A debit card. A savings account with automatic rounding — spend $3.75, and Chime would round up to $4 and save the quarter. No complexity. No upsells. The revenue model was interchange fees — the small cut merchants paid on every debit card transaction. If customers used their Chime cards, Chime made money. The business model aligned perfectly with customer behavior.

Growth was slow at first, then exponential. They spent heavily on digital marketing, particularly Facebook and Instagram, targeting younger customers who had never developed loyalty to traditional banks. Chime's brand was relentlessly friendly — colorful, simple, human. They positioned themselves as the anti-bank. Every communication emphasized what they didn't charge.

By 2018, they had over a million accounts. By 2019, five million. The growth wasn't just marketing. It was word of mouth. When you've been hit with overdraft fees your whole life and someone hands you an account that makes those fees impossible, you tell people. Chime's customer acquisition cost was high, but retention was exceptional. People who switched didn't switch back.

The product evolved carefully. They added SpotMe, an overdraft alternative that let customers go negative up to a certain amount with no fees. They added early direct deposit, giving customers access to their paychecks up to two days early. These weren't revolutionary features — they were basic courtesies that traditional banks had chosen not to offer. But to Chime's customers, they felt revolutionary.

Venture capital poured in. Sequoia, Menlo Ventures, DST Global. By 2020, Chime was valued at $14.5 billion. By 2021, $25 billion. They had become the most valuable neobank in America, more valuable than many traditional banks with physical branches and decades of history. The pandemic accelerated everything — stimulus checks deposited directly, people managing money entirely from their phones, branch banking feeling increasingly antiquated.

But the growth brought scrutiny. Regulators began asking harder questions about neobanks. Chime marketed itself as "banking with no fees," but they weren't technically a bank. The FDIC insurance came through their partner banks. When customers had issues, resolution paths were unclear. Some customers complained about frozen accounts, locked funds, unresponsive customer service. The problems that plague any fast-growing financial service.

In 2020, Chime was fined by California regulators for misleading marketing about their "bank" status. They adjusted their language, but the tension remained. They wanted the trust and legitimacy that came with being called a bank without the regulatory burden of actually being one.

The path to IPO kept shifting. They filed confidentially in 2021, riding the fintech boom. Then the market turned. IPOs dried up. Valuations collapsed across fintech. Chime waited. Their growth continued — by 2023, they had over 13 million customers — but the urgency faded. They were profitable on a core basis, generating revenue from interchange fees, not burning cash to acquire customers at any cost. They could wait.

The story Chime tells about itself is simple: they saw banks exploiting their customers and built an alternative. That story is mostly true. But the deeper pattern is older. Chime succeeded because traditional banks had spent decades optimizing for profits from fees rather than customer satisfaction, creating a massive opening for anyone willing to compete on fairness rather than extraction. The innovation wasn't technological. It was structural. And once demonstrated, it was hard to ignore.

Chime remains private, still building toward a public offering that may come in 2024 or 2025 or later. They have not exited. They have not failed. They exist in that strange space where a company has won its market but hasn't yet proven it can satisfy public market expectations. The path continues.
