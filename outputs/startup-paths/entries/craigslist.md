# Craigslist

***The classified ad that refused to grow***

> **Years:** 1995-present

In 1995, Craig Newmark was a software engineer at Charles Schwab in San Francisco, newly transplanted from the East Coast and trying to figure out how to meet people. He started sending emails to friends about interesting events around the city — art shows, tech meetups, things happening in the nascent web community. The list grew organically through forwards and word of mouth. People started asking if they could post their own events. Then apartment listings. Then job openings. By 1996, what had started as Craig's personal hobby had become craigslist — lowercase, informal, almost apologetic about existing.

Newmark ran it from his apartment. The site looked like a text file rendered in a browser because that's essentially what it was. No graphics. No branding. Just links and listings organized by category. When the volume got too high for him to handle alone, he incorporated in 1999 and brought on a single employee. Not to scale the business — to answer customer service emails.

The timing was accidentally perfect. eBay had shown that people would transact with strangers online. Newspapers were still printing classified ads at extortionate rates. The web was transitioning from novelty to utility. Craigslist offered what the internet promised and legacy media couldn't: a place to post anything, reach anyone, pay nothing.

By 2000, the site was in multiple cities. The dot-com boom was incinerating capital all around it. Startups with similar models — classifieds, marketplaces, local commerce — were raising tens of millions and hiring hundreds of employees. They had business development teams and sales forces and expensive ad campaigns. They had plans to dominate local commerce, to become destination sites, to capture network effects before anyone else. Craigslist had Craig Newmark and a handful of others answering support tickets.

The venture capitalists came calling. So did acquirers. Newmark said no, mostly. In 2004, he let eBay buy a 28% stake for an undisclosed sum — a hedge, a way to fund the operation without fundamentally changing it. But Newmark retained control. He stepped back from day-to-day operations and handed management to Jim Buckmaster, a programmer who shared his philosophy: keep it simple, keep it free, resist the obvious temptations.

The obvious temptations were enormous. Newspapers were bleeding classifieds revenue. Jobs, apartments, used cars — these were cash machines that moved online, and Craigslist owned the migration. The site charged fees for job listings in major cities and brokered apartment listings in New York, but almost everything else remained free. Competitors calculated what Craigslist could be worth if it acted like a normal business. The estimates ran into the billions.

But Craigslist didn't act like a normal business. It didn't optimize. It didn't redesign. It barely changed the interface. It didn't hire salespeople or build a mobile app until years after mobile became dominant. It didn't raise prices. It didn't expand into obvious adjacencies. When Airbnb and Uber showed how to monetize spare bedrooms and car rides, Craigslist continued letting people post both for free. When machine learning made it possible to detect and remove scams at scale, Craigslist relied mostly on user flags.

This created an opening. Dozens of startups launched to "unbundle" Craigslist — to take one category and do it properly, with venture funding and design polish and fraud protection. Apartment listings went to Zillow and Trulia. Job boards to Indeed and LinkedIn. Used goods to OfferUp and Facebook Marketplace. Personals to dating apps, then disappeared entirely when legislation made them legally risky. Each vertical that left took value with it.

But the site persisted. It remained the default for certain transactions — sublets in college towns, used furniture, gig work that didn't fit LinkedIn. The interface that looked hopelessly dated in 2005 looked exactly the same in 2025. The lack of features became a kind of feature. You could post quickly, search simply, deal directly. No algorithms deciding what you saw. No promoted listings. No upsells.

The business remained profitable — estimates suggested hundreds of millions in annual revenue with a staff of fewer than fifty people. The ratio was absurd. Instagram had been acquired for $1 billion with thirteen employees, but Instagram had growth. Craigslist had equilibrium. It printed money by refusing to chase more money.

eBay eventually sued in 2008, claiming Newmark and Buckmaster had diluted its stake and frozen it out. The court sided mostly with Craigslist. eBay sold its shares in 2015. The site remained private, controlled by Newmark and Buckmaster, who continued running it as they always had: minimal staff, minimal changes, maximum resistance to being anything other than what it was.

Newmark became a philanthropist, giving away hundreds of millions to journalism and veterans' causes. Buckmaster kept the servers running. The site's market share eroded year by year as better-funded competitors took pieces of what it had pioneered. But it never collapsed. It never sold. It never went public. It never hired thousands. It never expanded internationally with purpose. It just stayed there, doing its one thing, looking like 1999, refusing every path toward becoming something larger.

The outcome was both failure and success, depending on what you measured. If the goal was to maximize growth, capture markets, and build enterprise value, Craigslist left billions on the table. If the goal was to create something useful and keep it that way without letting incentives corrupt it, the site achieved exactly what its founders intended. They had built something the internet kept trying to replace, and thirty years later, it was still there, ugly and functional, the classified ad that wouldn't die because it never tried to become anything else.
