# Notion

***The slow build that won by being everything***

> **Years:** 2013-present

In 2013, Ivan Zhao was building his second startup, a tool for creating interactive websites without code. It wasn't going well. The market didn't care. But Zhao kept noticing something: every team that used his tool immediately repurposed it into something else. They turned it into a wiki. A task manager. A database. A place to think.

Zhao had studied cognitive science and design. He'd worked at Inkling, where he saw how people organized information. He believed software should be moldable — that users should be able to shape their tools to match how they actually worked, not the other way around. Every productivity tool forced people into rigid structures: notes here, tasks there, databases somewhere else. But thinking didn't work that way. Projects didn't work that way.

He started over. This time, he wouldn't build for a specific use case. He would build blocks — simple, composable pieces that users could arrange however they wanted. A page could contain text, or a checklist, or a table, or all three. Everything could nest inside everything else. It would be a blank canvas that could become anything.

His co-founder, Simon Last, built the technical foundation. Notion launched in 2015 with almost no features by conventional standards. It was slow, buggy, and confusing. The pitch was abstract: "A unified workspace." The first version barely worked. Users had to explain to each other what it was for.

But a small group got it immediately. They were the people who had twenty browser tabs open — Trello, Google Docs, Evernote, Asana, Airtable — and spent their days copying information between them. Notion promised to replace all of it. Not with a better version of each tool, but with a single flexible system.

The early growth was invisible. Notion had no viral mechanics, no growth hacks, no marketing. People found it through word of mouth, usually from someone who'd spent weeks configuring their personal system. The onboarding was terrible. The learning curve was steep. But once someone invested the time to set it up, they never left. Retention was extraordinary.

In 2017, the company nearly died. They were out of money. The product was still rough. Zhao moved the team to Kyoto to stretch their runway — rent was cheaper, they could focus. They lived together, worked together, barely spoke to the outside world. They rebuilt the product from scratch, making it faster, cleaner, more stable.

The relaunch in 2018 changed everything. Notion 2.0 was fast enough to feel responsive, polished enough to feel professional. The collaboration features actually worked. Teams could finally use it together. And the company made a crucial decision: education pricing would be free. Students flooded in.

This was the wedge. Students used Notion for everything — class notes, project management, personal journals, club organization. They graduated and brought it to their jobs. Entire companies adopted it because one person on the team insisted. The bottoms-up adoption was unstoppable.

Zhao resisted conventional growth tactics. No sales team. No aggressive monetization. No enterprise features that compromised the core flexibility. Notion would grow by being useful, or not at all. The company stayed small — under 50 people even as revenue climbed. They believed a small team building a great product would beat a large team building a mediocre one.

The market was crowded with productivity tools, all venture-backed, all racing to IPO. Evernote was in decline. Trello had sold to Atlassian. Asana and Monday.com were pushing upmarket. Coda and Airtable were raising hundreds of millions. Notion raised a modest Series A in 2018, a year after nearly shutting down.

But Notion had something none of them did: flexibility without complexity. Other tools either did one thing well or tried to do everything and became bloated. Notion's block-based system could be simple for beginners and infinitely customizable for power users. The same tool worked for a student's reading list and a company's product roadmap.

The community became the moat. Users created templates, tutorials, elaborate systems. They shared setups on Reddit and YouTube. A cottage industry emerged: Notion consultants, Notion coaches, Notion template marketplaces. The company didn't build most of the features users wanted — the users built them with blocks.

By 2020, Notion was one of the fastest-growing productivity apps in the world. The pandemic accelerated everything. Remote teams needed shared workspaces. Notion became the operating system for how companies worked. Valuation hit $2 billion on minimal funding. In 2021, it reached $10 billion.

Competitors tried to copy the approach. Microsoft built Loop. Google built Tables. Atlassian built Whiteboards. But they were adding flexibility to existing rigid systems. Notion had started from flexibility as the foundation. The difference was architectural, not featural.

Zhao remained CEO, still writing code, still obsessed with design. The company grew to hundreds of employees but kept the core belief: tools should adapt to people, not force people to adapt to tools. Revenue crossed $100 million, mostly from small teams paying $8 per user per month. No aggressive sales. No enterprise lock-in. Just a product people wanted to use.

The question was never whether Notion would succeed. Once users understood it, adoption was inevitable. The question was whether the company could scale the vision without compromising it. Whether blocks could remain simple while handling enterprise complexity. Whether a product built on flexibility could compete with products built for specific workflows.

Notion didn't win by being the best note-taking app or the best project manager. It won by letting people build their own version of both. It turned users into designers, gave them tools simple enough to understand and powerful enough to matter. In a market full of opinionated software, Notion succeeded by having almost no opinions at all — just pieces, and the freedom to arrange them.
