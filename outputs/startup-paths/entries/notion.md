# Notion

***The productivity tool that became a platform by refusing to be just one thing***

> **Years:** 2016-present

Ivan Zhao spent years building software that nobody wanted. At Inkling, a company making tools for interactive textbooks, he became obsessed with a problem that most people didn't recognize as a problem: the disconnect between how people think and how software forces them to work. Most productivity tools impose rigid structures—tasks go in task managers, notes go in note apps, wikis go in wiki software. But thinking doesn't work that way. Ideas evolve. Documents become databases. Notes turn into projects.

In 2013, Zhao and Simon Last started working on what would eventually become Notion. They didn't start with productivity software. They built a tool for creating interactive web content, something closer to a visual programming environment. It went nowhere. They pivoted to building tools for designers. That also failed. They were running out of money. Zhao moved to Kyoto, Japan, where the cost of living was low enough that he could keep working. Last joined him there. For months, they survived on ramen and persisted with an idea that had already failed twice.

The breakthrough came from synthesis rather than invention. What if they combined everything they'd learned about flexible interfaces, building blocks, and databases into a single tool? Not a note-taking app. Not a project manager. Not a wiki. Something that could be any of those things, or all of them at once. The insight was that people don't want different tools—they want one tool that adapts to how they actually work.

They launched Notion 1.0 in 2016. It was rough. Buggy. Slow. But something about it resonated. The first users were designers and engineers who immediately grasped what Zhao was trying to do. You could create a page that was simultaneously a document, a database, and a task list. You could nest pages infinitely. You could view the same information as a table, a board, a calendar, or a gallery. The interface used "blocks"—everything was a block, and blocks could be anything.

Early growth was organic and concentrated in tech circles. Notion wasn't trying to compete with Evernote or Microsoft. It was doing something different enough that direct comparison felt wrong. The company raised a small seed round in 2017, but remained deliberately small. Zhao was building for a specific kind of user: people who wanted to build their own systems rather than adapt to someone else's.

The inflection point came in 2018-2019 when students discovered Notion. College students, perpetually broke and digitally native, started using it to organize their entire lives. They built course databases, reading trackers, habit journals, and personal wikis. More importantly, they shared their setups. Notion templates became a phenomenon. YouTube filled with "Notion setup tours" and productivity enthusiasts showing off increasingly elaborate systems. This created a flywheel: new users came for the templates, learned the tool, then created their own templates to share.

The student adoption had a crucial side effect: it created a generation of users who would take Notion into their workplaces. By 2019, Notion wasn't just a personal productivity tool—it was becoming workplace infrastructure. Startups were running their entire operations in Notion: wikis, project management, HR, engineering docs, everything. The flexibility that made it powerful for individuals made it powerful for teams. You could build exactly the system you needed.

In 2020, Notion raised $50 million at a $2 billion valuation. The pandemic accelerated everything. Remote teams needed better collaboration tools, and Notion offered something unique: extreme flexibility without extreme complexity. Unlike Confluence or SharePoint, you didn't need an administrator to set it up. Unlike Google Docs, it could handle structured information. Unlike Airtable, it felt like a writing tool first.

But growth exposed tensions in the original vision. The same flexibility that made Notion powerful made it overwhelming. New users faced a blank page and infinite possibility—paralyzing rather than liberating. The company responded by adding templates, guides, and pre-built solutions. This helped, but it also pushed Notion toward becoming more prescriptive, more like the tools it had been built to replace.

Competition intensified. Microsoft added Notion-like features to OneNote. Coda offered similar flexibility with different trade-offs. Roam Research captured the attention of knowledge workers who wanted something more focused on linked thinking. Notion responded by building faster, adding collaboration features, and expanding into new categories. They launched Notion AI in 2023, integrating language models directly into the product.

By 2024, Notion had become genuinely large—millions of users, hundreds of employees, a valuation in the billions. The company that started with a founder eating ramen in Kyoto was now enterprise software. They were signing deals with Fortune 500 companies. The scrappy tool for hackers and students was being evaluated by procurement departments.

The core tension remains unresolved: Notion is powerful because it can be anything, but that means it's constantly at risk of becoming nothing in particular. Every feature added to serve one use case potentially complicates another. Every template that makes onboarding easier makes the tool slightly more opinionated. The blank page that once represented infinite possibility now represents a design challenge: how do you make flexibility accessible?

What Notion proved is that there was a massive market for tools that don't tell you how to work. The productivity software establishment—Microsoft, Google, Atlassian—had optimized for enterprise deployment and IT control. Notion optimized for individual agency and team flexibility. It bet that people wanted to build their own systems, and it was right.

Whether that remains true as Notion scales is the next chapter. The path from founder in Kyoto to workplace platform required refusing to be just one thing. The path from workplace platform to whatever comes next might require choosing.
