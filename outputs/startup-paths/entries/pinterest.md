# Pinterest

***The visual search engine that arrived when social media was already crowded — and thrived by serving a different need***

> **Years:** 2010-present

Ben Silbermann grew up collecting insects. As an adult working at Google in customer support, he found himself still drawn to collecting — now design ideas, travel photos, things he wanted to remember. The web was full of bookmarks and links, but he wanted something more tactile. Something that felt like pinning images to a corkboard.

In 2009, he left Google with co-founders Paul Sciarra and Evan Sharp to build what would become Pinterest. The initial idea was broader — an app called Tote for catalog shopping on mobile. It didn't work. Nobody used the shopping features. But they noticed users kept sharing images of products they liked, creating collections. That behavior — the collecting itself — became the product.

They launched Pinterest in March 2010 as an invite-only site. Growth was agonizingly slow. By summer, the site had fewer than 3,000 users. Silbermann personally wrote to the first 5,000 users, asking for feedback and offering his phone number. He visited Pinterest meetups in stranger's homes. His mother recruited her friends in Iowa.

The timing looked terrible. Facebook had 500 million users. Twitter was exploding. Instagram launched the same year Pinterest did. Social media seemed solved, saturated. Investors saw a photo-sharing site arriving too late to a crowded party.

But Pinterest wasn't competing in the same space. Facebook was about people you knew. Instagram was about moments. Pinterest was about aspirations — recipes you wanted to cook, rooms you wanted to design, places you wanted to visit. It was future-oriented where others were present-oriented. A tool for planning and dreaming rather than sharing and broadcasting.

The user base was also different. While tech startups chased young males in San Francisco, Pinterest's early adopters were women in the Midwest planning weddings, organizing craft projects, collecting recipes. Demographics that Silicon Valley routinely underestimated. The tech press barely covered it. Valley investors didn't understand it. Meanwhile, usage was doubling every month and a half.

By 2011, the growth became impossible to ignore. The site hit 10 million monthly users faster than any standalone site in history. Andreessen Horowitz led a $27 million Series A. The next year, Japan's Rakuten invested $100 million at a $1.5 billion valuation. Pinterest was a rocket ship that had launched without anyone watching.

The challenge shifted from getting users to managing scale. The infrastructure buckled constantly. The team grew from 10 to 200 in two years. Silbermann, an introvert who had been writing personal emails to users, was suddenly running a major company. He had never managed anyone before Google, and at Google he'd been in support, not engineering or product.

The business model question loomed. Pinterest drove enormous traffic to retail sites — users clicked through to buy things they'd pinned. But Pinterest captured none of that value. In 2013, they experimented with "Promoted Pins" — paid placement that looked native. Retailers loved it. Pinterest had accidentally built one of the web's most powerful buying intent signals. People pinning wedding dresses were far more likely to buy wedding dresses than people searching for them.

Then came the accusations. Artists and photographers complained about copyright infringement — their images pinned without attribution or permission. Etsy sellers found their products on Pinterest with links stripped away. Pinterest responded by making it easier for sites to opt out, adding better attribution, and positioning themselves as driving traffic rather than stealing content. The controversy faded but revealed tensions in the model.

Going public in 2019 exposed another challenge. Pinterest had 300 million monthly users and $1.1 billion in revenue. Impressive, but slow growth. Facebook had billions. Snap was capturing young users. TikTok was exploding. Pinterest's IPO was lukewarm, pricing below range. The stock dropped further. Wall Street wanted hockey stick growth. Pinterest offered steady utility.

The pandemic changed everything. People stuck at home started cooking, reorganizing, planning renovations. Pinterest usage surged. Revenue jumped 58% in 2020. The stock quintupled. Then the pandemic ended and growth stalled. Users who'd come for sourdough recipes left. The stock crashed back down.

Leadership turbulence followed. In 2022, Silbermann stepped down as CEO, staying on as executive chairman. Bill Ready, formerly of Google Commerce, took over. The company faced pressure to monetize internationally — strong usage in Europe and Latin America but little revenue. They experimented with e-commerce features, trying to capture transactions instead of just driving them elsewhere. They added video to compete with TikTok and YouTube. They emphasized creator tools to compete for influencers.

Through it all, Pinterest remained useful in a way few social platforms could claim. It wasn't addictive like TikTok or anxiety-inducing like Twitter. It didn't require performance like Instagram or oversharing like Facebook. It was a tool people returned to when planning a wedding, remodeling a kitchen, looking for recipes. Boring utility — the hardest thing to build and the easiest to undervalue.

The company today sits in an odd position. It proved a massive overlooked market existed — visual search for inspiration and planning. It survived the crowded social media wars by not really being social media. It went public and stayed independent while peers were acquired or collapsed. But it never achieved the explosive growth or cultural dominance investors expected. It became infrastructure — important, useful, underestimated. The very qualities that made it succeed made it easy to take for granted.
