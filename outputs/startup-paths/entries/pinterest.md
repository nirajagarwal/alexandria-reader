# Pinterest

***The network that won by not being social***

> **Years:** 2009-present

In late 2009, Ben Silbermann was working at Google but spending his evenings building iPhone apps that nobody used. His previous attempts — a shopping app, a reading app — had attracted maybe a hundred users combined. He wasn't a typical founder. He'd studied political science at Yale, worked briefly in consulting, then joined Google's customer support team. He wasn't technical. He wasn't a designer. He was just someone who noticed things.

What he noticed was how he collected things. Not buying them — collecting them. Bug specimens as a kid. Later, ideas for furniture he might want someday. Apartment layouts that felt right. His girlfriend, now wife, collected recipes she'd never made but wanted to remember. His mother kept travel destinations filed away. Everyone he knew was a curator of aspirations.

Silbermann teamed with Paul Sciarra, a college friend working in venture capital, and they hired Evan Sharp, a Yale architecture student who could design. In March 2010, they launched Pinterest. It wasn't a social network. It was a tool for collecting things you wanted to remember. A visual bookmarking service. You "pinned" images to boards organized by theme. That was it.

Nobody cared. After nine months, the site had 10,000 users. Most apps would have been declared dead. Silbermann took a different approach. He wrote to every single user personally. He included his phone number. When people signed up, he'd email and ask what they were pinning and why. He went to design meetups in Palo Alto and convinced people to try it while he watched over their shoulder.

The first real users were design bloggers. Then it spread to women planning weddings. Then to mothers looking for craft projects and recipes. The growth was slow but the retention was startling. People who started using Pinterest kept using Pinterest. They came back daily. They spent thirty minutes, an hour. They weren't socializing. They were building private museums of future selves.

The Valley didn't understand it. When Pinterest raised its Series A in 2011 from Andreessen Horowitz, many observers were confused. It was mostly women. It was about wedding dresses and cupcakes. Where was the virality? Where was the social graph? It looked like a hobby project, not a platform.

But the numbers told a different story. By 2011, Pinterest was one of the top ten social networks by traffic, despite having raised only $10 million. By 2012, it was driving more traffic to retailers than Twitter, LinkedIn, Reddit, and YouTube combined. Users weren't posting about themselves. They were posting about what they wanted. And that made Pinterest something else entirely: an interest graph, not a social graph. A map of intention.

The business model emerged naturally. People were already using Pinterest to shop. They'd pin a dress, come back to it later, click through to buy. Pinterest added "Buyable Pins" that let users purchase directly. They added search ads. Retailers realized that someone pinning a product was expressing purchase intent. A pin was a signal.

By 2015, Pinterest was valued at $11 billion. Silbermann had become an unlikely CEO of a company with 100 million users. The growth continued, but so did the questions. Pinterest was huge but not cool. It was profitable but not sexy. Facebook had social. Instagram had photos. Snapchat had young people. Pinterest had middle American women planning hypothetical kitchen renovations.

The company went public in 2019 at a $12 billion valuation. The stock fell immediately. Investors weren't sure what Pinterest was. Growth was slowing. The user base was overwhelmingly female in a way that made advertisers simultaneously excited and dismissive. The platform was used for planning, not impulse. High intent but long consideration.

Then came the pandemic. Suddenly everyone was at home looking at ways to improve their space, new recipes to try, home workouts, DIY projects. Pinterest usage surged. The company grew to 450 million monthly users. The stock tripled. Silbermann, who'd been writing personal emails to users a decade earlier, was running a company worth $75 billion.

But the story didn't end in triumph. As the world reopened, growth stalled. Users came back less frequently. The company struggled to expand internationally and to attract men. The ad business, while real, never achieved the same revenue per user as Instagram or Facebook. In 2022, Silbermann stepped down as CEO, staying on as executive chairman.

Pinterest remains profitable and large — over 450 million users, $3 billion in revenue. But it occupies a strange position. It's not a social network people check compulsively. It's not appointment viewing. It's something people use when they're planning a version of their life that hasn't happened yet. A catalog of aspirations. A collection of futures.

The path Pinterest took was improbable from the start. Wrong founder profile. Wrong growth pattern. Wrong demographic. No virality. Too slow. Too niche. Yet it worked because Silbermann built exactly what he wanted to exist and found millions of others who wanted it too. The company won by being genuinely different — not social, not urgent, not about status or identity. Just a place to put the things you hope to become.
