# Runway

***The AI video company that raised a billion dollars before making a product anyone could use***

> **Years:** 2018-present

In 2018, three young artists — Cristóbal Valenzuela, Anastasis Germanidis, and Alejandro Matamala — met at NYU's Interactive Telecommunications Program. They were not engineers trying to build a business. They were creative people frustrated by creative tools. Photoshop hadn't fundamentally changed in years. Video editing was still frame-by-frame tedium. The gap between imagination and execution felt unnecessarily wide.

They had been experimenting with machine learning models — the early generative systems coming out of academic labs. What if these tools could be wrapped in interfaces artists could actually use? What if you didn't need a PhD to apply a neural style transfer or remove a background? They called the project Runway, after the fashion show. A place where ideas walked.

The first version, released in 2019, was a desktop app that packaged open-source AI models into a usable interface. Green screen removal. Object detection. Style transfer. Nothing proprietary. Just access. It found an audience immediately among motion designers, indie filmmakers, TikTok creators — people who wanted the magic without the command line. It was Photoshop-like, but weirder. And it hinted at something larger.

OpenAI had just released GPT-2. DeepMind was making headlines with protein folding. The AI moment was beginning. Valenzuela and his co-founders realized they were sitting at the intersection of two waves: generative AI and creator tools. They raised a $2 million seed round in 2019.

The insight was correct. The timing was early. Generative models in 2019 were impressive in labs and bad in production. Image synthesis was blurry. Video was basically impossible. The infrastructure didn't exist. GPUs were expensive. Latency was fatal. Runway spent two years building incremental features while waiting for the models to catch up.

Then, in 2021, everything accelerated. OpenAI released CLIP and DALL-E. Stability AI open-sourced Stable Diffusion. Suddenly text-to-image wasn't science fiction. It was shipping. Runway pivoted hard. They stopped thinking of themselves as a tool company. They started thinking of themselves as a model company.

In 2022, they released Gen-1, a text-to-video model trained in partnership with academic researchers. It was rough — short clips, low resolution, occasional incoherence — but it worked. You typed a prompt. You got video. No other consumer product could do that. They raised $50 million.

Six months later, they released Gen-2. Better quality. Longer clips. More control. The demos were stunning. A teddy bear walking through Times Square. A drone shot over a forest that never existed. The creative community went wild. Every AI-curious filmmaker and motion designer wanted access. Runway's waitlist exploded.

But the product was not ready. Generation times were slow. Results were inconsistent. Most prompts produced garbage. The good outputs required dozens of iterations. The system consumed enormous compute. Each video cost Runway real money to generate. They were giving it away for free to build hype while bleeding cash on infrastructure.

In 2023, they raised $141 million at a $1.5 billion valuation. The pitch was clear: Runway would be the foundational model company for video, the way OpenAI was for text and Stability was for images. They would train their own models. They would build the infrastructure. They would own the stack.

Except video is orders of magnitude harder than images. A single frame of video is an image. A second of video is 24-30 images that need to be temporally coherent. A minute of video is 1,440-1,800 frames that need to follow physics, maintain object permanence, and respect narrative logic. The compute required is staggering. The training data is scarcer and messier. The model architectures are unsolved problems.

Runway kept shipping. Gen-2 got better. They added motion brush tools, camera controls, image-to-video. The interface became more sophisticated. But the core problem remained: generating good video was still expensive, slow, and unreliable. Professional filmmakers tested it and went back to After Effects. Hobbyists made viral clips and hit paywalls. The product was a demo, not a tool.

Meanwhile, competition emerged. Stability AI released Stable Video Diffusion. Pika Labs launched with better generation speed. Google had its own internal video models. Meta was researching video. The window of differentiation was closing.

By 2024, Runway had raised over $230 million and was reportedly raising more at a $4 billion valuation. They had built a team of world-class researchers. They had released multiple model versions. They had hundreds of thousands of users. But they still hadn't shipped a product that professionals would pay meaningful money to use at scale.

The founders had been right about the wave. They had been early in a way that mattered. They had built a brand and a community. But they had also made a bet: that being first in generative video would create a durable moat. That raising at escalating valuations would attract talent and compute. That the models would improve fast enough to justify the hype.

Now they are running a race. Behind them, larger companies with more resources are catching up. Ahead of them, the technical problems are not obviously solvable on any timeline that matches their valuation. Around them, the burn rate is high and the revenue model is unclear.

Runway is not dead. It may yet win. Generative video may become the platform they imagined. But the path from here to there is narrow, expensive, and crowded. They bet everything on being the company that turned imagination into video. Whether that bet pays off depends on models, markets, and time — and time, in AI, moves faster than any roadmap.
