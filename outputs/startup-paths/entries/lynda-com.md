# Lynda.com

***The patient build that became an acquisition***

> **Years:** 1995-2015

In 1995, when most people thought of online education as correspondence courses delivered by email, Lynda Weinman was teaching web design in her living room. She had written a book on HTML and discovered that people would pay to learn these new internet skills in person. Her husband, Bruce Heavin, suggested they videotape the classes. They could sell the tapes to people who couldn't attend in person.

The insight was simple: software was changing faster than traditional education could keep up. Universities moved slowly. Publishers moved slowly. But professionals needed to learn Photoshop, Dreamweaver, Flash — and they needed to learn now. Lynda and Bruce built a company around that urgency.

They called it Lynda.com, launched in 1995 as an online repository of web design tutorials. The business model was straightforward: charge for access to video courses taught by expert practitioners. No degrees. No credits. Just skills.

The internet was young. Streaming video was painful. Bandwidth was expensive. Most educational content online was text-based because video files were too large, too slow, too impractical. Lynda.com sold CD-ROMs alongside the website because downloading a single course could take hours.

But Lynda Weinman understood something crucial: her audience wasn't traditional students. They were working professionals who needed to learn specific tools to do their jobs. They didn't want theory. They wanted a screencast showing them exactly where to click. They would tolerate poor streaming quality if the content was immediately useful.

The company grew slowly. Bootstrapped at first, then with modest outside investment. They expanded beyond web design into photography, video editing, business software. Each new subject area followed the same pattern: hire an expert practitioner, have them record detailed software tutorials, package them into courses. The library grew course by course, subject by subject.

By the early 2000s, Lynda.com had established a pattern that would define its path: steady, incremental growth without drama. No viral moments. No pivot that saved the company. No near-death experiences. Just consistent execution of a model that worked. Monthly subscriptions gave users access to the entire library. More subscribers meant more revenue to fund more courses. More courses attracted more subscribers.

The technology improved as bandwidth got cheaper. Streaming became viable. The CD-ROMs disappeared. The website became the product. But the core model never changed: professionals paying monthly fees to access a library of software training taught by practitioners.

Competition emerged. Other companies tried to replicate the model. YouTube filled with free tutorials. Universities launched online course platforms. MOOCs promised to revolutionize education. Lynda.com kept growing. The free YouTube tutorials were inconsistent in quality and hard to navigate. The MOOCs focused on academic subjects, not vocational skills. The universities moved slowly. Lynda.com had a decade-long head start and a library of thousands of courses that grew every month.

By 2010, the company had over 100 employees and millions of subscribers. It was profitable. It had never taken substantial venture capital. Lynda Weinman and Bruce Heavin still owned most of it. The pattern held: slow, steady, profitable growth in a market that kept expanding as software became central to more industries.

Then the market shifted around them. LinkedIn was building an empire in professional networking. It had hundreds of millions of users, massive engagement, and a problem: how do you monetize professional identity beyond recruiting and advertising? LinkedIn CEO Jeff Weiner saw Lynda.com as the answer. Professional development. Skills. A way to keep users engaged beyond job searching.

The courtship was quiet. LinkedIn approached Lynda.com in 2014. The numbers were staggering: $1.5 billion in cash and stock. For a company that had bootstrapped its way to profitability, that had never raised significant venture capital, that had grown organically for nearly two decades, it was an exit that rewarded patience.

Lynda Weinman and Bruce Heavin sold in April 2015. At the time, some observers questioned whether LinkedIn had overpaid. Lynda.com had revenue around $200 million annually. The multiple seemed rich. But Weiner understood what he was buying: a decade of video content, a trusted brand in professional education, and most importantly, a business model that aligned perfectly with LinkedIn's professional network.

The integration was smooth. Lynda.com became LinkedIn Learning. The courses appeared in LinkedIn profiles. The recommendation engine connected skills gaps to relevant courses. Users who searched for jobs got prompted to learn skills for those positions. The library kept growing.

For Lynda Weinman, the story ended with validation. She had started teaching web design in her living room because she saw a need. She had built a company around meeting that need consistently, year after year, without shortcuts or drama. She had resisted the pressure to raise huge rounds, to grow faster, to pivot into something trendier. She had stayed patient.

When Microsoft acquired LinkedIn for $26.2 billion in 2016, Lynda.com came along as part of the package. The company she started with videotapes of her classes had become a piece of one of the largest tech acquisitions in history. Not because it disrupted anything. Because it built something useful and kept building it for twenty years.
