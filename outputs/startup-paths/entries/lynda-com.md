# Lynda.com

***The teacher who built an empire teaching teachers***

> **Years:** 1995-2015

Lynda Weinman was teaching web design at Art Center College of Design in Pasadena in the mid-1990s when she noticed something broken. Her students kept asking the same questions after class ended. They couldn't find good resources to continue learning. The web was exploding, everyone needed digital skills, but the books were too slow and the documentation too technical. So she started writing her own books — clear, visual guides to web design tools.

Her first book, "Designing Web Graphics," became a bestseller. Publishers wanted more. But Weinman saw something they didn't: books about software went obsolete the moment a new version shipped. The web itself was the better medium for teaching web skills. In 1995, she and her husband Bruce Heavin launched Lynda.com from their home in Ojai, California. The site offered tutorials, tips, and resources for web designers. Free at first, funded by book sales.

The business model emerged from necessity. By 1998, maintaining free content wasn't sustainable. They moved to a subscription model — $25 per month for unlimited access to video tutorials. This was radical. Most training was either free or one-time purchases. Subscriptions were for magazines and gyms, not learning. But Weinman had credibility. Designers trusted her. Enough paid to keep the lights on.

The format they settled on became their signature: a talking head in the corner of the screen, showing exactly what to do in the software, step by step. No fancy production. Just clear instruction. A designer could watch a 10-minute video at lunch and apply it that afternoon. The videos were task-focused, not conceptual. How to use layers in Photoshop. How to animate in Flash. How to code a dropdown menu. Practical, immediate, useful.

Growth was methodical. They hired instructors — not celebrities, but working professionals who could teach. They expanded beyond web design into photography, 3D animation, business skills, programming. Each new course category brought new subscribers. By 2002, they had 100 courses. By 2005, 1,000. The library became the moat. No competitor could replicate the breadth quickly enough.

The company stayed small by design. Lynda ran it like a teaching institution, not a tech startup. They didn't raise venture capital. They grew from revenue. Headquarters stayed in a repurposed lumberyard in Ojai, a small town 80 miles from Los Angeles. The culture was deliberate, unhurried, focused on quality. This insulated them from the boom-bust cycles crushing other dot-coms. When the bubble burst in 2000, Lynda.com just kept making courses.

By the late 2000s, the market shifted. YouTube trained people to expect free video. MOOCs emerged, promising education at scale. Competitors like Skillshare and Udemy launched with marketplace models — anyone could teach, pay-per-course pricing. Lynda.com stuck with subscriptions and curated instruction. It looked old-fashioned. But the targeting was clearer: professionals who needed reliable training, not hobbyists browsing free content. Corporations became the growth engine. HR departments bought site licenses. Lynda.com became the default training solution for Fortune 500 companies.

In 2013, they raised their first outside capital — a reported $186 million from Accel and Spectrum Equity. Not because they needed it to survive, but to scale faster. By then they had 3 million subscribers, 200 employees, and 2,000 courses. The funding let them expand internationally, improve technology, and accelerate content production. But it also changed the endgame. Venture capital has an exit timeline.

LinkedIn had been watching. They'd built the world's professional network but lacked a compelling reason for daily usage. Learning was the obvious answer. Professionals wanted to build skills. LinkedIn had data on what skills mattered. They made an offer in April 2015: $1.5 billion in cash and stock.

For Lynda Weinman, the decision was complicated. She'd built the company over 20 years without surrendering control. But she was 60. The market was moving toward platform wars — Google, Apple, Microsoft, Amazon all investing in learning. Staying independent meant competing with infinite resources. LinkedIn offered scale, reach, and a chance to make Lynda's content available to LinkedIn's 300 million members. She said yes.

The acquisition completed in May 2015. Lynda.com became LinkedIn Learning. The brand slowly dissolved. Weinman stayed through the transition, then stepped back. LinkedIn itself was acquired by Microsoft in 2016 for $26 billion. The Lynda.com videos are still there, rebranded, embedded in Microsoft's enterprise training offerings. But the company as Weinman built it — the small, deliberate, Ojai-based teaching institution — ended with the sale.

What made Lynda.com work wasn't first-mover advantage or venture scale. It was trust. Weinman built credibility as a teacher, then systematized teaching. She stayed focused on one thing — helping professionals learn software — while competitors chased broader visions. The subscription model created predictable revenue. The curated approach ensured quality. The corporate market provided scale without mass-market volatility.

The sale looks like triumph — $1.5 billion from a bootstrapped start. But it also reveals the limits of independence in the platform era. Even a profitable, growing, category-leading company faces pressure from giants who can afford to lose money. LinkedIn paid for access to content and credibility. Microsoft paid for data on professional skills. The teaching business became infrastructure for other ambitions.

Weinman built an empire teaching people to use tools. Then the tool-makers bought the teachers.
