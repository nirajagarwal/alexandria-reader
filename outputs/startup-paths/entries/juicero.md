# Juicero

***The $700 million monument to solving problems that didn't exist***

> **Years:** 2013-2017

In 2013, Doug Evans had a vision. A raw food enthusiast who'd built and sold an organic juice company, Evans believed the future of health was fresh-pressed juice in every home. Not juice from a bottle. Not juice from concentrate. Juice pressed from whole fruits and vegetables, extracted by thousands of pounds of force, consumed within moments of pressing. The kind of juice that cost $10 at boutique juice bars, now available at the touch of a button in your kitchen.

The problem, as Evans saw it, was that home juicers were terrible. They oxidized nutrients. They left pulp. They required cleaning. They produced inferior juice. What the world needed was a completely new approach: a machine with the pressing power of commercial juicers, paired with pre-portioned produce packs that eliminated prep work, waste, and variability. A Keurig for juice, but vastly more sophisticated.

Evans was charismatic and convinced. He spoke about Juicero the way Steve Jobs spoke about the iPhone—as inevitable, transformative, necessary. He attracted top talent from Apple and Tesla. He raised $3 million in seed funding, then $70 million in Series A and B rounds from some of Silicon Valley's most respected investors: Kleiner Perkins, Google Ventures, Campbell's Soup. By 2016, Juicero had raised $120 million.

The machine they built was genuinely impressive as an engineering artifact. It contained custom circuit boards, sensors, a QR code scanner, Wi-Fi connectivity, and a pressing mechanism that exerted 8,000 pounds of force. The industrial design was sleek. The tolerances were tight. One early prototype cost $750,000 to build. The first production units, priced at $699, were still sold at a loss.

The produce packs—what Juicero called "Produce Packs"—were equally sophisticated. Pre-chopped fruits and vegetables sealed in proprietary bags with QR codes that connected to the cloud. The machine would scan the code, verify the pack wasn't expired, then press the bag between two metal plates, extracting juice through a built-in filter. The packs cost $5-7 each and produced one 8-ounce glass of juice. They required cold chain logistics and had a week-long shelf life.

In March 2016, Juicero launched in three test markets. The company had lined up retail partnerships and built out subscription infrastructure. The press was initially positive—here was a company trying to revolutionize a category, backed by serious investors, with a founder who spoke passionately about transforming health. Early customers were Silicon Valley true believers, the kind who appreciated precision engineering and were willing to pay premium prices for optimized nutrition.

But the economics were punishing. Each machine cost more to build than the retail price. The produce packs required specialized facilities, cold storage, and rapid distribution. Juicero was manufacturing and distributing fresh produce at scale—essentially running a subset of the grocery business with none of the efficiencies. The company needed massive volume to reach profitability.

The volume never came. At $699 plus $5-7 per serving, Juicero was competing with $10 juice bars, where consumers could see the produce being pressed and drink immediately without a $699 capital outlay. It was competing with $50 home juicers, despite producing a different kind of juice through a vastly more complex process that most consumers couldn't perceive or value. It was competing with $4 cold-pressed juice bottles at Whole Foods.

By April 2017, facing slow sales, Juicero dropped the machine price to $399. It didn't help. The company was burning through capital. Then, in April 2017, Bloomberg published a video demonstration that became viral. Reporters showed that you could squeeze the produce packs by hand—without the $400 machine—and get roughly the same amount of juice in roughly the same time.

The video was devastating. It turned Juicero from an ambitious startup into a punchline overnight. Late-night comedians mocked it. Twitter was merciless. The narrative flipped completely: this wasn't sophisticated engineering solving a real problem; this was over-engineered absurdity, Silicon Valley hubris in physical form, a monument to venture capital's disconnect from consumer reality.

Juicero's defense was technically correct. The machine did extract slightly more juice with less effort and more consistency than hand-squeezing. The QR code scanning prevented using expired packs. The connected features enabled remote troubleshooting. But these arguments were beside the point. The video had revealed the fundamental problem: even if the machine did something marginally better, it was solving a problem—drinking cold-pressed juice at home—that most consumers didn't have or value at that price point.

The company tried to recover. They emphasized health benefits, convenience, the quality of the produce sourcing. They highlighted sustainability efforts. Nothing worked. Consumer perception had crystallized around those hands squeezing the bag. Even supporters couldn't escape the question: why does this need to exist?

Four months later, in September 2017, Juicero shut down. They offered refunds to customers. The company had raised $120 million and spent nearly all of it. Evans, in his final message to customers, maintained that Juicero had been a "remarkable journey" and expressed pride in the innovation.

The investors, collectively, had funded what became a cautionary tale. Not a cautionary tale about technology—the engineering was real—but about market need. Juicero had solved the problem of inconsistent home juicing, but most consumers either didn't juice at home, didn't see inconsistency as a problem worth solving, or were willing to accept inconsistency at a much lower price point. The company had built sophisticated supply chain and hardware solutions for a market that barely existed at the required price point, then scaled before discovering whether anyone wanted it badly enough to sustain the business model.

The produce packs that required a machine to press became the perfect metaphor for product-market misfit: solving a problem that was easier solved without the solution.
