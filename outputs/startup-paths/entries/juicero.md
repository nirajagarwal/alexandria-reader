# Juicero

***The $400 juicer that didn't need to exist***

> **Years:** 2013-2017

In 2013, Doug Evans believed he had cracked the code on fresh juice at home. Not juice from a bottle. Not juice you made yourself with a messy blender. Something in between — perfect, cold-pressed juice, made from pre-packaged produce, extracted by a machine so precisely engineered it would sell for $700.

Evans came from the raw food world. He'd founded Organic Avenue, a chain of juice bars in New York, and emerged convinced that Americans needed better access to fresh juice but lacked the time or equipment to make it properly. Cold-pressed juice — made by crushing produce under thousands of pounds of pressure rather than spinning blades — retained more nutrients and tasted cleaner. But traditional cold-press machines were industrial, expensive, and required actual produce and cleanup. Evans envisioned packets of pre-chopped, perfectly proportioned organic fruits and vegetables that you'd insert into a countertop press. The machine would do the rest.

He called it Juicero. The pitch was elegant: subscribe to receive packets of produce, each containing ingredients for a single glass of juice. Slide the packet into the Juicero press. The machine would scan a QR code to verify freshness, then apply four tons of force to extract every drop. Eight ounces of perfect juice in two minutes. No mess, no cleanup, no waste. Farm to glass.

Silicon Valley loved it. Kleiner Perkins led a $70 million Series B in 2015. Google Ventures joined. Campbell's Soup invested. Here was a company that could own the entire supply chain — growing produce, packing it, selling the hardware, and running the subscription. Razors and blades, but for wellness. Total funding eventually reached $120 million.

The Juicero press was a marvel of over-engineering. It contained hundreds of custom parts, precision sensors, circuit boards, and a motor strong enough to generate 8,000 pounds of force. It connected to WiFi to download packet data and receive software updates. It was made from aircraft-grade aluminum. The first units cost $699. In the offices, they called it "the Tesla of juicers."

They hired teams from Apple and NASA. They built a factory in Los Angeles designed to manufacture at scale. They negotiated with organic farms across California. They designed proprietary packets — made from multiple layers of food-safe material, with valves that directed juice into your glass while keeping pulp and fiber contained. Each packet cost roughly $5 to $8, depending on the blend.

The product launched in 2016. Early reviews were mixed. The juice tasted good — fresh, cold-pressed juice generally does. But the machine was loud, slow, and preposterously expensive. The packets had short shelf lives and required refrigeration. If you were traveling or the delivery was late, you'd be stuck with expired produce. The subscription model meant recurring costs that quickly exceeded what most people spent on juice. And it only made juice. For $700, you could buy a Vitamix that made smoothies, soups, nut butter, and yes, juice.

Still, the company pushed forward. They dropped the price to $400. They expanded into Whole Foods and Williams Sonoma. They raised more money. Silicon Valley investors who'd funded Tesla and Nest saw similar potential — a premium hardware product that owned a consumable category.

Then in April 2017, Bloomberg published a video. A reporter demonstrated that you could squeeze the Juicero packets with your bare hands and get roughly the same amount of juice as the machine produced. Slightly slower, slightly messier, but basically equivalent. Four tons of force, hundreds of engineering hours, $120 million in funding — and the machine was optional.

The video went viral. Late-night hosts mocked it. It became the symbol of everything wrong with Silicon Valley — overfunded, over-engineered solutions to problems that didn't exist, pitched to investors who'd never questioned whether the hardware added value. The company insisted the machine provided consistent pressure, tracked freshness, and would enable future features. But the narrative was set. Juicero was a punchline.

Sales collapsed. Retailers pulled the product. Evans stepped down as CEO. The board brought in a turnaround specialist, but the physics were unforgiving. The hardware had been sold at a loss, subsidized by expected subscription revenue. Without subscribers, the model broke. They couldn't pivot to selling just packets — without the machine, they were just pre-made juice in bags, competing with dozens of cheaper options.

In September 2017, four months after the video, Juicero shut down. They refunded customers who'd bought machines. The intellectual property was sold off. The factory closed. Employees scattered to other startups.

The postmortems were savage. How did smart investors fund a juicer that wasn't needed? Why did no one squeeze the packet by hand before investing? But the deeper lesson was about the difference between technical achievement and product necessity. Juicero solved a manufacturing problem — how to apply industrial force in a countertop form factor. It just never solved the right customer problem. People who wanted fresh juice either made it themselves or bought it. People who wanted convenience bought bottled juice. The narrow slice of customers who wanted fresh cold-pressed juice but couldn't squeeze a bag turned out to be very small.

Doug Evans moved to Brazil. The Juicero press became a collector's item, a monument to the moment Silicon Valley fell in love with its own complexity.
