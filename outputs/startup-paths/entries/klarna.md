# Klarna

***The buy now, pay later empire that made checkout frictionless and debt fashionable***

> **Years:** 2005-present

Sebastian Siemiatkowski, Niklas Adalberth, and Victor Jacobsson met at Stockholm School of Economics in 2005. They were in their early twenties. They watched friends abandon online shopping carts at checkout — too many steps, too much friction, credit card forms that felt like interrogations. E-commerce was growing, but trust remained fragile. Consumers feared fraud. Merchants lost sales to checkout anxiety.

The insight: flip the risk model. Instead of consumers trusting merchants with payment information, have a trusted intermediary assume the risk. Let people buy now, pay later. Make checkout a single click.

Sweden in 2005 was perfect terrain. High internet penetration, digitally literate population, strong credit infrastructure. The banking system was conservative — credit cards weren't ubiquitous. Invoicing was common. Klarna would digitize that cultural norm, making it instant.

They launched with merchants first. The pitch: we'll handle payment risk, you get higher conversion. Klarna guaranteed payment even if the customer defaulted. For consumers: buy without entering card details, pay within 14 or 30 days. No interest if paid on time. Simple.

Early traction came from fashion e-commerce. Young shoppers loved it. Try before you pay. Return what doesn't fit. Klarna absorbed the friction that killed impulse purchases. Merchants saw conversion rates jump 20-30%. Word spread.

The Swedish market was too small for the ambition they had. By 2010, they pushed into Norway, Finland, Denmark, Germany. Each country required new banking partnerships, credit models adjusted for local behavior. Germany was the prize — Europe's largest e-commerce market — but also the hardest. German consumers were debt-averse. Klarna positioned itself not as lending but as flexible payment. It worked.

Funding came steadily. Sequoia led a round in 2010 after watching Klarna's merchant retention numbers. When a platform has 80% repeat merchants and growing consumer habit, investors pay attention. By 2011, Klarna was processing millions of transactions monthly. The flywheel spun: more merchants attracted more consumers, more consumers attracted more merchants.

But competition arrived. PayPal launched installment products. Banks woke up. Afterpay emerged in Australia with a similar model but focused on younger demographics and smaller purchases. Klarna responded by going bigger and broader. Not just fashion — electronics, travel, anything. Not just invoices — split payments into installments. Not just online — partner with physical stores.

The U.S. expansion in 2015 was the bet that would define the next chapter. America had abundant credit but also credit card debt exhaustion. Millennials and Gen Z were skeptical of traditional credit. Klarna framed buy now, pay later as budgeting, not borrowing. No interest (if you paid on time), no hard credit checks, no revolving debt trap.

Partnerships accelerated everything. H&M, Zara, Macy's, Sephora. By 2019, Klarna had deals with 200,000 merchants globally. They launched a shopping app that aggregated deals and let users browse with Klarna-powered checkout embedded. The app wasn't just payments — it was discovery. Klarna became a destination.

The pandemic supercharged demand. E-commerce surged. Younger shoppers, stuck at home with stimulus checks, discovered buy now, pay later en masse. Klarna's valuation hit $45.6 billion in June 2021 — Europe's highest-valued private tech company. Siemiatkowski was everywhere — podcasts, profiles, the face of fintech's new wave.

Then the market turned. Interest rates rose. Tech valuations collapsed. Klarna's lending model — profitable when capital was cheap and defaults low — became expensive. Consumer debt started creeping up. Regulators in the U.K. and U.S. began scrutinizing buy now, pay later, asking if it encouraged overspending, particularly among young, financially precarious users.

Revenue was growing, but losses were too. Klarna had spent heavily on marketing, U.S. expansion, product development. In 2022, they raised at a $6.7 billion valuation — an 85% drop. Layoffs followed. Ten percent of staff in May, another round in November.

Siemiatkowski pivoted the narrative. Klarna wasn't just payments — it was AI-powered shopping. They leaned into machine learning for fraud detection, credit assessment, personalized recommendations. In 2024, Klarna announced its AI assistant handled two-thirds of customer service chats, replacing hundreds of human agents. Efficiency became the story when growth stalled.

But the core tension remained. Was buy now, pay later innovation or repackaged credit? Klarna argued it was both — and better. No compound interest, transparent terms, budgeting tools. Critics saw predatory lending with a millennial aesthetic. Studies showed users spent more with buy now, pay later. Defaults, while low compared to credit cards, were rising.

By 2024, Klarna was profitable in some markets, still burning cash in others. They filed confidentially for a U.S. IPO, targeting 2025. The narrative shifted again: not hyper-growth fintech darling but sustainable payments infrastructure. Slower, steadier, less glamorous.

The company that made checkout frictionless had revealed a deeper friction — between convenience and consequence. Klarna succeeded by removing the moment of hesitation before purchase. That was the product. Whether that moment existed for a reason — whether friction was feature or bug — remained unresolved.

Klarna became enormous by making spending easier. The outcome, still unfolding, will reveal whether easier was better.
