# GitLab

***The open-source company that turned transparency into competitive advantage***

> **Years:** 2011-present

In 2011, Dmitriy Zaporozhets needed a better way to collaborate on code. He was a Ukrainian developer working at a consulting firm, frustrated with the existing tools for hosting Git repositories. GitHub existed, but it was closed-source and cloud-only. For companies that wanted to keep their code behind their own firewalls, options were limited and expensive. So Zaporozhets did what developers do: he built his own solution. He called it GitLab and released it as open source.

For the first two years, GitLab was a side project. Zaporozhets maintained it while working full-time. Users found it, forked it, contributed to it. The open-source model meant anyone could install GitLab on their own servers, modify it to fit their needs, and contribute improvements back. A community formed around the project, but there was no company, no business model, no revenue.

Sid Sijbrandij, a Dutch entrepreneur, was one of those early users. In 2012, he started offering paid support for GitLab installations. He reached out to Zaporozhets with a proposal: turn GitLab into a real company. Zaporozhets became CTO, Sijbrandij became CEO, and in 2013, they officially founded GitLab Inc.

The business model they chose was unusual. They would keep GitLab open source—fully open source, not just "open core" with proprietary features. They would make money by offering a hosted version (GitLab.com) and selling an enterprise edition with additional features that large companies needed: better security, compliance tools, high availability. But even the enterprise edition's source code would be open. Customers could inspect every line. Transparency wasn't a marketing message; it was structural.

This created a strange dynamic with GitHub, which by 2013 had become the default choice for developers. GitHub had the brand, the network effects, the cultural cachet. But GitHub was closed-source and optimizing for individual developers and open-source projects. GitLab focused on what enterprises needed: the ability to run on their own infrastructure, audit the code, and integrate with existing systems. They weren't trying to beat GitHub at its own game. They were playing a different game.

In 2015, GitLab raised a $1.5 million seed round. They used it not to move fast and break things, but to systematize how they operated. Sijbrandij made a radical choice: GitLab would be remote-first, with no headquarters. Everyone worked from home. Every decision would be documented in the company handbook, which was public and version-controlled like code. Meeting notes were public. Strategy documents were public. Salary formulas were public. Even board meeting agendas were public.

This wasn't transparency theater. It was operational doctrine. When you make everything public by default, you can't hide dysfunction. Bad decisions get scrutinized. Conflicts have to be resolved in writing. It forced discipline. It also became a recruiting advantage. Developers—GitLab's customers and potential employees—valued transparency. They could read the handbook and know exactly what working at GitLab would be like before they applied.

The product expanded beyond just Git repository management. GitLab added continuous integration, continuous deployment, security scanning, project management—everything needed for the full software development lifecycle. The vision was to replace the patchwork of tools that development teams used with a single integrated platform. Competitors focused on best-of-breed point solutions. GitLab focused on integration.

The market shifted in their favor. Microsoft acquired GitHub in 2018 for $7.5 billion. Some developers panicked and looked for alternatives. GitLab was the obvious choice. Traffic spiked. Sign-ups surged. But the real benefit wasn't the exodus from GitHub—it was the validation. Microsoft's acquisition proved that developer tools could command massive valuations. GitLab raised a $100 million Series D at a $1.1 billion valuation four months after the GitHub acquisition.

They kept building. By 2020, GitLab had over 30 million users and 100,000 organizations using the platform. The business model worked: a large base of free users on GitLab.com and the open-source edition, with a small percentage converting to paying customers on the enterprise tier. The open-source community contributed features and bug fixes, reducing development costs. Transparency reduced coordination costs. Remote work reduced overhead.

In October 2021, GitLab went public. The IPO priced at $77 per share, valuing the company at $11 billion—higher than GitHub's acquisition price. On the first day of trading, shares jumped 35%. GitLab was now one of the most valuable developer tools companies in the world, and it had gotten there by doing almost everything differently than conventional wisdom suggested: open source instead of proprietary, transparent instead of secretive, remote instead of centralized.

The path GitLab took only worked because the founders made early choices that compounded over time. Open source attracted developers. Transparency attracted talent and trust. Remote work attracted global expertise. Each choice narrowed future options but created advantages competitors couldn't easily copy. By the time GitLab went public, its model was its moat.

Today GitLab competes with GitHub, which now has Microsoft's resources, and with a fragmented landscape of DevOps tools. The outcome isn't settled. But the path is legible: find what incumbents won't or can't do, build there, and let structural choices become strategic advantages.
