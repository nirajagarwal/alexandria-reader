# GitLab

***The company that built in public and bet it all on remote***

> **Years:** 2011-present

In 2011, Dmitriy Zaporozhets was a Ukrainian developer frustrated with the tools available for collaborating on code. GitHub had launched three years earlier and was growing fast, but it was closed-source and cloud-only. Many companies wanted Git repository management behind their own firewalls. Dmitriy started building an open-source alternative called GitLab as a side project.

Sid Sijbrandij, a Dutch entrepreneur, discovered GitLab while looking for solutions at his previous company. He saw what Dmitriy had missed: the open-source project could become a commercial product. In 2013, Sid cold-emailed Dmitriy with a proposal to turn GitLab into a company. They agreed to split it: Dmitriy would continue developing the open-source core while Sid would build the commercial entity around it. Sid put in $100,000. They never met in person before becoming co-founders.

The timing was fortunate. GitHub was dominant but served primarily individual developers and smaller teams. Enterprise software companies were slowly waking up to Git but wanted on-premise solutions their security teams would approve. GitLab offered both: a free open-source version anyone could install, and a paid enterprise edition with additional features. The business model was textbook open-core.

But GitLab made a choice that seemed reckless in 2013: the company would be fully remote from day one. No office. No headquarters beyond a legal address. Employees scattered across timezones. Most venture investors considered this disqualifying. Remote work was what consulting firms did, not venture-scale technology companies. How would they build culture? How would they move fast?

Sid had a different theory. Remote-first meant access to global talent without geographic constraints. It meant lower costs. It meant documentation over hallway conversations, which would force the clarity that growing companies needed anyway. Most importantly, it meant GitLab could be a laboratory for remote work itself — and could build tools for a future where remote collaboration was normal.

The early years were scrappy. GitLab gave away enormous value through its open-source version. Revenue came from companies that wanted support, or premium features like advanced access controls and compliance tools. The sales motion was unusual: many customers started with the free version, deployed it across their engineering teams, then came back months later to buy the enterprise edition. GitLab wasn't selling to prospects. It was converting users.

In 2015, GitLab got its first major validation: Khosla Ventures led a $4 million Series A. The pitch worked because GitLab had metrics VCs understood: thousands of installations, growing enterprise revenue, evidence of bottoms-up adoption inside large companies. But the remote structure still made investors nervous. One asked during diligence: "When are you planning to get a real office?"

The answer was never.

GitLab expanded beyond repository management into the full DevOps lifecycle: continuous integration, continuous deployment, security scanning, project management. The strategy was ambitious bordering on arrogant. They weren't just competing with GitHub. They were competing with Jenkins, CircleCI, Atlassian's suite, and a dozen other specialized tools. The pitch was "single application" — one platform instead of a stitched-together toolchain.

Enterprises bought it. Managing ten different tools meant ten different contracts, ten different security audits, ten different support relationships. GitLab offered simplicity. The product wasn't always best-in-class for each individual function, but the integration value was real. Large banks, telecoms, and government agencies signed six and seven-figure deals.

GitHub, meanwhile, had taken venture funding and was growing but seemed complacent. Then in 2018, Microsoft acquired GitHub for $7.5 billion. The open-source community panicked. Microsoft — the company whose CEO had once called Linux "a cancer" — now controlled the platform where open-source lived. Developers looked for alternatives. GitLab was ready. Migrations surged.

But GitLab had problems too. The "single application" strategy meant building enormous amounts of functionality. The codebase was huge, the product surface area sprawling. Some features felt half-baked. Competitors with focused products could move faster in their niches. The company had to balance breadth against depth, and often chose breadth. The product philosophy was controversial internally and externally.

The remote structure created its own challenges. Miscommunications took longer to resolve. Timezones meant decisions lagged. Some employees struggled with isolation. But GitLab responded by doing something almost unprecedented: they documented everything. Their internal handbook became public — over 2,000 pages covering everything from expense policies to product strategy to how they ran meetings. Transparency became brand.

In 2021, GitLab went public at a $15 billion valuation. It was the first fully remote company to IPO at that scale. The S-1 filing read like a manifesto for distributed work, filed months into a pandemic that had forced the world to admit remote work was possible. GitLab's revenue was over $200 million annually and growing 60% year-over-year. The company had 1,350 employees in 65 countries.

The public markets were less kind than venture investors. The stock fell through 2022 and 2023 as investors worried about competition from GitHub (now integrated with Microsoft's enterprise sales machine), slowing growth, and whether the single-application vision could really work. Revenue grew but losses mounted. The premium valuation started to look expensive.

GitLab today sits in an ambiguous position. It proved remote work could scale to a public company. It proved open-core could build a billion-dollar business. It converted millions of users into thousands of enterprise customers. But it's still fighting the question it started with: can an open-source alternative really compete with a closed-source giant backed by Microsoft's resources? The company is profitable on an adjusted basis, but GAAP profitability remains elusive. Growth continues but has moderated.

The path GitLab took — open-source to enterprise, remote from inception, single application over best-of-breed — wasn't the obvious one. Most investors would have told them to pick an office, pick a narrower product, pick a conventional structure. They didn't. The outcome isn't failure and isn't yet triumph. It's a live experiment in whether heterodox choices compound into defensibility or just make things harder.
