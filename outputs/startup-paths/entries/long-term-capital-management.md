# Long-Term Capital Management

***When the smartest guys in the room built a machine that nearly broke the world***

> **Years:** 1994-1998

In 1994, John Meriwether walked away from Salomon Brothers with a reputation and a plan. He had been the legendary head of the bond arbitrage group, the unit that made Salomon hundreds of millions through mathematical precision. Then came the Treasury auction scandal — not his doing, but he took the fall. Now he would build something new: a fund that would be purer, more sophisticated, less constrained by the messy politics of a Wall Street bank.

He assembled a team that read like a fantasy draft of financial intellect. Myron Scholes and Robert Merton, who would share the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1997. David Mullins, former vice chairman of the Federal Reserve. Eric Rosenfeld, Victor Haghani, Larry Hilibrand — all veterans of Salomon's arbitrage desk. The pitch was simple: these minds would find pricing inefficiencies in global markets and exploit them with leverage. Mathematical certainty applied to financial markets.

They named it Long-Term Capital Management. The "long-term" wasn't just branding — it signaled their strategy. They would make convergence trades: buy undervalued securities, short overvalued ones, and wait for prices to converge to their true relationship. The returns on each trade might be tiny — basis points — but with enough leverage, tiny became enormous.

Wall Street lined up to invest. LTCM's minimum was $10 million. They raised $1.25 billion in their first round, the largest start for a hedge fund in history. Investors included Merrill Lynch, J.P. Morgan, Chase Manhattan, and the chief executives of Merck and General Electric. The fund would charge 2% annual fees plus 25% of profits — more expensive than any competitor. People paid it. These weren't ordinary fund managers. These were scientists.

The early returns justified the hype. In 1995, LTCM returned 43% after fees. In 1996, 41%. In 1997, 17% — lower, but still exceptional, and that was after returning $2.7 billion to investors because they couldn't find enough good trades. The models worked. The market was inefficient, and they had found the seams.

The portfolio was vast and global. They traded U.S. Treasury bonds against German bunds. They bet on interest rate swaps. They played spreads in Japanese equity warrants. They sold volatility, writing options on equity indexes. Each position was hedged. Each was analyzed with Ph.D.-level rigor. And each was leveraged. By early 1998, LTCM had $125 billion in assets — on $4.8 billion of capital. Leverage of 25-to-1, and in some derivative positions, implicit leverage far higher.

The models said this was safe. The positions were hedged. The trades would converge. History suggested the maximum drawdown might be 20%. They built their risk models on decades of data showing how assets behaved. What the models didn't account for was what happens when the models themselves become the market.

By 1998, LTCM's success had spawned imitators. Dozens of funds ran similar strategies. When everyone is betting that spreads will narrow, spreads narrow. The easy profits disappeared. LTCM reached further — into emerging markets, into more exotic swaps, into territories where the data was thinner but the spreads were wider.

Then Russia defaulted. On August 17, 1998, the Russian government announced it would restructure its debt and freeze payments. Markets convulsed. Investors fled to safety. Every trade LTCM had made assumed markets would act rationally, that spreads would converge. Instead, spreads exploded. Safe government bonds surged in price. Riskier bonds collapsed. Nothing converged. Everything diverged.

LTCM's hedges failed because they were all hedged against each other. When every position moved in the same direction — toward panic — the correlations broke. The fund was built to withstand normal volatility. This wasn't normal. In a single week in August, LTCM lost $1.9 billion. By early September, capital had fallen to $600 million. With $125 billion in positions, the leverage was now 200-to-1.

The danger wasn't just to LTCM. The fund was counterparty to nearly every major bank on Wall Street. If LTCM collapsed, it would have to liquidate positions worth more than $100 billion into illiquid markets. The resulting fire sale could trigger a cascade of failures across the financial system. The markets were so interlinked that LTCM's failure might cause the failures of the institutions trying to collect from it.

On September 23, 1998, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York convened a meeting. Sixteen banks gathered in a conference room downtown. The Fed didn't force a bailout, but the message was clear: if LTCM failed catastrophically, everyone in the room would suffer. The banks agreed to a $3.65 billion recapitalization in exchange for 90% of the fund. LTCM's partners, including the Nobel laureates, were left with scraps of a fund that had been worth billions months earlier.

The fund unwound over the following months. The markets calmed. The positions converged, exactly as the models had predicted — but too late. Had LTCM survived September, it would have recovered. But survival required capital they no longer had.

LTCM dissolved in early 2000. The investors who bought in during the bailout actually made money as positions normalized. The original partners lost nearly everything. The models were right about the long term. They just couldn't survive the short term.

The lesson wasn't that the mathematics was wrong. It was that markets are made of people, and people panic. You can have perfect models and the smartest team ever assembled and still fail if you don't account for what happens when everyone is wrong at the same time. Intelligence isn't enough. Capital isn't enough. You also need to survive the moment when the world doesn't behave the way it's supposed to.
