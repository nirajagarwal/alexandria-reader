# Long-Term Capital Management

***When the smartest guys in the room built a machine that couldn't fail — until it did***

> **Years:** 1994-1998

In 1994, John Meriwether walked out of Salomon Brothers with a plan to build the most sophisticated hedge fund the world had ever seen. He had been forced out after the Treasury bond scandal, but his reputation as a master bond trader remained intact. More importantly, he had relationships with the kind of people who could turn finance into physics.

He assembled a partnership that read like a Nobel Prize committee. Myron Scholes and Robert Merton — who would win the Nobel in Economics in 1997 for their work on derivatives pricing. David Mullins, former vice chairman of the Federal Reserve. A team of PhD mathematicians and computer scientists from the Salomon arbitrage group. They weren't just traders. They were building a machine to extract inefficiency from global markets.

The pitch was elegant: markets occasionally misprice nearly identical securities. A German government bond and an Italian government bond with similar maturities should trade at predictable spreads. When they don't, you short the expensive one, buy the cheap one, wait for convergence, and collect the difference. It's arbitrage — theoretically risk-free profit from temporary dislocations.

But the spreads were tiny. To make real money, you needed massive leverage. Long-Term Capital Management started with $1.25 billion in investor capital — an enormous raise for a new fund. They immediately borrowed $125 billion more. The leverage ratio climbed from there.

The first three years were spectacular. Returns of 21%, then 43%, then 41%. By early 1998, LTCM managed $4.8 billion in equity with positions worth over $1 trillion in notional value. They had positions in every major market: U.S. Treasuries, European government bonds, Japanese stocks, emerging market debt, mortgage-backed securities. The computer models hummed. The partners collected hundreds of millions in management fees.

The models assumed that markets were rational, that price dislocations would converge, that historical correlations would hold, that liquidity would remain constant. The models were built on decades of data. They accounted for multiple standard deviations of risk. They were mathematically sound.

In May 1998, Russia began defaulting on its domestic debt. This should have been containable — LTCM's direct Russia exposure was modest. But the Russian default triggered something the models hadn't anticipated: a global flight to quality. Investors everywhere abandoned anything risky and piled into U.S. Treasuries.

Every one of LTCM's positions was a bet on convergence — that spreads would narrow, that correlations would normalize, that markets would be rational. Instead, everything moved in the same direction at once. Spreads widened. Positions that were supposed to be uncorrelated became perfectly correlated. The Italian bonds they were long fell. The German bonds they were short rose. Mortgage-backed securities collapsed. Emerging market debt cratered. Every bet lost simultaneously.

Worse: they couldn't exit. LTCM's positions were so large that attempting to unwind them would move markets against them. Other traders knew this. The fund was being picked apart by competitors who could see their forced selling. The leverage that amplified gains on the way up accelerated the destruction on the way down.

By August, LTCM had lost 44% of its capital. The partners injected their own money — $100 million from Meriwether alone. It didn't matter. The losses continued. $500 million in a single day. Then $700 million. By September, the fund was down to $600 million in equity supporting positions worth more than $100 billion. The leverage ratio exceeded 150 to 1.

The Federal Reserve convened an emergency meeting. Not to bail out LTCM directly — this wasn't a bank. But LTCM's collapse would force liquidation of massive positions across global markets. The shock could trigger a systemic crisis. Fourteen major banks were organized into a consortium. They injected $3.65 billion in exchange for 90% of the fund. It was a takeover, not a rescue.

The positions were unwound over months. By the time it was finished, the original investors had lost 90% of their capital. The partners lost nearly everything. Scholes and Merton's Nobel Prize became a symbol of hubris — awarded in October 1997, just as the fund was beginning its death spiral.

The aftermath revealed what the models had missed: that risk cannot be fully quantified, that rare events happen more often than statistics predict, that leverage transforms temporary losses into permanent catastrophe, that correlation becomes one in a crisis. LTCM had built a machine that worked perfectly — until it encountered a world that didn't conform to its assumptions.

Years later, Meriwether started another hedge fund. Several LTCM partners went on to prestigious roles in finance. The models got more sophisticated. The leverage remained.
