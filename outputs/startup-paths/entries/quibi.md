# Quibi

***The billion-dollar bet that nobody wanted***

> **Years:** 2018-2020

In 2018, Jeffrey Katzenberg had an idea that seemed to make perfect sense: people watch video on their phones, but nobody is making premium content designed specifically for mobile viewing. Not YouTube clips, not Netflix on a small screen — something built from the ground up for the way people actually use their phones. Quick bites of Hollywood-quality content, each episode under ten minutes, designed to be watched in portrait or landscape mode. He called it Quibi.

Katzenberg was not a typical startup founder. He had run Disney's animation renaissance in the 1990s, then co-founded DreamWorks. He knew entertainment. He knew hits. And he knew how to raise money. For Quibi, he raised $1.75 billion before launching a single product — the largest pre-launch funding round in Silicon Valley history. The pitch was irresistible: proven Hollywood executive, massive untapped market, technology that would revolutionize mobile video. Investors lined up.

As CEO, he recruited Meg Whitman, former head of eBay and HP. Two titans of their respective industries, combining entertainment and technology. The leadership team projected absolute confidence. They weren't trying to disrupt anything — they were inventing a new category. "Quick bites" of premium content for the mobile generation.

The technical innovation was real. Quibi's proprietary "Turnstyle" technology let viewers rotate their phones and see different shots — not just the same frame cropped differently, but distinct cinematography for each orientation. Directors could tell stories differently in portrait and landscape. It was genuinely novel. Katzenberg signed deals with every major Hollywood studio and recruited A-list talent: Chrissy Teigen, Liam Hemsworth, Steven Spielberg. They commissioned 175 shows for the first year.

But the business model revealed something about Katzenberg's assumptions. Quibi would be subscription-only, $4.99 with ads or $7.99 without. No free tier. No sharing clips on social media. No watching on TV. Phone only. They were betting that people would pay for the privilege of watching expensively-produced short videos on their phones during commutes and lunch breaks, and that these viewers would be so captivated they wouldn't miss the ability to share what they were watching.

The launch was set for April 6, 2020.

The world had other plans. COVID-19 lockdowns began in March. Suddenly, nobody was commuting. Nobody was killing time in line. People were stuck at home with their TVs, and Quibi only worked on phones. The entire premise — content for moments of mobility — evaporated overnight.

Katzenberg initially blamed the pandemic for everything. But the problems ran deeper. When Quibi launched, it became immediately clear that the market they'd imagined didn't exist — pandemic or not. The app attracted curiosity downloads from the massive marketing spend, but retention was abysmal. After the 90-day free trial, almost nobody paid. By late April, the app had dropped out of the top 50 free iPhone apps.

The content was expensive and well-made, but it didn't create the behavior change Katzenberg expected. People already had YouTube, TikTok, Instagram — free platforms with endless content and social sharing built in. Quibi offered neither the addictive scroll of social video nor the depth of long-form streaming. It was a product designed for a customer who didn't exist: someone willing to pay for premium short-form content they couldn't share, couldn't watch on TV, and had no way to discover except through algorithmic recommendations in a walled garden.

The no-sharing policy was particularly fatal. Entertainment in the smartphone era lives through virality. Quibi's content couldn't go viral — you literally couldn't send a clip to a friend. They were trying to create watercooler moments in a sealed room.

Three months after launch, Quibi had 1.5 million active users. Netflix had 183 million. Katzenberg started blaming the pandemic again in investor calls, but Whitman was more circumspect: maybe the product itself was wrong. They added the ability to cast to TVs, to take screenshots, to share clips — all the things they'd deliberately excluded. Nothing worked.

By September, it was clear the company was dying. The content library that cost hundreds of millions to produce was generating almost no subscription revenue. The $1.75 billion war chest was burning fast. Katzenberg and Whitman began exploring a sale. They approached Facebook, NBCUniversal, WarnerMedia. Nobody wanted it. The content was expensive and non-exclusive, the technology wasn't particularly valuable, and the user base was tiny.

On October 21, 2020 — less than seven months after launch — Katzenberg and Whitman announced they were shutting down. In his announcement, Katzenberg acknowledged "the idea itself wasn't strong enough to justify a standalone streaming service." The $1.75 billion had evaporated. Quibi sold its content library for parts. The technology was sold to Roku for under $100 million.

What's striking about Quibi is how perfectly it embodied a particular kind of failure: the expert's blind spot. Katzenberg knew entertainment. Whitman knew technology and business. Together they had credentials that made skepticism seem naive. They raised unprecedented amounts because investors trusted the pedigree more than they questioned the premise. But expertise in one domain doesn't transfer. Hollywood's rules — big budgets, exclusive content, controlled distribution — collided with mobile's rules — free, shareable, multi-platform. Quibi tried to impose the former on the latter. The market said no.

The story wasn't about the pandemic, though that didn't help. It was about building an expensive answer to a question nobody was asking. People didn't want premium mobile-only short-form video badly enough to pay for it and give up sharing. That was true in April 2020, and it would have been true in any other April. Katzenberg and Whitman bet $1.75 billion that they understood the future of entertainment. The future arrived and scrolled right past them.
