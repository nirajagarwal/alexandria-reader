# Quibi

***The billion-dollar bet that mobile video would be different***

> **Years:** 2018-2020

Jeffrey Katzenberg had run Disney's animation renaissance and co-founded DreamWorks. Meg Whitman had led eBay through explosive growth and run for governor of California. When they announced their new venture in 2018, investors lined up. Here were two proven executives with a simple thesis: people wanted premium short-form video designed for mobile phones. They called it Quibi — quick bites — and raised $1.75 billion before launching a single product.

The idea emerged from Katzenberg's belief that Hollywood had missed the mobile revolution. YouTube was amateur. Netflix was for the couch. Instagram was social. But nobody was making professional entertainment specifically engineered for phones — vertical video, episodes under ten minutes, designed to be watched while commuting or waiting in line. Katzenberg saw white space. He had connections to every studio and star in Hollywood. Whitman had the operational expertise to build the platform. The timing seemed perfect.

They spent two years before launch. The budget was staggering — $1.1 billion for content in year one alone. Steven Spielberg developed a horror series that could only be watched after dark. Guillermo del Toro, Idris Elba, Liam Hemsworth, Jennifer Lopez — A-list talent lined up. They shot in "Turnstyle" — a proprietary technology that let viewers rotate their phones between vertical and horizontal, with both versions filmed simultaneously. The shows weren't long-form chopped into pieces. They were architected for mobile from conception.

The business model was equally bold. Quibi would launch with two tiers: $4.99 with ads, $7.99 without. Content would disappear after viewing — no screenshots, no sharing clips, no watching on TV. This wasn't just mobile-first. It was mobile-only. The restrictions were features, not bugs. Quibi would be scarce in an age of infinite content, premium in an age of free.

They launched April 6, 2020.

The timing was catastrophic. COVID-19 had locked down America two weeks earlier. Nobody was commuting. Nobody was waiting in lines. The entire thesis — entertainment for in-between moments — assumed a world that had just stopped existing. People were home, watching Netflix on TVs. Quibi had optimized for a use case that vanished overnight.

But timing wasn't the only problem. The restrictions that seemed like features revealed themselves as liabilities. Users couldn't share clips, which meant no organic growth through social media. They couldn't cast to TVs, which made the vertical video feel gimmicky rather than revolutionary. The content itself, while expensive, wasn't generating buzz. A show about a woman shrunk to miniature size. A Dummy about a sex doll. Nothing broke through. The cultural conversation happened elsewhere.

The download numbers looked promising at first — 1.7 million in the first week. Then they collapsed. By May, the app had fallen out of the top 50 on both iOS and Android. Free trial conversions were dismal. Katzenberg blamed the pandemic in interviews. "If we knew on March 1 what we know now, you would say it's not a good idea to launch," he told the New York Times. But the problems ran deeper than circumstances.

YouTube had trained a generation to expect free. Netflix and Disney+ offered unlimited libraries for the same price or less. TikTok had proven that mobile video could be massive — but through user-generated content and algorithmic feeds, not Hollywood production values and curated releases. Quibi was premium in form but entered a market where premium was measured by volume, not quality per minute.

The content spend accelerated anyway. By summer they'd announced a second year of programming. But the users weren't coming. By June, only 72,000 people were paying subscribers. The company had spent over $1.75 billion and captured a rounding error of the streaming market. Advisors suggested pivoting — allow TV casting, enable sharing, drop the mobile-only restriction. Each suggestion undermined the original thesis. If Quibi became just another streaming service, what was the point?

October 21, 2020 — six months after launch — Katzenberg and Whitman announced Quibi was shutting down. "We feel that we've exhausted all our options," Whitman wrote to employees. They returned $350 million to investors — a remarkable outcome in startup failure, possible only because they'd barely spent half the war chest. The content library was sold piecemeal. Roku bought the technology and shows for an undisclosed sum reported to be under $100 million.

The post-mortems were immediate. Some blamed COVID-19. Some blamed the restrictions. Some blamed the content. But the deeper failure was conceptual. Katzenberg and Whitman had built for a user behavior they believed should exist rather than observing what did exist. They'd raised too much money, which removed the pressure to learn and adjust. They'd hired Hollywood royalty, which reinforced their assumptions rather than challenging them. And they'd launched into a market where the answer to "what should I watch on my phone?" was already answered — by TikTok, YouTube, Instagram, and every other streaming service that worked on mobile as well as it worked anywhere else.

Quibi proved that pedigree and capital can't manufacture a market. Sometimes the gap you see is a gap nobody wants filled.
