# IBM

***The giant that dominated computing, missed the revolution it created, and survived by becoming something else entirely***

> **Years:** 1911-present

In 1911, the Computing-Tabulating-Recording Company was formed through a merger of three businesses making scales, time clocks, and punch card tabulators. The tabulating machines, descended from Herman Hollerith's designs for the 1890 census, were the crown jewel. Thomas J. Watson Sr., a former NCR salesman facing legal troubles, took over in 1914. He renamed it International Business Machines in 1924 and built it into something unprecedented: a company that didn't just sell machines but controlled an entire ecosystem.

Watson's insight was that punch card data processing wasn't about hardware — it was about information management as a service. IBM leased rather than sold machines, maintained them, trained operators, and designed custom solutions. Customers became dependent. The punch cards themselves, sold separately, were a recurring revenue stream. By the 1950s, IBM had 90% market share in tabulating equipment. The US government filed an antitrust suit in 1952.

Then came computers. IBM's initial response to UNIVAC and other early electronic computers was skeptical. Watson Sr. famously estimated world demand for computers at maybe five. But his son, Thomas Watson Jr., saw differently. He forced IBM into computing, betting the company on the System/360 in the early 1960s — a family of compatible computers that could run the same software. It was the largest privately financed commercial project ever attempted. It nearly bankrupted IBM. It also created the modern computer industry.

System/360 succeeded beyond imagination. By the 1970s, IBM was computing. "No one ever got fired for buying IBM" became the industry's safest maxim. The company's market capitalization exceeded the GDP of many countries. Its research labs produced fundamental breakthroughs: DRAM, relational databases, RISC architecture. Its salesforce, in their white shirts and dark suits, were legendary. IBM didn't just dominate — it defined what business computing meant.

The antitrust pressure continued. A Justice Department suit filed in 1969 accused IBM of monopolistic practices. The case dragged on for thirteen years before the Reagan administration dropped it in 1982, calling it "without merit." But by then, IBM had already made the decision that would undo its dominance.

In 1980, IBM needed a personal computer quickly. The PC market was emerging — Apple, Commodore, Tandy — and IBM's normal development cycle was too slow. So they did something radical: they built the PC from off-the-shelf components. Intel processor. Microsoft operating system. Open architecture that anyone could clone. They assumed their brand and sales channels would maintain control.

The IBM PC launched in 1981 and became the industry standard almost immediately. But IBM had created the seeds of its own displacement. Because the architecture was open and the key components — CPU and OS — were controlled by Intel and Microsoft, competitors could build "IBM compatibles" for less. Compaq, Dell, Gateway flooded the market. IBM had commoditized itself.

Meanwhile, IBM missed everything that mattered. It missed the shift from mainframes to client-server. It missed networking. It missed the internet. It missed software as the locus of value. Through the 1980s and early 1990s, IBM kept optimizing for a world that was disappearing. When that world finally collapsed, IBM collapsed with it. Between 1990 and 1993, the company lost $16 billion — at the time, the largest corporate loss in American history. It laid off over 100,000 people. Analysts openly questioned whether IBM would survive.

Lou Gerstner, brought in from RJR Nabisco in 1993, saved IBM by abandoning almost everything it believed about itself. He kept the company together when conventional wisdom said to break it up. He shifted from products to services — helping other companies manage their computing infrastructure. He made IBM a consulting company that happened to also make hardware and software. Most radically, he admitted IBM didn't have all the answers. The company that once insisted customers use only IBM products became the integrator of everyone else's technology.

The transformation worked. By the time Gerstner left in 2002, IBM was profitable and growing again. But it was a different company. The subsequent decades saw IBM sell off its defining businesses: PCs to Lenovo in 2005, servers to Lenovo in 2014. It bought PwC Consulting, becoming even more services-focused. It invested billions in Watson, its AI initiative, with mixed results. It bought Red Hat for $34 billion in 2019, betting on hybrid cloud and open source — technologies antithetical to the IBM of old.

Today IBM still exists, still generates tens of billions in revenue, still employs hundreds of thousands. But it is no longer the giant that made computing possible and then controlled it. It missed the PC revolution it created, missed the internet, missed mobile, missed social, missed the cloud initially. It survived not by winning the new game but by finding a different role: the enterprise IT department's outsourced infrastructure, the integrator of other people's innovation.

The lesson of IBM is that dominance is not destiny. The company that owned computing became a bystander to the computing revolution. It survived, but survival meant becoming unrecognizable. The punch card company became the mainframe company became the PC company became the services company became the cloud integration company. At each turn, it carried forward a smaller piece of what it once was, until the thing that survived bore almost no resemblance to the thing that dominated. That it exists at all is remarkable. That it exists as something so diminished is the price of missing the future you created.
