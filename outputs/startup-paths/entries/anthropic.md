# Anthropic

***The safety-first AI lab that became OpenAI's rival***

> **Years:** 2021-present

In May 2021, seven researchers left OpenAI. The departure wasn't quiet. Dario Amodei had been OpenAI's Vice President of Research. His sister Daniela had been VP of Operations. They'd watched their former employer transform from nonprofit research lab to something harder to define—a capped-profit entity with a $1 billion investment from Microsoft, building products, chasing scale, and in their view, moving too fast.

They founded Anthropic with a specific thesis: AI safety couldn't be bolted on after the fact. It had to be foundational. The company would build large language models, but differently. The approach was called "Constitutional AI"—training models with explicit values and constraints from the start, making them more predictable, more aligned, safer.

The timing mattered. GPT-3 had demonstrated the power of large language models in 2020, but the technology still lived mostly in research papers and developer APIs. The public didn't yet understand what was coming. That gave Anthropic a window.

The founding team was formidable. Beyond the Amodei siblings, it included Chris Olah, a legendary figure in AI interpretability research, and several others who'd worked on GPT-3. They knew how to build these systems. They'd done it before. Now they wanted to do it right.

Funding came quickly. By 2022, Anthropic had raised $704 million, led by Sam Bankman-Fried's FTX and Alameda Research, with participation from Google, Spark Capital, and others. The SBF connection would later become awkward when FTX collapsed, but at the time it signaled something: serious money believed safety-focused AI could compete.

The technical approach was genuinely different. Constitutional AI meant training Claude—their model—with a two-step process. First, a model learns to critique its own outputs against a set of principles (the "constitution"). Then it's trained to prefer outputs that better satisfy those principles. The goal was models that were helpful, harmless, and honest by design, not just by filtering.

They published. A lot. Papers on interpretability, on constitutional AI, on scaling laws, on red-teaming. This wasn't a research lab hiding behind trade secrets. It was more like DeepMind's playbook—serious academic rigor, but building products too.

But OpenAI moved first and faster. ChatGPT launched in November 2022 and became the fastest-growing consumer application in history. Suddenly everyone understood what LLMs could do. The window closed. Anthropic was still in research mode when the race began.

They responded by March 2023 with Claude. Not a chatbot interface—that came later—but an API. The pitch was explicit: Claude was safer, more reliable, better at following instructions, less likely to hallucinate or say harmful things. For enterprises nervous about AI risk, that mattered.

Google invested again—heavily. By late 2023, they'd committed up to $2 billion, with an initial $500 million and future tranches tied to usage of Google Cloud. Amazon followed with up to $4 billion, with similar cloud commitments. Anthropic was suddenly playing the same game as OpenAI—raising massive capital from cloud providers who wanted the model layer locked to their infrastructure.

The product strategy evolved. Claude 2 in July 2023 brought a 100,000-token context window—far larger than GPT-4's at launch. That meant Claude could process entire codebases, long documents, whole books. For certain use cases, it was simply better. Then Claude Pro, a consumer subscription. Then Claude for Enterprise.

The positioning sharpened: Anthropic was the responsible choice. While OpenAI's Sam Altman testified to Congress about existential risk and called for AI regulation, Anthropic was actually building the safety features regulators would eventually require. They hired policy experts, published model cards, created systems for monitoring misuse.

Competition intensified. OpenAI, Google's Gemini, Meta's Llama, Mistral, Cohere—the foundation model space was suddenly crowded. But Anthropic had carved a niche. Enterprises that needed AI but feared reputational risk chose Claude. So did AI safety researchers. So did companies in regulated industries.

By 2024, the technical lead narrowed. Claude 3 launched in March with three variants—Opus, Sonnet, and Haiku—matching or exceeding GPT-4 on benchmarks. The context window hit 200,000 tokens. The safety features worked. The company had actual revenue—hundreds of millions—from API usage and enterprise contracts.

But the cost structure was brutal. Training runs cost hundreds of millions. Inference was expensive. Anthropic was burning through capital, though at a slower rate than some competitors because they hadn't yet bet everything on a consumer play. The Amazon and Google deals provided runway, but also dependence.

The market position became clearer: Anthropic wasn't going to win by being biggest. They'd win by being trusted. Perplexity, Notion, DuckDuckGo, and others built on Claude. Enterprises like Bridgewater, Lonely Planet, and the Boston Consulting Group chose it. The safety brand mattered.

The broader question remained unanswered: could a safety-first approach actually compete at the frontier? Or would the companies willing to move fastest, push hardest, and worry about safety later win the race? OpenAI had first-mover advantage. Google had distribution. Meta was open-sourcing. Anthropic had principles and performance.

By early 2025, Anthropic was neither the largest AI lab nor the fastest-growing. But it was one of the few that might survive the shakeout ahead. The Amodei siblings had made a bet: that as AI became more powerful, safety wouldn't be optional. That regulators would intervene. That enterprises would demand it. That the constitutional approach would scale.

The outcome wasn't yet written. But Anthropic had proven that leaving OpenAI to build the alternative wasn't hubris. It was viable. Whether viable meant successful, or just not-failed-yet, depended on what happened next in the race they'd helped define.
