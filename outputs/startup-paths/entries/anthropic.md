# Anthropic

***The safety researchers who became OpenAI's existential rival***

> **Years:** 2021-present

In early 2021, seven researchers walked out of OpenAI with a plan that seemed quixotic even by San Francisco standards. They would start a new AI lab focused on safety, raise money at a moment when OpenAI had a multi-year head start, and somehow compete while refusing to compromise on their core principle: advanced AI systems must be interpretable, controllable, and aligned with human values before they're deployed at scale.

The timing looked terrible. OpenAI had already released GPT-3 and was racing toward even larger models. DeepMind had Google's resources. The defectors — led by Dario Amodei, who had been OpenAI's VP of Research, and his sister Daniela, who had led policy — were essentially betting they could build a competitive AI lab that made safety its defining feature rather than an afterthought.

What gave them credibility was what had driven them out. Dario and several others had watched OpenAI transform from a nonprofit research lab into something increasingly commercial. The 2019 creation of a for-profit subsidiary, the $1 billion Microsoft investment, the shift toward rapid deployment — these moves made strategic sense but created exactly the pressures the founders had worried about. When you're racing, safety becomes friction.

They named the company Anthropic after the anthropic principle in physics — the idea that the universe's properties must be compatible with the conscious life observing it. The metaphor was deliberate: AI systems would need to be compatible with human flourishing, not just technically impressive.

The founding team brought unusual depth. Beyond the Amodeis, they recruited Tom Brown (lead author of the GPT-3 paper), Chris Olah (whose work on neural network interpretability had become foundational), Sam McCandlish, Jared Kaplan, and others who had been central to OpenAI's research breakthroughs. This wasn't a handful of disgruntled employees. It was a significant fraction of OpenAI's technical leadership.

Their first challenge was money. Training frontier AI models costs tens of millions of dollars per run. In April 2021, they raised $124 million led by Skype founder Jaan Tallinn and others in the effective altruism community who took AI risk seriously. By 2022, they had raised $700 million more, including $500 million from Alphabet (Google's parent company) and $300 million from FTX. That last source would become complicated.

While fundraising, they were building. Their technical approach centered on "constitutional AI" — training models to be helpful, harmless, and honest through a documented set of principles rather than opaque human feedback. The idea was to make AI alignment systematic and auditable. They published their research openly, including a detailed paper on their training methodology that made competitors uncomfortable by demonstrating how easy it was to jailbreak existing safety measures.

In March 2023, they launched Claude, their first consumer-facing chatbot. The timing was perfect. ChatGPT had exploded three months earlier, creating massive demand for AI assistants and proving the market existed. But ChatGPT had also surfaced every fear about AI safety — political bias, factual errors, users manipulating it into harmful outputs. Claude positioned itself as the responsible alternative.

The FTX collapse in November 2022 had forced an awkward pivot. The crypto exchange's implosion wiped out their promised $500 million. But the crisis revealed their strategy's soundness. While other AI labs looked like pure technology plays, Anthropic had framed itself as the safe choice for enterprises worried about AI risks. That positioning attracted a $300 million investment from Google in February 2023, then another $2 billion by October. Google saw Anthropic as both a hedge against OpenAI's Microsoft partnership and genuine technical innovation in safety.

Amazon followed with a $4 billion commitment in September 2023, structured as convertible notes. The tech giants were essentially buying insurance against AI risk while securing access to frontier models. Anthropic's total funding reached $7.3 billion by mid-2024 — a staggering sum that reflected both the capital intensity of the AI race and the premium enterprises would pay for safety.

Claude 2, released in July 2023, demonstrated their technical chops. It handled longer contexts than GPT-4, scored well on coding benchmarks, and exhibited fewer of the erratic behaviors that plagued competitors. More importantly, it gave honest answers about its limitations — a feature that built trust even as it made the model seem less impressive in demos.

The business model emerged clearly: enterprise contracts where safety wasn't marketing but a requirement. Financial services firms evaluating AI couldn't risk the reputational damage of a chatbot saying something catastrophically wrong. Healthcare and legal applications needed auditability. Anthropic wasn't the flashiest option, but for certain customers, they were the only option.

By 2024, the founding bet looked prescient. The AI safety debate had moved from fringe concern to regulatory priority. The EU's AI Act, US executive orders, and mounting public scrutiny meant that labs demonstrating genuine safety work had advantages beyond technical performance. Anthropic's constitutional AI approach, their published research on interpretability, their willingness to delay launches for safety testing — these created regulatory air cover that pure-speed competitors lacked.

The competition remained brutal. OpenAI had Microsoft, massive consumer adoption, and a head start. Google had DeepMind, compute resources, and integration across its product suite. But Anthropic had carved out a position that seemed increasingly defensible: the AI lab enterprises trusted when the stakes were high.

Whether they could maintain independence while taking billions from Google and Amazon remained an open question. The irony wasn't lost on observers: a company founded to escape commercial pressures had become dependent on exactly the tech giants whose AI ambitions it was supposedly checking. But the Amodeis had structured the deals carefully, maintaining control through governance provisions and mission-focused bylaws.

What they had proven was that "safety-first" could be a competitive strategy, not just a moral stance. In an industry racing toward ever-larger models with uncertain consequences, the researchers who slowed down to ask hard questions had built something formidable. The path from OpenAI's offices to standalone rival had taken just three years, billions in capital, and a bet that the world would eventually care as much about AI alignment as about AI capability.

So far, they were right.
