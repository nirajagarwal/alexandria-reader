# Twilio

***The API that made voice and text programmable***

> **Years:** 2008-present

In 2008, Jeff Lawson was building a startup called Nine Star when he hit a wall that every software founder knew: adding phone calls or text messages to an application meant navigating telecom carriers, signing contracts measured in six figures, and waiting months for provisioning. The telecommunications industry operated like it was still 1985. You couldn't just write code and send a text message. You had to become a telecom company first.

Lawson had been through the startup cycle before — an early employee at Amazon Web Services, then founder of a company called Versity that got acquired. He knew what developers needed because he was one. At Nine Star, he found himself wishing for something impossibly simple: an API that would let him send SMS messages with a few lines of code, the way you could spin up a server on AWS with a credit card and ten minutes.

So he stopped building Nine Star and started building that API instead. With co-founders Evan Cooke and John Wolthuis, he launched Twilio in 2008. The pitch was audacious in its simplicity: turn telecommunications into HTTP requests. Make phone calls and text messages as easy as any other web service.

The first product was brutally focused. Developers could sign up, get a phone number instantly, and start sending SMS or making calls through a REST API. No contracts. No minimums. No sales calls. Pay-as-you-go pricing that made sense to engineers who were used to AWS billing. The canonical demo was seven lines of code that sent a text message.

The timing was accidental genius. The iPhone had just launched. Mobile apps were about to explode. Every startup building for mobile would need to send verification codes, notification texts, or enable calling. Twilio arrived exactly when a generation of developers was learning to think mobile-first and API-first simultaneously.

Early adoption came from exactly where Lawson expected: developers at startups who were used to the AWS model and had no patience for telecom bureaucracy. Uber used Twilio to connect riders and drivers without exposing phone numbers. Airbnb used it for host-guest communication. The product spread through word of mouth on Hacker News and GitHub, not through enterprise sales teams.

The business model was unusual. Twilio bought telecommunications capacity wholesale from carriers, then resold it retail through the API with margin on top. The company was a telecom carrier, technically — but one that looked and felt like a developer platform. It required managing relationships with legacy telecom companies while building product for developers who expected instant gratification and RESTful design.

Fundraising came easier than for most infrastructure companies. Union Square Ventures led the Series A in 2009. Bessemer led the Series B in 2010. Investors saw what Lawson saw: every application was about to need communication capabilities, and no developer was going to build it themselves when they could make an API call instead. By 2012, Twilio had raised over $100 million and was processing billions of API requests annually.

But the model had a vulnerability. Twilio had margin pressure from two directions: carriers charging wholesale rates that Twilio couldn't fully control, and customers expecting prices to drop over time the way all cloud services did. The company needed volume — massive volume — to make the unit economics work. Every new use case mattered. Every new developer mattered. Growth couldn't slow down.

The product expanded in concentric circles. Voice APIs. Video APIs. Chat. Messaging across WhatsApp, Facebook Messenger, every channel. A platform called Flex for building entire contact centers. Acquisitions of companies like SendGrid for email. Twilio wasn't just a communications API anymore — it was becoming the programmable layer for all customer communication, every channel, every use case.

The company went public in 2016 at a $1.2 billion valuation. It was one of the first true developer-platform companies to IPO, and the market rewarded it. The stock doubled on the first day. Lawson rang the opening bell wearing a hoodie, surrounded by developers, typing "Hello World" in code on the exchange floor. The symbolism was perfect: a company built for developers, taken public on developers' terms.

But scale brought problems that APIs couldn't solve. As Twilio grew, the customer base shifted. The early adopters were startups and developers who understood self-service. But growth required selling to enterprises — companies that wanted account managers, custom contracts, and SLAs. The culture of "ask your developer" met the reality of procurement departments. The product remained developer-friendly, but the go-to-market motion had to accommodate both worlds simultaneously.

Competition intensified. Amazon launched its own communications APIs. MessageBird and Vonage built similar offerings. The moat wasn't technology — the APIs were relatively straightforward to replicate. The moat was ecosystem: the thousands of developers who already knew Twilio's API, the documentation, the libraries, the Stack Overflow answers. Switching cost wasn't about contracts. It was about muscle memory.

The pandemic in 2020 was a massive accelerant. Telemedicine, remote contact centers, delivery coordination — every industry suddenly needed programmable communications immediately. Twilio's revenue grew 55% that year. The stock soared. Then came the correction. By 2022, as tech valuations crashed, Twilio's stock fell 80% from its peak. Lawson announced layoffs — 11% of staff, then later 17%. The market was reassessing every SaaS company, especially ones with margin pressure and slowing growth.

Today, Twilio processes hundreds of billions of API requests annually and generates over $4 billion in revenue. It remains the default answer when developers need to add communications to an application. The vision of making telecommunications programmable has been realized. But the company exists in the uncomfortable space between platform and utility — essential infrastructure that customers expect to keep getting cheaper, requiring constant expansion into new channels and use cases to maintain growth.

The outcome is a kind of victory: Twilio changed how software is built. But it's also a reminder that being first and being right doesn't guarantee easy scaling. Infrastructure platforms need massive volume to thrive, and volume requires broadening beyond the early adopters who loved you first.
