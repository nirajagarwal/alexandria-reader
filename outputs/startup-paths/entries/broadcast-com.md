# Broadcast.com

***The right idea at the exact right moment — then gone***

> **Years:** 1995-1999

In 1995, Mark Cuban was running MicroSolutions, a systems integration company he'd eventually sell for $6 million. Todd Wagner was a lawyer. They were friends from Indiana University who shared a problem: they wanted to listen to Indiana basketball games in Dallas. Radio stations didn't stream online. Nothing streamed online reliably. The internet was still mostly text and tiny images loading one painful line at a time.

Cuban and Wagner saw the gap between what the internet could theoretically do and what it actually did. Streaming audio seemed obvious — radio over the internet — but the technology barely worked. RealAudio had launched in 1995, making it technically possible to send audio over dial-up connections, but nobody was building the infrastructure to do it at scale. The broadcast industry wasn't interested. Radio stations saw the internet as a curiosity, not a distribution channel.

They started AudioNet in September 1995, operating out of Cuban's house with one server. The first "broadcast" was a baseball game between KLIF radio in Dallas and a computer in Cuban's spare bedroom. It worked. Barely. The audio quality was terrible, the buffering constant, but it worked. Within months they were streaming dozens of radio stations. By early 1996, they had their first employee and actual office space.

The timing was everything. The internet was exploding but still primitive. Anyone who could make media work online had a massive first-mover advantage. AudioNet wasn't technically sophisticated — they were essentially setting up servers and connecting radio stations who wanted to experiment. But in 1995, that was enough. Nobody else was doing it.

The real insight came in early 1997. The web was becoming commercial. Companies were starting to believe the internet mattered. AudioNet had been focused on sports and radio — niche content for enthusiasts. But what if you could broadcast anything? Corporate events, investor presentations, concerts, lectures. What if every company needed to stream something eventually?

They rebranded to Broadcast.com in March 1998 and expanded aggressively. They weren't just streaming radio anymore. They were streaming Victoria's Secret fashion shows, presidential speeches, concerts, sporting events. Anything that could be broadcast on traditional media, they would put online. The business model was thin — mostly advertising, some licensing fees — but the growth was spectacular. By late 1998, they were broadcasting over 420 radio stations, 60 television stations, and hundreds of live events. Traffic was doubling every few months.

The company went public in July 1998. The IPO was perfectly timed for the peak of internet mania. The stock opened at $18, hit $74 by the end of the first day, and closed at $62.75. Mark Cuban and Todd Wagner became very wealthy on paper. The company's market cap reached $1 billion despite revenues of only $13.5 million and mounting losses.

The valuation made no sense by traditional metrics, but that didn't matter in 1998. Investors were betting on potential, on owning the future of media distribution. Broadcast.com was the future. Or at least, it was positioned as the future. The technology was still clunky. Most people were on dial-up connections. Streaming was jerky and unreliable. But everyone could see where it was heading. Bandwidth would get cheaper. Connections would get faster. Broadcast.com would be there first, owning the infrastructure.

That's what Yahoo believed when they acquired Broadcast.com in April 1999 for $5.7 billion in stock. At the time, it was one of the largest internet acquisitions ever. Yahoo was building a portal empire, trying to be everything to everyone. Media streaming fit their strategy. Cuban and Wagner became even wealthier — Cuban's stake was worth around $1.4 billion.

The acquisition looked brilliant for about eighteen months. Then the bubble burst. Yahoo's stock collapsed. The grand vision of the internet portal died. Broadband was coming, but not as fast as predicted. The business model for streaming media never materialized. Advertising rates collapsed. The infrastructure costs were enormous.

Yahoo rebranded Broadcast.com as Yahoo Broadcasting, then eventually just absorbed it into Yahoo's media properties. The distinct entity disappeared. The technology was integrated into Yahoo's platform, then gradually became obsolete as YouTube, podcasting, and later streaming services built better infrastructure and discovered actual business models.

Mark Cuban took his Yahoo stock and sold it before the crash — a move that distinguished him from most dot-com winners who rode their paper wealth back down to nothing. He used the money to buy the Dallas Mavericks and build a second fortune in sports and media investing. Todd Wagner pursued philanthropy and film production. Neither spent much time defending Broadcast.com's legacy.

The story of Broadcast.com is the story of perfect timing meeting imperfect execution meeting an irrational market. The idea was correct — media would move online, streaming would become ubiquitous. They were simply too early, and built the wrong infrastructure for the wrong business model. But being too early didn't matter when they could exit to a greater fool. Yahoo paid billions for a company that would never be worth billions, but Cuban and Wagner sold at exactly the right moment, before anyone realized that first-mover advantage in dot-com infrastructure was often just first-loser disadvantage.

They built something real — streaming media was their innovation, however primitive. They timed the exit perfectly. The company died quietly inside Yahoo. But the founders won.
