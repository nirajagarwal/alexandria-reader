# Napster

***The file-sharing revolution that remade music — and died for it***

> **Years:** 1999–2002

In the spring of 1999, an eighteen-year-old Shawn Fanning was failing out of Northeastern University. He spent his nights in the dorms writing code, trying to solve a problem his roommate kept complaining about: finding and downloading MP3s was a nightmare. Search engines barely indexed them. Links broke constantly. IRC channels were chaotic. Fanning wanted something easier — a service where you could search one central index but download files directly from other users' computers.

He called it Napster, after his childhood nickname. The first version took him about three months to write. By June 1999, he had a working beta. He showed it to his uncle, John Fanning, a serial entrepreneur who saw immediately what Shawn had built: not just a better MP3 search tool, but an entirely new architecture for distributing digital files. John helped Shawn incorporate, brought in a young entrepreneur named Sean Parker as co-founder, and they launched publicly that summer.

The growth was unlike anything the internet had seen. College students downloaded Napster and told their friends. Their friends told their roommates. Within six months, the service had hundreds of thousands of users. Within a year, millions. University network administrators watched in horror as Napster consumed massive portions of their bandwidth. Students stopped attending class to download music. The catalog was enormous — millions of songs, instantly accessible, completely free. Every album you'd ever wanted, every B-side, every live bootleg. If someone had it, you could find it.

The music industry watched with growing alarm. The Recording Industry Association of America sent Napster a cease-and-desist letter in December 1999. Napster argued they were protected by the Digital Millennium Copyright Act's safe harbor provisions — they didn't host the files, just facilitated connections between users. The RIAA wasn't convinced. In December 1999, they sued.

The lawsuit made Napster a cultural phenomenon. News coverage exploded. Suddenly everyone wanted to understand this software that terrified record labels. Downloads accelerated. By February 2001, Napster claimed 26.4 million users. The service was frequently overwhelmed. Users didn't care. They kept downloading.

The legal arguments were sophisticated, but the core question was simple: was Napster responsible for the copyright infringement its users committed? Napster's lawyers argued the company was just a technology platform, like a VCR manufacturer. The RIAA argued Napster's entire business model depended on infringement — the central index proved they knew exactly what files were being shared. Judge Marilyn Patel sided with the RIAA. In July 2000, she issued a preliminary injunction ordering Napster to shut down. An appeals court stayed the injunction temporarily, but the writing was clear.

Napster scrambled to survive. They tried to build a subscription service, negotiating with labels to legitimize the platform. The negotiations went nowhere — labels smelled blood. Some wanted Napster dead as an example. Others hoped to extract maximum value in a settlement. Meanwhile, users began migrating to new file-sharing networks — Gnutella, Kazaa, Morpheus — that had learned from Napster's legal vulnerability. These new networks were fully decentralized, with no central index to shut down. They were also harder to use and less reliable.

In March 2001, the appeals court ruled against Napster. The company was ordered to police its network for copyrighted material. This was functionally impossible — the whole point of Napster was near-instantaneous access to everything. Attempting to filter content meant breaking the user experience. In July 2001, Napster shut down the service entirely, promising to return with a legal subscription model. That version never launched.

By 2002, Napster was bankrupt. The company's assets were sold to Roxio for $5.3 million. Roxio later launched a legal music service using the Napster brand, but it bore no resemblance to the original. The real Napster — the peer-to-peer network that briefly made all recorded music freely available — was gone.

But its impact was permanent. Napster proved that digital distribution could be instant, global, and essentially free. It demonstrated that millions of people would engage in casual piracy if the experience was better than legal alternatives. It showed that decentralized networks were possible. It terrified entire industries and inspired a generation of entrepreneurs who understood that distribution was being fundamentally remade.

Apple launched iTunes in 2001, offering legal downloads at 99 cents per song — clunky compared to Napster, but legitimate. Spotify eventually cracked the code Napster couldn't, offering unlimited streaming with both free and paid tiers, compensating rights holders while giving users the access they wanted. Netflix learned the lesson early, building a streaming service before piracy destroyed film distribution the way it had music.

Shawn Fanning never built another company of comparable impact. Sean Parker went on to become Facebook's first president, bringing the same instinct for viral growth. The record labels won in court but lost the war — CD sales collapsed, and the industry spent a decade trying to rebuild around streaming economics that would have seemed unthinkable in 1999.

Napster lasted less than three years. It never made meaningful revenue. It died in bankruptcy. And it changed everything.
