# LinkedIn

***The slow build that became the professional web***

> **Years:** 2002-2016 (acquired by Microsoft)

Reid Hoffman was already wealthy when he started LinkedIn. He'd been PayPal's COO, part of the "PayPal Mafia" that would seed dozens of companies. But PayPal had taught him something: online networks could change how money moved. Maybe they could change how careers moved too.

The idea wasn't revolutionary. Professional networking had always mattered — it was how most people found jobs, closed deals, made connections. The internet had dating sites, friend networks, hobby forums. But no one had built the infrastructure for professional identity online. Hoffman saw white space.

He started LinkedIn in his living room in late 2002 with a small team, mostly PayPal alumni. The first version launched May 2003. It was spare — a profile, a list of connections, not much else. The value proposition was simple: your professional network, visible and searchable. Put your resume online. Connect to colleagues. See who knew whom.

Growth was slow. Painfully slow. By the end of 2003, LinkedIn had around 80,000 users. For a social network, that's nothing. The product needed network effects to work — you only joined if people you knew were already there. Hoffman and his team had to seed it carefully, targeting industries and companies where density mattered. They started with tech and venture capital, where Hoffman's network was strongest.

The early product decisions were crucial. LinkedIn insisted on real names and real professional identities. No pseudonyms, no fantasy personas. This made it less fun than Friendster or MySpace, but more credible. It was a resume site that happened to be social, not a social site trying to be professional. That distinction mattered.

Monetization was unclear for years. LinkedIn raised modest venture rounds but burned slowly. Hoffman knew from PayPal that network effects took time. In 2005, they introduced paid subscriptions — premium features for recruiters and job seekers willing to pay for better search and messaging. It worked, barely. Revenue trickled in.

The breakthrough came from job listings. In 2005, LinkedIn launched a jobs product. Companies could post openings. Users could apply. More importantly, users could see which jobs their connections could refer them to. This closed a loop: people joined to network, stayed to job hunt, and brought their networks in when they changed jobs. The flywheel started turning.

By 2008, LinkedIn had crossed 20 million users. Still small compared to Facebook, but large enough to be useful. If you were a white-collar professional, LinkedIn had enough of your industry that checking it made sense. Recruiters lived there. The network was achieving critical mass.

The 2008 financial crisis nearly killed them. Hiring froze. Job listings revenue collapsed. LinkedIn survived by doubling down on recruiter subscriptions. Companies still needed to hire eventually, and LinkedIn was where candidates were. They cut costs, weathered the storm, and came out stronger as hiring resumed in 2010.

LinkedIn went public in May 2011, one of the first major social networks to do so. The IPO priced at $45 and opened at $83, closing the first day over $94. The market valued LinkedIn at $9 billion. The professional network no one thought was sexy had become a giant.

The public years were profitable but complicated. LinkedIn kept growing — 100 million users in 2011, 200 million by 2013, 400 million by 2015. They expanded internationally, acquired companies (including online learning platform Lynda.com for $1.5 billion), and kept refining the product. But growth was slowing. Engagement remained weak compared to Facebook or Twitter. Users visited LinkedIn when they needed something — a job, a connection, information about a company — then left. It was useful, not addictive.

Wall Street soured on LinkedIn in 2016. The company missed guidance, and the stock crashed 40% in a single day. Suddenly LinkedIn looked vulnerable. Had it reached its ceiling? Would newer networks eat its lunch?

Microsoft offered $196 per share in June 2016 — $26.2 billion total, a 50% premium over the pre-crash price. Microsoft wanted LinkedIn's data, its network graph, its professional identity system. CEO Satya Nadella saw LinkedIn as infrastructure for the productivity suite Microsoft was building. Hoffman, still on the board, saw a good exit for shareholders and a stable home for the product.

The acquisition closed that December. LinkedIn remains largely independent within Microsoft, but integrated where it makes sense. The IPO had valued LinkedIn at $9 billion. The exit came at $26 billion. For a network that grew slowly, iterated carefully, and never became a cultural phenomenon, that's a remarkable path.

LinkedIn succeeded by solving a real problem without trying to be exciting. Professional networking was always transactional. LinkedIn made those transactions efficient. It didn't need to be where you spent your day. It needed to be where you went when your career required it. That was enough.
