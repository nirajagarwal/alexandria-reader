# Medici Bank

***The dynasty that invented modern finance and died from its own ambitions***

> **Years:** 1397-1494

In 1397, Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici opened a small bank in Florence with two employees and limited capital. He had worked for years managing the Rome branch of his cousin's bank, learning the mechanics of papal finance, foreign exchange, and the wool trade. When the partnership dissolved, Giovanni returned to Florence with connections, expertise, and a clear insight: the Catholic Church needed better financial infrastructure, and whoever controlled that infrastructure would become indispensable.

The market was fragmented. Italian banking consisted of family partnerships, each managing money for merchants, nobles, and the Church. Most were local operations. Some had failed spectacularly — the Bardi and Peruzzi banks had collapsed in the 1340s after lending too much to English kings who defaulted. Trust was scarce. Capital was expensive. The Church, flush with tithes from across Europe, needed to move money between Rome and distant dioceses, but canon law forbade usury. The financial system was a puzzle of restrictions, opportunities, and risks.

Giovanni solved it with structure. Instead of one bank, he created a holding company with subsidiary partnerships in major cities — Rome, Venice, Geneva, Bruges, London. Each branch was legally independent, jointly owned by the Medici and local partners who provided capital and absorbed losses. Giovanni held the controlling stake but limited his exposure. If a branch failed, it wouldn't bring down the whole system. He called the loans "gifts" and charged "fees" to avoid usury prohibitions. He managed the Pope's accounts with discretion. By 1420, the Medici Bank was the largest in Europe.

Giovanni's son Cosimo inherited the bank in 1429 and understood that finance and power were inseparable. Florence was a republic, but republics had factions. The Albizzi family dominated and saw the Medici as threats. In 1433, they exiled Cosimo. It lasted one year. The Medici had financed too many people, employed too many workers in their textile businesses, held too much debt from too many Florentine families. The city's economy suffered. Cosimo returned in 1434 not as a banker but as the uncrowned ruler of Florence, controlling elections, appointing allies, funding public works. The bank became the engine of a political machine.

Under Cosimo, the bank expanded aggressively. Branches in Milan, Pisa, Avignon. Loans to dukes, kings, and popes. The Medici financed wars, mining operations, textile production. They diversified into trade — silk, wool, alum. They didn't just lend money; they structured deals, held equity, took commissions. The London branch financed English wool exports. The Rome branch managed papal revenues from across Christendom. The Geneva branch handled the accounts of Burgundian nobles. At its peak in the 1450s, the Medici Bank had ten branches, dozens of junior partners, and a balance sheet larger than most sovereign states.

But complexity created fragility. Each branch manager operated semi-autonomously, chasing profits and prestige. Some made brilliant trades. Others made catastrophic loans. The Bruges branch lent heavily to Charles the Bold, Duke of Burgundy, financing his wars against France and Swiss cantons. Charles lost. He died in battle in 1477, and the loans died with him. The London branch financed Edward IV's wars during England's Wars of the Roses. When Edward was deposed, the debts became uncollectable. The branch declared bankruptcy in 1478.

Cosimo's grandson Lorenzo inherited the bank in 1469 and the political machine that came with it. He was a poet, a patron, a diplomat — not a banker. He understood power but not risk management. He appointed loyalists to run branches, prioritizing political reliability over financial competence. The Rome branch manager, Francesco Sassetti, made a catastrophic loan to Charles the Bold just before his death. The Lyon branch (replacing Geneva) extended credit to French nobles who never paid. Lorenzo himself borrowed from the bank to fund lavish public spectacles, architectural projects, and political bribes. He treated the bank as a treasury for statecraft.

By 1480, the bank was bleeding. The London and Bruges branches were closed. The Rome and Lyon branches were insolvent but kept open to maintain political relationships. Venice and Milan were struggling. Only the Florence branch remained profitable, propped up by local textile businesses and political dominance. Lorenzo could have restructured, consolidated, cut losses. Instead, he diverted deposits to cover shortfalls, using new money to pay old debts — a Renaissance Ponzi scheme.

In 1492, Lorenzo died. His son Piero inherited a bank that existed more on paper than in reality. Two years later, French armies invaded Italy. Piero fled Florence. The Medici were exiled. Their political machine collapsed. Creditors demanded payment. There was nothing left. The bank that had financed popes and kings, that had invented the holding company structure, that had turned banking into statecraft, dissolved into a series of unpaid debts and abandoned ledgers.

The building blocks survived. Double-entry bookkeeping, bills of exchange, foreign exchange networks, subsidiary structures — these became the infrastructure of European capitalism. But the Medici Bank itself ended as it had begun: a family partnership that grew too fast, diversified too broadly, confused political power with financial sustainability, and died when the music stopped. The dynasty continued — later Medicis became popes, queens, grand dukes. But the bank that made them, that proved finance could build empires, vanished in a cascade of bad loans, political overreach, and structural rot. It lasted ninety-seven years.
