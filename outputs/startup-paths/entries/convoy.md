# Convoy

***The freight broker that tried to eliminate freight brokers***

> **Years:** 2015-2023

In 2015, Dan Lewis and Grant Goodale looked at the $800 billion trucking industry and saw what every tech founder sees in legacy sectors: inefficiency, middlemen, and fragmentation. Lewis had been CEO of Amazon's grocery business. Goodale had built mapping systems at Microsoft. They knew logistics. They knew software. The gap seemed obvious.

American trucking runs on a network of 20,000 freight brokers — intermediaries who match companies that need stuff moved with truckers who have capacity. Brokers work phones and spreadsheets. They take cuts of 15-20%. The system works, but it's expensive, opaque, and slow. Lewis and Goodale believed software could do better.

They raised $2.5 million from Greylock and Y Combinator. The pitch was elegant: build an app that connects shippers directly to truckers. Cut out the middleman. Lower costs. Increase transparency. Use data to optimize routing and pricing. It was Uber for freight — a comparison investors loved and that would prove both clarifying and cursed.

The first version launched in Seattle. Truckers could browse available loads on their phones. Shippers could book capacity with a few taps. No haggling. No broker fees. Just technology matching supply and demand.

Early traction was real. Truckers liked the transparency. Shippers liked the prices. By 2016, Convoy had raised $18 million from Cascade Investment — Bill Gates's personal investment vehicle. By 2017, another $62 million. By 2018, $185 million at a $1 billion valuation. Bono invested. Jeff Bezos invested. The story wrote itself: tech-savvy founders bringing Silicon Valley magic to an unsexy industry.

Convoy grew fast. They expanded beyond the Pacific Northwest into Texas, then the Southeast, then nationwide. They built sophisticated algorithms to predict pricing and demand. They hired hundreds of sales people to sign up both truckers and shippers. Revenue scaled from millions to hundreds of millions.

But the business never quite worked the way the pitch suggested. The vision was a platform — lightweight, software-driven, connecting two sides of a marketplace with minimal overhead. The reality was operations-heavy and margin-thin. Trucking wasn't like ridesharing. A shipper booking a load isn't like someone hailing a ride. The stakes are higher. The reliability requirements are absolute. When a truck doesn't show up or a load gets damaged, you can't just cancel and try again.

Convoy discovered what brokers had always known: the value isn't just matching. It's relationships, trust, problem-solving, and absorbing risk. When weather delays a shipment or a trucker breaks down, someone needs to fix it. That someone was increasingly Convoy's operations team — effectively becoming the brokers they had promised to eliminate.

The unit economics were stubborn. Take rates — the percentage Convoy kept from each transaction — hovered around 15%, roughly the same as traditional brokers. But Convoy had Silicon Valley costs: engineers, data scientists, product managers, venture-scale growth targets. Traditional brokers had sales guys and phones.

The pandemic initially seemed like a validation. Freight volumes surged. Capacity tightened. Spot rates spiked. Convoy's revenue hit $1 billion in 2021. They raised $260 million at a $3.8 billion valuation. But the boom masked the structural challenges. In a hot market, even inefficient models make money.

When freight markets cooled in 2022, the problems surfaced. Shipping volumes fell. Excess trucking capacity returned. Spot rates collapsed. Convoy's growth stalled. The company had 1,500 employees and a cost structure built for hypergrowth in an industry entering a downturn.

Competitors were everywhere. Legacy brokers like C.H. Robinson had digitized. Uber Freight existed. Dozens of venture-backed startups chased the same thesis. The industry hadn't been disrupted — it had gotten more competitive without fundamentally changing.

Convoy tried to adapt. They launched their own trucks — shifting from pure platform to hybrid asset operator. They cut staff. They focused on profitability over growth. But venture math is unforgiving. A company valued at $3.8 billion needs an exit that justifies that valuation. Steady, modest profitability in a cyclical, low-margin business doesn't get there.

By 2023, runway was short. Convoy sought buyers. Flexport, itself struggling, agreed to acquire Convoy — not for the valuation, but for the technology and customer relationships. Then Flexport backed out. In October 2023, Convoy shut down. They laid off 1,000 employees with one week of severance. The assets were sold in pieces. Customers scrambled to rebook loads. Truckers were left unpaid for completed hauls.

Convoy had raised $1 billion across its life. It moved freight for major corporations. It built real technology. But technology alone couldn't overcome the economics of a business where margins are thin, relationships matter, and being right about the future doesn't make you profitable today.

The insight was correct: trucking brokerage was ripe for software. The mistake was believing software could replace the messiness entirely. The path from insight to sustainable business required either lower costs than incumbents or higher value to customers — ideally both. Convoy achieved neither decisively enough. In the end, they became expensive brokers in an industry that already had plenty of cheap ones.
