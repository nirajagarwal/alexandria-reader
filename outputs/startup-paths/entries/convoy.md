# Convoy

***The freight broker that bet automation could displace relationships — and lost***

> **Years:** 2015-2023

Dan Lewis and Grant Goodale founded Convoy in Seattle in 2015 with a thesis that seemed obvious to anyone who had worked in tech: trucking, a $800 billion industry in the United States, ran on phone calls, faxes, and personal relationships. Freight brokers — middlemen connecting shippers with truckers — took 15-20% margins for making matches that software should handle instantly. Lewis, a former Amazon executive, and Goodale, with a background in logistics, believed they could build the "Uber for trucking."

The pitch attracted immediate attention. Y Combinator backed them. Then came a $2.5 million seed round. Then $62 million from Greylock, with Reid Hoffman and Jeff Bezos participating. By 2018, Convoy had raised over $400 million at a $1 billion valuation. The story wrote itself: tech-enabled marketplace eliminating inefficient middlemen, network effects creating winner-take-all dynamics, Amazon veterans bringing operational discipline to a backward industry.

The product was elegant. Shippers posted loads on Convoy's platform. Algorithms matched them with available trucks, optimizing for distance, timing, and price. Trucks got better utilization. Shippers got lower costs and real-time tracking. No phone calls. No negotiations. Just software doing what software does best.

Early traction validated the model. Fortune 500 companies — Unilever, Anheuser-Busch, Procter & Gamble — signed on. Truck drivers downloaded the app. The marketplace grew. Convoy expanded from the Pacific Northwest across the country. Revenue climbed from millions to hundreds of millions. The company hired aggressively, building engineering teams and logistics operations. They raised more: $185 million in 2019, another $400 million in 2021 at a $3.8 billion valuation. Competitor Uber Freight validated the space. So did dozens of other well-funded startups attacking the same problem.

But the thesis contained flaws that money couldn't fix.

Trucking isn't like ride-sharing. A passenger needs a ride once, gets in a car, arrives at their destination. The relationship is transactional and brief. Freight is different. A shipper moving goods consistently needs reliability above all else. They need to know the truck will show up, the load will arrive intact, the driver will handle special requirements. These assurances come from relationships built over years. A freight broker who answers the phone at 2 AM when a truck breaks down in Wyoming. A dispatcher who knows which drivers handle refrigerated goods carefully. Trust built through repetition.

Convoy's algorithms optimized for efficiency. But efficiency isn't the only variable shippers optimize for. When a load is time-sensitive or high-value, they call the broker they trust, even if it costs more. When a truck driver has a choice between a load from an app and one from a dispatcher who has steered them to good routes for five years, relationships matter.

The other problem was market structure. Convoy didn't create a two-sided marketplace so much as insert themselves into an existing, fragmented market where relationships already existed. The largest freight brokers — C.H. Robinson, XPO Logistics — had decades of shipper relationships and carrier networks. Local brokers had deep knowledge of regional routes and drivers. Convoy could win loads on price, but price competition in a commoditized market meant thin margins. The 15-20% broker margin they planned to capture turned out to be hard-won and defended.

Technology provided advantages, but not insurmountable ones. Competitors built their own platforms. Incumbent brokers digitized their operations. The gap between Convoy's tech-first approach and traditional brokers narrowed. Meanwhile, Convoy discovered they needed many of the things they thought technology would eliminate: customer service representatives, account managers, people calling truckers when loads fell through. Automation worked for simple loads on predictable routes. Complex logistics still required humans.

Then the market turned. The pandemic created a freight boom in 2020 and 2021 — too many goods moving, not enough trucks, surging prices. Convoy's revenue grew, hitting $1 billion in 2022. But the boom was temporary. By mid-2022, the freight market softened. Shipping volumes declined. Truck capacity exceeded demand. Prices fell. Margins compressed. The economic tailwinds that had masked unit economics reversed.

Convoy burned through capital faster than it could achieve profitability. The company had raised over $1 billion, but that funded expansion, not sustainable operations. Layoffs began in 2022 — 7% of staff. Then more in early 2023 — another 11%. The company explored strategic options. Negotiations with potential acquirers went nowhere. The valuation that made sense in 2021 didn't reflect reality in 2023.

In October 2023, Convoy shut down. No buyer materialized. The company sent a message to shippers and carriers that operations would cease in weeks. Employees learned via memo. Eight years, over a billion dollars in funding, thousands of employees, hundreds of enterprise customers — and it ended not with acquisition or IPO, but with wind-down.

The assets scattered. FlexPort acquired some technology and hired some staff. C.H. Robinson, one of the incumbents Convoy sought to displace, picked up customers. The marketplace disappeared. Shippers called the brokers they'd kept relationships with all along.

Convoy bet that software could replace relationships in an industry built on relationships. They raised enough capital to test the thesis at scale. The thesis was wrong. Not completely — technology improved logistics, and the industry digitized faster because of companies like Convoy. But the core assumption — that algorithms could disintermediate trust — proved false. Trucking wasn't ride-sharing. The network effects didn't materialize. The margins didn't appear. And when the market turned, the path led to closure.
