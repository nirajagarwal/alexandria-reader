# Intel

***The chip giant that invented Moore's Law and then fell behind it***

> **Years:** 1968-present

In 1968, Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore left Fairchild Semiconductor with a simple pitch: memory chips would replace magnetic core memory. They didn't need to explain much more. Noyce had co-invented the integrated circuit. Moore had articulated what would become Moore's Law — the observation that transistor density doubled roughly every two years. Venture capitalist Arthur Rock raised $2.5 million in two days, mostly from calls to people who trusted Noyce's name. The company was called Intel, short for Integrated Electronics.

The founding team understood silicon physics better than almost anyone alive. Moore brought deep technical knowledge. Noyce brought credibility and vision. Andy Grove, employee number three, brought operational discipline that bordered on paranoia. This combination — vision, credibility, execution — would define Intel for decades.

Their first product, the 3101 Schottky bipolar memory chip, shipped in April 1969. It worked but faced competition. The breakthrough came in 1970 with the 1103 DRAM chip, the first commercially viable dynamic random-access memory. By 1972, it was the best-selling semiconductor in the world. Intel had created the memory chip industry.

But the real pivot happened almost by accident. In 1969, a Japanese calculator company called Busicom asked Intel to design twelve custom chips. Ted Hoff, an Intel engineer, proposed something radical: instead of custom chips, build one general-purpose chip that could be programmed. The 4004, released in 1971, was the first commercial microprocessor. Intel bought back the rights from Busicom for $60,000.

For years, Intel didn't understand what they had. Microprocessors were a side business. Memory chips generated the revenue. The 8080 processor, released in 1974, found its way into early personal computers like the Altair, but Intel saw this as a niche market. The company was a memory company that happened to make processors.

The crisis came from an unexpected direction. In the early 1980s, Japanese manufacturers — backed by government support and massive capital investment — flooded the memory market with high-quality, low-cost DRAM chips. Intel's margins collapsed. By 1984, the company was losing money on memory for the first time. The business that had defined Intel was becoming unsustainable.

Grove and Moore faced the decision that defines many startup paths: what are we actually building? In Grove's retelling, Moore asked him: "If we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what do you think he would do?" Grove answered immediately: "He would get us out of memories." Moore stared at him and said, "Why shouldn't you and I walk out the door, come back, and do it ourselves?"

They did. Intel exited memory — laying off thousands, closing factories, abandoning the market they had created. It was wrenching. But by 1986, they were solely a microprocessor company. The 386 chip, released that year, established Intel's dominance in PC processors. The IBM PC architecture had created a standard. Intel owned the brain.

The next fifteen years were conquest. Intel didn't just make chips; they defined the pace of computing. Moore's Law became a marketing tool and a forcing function. Every eighteen to twenty-four months, a faster chip. "Intel Inside" stickers turned a component into a brand. By the mid-1990s, Intel had over 80% of the PC processor market and margins that made them one of the most profitable companies on Earth.

But success created rigidity. Intel optimized everything around high-performance PC and server processors. When the market shifted toward mobile in the 2000s, Intel stumbled. The ARM architecture — low-power, modular, designed for phones — was everything Intel's x86 wasn't. Intel tried to adapt with Atom processors, but the company's culture and economics were built for high-margin PC chips, not the lower-margin, power-efficient mobile world.

Apple's decision to use Intel processors in Macs (2006-2020) looked like validation but proved temporary. When Apple shifted to their own ARM-based chips in 2020, it exposed Intel's vulnerability. The company that had defined processor performance was losing on multiple fronts: Apple designing their own chips, AMD resurgent with better architecture, TSMC and Samsung outpacing Intel's manufacturing.

Intel's manufacturing advantage — once unassailable — eroded through a series of delays. The 10-nanometer process, expected around 2015, shipped in volume only in 2019. The 7-nanometer process (later renamed "Intel 4") faced similar delays. Companies that had relied on Intel's fabrication prowess — the "fabs" that made Moore's Law real — looked elsewhere.

By 2021, Intel was in a position almost unthinkable two decades earlier: catching up. New CEO Pat Gelsinger, an Intel veteran, announced an ambitious plan to rebuild Intel's manufacturing leadership and become a foundry for other companies' chip designs. The U.S. government, suddenly aware that advanced chip manufacturing had moved to Asia, backed this with billions in subsidies through the CHIPS Act.

Intel today is not failing, but it is no longer defining the future. It remains massive, profitable, essential to data centers and PCs. But the company that invented the microprocessor, that made Moore's Law a self-fulfilling prophecy, that turned silicon into the most important material of the information age, is now fighting to catch up to the cadence it once set. The path from dominance to challenger wasn't sudden collapse. It was the slow accumulation of decisions optimized for yesterday's market, the weight of success making it harder to see what comes next, the discovery that what made you a giant can make you slow.
