# Zomato

***The restaurant guide that became a delivery empire — by surviving everything***

> **Years:** 2008-present

In 2008, Deepinder Goyal was a management consultant at Bain in Delhi, frustrated by a simple problem: finding the office lunch menu. The company kept menus in binders. They were outdated, incomplete, and nobody wanted to maintain them. Goyal and his colleague Pankaj Chaddah built a simple internal tool to digitize them. Colleagues loved it. Then someone asked: why not for all of Delhi?

They launched Foodiebay — quickly renamed Zomato after legal threats — as a restaurant discovery platform. Just listings, menus, and reviews. No transactions. India's internet penetration was low, smartphones didn't exist yet, and most restaurants had never heard of online reviews. But the insight was right: information asymmetry around restaurants was massive, and someone would solve it.

The early model was brutally manual. They walked into restaurants with cameras and notebooks, photographing menus, recording hours, mapping locations. Restaurant owners were skeptical or hostile. Why would anyone look up restaurants online? But Zomato wasn't really for restaurant owners. It was for the small but growing segment of urban Indians who were online and wanted to know where to eat.

By 2010, they had coverage of major Indian cities and raised their first funding from Info Edge, the company behind Naukri.com. The money let them expand faster, but the model remained simple: ad revenue from restaurants who wanted visibility. No booking fees, no transaction cuts. Just eyeballs.

Then came the global expansion that nearly killed them. Between 2012 and 2015, Zomato went on an acquisition spree, buying restaurant listing sites in over 20 countries. The logic seemed sound: restaurant discovery was universal, they could scale the model globally, and being first mattered. They raised $223 million and operated in territories from New Zealand to Portugal to Lebanon.

It was a disaster. Local food cultures varied wildly. What worked in Delhi didn't work in Dubai. Each market needed different sales strategies, different user behaviors, different restaurant relationships. Zomato spread itself impossibly thin, burning cash without achieving dominance anywhere. By 2015, the company was imploding. They laid off 10% of staff and shut down operations in multiple markets. The press wrote obituaries. The consensus was they'd overextended, misunderstood markets, and would join the graveyard of Indian startups that tried to go global too fast.

But Goyal didn't shut down. He retreated. They pulled out of unprofitable markets, focused on core territories, and made a brutal shift: food delivery. The model they'd spent seven years perfecting — passive discovery, ad revenue — was being obliterated by delivery platforms. Swiggy had launched in India in 2014 and was growing explosively. Customers didn't want restaurant information; they wanted food at their door.

Zomato launched delivery in 2015, late to its own market. They had the restaurant relationships and user base but no logistics infrastructure. Building delivery from scratch meant recruiting riders, mapping delivery zones, negotiating new contracts with restaurants who now saw them as competitors to Swiggy. They raised more money and spent it on rider subsidies, restaurant commissions, and user discounts — the classic playbook of marketplace warfare.

The battle with Swiggy became existential. Both companies hemorrhaged money, competing on delivery times, restaurant selection, and discount depth. Uber Eats entered India in 2017, making it a three-way war. Zomato's advantage was its brand recognition from the listings business, but that only went so far when users cared about who delivered fastest and cheapest.

By 2019, food delivery in India was consolidating. Uber Eats was struggling. Zomato was still burning cash but had survived long enough to matter. In 2020, Uber sold Uber Eats India to Zomato for $350 million in stock. Suddenly, Zomato was a duopoly player with Swiggy, and the unit economics started improving. COVID accelerated everything. With restaurants closed for dine-in, delivery became essential infrastructure overnight.

In July 2021, Zomato went public on the Indian stock exchange at a $8.6 billion valuation, the first Indian unicorn IPO of the startup generation. The listing was oversubscribed 38 times. For a company that had nearly collapsed six years earlier, it was vindication. But the story didn't end triumphant.

Post-IPO, Zomato faced new pressures: profitability expectations, activist shareholders, regulatory scrutiny over gig worker conditions, and continued cash-burn competition with Swiggy. Goyal pushed into quick commerce with Blinkit (acquired in 2022), trying to replicate the food delivery playbook for groceries. The stock fluctuated wildly. Some quarters showed profits; others showed losses.

What Zomato became was a survivor. Not the elegant global platform it once imagined, but a scrappy, adaptive Indian company that pivoted when necessary, retreated when overextended, and fought when it had to. The restaurant guide became a delivery logistics company became a quick commerce bet. The through-line was Goyal's willingness to abandon what wasn't working and chase what was, even if it meant junking years of accumulated advantage.

The path from Bain consultant to public company founder ran through near-death, global overreach, brutal competition, and market pivots that redefined the business multiple times. Zomato exists today not because of the original insight — digitizing menus — but because it outlasted competitors, survived its own mistakes, and adapted faster than the market killed it.
