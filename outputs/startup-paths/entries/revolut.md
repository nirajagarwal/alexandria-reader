# Revolut

***The neobank that grew too fast to stay small and too aggressive to stay clean***

> **Years:** 2015-present

Nikolay Storonsky and Vlad Yatsenko met at Credit Suisse and Lehman Brothers, where they watched legacy banks charge customers for the privilege of using their own money. Foreign exchange fees, overdraft penalties, monthly maintenance charges — revenue streams dressed as services. In 2015, they launched Revolut with a simple pitch: a prepaid debit card that eliminated foreign exchange fees and offered interbank rates for currency conversion. For Europeans traveling or working across borders, it was immediately obvious. Download an app, order a card, spend abroad without getting fleeced. The first 100,000 customers signed up in eight months.

The initial product was narrow but sharp. A mobile-first banking alternative targeting frequent travelers and digital natives who were allergic to branch banking. Storonsky, who had a physics degree and a trader's instinct for arbitrage, saw financial services as bloated legacy systems ripe for disruption. Revolut would be faster, cheaper, and built on modern infrastructure. They raised a small seed round and started expanding features: cryptocurrency exchange, stock trading, savings vaults, travel insurance. The app became less a card and more a financial super-app, aggregating services that traditionally required separate institutions.

Growth was exponential. By 2017, Revolut had a million users. By 2018, two million. The company raised venture funding at escalating valuations, reaching unicorn status within three years. The velocity was intoxicating. Storonsky ran the company like a high-frequency trading desk — data-driven, ruthlessly efficient, optimized for speed. Employees worked punishing hours. Turnover was high. Storonsky defended the culture as necessary for the mission, pointing to the user numbers as validation. Revolut was growing faster than Monzo, faster than N26, faster than any neobank in Europe. The race was everything.

But speed created friction. In 2018, the Telegraph reported a toxic work culture, with employees describing 80-hour weeks, impossible targets, and a founder who berated staff publicly. Storonsky dismissed the criticism as sensationalism from people who couldn't handle startup intensity. Then came regulatory scrutiny. Revolut operated under an e-money license, which allowed payment services but not full banking activities. They applied for a banking license in the UK in 2017, expecting quick approval. It took four years. Regulators had concerns: anti-money laundering controls that struggled to keep pace with user growth, compliance systems built for scale but not rigor, internal reports of flagged transactions that weren't properly investigated.

Revolut expanded geographically anyway. The US, Australia, Singapore, Japan — market entry became a metric of success. But each jurisdiction brought new regulatory complexity, and the company's operational discipline hadn't scaled with its ambition. In 2020, a Wired investigation revealed deeper issues: compliance officers overruled by growth teams, a company culture that celebrated speed over process, automated systems that missed obvious red flags. Former employees described a company that wanted the prestige of banking without the constraints. Revolut denied wrongdoing but the pattern was clear: growth had outpaced governance.

The pandemic turbocharged neobanking. Traditional banks closed branches, and digital-first finance became default. Revolut's user base exploded to 15 million by 2021, then 20 million by 2022. Revenue grew alongside it, driven by interchange fees, subscription tiers, and crypto trading during the 2021 boom. The company raised $800 million at a $33 billion valuation, making it one of Europe's most valuable private tech companies. Storonsky's thesis seemed validated: move fast, dominate users, monetize later.

But the regulatory reckoning continued. In 2022, Revolut finally secured a UK banking license — restricted and supervised, not the clean approval they had hoped for. The company had to rebuild compliance infrastructure, hire experienced risk officers, and slow down feature launches to satisfy regulators. The Australian regulator issued warnings about misleading customers on savings rates. Lithuania, where Revolut held its European banking license, conducted audits that found deficiencies in transaction monitoring. The company was still growing, but the path to legitimacy was grinding and expensive.

By 2023, the macro environment had shifted. Crypto crashed, wiping out a lucrative revenue stream. Growth metrics slowed as the European market saturated and customer acquisition costs rose. Revolut remained profitable on paper, but the valuation multiple compressed. Late-stage investors who had priced in hyper-growth started asking harder questions about sustainable unit economics and regulatory risk. Competitors like Wise had built slower but steadier, with cleaner compliance records and less cultural baggage.

Storonsky remained defiant. He positioned Revolut as a global financial super-app, the WeChat of finance for a mobile-first generation. The company kept expanding: crypto staking, business accounts, international remittances, even phone plans. The strategy was still aggregation, still scale. But the startup that had thrived by moving faster than regulators now had to prove it could operate like the institution it wanted to replace.

Revolut's path reveals the tension between growth and governance in fintech. The company achieved scale that legacy banks envied, but the shortcuts taken early created debts paid later. It remains one of Europe's most valuable startups, but whether it becomes a durable financial institution or a cautionary tale about regulatory arbitrage is still unresolved. The company that moved too fast to fail now has to move carefully enough to survive.
