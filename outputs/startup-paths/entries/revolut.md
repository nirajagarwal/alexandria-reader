# Revolut

***From fintech disruptor to regulated bank: the high-speed, high-pressure path to European dominance***

> **Years:** 2015-present

In 2015, Nikolay Storonsky was working at Credit Suisse, frustrated every time he traveled. Foreign exchange fees ate into his spending. Currency conversion was opaque, expensive, and controlled by banks that had no incentive to make it better. His co-founder, Vlad Yatsenko, came from Deutsche Bank's technology side. They both saw the same thing: retail banking was ripe for disruption, and mobile technology made it possible to rebuild financial services from scratch.

They started with a simple idea: a prepaid debit card that let you spend abroad at interbank exchange rates, no markup. Launch a mobile app, link it to a card, and let people hold multiple currencies in one account. The target was obvious—young Europeans who traveled frequently, lived across borders, and were comfortable managing money through their phones. The kind of customer who would trade the reassurance of a bank branch for better rates and a slicker interface.

Revolut launched in the UK in July 2015. Within months, they had tens of thousands of users. The product worked. The proposition was clear. But Storonsky wasn't building a travel money app. He was building a bank replacement, and he was building it fast.

The next eighteen months were a blur of feature releases. Stock trading. Cryptocurrency exchange. Budgeting tools. Vaults for savings. Insurance products. Revolut added functionality at a pace that made traditional banks look fossilized. Each feature served two purposes: deepen engagement, and expand the surface area of financial services Revolut could capture. The strategy was Amazon-like—land with a wedge product, then expand horizontally until you own the customer relationship.

Growth was ferocious. By 2017, Revolut had a million users. By 2018, two million. They expanded across Europe, launching in market after market with minimal friction because the core infrastructure was already built. Funding followed the user growth. Series B in 2017 at a $250 million valuation. Series C in 2018 at $1.7 billion. Suddenly Revolut was a unicorn, one of Europe's fastest-growing fintech companies, and Storonsky was being compared to Travis Kalanick—aggressive, relentless, willing to push boundaries.

The comparison wasn't accidental. Inside Revolut, the culture was intense. Employees worked long hours. Targets were aggressive. Performance reviews were blunt. Storonsky ran the company like a trading floor—high pressure, high churn, results above all. Some thrived. Many burned out. By 2019, reports surfaced of toxic work conditions, impossible expectations, and a revolving door of departures. Storonsky's response was unapologetic: this was what it took to build a company at this speed.

But speed created other problems. In 2019, compliance issues surfaced. Revolut had scaled so fast that its anti-money laundering systems struggled to keep pace. Regulators raised concerns. Media scrutiny intensified. The narrative shifted from scrappy disruptor to reckless growth machine. Storonsky had always operated in the grey zones of regulation—launching features before seeking full approval, testing boundaries, moving faster than regulators could respond. It worked until it didn't.

The wake-up call came in the form of banking licenses. Revolut had operated under e-money licenses, which allowed payment services but not full banking capabilities. To truly compete with incumbents, they needed proper banking licenses in key markets. That meant submitting to regulatory oversight, building compliance infrastructure, and slowing down. It was antithetical to everything that had made Revolut successful.

The UK banking license took three years. Revolut applied in 2018 and received conditional approval in 2021. During that time, they rebuilt their compliance systems, hired hundreds of risk and regulatory staff, and accepted that the wild west phase was over. They secured a European banking license in Lithuania in 2018, which helped, but the UK license was symbolic—proof they could operate within the system.

Meanwhile, COVID hit. For a company built on international spending, a global pandemic that grounded travelers should have been catastrophic. Instead, Revolut pivoted. They pushed savings products, stock trading, and cryptocurrency harder. Stuck-at-home users started trading GameStop and Dogecoin through Revolut. The app became a gateway to retail investment mania. User numbers kept climbing.

By 2021, Revolut had 15 million users. Series E funding valued the company at $33 billion, making it the UK's most valuable fintech startup. They expanded into the US, Japan, and Australia. They added business accounts, freelancer tools, and salary advance features. The original vision—a bank replacement—was becoming real.

But the path had costs. Employee churn remained high. Regulatory scrutiny never fully subsided. Profitability was elusive—the company burned cash on customer acquisition, feature development, and geographic expansion. Storonsky remained convinced that scale was everything, that once Revolut reached critical mass, network effects and cross-selling would generate sustainable margins.

In 2024, Revolut finally turned a profit. Annual revenue crossed $2 billion. User numbers exceeded 40 million. The company was no longer a startup but a financial institution—regulated, complex, and operationally mature. The wild early days were gone. What remained was a company that had successfully disrupted retail banking in Europe, built a product millions trusted with their money, and forced incumbents to modernize or die.

Storonsky is still CEO. The company is still private, though IPO rumors circulate constantly. The path from frustrated trader to banking kingpin took a decade of relentless execution, regulatory battles, and cultural controversy. Revolut proved that banking was vulnerable to disruption. Whether it proved that disruption could be done sustainably remains an open question.
