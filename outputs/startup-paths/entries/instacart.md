# Instacart

***The decade-long marathon from desperate funding rounds to grocery establishment***

> **Years:** 2012-present

Apoorva Mehta quit Amazon in 2012 after building supply chain systems that moved millions of packages. He had tried twenty startup ideas in the preceding months — apps for lawyers, for DJs, for finding parking spots. None worked. Then he noticed he was ordering everything online except groceries. The opportunity seemed obvious once stated: hire people to shop and deliver from existing stores. No inventory, no warehouses, just labor and software.

He launched in San Francisco in June 2012. The first orders came from his own apartment, fulfilled by contractors he found on Craigslist. The unit economics looked plausible immediately. Customers paid fees and tipped. Shoppers earned above minimum wage picking and delivering. Instacart took a margin. The technology challenge was logistics — routing, timing, inventory prediction — exactly what Mehta knew from Amazon. Within months Sequoia led a seed round. Within a year they were in ten cities.

The model scaled by partnerships. Instacart didn't build grocery stores; they built APIs to connect stores with last-mile delivery. Whole Foods signed as their first major partner in 2014. For grocers, Instacart was defense against Amazon. For Instacart, grocers were grateful customers who paid commission on every order and granted access to inventory systems. The network effects worked backwards from typical platforms: more shoppers attracted more retailers, which attracted more customers, which justified hiring more shoppers.

But Amazon had been building its own grocery capability since acquiring Webvan's patents in 2010. In 2013 they began testing AmazonFresh. Instacart found itself racing against infinite capital. The pressure forced growth at any cost. They expanded to Canada. They signed Costco, then Petco, then CVS. They raised $44 million in 2014, then $220 million in 2015, then $400 million in 2016. The valuations climbed: $2 billion, then $3.9 billion, then $7.9 billion. Each round came with pressure to grow faster than burn rate could suggest was wise.

The labor model became the liability. Instacart initially classified shoppers as contractors, which kept costs variable and scaling simple. But California and other states were tightening gig economy rules. In 2015, facing a class action lawsuit, Instacart settled for $4.6 million and agreed to reclassify some workers as part-time employees. This fragmented their workforce into full-service shoppers who picked and delivered, and in-store shoppers who only picked. The change increased complexity while lawsuits continued.

Then margins compressed. In 2017, facing competition from Amazon, Postmates, DoorDash, and Uber Eats all adding grocery delivery, Instacart cut shopper pay and eliminated tips within the app interface. The backlash was immediate. Shoppers organized boycotts. The press coverage was brutal. Mehta reversed the tip policy within days, but trust had fractured. The unit economics that looked plausible in 2012 now depended on squeezing labor costs in ways that generated negative headlines.

By 2018 the private valuation had reached $7.9 billion but growth was decelerating. Whole Foods, their anchor partner, had been acquired by Amazon and was shifting delivery in-house. Instacart needed new anchors. They pivoted to advertising: offering CPG brands the ability to promote products within the Instacart app. This turned grocers into media real estate. The margin on ads was far higher than the margin on delivery commissions.

The strategy worked. By 2019 advertising revenue was material. They raised another $600 million at a $7.6 billion valuation — flat from the year before, a warning sign — but the business was diversifying beyond pure logistics. Then March 2020 arrived. Pandemic lockdowns made grocery delivery essential overnight. Orders surged 150%. Instacart hired 300,000 new shoppers in weeks. Suddenly the decade-long slog toward profitability seemed irrelevant. They were infrastructure.

The demand spike forced operational discipline. They couldn't afford the chaos of earlier years while handling 500% volume increases. They systematized fraud prevention, shopper quality metrics, and retailer integrations. The advertising business accelerated as CPG brands fought for visibility in an app that now reached tens of millions of households weekly. By 2021, Instacart was processing $2 billion in monthly grocery sales. Sequoia led another round at a $39 billion valuation.

Then the pandemic subsided. Order volumes plateaued. The 2021 valuation proved unsustainable. By 2023, preparing for IPO, Instacart faced a down round. Mehta had stepped aside as CEO in 2021, replaced by Fidji Simo from Facebook. Simo cut costs, pushed profitability, and filed to go public at a $10 billion valuation — a 75% decline in two years. The company went public in September 2023 at a market cap around $10 billion. The stock traded sideways for months.

What Instacart proved was that being first meant surviving longest. DoorDash ultimately won restaurant delivery. Amazon won e-commerce. But Instacart owned the decade of grocery partnerships built before anyone else committed to the infrastructure. They survived by not needing to own stores, by monetizing retailer anxiety about Amazon, and by discovering that grocery apps were advertising platforms. The outcome wasn't the triumph the $39 billion valuation suggested. It was survival, profitability, and public markets. For a company that nearly died multiple times, that counted as victory.
