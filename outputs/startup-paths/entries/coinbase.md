# Coinbase

***From dorm room bet to public market validation of crypto's place in finance***

> **Years:** 2012-present

In the summer of 2012, Brian Armstrong was a software engineer at Airbnb watching Bitcoin break $10 for the first time. He had discovered it a year earlier through a link on Hacker News and spent his evenings trying to buy some. The experience was miserable. Exchanges were offshore, unreliable, and felt like navigating the internet's underbelly. Wire transfers disappeared. Websites went down. Customer service didn't exist. The technology was revolutionary; the infrastructure was a joke.

Armstrong had joined Airbnb during Y Combinator and learned what product-market fit looked like. Bitcoin had it—people desperately wanted to buy it. But the on-ramps were failing them. He wrote a simple prototype over a weekend: a way to buy Bitcoin that didn't feel like visiting a casino in a jurisdiction without laws. He called it Coinbase, applied to Y Combinator's summer 2012 batch, and was accepted.

The co-founder he found was Fred Ehrsam, a former Goldman Sachs trader who had independently become obsessed with Bitcoin. Ehrsam brought financial markets credibility to Armstrong's product instincts. They were building in a space that attracted libertarian idealists and get-rich-quick speculators in equal measure. Armstrong and Ehrsam positioned themselves differently: boring, compliant, trustworthy. They would be the Charles Schwab of crypto.

The early product was dead simple. Connect your bank account. Buy Bitcoin. Sell Bitcoin. No trading interface, no advanced features, no leverage. They launched to the public in October 2012 with Bitcoin around $11. By early 2013, it was past $100. By April, it hit $266 before crashing to $50. Coinbase kept working through the volatility. When Mt. Gox, then the world's largest exchange, catastrophically collapsed in 2014 after losing 850,000 Bitcoin to hacks and mismanagement, Coinbase's bet on compliance and security looked prescient rather than paranoid.

They spent years building the foundation no one saw. Bank relationships. Regulatory licenses, state by state. Security infrastructure. The company hired lawyers before it hired marketers. When competitors moved fast and broke things, Coinbase moved slowly and built trust. It was unsexy work in a sexy market. But it created a moat.

The business model was brutally simple: transaction fees. Every time someone bought or sold crypto on Coinbase, the company took a cut. When Bitcoin was volatile, people traded constantly. When it was climbing, new users flooded in. When it crashed, volume spiked as people panic-sold. Coinbase made money in every market condition except boredom.

That didn't mean the path was smooth. In 2017, Bitcoin went from under $1,000 in January to nearly $20,000 in December. Coinbase added millions of users in weeks. The infrastructure buckled. The site went down during peak trading hours. Customer service tickets piled up for months. People couldn't withdraw their money. The company that had built its reputation on reliability was failing at scale. Armstrong hired aggressively, but hiring doesn't solve crisis problems—it creates new coordination problems during a crisis.

The 2018 crash was worse. Bitcoin fell from its December 2017 peak to below $4,000. Crypto was declared dead by every outlet that had declared it the future a year earlier. Trading volume collapsed. Coinbase laid off 15% of its staff. The question wasn't whether crypto would recover, but whether it would do so before Coinbase burned through its capital.

The company survived by diversifying. It launched Coinbase Pro for professional traders. It built custodial services for institutions that wanted crypto exposure without operational risk. It added new cryptocurrencies beyond Bitcoin and Ethereum. Each new asset was another revenue stream, another reason for users to return. The platform slowly transformed from a Bitcoin on-ramp to a crypto ecosystem.

When crypto roared back in 2020 and 2021—fueled by pandemic stimulus, institutional adoption, and retail mania—Coinbase was ready. Revenue hit $7.8 billion in 2021. In April 2021, the company went public via direct listing on NASDAQ at a valuation over $85 billion. It was a landmark: a crypto company so established that it could list on a traditional exchange, so integrated into the financial system that regulators allowed it.

But the validation was also vulnerability. Public meant scrutiny. It meant quarterly earnings calls during crypto winters. It meant being accountable to shareholders who might not understand or believe in the mission. When crypto crashed again in 2022—triggered by the collapse of Terra/Luna, the bankruptcy of Three Arrows Capital, the fraud at FTX—Coinbase stock fell 80%. Revenue dropped 58%. The company laid off 18% of staff in June 2022, then another 20% in January 2023.

The FTX collapse in November 2022 was particularly revealing. While Sam Bankman-Fried's exchange imploded amid revelations of commingled customer funds and fraudulent lending, Coinbase's boring compliance culture looked genius. Customers fled centralized exchanges, but many who stayed picked Coinbase precisely because it had spent years being the responsible adult. The crisis clarified the value of what Armstrong had built: not the most exciting platform, not the cheapest fees, but the one least likely to disappear with your money.

Through 2023 and 2024, Coinbase evolved again. It launched Base, a layer-2 blockchain, positioning itself as infrastructure provider rather than just exchange. It expanded internationally despite U.S. regulatory hostility under SEC chair Gary Gensler. It fought legal battles over whether its listed tokens were securities, becoming crypto's reluctant policy champion. Revenue recovered as Bitcoin climbed back above $60,000.

Armstrong remained CEO throughout, unusual for a company now over a decade old. His product was working exactly as designed: bringing crypto to mainstream users by making it feel as safe as logging into their bank account. Coinbase processed hundreds of billions in trading volume annually. It held custody of assets worth over $100 billion. It employed over 3,000 people. It had proven that a crypto company could be boring, compliant, and successful simultaneously.

The company's path revealed a less obvious truth about startup success: sometimes the hardest thing to build is the infrastructure no one notices until it's not there. Coinbase won by being the plumbing.
