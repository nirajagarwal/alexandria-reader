# Instagram

***The photo app that won by subtracting***

> **Years:** 2010-2012 (acquired); 2012-present (within Meta)

Kevin Systrom wanted to build a location-based social network. It was 2010, and Foursquare was hot. Everyone was building check-in apps. Systrom, a Stanford grad who'd worked at Google and spent time at a startup called Odeo that became Twitter, raised $500,000 for something called Burbn — a cluttered app where you could check in to locations, make plans with friends, earn points for hanging out, and post photos.

Burbn launched to a small group of beta users. It was a mess. Too many features, too much friction, no clear reason to use it. But Systrom noticed something in the data: people weren't really checking in. They were posting photos. And when they posted photos, they kept using the app.

He brought in Mike Krieger, a Brazilian engineer he'd met at Stanford, as co-founder. They made a brutal decision: strip everything else away. No check-ins, no points, no plans. Just photos. Just sharing. But with two crucial additions — filters to make phone photos look good, and a feed that made sharing feel social and immediate.

They rebuilt the app in eight weeks. They called it Instagram.

It launched on October 6, 2010, in the iOS App Store. Within hours, the server crashed. Twenty-five thousand people signed up the first day. By the end of the first week, they had 100,000 users. Systrom and Krieger spent those early days frantically scaling infrastructure they'd built for maybe a few thousand people.

The timing was perfect. The iPhone 4 had just launched with a dramatically better camera. People suddenly had a device in their pocket that could take decent photos. But phone photos still looked mediocre. Instagram's filters — Valencia, X-Pro II, Earlybird — made them look like something you'd want to share. The square format evoked Polaroids. The whole experience felt like a return to physical photos, but instant and social.

The app was fast. Stupidly fast. Photos uploaded while you were still applying filters. The feed refreshed instantly. Every interaction felt responsive. This wasn't an accident. Systrom and Krieger obsessed over speed, cutting features and complexity to keep the app snappy. While other photo apps added features, Instagram stayed focused.

By December 2010, Instagram had one million users. Celebrities started joining. Media outlets started pulling photos from Instagram for stories. The hashtag became a discovery mechanism — not invented by Instagram, but embraced and amplified. The app became a network, then a platform, then a cultural force.

Venture capitalists noticed. In February 2011, Instagram raised $7 million from Benchmark and others at a $25 million valuation. By April 2012, they raised another $50 million at a $500 million valuation. The company had thirteen employees and thirty million users. They still hadn't released an Android version. They had no revenue model.

Then Facebook made an offer: $1 billion in cash and stock. It was April 2012. Instagram had been live for eighteen months. The price shocked people — a billion dollars for a company with no revenue, barely a dozen employees, and an app that just let you share photos with filters. Tech observers called it a bubble. Skeptics said Facebook was panicking, overpaying for a threat.

Systrom and his board took the deal. Days before the acquisition closed, Instagram finally launched on Android. One million downloads in twelve hours. The acquisition closed at the end of April. By the time regulatory approval came through, Facebook's stock had fallen and the deal's value dropped to around $700 million. Still the biggest acquisition for a venture-backed startup with no revenue in history.

Inside Facebook, Instagram initially maintained independence. Systrom reported to Mark Zuckerberg but ran Instagram as a separate app, separate team, separate brand. This was deliberate. Facebook knew it had a reputation problem with young users. Instagram was cool. Facebook was where your parents were. The strategy was to own the platform but let it breathe.

Instagram added video in 2013. Stories in 2016 — a direct copy of Snapchat's core feature, executed better and distributed to Instagram's massive user base. Snapchat never recovered. Instagram added shopping, IGTV, Reels (copying TikTok). The app that succeeded by subtracting became a Swiss Army knife of features.

Growth exploded. By 2018, Instagram had a billion users. It became the center of influencer culture, of visual internet culture, of how young people presented themselves online. The business model finally arrived: advertising, inserted seamlessly into the feed, targeted with Facebook's infrastructure. Revenue estimates put Instagram at over $20 billion annually by 2020 — more than twenty times what Facebook paid.

But tension grew. In 2018, Systrom and Krieger left Facebook. The official story was amicable. The real story involved increasing interference from Zuckerberg and Facebook's central product team, pressure to integrate more deeply with Facebook's core products, loss of autonomy. The founders who'd built Instagram by saying no walked away when they couldn't say no anymore.

Today Instagram is one of the most valuable pieces of Meta's empire. Over two billion users. A printing press for advertising revenue. The place where culture happens, where brands live, where influence is built and measured. It's also more cluttered, more algorithmic, more designed to maximize engagement than to let you share photos with friends.

The app that won by subtracting became valuable by adding back everything it once removed. The founders who knew when to say no sold to someone who knew when to say yes to everything. And the platform built on the simplicity of photos and filters became an empire of stories and reels and shops and ads — unrecognizable from the app that crashed on its first day because too many people wanted something simple.
