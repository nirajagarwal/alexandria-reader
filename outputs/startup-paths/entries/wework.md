# WeWork

***The charisma that built an empire and the delusion that destroyed it***

> **Years:** 2010-2019 (bankruptcy 2023)

Adam Neumann and Miguel McKelvey met in New York in the late 2000s, two men with complementary obsessions. McKelvey, an architect, cared about space and community. Neumann, an Israeli entrepreneur with magnetic intensity, cared about scale and destiny. They started with a simple observation: office space was rigid, expensive, and isolating. Freelancers and small companies needed flexibility. Real estate was ripe for disruption.

Their first experiment, in 2008, was called Green Desk — shared office space with an environmental angle. It worked, but felt small. In 2010, they sold their stake and launched WeWork. The model was straightforward: lease buildings long-term at low rates, renovate them into beautiful shared workspaces with beer on tap and phone booths for privacy, then sublease desks and offices short-term at premium prices. The arbitrage was the business. The community was the brand.

It caught fire. Not just in New York, but in the cultural moment. The 2008 financial crisis had shattered faith in traditional employment. Millennials flooded into freelancing and startups. Remote work was ascending. WeWork wasn't selling desks — it was selling belonging, hustle culture, the romance of entrepreneurship. Neumann positioned the company not as real estate but as a technology platform, a lifestyle brand, a movement. "We are here in order to change the world," he said. Repeatedly. Investors believed him.

The growth was staggering. WeWork expanded to dozens of cities. It raised billions from SoftBank's Vision Fund, led by Masayoshi Son, who saw in Neumann a visionary worthy of Uber-scale capital. By 2019, WeWork was valued at $47 billion. It operated 500 locations in 29 countries. It employed 12,500 people. Neumann's personal wealth topped $4 billion. He bought properties, a wave pool company, a private jet. He trademarked the word "We" and sold it to his own company for $5.9 million.

Behind the valuation was a structure that made no sense. WeWork signed long-term leases and offered short-term memberships. In a downturn, revenue would evaporate while rent obligations remained. The unit economics were catastrophic — the company lost $219,000 per hour in 2018. But Neumann insisted this was a technology company destined for Amazon-scale dominance. SoftBank kept writing checks.

The governance was worse. Neumann controlled the company through supervoting shares. He sat on both sides of deals, leasing buildings he personally owned to WeWork. His wife, Rebekah, held the title of Chief Brand Officer and interviewed employees about their "energy." The company launched WeGrow, a school for their children's friends. WeLive, experimental co-living spaces. The ambition was totalizing — a members' club for life itself.

In August 2019, WeWork filed to go public. The S-1 prospectus was a disaster. It revealed losses of $1.9 billion on $1.8 billion in revenue. It detailed Neumann's self-dealing, his stock sales before the IPO, his retention of control even if he owned no shares. It called the company a "technology" company while describing a real estate landlord-tenant arbitrage. Investors revolted. Journalists piled on. The roadshow collapsed before it began.

Within weeks, the IPO was pulled. Neumann was forced out as CEO with a $1.7 billion exit package — $1 billion for his stock, $500 million in credit, $185 million in "consulting fees." Employees who had joined for equity watched their shares become worthless. SoftBank took control, pumped in $9.5 billion more, and slashed the valuation to $8 billion. Thousands were laid off. The pandemic in 2020 obliterated demand for shared office space. The arbitrage model revealed itself: when revenue disappeared, the leases remained, a noose tightening.

WeWork limped along, restructured, went public via SPAC in 2021 at a $9 billion valuation. It bled cash, closed locations, renegotiated leases. By 2023, the company filed for bankruptcy, its stock delisted, its brand a punchline. SoftBank lost over $11 billion. Early employees lost their equity. Landlords fought over unpaid rent. Neumann, banned from management, launched a new venture and said he felt misunderstood.

The story of WeWork is not complicated. A real estate arbitrage was dressed up as a technology revolution. A charismatic founder convinced investors that vision justified valuation. A friendly investor kept funding losses instead of demanding profitability. The business model required perfect conditions — rising demand, falling interest rates, infinite capital — and when conditions changed, it collapsed.

What makes it remarkable is the scale of the delusion and the transparency of the warning signs. The S-1 laid it bare, and still the company was worth billions for years after. The path wasn't hidden — it was visible from the beginning. Neumann told everyone what he believed: that community was worth more than unit economics, that mission justified governance, that if you believed hard enough, reality would bend. For a while, it did. Then it didn't.
