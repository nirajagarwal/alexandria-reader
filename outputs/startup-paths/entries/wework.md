# WeWork

***The charisma that built an empire on other people's leases***

> **Years:** 2010-2019 (bankruptcy 2023)

Adam Neumann and Miguel McKelvey met in New York in the late 2000s. Neumann was an Israeli immigrant with serial entrepreneur energy and no successful exits. McKelvey was an architect who had grown up in a commune. They shared a building in Brooklyn where they rented desk space to freelancers. They called it Green Desk. The landlord liked it enough to buy them out.

With that money, they started WeWork in 2010. The concept was simple: lease long-term commercial real estate, break it into smaller units, add design and community, rent it short-term at a premium. Arbitrage on flexibility. The freelance economy was exploding. Startups needed space but couldn't sign ten-year leases. Traditional office space felt corporate and dead. WeWork would be different — energetic, designed, communal. They opened their first location in SoHo.

It worked immediately. Demand exceeded supply. The value proposition was clear: pay more per square foot, get less commitment and more amenity. For many small companies and freelancers, this was obviously worth it. WeWork expanded to more Manhattan locations, then Brooklyn, then Boston, then London. By 2014 they had forty locations. The growth curve was extraordinary.

Neumann was the face. Charismatic, visionary, intoxicating to be around. He talked about WeWork not as a real estate company but as a technology company, a community company, a way-of-life company. He spoke of elevating the world's consciousness. Investors responded. Benchmark led a Series A. JPMorgan backed them. Then, in 2014, SoftBank noticed.

Masayoshi Son, SoftBank's founder, saw in Neumann a fellow believer in transformation at scale. He invested. Then invested more. Then made WeWork a core holding of the Vision Fund, the $100 billion vehicle Son raised to reshape global technology. Between 2017 and 2019, SoftBank poured over $10 billion into WeWork. The valuation reached $47 billion. WeWork became the most valuable startup in New York, ahead of any tech company.

The model scaled fast because it required limited capital. WeWork didn't buy buildings — it leased them, often with landlord-funded build-outs. It could open locations quickly. By 2018, WeWork had 400,000 members in 280 cities. Revenue was approaching $2 billion. Neumann expanded the brand into WeLive (co-living) and WeGrow (a school for his children and others). He spoke of a future where WeWork was not a product but an operating system for life.

But the unit economics were upside down. WeWork signed long leases and rented short. When occupancy was high, margins looked good. When it wasn't, or when the market turned, the fixed costs became an anchor. The company lost $1.9 billion in 2018. It was burning cash at a rate that required continuous capital infusion. SoftBank kept writing checks, confident that scale would eventually tip the model into profitability.

Inside, the culture reflected Neumann's excesses. Company retreats featured mass drinking, meat-eating competitions, and Neumann's wife Rebekah — who held the invented title of Chief Brand Officer — firing employees for bad energy. Neumann bought buildings and leased them back to WeWork. He trademarked "We" and sold it to the company for $5.9 million. He took over a billion dollars off the table in secondary sales while the company drowned in red ink.

In 2019, WeWork filed to go public. The S-1 revealed what insiders already knew and outsiders could now see. The losses were staggering. The corporate governance was a joke. Neumann controlled the board through super-voting shares. The business model looked less like technology and more like overleveraged real estate with a ping-pong table. Analysts tore it apart. The valuation tanked from $47 billion to below $20 billion, then lower. WeWork pulled the IPO.

Neumann was forced out. SoftBank took control, wrote down the investment, and injected another $10 billion to keep the company alive. They valued it at $8 billion — an 80% collapse in weeks. Neumann walked away with a $1.7 billion exit package. Shareholders sued. Employees holding stock saw their paper wealth evaporate.

The company limped forward under new leadership, cutting locations, renegotiating leases, trying to make the model sustainable. Then COVID-19 hit. Remote work exploded. Demand for shared office space collapsed. WeWork's lease obligations became existential. Landlords fought. Members canceled. Losses mounted.

In 2023, WeWork filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. The company that had been worth $47 billion was now worth functionally nothing. SoftBank's investment — the largest venture bet in history — was a near-total loss. Neumann moved on to other ventures, still talking about consciousness and transformation, still raising money.

The story is sometimes told as fraud. It wasn't. It was belief mistaken for vision, scale mistaken for product-market fit, and charisma mistaken for business model. WeWork worked as a product. People wanted it. But the financial structure was a shell game, and when the music stopped, the shell was empty. The insight was real. The execution was a monument to the idea that growth can paper over anything — until it can't.
