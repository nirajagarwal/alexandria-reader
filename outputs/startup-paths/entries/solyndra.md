# Solyndra

***The spectacular failure that became a political weapon***

> **Years:** 2005-2011

In 2005, Chris Gronet saw an opportunity in solar's biggest constraint. Traditional solar panels — flat rectangles of crystalline silicon — could only capture light hitting them directly. They required expensive mounting systems, careful angling, optimal positioning. Gronet, a semiconductor industry veteran who had helped build Applied Materials into a manufacturing giant, thought he could do better.

His insight: cylindrical solar cells. Tubes instead of panels. They would capture sunlight from all angles throughout the day, requiring no tracking systems. They could be laid flat on commercial rooftops without the heavy mounting racks traditional panels demanded. Installation would be faster, cheaper, simpler. The tubes would use copper indium gallium selenide — CIGS — a thin-film semiconductor that required far less silicon than conventional cells.

This last point mattered enormously. In 2005, a global silicon shortage had sent prices soaring. Silicon-based solar companies were scrambling for supply. Gronet's tubes would sidestep that constraint entirely. He founded Solyndra with backing from CMEA Capital and U.S. Venture Partners.

The technology worked. The company built a working prototype. The cylindrical cells did capture light from multiple angles. The installation story made sense — commercial rooftops were indeed a massive market, and building managers cared more about installation cost than theoretical maximum efficiency. Solyndra raised $78 million in Series B funding in 2007.

Then came 2008. The financial crisis froze capital markets. Solyndra had been planning an IPO. Instead, the company needed a rescue. The Obama administration, elected on a platform including clean energy investment, had just received authority to issue loan guarantees under a program created during the Bush administration. Solyndra applied.

What happened next would become the most politically contentious loan guarantee in American history. In March 2009, Solyndra received a $535 million loan guarantee — the first issued under the program. Vice President Biden attended a September 2009 groundbreaking. President Obama visited the new factory in May 2010, holding up Solyndra as proof that clean energy meant American jobs and manufacturing leadership.

But the market was shifting beneath them. Chinese manufacturers, backed by massive government subsidies and access to cheap capital, were flooding the market with conventional silicon panels. Prices were falling faster than anyone had predicted. The silicon shortage that had been Solyndra's strategic opportunity disappeared. By late 2009, silicon was cheap again.

Solyndra's technology still worked. Their cylindrical cells still captured light from all angles. But the economic logic had reversed. When silicon was expensive, avoiding it made sense. When silicon was cheap, Solyndra's more complex manufacturing process became a liability rather than an advantage. Their cost per watt couldn't match Chinese panels on a free-falling price curve.

The company burned through cash trying to scale manufacturing fast enough to reach cost competitiveness. They raised more private capital — $535 million more between 2009 and 2011, mostly from existing investors including Argonaut Ventures, the investment arm of the Walton family. They restructured the government loan to allow private investors first claim on assets if the company failed. They built a second factory.

None of it mattered. The Chinese price decline was too steep, too fast. By summer 2011, Solyndra was selling panels for less than it cost to make them, with no path to profitability visible. On August 31, 2011, the company ceased operations. On September 6, it filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. 1,100 workers lost their jobs immediately.

The political explosion was instant and massive. Congressional Republicans had been skeptical of the loan guarantee from the start. Now they had a scandal. FBI agents raided Solyndra's headquarters. Congressional hearings began. Emails between White House officials and the Office of Management and Budget became front-page news. Had the administration rushed the loan for political reasons? Had they ignored warnings about Solyndra's viability?

The investigations found no evidence of criminal wrongdoing. What they revealed was a government loan program moving with unusual speed, a company caught by unpredictable market changes, and a clean energy sector experiencing cost declines faster than any forecasting model had predicted. Those declines — devastating to Solyndra — would eventually make solar power cost-competitive with fossil fuels, one of the great technology victories of the decade.

For Gronet and Solyndra, it didn't matter. The bankruptcy proceedings dragged on. The government loan guarantee program, which would ultimately turn a profit across its entire portfolio, was politically toxic. Solyndra became a shorthand for government waste, crony capitalism, picking winners and losers.

The technology itself was acquired by SQN Capital Management for $7.5 million — a fraction of the $2 billion invested. The manufacturing equipment was auctioned. The factory sat empty. The story became a cautionary tale about government intervention in markets, about the dangers of betting on specific technologies, about the perils of being too early or too confident in a market about to undergo massive disruption.

What the story actually revealed was simpler and more brutal: in a commodity market experiencing radical price decline, technical elegance cannot overcome economic physics. Solyndra's cylinders captured more light — but when panels became cheap enough to just install more of them, elegance became irrelevant.
