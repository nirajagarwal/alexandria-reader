# Elastic

***From open source project to public company — by staying stubborn about search***

> **Years:** 2012-present

In 2012, Shay Banon had already been working on the same problem for nearly a decade. He'd started in 2004 building a search engine for his wife's recipes, then turned that into Compass, an open source library. When Compass hit its limits, he started over. The result was Elasticsearch — a distributed search engine built on Apache Lucene that could scale horizontally, handle massive amounts of data, and return results in milliseconds.

By 2012, Elasticsearch had become popular among developers. Companies were using it for everything from log analysis to e-commerce search to security analytics. But it was just software — free, open source, no company behind it. Banon was consulting, making enough to live on. The project had momentum but no business model. That's when he made the decision that would define everything: incorporate as a company, take venture funding, build a business around open source software that anyone could download and use for free.

The bet was simple: give away the core product, charge for the stuff enterprises need — support, training, proprietary features for security and monitoring. It was the open core model, already proven by companies like Red Hat and MySQL. But it required walking a tightrope. Make the open source version too limited, and the community abandons you. Make it too complete, and no one pays. Elastic's approach was to keep the search engine itself fully open while building commercial features around management, security, and monitoring.

The model worked. By 2014, Elastic had raised $70 million in a Series C at a $700 million valuation. The company now had a team, a sales force, a support organization. But it also had a problem that every successful open source company eventually faces: cloud providers. Amazon Web Services launched Elasticsearch Service in 2015 — taking Elastic's open source code, hosting it, and selling it as a managed service. Amazon didn't contribute code back. They didn't employ Elasticsearch developers. They just took the software, wrapped AWS infrastructure around it, and competed directly with the company that had built it.

Elastic's response was to double down on product. They acquired companies, built new capabilities, expanded from pure search into a full observability and security platform. They launched Elastic Cloud, their own hosted offering. The strategy was to move so fast that even if AWS could copy yesterday's features, they'd always be behind on tomorrow's. It meant constant innovation, constant releases, constant expansion of what "search" could mean.

Revenue grew. By 2018, Elastic went public at a $2.5 billion valuation. The IPO document revealed the scale: thousands of customers including Cisco, Microsoft, Walmart, Verizon. Revenue of $159 million in fiscal 2018, up 74% from the prior year. Still unprofitable — the company was spending heavily on R&D and sales — but growing fast. The market rewarded growth. On the first day of trading, shares popped 94%.

But the AWS problem hadn't gone away. In 2019, Amazon forked Elasticsearch, creating Open Distro for Elasticsearch with their own Apache 2.0 licensed additions. They positioned it as a response to Elastic adding proprietary features. Elastic responded by changing their license in 2021 — moving away from Apache 2.0 to a dual license that explicitly forbade cloud providers from offering it as a service without contributing back. It was a direct shot at Amazon. The open source community split. Some saw it as a necessary defense. Others saw it as betraying open source principles. Amazon responded by forking the project entirely, creating OpenSearch.

Now there were two: Elasticsearch, controlled by Elastic, and OpenSearch, backed by Amazon and truly open source. The split could have been fatal. Instead, it clarified the choice. Companies that wanted the full innovation velocity, the integrated platform, the company behind the code — they went with Elastic. Companies that wanted the guarantee of open source, the AWS integration, the promise of community control — they had OpenSearch.

Elastic kept growing. Revenue hit $1 billion in fiscal 2023. The company was finally profitable. The product had evolved far beyond search — it was now an observability platform, a security platform, an application performance monitoring tool. Search was still the core, but the use cases had multiplied. Companies used it to monitor infrastructure, detect security threats, analyze user behavior, power AI applications. The insight from 2004 — that search is a fundamental building block — kept proving true in new contexts.

By 2024, Elastic's market cap was over $7 billion. The company had survived the AWS threat not by winning the license argument but by outbuilding the competition. Every quarter brought new features, new integrations, new capabilities. The open core model had worked, even after the messiness of the license change. Enterprises paid for the platform, the support, the roadmap influence, the guarantee that someone was accountable.

The path from Banon's recipe search to a billion-dollar public company was neither smooth nor obvious. It required betting on open source when that seemed risky, taking VC money when that meant pressure to grow, fighting Amazon when that seemed unwinnable, and changing licenses when that meant controversy. At every turn, the company could have chosen safety — keep it small, stay pure open source, avoid the fights. Instead, they chose to build the company that could sustain the innovation the software required. The search engine that started as a side project became infrastructure that millions of developers depended on, because someone was willing to turn the project into a business and the business into a platform.
