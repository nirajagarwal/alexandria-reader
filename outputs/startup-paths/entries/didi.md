# Didi

***The national champion that won a war and lost its way***

> **Years:** 2012-present

In 2012, a former Alibaba executive named Cheng Wei saw the chaos of Beijing's streets and recognized an opportunity. Taxis were scarce, drivers were hard to find, and the rise of smartphones made coordination possible. He launched Didi Dache — "beep beep taxi" — as a simple taxi-hailing app. Within months, a nearly identical app called Kuaidi Dache appeared, backed by Alibaba. Cheng's Didi had Tencent. The battle lines were drawn not by the startups themselves but by China's internet giants, who saw ride-hailing as a strategic front in their war for mobile payments and user data.

What followed was subsidy warfare on a scale Silicon Valley had never seen. Both companies burned cash to attract drivers and riders. Trips were essentially free. A ride across Beijing might cost one yuan — about fifteen cents. The subsidies weren't sustainable, but they didn't need to be. They needed to create habits. Tencent and Alibaba poured in hundreds of millions. By 2014, both companies were booking millions of rides daily and bleeding money at astonishing rates. The unit economics were catastrophic, but the land grab was working.

In February 2015, the war ended abruptly. Didi and Kuaidi merged. The combined entity — Didi Kuaidi, later simplified to Didi Chuxing — controlled over 90% of China's ride-hailing market. Cheng Wei became CEO of a company that had won by attrition. The deal made sense: both companies were burning capital with no clear path to victory. Together, they could stop the subsidy war and start building a real business.

Then Uber arrived. Travis Kalanick saw China as existential. If Uber couldn't win there, it couldn't be the global platform it claimed to be. Uber China launched with heavy subsidies and Kalanick's characteristic aggression. Didi responded in kind. The subsidy war reignited, more expensive than before. Uber had Silicon Valley capital; Didi had Chinese investors and the tacit support of a government that preferred domestic champions. By 2016, Uber was spending over a billion dollars a year in China and losing every dollar.

In August 2016, Uber surrendered. It sold Uber China to Didi in exchange for equity in the combined company. Kalanick joined Didi's board. The deal was presented as a merger, but everyone understood it as defeat. Uber had lost China. Didi had won. It controlled nearly the entire ride-hailing market in the world's most populous country. Investors valued the company at $50 billion. An IPO seemed inevitable, and triumphant.

But the victory was pyrrhic. With competition eliminated, Didi raised prices. Drivers complained about falling income. Riders complained about worse service. The app, bloated from years of feature additions, became sluggish and confusing. More seriously, incidents began accumulating. In 2018, two passengers were murdered by drivers using Didi's carpooling service, Hitch. The second killing, of a 20-year-old woman who had complained to Didi about her driver's behavior before being killed, sparked national outrage.

The company shut down Hitch and went into crisis mode. Executives issued apologies. The app added safety features. But the damage was done. Didi had grown too fast, prioritized growth over safety, and operated with the arrogance of a company that faced no competition. The government launched investigations. Regulators circled. The IPO, once imminent, was delayed indefinitely.

Didi attempted to diversify. It expanded into bike-sharing, food delivery, autonomous vehicles, and international markets. None gained traction. The core business remained ride-hailing in China, and that business faced new headwinds. Local competitors, emboldened by Didi's troubles, reentered the market. Meituan, the food delivery giant, launched its own ride-hailing service. Didi's dominance eroded at the margins.

In June 2021, Didi finally went public, listing on the New York Stock Exchange at a $68 billion valuation. The timing was catastrophic. Days after the IPO, Chinese regulators announced a cybersecurity investigation and ordered Didi's app removed from app stores. New user acquisition stopped overnight. The stock cratered. Didi had chosen to list in New York over objections from Beijing, and Beijing retaliated. The company became a cautionary tale about the risks of defying Chinese regulators in pursuit of foreign capital.

By December 2022, Didi delisted from New York and began preparations to list in Hong Kong, closer to home and regulatory oversight. The company was a shadow of its IPO-day self. Its valuation had collapsed. Its dominance was gone. Cheng Wei, once hailed as a visionary, was now associated with hubris and miscalculation.

Didi had won the subsidy wars. It had outlasted Uber. It had built a platform used by hundreds of millions. But it had never learned to operate without crisis, never built a business that worked without subsidies or monopoly pricing, and never navigated the political realities of operating in a country where success at scale invites scrutiny and control. The company that won everything lost its way in victory. It remains operational, diminished, a national champion that discovered winning the war was easier than managing the peace.
