# Didi

***The Chinese giant that conquered Uber, then drowned in regulation***

> **Years:** 2012-present

In 2012, Cheng Wei left Alibaba with a simple observation: Beijing's taxi system was broken. Drivers circled empty while passengers waited in the rain. The match was inefficient, the experience miserable. Cheng had worked in sales at Alibaba for eight years. He understood marketplaces. He understood China. He started Didi Dache — literally "beep-beep call a car" — as a taxi-hailing app.

The timing was perfect. Smartphones were penetrating Chinese cities. WeChat and Alipay made mobile payments ubiquitous. Local governments hated the taxi medallion system they'd created but couldn't reform. A private company could route around it.

Within months, a competitor emerged. Kuaidi Dache launched with backing from Alibaba. The market split. Didi took investment from Tencent. What followed was one of history's most expensive subsidy wars. Both companies burned cash to acquire drivers and riders. A ride that cost 20 yuan might be subsidized down to 5. Drivers got bonuses for accepting rides. The apps were paying both sides of every transaction.

By 2014, the two companies were spending hundreds of millions on subsidies. Neither could win. Neither could stop. Tencent and Alibaba were fighting a proxy war through their portfolio companies. Then, in February 2015, something unusual happened: the enemies merged. Didi and Kuaidi became Didi Kuaidi, later just Didi Chuxing. The combined company controlled 80% of China's ride-hailing market.

The subsidy war ended. Prices rose. Didi turned its attention to a new enemy: Uber.

Uber entered China in 2013, viewing it as the ultimate prize. Travis Kalanick threw resources at the market. Uber China burned through $2 billion in two years. But Didi had advantages Uber couldn't match. It was integrated with WeChat and Alipay — the infrastructure of Chinese digital life. It understood Chinese cities, Chinese drivers, Chinese regulations. It had relationships with local governments that Uber, foreign and brash, couldn't build.

Didi also had deeper pockets. It raised $7.3 billion in 2016 alone, backed by Apple, Alibaba, and Tencent. It outspent Uber in subsidies. It launched premium services Uber couldn't match. By mid-2016, Uber was losing $1 billion a year in China with no path to profitability. In August, Uber surrendered. It sold Uber China to Didi in exchange for 17.7% of the combined company. Kalanick joined Didi's board.

Didi had won the Chinese ride-hailing market. It controlled 90% of rides. It was valued at $50 billion. It began expanding — into bike-sharing, food delivery, autonomous vehicles. It launched in Latin America, Australia, Japan. An IPO was inevitable. The company seemed unstoppable.

Then, in May 2018, a Didi driver in Zhengzhou raped and murdered a 21-year-old flight attendant. The case went viral. Details emerged: the victim had complained to Didi about the driver's behavior before the ride ended. Didi had not responded. Three days later, another young woman in Wenzhou was raped and murdered by a Didi driver. The company had ignored complaints about this driver too.

China erupted. The murders exposed systemic failures in Didi's safety protocols. The company had prioritized growth over rider safety. Background checks were inadequate. Complaints went unaddressed. The government suspended Didi's Hitch carpooling service indefinitely. The company issued groveling apologies. Its founder did a mea culpa tour. But the damage was done. Didi was no longer the scrappy disruptor. It was a monopoly that had gotten passengers killed.

The company cleaned up its act — better background checks, safety features, emergency buttons. But the regulatory environment had shifted. Beijing was clamping down on tech giants. Didi was now seen as part of the problem: a company that had grown too powerful, too fast, with too little oversight.

In June 2021, Didi went public in New York, raising $4.4 billion at a $68 billion valuation. Two days later, Chinese regulators announced an investigation into the company's data security practices. Within a week, Didi was ordered to stop signing up new users. App stores were ordered to remove it. The stock collapsed.

The government's message was clear: Didi had gone public without permission. Its data — trip records, location data, faces — belonged to China. Listing in America was a security risk. Didi had ignored warnings. Now it would pay.

The company spent the next year in regulatory purgatory. New user signups were frozen. The app was unavailable for download. Revenue stagnated. The stock traded 80% below its IPO price. In July 2022, Didi announced it would delist from the New York Stock Exchange and pursue a listing in Hong Kong instead. In December 2022, regulators finally allowed it to restore its app.

But the company had been broken. The delisting cost billions. The Hong Kong IPO never happened. Competitors had grown during Didi's paralysis. The company laid off thousands. Cheng Wei, once hailed as a visionary, was now seen as a cautionary tale.

Didi still operates. It still dominates Chinese ride-hailing. But the path from here is unclear. The company that beat Uber discovered that in China, the real opponent was never a competitor. It was the state. And against the state, there is no winning — only permission to continue.
