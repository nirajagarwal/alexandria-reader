# MySpace

***The social network that won first and lost everything***

> **Years:** 2003–2019 (acquired 2005, declined 2008–2011, sold 2011, zombie 2011–2019)

---

In August 2003, Tom Anderson and Chris DeWolfe launched MySpace from the offices of eUniverse, a direct marketing company in Santa Monica that specialized in spam email and questionable internet businesses. They built it in ten days. The goal was simple: copy Friendster, the hot new social network that was buckling under its own growth, and do it faster and looser.

Anderson had studied film and loved bands. DeWolfe was a former finance guy who understood internet traffic. Neither was a visionary technologist. They were hustlers who saw an opening. Friendster was slow, its servers crashed constantly, and its executives were cracking down on fake profiles and creative expression. MySpace would be the opposite: fast, permissive, and open to whoever wanted to show up.

The earliest users were musicians and fans. Anderson, who made his own profile the default "friend" for every new user, positioned himself as the welcoming face of the platform. His smiling profile picture became the most viewed image on the early internet. Bands realized they could host music, customize their pages with garish HTML and CSS, and connect directly with fans without a record label. Teenagers realized they could make their profiles look however they wanted—glittering backgrounds, autoplaying songs, chaotic layouts. By early 2004, MySpace was growing faster than any social network in history.

The customization was everything. On Friendster, you filled out a form. On MySpace, you *built* something. It was messy, ugly, personal. Users taught themselves HTML to make their pages reflect who they were—or who they wanted to be. Top 8 friend rankings created social drama. Comment walls became the beating heart of teenage social life. The platform felt alive in a way the sterile, controlled interfaces of other networks did not.

In July 2005, Rupert Murdoch's News Corporation bought MySpace for $580 million. It seemed like a bargain. MySpace had more users than any social network in the world and was growing exponentially. Murdoch saw it as the future of media: a captive audience of young people he could monetize with advertising and cross-promote with his traditional media properties. Anderson and DeWolfe stayed on to run it. The checks cleared. They were victorious.

But MySpace had already made its fatal error: it had never built anything sustainable underneath the growth. The code was a mess—a tangle of hacks piled on hacks. Servers groaned under traffic. Engineers spent their time keeping the site online, not building new features. The business model was pure advertising arbitrage: buy traffic cheap, plaster the site with ads, and hope revenue outpaced costs. There was no moat, no lock-in, no reason users had to stay beyond inertia.

In September 2006, a Harvard dropout named Mark Zuckerberg opened Facebook to anyone with an email address. Facebook was clean, fast, and uniform. You couldn't customize your profile into a glittering horror show. It was boring. It was also a relief. Facebook felt like a utility. MySpace felt like work. And Facebook had something MySpace never did: a disciplined technical foundation and a clear vision of what a social network should become.

The shift happened slowly, then all at once. In 2008, Facebook overtook MySpace in global users. By 2009, MySpace was in freefall. News Corp had installed executives from traditional media who didn't understand the internet. They pushed top-down initiatives—music deals, video partnerships, redesigns—that ignored what users wanted. Spam and fake profiles overran the platform. Technical debt made every change agonizingly slow. The best engineers left. The musicians and early adopters migrated to Facebook, Twitter, Tumblr—anywhere that felt less like a digital graveyard.

DeWolfe was forced out in 2009. News Corp replaced him with a rotating cast of executives who tried to "fix" MySpace with redesigns and pivots. Each change alienated the remaining users without attracting new ones. In June 2011, News Corp sold MySpace to Specific Media for $35 million—a 94% loss from the purchase price. Justin Timberlake became a minority investor, which generated headlines but changed nothing.

MySpace spent the next eight years as a zombie. Specific Media rebranded it as a music discovery platform, then as a social entertainment destination, then as something else. None of it worked. The site still existed, but almost no one used it. In 2019, during a server migration, MySpace accidentally deleted twelve years of music uploads—50 million songs from 14 million artists. The loss barely registered as news. Everyone had already moved on.

The lesson wasn't that MySpace picked the wrong features or made bad acquisitions. The lesson was that winning first means nothing if you can't build for what comes after. MySpace had the users, the momentum, the brand, and the capital. It lost because it was held together with duct tape and hope, because its leaders didn't understand what they'd built, and because when a disciplined competitor arrived, there was nothing beneath the surface to defend. The decline was so total that by the end, the question wasn't why MySpace failed—it was how it had ever succeeded at all.
