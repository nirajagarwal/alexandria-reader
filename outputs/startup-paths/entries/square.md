# Square/Block

***The payments company that became a Bitcoin treasury***

> **Years:** 2009–present

In 2009, Jack Dorsey had a problem. Not his problem — a problem he'd watched his friend Jim McKelvey struggle with. McKelvey, a glassblower, had just lost a sale because he couldn't accept a credit card. The customer walked away. Thousands of dollars gone because the existing payment infrastructure assumed you were a big retailer with dedicated terminals, merchant accounts, and IT support.

Dorsey had been thinking about payments since he was a teenager in St. Louis. He'd built dispatch software at fifteen, fascinated by the coordination of things moving through cities. Twitter had emerged from that same obsession — status as location, social graphs as infrastructure. Now he and McKelvey sketched something simpler: turn a smartphone into a cash register. A square reader that plugged into the headphone jack. Software that made merchant accounts invisible.

They launched Square in 2010. The first readers were literally square — white, elegant, impossibly simple. You plugged it in, swiped a card, handed the phone to the customer to sign with their finger. That was it. No application process, no monthly fees, no specialized hardware. Just 2.75% per swipe.

The incumbent payment processors — First Data, Verifone, the banks — had spent decades building complexity. Complex pricing, complex hardware, complex approval processes. Square made complexity disappear. Coffee shops, food trucks, art fairs, farmers markets — suddenly everyone could take cards. The addressable market wasn't big retailers. It was everyone else.

The banks noticed. They tried to block Square, claiming fraud risk and PCI compliance issues. Visa pulled approval briefly in 2011. Square was routing millions in transactions without the traditional controls, and the card networks were nervous. But the users were real, the transactions were real, and the fraud rates were manageable. The approvals came back. Square kept growing.

By 2012, they were processing $6 billion annually. Starbucks invested $25 million and began accepting Square payments. The validation was enormous. But Dorsey and his team were already seeing the limits of the pure payments play. Stripe was emerging with better developer tools for online payments. PayPal was entrenched in e-commerce. The big opportunity was offline commerce — not just payment processing but the entire point-of-sale ecosystem.

Square Register launched in 2013: a complete register system with iPad stand, receipt printer, cash drawer. Square Capital followed — instant loans based on transaction history. If you processed $50,000 a month through Square, they knew your cash flow better than any bank. They could lend you $10,000 and collect repayment automatically from future sales. No credit check, no paperwork. Just data.

The IPO came in 2015 at $9 per share, valuing the company at $2.9 billion. The stock promptly fell to $8. Investors worried about competition, margins, and the path to profitability. Payment processing was brutally competitive. Amazon was launching payment products. Apple Pay was going to change everything. The growth story felt fragile.

Then Cash App became the story. Square had launched it in 2013 as a peer-to-peer payment app — competition for Venmo. It went nowhere for years. But around 2016, something shifted. Cash App became popular with a different demographic than Venmo. Younger, more diverse, often underbanked. They used it not just for splitting bills but as primary financial infrastructure. Direct deposit, debit card, savings. Square added Bitcoin buying in 2018. Stocks in 2019. Tax filing.

The Cash App Card — a free debit card with instant spending from your Cash App balance — became cultural. Glossy black, customizable with laser-etched signatures. Rappers mentioned it in songs. It showed up in Instagram stories. Square was accidentally building a bank for people who didn't trust banks.

By 2020, Cash App had 30 million active users and was generating more revenue than Square's core seller business. The pandemic accelerated everything. Small businesses needed digital payments. Stimulus checks went into Cash App accounts. Bitcoin surged. In Q4 2020, Cash App generated $1.76 billion in Bitcoin revenue alone.

Dorsey rebranded the company to Block in 2021. The name meant to signal scope beyond payments — blockchain, music, financial infrastructure. More immediately, it signaled that this was no longer just Square the card reader company. Block was Cash App, Tidal (the music streaming service they'd acquired), and increasingly, Bitcoin.

The Bitcoin position became defining. Block started buying Bitcoin for its corporate treasury in 2020. By 2024, they held over 8,000 BTC — around $500 million at various prices. Dorsey, who'd stepped down as Twitter CEO in 2021, made Bitcoin the centerpiece of Block's strategy. Not payments via Bitcoin. Bitcoin itself. Custody tools, Lightning infrastructure, mining technology. Block became simultaneously a profitable payments company and a Bitcoin ideology company.

The stock reflected the duality. When Bitcoin crashed in 2022, Block fell from $280 to $60. When Bitcoin recovered in 2024, Block followed back to $80. The cash-generating payment businesses threw off billions. The Bitcoin bets created volatility. Analysts didn't know whether to value it as a fintech company or a Bitcoin proxy.

The seller business kept grinding. Square Register became Square Terminal, then Square for Restaurants, Square for Retail, Square Appointments for service businesses. Each vertical added software revenue — payroll, inventory management, marketing tools. The margins were better than payment processing. The retention was higher. By 2024, Block had 4 million seller locations and revenue of $5.6 billion.

The path from card reader to Bitcoin treasury wasn't planned. It was accumulation — each product a response to what users needed or what technology enabled. Dorsey stayed close to both the code and the ideology. Where Stripe went global and infrastructure-focused, Block went cultural and consumer-focused. Both were solving payments. Neither looked like the other.

By 2025, Block was worth $46 billion, employed 13,000 people, and was profitable. It had survived competition from everyone, regulatory scrutiny, and Dorsey's own attention split between companies. The original insight — payments should be simple — had spawned an empire. The Bitcoin bet remained unresolved. The company existed in two modes at once: infrastructure that worked and vision that might.
