# Square/Block

***The payments company that became a Bitcoin believer***

> **Years:** 2009-present

Jack Dorsey had already co-founded Twitter when he started thinking about his next company. The idea came from a personal frustration shared by his friend Jim McKelvey, a glassblower in St. Louis. McKelvey lost a sale because he couldn't accept credit cards. In 2009, this was a universal problem for small merchants — accepting card payments required expensive equipment, long approval processes, and a relationship with a payment processor that treated small businesses as barely worth the trouble.

The insight was simple: smartphones had everything needed to process payments except one thing. Dorsey and McKelvey built that thing — a small square dongle that plugged into the headphone jack and turned any iPhone into a card reader. The software was elegant. The pricing was transparent. Square launched in 2010, and within months, people were using it everywhere — at farmers markets, in taxis, at food trucks.

The timing was perfect. The iPhone had created millions of potential point-of-sale terminals. The financial crisis had created millions of people looking for side income, selling things, driving for hire, doing contract work. Square gave them a way to get paid professionally. The company grew explosively.

But the dongle was never the business. The dongle was the hook. The business was the payment processing — taking a small percentage of every transaction. Square's challenge was that payment processing has thin margins and powerful incumbents. The established processors had relationships with banks, economies of scale, and no reason to welcome a new competitor. Square needed to build everything from scratch: fraud detection, compliance systems, relationships with card networks, reserve funds to cover chargebacks.

The early years were financially brutal. Square processed billions in payments but lost money on every transaction. Fraud was rampant — criminals quickly learned they could use stolen cards with Square dongles, and the company had to eat the losses. By 2011, Square was processing $2 billion annually but hemorrhaging cash. Venture capitalists poured in hundreds of millions, betting that scale would eventually bring profitability.

Dorsey's dual role became controversial. He was CEO of Square while also serving as executive chairman of Twitter. Critics questioned whether anyone could run two companies. Inside Square, there were tensions about strategy. The company kept expanding — not just dongles but larger card readers, a full point-of-sale system for restaurants and retail stores, business loans, payroll services. Each new product was elegant and well-designed, but each also complicated the path to profitability.

The breakthrough came from software. Square developed Cash App in 2013, initially as a simple way to send money to friends. It was Venmo-like but simpler, with one crucial difference: Square owned the payment infrastructure underneath. Cash App didn't need to be immediately profitable because it fed into Square's larger ecosystem. People who used Cash App were more likely to become Square merchants. Merchants who used Square were more likely to recommend Cash App to customers.

Then Cash App found an unexpected killer feature: Bitcoin. In 2018, Square added the ability to buy and sell Bitcoin through Cash App. The timing was fortuitous — cryptocurrency was entering a new bull market. Millions of users who had never owned Bitcoin suddenly could buy it with a few taps. Square took a small spread on each transaction, but the volume was enormous. By 2020, Bitcoin trading was generating billions in revenue.

Dorsey had become personally obsessed with Bitcoin. He talked about it constantly, framing it not as an investment but as the native currency of the internet. In 2021, he took the symbolic step of renaming Square to Block, signaling a transformation from payments company to something broader — a builder of tools for economic empowerment, with Bitcoin at the center.

The name change coincided with Dorsey leaving Twitter (for the second time) to focus entirely on Block. The company's strategy became clearer: own the full stack of financial services for individuals and small businesses. Cash App for consumers. Square for merchants. Tidal for music streaming (acquired in 2021). TBD for Bitcoin and decentralized finance infrastructure. Spiral for Bitcoin open-source development.

Critics saw empire-building and distraction. Believers saw an ecosystem. The financial results were mixed. Cash App became hugely profitable, driven by Bitcoin trading and a growing user base that treated it as their primary bank account. The original Square business stabilized, profitable but no longer high-growth. The newer ventures — music streaming, Bitcoin development — were expensive bets.

What started as a simple dongle for accepting credit cards had become a sprawling financial services company with a cryptocurrency philosophy. Block in 2024 processes over $200 billion annually, has over 50 million Cash App users, and holds billions in Bitcoin on its balance sheet. Its stock price fluctuates with Bitcoin sentiment as much as with business fundamentals.

The company Dorsey built solved the problem he set out to solve — any merchant can now accept payments easily. But the path from there led somewhere unexpected. The payment processor became a true believer, convinced that the entire financial system it had learned to navigate would eventually be replaced by something decentralized. Whether that belief is visionary or delusional remains unresolved. Block is placing a very large bet that the future of money looks nothing like its past.
