# Xiaomi

***The outsider that built an empire by making premium feel cheap***

> **Years:** 2010-present

Lei Jun was already wealthy when he founded Xiaomi in 2010. He had made his fortune building Kingsoft, one of China's earliest software companies, and investing in dozens of startups. At forty years old, he could have retired. Instead, he became obsessed with a question: why did Chinese consumers have to choose between expensive foreign phones and cheap domestic garbage?

The smartphone market in 2010 was stratifying. Apple owned the premium tier. Samsung chased close behind. Chinese manufacturers like ZTE and Huawei competed on price, producing phones that worked but felt plasticky and ran bloated Android skins. The mid-market — devices with flagship specs at accessible prices — barely existed. Apple's margins were enormous. The components cost a fraction of retail price. Lei Jun saw arbitrage: what if a company built premium hardware, sold it near cost, and made money on services and ecosystem lock-in instead?

He recruited seven co-founders, all veterans of Google, Microsoft, and Motorola's China operations. They worked in secret for months, building MIUI — a heavily customized Android interface that felt faster and looked better than stock. They released it as a ROM for enthusiasts to flash onto existing phones. Within weeks, hundreds of thousands of users had installed it. Xiaomi had an audience before it had hardware.

The first Xiaomi phone launched in August 2011 for 1,999 yuan — about $310. It had a dual-core processor, specs matching phones that cost twice as much. But the real innovation was distribution: Xiaomi sold only online, only in limited flash sales that sold out in minutes. No carrier partnerships. No retail markup. No advertising budget. The scarcity was partly supply-chain reality, partly theater. Each sellout generated media coverage. Fans who secured a phone felt like winners.

Lei Jun studied Steve Jobs obsessively. He wore dark shirts and jeans at product launches. He spoke in the same cadence — pauses for applause, emphasis on design philosophy, "one more thing" reveals. Western press called him a copycat. Chinese consumers didn't care. The products delivered. MIUI updated weekly based on forum feedback. Xiaomi felt participatory, not corporate. Fans called themselves "Mi Fen" — Xiaomi fanatics. They crowdsourced translations, organized meetups, defended the brand online without payment.

By 2014, Xiaomi was valued at $46 billion, the world's most valuable startup. It had overtaken Samsung to become China's top-selling phone brand. Lei Jun announced expansion into India, Brazil, and Southeast Asia — markets where consumers wanted smartphones but couldn't afford iPhones. The flash-sale model worked everywhere. Xiaomi seemed unstoppable.

Then the model broke. As Xiaomi scaled, the online-only strategy became a weakness. Competitors like Oppo and Vivo blanketed second- and third-tier Chinese cities with physical stores. They paid retailers to push their phones. Rural consumers wanted to touch devices before buying, wanted same-day replacement if something broke. Xiaomi's logistical advantage in tier-one cities meant nothing in Henan province. By 2016, Xiaomi's China market share had collapsed from first to fifth.

Worse, the hardware economics that worked at startup scale failed at giant scale. Selling near cost meant razor-thin margins. A single component shortage or currency fluctuation could turn profit into loss. Xiaomi's services revenue — app store commissions, advertising in MIUI, accessories — grew, but not fast enough. The company wasn't making money on ecosystem lock-in. It was just selling cheap phones cheaply.

Lei Jun responded by doing what he'd mocked others for: opening retail stores. Mi Homes launched in 2016, clean minimalist spaces that resembled Apple Stores but sold not just phones — smart home devices, luggage, backpacks, rice cookers. Xiaomi had expanded into consumer electronics years earlier, using its fan base and distribution to launch products that undercut incumbents the way its phones had undercut Samsung. The stores became showcases for this ecosystem. A customer buying a phone might leave with a robot vacuum and a smart speaker.

The retail expansion worked. By 2018, Xiaomi's shipments recovered. It went public in Hong Kong, though the IPO valued the company at $54 billion — less than its last private valuation. Investors worried about margin pressure and competition. Lei Jun owned the narrative that Xiaomi was an internet company, but the financials looked like a hardware manufacturer's.

India saved Xiaomi's growth story. It became the top-selling brand there, replicating the flash-sale playbook before competitors caught up, then building out logistics and retail. Chinese rivals focused on China. Samsung seemed complacent. Xiaomi owned the mid-market. By 2021, it had passed Apple to become the world's second-largest phone maker by volume, behind only Samsung.

But volume without margin is a treadmill. Xiaomi's net profit margin hovers around 5%, compared to Apple's 25%. It sells five times as many phones as Apple but makes a fraction of the profit. The ecosystem strategy generates revenue but not dominance — Xiaomi's smart home devices are popular, but so are competitors'. The company is enormous, successful, and stuck. It cannot raise prices without losing its identity. It cannot cut costs without sacrificing quality. It has taken the path of scale without the prize of margin.

Lei Jun is sixty now, still CEO, still wearing black shirts at product launches. Xiaomi is no longer the scrappy outsider. It is the incumbent, defending share in India while losing ground in China to younger challengers who replicate its old playbook. The company that proved you could build premium hardware cheap has discovered the harder truth: you can get big that way, but you might never get rich.
