# Xiaomi

***The insurgent that became the system***

> **Years:** 2010-present

In April 2010, Lei Jun gathered seven co-founders in a Beijing apartment to drink millet congee and plan a revolution. Lei was already wealthy from serial entrepreneurship and angel investing — he'd founded Kingsoft and backed dozens of Chinese internet companies. At forty, he could have retired. Instead, he wanted to build China's Apple.

The insight was structural. In 2010, Chinese smartphone buyers faced a brutal choice: cheap Android phones that were terrible, or expensive imports they couldn't afford. The domestic brands — Lenovo, Huawei, ZTE — focused on carrier relationships and enterprise sales. They treated consumers as an afterthought. Apple's iPhone was aspirational but priced for the wealthy. The middle was empty.

Lei and his co-founders — engineers from Google, Microsoft, and Motorola — saw the gap. They would build premium phones at mid-tier prices. But the real innovation wasn't hardware. It was distribution.

Xiaomi launched without retail stores, without carrier partnerships, without traditional advertising. They sold online only, in flash sales that created artificial scarcity. They built MIUI, a custom Android skin, and released it to enthusiasts months before the first phone shipped. By the time the Mi 1 launched in August 2011 at 1,999 yuan (roughly half an iPhone's price), 300,000 users were already running MIUI. They weren't buying a phone. They were joining a movement.

The first batch sold out in 34 hours. The second in minutes. The scarcity was partly real — Xiaomi was capital-constrained and couldn't build inventory — but mostly strategic. Flash sales generated hype. Online-only eliminated retail markups. The company saved marketing budget and passed savings to customers. They called it the "internet model" of hardware.

Lei Jun became China's Steve Jobs, presenting products in jeans and black shirts, building a cult of personality. But the real genius was operational. Xiaomi sold near cost on hardware and planned to profit on services — app store revenue, advertising, content. They borrowed Amazon's playbook: sacrifice margin for market share, monetize later.

It worked violently well. By 2014, Xiaomi was the world's third-largest smartphone maker. Valuation hit $46 billion. They expanded into TVs, routers, power banks, fitness trackers — an ecosystem of cheap, well-designed devices. In 2015, they shipped 70 million phones.

Then the model broke.

The Chinese market matured. Flash sales stopped feeling exciting and started feeling annoying. Competitors like Oppo and Vivo flooded second and third-tier cities with retail stores and celebrity endorsements — the traditional model Xiaomi had rejected. Huawei moved upmarket with better cameras and premium materials. Xiaomi's online-only distribution, once an advantage, became a ceiling.

Worse, the hardware-as-loss-leader strategy never generated the services revenue Lei had promised. Chinese users didn't spend much in app stores. Advertising was competitive. The ecosystem products had thin margins. Xiaomi was selling enormous volumes at minimal profit.

In 2016, shipments dropped to 55 million. The valuation cratered. Lei Jun, who'd stepped back from operations, returned as CEO. The media called it a collapse. Internally, Xiaomi called it a correction.

The comeback wasn't revolutionary. It was conventional. Xiaomi opened physical stores — thousands of them. They moved into India, Indonesia, Europe. They launched premium phones to compete with Apple and Samsung. They streamlined the product line. They stopped pretending hardware was a side business and started optimizing for margin.

By 2018, they were shipping 120 million phones. The IPO in Hong Kong raised $4.7 billion at a $54 billion valuation — below the 2014 peak, but still one of the largest tech listings ever. Lei Jun rang the opening bell, then bowed deeply to employees and investors.

The company Lei built is now enormous: second-largest smartphone maker worldwide, top-seller in India, dominant in smart home devices. But it's not the insurgent anymore. It's the incumbent. The flash sales are gone. The online-only purity is gone. Xiaomi sells phones in malls, advertises on billboards, competes on specs and price like everyone else.

What remains is the efficiency. Xiaomi still sells quality devices cheaper than competitors. The ecosystem still works — phones, earbuds, scooters, rice cookers, all connected, all affordable. They never cracked services revenue, but they cracked scale. Revenue in 2023 exceeded $37 billion.

Lei Jun still presents products on stage, still wears dark shirts, still quotes Steve Jobs. But Xiaomi's story isn't Apple's. Apple captured value by building a walled garden and charging premium prices. Xiaomi captured volume by making technology accessible to millions who couldn't afford premium prices. They democratized quality. The cost was becoming what they disrupted: another phone company, fighting for points of margin and market share.

The revolution succeeded. Then it normalized. The insurgent won by becoming the system.
