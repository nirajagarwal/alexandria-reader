# Oculus

***The dream that became real and then became Facebook***

> **Years:** 2012-2014 (as independent company)

Palmer Luckey was nineteen, living in a trailer in his parents' driveway in Long Beach, and building virtual reality headsets from parts he bought on internet forums. By 2012, he had assembled seventeen prototypes, each one attempting to solve the fundamental problem that had plagued VR for decades: the technology made people nauseous, the field of view was terrible, and the equipment cost tens of thousands of dollars.

VR had failed repeatedly. In the 1990s, companies burned hundreds of millions trying to make it work. Nintendo's Virtual Boy was a disaster. Military and research applications existed, but consumer VR was considered dead — a technology that would always be five years away.

Luckey's insight wasn't entirely new technology. It was that smartphone components had gotten good enough and cheap enough to make VR possible. The gyroscopes, accelerometers, and high-resolution displays that powered iPhones could be repurposed. His sixth prototype, which he called the Rift, used a 5.6-inch smartphone screen, weighed less than a pound, and had a 90-degree field of view. It still looked like something assembled in a garage because it was.

He posted about it on Meant to be Seen 3D, a forum for VR enthusiasts. John Carmack noticed. Carmack — the legendary programmer who created Doom and Quake — had been experimenting with VR for years. He asked Luckey to send him a prototype. Within weeks, Carmack had jury-rigged the Rift to run a demo of Doom 3. He showed it at E3 2012, the gaming industry's biggest trade show, and the response was pandemonium. People waited in line for hours to try it.

The demo lasted ninety seconds, but it was enough. For the first time in twenty years, VR worked. You put on the headset and you were somewhere else. The nausea was minimal. The immersion was total. Gaming press called it a revelation.

Luckey incorporated Oculus VR in July 2012. He brought on Brendan Iribe, a serial entrepreneur who had sold his previous company to Autodesk, as CEO. They hired engineers from id Software, Valve, and NASA. The team was small but loaded with people who had been waiting for VR to work their entire careers.

They needed money to manufacture. Venture capital seemed unlikely — VR had burned investors before — so they turned to Kickstarter. The campaign launched in August 2012 with a goal of $250,000. They raised that in four hours. By the time the campaign closed, they had $2.4 million from 9,522 backers. It was one of the most successful hardware campaigns Kickstarter had seen.

Development kits shipped in early 2013. Developers devoured them. Suddenly, VR demos were everywhere — at conferences, in arcades, in dorm rooms. The technology was still rough. The resolution was low enough that you could see individual pixels. The software ecosystem barely existed. But it worked, and that was enough.

Venture capital arrived. Marc Andreessen and Peter Thiel led a Series A. By December 2013, Oculus had raised $91 million across two rounds at a valuation approaching $400 million. Iribe and Luckey talked about shipping a consumer product by 2015, priced around $300.

Then Facebook called.

Mark Zuckerberg had tried an Oculus demo and become obsessed. He saw VR not as a gaming peripheral but as the next computing platform — a successor to mobile. Facebook's growth was slowing. The company needed new frontiers. Zuckerberg wanted Oculus.

Negotiations happened fast. Facebook offered $2 billion — $400 million in cash, the rest in stock. Iribe and Luckey hesitated. The company was eighteen months old. The consumer product wasn't even finished. But Facebook offered something no amount of venture capital could: infinite runway. They could hire anyone, spend anything, take as long as they needed.

The deal closed in March 2014. The gaming community erupted. Oculus had taken Kickstarter money from gamers who believed in an independent VR future. Now they were owned by a social media company that epitomized surveillance capitalism. Notch, the creator of Minecraft, cancelled the Oculus version of his game. Backers felt betrayed.

Luckey defended the deal on Reddit and got shredded. He argued that Facebook's resources would make VR happen faster and better. The critics argued that Facebook would hollow out the vision and turn VR into an advertising platform.

Both were partly right.

Under Facebook, Oculus did exactly what Luckey said it would. The engineering team exploded to hundreds. They solved hard problems — inside-out tracking, hand controllers, standalone headsets. The first consumer Rift shipped in 2016 at $599 plus the cost of a gaming PC. Reviews were strong, but the price and hardware requirements limited adoption.

Facebook kept funding. Oculus Quest launched in 2019 as a standalone headset requiring no PC or phone. It was cheaper, simpler, and actually sold. By 2021, Quest 2 had sold millions of units. VR had finally crossed into the mainstream.

But the original vision — an independent VR company building the metaverse — was gone. Facebook rebranded itself as Meta in 2021, with VR at the center of its strategy. Iribe left in 2018. Luckey was pushed out in 2017 after political donations and controversial statements made him a liability. The technology they built became a piece of Facebook's larger ambition.

Oculus succeeded. VR works now because of what Luckey started in that trailer. But the independent path, the one where Oculus shaped its own future, ended the moment Zuckerberg made the call.
