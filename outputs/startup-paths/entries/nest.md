# Nest

***The hardware startup that made thermostats desirable and proved Apple alumni could build atoms, not just bits***

> **Years:** 2010-2014 (as independent company)

In 2010, Tony Fadell was done with Apple. He had spent nearly a decade there, leading the team that built the iPod and the first three generations of the iPhone. He was exhausted, burned out from the intensity of working under Steve Jobs, and ready to step away from consumer electronics entirely. He bought a vacation home in Tahoe. The contractor asked him what kind of thermostat he wanted.

Fadell looked at the options and was appalled. Every thermostat was ugly, confusing, and seemingly designed to be ignored once installed. They were products no one thought about, made by companies that had stopped innovating decades ago. The market was dominated by Honeywell and a handful of other industrial players who treated thermostats as commodity infrastructure. Fadell saw something else: a product at the center of the home that people interacted with daily but that had been abandoned by design.

He called Matt Rogers, a former Apple engineer who had worked on the iPhone. They started sketching. Not business plans — actual product sketches. What if a thermostat was beautiful? What if it learned your schedule instead of requiring programming? What if it could save energy without anyone noticing? They founded Nest Labs in 2010 with $80,000 of their own money and began hiring a team of former Apple engineers who understood how to make hardware that people might actually love.

The first Nest Learning Thermostat shipped in October 2011 at $249 — roughly ten times the price of a basic programmable thermostat. It was a metal puck with a circular interface you turned to adjust temperature. It had a motion sensor so it knew when you were home. It connected to WiFi and learned your patterns over time, automatically creating a schedule. It showed you a green leaf when you were saving energy. And it looked like something you might want to show visitors rather than hide behind a bookshelf.

Best Buy agreed to stock it, which was crucial. Most thermostats were sold through HVAC installers who had no incentive to recommend a premium product that homeowners could install themselves. Nest needed retail presence. But even with distribution, success was far from guaranteed. They were asking people to spend hundreds of dollars to replace a product that, while annoying, basically worked. The value proposition depended on energy savings that wouldn't materialize for months.

The press loved it. Tech journalists wrote about a thermostat like it was a new iPhone. That was Fadell's bet paying off: design and brand mattered even in a category everyone assumed was pure utility. Early adopters bought it as much to make a statement about their taste as to save on heating bills. The product spread through word of mouth among a particular class of homeowner — tech workers, design professionals, people who read Dwell magazine.

Money followed attention. Nest raised $80 million in Series B funding in 2011, then $150 million in Series C in 2012. Kleiner Perkins and Google Ventures led rounds. The valuations climbed: $800 million, then over $2 billion. Nest became the canonical example of a hardware startup that could scale, that could build a brand, that could attract venture capital despite manufacturing complexity and inventory risk.

But Nest needed to become more than one product. In 2013, they acquired Dropcam, a home security camera company, for $555 million. The vision was expanding: not just a thermostat, but a platform for the connected home. In October 2013, Nest launched Nest Protect, a smoke and carbon monoxide detector that cost $129 and could tell you in plain language what was wrong instead of just shrieking. It had the same design sensibility, the same attention to interaction detail.

Then Nest Protect had a problem. The device included a feature called Nest Wave — you could wave your hand under it to silence a false alarm. In April 2014, Nest realized this could be dangerous. In a real fire, smoke might trigger the wave sensor and silence the alarm. They stopped selling the device and disabled the feature remotely in existing units. It was the right move, but it showed the stakes of hardware. A software bug can be patched. A hardware recall is existential.

Two months earlier, in January 2014, Google had acquired Nest for $3.2 billion in cash. It was one of Google's largest acquisitions ever. Larry Page said almost nothing publicly about what Google planned to do with Nest, which should have been a warning. Fadell stayed on to run Nest as an independent subsidiary within Google, reporting directly to Page.

The acquisition made sense on paper. Google wanted a foothold in the connected home. Nest had the design talent and brand that Google's own hardware efforts lacked. But the cultures never meshed. Nest operated like a premium hardware company with Apple's design standards. Google operated like an ads company that gave away software to collect data. Nest employees were used to secrecy and hardware schedules. Google employees were used to launching betas and iterating in public.

Fadell left Nest in 2016 amid reports of internal turmoil and delayed products. The company had grown from 200 to 1,200 employees under Google but shipped little new hardware. The thermostat that launched Nest kept selling, but the broader vision of a Nest platform controlling every device in your home never materialized. Google eventually folded Nest into its hardware division, and the brand became one of many Google products rather than the independent jewel it had been.

But Nest had already proved what it needed to prove. A hardware startup could build a premium consumer electronics brand from nothing. You could take an unloved category and make it aspirational. Apple alumni could build companies, not just products. And the connected home was real, even if Nest wouldn't be the one to define it. The thermostat on the wall was no longer just infrastructure. Someone had noticed it.
