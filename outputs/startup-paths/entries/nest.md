# Nest

***The hardware startup that made thermostats desirable and taught Apple how to think about the home***

> **Years:** 2010-2014 (acquired), 2014-present (as Google Nest)

Tony Fadell left Apple in 2008 after a decade building the iPod and helping birth the iPhone. He had no plan to start another company. He was building a house in Tahoe, wrestling with thermostats that seemed designed to frustrate, and wondering why the most expensive component of home energy use was controlled by the ugliest, least thoughtful device in the house.

The insight was simple: thermostats hadn't changed in thirty years because utility companies and HVAC contractors controlled distribution, and they had no incentive to make them better. A thermostat that actually reduced energy use would reduce utility revenue. One that was easy to install would threaten contractor margin. The market structure itself prevented innovation.

Fadell saw an opening. Smartphones had made sensors cheap and connectivity ubiquitous. The Prius had proven that people would pay premium prices for products that combined technology with environmental conscience. And crucially, big-box retailers like Best Buy were desperate for premium products that could compete with Apple Stores for foot traffic. There was a distribution end-run available.

He called Matt Rogers, a former Apple engineer who had worked on the iPod and iPhone. Rogers was twenty-eight and had just left Apple himself. They spent 2009 prototyping in Fadell's garage — the mandatory Silicon Valley origin story, except Fadell already had the credibility and wealth to make it optional. They incorporated Nest Labs in 2010.

The first challenge was technical: thermostats connect to decades-old HVAC systems using arcane wiring standards. One wrong wire could brick a furnace. Fadell and Rogers built a database of HVAC systems and created an installation flow that asked homeowners simple questions and translated answers into correct wiring configurations. They made it possible for normal people to install without calling an electrician.

The second challenge was behavioral. People don't program their thermostats because programming is tedious and life is unpredictable. Nest would learn instead. Motion sensors detected when people were home. Temperature adjustments trained the system on preferences. Over time, it would anticipate needs without explicit instruction. The interface was a circular dial — one moving part, like the original iPod — that felt satisfying to turn.

The third challenge was aesthetic. Fadell insisted on a product that looked like jewelry, not infrastructure. The Nest Learning Thermostat launched in October 2011 at $249 — more than triple the price of a conventional programmable thermostat. It sold directly to consumers online and through Apple and Best Buy stores. The pitch wasn't utility bill savings (true but unromantic), it was design and intelligence. Nest made people *want* to show their friends their thermostat.

They raised $80 million in Series B funding in 2011 from Kleiner Perkins and Google Ventures. Revenue hit $300 million in 2013. The thermostat was a hit, but Fadell knew one product wasn't a company. The vision was the conscious home — devices that sensed, learned, and communicated.

In October 2013, Nest acquired Dropcam, a home camera company, for $555 million. In November, they launched Nest Protect, a smoke and carbon monoxide detector that talked to you instead of just shrieking. It was beautiful — a white circle that glowed different colors — and smart enough to warn you before triggering the alarm. "Heads up, there's smoke in the kitchen."

Then it broke. In April 2014, Nest discovered that a wave-to-silence feature could be accidentally triggered, potentially disabling alarms. They recalled the feature via software update and stopped selling Protect for months. It was a reminder that making infrastructure products "smart" meant that software bugs could now kill you.

But by then, Nest was already inside Google. In January 2014, Google acquired Nest for $3.2 billion in cash. It was a defensive move as much as an offensive one. Apple was rumored to be building a home platform. Amazon had just launched Echo. Google couldn't afford to be shut out of the home.

The deal made sense on paper. Google got hardware design talent, a premium brand, and a beachhead in the home. Nest got distribution, infinite capital, and Google's AI capabilities. Fadell stayed on as CEO and Nest remained a separate entity under Alphabet.

The integration was disaster. Nest's culture valued secrecy and ruthless product focus, inherited from Apple. Google's culture valued transparency and radical collaboration. Fadell ran Nest like a hardware dictatorship, killing projects and berating teams. Employees fled. Dropcam's founders left within two years. Product launches slowed to a crawl.

The promised AI integration never materialized in meaningful ways. The smart home platform — Works with Nest — gained partners but never achieved the seamless interoperability Google had promised. Meanwhile, Amazon's Echo was rapidly becoming the voice interface to the connected home, the role Google had assumed Nest would fill.

Fadell left in 2016. Google folded Nest back into its hardware division in 2018, ending the independence experiment. The brand survived, but as a line within Google's product portfolio, not the hub of the smart home ecosystem.

Nest proved that hardware startups could succeed in the smartphone era by combining Apple-quality design with internet connectivity and intelligence. It demonstrated that consumers would pay premiums for beautifully designed home products if they were sold through the right channels. It showed that legacy industries protected by distribution oligopolies were vulnerable to end-runs through new retail channels.

But it also demonstrated the difficulty of building platforms from scratch, the perils of acquirer integration, and the limits of founder-CEO management styles that worked at Apple but failed in more collaborative cultures. The thermostat succeeded. The vision of the conscious home fractured across Amazon, Google, and Apple platforms instead.

Nest is still sold. The products work. They are better thermostats. But the company that made thermostats an object of desire became just another product line in Google's sprawling empire, a what-if story about the smart home company that nearly was.
