# Robinhood

***The app that democratized trading and nearly destroyed itself***

> **Years:** 2013-present

In 2013, Vlad Tenev and Baiju Bhatt were building software for high-frequency trading firms in New York. They watched these firms execute millions of trades per second, paying almost nothing in fees, while ordinary people paid $7-10 every time they bought or sold a stock. The asymmetry bothered them. They had both grown up in families that came to America seeking opportunity — Tenev's parents were Bulgarian immigrants who worked multiple jobs — and the barriers to investing felt like gatekeeping.

They decided to build a stock trading app with zero commission fees. The idea seemed impossible. Every brokerage made money from commissions. But Tenev and Bhatt had noticed something: the high-frequency traders made money not just from trading, but from payment for order flow — selling their orders to market makers who profited from the bid-ask spread. If they could route retail orders the same way, they could eliminate commissions entirely.

The first version of Robinhood launched in 2014 with a waitlist of nearly one million people. The demand was staggering. Young people who had never opened a brokerage account downloaded the app. The interface looked nothing like E-TRADE or Schwab — it looked like a game. Stocks moved up and down in real-time. You could buy a fraction of a share. The friction had been removed.

The growth was exponential. Robinhood added 3 million accounts in 2017, then 5 million more in 2018. Traditional brokerages watched in horror as their most valuable demographic — young, tech-savvy investors — migrated to an app that charged nothing. By 2019, Schwab, E-TRADE, and TD Ameritrade had all eliminated commissions, attempting to compete. But they were playing catch-up. Robinhood had something they couldn't replicate: cultural momentum. It had turned investing into something cool.

The business model worked better than anyone expected. Payment for order flow generated hundreds of millions in revenue. Robinhood Gold, a premium subscription service, added another revenue stream. Margin lending — letting users borrow money to trade — proved lucrative. The company raised money at increasingly absurd valuations: $1.3 billion in 2017, $5.6 billion in 2018, $11.7 billion in 2020.

But the gamification that made Robinhood addictive also made it dangerous. The app sent push notifications when stocks moved. It used confetti animations when users made trades. It encouraged daily engagement. Studies began showing that Robinhood users traded far more frequently than users of traditional brokerages, often to their own detriment. Critics called it a casino dressed up as a investing platform.

In June 2020, a 20-year-old Robinhood user named Alex Kearns saw a negative balance of $730,000 in his account. He had been trading options, a complex financial instrument that Robinhood had made remarkably easy to access. The interface showed he owed three-quarters of a million dollars. He didn't understand that it was likely a temporary display error — that his positions would settle differently. He wrote a note asking how someone with no income could rack up such debt. Then he killed himself. His family found out days later that he had actually made money on the trade.

Robinhood added more educational materials. They restricted options trading for inexperienced users. But the fundamental tension remained: the easier they made trading, the more money they made, but the more vulnerable users became to their own worst instincts.

Then came January 2021. A community on Reddit called WallStreetBets began organizing a coordinated buying campaign on GameStop, a struggling video game retailer that hedge funds had heavily shorted. The idea was simple and beautiful: if enough retail investors bought GameStop stock, they could trigger a short squeeze that would force hedge funds to buy shares at any price to cover their positions. Robinhood users led the charge.

GameStop's stock price went from $20 to $483 in three weeks. AMC, BlackBerry, and other "meme stocks" followed. Robinhood's servers strained under the volume. Then, on January 28, Robinhood restricted buying of GameStop and other volatile stocks. Users could sell, but not buy.

The fury was instant and bipartisan. Robinhood claimed it had no choice — clearinghouses had raised collateral requirements to $3 billion overnight, and the company needed to prevent a liquidity crisis. But to users, it looked like Robinhood had sided with hedge funds against the little guy. The company that promised to democratize finance had shut down democracy at the crucial moment.

Congressional hearings followed. Vlad Tenev, wearing a suit instead of his usual hoodie, explained clearinghouse mechanics to skeptical politicians. Conspiracy theories flourished. Lawsuits piled up. The company raised $3.4 billion in emergency funding to stay solvent.

Five months later, Robinhood went public at a $32 billion valuation. The IPO was rocky — the stock dropped 8% on the first day. Tenev and Bhatt had allocated 35% of shares to Robinhood users themselves, another attempt at democratization, but many flipped the stock immediately for a quick profit.

By 2024, Robinhood had stabilized but matured. The company had 23 million funded accounts. It had expanded into cryptocurrency, retirement accounts, and credit cards. The stock traded far below its IPO price, then recovered, then fell again. The confetti animations were gone, removed after the backlash. The company had evolved from insurgent to institution, but the question remained: had it democratized investing or simply made it easier for people to hurt themselves?

The founders still believed in the original mission. Tens of millions of people who never would have invested now owned stocks. But the path from idealism to reality had been littered with unintended consequences, regulatory crises, and at least one tragedy that could never be undone.
