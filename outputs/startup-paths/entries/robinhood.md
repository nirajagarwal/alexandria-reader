# Robinhood

***The democratization pitch that made trading free and regulators furious***

> **Years:** 2013-present

Two Stanford physics graduates, Vlad Tenev and Baiju Bhatt, had spent years building high-frequency trading systems for hedge funds in New York. They watched the 2011 Occupy Wall Street protests from their office windows. The irony wasn't lost on them: they were building tools that gave millisecond advantages to the already rich while protesters camped outside demanding financial system reform.

The insight came from watching their friends. Millennials had smartphones and appetites for investing but faced two barriers: commission fees that made small trades uneconomical, and interfaces designed for professional traders. Existing brokerages charged $7-10 per trade. For someone investing $100, that was a 10% haircut before the market even moved. The big firms didn't care — they wanted customers with six-figure portfolios.

Tenev and Bhatt saw the arbitrage. What if you could build a brokerage with no trading commissions? Mobile-first. Simple. The revenue model was unconventional but legal: payment for order flow. Instead of charging customers, they'd route trades to high-frequency trading firms who would pay for the right to execute those orders. The spread the market makers captured was tiny per trade but added up across millions of transactions. Customers got free trades. Robinhood got paid. The market makers got flow.

They launched in 2013 with a waitlist that hit one million people before the app even opened. The branding was perfect: Robin Hood, stealing from the rich (Wall Street's trading advantages) to give to the poor (young investors). The app had a gamified interface with confetti animations when you made a trade. No jargon. No complexity. Just swipe to buy.

The incumbents dismissed them. Charles Schwab and E-TRADE had spent decades building trust and infrastructure. They had branches and advisors and regulatory credibility. Robinhood was a toy, they said. Kids playing with stocks they didn't understand.

But the kids kept coming. By 2016, Robinhood had over one million users. By 2018, four million. The average account was small — a few thousand dollars — but the volume was staggering. Free trading turned investing into entertainment. People checked the app like Instagram. The most popular stocks weren't blue-chip value plays but volatile meme stocks and cryptocurrencies.

The growth brought scrutiny. In 2019, a 20-year-old customer died by suicide after seeing what he thought was a $730,000 negative balance from options trading — actually a temporary display error before trades settled. His family sued. Critics argued Robinhood made complex financial instruments too accessible to unsophisticated investors. The company added educational features and warnings, but the fundamental tension remained: the business model required volume, and volume came from frequent trading by users who often didn't understand what they were doing.

Then came January 2021 and GameStop. A community on Reddit's WallStreetBets coordinated to buy GameStop stock and squeeze short-sellers. The stock went from $20 to $483 in weeks. Robinhood was the platform of choice. When the clearinghouse that processed Robinhood's trades demanded billions in additional collateral due to the volatility, Robinhood restricted buying in GameStop and other meme stocks.

The backlash was instant and ferocious. Users accused Robinhood of protecting hedge funds at retail investors' expense. Congressional hearings followed. Conspiracy theories flourished. The reality was more mundane: Robinhood had a liquidity crisis and couldn't meet clearinghouse requirements. But the optics destroyed the company's populist image. The platform that promised to democratize finance had stopped people from buying stocks at the moment retail investors felt they had Wall Street on the ropes.

The company went public six months later at a $32 billion valuation. The IPO filing revealed the business model more starkly: 81% of revenue came from payment for order flow, with options trading — the riskiest products — generating the most per-customer revenue. The same tools that enabled free trading also created incentives for Robinhood to encourage risky, frequent trades.

By 2022, the contradictions were impossible to ignore. Regulators were circling. The SEC was moving to potentially ban payment for order flow. Competition had evaporated Robinhood's core advantage — within two years of Robinhood's launch, every major brokerage had moved to commission-free trading. Customer growth slowed as markets fell and young investors lost money. The stock that IPO'd at $38 fell to $8.

The company pivoted toward becoming a full-service financial platform: checking accounts, retirement products, credit cards. The confetti animations were toned down. The marketing emphasized responsibility over disruption. Robinhood was trying to mature into the kind of established financial institution it had once positioned itself against.

The story reveals a recurring startup paradox: growth that comes from lowering barriers can attract customers who shouldn't have been excluded by those barriers in the first place. Making something free and easy doesn't make it safe. A user interface that removes friction from trading also removes the natural pause that might prevent a bad decision.

Robinhood did make trading accessible to millions who couldn't afford it before. It forced established firms to drop fees worth billions annually. But accessibility isn't democratization if what you're accessing are products designed to transfer wealth from amateurs to professionals. The company that named itself for a folk hero ended up teaching a generation an expensive lesson: when you're not paying for the product, you are the product — even when that product is your own money.
