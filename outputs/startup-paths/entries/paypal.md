# PayPal

***The accidental payments giant born from a failed Palm Pilot money-beaming experiment***

> **Years:** 1998-present

In December 1998, Max Levchin and Peter Thiel founded Confinity in Palo Alto with $300,000 in funding and an idea that seemed clever at the time: beaming money between Palm Pilots using infrared technology. Levchin, a Ukrainian immigrant and cryptography expert who'd dropped out of the University of Illinois, had the technical chops. Thiel, a former securities lawyer and hedge fund manager with contrarian instincts, had the vision and connections. They thought they were building the future of digital wallets.

They were wrong about the Palm Pilot part.

The infrared demo worked — you could literally beam dollars from one device to another at trade shows — but nobody actually wanted to do this in real life. The market for Palm Pilot money transfers turned out to be roughly zero. By late 1999, Confinity had pivoted to web-based payments, launching a service that let anyone with an email address send money to anyone else. The insight wasn't technological. It was behavioral: on the early internet, there was no easy way to pay another person.

The breakthrough came from desperation and luck in equal measure. To gain users, Confinity started paying people $10 to sign up and $10 more for each referral. It was expensive and arguably insane, but it worked. Users flooded in. More importantly, many were eBay power sellers who desperately needed a better way to accept payments than mailing checks or using Western Union. PayPal — the name of Confinity's email payment service — became the de facto payment method on eBay through pure viral growth.

Then things got complicated.

In March 2000, Confinity merged with X.com, an online bank founded by Elon Musk with his Zip2 proceeds. Musk had also pivoted to payments. The merger was supposed to combine resources and eliminate competition, but it created immediate chaos instead. Two strong-willed CEOs, two different visions, two engineering teams with different code bases. Within months, the board ousted Thiel as CEO and installed Musk.

The company was burning millions monthly on customer acquisition. Fraud was rampant — criminals loved that you could create accounts instantly and move money between them. The Russian mafia particularly appreciated the service. Engineering had to build fraud detection systems in real-time while keeping the platform running. Several times they came within weeks of running out of money.

Musk wanted to build a full-service financial platform. The product team, led by Levchin, wanted to focus relentlessly on payments. The tension boiled over. In October 2000, while Musk was on his honeymoon in Australia, the executive team and board executed what amounted to a coup, replacing him with Thiel. Musk remained the largest shareholder but was out as CEO. The company committed fully to payments and renamed itself PayPal.

The timing was terrible. The dot-com bubble had burst. Funding was impossible. PayPal was still burning cash and fighting fraud. But eBay momentum was undeniable. By early 2001, PayPal was used in over 25% of eBay auctions. eBay had its own payment service, Billpoint, but it was clunky and unpopular. Sellers and buyers had chosen PayPal.

PayPal went public in February 2002 at $13 per share, raising $61 million. The IPO was successful despite the post-bubble wreckage around them, proving the business model worked. The stock jumped to $20 on the first day.

Then eBay made its move. In July 2002, just four months after the IPO, eBay acquired PayPal for $1.5 billion in stock. For the founders and early employees, it was a massive win. Musk made around $165 million. Thiel made around $55 million. Levchin made around $34 million. The team that had nearly run out of money two years earlier was suddenly very wealthy.

The deal was controversial among the PayPal team. Some saw it as selling out too early. Others recognized that eBay could have destroyed them by integrating Billpoint more aggressively or banning PayPal from the platform. Independence was an illusion when 70% of your volume came from one marketplace.

Under eBay, PayPal grew but stagnated. It became a cash cow for the parent company but lost its startup edge. The tight-knit founding team scattered. They became known as the PayPal Mafia — a diaspora of founders and early employees who went on to create or fund an absurd concentration of consequential companies. Musk founded SpaceX and Tesla. Thiel co-founded Palantir and became a legendary investor. Reid Hoffman founded LinkedIn. Steve Chen, Chad Hurley, and Jawed Karim founded YouTube. Jeremy Stoppelman and Russel Simmons founded Yelp. David Sacks founded Yammer. The list goes on.

In 2015, eBay spun out PayPal as an independent public company again, recognizing that the integration had become a strategic straitjacket. PayPal was worth $46 billion at the spin-out — more than eBay itself. The company that started as a Palm Pilot demo had become the dominant digital payments processor, handling billions in transactions annually.

The story is often told as visionary founders who saw the future of payments. The reality is messier and more interesting. They stumbled into the right market by accident after their first idea failed. They grew through an unsustainable customer acquisition strategy that happened to work. They fought off fraud, internal conflict, and near-bankruptcy. They sold to their biggest customer not because they wanted to but because the alternative was riskier. And the wealth and experience from that outcome seeded an entire generation of Silicon Valley companies.

PayPal's path wasn't visionary. It was contingent, reactive, and lucky in the ways that matter most.
