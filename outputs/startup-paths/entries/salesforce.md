# Salesforce

***The cloud evangelism that built an empire***

> **Years:** 1999-present

In early 1999, Marc Benioff had everything a software executive could want at Oracle: status, wealth, a direct line to Larry Ellison. But he'd spent months on sabbatical in Hawaii, and the distance gave him clarity about what he hated in enterprise software — the installations, the consultants, the eighteen-month implementations that failed more often than they succeeded. He saw companies like Amazon delivering software through a browser, no CD-ROMs required, and wondered why business applications couldn't work the same way.

The technical insight wasn't original. Several companies were already attempting web-based CRM. But Benioff understood something deeper: the enterprise software business model was rotten at its foundation. Companies paid millions upfront for software they might never successfully deploy. The vendors got paid regardless. Benioff wanted to flip it — make the software a service, charged monthly, where the vendor only succeeded if the customer actually used it.

He recruited Parker Harris, Dave Moellenhoff, and Frank Dominguez — engineers who could build fast. They launched with a manifesto more than a product. Benioff rented a pier in San Francisco for an event staged as a protest against software, complete with fake protestors holding "THE INTERNET IS FOR EVERYONE" signs. The theatrics were calculated: pick a fight with the entire enterprise software establishment, position Salesforce as the revolution.

The early product was primitive. Web-based CRM with basic contact management and opportunity tracking. Nothing Oracle or Siebel couldn't demolish on features. But it loaded in a browser, required no IT department, and cost $50 per user per month. Small businesses signed up because they had no alternative — traditional CRM was priced for enterprises. Inside the first year, Salesforce had thousands of customers, though "customers" meant small sales teams trying it out, not the enterprises Benioff really wanted.

The problem was credibility. IT departments at large companies considered browser-based software a toy. Security concerns were real — this was 1999, nobody trusted "the cloud" yet because nobody called it that. And Salesforce's infrastructure kept failing. In 2001, the service went down repeatedly. Customer data seemed at risk. For a company selling "no software," the software had better work flawlessly. It didn't.

Benioff made two decisions that saved the company. First, he invested heavily in infrastructure before it was fashionable, building redundancy that competitors dismissed as paranoid overkill. Second, he stopped pretending Salesforce was just simple CRM. In 2003, he launched the AppExchange, a platform for third-party developers to build on top of Salesforce. This was the real vision: not a CRM product, but an operating system for business applications. If developers built on Salesforce, enterprises would have to take it seriously.

The AppExchange nearly bankrupted them. Building a platform meant rewriting core architecture. It took eighteen months and diverted resources from sales. Revenue growth stalled. Investors questioned whether Benioff had lost focus. But when it launched, something unexpected happened: enterprises that had dismissed Salesforce as a small-business tool suddenly saw possibility. If their developers could customize it, extend it, integrate it, then maybe it wasn't a toy. Maybe it was the future.

By 2004, Salesforce was signing six-figure contracts with real enterprises. The IPO that year raised $110 million and valued the company at $1.1 billion. Wall Street remained skeptical — this was still subscription revenue, still unprofitable by traditional metrics. But Benioff had proven the model worked. More importantly, he'd proven enterprises would trust the cloud.

What followed was a decade of relentless expansion. Salesforce bought its way into new markets: Service Cloud for customer support, Marketing Cloud through the Exact Target acquisition, Commerce Cloud, Analytics Cloud. Each acquisition followed the same pattern — buy a point solution, integrate it into the platform, sell it to the installed base. The strategy was expensive and dilutive, but it worked because Salesforce had what competitors didn't: customer lock-in through the platform.

The platform created a moat unlike anything in enterprise software. Once a company built custom applications on Salesforce, switching became nearly impossible. And because third-party developers also built on the platform, each customer became more entrenched over time. Salesforce wasn't selling software anymore. It was selling an ecosystem.

Competitors tried to catch up. Microsoft, Oracle, SAP all launched cloud CRM products. But they were too late and too compromised. They couldn't fully commit to subscription revenue while protecting their legacy software businesses. Benioff had no legacy to protect. He'd built Salesforce from the beginning as cloud-only, subscription-only, multi-tenant architecture. The technical decisions that seemed risky in 1999 became insurmountable advantages by 2010.

By 2020, Salesforce generated over $20 billion in annual revenue and employed 50,000 people. The market cap exceeded $200 billion. Benioff had become enterprise software royalty, buying Time magazine and building a tower that dominated San Francisco's skyline. The company that started as a protest against software had become the establishment.

But the path showed cracks. Customer complaints about complexity and cost increased. The platform had grown so sprawling that implementations again required armies of consultants — the very problem Benioff set out to solve. Activist investors pushed for profitability. The subscription model that seemed revolutionary in 1999 was now standard, and competitors had learned to execute it. Growth slowed.

Salesforce endures as a giant, but the revolution it promised has calcified into something closer to what it disrupted. The cloud won, which means the cloud stopped being a differentiator. What remains is a platform with extraordinary lock-in, a customer base too invested to leave, and a business model that redefined enterprise software. Whether that's triumph or cautionary tale depends on what you thought the revolution was supposed to achieve.
