# Deliveroo

***The logistics company that thought it was a platform***

> **Years:** 2013-present

Will Shu was an investment banker at Morgan Stanley in New York when he moved to London in 2013. He found himself working late nights, unable to get food delivered from the restaurants he actually wanted to eat from. The pizza chains would deliver. The curry houses would deliver. But the good restaurants — the ones with real chefs and proper kitchens — closed their dining rooms at ten and went home.

This wasn't really a technology problem. It was a logistics problem pretending to be a platform problem, which would define everything that followed.

Shu recruited his childhood friend Greg Orlowski, and they launched Deliveroo with a simple premise: build a fleet of drivers on scooters and motorcycles who could pick up food from restaurants and deliver it hot. Not a marketplace connecting existing delivery infrastructure. An actual logistics network, employed or contracted directly, wearing branded jackets, carrying branded thermal bags.

The first question was whether restaurants would participate. The second was whether customers would pay enough to make the unit economics work. The third — which everyone missed at first — was whether the model could ever be profitable.

London in 2013 was ready. Young professionals with disposable income, a restaurant scene that skewed toward small independent operators without their own delivery infrastructure, and a dense urban geography that made bicycle and scooter delivery practical. Deliveroo launched in Chelsea and Kensington, deliberately targeting affluent neighborhoods where customers would pay premium delivery fees and order from expensive restaurants.

They raised a seed round. Then a Series A. The growth numbers were extraordinary. Customers loved it. Restaurants loved the incremental revenue. Venture capitalists saw the next Uber — a platform business that could scale globally, taking a percentage of every transaction without the messy overhead of employment.

Except it wasn't a platform business. It was a logistics business. Every order required a human on a vehicle navigating traffic, waiting at restaurants, finding addresses, dealing with weather. The more Deliveroo grew, the more humans it needed. The faster it wanted deliveries, the more drivers it needed waiting for orders. The better the service, the higher the cost.

By 2016, Deliveroo was expanding aggressively — France, Germany, Spain, Australia, Hong Kong, Singapore. Amazon invested £575 million. The company was valued at over $2 billion. Shu was profiled as a visionary. The metrics looked incredible: gross transaction value growing 600% year-over-year, expansion into dozens of cities, millions of orders.

But the unit economics never quite worked. Take a £20 order. The restaurant keeps £15. Deliveroo takes £5. Out of that £5, they pay the driver £4. That leaves £1 for platform costs, customer acquisition, technology, headquarters overhead. And that's assuming the order is perfectly efficient — one driver, one restaurant, immediate pickup, no delays, no customer service issues.

They tried everything. Raised delivery fees. Lowered restaurant commissions initially to hook them, then raised them later. Introduced subscription programs. Built "dark kitchens" — facilities where multiple restaurant brands operated out of the same kitchen, optimized purely for delivery. Pushed drivers toward self-employment to avoid minimum wage and benefits costs.

The dark kitchens were particularly telling. If you're a platform, you don't build kitchens. If you're a logistics company trying to solve a supply problem, you do. Deliveroo was building physical infrastructure to make its service work, admitting through revealed preference that this wasn't a capital-light marketplace.

Competitors multiplied. Just Eat, Uber Eats, DoorDash. The fight became vicious. Customers multi-homed, comparing delivery fees and times across apps. Restaurants played platforms against each other. Drivers cherry-picked orders. Customer acquisition costs soared. No one could raise prices without losing volume.

The pandemic should have been salvation. Everyone stuck at home, restaurants closed for dining, delivery the only option. Deliveroo's order volume exploded. But so did costs. More drivers needed. Longer delivery times because everyone was ordering at once. Restaurant capacity constraints. And still, no profit.

In March 2021, Deliveroo went public on the London Stock Exchange in one of the most anticipated IPOs in years. The company priced shares at £3.90, valuing it at £7.6 billion. Within minutes of trading, the stock collapsed. By end of day, down 30%. Investors finally did the math on paper and realized what had been true all along: the business didn't work at scale.

The fundamental problem was simple. Food delivery is logistics-intensive, time-sensitive, and low-margin. You're competing with restaurants doing their own delivery, with customers picking up food themselves, with people just cooking at home. The wedge you're inserting — fast delivery from restaurants that don't deliver — creates value, but not enough value to support the cost structure of a global technology company with venture capital expectations.

Deliveroo continued operating, pulling back from some markets, doubling down on others. Amazon increased its stake. The company remains public, still not profitable, still trying to optimize its way to unit economics that work. The stock trades around £1, far below its IPO price, far below its private valuation.

The story Deliveroo told itself and investors was about technology and platforms. The reality was about motorcycles and thermal bags. Somewhere in between those two narratives was a business that could work — but probably not at the scale or valuation that venture capital required. They built something useful and real. They just couldn't make it into what everyone wanted it to be.
