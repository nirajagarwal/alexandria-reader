# Deliveroo

***The algorithm that ate the riders***

> **Years:** 2013-present

In 2013, Will Shu was working in finance in London and found himself frustrated by a simple problem: the good restaurants didn't deliver. Pizza and Chinese food would come to your door, but the interesting new place down the street wouldn't. Someone else would have built a marketplace connecting restaurants to customers. Shu built a logistics company that happened to move food.

The insight was operational. Restaurants didn't deliver because delivery was hard — managing drivers, coordinating timing, handling the complexity of multiple simultaneous orders. Uber had shown that you could coordinate distributed fleets through software. Shu, along with childhood friend Greg Orlowski, saw that model could work for food. They raised £200,000 in seed funding and launched in London with a fleet of riders on scooters and bikes, distinctive in their teal and white.

The early proposition was premium. Deliveroo focused on popular restaurants that didn't deliver — the dinner spot you'd visit in person but couldn't get brought home. They took a commission from restaurants and charged customers a delivery fee. The economics were challenging from the start. Food delivery has thin margins. But the market was exploding. Just Eat dominated the takeaway space in the UK, but they were a marketplace — they didn't control delivery. Uber was preparing to launch Uber Eats. In the US, Grubhub and DoorDash were scaling rapidly.

Deliveroo's advantage was the rider network and the algorithm that coordinated it. As they grew, the system got smarter — predicting demand, positioning riders, optimizing routes. They expanded city by city across the UK, then into Europe, Asia, Australia. By 2016, they were operating in twelve countries. Investment poured in — $275 million in a Series E led by Bridgepoint and Fidelity, valuing the company at over $1 billion. Amazon would later invest $575 million.

The growth was ferocious but the unit economics were brutal. Every order cost more to fulfill than it generated in immediate revenue. The theory was scale: enough density of restaurants and riders in a city, and the system would become efficient. Riders could do multiple deliveries per hour. Fixed costs would spread. Network effects would kick in — more restaurants meant more customers meant more riders meant faster delivery meant more restaurants.

But scale brought different problems. As Deliveroo grew, the rider network became the central tension. Riders were classified as independent contractors, not employees — the same model Uber used. It meant flexibility for Deliveroo and riders could work when they wanted. But it also meant minimum wage laws didn't apply, no sick pay, no benefits. As the company optimized for efficiency, riders found themselves squeezed. The algorithm determined who got which orders. Pay per delivery shrank. Wait times at restaurants went unpaid.

In 2016 and 2017, riders across the UK went on strike. The demands were simple: better pay, recognition as workers not contractors. Deliveroo argued the flexibility model was what riders wanted. Some riders did want flexibility — students, people with other jobs. Others wanted full-time work with protections. The company faced similar battles in France, Belgium, the Netherlands. Courts in various countries ruled in various ways on employment status. The legal landscape was fragmenting.

Deliveroo responded by investing in the rider experience in some ways — equipment, insurance, support. But the fundamental model didn't change. The pressure was competitive. DoorDash, Uber Eats, Just Eat (which merged with Takeaway.com) were all fighting for the same restaurants and customers. No one could afford to worsen their economics by reclassifying riders as employees.

The pandemic changed everything temporarily. Lockdowns made delivery essential. Order volume exploded. Deliveroo's revenue jumped. But so did scrutiny. Riders were now essential workers, delivering food while most people stayed home, with limited protective equipment and the same precarious employment status. The company provided some additional support but maintained the contractor model.

In 2021, Deliveroo went public on the London Stock Exchange. It was supposed to be a triumphant debut — a British tech success story. Instead, it was one of the worst IPOs in London's history. The shares opened at £3.90, below the £3.90 offer price, and immediately fell. They dropped 30% on the first day. Institutional investors had balked. The employment model was a liability. Profitability was distant. Competition was intensifying. Amazon's investment meant competition concerns in some markets.

The company continued operating, iterating on the model. They launched Deliveroo Editions — delivery-only kitchens optimized for the platform. They expanded the grocery delivery business. They kept refining the algorithm. In some markets they grew, in others they retreated. They pulled out of Germany, the Netherlands, Australia — markets where they couldn't reach sufficient scale or where economics didn't work.

By 2024, Deliveroo had achieved profitability in some quarters, but remained a fraction of the valuation investors had once imagined. The rider model remained contested — legally, ethically, economically. The company that had solved the logistics problem of premium restaurant delivery never solved the human problem at its center: how to build a business on the labor of people you don't employ, in a market where margins barely exist.

The algorithm worked. The riders kept riding. The business persisted, neither triumph nor failure, but a kind of grinding equilibrium — optimized for everything except the people who made it run.
