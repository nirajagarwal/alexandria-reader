# Skype

***The technology that won the war it started, then lost the peace***

> **Years:** 2003-present

In August 2003, two Europeans launched software that let people make free phone calls over the internet. Niklas Zennström and Janus Friis, the Swedish-Danish duo behind the file-sharing service Kazaa, had noticed something: internet connections were getting faster, but international calls still cost a fortune. The telecommunications industry had spent a century building a toll system. Skype would route around it entirely.

The technology wasn't new. Voice over Internet Protocol had existed for years. What Skype did was make it work. Previous VoIP services required complicated setups, suffered from poor quality, and often failed to connect through firewalls. Skype used peer-to-peer networking—the same architecture that made Kazaa so hard to shut down—to route calls through the network of users themselves. If you couldn't connect directly, your call bounced through another Skype user's computer. It was decentralized, resilient, and impossible for telecom companies to block without blocking the entire internet.

The product launched with no marketing budget. It didn't need one. Within months, users were downloading it at a rate of 10,000 per day. Within a year, a million people were using it. The value proposition was obvious: call anywhere in the world, for free, with better quality than a phone line. Early adopters were immigrants calling home, expats staying connected, businesses cutting travel budgets. Skype gave them a simple blue button that said "Call." Everything else happened invisibly.

By 2005, Skype had 53 million users and was adding 150,000 new ones each day. The founders had built a business model: Skype-to-Skype calls were free, but calling actual phones—"SkypeOut"—cost money. Not much money, but money. Enough that by 2005, Skype was profitable. Revenue hit $7 million. That September, eBay bought Skype for $2.6 billion.

The logic seemed sound. eBay imagined buyers and sellers communicating through Skype instead of email. They imagined voice becoming part of the commerce experience. What they actually bought was a fast-growing VoIP company that had almost nothing to do with online auctions. The integration never happened. Skype kept growing—200 million users by 2008—but it grew as a standalone property that eBay didn't understand and couldn't figure out how to monetize beyond the model Skype already had.

In 2009, eBay admitted the mistake and sold 70% of Skype to a group of investors led by Silver Lake Partners for $1.9 billion. The founders bought back in. For a brief moment, Skype was independent again, profitable again, growing again. By 2010, it had 660 million users and was handling 40% of all international voice traffic. The company that had set out to destroy the telecom industry had actually done it. Calling had become free. The internet had won.

Then Microsoft came. In May 2011, they paid $8.5 billion—more than three times what eBay had sold for two years earlier. Microsoft had tried to build competitors. They'd launched Windows Messenger, then Windows Live Messenger, then rebranded it again. Nothing caught Skype. So they bought it.

The acquisition made strategic sense. Microsoft needed a communications platform for the mobile and cloud era. Skype had 170 million connected users each month and the verb "to skype" had entered the language. Microsoft integrated it into everything: Windows, Xbox, Outlook, eventually Teams. They made Skype accounts work with Microsoft accounts. They added features: group video, screen sharing, live subtitles.

But they also broke what made Skype special. The peer-to-peer architecture—the thing that made Skype impossible to block and possible to scale—was too expensive to maintain and too hard to monetize. Microsoft moved everything to centralized servers. Skype became just another cloud service, dependent on Microsoft's infrastructure, subject to Microsoft's priorities. The decentralized magic was gone.

Meanwhile, the world moved on. FaceTime made video calls native to iPhones. WhatsApp added voice calls and had a billion users. Zoom emerged from nowhere to own business video. Discord captured gaming. The pandemic should have been Skype's moment. Instead, everyone learned that Zoom worked better. "Can you Skype?" became "Can you Zoom?"

Microsoft tried. They kept updating Skype, redesigning it, rewriting it. None of it mattered. The network effects that once protected Skype now protected its competitors. By 2017, Microsoft had shifted attention to Teams—essentially Skype rebuilt for the Slack era. Skype still exists. Hundreds of millions of people still have accounts. But it exists the way Internet Explorer exists: maintained, but no longer the future.

The story of Skype is the story of a perfect startup that won completely and then lost anyway. They identified a brutal inefficiency—expensive international calls—and eliminated it using a technology—peer-to-peer VoIP—that incumbents couldn't easily replicate. They scaled faster than anyone thought possible. They built a verb. They changed behavior forever. Free calls over the internet aren't special anymore because Skype made them normal.

But winning the war didn't mean winning the peace. The telecommunications industry lost, but Skype didn't inherit their profits. Free calling became table stakes. The technology that made Skype impossible to kill also made it impossible to evolve. The acquisitions that should have provided resources instead diluted the mission. And the window between "too early" and "too late" closed faster than anyone expected. Skype proved you could destroy an industry and still not survive the destruction.
