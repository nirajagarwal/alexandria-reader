# Wirecard

***The accounting fraud that fooled everyone***

> **Years:** 1999-2020

In 1999, Markus Braun took over a struggling payment processor in Munich called Wirecard. The company handled transactions for online gambling sites and adult entertainment — the businesses that traditional banks wouldn't touch. It was unglamorous work in an unglamorous industry, but Braun saw opportunity. He had a PhD in accounting and a vision: transform Wirecard into a global payments giant that would rival PayPal and challenge the old banking establishment.

For years, Wirecard grew steadily in the shadows. It processed payments for merchants operating in regulatory gray zones across Europe and Asia. The business model was simple: charge higher fees than traditional processors because clients had nowhere else to go. Braun reinvested profits into technology and acquisitions, gradually moving the company upmarket toward legitimate e-commerce clients. In 2005, Wirecard listed on the Frankfurt stock exchange. By 2008, it had joined Germany's TecDAX technology index.

Then Wirecard discovered something more lucrative than processing payments: telling a story about processing payments. Braun began positioning the company as a fintech innovator, the German answer to Silicon Valley. He spoke in buzzwords about digital transformation, artificial intelligence, and the future of commerce. Wirecard wasn't just a payments processor — it was building the infrastructure for the cashless society. Investors bought the vision. Between 2008 and 2018, the stock rose over 1,500 percent.

The growth looked extraordinary on paper. Wirecard reported surging revenues and profits, particularly from its operations in Asia. The company claimed to be processing billions in transactions for major partners like Softbank and Alibaba. In 2018, Wirecard replaced Commerzbank in the prestigious DAX 30, Germany's blue-chip stock index. Braun was celebrated as a visionary CEO. The company's market value reached €24 billion. For a brief moment, Wirecard was worth more than Deutsche Bank.

But journalists and short sellers had been asking uncomfortable questions for years. The Financial Times began publishing investigations in 2015, alleging that Wirecard used forged contracts and phantom customers to inflate revenues. Internal documents suggested round-tripping: money cycled through shell companies to create the appearance of legitimate transactions. Wirecard's third-party acquirers in Asia — the local partners supposedly processing billions in payments — appeared to be fronts.

Wirecard responded aggressively. The company hired investigators to surveil journalists. It filed criminal complaints alleging market manipulation. German financial regulators, convinced that short sellers were attacking a national champion, temporarily banned short-selling of Wirecard stock. The company's auditor, EY, signed off on the accounts year after year. Most investors dismissed the allegations as conspiracy theories or anti-German bias.

Jan Marsalek, Wirecard's chief operating officer, managed the Asian operations where most of the questioned revenues originated. An Austrian who claimed fluency in seven languages and connections to intelligence agencies, Marsalek was Braun's enforcer and fixer. He cultivated relationships with politicians and regulators across Southeast Asia. When questions arose about specific transactions or partners, Marsalek provided documentation that seemed to settle doubts. The growth in Asia was real, he insisted — journalists just didn't understand how business worked in emerging markets.

In 2019, pressure mounted. The Financial Times published more allegations. Wirecard's new auditor, KPMG, launched a special investigation. The Singapore office that supposedly processed enormous transaction volumes turned out to be a small shopfront. Partners in the Philippines and elsewhere couldn't be verified. In June 2020, Wirecard announced that €1.9 billion in cash — nearly a quarter of its balance sheet — didn't exist. The money had supposedly been held in escrow accounts at two Philippine banks. The banks confirmed no such accounts existed.

Braun resigned. Days later, he was arrested attempting to board a flight from Austria to Belarus. Marsalek disappeared. Investigators later determined he had fled to Russia with assistance from intelligence contacts, though this has never been confirmed. Wirecard filed for insolvency a week after announcing the missing money.

As investigators picked through the ruins, the scope of the fraud emerged. Much of Wirecard's growth had been fictional for years, possibly dating back to before it joined the DAX. The third-party acquirer business in Asia, which generated the majority of reported profits, was largely invented. Money was shuffled between entities in a complex shell game. Real revenues from legitimate payment processing were mixed with fabricated numbers from phantom transactions. EY had audited the accounts for over a decade without catching the fraud, allegedly because Wirecard provided forged bank statements and Marsalek choreographed site visits to create the illusion of bustling operations.

The collapse devastated thousands of retail investors and pension funds. German regulators faced intense criticism for protecting Wirecard instead of investigating it. The scandal revealed uncomfortable truths about how easily financial markets can be deceived when a company tells the story investors want to hear — particularly when that story involves European technology competing with American dominance.

Braun was convicted of fraud and market manipulation in 2024, sentenced to eleven years in prison. He maintained his innocence, claiming he had been deceived by Marsalek and others. Marsalek remains at large, reportedly in Russia. EY paid settlements but admitted no wrongdoing. The question investigators never fully answered: when did the fraud begin? Was Wirecard ever the company it claimed to be, or was the entire ascent built on fiction?
