# Wirecard

***The fraud that fooled everyone until it couldn't***

> **Years:** 1999-2020

Wirecard began in 1999 as a payment processor for online gambling and pornography — the kinds of businesses that traditional banks didn't want to touch. Markus Braun, an Austrian consultant with a PhD in information systems, took control in 2002 and saw an opportunity. Europe's payment infrastructure was fragmented. E-commerce was exploding. Wirecard could be the platform that connected merchants to customers across borders, handling the messy work of reconciling currencies, regulations, and risk.

The pitch was compelling: Wirecard wasn't just processing payments, it was providing the entire technology stack. While competitors like PayPal focused on consumer-facing products, Wirecard sold itself as the behind-the-scenes infrastructure for enterprise clients. By the mid-2000s, it had moved beyond its sketchy origins to sign legitimate retailers. The company went public in 2005.

Braun was methodical, reserved, technical. He spoke the language of digital transformation before it became a cliché. Germany's business establishment embraced him. Here was a genuine tech company emerging from Munich — not another industrial manufacturer, but a payments platform that could compete with Silicon Valley. By 2018, Wirecard joined the DAX 30, replacing Commerzbank. It was worth more than Deutsche Bank. Braun became the first tech CEO in the DAX.

The growth story was extraordinary. Revenue grew from €40 million in 2005 to €2 billion by 2017. The stock price climbed relentlessly. Wirecard announced partnerships with Softbank, signed deals with major airlines, and launched a mobile banking app. It was expanding into Asia, where Braun claimed the real growth would come. The company projected it would handle €1 trillion in payment volume by 2025.

But the story had problems. As early as 2008, there were questions. A report by a forensic analyst named Matthew Earl raised concerns about accounting irregularities. The Financial Times began investigating in 2015, publishing articles that questioned Wirecard's revenue in Asia, alleged round-tripping of funds, and suggested the company was inflating profits. Braun dismissed the reports as attacks from short sellers trying to manipulate the stock. German regulators investigated — the Financial Times journalists, not Wirecard. BaFin, Germany's financial regulator, banned short-selling of Wirecard shares. Ernst & Young, the auditor, signed off on the books year after year.

The scale of the deception became clear in June 2020. Wirecard announced that €1.9 billion in cash — representing nearly a quarter of its balance sheet — didn't exist. The money was supposedly held in escrow accounts at two Philippine banks for the benefit of Wirecard's merchant acquiring business. The banks denied ever holding the funds. EY refused to sign off on the 2019 audit. Within days, Wirecard admitted the cash "probably doesn't exist."

Braun resigned and was arrested. The company filed for insolvency. The stock, which had been worth €104 billion at its peak, became worthless. As prosecutors dug into the wreckage, the picture that emerged was staggering. The fraud wasn't a recent desperation move — it appeared to have been running for years, possibly since 2015 or earlier. Revenue from Wirecard's Asian operations, the supposed engine of growth, had been fabricated. A network of shell companies with vague business purposes had circulated money to create the appearance of legitimate transactions. Internal documents suggested senior executives knew. The company had been booking revenue from partners that didn't exist and customers who had never signed contracts.

Jan Marsalek, the company's COO and Braun's deputy, disappeared. Intelligence agencies later reported he had fled to Belarus and then Russia. Before his disappearance, there were allegations he had connections to Russian intelligence services, that Wirecard had been conducting espionage on journalists investigating the company, and that parts of the operation functioned less as a tech company and more as a sophisticated money-laundering network.

The questions multiplied. How did EY miss it during annual audits? Why did BaFin defend Wirecard so aggressively? Why did German politicians embrace Braun so enthusiastically? The answers pointed to a system that wanted to believe. Germany wanted a tech champion. The financial establishment wanted to believe the growth story. Auditors relied on documents that looked legitimate. Regulators trusted a DAX-listed company more than suspicious journalists.

Braun went to trial in 2022, maintaining he was deceived by Marsalek and others. The prosecution argued he orchestrated the fraud from the beginning. As of 2024, the trial continues, with over 200 hours of testimony. Marsalek remains at large. Investors lost billions. The insolvency administrators recovered a fraction of the company's claimed assets. 

Wirecard's technology platform, the actual operational business that processed real payments, was sold off in pieces. Some of it still functions, stripped of the brand and the fiction, processing transactions for merchants who need payment infrastructure. The legitimate business existed. It just wasn't worth €24 billion. It was never the revolutionary platform Braun described. It was a mid-tier payment processor that its CEO wrapped in a story too compelling to question — until the story demanded cash that didn't exist.
