# Alibaba

***The teacher who built China's Amazon before Amazon could***

> **Years:** 1999-present

Jack Ma was an English teacher in Hangzhou who had failed the college entrance exam twice and been rejected from dozens of jobs, including KFC. In 1995, on his first trip to the United States, a friend showed him the internet. Ma searched for "beer" and found results from America, Germany, Japan — but nothing from China. He searched for "China" and found almost nothing. The insight was immediate: Chinese businesses needed to be online, and whoever connected them would ride a historic wave.

His first two internet ventures failed. But in 1999, Ma gathered seventeen friends in his apartment and pitched them on Alibaba — a B2B marketplace connecting Chinese manufacturers to overseas buyers. The name, he explained, was globally recognizable and started with A, ensuring top placement in directories. The timing was perfect. China was about to join the WTO. Small manufacturers desperate for export channels had no way to reach foreign buyers except through expensive trade shows. Foreign buyers had no efficient way to source from China's vast factory network.

Alibaba launched as a directory with ambitious promises: "We will make it easy to do business anywhere." The model was simple — manufacturers posted product listings, buyers found them, deals happened offline. Revenue came from premium listings and membership fees. But the execution required navigating China's nascent internet infrastructure, building trust in a market where neither buyers nor sellers trusted online transactions, and competing against established players with more resources.

The dot-com crash of 2000 nearly killed them. Foreign investment dried up. Competitors collapsed. Ma had raised $25 million from Goldman Sachs and other investors in late 1999, but burned through it chasing growth. He made two critical decisions: cut expenses to extend runway, and pivot focus from international buyers to the domestic Chinese market that everyone else was ignoring. While competitors chased sexy consumer plays, Alibaba went after the unglamorous work of digitizing business-to-business commerce for small enterprises.

The breakthrough came not from Alibaba.com but from a defensive move. In 2003, eBay entered China by acquiring EachNet, instantly becoming the dominant consumer marketplace. Ma saw eBay's expansion as an existential threat — if they controlled consumer commerce, they would eventually move into B2B and crush Alibaba. He launched Taobao, a consumer-to-consumer marketplace, to fight back.

Everything eBay did, Taobao did differently. eBay charged listing fees; Taobao was free. eBay had a Western interface; Taobao looked like a Chinese bazaar. eBay relied on PayPal; Taobao created Alipay, an escrow service that held payment until buyers confirmed receipt — critical in a low-trust environment. eBay prohibited direct communication between buyers and sellers; Taobao made chat central via Aliwangwang, turning transactions into relationships.

The strategy worked beyond Ma's projections. By 2006, Taobao had 67% of the Chinese consumer marketplace. eBay withdrew. What started as defense became offense. Taobao wasn't just winning — it was creating the infrastructure for e-commerce in a country where credit cards barely existed, logistics were primitive, and fraud was endemic.

Alipay became more than a payment processor. It became the foundation for Ant Financial, eventually processing more transactions than Visa and Mastercard combined in China. Taobao spawned Tmall, a premium platform for established brands. The logistics problem — how to deliver packages across a country with poor infrastructure — led Alibaba to invest in Cainiao, a data-driven logistics network coordinating thousands of delivery companies.

Each solution to a Chinese market problem became a competitive moat. Amazon entered China in 2004 with capital and expertise but never gained traction. They applied American playbooks to Chinese problems. Alibaba had built specifically for China's unique conditions: low trust, poor logistics, mobile-first consumers skipping desktop entirely, a willingness to blend social and commercial in ways Western users found intrusive.

The 2014 IPO on the New York Stock Exchange raised $25 billion — the largest IPO in history at the time. Alibaba's market cap exceeded $200 billion. Ma became China's richest man and a global celebrity, giving TED talks and meeting world leaders. The company expanded into cloud computing, entertainment, brick-and-mortar retail, and artificial intelligence.

But scale brought scrutiny. Chinese regulators grew concerned about Alibaba's market power. In 2020, authorities halted Ant Financial's IPO days before launch — it would have been the largest IPO ever. In 2021, Alibaba was fined $2.8 billion for anticompetitive practices. Ma, once celebrated by the government, disappeared from public view for months. The company was forced to restructure, spinning off divisions and accepting increased regulatory oversight.

Alibaba remains dominant in Chinese e-commerce, with platforms generating over $1 trillion in annual GMV. It faces new competitors like Pinduoduo and ByteDance's Douyin, and navigates geopolitical tensions that complicate international expansion. The company that built China's digital commerce infrastructure now operates in an environment where its success makes it a target.

Ma stepped down as chairman in 2019, leaving a company that had transformed from eighteen people in an apartment to a global giant employing over 250,000. The English teacher who couldn't get hired built the marketplace that connected the world to Chinese manufacturing, then created the tools that brought millions of Chinese businesses online. The path wasn't Amazon's path transposed to China. It was a completely different trail, cut specifically for terrain no one else understood.
