# Tencent

***From messaging clone to digital empire***

> **Years:** 1998-present

In November 1998, five engineers sat in a cramped apartment in Shenzhen, launching a messaging service nobody wanted. Ma Huateng — known as Pony Ma — and his four co-founders from China Telecom had built OICQ, a direct copy of the Israeli instant messenger ICQ. They had no business model. They had barely enough server capacity for a few thousand users. Within months, they would be desperately trying to sell the whole operation for $1 million.

China's internet in 1998 was dial-up, expensive, and mostly empty. Portal sites dominated. The idea that Chinese users would sit at internet cafes sending messages to each other seemed questionable at best. But OICQ grew faster than Tencent's servers could handle. By early 1999, they had a million users and no money. The servers kept crashing. Ma and his team took turns sleeping in the office, manually rebooting machines through the night.

ICQ's owner, AOL, sent a cease-and-desist. OICQ became QQ — supposedly because the Q looked like a cute character. The rename turned out to be lucky. QQ sounded friendlier, more Chinese. But friendly didn't pay bills. Ma pitched every VC in China. Most said instant messaging was worthless — how do you monetize people chatting? Sina and Sohu had already rejected buying Tencent for $1 million. Ma kept reducing the asking price. No takers.

Then IDG and PCCW, a Hong Kong telecom, each invested $2.2 million for 20% of the company. The deal valued Tencent at $22 million, barely enough to keep the lights on. Ma used the money to buy better servers and hire engineers. QQ kept growing, but revenue remained a fantasy. The dot-com crash of 2000 made their situation desperate. The investors wanted out. Ma again tried to sell — this time to China Telecom for $6 million. China Telecom passed.

The breakthrough came from an unexpected place: teenage users customizing their QQ avatars and chat interfaces. In 2001, Tencent started selling virtual items — outfits for QQ avatars, custom emoticons, special chat features. It seemed frivolous. It printed money. Chinese users, especially young ones, paid real currency for digital status symbols. Revenue exploded. By 2002, Tencent was profitable.

Ma saw something others missed: QQ wasn't just a messaging app. It was digital identity. Users collected QQ numbers the way Americans collected phone numbers. A good QQ number — short, memorable — became valuable. Tencent started auctioning premium numbers. They launched QQ Show, essentially a virtual clothing store for avatars. Revenue kept climbing.

In 2004, Tencent went public in Hong Kong, raising $200 million at a $620 million valuation. The company had 291 million registered users. Skeptics questioned whether virtual items could sustain a public company. Ma had already moved to the next phase: gaming.

Tencent launched QQ Game in 2003, embedding casual games directly into the messenger. Users could play while chatting. The games themselves were free, but power-ups, special items, and premium features cost money. The model worked instantly. By 2004, QQ Game was the largest online gaming platform in China. Tencent then began licensing Korean MMORPGs, localizing them for Chinese users, and distributing through QQ. The built-in user base meant every game launched with millions of potential players.

The pattern became clear: Tencent would watch other companies innovate, wait to see what worked, then build their own version and distribute it through QQ. When Facebook launched, Tencent launched Qzone. When Twitter emerged, Tencent launched Weibo. When YouTube appeared, Tencent launched QQ Video. Critics called them copycats. Ma called it learning from the market.

But copying had limits. By 2010, QQ faced a crisis. Mobile was arriving. QQ was built for PC. Smartphones needed different interfaces, different usage patterns. Worse, a startup called Kik had launched a mobile messenger that was faster, simpler, cleaner than QQ. Chinese copycats immediately followed. Tencent seemed slow, bloated, trapped by their PC success.

An internal team — not Ma's chosen executives — built WeChat in 2011. It was mobile-first, stripped down, designed for smartphones. Ma initially dismissed it as redundant with QQ. The team persisted. When WeChat launched, it caught fire. Within a year, 100 million users. Within two years, 300 million. It cannibalized QQ, but Ma let it. Better to disrupt yourself than let someone else do it.

WeChat became something beyond messaging. Users paid bills, ordered food, hailed taxis, transferred money, all inside WeChat. Western observers called it a "super app" and wondered why nothing like it existed outside China. The answer was simpler than it seemed: Tencent owned the identity layer. WeChat wasn't just an app; it was infrastructure.

Ma had learned from the near-death experiences. Tencent diversified relentlessly. They became the largest gaming company in the world, buying stakes in Riot Games, Epic Games, Supercell, Activision Blizzard. They invested in hundreds of Chinese startups — JD.com, Meituan, Pinduoduo, Didi. When entrepreneurs pitched competitors, VCs would ask: "What happens when Tencent copies you?" Often, the answer was: "We take their investment instead."

By 2017, Tencent's market cap exceeded $500 billion, making it one of the world's most valuable companies. Revenue came from gaming (40%), social networks (25%), online advertising (18%), and fintech (17%). The company that almost sold for $1 million was worth half a trillion.

Then Chinese regulatory pressure arrived. In 2018, the government froze new game approvals for months, crushing Tencent's gaming revenue. In 2021, regulators called gaming "spiritual opium" and imposed strict limits on youth gaming. Tencent's stock fell 50% from its peak. The company that had thrived in regulatory gray zones now faced direct government intervention. Ma became less visible, more careful. Tencent shifted from growth to stability, from expansion to entrenchment.

The five engineers who couldn't give away their company in 1999 had built an empire worth $400 billion by 2024. But the path was never assured. They survived because they were willing to copy, iterate, cannibalize themselves, and — when necessary — let the market tell them what they'd built mattered more than what they'd intended to build. QQ wasn't supposed to be digital identity. WeChat wasn't supposed to be financial infrastructure. Tencent wasn't supposed to own half of Chinese internet commerce. But in technology, the company that survives is rarely the one that sticks to the plan.
