# Dollar Shave Club

***The viral video that built a billion-dollar rebellion***

> **Years:** 2011-2016

Michael Dubin had never worked in consumer goods, knew nothing about manufacturing razors, and had no interest in playing by the rules of an industry dominated for a century by two giants. He was a digital marketer and improviser who, in 2011, kept noticing how much he hated buying razors. Not the shaving part — the buying part. The locked cases at drugstores. The absurd prices. The marketing that insisted five blades were better than four, and six would be better still, as if shaving were Formula One racing.

At a holiday party, he met Mark Levine, who had a warehouse full of twin-blade razors from a manufacturing contact in Korea. Levine was trying to figure out what to do with them. Dubin saw the angle immediately: men didn't want space-age technology. They wanted a decent razor without the hassle and markup. They'd been conditioned to think razor buying had to be complicated and expensive, but what if it didn't?

Dollar Shave Club launched in March 2012 with a simple value proposition: quality razors delivered to your door for a dollar a month. No drugstore. No locked cases. No twenty-dollar cartridges. Just subscribe and forget about it. The model borrowed from subscription services that had worked in other categories, but razors were still dominated by retail shelf space, and shelf space was controlled by Gillette and Schick.

The launch video cost $4,500 to produce. Dubin wrote it himself, drawing on his improv background. He delivered deadpan lines like "Do you think your razor needs a vibrating handle, a flashlight, a back-scratcher, and ten blades?" while walking through the warehouse. "Our blades are fucking great," he said, straight to camera. The tone was irreverent, honest, anti-corporate. It felt like a friend telling you that you'd been getting ripped off your whole life.

The video went viral within hours. Twelve thousand people signed up the first day. The site crashed. Orders piled up faster than they could fulfill them. They scrambled to keep up, shipping razors in person when logistics fell apart. Within forty-eight hours, the video had been viewed millions of times. This wasn't advertising. This was a cultural moment. Men shared it because it articulated a frustration they hadn't known they were allowed to have.

Gillette controlled 70 percent of the U.S. razor market. They'd built that dominance through relentless innovation theater — new blade configurations, new features, new reasons to charge more. Their margins were extraordinary. They owned the retail shelf space and spent hundreds of millions on advertising. Dollar Shave Club couldn't compete on those terms, so they didn't try. They competed on honesty, convenience, and the internet.

The subscription model was the engine. Once someone signed up, they kept getting razors. No decision fatigue. No forgetting to buy them. The customer acquisition cost was high initially, but lifetime value was strong. Dubin focused on content and brand. He produced comedy videos, blog posts, and social media that reinforced the same message: we're not trying to scam you. The brand voice was consistent, funny, and unpretentious.

Revenue grew from $4 million in 2012 to $152 million in 2015. But scale brought complexity. They needed to expand beyond the basic razor. They launched shave butter, wipes, hair products — building out a men's grooming line. Each new product was a bet on whether the brand could carry beyond the original insight. Some worked. Others didn't. The logistics of subscription fulfillment, inventory management, and customer service were harder than making a viral video.

Competitors emerged. Harry's launched in 2013 with a similar model but positioned slightly upmarket. Unilever bought them in 2016. Gillette, slow to react initially, launched Gillette Shave Club in 2015. The giants were waking up. Dollar Shave Club had proven the model worked, which meant everyone would copy it.

Dubin raised $163 million over multiple funding rounds. Investors bet on the brand, the recurring revenue, and Dubin's ability to grow beyond razors into a broader direct-to-consumer men's brand. But the unit economics were hard. Customer acquisition costs rose as competition increased. Retention was strong, but growth required constant marketing spend. The company wasn't yet profitable.

In July 2016, Unilever acquired Dollar Shave Club for $1 billion in cash. It was one of the largest acquisitions of a digital-first consumer brand at the time. Unilever wasn't buying manufacturing capacity or patents. They were buying a brand that had shown there was a different way to reach customers, a customer base of 3.2 million subscribers, and a model that could scale globally with their resources.

For Dubin and early investors, the outcome was triumph. For Gillette, it was a warning that had come too late. Dollar Shave Club hadn't beaten them with better blades. They'd beaten them by realizing the game had changed. Customers didn't need to be sold. They needed to be served. And the internet made it possible to reach them without a retail middleman.

The company continued under Unilever, but the story's climax was the sale. A comedian with no industry experience had taken on a century-old duopoly and forced them to rethink everything. Not by out-innovating them in product, but by out-thinking them in distribution, brand, and customer relationship. The locked cases stayed locked, but millions of men stopped going to the drugstore.
