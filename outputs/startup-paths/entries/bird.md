# Bird

***The scooter company that rode hype to a $2.5 billion valuation and crashed into reality***

> **Years:** 2017-2023

Bird arrived at exactly the right moment, or so it seemed. In September 2017, Travis VanderZanden dropped electric scooters on the sidewalks of Santa Monica without asking permission. He knew the playbook — he'd been an early executive at both Lyft and Uber, watching them bulldoze regulations and reshape cities. Scooters would be the same, only faster.

The insight was genuinely simple: dockless bikes were spreading through Chinese cities, but Americans didn't want to pedal. Electric scooters required no effort and felt fun. The first-mile, last-mile problem was real. Parking was terrible. Traffic was worse. If you could unlock a scooter with your phone and ride it three blocks for a dollar, you would.

VanderZanden raised $15 million before launch. Within weeks, Bird scooters covered Venice Beach. Within months, they were in a dozen cities. The unit economics story was compelling: charge scooters $1 to unlock plus 15 cents per minute, pay gig workers ("chargers") a few dollars to collect and recharge them overnight, achieve profitability per ride within months as scooters lasted longer.

The growth was extraordinary. Lime launched two months later. Spin, Skip, and a dozen others followed. Cities woke up to sidewalks covered in scooters. Some banned them. Others created permit systems. Bird hired lobbyists and held press conferences about sustainable transportation. They raised $100 million, then $150 million. By March 2018 — six months after launch — Bird hit a $1 billion valuation.

The venture capital poured in because the TAM slides were intoxicating. Every car trip under two miles was addressable. Every college campus, every downtown, every tourist district. Sequoia led a $300 million round at a $2 billion valuation in June 2018. Fifteen months old, still unprofitable, Bird was worth more than most century-old companies.

But the cracks showed early. The scooters broke constantly. Xiaomi manufactured them cheaply; riders treated them roughly. The original models lasted weeks, not months. Vandalism was rampant. Cities impounded thousands. Chargers gamed the system, hoarding scooters or falsifying charges. Customer acquisition costs stayed high because nobody was loyal — they'd ride whichever scooter was closest.

Bird pivoted to more durable scooters, but they cost $500-800 each instead of $200. The company began deploying Bird Zero and Bird One models designed to last a year. Operations got more sophisticated. They hired operations managers in each city, built relationships with municipal governments, created exclusive permit deals.

The cash burn was staggering. Bird spent money on scooters, on chargers, on city lobbying, on marketing, on expanding to Europe. They raised $275 million more in October 2018, hitting a $2.5 billion valuation. VanderZanden talked about going public. The company had 10 million rides. They projected profitability by 2020.

Then the music stopped. The 2019 venture market cooled. WeWork's implosion made investors skittish about money-losing companies with charismatic founders. Bird tried to raise $250 million and struggled. They pulled back from smaller cities. They laid off 30% of staff in March 2020, right as COVID-19 shut down urban transit entirely.

The pandemic should have been Bird's moment — outdoor transportation, social distancing, avoiding public transit. Instead, rides collapsed. Cities locked down. The scooters sat unused. Bird renegotiated contracts, deferred payments to suppliers, laid off more staff. They raised debt instead of equity, accepting worse terms.

When cities reopened, the riders didn't fully return. Some had bought bikes. Others worked from home. The romance of scooters had faded into the reality of them: a sometimes-convenient, often-broken way to go short distances when nothing else was available. The competition consolidated. Lime survived. Spin sold to Tier. Skip shut down. Bird acquired Scoot and Circ in fire sales.

In May 2021, Bird announced a SPAC merger valuing the company at $2.3 billion. The deal closed in November. The stock opened at $8.40 and immediately fell. By mid-2022, it traded under $1. The company reported $200 million in revenue but burned $300 million in cash. They did another layoff. Then another.

The final decline was administrative. Bird's stock dropped below $1 for 30 consecutive trading days, triggering a Nasdaq delisting warning. They did a 1-for-15 reverse stock split. It didn't help. In December 2022, the company warned it might not survive. In September 2023, Bird filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. The assets sold to Bird Canada for $25 million — 1% of the peak valuation.

VanderZanden had moved on years earlier, stepping back from operations after the SPAC deal. The story he'd told wasn't quite wrong: people would pay to ride scooters. But they wouldn't pay enough to cover the cost of hardware, operations, regulations, competition, and capital. The unit economics never worked at scale. The total addressable market existed, but the defensible, profitable portion was vastly smaller than projected.

Bird rode the 2017-2019 venture wave perfectly. They moved fast, raised fast, grew fast. The collapse revealed what the boom obscured: that speed and capital cannot manufacture a sustainable business, that regulatory arbitrage is not a moat, and that being first to market matters less than being first to profitability. The scooters are still out there in some cities, mostly run by Lime now, a reminder of the path not taken and the billions that rode it nowhere.
