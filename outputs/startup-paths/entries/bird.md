# Bird

***The scooter boom that became a cautionary tale about unit economics***

> **Years:** 2017-2023

Bird didn't invent the electric scooter, but in September 2017, Travis VanderZanden invented the idea that scooters could be left anywhere, ridden by anyone, and somehow make money. He'd been at Uber and Lyft — he understood the religion of growth, the faith that scale solves all problems. He saw cars as too heavy, bikes as too effortful, and scooters as the perfect last-mile solution. The insight was simple: people will pay $1 to unlock and 15 cents per minute to ride something they find on a street corner.

The early days were pure land-grab. Bird dropped scooters in Santa Monica without asking permission. The city responded with cease-and-desist letters. Bird kept dropping scooters. The friction created press. The press created users. Within months, riders were taking 250,000 trips. VanderZanden raised $15 million, then $100 million, then $300 million. Sequoia led. The valuation hit $1 billion nine months after launch — the fastest in startup history at the time. The story wrote itself: micro-mobility would replace car ownership, reduce emissions, remake cities. Investors saw Uber-scale returns. No one wanted to miss it.

The competition arrived instantly. Lime launched weeks after Bird. Skip, Spin, Jump, and dozens of others followed. Each needed to blanket cities before rivals did. Each raised huge rounds. The playbook was identical: flood streets with scooters, subsidize rides, capture territory, worry about profits later. Cities initially banned them, then created permit systems. Getting a permit meant proving you could operate at scale. Operating at scale meant burning capital. The incentives pushed everyone toward the same unsustainable expansion.

The unit economics were broken from the start, though few said so publicly. A scooter cost $300-400. It lasted maybe three months before vandalism, weather, or wear destroyed it. Revenue per scooter was perhaps $100 per month in good markets, less in others. Maintenance, charging, rebalancing, customer support — the costs accumulated. The gig workers who charged scooters overnight earned $5-20 per device. They hunted scooters like bounty. Some hoarded them, some damaged competitors' units. The operational chaos was immense.

Bird kept raising. By 2018, the company had raised $415 million at a $2 billion valuation. The narrative was familiar: they were first, biggest, best-known. They'd achieve density that competitors couldn't match. Density would drive down per-unit costs. Scale would bring leverage with cities, manufacturers, chargers. The economics would flip. Investors who'd seen Amazon lose money for years before dominating retail believed the pattern would repeat.

It didn't. The fundamentals never improved. Scooters remained expensive and fragile. Riders treated them roughly because they owned nothing. Vandalism was constant — scooters thrown in rivers, lit on fire, hoarded in garages. Bird introduced sturdier models, but durability added cost and weight, making scooters less pleasant to ride. The company tried owning its supply chain, then outsourcing it, then partnering with manufacturers. Nothing solved the core problem: the thing being rented wore out faster than it generated profit.

The pandemic nearly killed them. Cities emptied. Scooter trips collapsed. Bird furloughed 30% of staff, then laid off hundreds more. VanderZanden took the company public via SPAC in late 2021 at a $2.3 billion valuation — down from the $3 billion private peak but still remarkable given the losses. The SPAC provided $428 million in capital. The pitch was the same: scale would work. The post-pandemic world would embrace micro-mobility. Profits were coming.

The stock immediately collapsed. By mid-2022, Bird traded below $1. The company was burning $20-30 million per quarter with no path to break-even. Competitors were dying too — Skip sold to Lime, Jump shut down, dozens of smaller players disappeared. Lime survived by raising another billion dollars and finding, somehow, a path to positive cash flow in some markets. Bird couldn't. The company withdrew from dozens of cities, sold off scooters, cut staff again.

In December 2023, Bird filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. The company that nine months after launch was worth $1 billion was now worth essentially nothing. The bankruptcy plan involved selling assets, stiffing creditors, and maybe continuing some operations under new ownership, though the details remained unclear. Shareholders were wiped out. VanderZanden was gone. The scooters, where they remained, looked like artifacts from a brief collective delusion.

The story of Bird is the story of mistaking adoption for viability. Millions of people rode the scooters. The product worked — it moved humans from point A to point B pleasantly enough. But the business never worked. The romantic vision — scooters as climate solution, as liberation from car dependency — collided with the arithmetic. A thing that costs $400 and breaks in three months while earning $300 total cannot be the foundation of a billion-dollar company, no matter how fast you scale it. The path from idea to failure was paved with capital that believed scale would solve physics.
