# Ola

***The Uber of India that couldn't outrun Uber***

> **Years:** 2010–2024

In 2010, Bhavish Aggarwal was a 25-year-old computer science graduate working at Microsoft Research in Bangalore. One weekend, he hired a cab for an out-of-town trip. Halfway through, the driver stopped to pick up friends without asking. When Aggarwal objected, the driver abandoned him on the highway. The humiliation of standing roadside, trying to flag down another ride, planted a question: why was this so broken?

India's taxi market was fragmented chaos. Auto-rickshaws negotiated fares. Black-and-yellow taxis were scarce and unreliable. Call-a-cab services existed but relied on phone dispatchers, limited fleets, and no accountability. Aggarwal saw the gap between what existed and what could exist. He recruited his college friend Ankit Bhati, also an engineer, and they quit their jobs. They started with a simple idea: aggregate taxi operators onto a single platform. No fleet ownership. Just software connecting riders and drivers.

They called it Ola Cabs. The name came from the Spanish greeting—friendly, easy to remember. They launched in Mumbai in December 2010 with just six cars. Aggarwal personally answered customer calls. Bhati debugged code. The first month, they did 20 rides. The insight wasn't technology—it was arbitrage. India had thousands of small taxi operators with idle cars. Ola would connect supply to demand, take a commission, and let drivers keep earnings. Unlike Western ride-hailing, which disrupted traditional taxis, Ola partnered with them.

Growth was methodical. They expanded city by city: Bangalore, Delhi, Pune. They offered drivers upfront guarantees to join, absorbing risk to build supply. They localized aggressively—cash payments, Hindi language support, low-end phone compatibility. By 2013, they had thousands of cars and millions in funding from Tiger Global and Matrix Partners. India's smartphone boom was accelerating. Middle-class Indians wanted convenience. Ola was positioned to own the market.

Then Uber arrived.

Uber entered India in August 2013, launching in Bangalore. It had global scale, deeper pockets, and a battle-tested playbook. Uber's approach was blunt: subsidize rides until you dominate. Ola responded with equal aggression. Both companies burned cash to win drivers and riders. Ride prices collapsed. Drivers played the platforms against each other, collecting incentives from both. The subsidy war turned ride-hailing into a loss-leader for market share.

Ola adapted faster than Uber expected. It launched Ola Auto, integrating auto-rickshaws—something Uber initially ignored. It added Ola Share for carpooling, which resonated in price-sensitive India. It went hyperlocal: partnerships with state governments, regional language apps, integrations with India's digital payment systems. While Uber felt foreign, Ola positioned itself as the Indian champion. Investors believed the playbook: the local player wins in emerging markets. SoftBank poured in $2 billion. Ola's valuation hit $7 billion.

But Uber didn't quit. It hired local talent, adopted Indian features, and outlasted Ola in the cash burn war. By 2017, both companies were hemorrhaging money. Uber was losing a million dollars a day in India. Ola wasn't far behind. Drivers complained about declining earnings. Riders complained about surge pricing. Profits seemed impossible. The market was huge but unprofitable.

Ola pivoted to adjacencies. It launched Ola Electric in 2017 to build electric scooters and eventually cars, betting on India's EV transition. It expanded into international markets—Australia, New Zealand, the UK—attempting to prove it wasn't just a regional player. It added food delivery, financial services, logistics. The strategy was conglomerate diversification: if ride-hailing didn't work, something else might.

Nothing worked as well as the core business, and the core business didn't work. Ola's ride-hailing unit never turned a consistent profit. The food delivery arm folded. International expansion was expensive and slow. Ola Electric launched scooters in 2021 with major fanfare—India's first mass-market EV startup. Sales were strong initially, but reports surfaced of quality issues, poor service, and battery fires. Customers complained. The brand took hits.

Meanwhile, competition intensified. Uber merged with its Russian rival Yandex in Eastern Europe, freeing capital for India. Local competitors like Rapido undercut Ola on price. The subsidy war never truly ended—it just became permanent. Ride-hailing in India turned into a low-margin, high-volume grind. Ola's valuation began sliding. SoftBank, once the biggest believer, marked down its investment.

By 2022, Ola's ride-hailing market share was roughly even with Uber, but neither was profitable. Ola Electric became the company's main story. Aggarwal shifted focus, positioning Ola as an EV infrastructure play, not just a ride-hailing company. The company went public in 2024 through an IPO for Ola Electric at a valuation of $4 billion—a comedown from the $7 billion peak. The listing was turbulent. Share prices dropped after debut. Analysts questioned burn rates and quality control.

Ola still exists, but the arc bent from inevitable dominance to survival mode. It didn't fail—it endures. But it didn't win. The story reveals the trap of winner-take-all markets where both winners and losers bleed. It shows that local advantage isn't decisive when capital and willpower are infinite. And it demonstrates that pivoting to adjacent markets works best when the core is strong, not when it's bleeding out. Ola had first-mover advantage, local knowledge, and billions in funding. It still couldn't outrun a global competitor willing to lose money longer.
