# Ola

***The billion-dollar dream that couldn't outrun its burn rate***

> **Years:** 2010-2022

Bhavish Aggarwal and Ankit Bhati met at IIT Bombay, where engineering students dreamed of either Silicon Valley or India's growing tech sector. They chose India. In 2010, after a bad experience with an unreliable cab driver on a trip to Bangalore, Aggarwal saw the gap: India had no Uber, no reliable ride-hailing, just a fragmented mess of local taxi services and autorickshaws operating on negotiation and chaos.

They launched Ola Cabs in Mumbai in December 2010. The model was simple—aggregate existing taxi and auto-rickshaw drivers onto a platform, add GPS tracking, enable cashless payments, and guarantee reliability. India's middle class was growing. Smartphones were spreading. The timing felt right.

Early traction came fast. Within a year, Ola expanded to Bangalore, Delhi, Chennai. They raised seed funding from Rehan Yar Khan and angel investors who saw the Indian opportunity. The platform solved real pain: no more haggling, no more uncertainty about whether a cab would show up, no more safety concerns for women traveling alone at night.

Then in 2013, Uber entered India.

Aggarwal didn't retreat. He doubled down. Ola raised $19 million from Matrix Partners and Tiger Global. They expanded aggressively to smaller cities Uber ignored—Jaipur, Chandigarh, Indore. They understood local conditions in ways Uber didn't: cash payments dominated, drivers spoke regional languages, regulations varied wildly by state. Ola built for India's reality, not Silicon Valley's idealized version of it.

The battle was brutal. Both companies burned cash on driver incentives and rider subsidies. A ride that cost ₹100 might generate ₹20 of actual revenue after incentives. Drivers played both platforms against each other, collecting bonuses from Ola in the morning and Uber in the afternoon. Customers became addicted to artificially cheap rides, expecting ₹50 trips across town that cost ₹200 in a traditional cab.

But Ola had advantages. They secured investment from SoftBank's Masayoshi Son in 2014—$210 million that gave them firepower to match Uber's war chest. They added autorickshaws to the platform, something Uber couldn't easily replicate. They built Ola Money, a digital wallet, betting that owning the payment layer would create stickiness. By 2015, Ola claimed 80% market share in India.

Then the expansion began. Not just more Indian cities, but new verticals. Ola Shuttle for shared commutes. Ola Corporate for business travel. Ola Outstation for intercity trips. Each required different supply, different economics, different operations. The platform was becoming complex, the unit economics murky.

In 2017, Aggarwal announced Ola would build electric vehicles. Not just operate them—manufacture them. Ola Electric would produce scooters and eventually cars, vertically integrating from platform to vehicle. It was Elon Musk's playbook, applied to India's masses.

The logic was appealing: India was the world's largest two-wheeler market, pollution was choking cities, and government subsidies supported electric vehicles. If Ola could own the full stack—from vehicle manufacturing to ride-hailing to charging infrastructure—they could capture value at every layer.

Reality was harder. Building EVs required different expertise, different capital, different timelines. The first Ola Electric scooter launched in 2021, years behind schedule. Quality issues plagued early models—batteries caught fire, software glitched, service centers were overwhelmed. The ambition had outpaced execution.

Meanwhile, the core ride-hailing business was struggling. Uber had fought to a stalemate in Indian cities. Neither company made money. Both relied on continuous infusions of venture capital to sustain operations. COVID-19 in 2020 collapsed demand. Ola laid off 1,400 employees. Rides stopped. The cash burn became unsustainable.

Post-pandemic, demand recovered, but so did competition. Uber remained entrenched. Rapido, a bike-taxi startup, was cheaper and faster for short trips. Traditional autorickshaws returned. The moat Ola had built—brand, network effects, operational excellence—turned out to be shallower than believed.

By 2022, Ola's valuation had peaked at $7.3 billion, but the path to profitability remained unclear. The ride-hailing business lost money on every ride after accounting for driver incentives, customer discounts, and operational costs. Ola Electric was burning through capital building factories and managing recalls. The company had raised over $3.8 billion in total funding, making it one of India's most valuable startups—but also one of its most cash-hungry.

Aggarwal had bet that scale would solve the problem—that getting big enough would flip unit economics, that dominating India would create a fortress, that vertical integration would capture value others missed. But scale without profitability just meant losing more money faster. The dream of building India's first true consumer tech giant was consuming capital at a rate that made sustainability a distant hope.

Ola exists today, still private, still operating, still burning. It won the battle against Uber in India. It built the country's most recognized ride-hailing brand. It launched an electric vehicle business that sells tens of thousands of scooters per month. But it never found the path from dominance to profit, from ambition to sustainability. The billion-dollar valuation masked a simple truth: the business model worked for riders and drivers, subsidized by venture capital. Whether it could work for shareholders remained an open question.
