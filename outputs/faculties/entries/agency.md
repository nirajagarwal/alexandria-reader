# Agency

You experience yourself as an agent—someone who can choose, act, and make things happen. This isn't an illusion, but it's more fragile and more constructed than it feels.

The feeling of agency comes in two parts: the sense that you're initiating actions, and the sense that your actions cause outcomes. Daniel Wegner spent his career showing how these can dissociate. The experience of willing something doesn't prove you caused it—your conscious intention and your actual control can come apart. His experiments demonstrated that people claim authorship of actions they didn't cause when the timing and context create the right impression. You feel like you made something happen even when someone else triggered it. Conversely, you can genuinely control something while feeling no sense of agency over it.

The clinical cases make this more vivid. Patients with anarchic hand syndrome have a hand that acts—reaches for objects, buttons shirts, interferes with the other hand—but they don't experience it as theirs. The motor control is intact; the sense of agency is gone. One patient's left hand would light cigarettes while his right hand tried to put them out. The hand belongs to them anatomically but not experientially. On the other side, patients with delusions of control insist they're being moved by external forces—the CIA, aliens, demons. Their actions are genuinely theirs, but the subjective signature of agency has failed.

What these cases reveal is that agency is constructed post-hoc. Your brain generates actions before you're consciously aware of deciding to act—Benjamin Libet's famous finding showed readiness potentials preceding conscious intention by about half a second. This doesn't mean free will is an illusion; it means the conscious experience of deciding is part of how your brain integrates information about what it's already doing. The feeling of agency is real, but it's a model, not a direct readout. And models can be wrong.

Depression degrades agency in a specific way. Not the ability to act—many depressed people keep functioning—but the sense that acting makes a difference. Martin Seligman's learned helplessness research showed that repeated experiences of uncontrollable outcomes produce a state where you stop trying even when control becomes possible. The faculty isn't damaged; it's suppressed. The person has learned, incorrectly but powerfully, that agency doesn't work. Effective therapy often means reconstructing the connection between action and outcome through small, controllable experiences.

What stands against agency in modern life is less about control than about tractability. You face an overwhelming number of choices with unclear consequences. The gap between action and feedback grows—you can't see what your efforts produce. Bureaucratic systems, algorithmic feeds, long causal chains: these all erode the felt connection between what you do and what happens. The result isn't paralysis but a drift toward passivity, where you consume and react rather than initiate and shape.

The people who maintain strong agency do several things. They shrink the feedback loop—they work on problems where they can see results quickly, where the connection between effort and outcome is direct. Writers write daily and see pages accumulate. Craftspeople work with materials that respond immediately. Athletes get bodily feedback in real time. This isn't about achievement; it's about keeping the machinery of agency running. You need regular experiences of doing something and watching it work.

They also distinguish between process control and outcome control. You can control showing up and doing the work; you can't control whether the work succeeds in the world. Agency lives in the former, not the latter. Viktor Frankl noticed in the camps that people who maintained a sense of agency—who chose their attitude, who helped others, who set small tasks—survived better psychologically. The circumstances were as uncontrollable as circumstances get, but agency isn't about controlling circumstances. It's about controlling response.

Agency also requires costs. Real choice means real alternatives, which means real sacrifice. When you can keep all options open, you never exercise agency—you just sample. Søren Kierkegaard saw this clearly: the person who can't commit is fleeing the responsibility of agency. Deciding means foreclosing, which means accepting loss, which feels bad. But refusing to foreclose means you never actually do anything. The faculty requires you to burn your boats occasionally.

The collective dimension matters more than individualist cultures acknowledge. Agency is distributed—you act through institutions, teams, families, communities. The entrepreneur who says "I built this" is confusing narrative simplicity with causal reality. You have agency, but it's always embedded in systems of mutual enablement and constraint. Recognizing this doesn't diminish individual agency; it locates it accurately. You're not a ghost in a machine; you're a node in a network, and the network structure shapes what your agency can accomplish.

The faculty exists to make you an author of your life—not its sole author, and not its controlling author, but a genuine contributor to how things go. When it's working well, you experience your actions as yours, you see the connection between what you do and what happens, and you can adjust. When it's working badly, you feel like a passenger, a victim, or a ghost. The difference matters enormously. Cultivate experiences where you act and see results. Distinguish what you control from what you don't. Commit to things, accept the costs, and stop trying to keep all options open. The faculty is strong, but you have to use it. If you don't, it atrophies, and you become someone things happen to rather than someone who makes things happen.
