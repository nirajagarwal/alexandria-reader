# Attachment

You form bonds, and the pattern of how you formed the first ones shapes how you form all the others.

The foundational work is Mary Ainsworth's Strange Situation, developed in the 1970s. She observed one-year-olds with their mothers in a laboratory playroom: mother present, stranger enters, mother leaves, mother returns. The attachment pattern showed not in the separation but in the reunion. Secure children were distressed when mother left, but when she returned, they sought comfort, were soothed, and went back to playing. Anxious children clung and couldn't be consoled. Avoidant children ignored the return, seemingly indifferent. Disorganized children showed contradictory behaviors—approaching while looking away, freezing, falling to the floor. The patterns were stable across sessions and predictive across years.

This wasn't about how much the child loved the parent. It was about what the child had learned to expect. Secure attachment develops when a caregiver is consistently responsive—not perfect, just reliable enough that the child internalizes: when I'm distressed, help is available. Anxious attachment comes from inconsistency—sometimes responsive, sometimes not, creating hypervigilance. Avoidant attachment develops when seeking comfort is repeatedly ineffective or punished, so the child learns to suppress the signals. Disorganized attachment appears when the caregiver is frightening—the person who should be the solution to fear becomes its source, creating an irresolvable dilemma.

The critical insight: these patterns aren't fixed traits but learned strategies. The infant is solving a problem—how to maintain proximity to a caregiver—and the solution depends on what works. The patterns become templates, what researchers call "internal working models"—expectations about relationships that operate largely outside awareness. You don't remember forming them, but you carry them forward.

The longitudinal studies show persistence. Children classified as securely attached at one year are more likely to have better peer relationships at age ten, more intimate friendships at age sixteen, healthier romantic relationships at age twenty-five. Insecure attachment predicts higher rates of anxiety, depression, and personality disorders. But the predictions are probabilistic, not deterministic. Attachment patterns can change—through later relationships, through therapy, through earned security that comes from making sense of early experience. The pattern is influential but not fate.

The neuroscience adds detail. Secure attachment is associated with better regulation of the stress response system—lower baseline cortisol, faster return to baseline after stress. Disorganized attachment shows up in altered patterns of brain development, particularly in regions involved in emotion regulation and threat detection. The body encodes early relational experience. This is why attachment isn't just about feelings—it's about physiology, about how your nervous system learned to manage threat and safety.

Adult attachment research, pioneered by Cindy Hazan and Phillip Shaver, shows the patterns continue. People with secure attachment navigate conflict better, trust more easily, balance autonomy and intimacy. Anxious attachment shows up as preoccupation with relationships, fear of abandonment, reassurance-seeking that paradoxically pushes partners away. Avoidant attachment appears as discomfort with closeness, emphasis on self-reliance, withdrawal under stress. You can see your pattern in how you handle a partner's distress, in what happens when you feel vulnerable, in whether you move toward connection or away from it when things get difficult.

The damage from early insecure attachment isn't the absence of love—most insecure children are loved—but the absence of consistent safety. The child who learns that showing need leads to rejection develops a strategy that serves them in that environment but becomes maladaptive elsewhere. The hypervigilance that protected an anxiously attached child in an unpredictable household becomes exhausting anxiety in adult relationships. The self-reliance that an avoidant child developed to survive emotional unavailability becomes defensive isolation when someone tries to get close.

Modern life interferes. Secure attachment requires parental availability, but economic pressures make that harder. The research on daycare quality shows that what matters isn't institutional versus home care but consistency and responsiveness—children need stable caregivers who are reliably attuned, whether that's parents or paid care. Parental depression, stress, trauma—these interfere with the responsiveness that secure attachment requires. The conditions for healthy attachment are social, not just individual.

Therapy that works for attachment injuries—whether EMDR, emotionally focused therapy, or psychodynamic work—seems to operate by creating a new relational experience. The therapist provides the consistent responsiveness that was missing, creating what's called a "corrective emotional experience." Over time, with enough disconfirming evidence, the internal working model updates. Earned security is possible, though it requires sustained work and usually a relationship that's reliably different from what you learned to expect.

The faculty exists because humans are obligately social. Infants can't survive alone. Attachment is the system that keeps them close to caregivers who keep them alive. But it's more than survival—secure attachment is the foundation for exploration. Ainsworth saw this: securely attached children used the mother as a secure base, venturing further into the playroom because they trusted she'd be there if needed. The capacity for autonomy grows from the confidence that connection is available.

You arrived with attachment as equipment, but the calibration happened early, and it shapes everything that follows—how you form friendships, how you love, how you respond to your own children, whether you can ask for help, what you do when you're frightened. The pattern feels like identity, but it's learned. That means it can be learned differently, though learning differently requires understanding what you learned first. The system is working exactly as designed; the question is whether the design still serves the life you're trying to build.
