# Intention

You form intentions constantly—deciding to make coffee, choosing to call a friend, committing to a project—but most of what you do runs without them. Your heart beats, you shift your weight, you finish familiar sentences. The boundary between intended action and automatic behavior matters more than it seems, and it's less clear than you'd think.

Michael Gazzaniga's split-brain studies revealed something unsettling: people act first and explain afterward, constructing reasons that feel like they preceded the action but didn't. In one experiment, a patient's right hemisphere (which can't speak) saw an instruction to walk. He stood up. Asked why, his left hemisphere—which hadn't seen the instruction—immediately generated a plausible story: "I was going to get a Coke." The explanation came after the action, but it felt, to him, like the reason for it.

Benjamin Libet's experiments made this worse. He found that brain activity indicating a decision to move—the "readiness potential"—appeared about 350 milliseconds before subjects reported consciously intending to move. The action seemed to begin before the intention. This sparked decades of debate about free will, but the practical point is simpler: intention isn't always the source. Sometimes it's the narration.

The clinical cases show what happens when intention breaks. Patients with anarchic hand syndrome have a limb that acts independently—buttoning a shirt while the other hand unbuttons it, reaching for objects the person doesn't want. The actions are purposeful but not intended by the person. The motor system is intact; the connection to intention is severed. What you think of as "your" action turns out to require a specific faculty keeping your limbs aligned with your purposes.

Utilization behavior goes further. Some patients with frontal lobe damage can't resist acting on environmental cues. Put glasses in front of them, they put them on—even if they're already wearing glasses. Place a pen in their hand, they start writing. The environment triggers action directly, bypassing intention. François Lhermitte documented cases where patients would automatically perform complex sequences—making coffee, setting a table—triggered entirely by context. Intention is what stands between stimulus and response. Without it, you become a machine that completes patterns.

But intention isn't just control—it's also commitment across time. When you form an intention to run in the morning, you're trying to bind your future self to a decision made now. This often fails. The gap between forming an intention and acting on it is where most plans dissolve. Implementation intentions—Peter Gollwitzer's research—work better: "When X happens, I'll do Y." Specificity bridges the gap. Vague intentions ("I'll exercise more") don't constrain future action. Precise if-then plans do.

The obstacles are partly external. Environments full of cues pull you into unintended actions—picking up your phone, opening a browser, starting a snack. Each context is a field of affordances that trigger behavior. Your phone affords checking. The open bag affords eating. James Gibson's theory of affordances explains why changing your environment is more effective than trying to fortify your intentions. Removing the cue works better than resisting it.

But the deeper obstacle is how intentions decay. You form them when motivation is high, but they have to survive motivational troughs. Thomas Schelling called this "the intimate contest for self-command"—the morning self makes plans the evening self will have to execute, and the evening self has different preferences. Intentions are bets that your future self will cooperate. Commitment devices—pre-commitments that make defection costly—work because they don't rely on future intention. You give your friend your credit card, you sign up with money on the line, you remove the option. The faculty of intention is limited; engineering around its limits is practical.

The people who use intention well seem to understand that most behavior should be automatic. They don't rely on deciding every action in real time. Instead, they build routines—sequences that run without deliberation once triggered. Charles Duhigg's work on habits shows that cue-routine-reward loops, once established, bypass intention. You don't intend to brush your teeth; the bathroom triggers the sequence. Willpower is finite; automaticity is not. The goal isn't constant intentionality but strategic use—decide once, automate, save intention for what matters.

Writing down intentions matters. Implementation intentions only work if they're specified. David Allen's Getting Things Done system isn't mystical—it's offloading. An unwritten intention occupies working memory, draining the resource you need to form new intentions. Captured externally, intentions become more reliable. They stop being mental load and become retrieval cues.

Intention is what transforms impulse into action directed by purpose. It's the faculty that makes you an agent instead of a response machine. But it's expensive, limited, and often post-hoc. The mistake is thinking you need more of it. You need better use—automate what you can, engineer your environment, specify triggers, and reserve intention for choices that matter. The faculty exists to steer, not to manually operate every movement. Knowing the difference is how you stop exhausting it on trivia.
