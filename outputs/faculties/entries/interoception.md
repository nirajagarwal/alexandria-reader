# Interoception

You have a sense you don't think about because it's pointing inward. The conventional five senses face the world—vision, hearing, touch, taste, smell. But there's a sixth that monitors the state of your body: heart rate, breathing, hunger, thirst, temperature, muscle tension, gut signals, bladder fullness, pain. This is interoception, and you're using it constantly without noticing.

The faculty wasn't named until 1906, when Charles Sherrington distinguished it from exteroception (outward-facing senses) and proprioception (body position and movement). But the scientific investigation really began in the 1990s when Antonio Damasio started asking why his patients with ventromedial prefrontal cortex damage couldn't make decisions. They had intact reasoning, intact memory, normal IQ—but they'd deliberate endlessly over trivial choices. The answer: they'd lost access to somatic markers, bodily signals that flag options as good or bad. Decision-making, it turns out, isn't purely cognitive. You need the body's input.

The insula is the primary hub. This cortical region receives signals from internal organs and integrates them into conscious awareness. Damage here impairs the ability to sense your own heartbeat, to recognize when you're anxious, to know if you're about to vomit. Hugo Critchley's research showed that people vary widely in interoceptive accuracy—some can count their heartbeats precisely without touching their pulse, others are wildly off. This variation matters. Poor interoceptors struggle with emotion regulation because they can't detect the early physical signals of distress. By the time they notice something's wrong, they're already dysregulated.

The clinical cases are revealing. Patients with alexithymia—difficulty identifying and describing emotions—also show impaired interoception. They can't name what they feel because they can't sense the bodily component of feeling. Anxiety disorders often involve interoceptive amplification: normal bodily signals get misinterpreted as dangerous. A slightly elevated heart rate becomes "I'm having a heart attack." Panic attacks are interoceptive storms—the system floods awareness with alarm signals that trigger more alarm. The feedback loop spirals.

Anorexia provides another window. Researchers found that people with anorexia show altered insula activity and poor interoceptive awareness, particularly around hunger and fullness cues. They can't reliably sense when they need food. The disorder isn't purely about body image—there's a fundamental disconnect between brain and body signals. Effective treatment requires rebuilding this connection, not just correcting thoughts.

Modern life degrades the faculty in systematic ways. Chronic stress keeps you in sympathetic activation—elevated cortisol, shallow breathing, muscle tension—until these states become background noise you stop registering. Sedentary work means fewer demands on the cardiovascular and muscular systems, less variation in internal state, dimmer signals. Constant distraction—screens, notifications, task-switching—trains attention outward and away from the body. You can spend an entire day never checking in with what you actually need.

The people who use this faculty well have figured out something simple: you have to practice attending to internal signals, and you have to do it when they're quiet, not just when they're screaming. Contemplative traditions have known this for centuries. Meditation, particularly body-scan practices, is systematic interoceptive training. You're learning to detect subtle sensations—the quality of the breath, areas of tension, the felt sense of emotions arising. Athletes develop refined interoceptive awareness because performance depends on reading exertion, fatigue, and recovery accurately. They know the difference between pain that means injury and pain that means effort.

Bessel van der Kolk's work on trauma showed that traumatized individuals often dissociate from bodily sensation—the signals are too threatening. Healing requires carefully rebuilding interoceptive tolerance, learning to sense the body again without being overwhelmed. His approach uses yoga, somatic experiencing, and other body-based practices because you can't think your way back into your body. You have to sense your way back.

The faculty also grounds emotional life. Lisa Feldman Barrett's research challenges the idea that emotions are universal, hardwired programs. Instead, the brain is constantly predicting bodily needs and interpreting internal signals in context. Emotion is constructed from interoceptive data. When you feel anxious, that's your brain's interpretation of arousal patterns—elevated heart rate, shallow breathing, muscle tension. The same physiological pattern might be interpreted as excitement in a different context. Improving interoceptive awareness doesn't just help you sense your body; it helps you understand your emotions more accurately.

There's a practical test: can you tell when you need to eat before you're ravenous? When you need water before you're parched? When you're tired before you're exhausted? When tension is building before you're overwhelmed? These aren't minor conveniences. They're the difference between responding to needs appropriately and lurching between states of depletion and emergency compensation.

The faculty exists to keep you alive—regulating homeostasis, signaling threats, coordinating responses. But it also makes feeling possible. Without interoception, there's no emotional depth, no gut instinct, no sense of being a self that inhabits a body. The signal from inside is how you know you're here. You already have this equipment. The question is whether you're listening.
