# Reciprocity

You keep track of who did what for whom, and you do it automatically. You feel indebted when someone helps you. You feel cheated when your help isn't returned. You feel satisfaction when accounts balance. This isn't learned behavior—it's equipment you arrived with, and it runs deeper than fairness.

Robert Trivers worked out the evolutionary logic in 1971. Reciprocal altruism only evolves when creatures can recognize each other, remember interactions, and live long enough for favors to be returned. Humans have all three, and the faculty is sophisticated. You don't just track "John helped me"—you track the size of the help, the cost to John, your ability to repay, how recently it happened, and whether John's other relationships suggest he's a reliable reciprocator. You're running complex social accounting without thinking about it.

The universality matters. Every culture studied has reciprocity norms. The anthropologist Marcel Mauss documented gift-giving across societies and found the same three obligations: to give, to receive, and to repay. The specifics vary—what counts as timely repayment, whether exact equivalence is required—but the basic structure is constant. You're working with human equipment, not cultural invention.

The faculty generates powerful emotions. The feeling of indebtedness is aversive—people hate having unpaid obligations. Studies show subjects will pay more to discharge a debt than the favor was worth. The feeling of being cheated is even stronger. When people play ultimatum games and receive unfair offers, brain imaging shows activation in the anterior insula—the same region associated with disgust. You don't just dislike unfairness; you find it viscerally repulsive. The emotion pushes you to reject the offer even when accepting would leave you better off materially. The faculty cares about the relationship, not just the transaction.

The clinical cases reveal how fundamental this is. People with damage to the ventromedial prefrontal cortex can still reason about fairness in the abstract but no longer *feel* the wrongness of exploitation. They make purely calculating decisions in social exchanges, and other people sense something wrong. The emotional component isn't optional decoration—it's what makes reciprocity work. Without the feeling of obligation and the sting of betrayal, you'd defect too often and get frozen out.

Modern life attacks this faculty from multiple directions. Scale is the first problem. Reciprocity evolved for groups where you knew everyone and would interact repeatedly. In a city of millions, most interactions are anonymous and one-off. The equipment still fires—you feel the obligation to return a stranger's kindness—but there's often no mechanism for repayment, and the emotional ledger doesn't close. This creates a low-grade discomfort.

Market transactions corrupt the signal. When everything is monetized, the reciprocity machinery can't distinguish genuine favors from commercial exchanges. If a neighbor helps you move and you offer money, you've insulted them—you've reclassified a favor as a transaction. But if you don't reciprocate at all, you've violated the relationship. The faculty works on non-monetary exchanges; introducing money scrambles the accounting.

Digital interaction makes everything worse. You consume content, benefit from people's work, feel vaguely that you should reciprocate—but clicking "like" doesn't satisfy the machinery. The action is too cheap, too distant. Meanwhile, you can be helped by people you'll never meet, whose names you don't know, whose work compounds across years. The reciprocity equipment isn't calibrated for this. It generates ambient guilt with no outlet.

The people who use this faculty well understand it operates on relationships, not transactions. They track obligations and discharge them, but they also give freely when they can afford it, knowing some gifts won't be repaid by the recipient but will be returned by someone else later. They build networks where reciprocity circulates rather than balancing every ledger immediately. They know that being in someone's debt can strengthen a relationship if both parties trust the eventual balance. They understand that refusing help can be as damaging as refusing to help—you deny someone the satisfaction of repaying their obligation to the network.

They're also careful about what they accept. Taking a favor from someone untrustworthy creates an obligation you'll regret. Taking something you can't repay puts you in a weak position. The faculty binds you; you want to be thoughtful about what binds you. And they know to make their help visible—not for praise, but so the other person can feel the obligation and reciprocate. Hidden generosity prevents the relationship from forming.

The faculty exists because human survival depended on cooperation that wasn't kinship-based. You needed to help non-relatives and trust they'd help you later. This only works if everyone tracks obligations and feels the pull to settle them. The emotions—gratitude, indebtedness, resentment at cheating—are enforcement mechanisms. They make you a reliable reciprocator, which makes others willing to cooperate with you.

The faculty is social glue. It converts individual acts into relationships and relationships into communities. When it works well, you're embedded in a web of mutual obligation that supports you when you need it and gives you opportunities to contribute when you can. When it degrades—through anonymity, through monetization, through scale that prevents completion—you're left either calculating every exchange or feeling guilty about imbalances you can't resolve. The faculty isn't optional, and it doesn't turn off in modern conditions. You're running social accounting software designed for a different environment. Understanding that won't make the guilt disappear, but it helps you know what you're working with and where to direct the energy. Give where you can. Receive graciously. Reciprocate when possible. Build relationships where the accounting can close. The equipment works when the social architecture supports it.
