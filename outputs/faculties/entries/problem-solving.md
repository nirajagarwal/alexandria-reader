# Problem-Solving

You face problems constantly, and most of what you've been told about solving them is theater.

The standard advice—brainstorm, think outside the box, be creative—mistakes the performance of problem-solving for the actual work. Karl Duncker's candle problem from 1945 shows what really happens. Give people a candle, matches, and a box of tacks, and ask them to attach the candle to the wall so wax doesn't drip on the floor. Most people try to tack the candle directly or melt it to the wall. The solution: empty the box, tack it to the wall, use it as a platform. The box was holding tacks, but it's also a box. People miss this because of functional fixedness—objects get locked into their current role. The problem isn't lack of creativity. It's perceptual. You can't solve what you can't see.

This matters because problem-solving begins with representation, and most problems are represented badly the first time. The Gestalt psychologists understood this. Max Wertheimer studied how children solve the parallelogram problem—finding its area. If you teach the formula, they can apply it mechanically. But show them that you can restructure the parallelogram into a rectangle by moving one piece, and they understand. The representation changed. The problem became solvable not through more effort but through reseeing.

Herbert Simon and Allen Newell spent decades studying how people actually solve problems. They recorded chess players, cryptarithmetic solvers, theorem provers—anyone working through something difficult. What they found: problem-solving is search through a space of possibilities, and most people search badly. Experts don't search randomly. They recognize patterns that narrow the space immediately. A chess master looks at a position and sees not thirty possible moves but three worth considering. The skill is constraint—knowing what not to try.

The research on insight is revealing. When people solve problems suddenly—the aha moment—brain imaging shows activity in the anterior superior temporal gyrus, associated with making remote connections. But insight isn't magic. It happens when you stop searching the wrong space. The nine-dot problem: connect nine dots in a 3x3 grid with four straight lines without lifting your pen. People fail because they assume the lines must stay inside the boundary formed by the dots. Once you see you can go outside, it's trivial. The impasse wasn't computational. It was a constraint you imposed that wasn't in the problem.

Incubation works, but not the way people think. Step away from a problem, and sometimes the solution appears. Ap Dijksterhuis showed that unconscious thought can outperform conscious deliberation on complex problems with many variables. But this isn't mystical. Your unconscious isn't smarter—it's just different. It doesn't get stuck in the same ruts. Conscious thought fixates; stepping away lets the fixation dissolve. The utility isn't "stop thinking." It's "stop thinking *this way*."

The largest obstacle to problem-solving is the feeling that you understand the problem when you don't. People leap to solutions before they've examined the structure. Donald Schön studied professionals—architects, therapists, urban planners—and found that the good ones spend more time framing the problem than solving it. The problem space is where the work happens. If your representation is wrong, all your effort searches the wrong space.

Analogical reasoning helps when you can find it. Dedre Gentner's work shows that people can transfer solutions from one domain to another if they see the structural similarity. But they usually don't. The fortress problem and the tumor problem are structurally identical—both require converging weak forces to create a strong one—but people don't see it unless you tell them to look for an analogy. Your experience holds solutions, but they're latent. Making them available requires seeing through surface differences to underlying structure.

Problem-solving degrades under pressure, but not the way you'd expect. Yerkes-Dodson showed this a century ago: performance follows an inverted U with arousal. Too little, you don't engage. Too much, you narrow. Under high pressure, you revert to dominant responses—what's most practiced, not what's most appropriate. This is why people make obvious mistakes in emergencies. The faculty shuts down when it's needed most. The answer isn't "relax." It's practice the non-obvious responses until they become dominant.

Collaborative problem-solving has a strange property: it can be worse than individual work. Groups fixate collectively. The first solution suggested becomes an anchor, and subsequent discussion rarely escapes it. Brainstorming generates fewer ideas than the same number of people working alone and pooling results. The benefit of groups isn't more ideas—it's diversity of representation. A group of people who see the problem differently can break each other's fixations, but only if the structure allows it. Most meetings don't.

The people who solve problems well don't have a method. They have dispositions. They tolerate confusion longer. They generate multiple representations before committing. They ask what constraints they're assuming that aren't in the problem. They look for structural similarities to things they've solved before. They know when to push and when to step away. They're willing to be wrong about what the problem is.

The faculty exists to get you unstuck, but it needs the right starting point. If your representation is wrong, effort is waste. If you can't see past functional fixedness, you're trapped. If you leap to solutions, you solve the wrong problem. Problem-solving isn't cleverness—it's the discipline of seeing clearly before acting, the patience to search the right space, and the flexibility to rebuild your understanding when it's wrong. The equipment works when you stop performing problem-solving and start examining what you're actually looking at.
