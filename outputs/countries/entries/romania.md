# Romania
*Between Empires*

Romania occupies a peculiar position in European consciousness—recognizable but obscure, familiar but poorly understood. Wedged between the Balkans and Central Europe, shaped by Latin language and Orthodox faith, carved up by empires and reassembled repeatedly, it remains Europe's in-between country. Neither quite Western nor Eastern, neither fully developed nor struggling, it exists in a productive but uncomfortable liminal space. To understand Romania is to understand what happens when identity precedes statehood, when language survives conquest, when geography dictates vulnerability.

## The Land

The Carpathian Mountains arc through Romania's center like a horseshoe, dividing Transylvania from the surrounding plains and creating natural fortification that has shaped settlement patterns for millennia. The Danube forms much of the southern border before emptying into the Black Sea through Europe's second-largest delta—a vast wetland wilderness. Between mountains and river lie three historical regions: Transylvania, a highland plateau once ruled from Budapest; Wallachia, the southern plain oriented toward Constantinople; and Moldavia, the eastern hills caught between Ottoman and Russian ambitions. The mountains hold forests, minerals, and defensive positions. The plains grow grain. The climate swings between continental extremes—hot summers, bitter winters. Most Romanians live in cities now, but the countryside still defines the national imagination: villages, Orthodox churches with painted exteriors, shepherds in the high pastures.

## The History

Modern Romania is a 19th-century invention built on ancient foundations. The Romans conquered Dacia in 106 CE and left behind a Latinate language that survived centuries of Slavic, Magyar, and Turkish influence—a linguistic island in a Slavic sea. After Rome withdrew, waves of migrants swept through: Goths, Huns, Avars, Bulgars, Magyars. By the medieval period, three principalities had emerged: Wallachia and Moldavia under Ottoman suzerainty, Transylvania under Hungarian then Habsburg rule. Vlad III of Wallachia, the historical Dracula, became famous for his brutal resistance to Ottoman expansion in the 15th century.

The modern Romanian state began in 1859 when Wallachia and Moldavia united, gaining full independence from the Ottomans in 1878. After World War I, Romania expanded dramatically, absorbing Transylvania from defeated Austria-Hungary and creating Greater Romania. This triumph lasted barely two decades. In 1940, Nazi Germany and Soviet Russia forced Romania to cede territory to Hungary, Bulgaria, and the USSR—a dismemberment that still shapes national grievances.

Romania fought on the Axis side until 1944, then switched to the Allies as Soviet armies approached. The postwar communist regime, eventually led by Nicolae Ceaușescu, combined Stalinist economics with nationalist rhetoric and Byzantine personality cult. Ceaușescu demolished historic Bucharest neighborhoods to build his colossal Palace of the Parliament. His overthrow and execution in December 1989—the only violent end to a communist regime that year—was televised live. Since then, Romania has joined NATO and the EU while struggling with corruption and brain drain.

## The Culture

Romanian culture balances Latin language with Orthodox spirituality, Western aspirations with Balkan realities. The language connects Romanians to the Romance world—they can half-understand Italian or Spanish—while their religious calendar and social rhythms align with Orthodox Christianity. This duality runs deep: intellectuals historically studied in Paris, but peasants looked to monasteries for guidance.

Family ties remain strong, though rapidly urbanizing. Gender roles are conservative by Western European standards, though women have always worked. Hospitality is serious business—refusing offered food or drink causes genuine offense. There's a fatalistic humor about corruption and dysfunction, expressed in acidic jokes and resigned shrugs. Trust in institutions is low; trust in personal networks is everything.

The cultural pantheon includes sculptor Constantin Brâncuși, whose minimalist forms influenced modern art; playwright Eugène Ionesco, pioneer of the Theater of the Absurd (though he wrote in French); philosopher Emil Cioran, whose aphoristic pessimism made him a cult figure; and filmmaker Cristian Mungiu, whose work exemplifies the Romanian New Wave cinema. Folk traditions survive in rural areas—intricate wood carvings, painted monasteries in Bucovina, the ritual dances that vary by region. Dracula tourism is lucrative and slightly embarrassing; Bram Stoker never visited Romania.

There's persistent tension between Transylvania and the Old Kingdom (Wallachia and Moldavia). Transylvanians consider themselves more orderly, less Balkan, closer to Central Europe. Significant Hungarian and Roma minorities complicate the nationalist narrative. The Roma, in particular, face severe discrimination despite official denials.

## The Challenge

Corruption and state capture threaten Romania's European future. Despite EU membership since 2007, powerful networks of politicians, judges, prosecutors, and businesspeople systematically loot public resources. Infrastructure projects stall for years. Healthcare is underfunded and riddled with informal payments. A massive anti-corruption drive prosecuted thousands, including ministers and mayors, before being partially dismantled by political pressure. The judiciary wavers between independence and capture.

The result is hemorrhage. Romania has lost over three million citizens since 1989—doctors, nurses, engineers, skilled workers—mostly to Western Europe. Young people grow up planning to leave. Villages empty out. The brain drain is existential: what happens when a nation's most ambitious and educated systematically abandon it?

Meanwhile, democratic institutions weaken. Political parties are vehicles for patronage rather than ideology. Parliamentary debates are theatrical. Media ownership concentrates in opaque hands. Disinformation spreads easily in a landscape of low institutional trust. The country is wealthy enough to avoid crisis but too dysfunctional to properly develop. It's trapped in the middle income, middle corruption equilibrium.

## The Contribution

Romania's deepest gift is linguistic survival—proof that identity can outlast conquest. The Romanian language, a Romance outlier surrounded by Slavic and Hungarian neighbors, survived a millennium of foreign rule through sheer tenacious use. This matters beyond linguistics: it demonstrates how culture persists through political catastrophe.

Brâncuși's sculptures—"Bird in Space," "The Kiss," "Endless Column"—reduced form to essence and influenced a century of modern art. Ionesco's absurdist plays captured the horror of totalitarianism and bureaucratic dehumanization before they became academic topics. Cioran's philosophical pessimism, darker even than Nietzsche's, articulated an Eastern European experience of history as nightmare. The Romanian New Wave cinema—long takes, moral ambiguity, deadpan humor—has won international acclaim for its unflinching examination of post-communist reality.

Romanian mathematicians and engineers contribute disproportionately to global research, though usually from Western universities. The Danube Delta is a UNESCO biosphere reserve, critical habitat for millions of migratory birds. And the monasteries of Bucovina, with their exterior frescoes depicting biblical scenes in brilliant blues and reds, represent a unique fusion of Byzantine and local artistic traditions.

## A Moment

On December 21, 1989, Nicolae Ceaușescu stood on the balcony of the Communist Party headquarters in Bucharest, preparing to give a speech to a carefully orchestrated crowd. For decades, such rallies had proceeded according to script—thunderous applause, rhythmic chanting, the choreography of totalitarian theater. But something broke. You can watch it on video: Ceaușescu mid-sentence, confusion crossing his face as the crowd's mood shifts. Booing begins. Then shouting. The dictator raises his hand for silence—it doesn't come. His wife Elena tugs his sleeve. They retreat inside as the square erupts in chaos.

Within three days, the regime collapsed. The army switched sides. Ceaușescu and Elena fled Bucharest by helicopter but were captured, subjected to a hasty trial by a military tribunal, and executed by firing squad on Christmas Day. Their bodies were shown on television—an abrupt, violent punctuation mark ending 42 years of communist rule.

That moment on the balcony—when the performance collapsed, when fear suddenly evaporated—captures something essential about Romania. The country had endured centuries of external domination and internal tyranny through stubborn endurance, through doubling, through maintaining private truths beneath public performances. But when the performance broke, it broke completely. The revolution was messy, violent, incomplete. Old elites rebranded themselves as democrats. But the spell was shattered. No one could command that obedience again. The moment revealed both Romania's talent for sudden rupture and its difficulty building stable alternatives.
