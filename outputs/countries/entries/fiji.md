# Fiji
*Pacific Paradise, Melanesian Reality*

Three hundred and thirty islands scattered across a million square kilometers of Pacific blue—only a hundred inhabited, most too small or steep for settlement. This is the image: palm-fringed beaches, turquoise lagoons, coral reefs. The tourism poster is real enough. But beneath it lies something more complex: a Melanesian archipelago wrestling with the legacies of colonial indenture, military coups, and the fundamental question of who belongs.

## The Land

Volcanic peaks draped in rainforest, surrounded by coral reefs and shallow seas. The two main islands—Viti Levu and Vanua Levu—hold most of the population. The interior is rugged and green; the coasts are where people live. Cyclones batter the islands regularly, some with devastating force. The reefs protect the shores but are themselves vulnerable to warming seas and bleaching. Fresh water flows abundantly. The soil is fertile where it's flat enough to farm—mainly sugarcane country now, though the mountains still grow taro and cassava in traditional gardens. Climate is tropical but tempered by ocean breezes. The smaller islands are often too remote, too dry, or too exposed for permanent settlement.

## The History

Melanesian settlement three and a half thousand years ago, arriving in outrigger canoes from the west. Distinctive pottery, fortified villages, a culture of seafaring and warfare. European contact in 1643—Tasman sailed through but didn't land. Tongans raided and settled parts of the islands in the 18th century, bringing Polynesian influence. Cannibalism was practiced; missionaries who arrived in the 1830s were sometimes eaten. Ratu Seru Cakobau, a powerful chief, converted to Christianity in 1854 and eventually ceded the islands to Britain in 1874, fearing American annexation and unable to repay debts to white traders.

The British decision that followed shaped everything: bring in indentured laborers from India to work sugar plantations rather than disrupt indigenous life. Between 1879 and 1916, sixty thousand Indians arrived under brutal contracts—five years of labor, then choice to return or stay. Most stayed. By independence in 1970, Indo-Fijians nearly equaled indigenous Fijians in number. The constitution tried to balance communal rights, but the tension was baked in: a country divided between indigenous owners of the land and descendants of laborers who'd made it productive.

Four military coups since 1987—all justified as protecting indigenous interests when Indo-Fijian political power seemed ascendant. Democracy restored and interrupted, restored and interrupted. The pattern held until 2006 when Frank Bainimarama, himself indigenous Fijian, seized power and attempted to move beyond ethnic politics. His authoritarian rule lasted until 2022, when he finally lost an election he allowed to be fair.

## The Culture

Two parallel worlds that sometimes meet, often don't. Indigenous Fijian society organized around chiefs, villages, clans—*vanua*, the connection between people and ancestral land, remains central. Christianity absorbed rather than replaced older cosmology; Methodist and Catholic churches anchor village life. Kava ceremonies, elaborate gift exchanges, communal obligation. Gender roles traditionally rigid, though changing in towns.

Indo-Fijian culture maintained Hindu and Muslim practices through generations, built temples and mosques, kept festivals and languages alive. Hindi mixed with Fijian words creates a distinct dialect. But land couldn't be owned—only leased from indigenous Fijians—which meant permanent insecurity. Many left after the coups: Australia, New Zealand, Canada. Those who remained adapted, yet the two communities largely live separately, intermarry rarely, attend different schools.

English is the bridge language, but it doesn't erase the division. In towns and tourism zones, mixing happens more easily. Younger generations are less rigid. Still, politics follows ethnic lines with depressing regularity. The Indo-Fijian population has declined to 38 percent through emigration. Rotumans, Banabans, Chinese, Europeans add smaller pieces to the puzzle.

## The Challenge

The question never resolved: what does citizenship mean when land rights are communal and ethnic? Indigenous Fijians hold 87 percent of land under inalienable communal ownership—a protection against dispossession, yes, but also a permanent marker of who is truly Fijian and who is not. Indo-Fijians farm leased land with no security of tenure. When leases expire, renewal is uncertain. Some indigenous landowners want the land back; some are willing to renew. The productivity of agriculture depends on this unresolved tension.

Climate change accelerates the crisis. Rising seas threaten coastal villages. Cyclones intensify. Some communities already relocate inland, but land is communal—where do climate refugees go if their clan's land is elsewhere? The economy depends heavily on tourism, itself vulnerable to global shocks and environmental degradation. Sugar production has declined for decades. Remittances from the diaspora matter enormously—another marker of instability.

Democracy returned in 2022, but the fundamental questions remain. Can a multicultural state function when one group holds permanent land rights and others are permanent guests? Can economic development happen without land tenure reform? Can climate adaptation occur within communal ownership structures?

## The Contribution

The very attempt to make multiculturalism work in a small Pacific nation offers something worth studying—both the failures and the moments of success. Rugby sevens excellence, gold medal at Rio 2016, a source of genuine national unity. The distinctive Fijian Hindi dialect, evolved over five generations in isolation from India. Environmental conservation efforts, marine protected areas, some of the Pacific's most intact reefs despite the pressures. Christian-indigenous syncretism that created something neither wholly Melanesian nor wholly missionary.

And the diaspora itself—tens of thousands of skilled Fijians, both indigenous and Indo-Fijian, contributing to Australia, New Zealand, the United States, bringing Pacific perspectives to global conversations. The literature of exile and belonging: poems, novels, academic work examining what it means to be from a place where belonging is contested.

## A Moment

May 14, 1987. Lieutenant Colonel Sitiveni Rabuka walks into Parliament with ten masked soldiers. The month-old government—coalition led by an Indo-Fijian, Timoci Bavadra—is in session. Rabuka orders everyone out at gunpoint. Bavadra and his ministers are arrested. The coup takes eleven minutes. Rabuka declares he's protecting indigenous Fijian interests against Indian domination.

Governor-General Ratu Sir Penaia Ganilau—traditional chief, decorated war veteran, symbol of indigenous authority—tries to negotiate a restoration. Rabuka agrees, then changes his mind. A second coup in September. The constitution is abrogated. Fiji becomes a republic, leaving the Commonwealth. Ganilau, the embodiment of chiefly dignity, weeps publicly at what has been done.

Thousands of Indo-Fijians flee. Brain drain, capital flight, sugar industry decline. The pattern is set for three more coups over the next two decades. The eleven minutes in Parliament reveal the fragility of democratic multiculturalism when guns and ethnic identity align. Also this: Rabuka eventually apologized, admitted he'd been wrong, worked toward reconciliation. Redemption is possible. But the eleven minutes happened, and what they broke has never fully healed.
