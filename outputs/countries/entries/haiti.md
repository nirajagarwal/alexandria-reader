# Haiti
*The First Black Republic*

The world's first successful slave revolt produced the world's second independent nation in the Americas—and the world has punished Haiti for it ever since. This half of an island in the Caribbean became, in 1804, something that terrified every colonial power: proof that enslaved Africans could overthrow their masters, establish self-governance, and claim full humanity. The price of that revolution has been two centuries of isolation, intervention, exploitation, and catastrophe. Yet Haiti endures, its culture vibrant even as its institutions fracture, its people resilient even as the ground beneath them—literal and political—keeps shifting.

## The Land

Haiti occupies the western third of Hispaniola, sharing the island with the Dominican Republic. Mountains dominate—the Massif du Nord, Massif de la Hotte, Massif de la Selle—leaving only narrow coastal plains for agriculture. The land was once forested; now it is almost entirely denuded, the trees cut for charcoal in a country where most people lack electricity. Erosion follows deforestation, topsoil washing into the sea with each hurricane. The Caribbean washes three coasts, the water a mocking turquoise against brown hillsides. The capital, Port-au-Prince, sprawls across a bay, climbing the mountains in shanties that collapse when the earth shakes. The soil could be rich; the location could be paradise. But the land has been worked to exhaustion by people who have never owned enough of it to care for it properly.

## The History

The indigenous Taíno were largely exterminated after Columbus landed in 1492. France claimed the western part of the island in 1697, renaming it Saint-Domingue, and made it the world's richest colony through sugar and coffee plantations worked by half a million enslaved Africans under conditions of extraordinary brutality. In 1791, those enslaved people rose. The revolution that followed was complex—shifting alliances between enslaved people, free people of color, competing European powers—but its outcome was singular. By 1804, under Jean-Jacques Dessalines, Haiti declared independence, the first Black republic in history.

France demanded reparations for lost "property"—including the enslaved people who had freed themselves. In 1825, under threat of military invasion, Haiti agreed to pay 150 million francs, a debt that took over a century to discharge and crippled the nation's economy from birth. The United States, terrified of the example, didn't recognize Haiti until 1862.

The 19th century brought political instability—coups, assassinations, brief dictatorships. The 20th brought American occupation (1915-1934), the Duvalier dictatorships (1957-1986)—father François "Papa Doc" and son Jean-Claude "Baby Doc"—whose Tonton Macoutes terrorized the population. Democracy arrived briefly in 1990 with Jean-Bertrand Aristide, who was promptly overthrown in a coup, restored by American intervention, and later forced into exile amid accusations of corruption and violence. The 2010 earthquake killed over 200,000 people. The 2021 assassination of President Jovenel Moïse left a power vacuum that gangs have filled. The Haitian state, such as it was, has effectively collapsed.

## The Culture

Haitian culture is Creole—a fusion of African traditions, French influence, Taíno remnants, and its own revolutionary innovations. The language, Kreyòl, is distinct from French, though French remains the language of the educated elite, a linguistic divide that mirrors class divisions. Vodou, often sensationalized by outsiders, is the majority religion, blending West African spiritual practices with Catholic saints. It is not primitive superstition but a coherent cosmology, a way of relating to ancestors and spirits, of understanding suffering and seeking protection.

Haitian art is internationally recognized—bright, bold paintings that emerged from the Centre d'Art founded in 1944, now a distinctive tradition. Music matters deeply: rara bands during Carnival, konpa dance music, roots music that carries political messages. Literature has produced major figures—Jacques Roumain's *Masters of the Dew*, Dany Laferrière, Edwidge Danticat—who write Haiti's complexity against the world's simplifications.

The family structure is often matrifocal, women bearing enormous responsibility amid male emigration and violence. Haitians are proud, their revolution a source of dignity even as their present is a source of despair. The diaspora is vast—over two million Haitians live abroad, mostly in the United States, Dominican Republic, and Canada, sending remittances that keep the economy minimally functional.

## The Challenge

Haiti has become what political scientists call a "failed state"—the government controls almost nothing outside Port-au-Prince, and barely that. Gangs, some with over a thousand members, control most of the capital and major roads. They kidnap for ransom, extort businesses, rape strategically to terrorize neighborhoods. The police are outgunned and often complicit. There is no functioning judiciary. The economy, such as it is, runs on remittances and international aid.

Cholera, imported by UN peacekeepers after the 2010 earthquake, has become endemic. Malnutrition is widespread. The Dominican Republic, sharing the island, has built a wall and conducts mass deportations of Haitians, even those born on Dominican soil. Climate change brings stronger hurricanes to a country with no infrastructure to withstand them.

The deeper challenge is this: Haiti has never been allowed to succeed. The initial debt imposed by France, the repeated foreign interventions, the exploitation by its own elites, the international community's preference for aid projects over genuine development—all conspire to keep Haiti dependent and dysfunctional. And now the institutions that might stabilize it don't exist.

## The Contribution

Haiti's existence is its contribution. The slave revolt succeeded when every expert predicted it would fail. It proved that Black people could govern themselves, could form a nation, could claim the enlightenment values that Europe preached but selectively practiced. Every subsequent anti-colonial movement owes something to Haiti. Every Black republic traces its lineage, consciously or not, to 1804.

Culturally, Haiti gave the world Vodou as a sophisticated religious system, not the Hollywood caricature. It gave art that influenced the primitivism that shaped 20th-century modernism. It gave literature that explores the complexities of race, revolution, and postcolonial identity. The Haitian Revolution forced the question: What does freedom actually mean? If the enslaved can free themselves, what claims can any empire make to moral superiority?

Practically, Haitian emigrants have enriched every country that received them—doctors, nurses, artists, laborers, scholars. They carry Haiti with them, a portable dignity that no catastrophe has quite extinguished.

## A Moment

January 12, 2010, 4:53 PM. The earthquake lasted 35 seconds. The Presidential Palace collapsed. The National Assembly collapsed. Schools, hospitals, homes—everything built of concrete blocks and hope—came down. Bodies lay in the streets for days, the living too numerous and the dead too many. Mass graves were dug. The international community arrived with aid, with soldiers, with promises.

But the moment that captures Haiti isn't the earthquake—it's what happened next. Tent cities sprung up, informal economies restarted, women resumed selling mangoes and rice, musicians played, churches held services in the rubble. Haitians didn't wait for salvation. They began rebuilding immediately, with almost nothing, because they had always rebuilt with almost nothing.

And then the aid money—billions promised—disappeared into the gap between international organizations and Haitian reality. Temporary shelters became permanent. The rubble was never fully cleared. The reconstruction mostly didn't happen.

This is Haiti: catastrophe, resilience, abandonment, survival. The earthquake was natural. The failure to recover was political. The persistence in trying anyway—that's the spirit that overthrew slavery and has outlasted two centuries of punishment for doing so.
