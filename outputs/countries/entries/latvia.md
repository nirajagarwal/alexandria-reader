# Latvia
*Between Empires*

Latvia sits at the edge of the Baltic Sea, a small nation that has spent most of its history under someone else's rule. It earned independence twice in a single century—first from the collapsing Russian Empire in 1918, then from the collapsing Soviet Union in 1991. Both times it had to rebuild nearly everything: government, economy, national identity itself. The country's central question has always been survival—how does a small people maintain their language, their culture, their existence when caught between greater powers? The answer has required both stubbornness and adaptability, both fierce memory and pragmatic reinvention.

## The Land

Latvia is forests and coastline. Two-thirds of the country is covered in pine, birch, and spruce—the same northern woods that stretch across Scandinavia and Russia. The land is flat, carved by glaciers, webbed with rivers and lakes. The Gulf of Riga indents the Baltic coast, providing natural harbors that made this territory valuable to merchant empires. The Daugava River runs through the heart of the country, through Riga, the capital that has been contested by Germans, Swedes, Poles, and Russians for eight centuries. The climate is harsh—Baltic winters are long and dark, summers brief and bright. The land provides timber, amber, and peat. It does not provide much else. Location has been both curse and opportunity: Latvia sits on the trade routes between Russia and Western Europe, which brought wealth and also invasion.

## The History

Latvians are a Baltic people, distinct from both Slavs and Germans, speaking a language closer to Sanskrit than to Russian. For centuries they were peasants ruled by foreign elites—German knights and bishops who conquered the region in the thirteenth century and turned the native population into serfs. The Livonian Order built stone castles; the Hanseatic League built trading posts. Latvia was ruled by Germans, then Swedes, then Russians, but never by Latvians themselves until the twentieth century.

Independence came in 1918, amid the chaos of World War I and the Russian Revolution. For twenty-two years, Latvia was a functioning democracy, then an authoritarian state under Kārlis Ulmanis, then nothing at all. The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact of 1939 delivered the Baltic states to Stalin. Soviet occupation began in 1940, followed by Nazi occupation in 1941, followed by Soviet reoccupation in 1944. Between the deportations, the Holocaust, and the war, Latvia lost a third of its population. The Soviets remade what remained: collective farms, Russian-language schools, industrial cities filled with Russian workers. By 1989, ethnic Latvians were barely a majority in their own country.

The second independence came in 1991, during the final collapse of the USSR. Latvia joined NATO and the European Union in 2004, anchoring itself to the West. It adopted the euro in 2014. The question of how to be Latvia—distinct, sovereign, viable—remains urgent.

## The Culture

Latvian culture is defined by two things: the language and the forest. The language survived seven centuries of foreign rule because peasants spoke it to each other in the fields and woods where their overlords did not bother to listen. It is ancient, grammatically complex, rich in words for snow and trees and darkness. Preserving it became synonymous with preserving the nation. The Song and Dance Festival, held every five years, draws tens of thousands of singers—the entire culture gathering to affirm it still exists.

The relationship to nature is not romantic but deep. Latvians are a northern people: reserved, individualistic, stoic. They mark the solstices with ancient rituals older than Christianity. They forage for mushrooms and berries with knowledge passed down through generations. The forest is where you go to be alone, to think, to remember who you are beneath the layers of empire.

Society is still marked by the Soviet legacy. A quarter of the population is Russian-speaking, concentrated in eastern cities and in Riga. For years after independence, they were non-citizens—residents without voting rights until they passed Latvian language exams. The tension between ethnic Latvians and Russian speakers defines much of politics. Russian-language schools persist; bilingualism is common but contested. The divide is not just linguistic but existential: whether Latvia is a nation-state for Latvians or a multicultural democracy. The question remains unresolved.

## The Challenge

Latvia is emptying. Since independence, more than a quarter of the population has left—mostly young people seeking better wages in Western Europe. The country has one of the world's fastest-shrinking populations. Rural areas are dying; forests reclaim abandoned farmland. Riga holds together, but the periphery dissolves.

The economic problem is structural. Latvia joined the EU as a low-wage economy and remains one. Emigration drains talent faster than the economy can create opportunities. Corruption and inequality persist despite reforms. The Russian-speaking minority still feels excluded from full citizenship. And always, there is Russia itself—the neighbor that occupied Latvia for fifty years and still views the Baltic states with revanchist nostalgia. NATO membership provides security guarantees, but the fear of what Moscow might do if the West faltered is not theoretical. Latvia remembers 1940.

## The Contribution

Latvia gave the world the Latvian Riflemen—the elite units that fought for independence and then, tragically, became the Bolsheviks' praetorian guard in the Russian Civil War. It gave Mikhail Tal, the chess grandmaster known for sacrificial brilliance. It gave Mark Rothko, born in Dvinsk (now Daugavpils), whose abstract expressionism sought transcendence beyond language. 

It preserved a language that is a living link to Indo-European antiquity. Its Song Festival tradition—hundreds of choirs singing in a stadium—became a model for national cultural affirmation. The Singing Revolution of 1988-1991, when Estonians, Latvians, and Lithuanians sang their way to independence, was non-violent resistance at its most moving. Latvia proved that a small nation can survive empires through sheer stubbornness, through holding on to what makes it distinct until the empires finally collapse.

## A Moment

On August 23, 1989, two million people joined hands across Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania—a human chain stretching 675 kilometers from Tallinn to Vilnius. They were commemorating the fiftieth anniversary of the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, the agreement that had delivered them to Stalin. The Baltic Way, as it was called, was broadcast worldwide. Soviet authorities were stunned. How do you suppress a chain of people holding hands and singing?

In Latvia, the chain ran through forests and fields, through small towns and city streets. People stood in the August evening, linking hands with strangers, with neighbors, with the ghosts of the deported and the murdered. They sang folk songs in Latvian. They sang because singing was what had kept the language alive when speaking it openly meant punishment. Two years later, the Soviet Union would be gone. The chain was not a protest; it was a declaration: we are still here. We remember. We will not disappear.

It was the most Latvian thing imaginable—quiet, stubborn, impossible to ignore.
