# Panama
*The Bridge Between Worlds*

Panama exists because geography demanded it. A narrow isthmus connecting two continents, separating two oceans, it has spent its entire history being crossed, controlled, and fought over. The canal made it rich and dependent in equal measure. What remains, beneath the shipping lanes and banking towers, is the question of whether a country defined by being a passage can become a destination in its own right.

## The Land

Twenty-nine thousand square miles of tropical isthmus, at its narrowest point just fifty miles wide. The Caribbean to the north, the Pacific to the south, Colombia to the east, Costa Rica to the west. Mountain ranges run like a spine down the center—the Cordillera de Talamanca in the west, the Serranía de Tabasará in the east—dividing the country into distinct Caribbean and Pacific watersheds. Dense rainforest covers much of the interior, among the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth. The climate is tropical: hot, humid, with a pronounced dry season from January to April. Most of the population clusters around the canal zone and Panama City, leaving vast stretches of jungle virtually uninhabited. The land itself is the country's fortune and its fate.

## The History

Indigenous peoples—Kuna, Emberá, Ngäbe-Buglé—lived here for millennia before Spanish conquistador Vasco Núñez de Balboa crossed the isthmus in 1513 and became the first European to see the Pacific from the Americas. Spain immediately grasped the strategic importance: Panama became the crucial link in the empire's network, where Peruvian silver was hauled across land to be shipped to Europe. Pirates and disease made it a graveyard. After independence from Spain in 1821, Panama became a province of Gran Colombia, then Colombia itself.

The modern nation was born from an American imperial project. When Colombia refused to grant favorable terms for a canal, Theodore Roosevelt backed a Panamanian independence movement in 1903. The revolution succeeded in three days. The new country immediately granted the United States control of a ten-mile-wide Canal Zone "in perpetuity." The canal opened in 1914—an engineering marvel that cut global shipping routes by thousands of miles and made Panama synonymous with transit.

For most of the twentieth century, Panama existed in a state of semi-sovereignty. The canal zone was American territory; the economy revolved around American needs. Military dictatorships alternated with fragile democracies. General Omar Torrijos negotiated the 1977 treaties that would eventually return the canal to Panama, but died in a suspicious plane crash in 1981. Manuel Noriega, initially a CIA asset, became increasingly erratic and was removed by American invasion in 1989—twenty-six thousand troops, hundreds of civilian deaths, the presidential palace bombed.

The canal transferred to Panamanian control at midnight on December 31, 1999. Since then, the country has managed it competently, expanded it significantly, and built an economy around services, shipping, and banking—much of it serving interests that prefer Panama's discretion.

## The Culture

Spanish is the official language, but the country is more diverse than its Latin American neighbors suggest. Indigenous groups maintain distinct languages and territories, particularly the Kuna, whose semi-autonomous comarca on the Caribbean coast represents one of Latin America's most successful indigenous self-governance experiments. The capital has absorbed waves of immigration—Chinese, Indian, Lebanese, European—creating a cosmopolitan commercial culture. Afro-Panamanians, descended from canal workers and escaped slaves, constitute a significant minority, particularly in Colón and Bocas del Toro.

The culture balances between North and South American influences. Music draws from Colombian cumbia, Cuban son, and American jazz—Rubén Blades, the salsa legend and former presidential candidate, embodies this fusion. Pollera dresses at festivals preserve Spanish colonial traditions; reggaeton dominates the clubs. Roman Catholicism remains the dominant religion, mixed with Afro-Caribbean spiritual practices in some communities.

Family ties are strong; nepotism is endemic. A handful of families—the *rabiblanco* oligarchy, named for their alleged white-tipped tails—have dominated politics and business for generations. Social mobility exists, especially in banking and shipping, but the elite remains remarkably closed.

The national identity is fragile, still forming. What does it mean to be Panamanian beyond controlling the canal? The question hasn't been fully answered.

## The Challenge

Panama's prosperity is real but precarious. GDP per capita exceeds most of Latin America, driven by the canal, banking, and services. But inequality is staggering—gleaming towers in Panama City overlook shantytowns without clean water. Indigenous communities remain marginalized. Corruption is systematic: the Panama Papers scandal in 2016 revealed the country as a global hub for money laundering and tax evasion, though this surprised no one familiar with Panamanian banking.

The economy remains dangerously dependent on the canal. What happens when shipping patterns shift, when automation reduces revenue, when climate change alters trade routes? Diversification efforts—tourism, finance, logistics—exist, but none approaches the canal's centrality.

Political culture suffers from weak institutions and strong personalities. Democracy functions, elections are mostly clean, but power remains concentrated. Environmental destruction proceeds unchecked in pursuit of development. The Darién Gap—the roadless jungle border with Colombia—serves as a migration corridor and narcotics route, a zone of violence the state barely controls.

The country built around a canal must decide what else it wants to be.

## The Contribution

The canal itself stands as one of humanity's great engineering achievements—not Panama's creation, but now its responsibility, managed more efficiently than skeptics predicted. The 2016 expansion, adding wider locks to accommodate modern container ships, demonstrated technical competence and strategic vision.

Rubén Blades gave Latin music a social conscience, his songs addressing political corruption and working-class struggle with literary sophistication. Roberto Durán, "Manos de Piedra," became one of boxing's all-time greats. Ricardo Martinelli's entrepreneurial success—before his spectacular political corruption—showed what Panamanian capitalism could achieve.

The country's indigenous communities, particularly the Kuna, have preserved cultural autonomy against assimilationist pressures, offering a model of self-governance in territories officially recognized by the state. Their mola textiles, with geometric designs of startling complexity, are minor masterpieces of textile art.

Panama's biodiversity—a consequence of its unique position connecting continents—has made it invaluable for ecological research. The Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute, based on Barro Colorado Island in the canal, has produced foundational work in tropical ecology.

## A Moment

August 15, 1914. The SS *Ancon*, a cement boat, becomes the first ship to officially transit the Panama Canal. The journey from Atlantic to Pacific takes nine hours and forty minutes. Tens of thousands of workers died to make this passage possible—twenty-five thousand during the French attempt, five thousand during the American construction. Most were Caribbean laborers, their deaths unrecorded, their bodies unmourned.

The canal's chief engineer, George Washington Goethals, watches from shore. President Woodrow Wilson has chosen not to attend—World War I has just begun in Europe, and the canal's opening, planned as a global celebration, proceeds in relative silence. No grand ceremony, no triumphant speeches. The *Ancon* simply passes through, proving the thing works.

In the decades that follow, fourteen thousand ships will transit annually. Economies will reorganize around the shorter route. Panama will collect tolls while American governors administer the Zone as colonial territory. Not until 1964, when Panamanian students are shot for raising their flag in the Zone, will the relationship begin to change. Not until 1999 will Panama fully control what crosses its territory.

The *Ancon*'s passage was both beginning and symbol: Panama as the place where others achieve their purposes, the bridge that gets crossed, the service provider that enables global trade while struggling to define its own trajectory.
