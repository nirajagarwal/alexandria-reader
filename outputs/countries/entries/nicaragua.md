# Nicaragua
*Revolution's Hollow Promise*

Central America's largest country has lived the whole catastrophic arc of leftist revolutionary promise: from Somoza dictatorship to Sandinista liberation to Cold War proxy conflict to corrupt democracy to a new dictatorship wearing the old revolutionary mask. What happens when the liberators become oppressors? Nicaragua answers with bitter clarity.

## The Land

Nicaragua stretches from the Caribbean to the Pacific, dominated by two massive lakes—Managua and Nicaragua—that shape settlement and transport. The Pacific lowlands hold most of the population: Managua, León, Granada. The interior highlands are cooler, coffee-growing country. The Caribbean coast, isolated by mountains and swamp, remains culturally distinct—Afro-Caribbean, indigenous, English-speaking, neglected. Volcanoes line the Pacific edge, periodically devastating and continually enriching the soil. The land is fertile when it's not burning or flooding. Earthquakes have destroyed Managua repeatedly; the 1972 quake leveled the capital and catalyzed the revolution by exposing the Somoza regime's grotesque corruption. Geography has made Nicaragua perpetually interesting to empire—a potential canal route, a strategic isthmus—and perpetually vulnerable to foreign intervention.

## The History

Spain colonized Nicaragua in the 1520s, establishing rival cities: conservative Granada, liberal León. That liberal-conservative split would fuel civil wars throughout the nineteenth century. After independence in 1821, Nicaragua endured decades of chaos punctuated by William Walker's surreal 1856 invasion—an American filibuster who briefly made himself president, legalized slavery, and burned Granada before being expelled. The United States occupied Nicaragua from 1912 to 1933, fighting Augusto César Sandino's guerrillas. When the Marines left, they installed Anastasio Somoza García, whose family would rule as personal fiefdom until 1979. The Somozas were reliably anti-communist, reliably corrupt, and reliably brutal.

The Sandinista National Liberation Front, named for Sandino, overthrew the dynasty in 1979 after a popular uprising. The revolution promised social justice, literacy, land reform. It delivered some of that—Nicaragua's literacy rate rose dramatically—but also authoritarian tendencies, economic mismanagement, and a brutal civil war funded by Reagan's America. The Contras, counterrevolutionaries backed by the CIA, fought the Sandinistas through the 1980s, devastating the country. Elections in 1990 ended Sandinista rule. Democracy lasted two decades before Daniel Ortega, the old revolutionary, returned and consolidated power into a new dictatorship.

## The Culture

Nicaragua is mestizo, Catholic, and deeply marked by violence. Extended family networks provide the social safety net the state never has. Machismo runs strong, but women have historically played visible roles—in revolution, in literature, in daily economic survival. The culture carries traces of indigenous peoples largely erased by conquest and disease, and the Caribbean coast maintains Afro-Caribbean traditions separate from the Spanish-speaking mestizo majority.

Nicaraguans are notably warm and ironic, capable of profound generosity and profound cynicism in the same breath. Baseball, not soccer, is the national sport—a legacy of American occupation. The poetic tradition is outsized for such a small country: Rubén Darío modernized Spanish-language poetry, and Ernesto Cardenal fused liberation theology with verse. Revolutionary murals still decorate walls in Managua and León, though their meaning has curdled. There's a weary recognition that every promise—Somoza's stability, the Sandinistas' justice, democracy's prosperity—has been betrayed. Trust is scarce; survival strategies are refined.

## The Challenge

Daniel Ortega and his wife Rosario Murillo have constructed a family dictatorship that mocks the revolutionary ideals they once claimed. Since returning to power in 2007, Ortega has dismantled democratic institutions, imprisoned opponents, closed universities and newspapers, expelled the Catholic Church when it protested, and rigged elections so blatantly that international observers refuse to legitimize them. The 2018 uprising against his regime killed over three hundred protesters; the repression continues.

The economy depends on remittances from Nicaraguans who fled. Youth unemployment is crushing. Infrastructure crumbles. The same revolutionary rhetoric that once inspired liberation now justifies oppression. And there's no obvious path forward—Ortega has systematically destroyed civil society, imprisoned or exiled opposition leaders, and militarized loyalty through patronage networks. Nicaragua has lived through dictatorship, revolution, civil war, and democracy. Now it endures dictatorship again, this time wearing the mask of revolutionary legitimacy. The betrayal is complete.

## The Contribution

Rubén Darío gave the Spanish language its modernist revolution—lush, musical, cosmopolitan poetry that influenced every poet who followed. His work made Nicaragua synonymous with literary innovation in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

The Sandinista Revolution, whatever its failures, represented a genuine attempt at indigenous Latin American socialism, independent of Soviet models. It inspired leftists globally and terrified Washington enough to trigger the Iran-Contra scandal. The revolution's arc—from liberation to civil war to electoral defeat to authoritarian restoration—is a cautionary tale about revolutionary movements, but it was never cynical at the start.

Ernesto Cardenal, priest and poet, embodied liberation theology's fusion of Christian faith and social justice. His poetry made the political sacred and the sacred political. Even after the revolution's collapse, his work endures.

## A Moment

In Managua, the old cathedral stands roofless, shattered by the 1972 earthquake, never rebuilt. It's been fifty years. The regime has constructed new buildings—monuments to the Ortega-Murillo dynasty, garish and vulgar—but the cathedral remains a ruin in the city center, pigeons nesting in what was the nave.

Inside, you can see sky through the broken dome. The walls are cracked but standing. Homeless families shelter in the shadows. Tour guides bring visitors to marvel at the destruction, but no one explains why it was never repaired. There was Somoza's theft of reconstruction funds, then the revolution's other priorities, then the war, then poverty, then Ortega's return with different priorities.

The ruined cathedral is Nicaragua in stone: abandoned by those who claimed to lead it, enduring without repair, occupied by those trying to survive, open to the elements, beautiful in its brokenness, waiting for a restoration that never comes. The roof won't be replaced. Everyone knows this. But the walls stand, and people still slip inside seeking shelter, and the space that was meant for worship now serves as monument to every broken promise the country has received.
