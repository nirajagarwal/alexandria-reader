# Bhutan
*Happiness as National Policy*

A Buddhist kingdom in the Eastern Himalayas that measured its success not by GDP but by Gross National Happiness, preserved its culture through deliberate isolation, and then opened to the world on its own terms. Wedged between India and China, never colonized, it offers something rare: a working alternative to the assumptions of modern development.

## The Land

Mountains and valleys. Bhutan occupies the southern slopes of the Eastern Himalayas—no point lower than 5,000 feet, peaks above 24,000. Glacial rivers cut through dense subtropical forests in the south, rising through temperate zones into alpine meadows and permanent snow. The capital Thimphu sits at 8,000 feet. Most people live in the fertile central valleys where rice terraces climb the hillsides. Seventy percent of the country remains forested—protected by law and Buddhist reverence for nature. The land is steep, remote, roadless in places. It isolated Bhutan for centuries and still shapes daily life. No traffic lights in the entire country. Prayer flags flutter on every mountain pass.

## The History

The Drukpa school of Buddhism unified these valleys in the 17th century under Ngawang Namgyal, who built the fortress-monasteries (dzongs) that still dominate every district. For centuries, a dual system governed—temporal power held by kings, spiritual authority by the Je Khenpo, chief abbot. Tibet influenced culture, India controlled foreign policy, but Bhutan remained independent. The British never conquered it; they signed a treaty in 1910 acknowledging Bhutanese sovereignty while guiding its external relations.

The Wangchuck dynasty began in 1907. The third king opened the country to some modernization in the 1960s—roads, schools, postal service. The fourth king, Jigme Singye Wangchuck, invented Gross National Happiness in 1972 as an explicit rejection of GDP-driven development. His son, the Oxford-educated fifth king, surprised everyone in 2008 by converting the absolute monarchy to a constitutional one, though the king remains deeply revered. Democracy was imposed from above on a population that didn't request it.

## The Culture

Buddhism permeates everything—not as Sunday religion but as organizing principle. Monks in maroon robes walk the streets of Thimphu. Government offices contain altars. Laws protect sentient life (the death penalty is banned, and Buddhism frowns on killing animals, though people eat meat imported from India). The national dress—gho for men, kira for women—is required in government offices, schools, and religious sites. Archery is the national sport, accompanied by elaborate ritual and drinking.

The culture values restraint, hierarchy, community over individualism. Family matters; arranged marriages remain common, though love matches are increasingly accepted. Women have strong property rights by regional standards. Dzongkha is the official language but at least nineteen others exist in this country of 750,000—Sharchops in the east, Lhotshampas (ethnic Nepalis) in the south. Television arrived only in 1999. The government long restricted tourist numbers through high daily fees, protecting culture from mass tourism's erosive effects.

## The Challenge

The tension between preservation and modernity intensifies. Young people leave for cities or abroad—rural villages empty as farming loses appeal. The population skews young, educated, restless. What happens when a generation raised on smartphones and Western media no longer accepts the old arrangements? Unemployment among educated youth reaches twenty percent.

The treatment of ethnic Nepalis remains a stain. In the 1990s, policies meant to protect Bhutanese culture drove out over 100,000 Lhotshampas—now stateless refugees in Nepal. The government denies forced expulsion but the camps existed for decades. Most have resettled in the West. The wound remains.

Climate change threatens directly. Glaciers retreat, glacial lakes swell—the risk of catastrophic flooding grows. Agriculture depends on stable seasons. The tourism model requires pristine nature. Gross National Happiness measured happiness, but can the formula survive when the Himalayas themselves are changing?

## The Contribution

The idea that a nation might optimize for something other than growth. Gross National Happiness wasn't just rhetoric—Bhutan built it into policy through nine domains: psychological wellbeing, health, education, time use, cultural diversity, good governance, community vitality, ecological diversity, living standards. Every policy was screened through a GNH assessment. The approach influenced global discussions on alternative development models, on measuring what matters.

Bhutan demonstrated that a small country could engage with modernity while protecting its culture—not through isolation but through deliberate choices. The constitution requires 60 percent forest cover. Tobacco sales are banned. Plastic bags prohibited. The country is carbon negative, absorbing more than it emits. Whether these policies prove sustainable is uncertain, but they proved possible.

## A Moment

In the courtyard of Punakha Dzong, where two rivers meet, the annual Punakha Tsechu draws thousands. Dancers in silk brocade and carved wooden masks perform cham—ritual dances that have continued for centuries. Atsaras, masked clowns, cavort through the crowd, blessing people with wooden phalluses, making jokes, keeping the sacred from becoming solemn. Monks play long horns and drums. Children watch wide-eyed, learning stories of Guru Rinpoche, the 8th-century saint who brought Buddhism across the Himalayas.

An old woman spins a prayer wheel as she watches. Her grandson films on his smartphone, posting to Instagram. Both are reverent. Both are Bhutanese. The ancient fortress walls rise around them, whitewashed and red-trimmed, prayer flags strung from the roof. Peaks loom beyond. The dances tell of demons defeated, of wisdom preserved, of continuity across centuries. Whether that continuity can survive the forces now at work—democracy, globalization, climate change, modernity's restless momentum—no one knows. But for now, in this courtyard, the old ways remain vivid and actual. The kingdom that chose happiness over wealth still dances its sacred stories into the mountain air.
