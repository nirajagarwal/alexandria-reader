# Palestine
*The Unfinished Nation*

Palestine is not a conventional state—it is a people, a territory, an ongoing struggle for recognition, and a symbol of dispossession that resonates far beyond its borders. Divided between the West Bank and Gaza Strip, governed by rival authorities, occupied by Israel for over five decades, Palestine exists in a permanent state of incompletion. Yet Palestinians remain, asserting identity through memory, culture, and resistance against the erasure that has defined their modern history.

## The Land

Palestine occupies two non-contiguous territories: the West Bank, a roughly kidney-shaped area east of Israel proper, and the Gaza Strip, a narrow coastal sliver along the Mediterranean. The West Bank includes hills, olive groves, ancient cities like Hebron and Bethlehem, and the Jordan Valley. Gaza is flat, sandy, densely packed—one of the most crowded places on Earth. Both territories are small: together about 6,000 square kilometers, slightly larger than Delaware.

Jerusalem, claimed as capital by both Palestine and Israel, sits at the heart of the dispute—holy to Muslims, Jews, and Christians, divided by the 1949 armistice line, annexed by Israel in 1967, its eastern sector claimed by Palestinians as their future capital. The land itself is scarce in water, rich in symbolic weight.

## The History

Palestine's modern tragedy begins with the collapse of Ottoman rule in 1917 and the Balfour Declaration, in which Britain promised "a national home for the Jewish people" in a land where Arabs formed the overwhelming majority. Jewish immigration accelerated through the British Mandate. In 1947, the UN voted to partition Palestine into Jewish and Arab states. Civil war followed. In 1948, Israel declared independence; Arab armies intervened and lost. Some 700,000 Palestinians fled or were expelled—the *Nakba* (catastrophe) that remains the defining trauma.

Those who remained in Israel became a minority. Those in the West Bank fell under Jordanian rule; those in Gaza under Egyptian administration. The 1967 Six-Day War brought both territories under Israeli military occupation, where they remain. The Palestine Liberation Organization, founded in 1964, turned to armed struggle and international diplomacy. Two Intifadas—mass uprisings in 1987 and 2000—cost thousands of lives. The Oslo Accords of 1993 created the Palestinian Authority but delivered no state. Israel continued expanding settlements. In 2006, Hamas won elections in Gaza, then seized control in 2007, splitting Palestine between two rival governments. Blockade and periodic wars followed.

Peace talks have collapsed repeatedly. Israeli settlements now house over 700,000 people in the West Bank and East Jerusalem, making any contiguous Palestinian state nearly impossible. The "two-state solution" remains official international policy while slipping further from reality.

## The Culture

Palestinian identity is forged in dispossession. Refugees maintain keys to houses destroyed generations ago. The *keffiyeh* (checkered scarf) became a symbol of resistance. Dabke dancing, embroidered *thobe* dresses, and *tatreez* embroidery assert cultural continuity. Families trace lineage to specific villages, memorizing landscapes they may never see.

Islam predominates, but Palestine also holds significant Christian communities—among the world's oldest—now dwindling through emigration. Society remains traditionally conservative, structured around extended families and clans, though urban areas like Ramallah show Western influence. Women's participation in public life has increased, though patriarchal norms persist.

Literature and film obsess over exile and return: Mahmoud Darwish's poetry, Ghassan Kanafani's novels, the films of Elia Suleiman. Palestinian cuisine—*musakhan*, *maqluba*, olive oil—asserts rootedness. Universities in Nablus and Birzeit maintain intellectual life under occupation.

Gaza and the West Bank increasingly diverge: the former under Islamist Hamas, isolated and impoverished; the latter under the secular-nationalist Fatah, fragmented by settlements, dependent on international aid. Palestinian society splits between those living under occupation, those in refugee camps in neighboring countries, and a global diaspora.

## The Challenge

Palestine faces the challenge of non-existence in conventional terms. It has no sovereign control over borders, airspace, water resources, or movement. The Palestinian Authority governs fragmented enclaves in the West Bank, coordinating security with Israel but controlling little. Hamas rules Gaza as an isolated statelet, blockaded by Israel and Egypt, repeatedly devastated by war. Neither authority commands full legitimacy.

Israeli settlements fragment the West Bank into disconnected zones. Checkpoints restrict movement. House demolitions continue. East Jerusalem is slowly incorporated into Israeli Jerusalem. The occupation has lasted 57 years—most Palestinians have known nothing else.

Meanwhile, the international community pays lip service to Palestinian statehood while doing nothing to bring it about. Arab states have increasingly normalized relations with Israel, sidelining Palestine. Internal Palestinian divisions prevent unified strategy. The two-state solution appears dead, the one-state solution unthinkable to Israel, and permanent occupation the default reality.

Gaza's population endures repeated wars, unemployment near 50%, contaminated water, and collapsing infrastructure. The West Bank endures daily indignities of military rule. Young Palestinians see no future—leading many to emigrate, others to violence, most to despair.

## The Contribution

Palestine has given the world a vocabulary of dispossession and resistance. The Palestinian struggle became a symbol for anti-colonial movements globally, shaping liberation movements from South Africa to Latin America. Mahmoud Darwish is among the 20th century's greatest Arab poets—his verses on exile and identity transcend their immediate context. Edward Said, Palestinian-American intellectual, transformed postcolonial studies with *Orientalism*.

Palestinian embroidery, with its intricate regional patterns, preserves centuries of craft tradition. Palestinian cuisine influenced Levantine food culture. The Palestinian struggle forced international law to grapple with questions of occupation, settlement, and self-determination that remain unresolved.

Perhaps most significantly, Palestinians have sustained national identity through three generations of statelessness—a testament to the power of collective memory and cultural persistence in the face of erasure.

## A Moment

In 2018, the Great March of Return began in Gaza. Every Friday for months, thousands of Palestinians gathered at the fence separating Gaza from Israel, demanding the right to return to lands their families fled in 1948. They brought tents, flags, tires to burn for smoke cover. Israeli snipers watched from the other side.

The marches were mostly unarmed—families with children, young men with stones, protesters with slingshots. Israel treated the fence as an absolute border and opened fire on those who approached. Over nine months, more than 200 Palestinians were killed, thousands shot in the legs—medics documented a systematic targeting of knees to cause permanent disability.

The images went global: a young woman in a hijab shot while running away; a medic killed while treating the wounded; amputees returning to protest on crutches. The marches continued through tear gas and live fire, week after week. They achieved nothing tangible—the blockade remained, no refugees returned, the world watched and moved on.

But the Great March revealed something essential about Palestine: the insistence on presence, the refusal to disappear, the assertion of a right that cannot be exercised but will not be renounced. Week after week, they came to the fence, knowing they would be shot, returning anyway. It was defiance, desperation, and determination—the essence of a people who remain despite everything designed to make them leave.
