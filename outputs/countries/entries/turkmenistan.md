# Turkmenistan
*The Hermit Dictatorship*

Central Asia's most isolated state, ruled by one of the world's most eccentric personality cults. A land of ancient Silk Road cities and vast natural gas reserves, now sealed behind a regime that builds golden statues to itself while its people queue for bread. The question Turkmenistan poses is simple and terrible: What happens when resource wealth meets absolute power in a society with no tradition of resistance?

## The Land

Desert dominates—the Karakum covers eighty percent of the country, a furnace landscape of sand and scrub stretching from the Caspian Sea to the Amu Darya River. Temperatures reach fifty degrees Celsius in summer. Most of the five million population clusters in oases along the southern edge near the Iranian and Afghan borders, or in the capital Ashgabat. The Kopet Dag mountains form a natural wall with Iran. Underground lies the world's fourth-largest natural gas reserves—enough wealth to transform the nation, instead funding marble palaces and gold-plated monuments. The Caspian coast offers relief and fishing, but the inland sea is dying, its level dropping as rivers are diverted for cotton irrigation that never quite succeeds in making the desert bloom.

## The History

Turkmen tribes—Teke, Yomud, Ersary—lived as pastoral nomads for centuries, occasionally raiding Persia, loosely governed by Khiva and Bukhara. Russia conquered the region brutally in the 1880s; the Geok Tepe fortress massacre killed fifteen thousand. Soviet rule forcibly settled the nomads, collectivized livestock, and drew arbitrary borders in 1924 that created a Turkmen Soviet Socialist Republic. Moscow industrialized modestly—oil, cotton, carpets—and enforced Russian language and atheism while tolerating some cultural identity. When the USSR collapsed in 1991, Communist Party boss Saparmurat Niyazov found himself president of an independent state he hadn't sought.

Rather than reform, he built a dictatorship. Renaming himself Turkmenbashi—"Father of All Turkmen"—he created a personality cult that made Stalin look modest. His book *Ruhnama* (Book of the Soul) became mandatory reading in schools, mosques, and driving tests. He built a rotating golden statue of himself. He renamed months after himself and his mother. When he died in 2006, dentist Gurbanguly Berdimuhamedow took power, ruled for fifteen years with slightly less megalomania, then handed power to his son Serdar in 2022. The dynasty continues.

## The Culture

Turkmen identity centers on tribal affiliation, carpet-weaving traditions, and the Akhal-Teke horse—a slender, metallic-coated breed celebrated in national symbolism. Ninety percent are Muslim, mostly Sunni, but practice is constrained by state control. The traditional extended family (*taý*) remains central despite urbanization. Women wear colorful headscarves; men still value horsemanship. The Turkmenbashi-mandated *Ruhnama* attempted to create a synthetic national mythology mixing tribal history, folk wisdom, and dictator worship—it failed as literature but succeeded as control mechanism.

Russian remains widely spoken despite official promotion of Turkmen. The cities—especially Ashgabat, rebuilt as a showcase of white marble and gold—feel surreal, empty monuments to authoritarian vanity. Outside, life proceeds quietly. People joke carefully, trust no one, avoid politics. The culture is one of silence and endurance. Weddings remain elaborate, expensive affairs. Carpets are still woven by hand, though UNESCO-protected traditions now serve tourist propaganda more than living art. The Turkmen horse features on the state emblem, in parades, in presidential photo ops—national symbol as political prop.

## The Challenge

Total information control. Turkmenistan ranks among the world's most censored states—no independent media, internet heavily restricted, international calls monitored, foreign travel nearly impossible for ordinary citizens. The state denies COVID-19 ever reached the country, a lie so brazen it revealed the regime's essential madness. Natural gas wealth flows to the presidential family and marble construction projects while hospitals lack basic supplies and food shortages are routine. Malnutrition is rising. Young men are conscripted into a military that serves no purpose but regime protection.

The challenge is that there is no challenge—no opposition, no civil society, no public dissent. Those who speak out disappear into prison or psychiatric detention. The rare activist in exile has no domestic base. Unlike Uzbekistan, which has attempted modest reforms, Turkmenistan has doubled down on isolation and control. The gas runs out eventually. When it does, there's no alternative economy, no educated workforce, no institutions. Just monuments and silence.

## The Contribution

Turkmen carpets—intricate geometric patterns in deep reds and blacks—represent one of Central Asia's finest craft traditions, each tribal group with distinctive motifs. The Akhal-Teke horse, bred for desert warfare, is genuinely remarkable—graceful, heat-resistant, loyal. Ancient Merv, though now mostly ruins in the eastern desert, was once the world's largest city, a Silk Road hub where Persian poetry and Islamic scholarship flourished. Those ruins remind us that this land produced greatness once.

Modern Turkmenistan's contribution is darker but instructive: a case study in how natural resource wealth can enable rather than end authoritarianism, and how a society can be sealed so completely that change becomes unimaginable. If you want to understand what extreme isolation does to a nation, Turkmenistan provides the answer.

## A Moment

In 2010, President Berdimuhamedow appeared on state television demonstrating his skills as a disc jockey, rapping clumsily while his cabinet sat frozen in awkward applause. The performance was excruciating—the aging autocrat in sunglasses, bobbing to his own off-beat rhymes, while ministers who dared not laugh or leave clapped mechanically. The whole nation was forced to watch.

It was absurd, but it was also a demonstration of power. North Korea's Kims stage military parades; Turkmenistan's leaders stage exercises in humiliation. The message was clear: I can do anything, and you will witness it, and you will applaud. The DJ routine aired again and again on the single state channel. Outside, in Ashgabat's empty marble streets, someone was hungry. In the Karakum, dust blew over roads no one used. In exile, a handful of activists posted videos no one inside could see. The gold statue kept rotating, catching the desert sun, while below it a nation waited for nothing.
