# Kuwait
*Oil Wealth, Desert Memory*

A sliver of desert between empires, Kuwait built itself on pearl diving and trade before oil transformed it into one of the world's wealthiest nations. It is simultaneously ancient and modern, tribal and cosmopolitan, generous and restrictive. The money came fast—faster than any society could absorb without distortion. What remains is a small country of immense wealth, caught between the traditional structures that legitimize its ruling family and the modern expectations that wealth inevitably generates.

## The Land

Kuwait occupies 17,818 square kilometers at the head of the Persian Gulf, mostly flat desert punctuated by low hills and dry wadis. Summer temperatures regularly exceed 50°C; rainfall averages less than 140mm annually. There are no permanent rivers. Before oil, this was among the most inhospitable territory on earth—survival depended on fishing, pearl diving, and trade with the interior. The oil lies beneath the desert and offshore, discovered in 1938, commercially exploited after World War II. The land itself provides almost nothing except what can be extracted from beneath it. Even fresh water must be desalinated. Kuwait City, where ninety percent of Kuwaitis live, is an air-conditioned mirage built on petroleum revenue.

## The History

The Al Sabah family established Kuwait Town in 1613, creating a merchant port that thrived on the pearl trade and as a waystation between the Gulf and the desert interior. For two centuries Kuwait navigated between Ottoman and British imperial interests, maintaining autonomy through careful diplomacy. In 1899, Sheikh Mubarak signed a treaty making Kuwait a British protectorate—protecting it from Ottoman absorption while surrendering foreign policy control.

Oil production began in 1946, transforming everything. Kuwait gained full independence from Britain in 1961. Iraq immediately claimed the territory—a claim that would simmer for three decades before exploding into invasion in August 1990. Saddam Hussein's forces occupied Kuwait for seven months, looting systematically and setting oil wells ablaze. The American-led Gulf War liberated Kuwait in February 1991, but not before Iraqi forces had destroyed much of the country's infrastructure and killed thousands. The invasion remains the defining trauma of modern Kuwaiti consciousness—proof that wealth without powerful protectors is vulnerability.

## The Culture

Kuwait's population is roughly 4.6 million, but only 1.4 million are citizens. The majority are foreign workers—South Asians, Arabs, Filipinos—who build, clean, and staff the country but possess no political rights and limited social standing. Among citizens, tribal affiliations remain powerful despite urbanization and oil wealth. The Al Sabah family maintains political control through a combination of traditional legitimacy, patronage, and strategic concessions.

Islam shapes public life—though with less severity than in Saudi Arabia. Women gained the vote in 2005, drive cars, and work in professional fields, but face legal discrimination in marriage, divorce, and inheritance. The diwaniya—traditional male gatherings in private homes—remain central to political and social life, where discussion is free but formal political opposition is constrained. Kuwaiti citizens enjoy cradle-to-grave welfare, generous state employment, and subsidized everything. This creates a society simultaneously cosmopolitan and insular, wealthy and anxious, generous toward citizens and exploitative toward foreign labor.

The culture is one of generous hospitality layered over deep hierarchies—citizen and non-citizen, tribal and non-tribal, men and women, old families and new money.

## The Challenge

The challenge is what comes after oil. Kuwait's entire social contract—generous welfare, subsidized living, guaranteed employment for citizens—depends on petroleum revenue. The state owns the oil; the oil funds the state; the state buys political acquiescence. But oil is finite, and the demographic mathematics are unsustainable. A small citizen population sits atop a much larger workforce of non-citizens who have no path to citizenship and no political voice.

Economic diversification remains largely aspirational. Kuwait has one of the world's highest per capita incomes but produces almost nothing except oil. The bureaucracy is bloated, the private sector anemic. Young Kuwaitis expect state jobs; the state runs out of meaningful work to assign them. Meanwhile, climate change threatens a region already at the edge of human habitability. Kuwait must simultaneously diversify its economy, reform its labor system, expand political participation, and prepare for a hotter, drier future—all while maintaining the social consensus that has kept the Al Sabah in power for four centuries.

The fundamental question: Can a society built entirely on petroleum revenue transition to something sustainable before the oil runs out?

## The Contribution

Kuwait gave the Arab world its most vibrant early television industry—Kuwait TV in the 1960s and 70s produced programming watched across the region, from children's shows to political satire. The country pioneered Gulf cultural production before oil wealth made everyone competitors.

Kuwait also demonstrated that small, wealthy states can maintain independence through strategic alliance rather than military power. The 1990 invasion was a catastrophic failure of this strategy, but the rebuilding and the endurance of the Al Sabah showed another path: legitimacy through competence, survival through adaptation.

More quietly, Kuwait has preserved architectural and cultural memory even as modernization erased much of the physical past. The Kuwait National Museum, destroyed in the Iraqi invasion and later rebuilt, maintains one of the Gulf's most comprehensive collections of Islamic art. The Al Sadu House preserves traditional Bedouin weaving. These are modest gifts, but they represent something valuable—a refusal to let oil money completely erase what came before.

## A Moment

On the night of August 1, 1990, Iraqi tanks crossed the border. By morning, Saddam's forces controlled most of the country. The Emir fled to Saudi Arabia; resistance fighters went underground. For seven months, Kuwaitis lived under occupation—systematic looting, arbitrary arrests, torture, summary executions. Iraqi soldiers took everything: cars, hospital equipment, museum artifacts, even traffic lights. They set oil wells ablaze, creating an apocalyptic landscape of fire and smoke.

Liberation came in February 1991 with overwhelming American firepower. But the Kuwait that emerged was traumatized—physically destroyed, psychologically scarred, politically transformed. The invasion vindicated every anxiety about vulnerability, about depending on others for security, about the jealousy that wealth generates. It also created a permanent debt to the United States and a permanent fear of Iraq—both of which shape Kuwaiti politics to this day.

There's a photograph from March 1991: a Kuwaiti man stands before hundreds of burning oil wells, their black smoke blotting out the sun. The desert is dark at noon. He's holding a flag—defiant, exhausted, home. That image captures something essential about Kuwait: small, vulnerable, determined to survive on its own terms despite geography, despite neighbors, despite everything that should have destroyed it.
