# Tajikistan
*Mountains and Dependence*

The poorest of the former Soviet republics occupies the Pamir and Alay mountains where Central Asia meets the peaks of Afghanistan, China, and Kyrgyzstan. Ninety-three percent of Tajikistan is mountainous. The country's identity rests on being Persian-speaking in a Turkic region, but its modern reality is defined by remittances, Russian military bases, and a civil war that never fully ended. Independence came unwanted, and the state that emerged has struggled to justify itself beyond the grip of one family.

## The Land

Tajikistan is vertical. The Pamir Mountains—the "Roof of the World"—dominate the east, where peaks exceed 7,000 meters and the landscape resembles Tibet more than the steppes. The Fergana Valley in the northwest is the only significant lowland, an agricultural pocket cut off by Uzbekistan's arbitrary borders. Rivers cascade from glaciers through deep gorges toward the Amu Darya. The climate swings from continental to alpine to desert depending on elevation. Most people cluster in the western valleys, leaving vast mountain spaces nearly empty. Earthquakes are frequent. The land is spectacular and unforgiving—it isolates villages, complicates governance, and has made Tajikistan poor despite its water wealth.

## The History

Tajiks are descendants of the sedentary Persian-speaking peoples who inhabited Transoxiana and Bactria for millennia—Sogdians, Bactrians, and others absorbed into the Islamic world after the Arab conquest. They built cities like Samarkand and Bukhara, but these urban centers fell to Uzbekistan when Stalin drew Central Asia's borders in the 1920s. Soviet Tajikistan was an afterthought, the poorest republic, its cultural heartlands elsewhere. When the USSR collapsed in 1991, Tajikistan was unprepared. Civil war erupted almost immediately—secularist government against Islamic and regional opposition. The conflict killed 50,000 to 100,000 and displaced a million. Russia brokered peace in 1997, installing Emomali Rahmon, a collective farm chairman from Kulob, who has ruled ever since, transforming presidency into dynasty. His son now runs customs and the anti-corruption agency.

## The Culture

Tajiks define themselves as Persian-speaking in a Turkic sea. The Tajik language is essentially a dialect of Dari/Persian, and the culture looks toward Iranian civilization—Rudaki, Ferdowsi, the poetry that predates Turkic arrival. But Soviet rule severed these connections, imposing Cyrillic and linking Tajikistan to Moscow, not Tehran. Islam, mostly Sunni Hanafi, shapes daily life more than state ideology, though the government suppresses political Islam after the civil war. Pamiris in the east follow Ismaili Islam and speak distinct languages—they're Tajik citizens but culturally separate. Family and regional loyalty trump national identity. Men leave for Russia seeking construction work; women and elderly tend villages. Traditional gender roles persist despite Soviet-era education. The culture is mountain culture—hospitality, honor codes, isolation, deep suspicion of outsiders and central authority.

## The Challenge

Tajikistan survives on remittances. Forty to fifty percent of GDP comes from Tajik men working in Russia, mostly construction and manual labor. When Russia's economy contracts, Tajikistan starves. The country produces little—cotton, aluminum, some hydropower—and the civil war destroyed what industrial capacity existed. Rahmon's government is kleptocratic; his family controls key sectors while presenting a personality cult (he renamed himself "Leader of the Nation" and erected a 165-foot flagpole). Political opposition is crushed, opponents imprisoned or exiled. The border with Afghanistan bleeds drugs and instability. Chinese debt is mounting as Beijing builds roads and extracts resources. Young people see no future except migration. The state offers neither prosperity nor freedom nor even competent governance—only patronage networks and the memory of war.

## The Contribution

Tajikistan preserves Persian culture in Central Asia. Its language connects modern Tajiks to Rudaki, the ninth-century poet considered the father of Persian literature, who was born near Samarkand. The country maintains traditions of hospitality and poetry that predate both Soviet and Islamic periods. Tajik scholars have contributed to Islamic theology and philosophy. The Pamir Highway, though built by Soviets, showcases some of Earth's most dramatic landscapes. More modestly, Tajikistan's persistence—surviving civil war, poverty, and authoritarian misrule—demonstrates the resilience of mountain peoples who have endured empires from Alexander to Stalin. The nation's weakness is also its witness: it shows what happens when borders are drawn for imperial convenience, when independence comes without preparation, and when elites choose extraction over nation-building.

## A Moment

In 2015, Gulmurod Khalimov, the commander of Tajikistan's elite police force and a U.S.-trained counterterrorism specialist, appeared in an ISIS video denouncing America and declaring allegiance to the caliphate. He had trained at Blackwater facilities, attended courses in the United States and United Arab Emirates, and led Tajikistan's OMON special forces. Now he called for jihad against the regime that had employed him. The defection stunned Western security officials who had vetted him repeatedly. Khalimov had seen both systems—the corrupt authoritarianism of Rahmon's Tajikistan and the professed values of Western counterterrorism—and chosen neither, opting instead for apocalyptic certainty. He was killed in a Russian airstrike near Raqqa in 2017. His betrayal revealed the fragility of Tajikistan's state: even its most trusted enforcers could abandon it, finding more meaning in transnational extremism than in a nation that existed only on Soviet-drawn maps.
