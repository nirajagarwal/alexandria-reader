# Venezuela
*Oil-Cursed Republic*

Venezuela possesses everything needed to prosper—vast oil reserves, fertile land, Caribbean coastline, educated people—and has spent a century squandering it. Born in Simón Bolívar's revolutionary fire, enriched by petroleum wealth that should have built a modern state, it has instead become a textbook case of the resource curse: authoritarian decay, economic collapse, mass exodus. The question isn't why Venezuela struggles—it's how a nation so advantaged fell so far.

## The Land

Venezuela occupies South America's northern coast, where the Andes meet the Caribbean and the Orinoco River drains into vast llanos grasslands. The country divides into four distinct zones: coastal mountains where most people live, including Caracas wedged in a valley at 3,000 feet; the Orinoco basin's sweeping plains; the Guiana Highlands' ancient table mountains; and Lake Maracaibo's oil-rich basin. Angel Falls plunges 3,212 feet from Auyán-tepui, the world's highest uninterrupted waterfall. Beneath the ground lies the Orinoco Belt—crude oil reserves possibly exceeding Saudi Arabia's, though much of it heavy and expensive to extract. The land provided abundance; what humans did with it is another story.

## The History

Simón Bolívar liberated Venezuela from Spain in 1821, dreaming of a united Gran Colombia stretching from Panama to Peru. The dream collapsed within a decade into bickering republics. Venezuela cycled through caudillos and constitutions until oil transformed everything. Commercial production began in 1914; by the 1920s, petroleum had replaced coffee as the economic engine. Juan Vicente Gómez ruled as dictator from 1908 to 1935, modernizing infrastructure while enriching himself spectacularly from oil concessions. 

Democracy emerged fitfully after 1958, dominated by two parties alternating power while oil revenues funded generous welfare. Hugo Chávez, a failed coup leader, won the 1998 election promising "Bolivarian Revolution" for the poor. High oil prices funded massive social spending; Chávez dismantled institutional checks, packed the courts, and centralized power. When he died in 2013, successor Nicolás Maduro inherited declining oil prices and a hollowed-out state. The economy collapsed. By 2024, over seven million Venezuelans—a quarter of the population—had fled.

## The Culture

Venezuela developed a distinctive Caribbean-Andean culture mixing Spanish colonial heritage, African influences from slavery, and indigenous elements. Venezuelans are famously gregarious, passionate about baseball (a rare South American exception to soccer dominance), and fiercely proud despite current circumstances. The country has produced remarkable beauty queens—more Miss Universe winners than almost anywhere—and internationally successful musicians in salsa and joropo, the traditional plains music. 

Family ties remain paramount; the exodus has torn apart networks that sustained people through previous crises. Urban Venezuelans were once solidly middle class, vacationing in Miami, sending children to university. That world has evaporated. Class divisions that Chávez exploited between Caracas' wealthy and the barrio poor have been replaced by simpler categories: those who left and those who couldn't. A culture built on oil wealth and political patronage now survives on remittances and improvisation.

## The Challenge

Venezuela has experienced economic collapse of a scale rarely seen outside wartime—GDP contracted by three-quarters between 2013 and 2020, hyperinflation rendered the currency worthless, basic goods disappeared. Oil production, once three million barrels daily, fell below 700,000. This wasn't natural disaster or external invasion but policy failure: nationalizing industries, printing money, imposing price controls that destroyed production, driving out expertise. 

The political system has ossified into competitive authoritarianism. Maduro clings to power through military loyalty, Cuban security advisors, and willingness to deploy violence. Opposition figures are imprisoned, exiled, or banned from office. Elections are manipulated. Meanwhile, criminal gangs and Colombian guerrillas control swaths of territory. The challenge isn't just economic recovery—it's rebuilding institutions deliberately destroyed, restoring trust utterly broken, convincing millions who fled to return. Venezuela needs what failed states need: functioning government. The oil money that should have built one instead corroded everything it touched.

## The Contribution

Venezuela gave the world its most famous liberator. Simón Bolívar freed not just Venezuela but Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Bolivia (named for him), imagining a unified Latin American republic that could rival Europe and North America. His failures proved as instructive as his victories—the difficulties of imposing unity on diverse territories, of translating military success into stable governance. 

The country has contributed significant artists: Teresa Carreño, the 19th-century pianist who played for Lincoln; Jesús Soto and Carlos Cruz-Díez, kinetic and optical art pioneers; conductor Gustavo Dudamel emerging from El Sistema, the youth orchestra program that has spread globally. Venezuelan cuisine gave the world arepas and pabellón criollo. Writers like Rómulo Gallegos and Arturo Uslar Pietri shaped Latin American literature. These contributions remind us that Venezuela was—and could be again—more than its current catastrophe.

## A Moment

In February 2014, student protests against inflation and crime spread across Venezuela. The government responded with characteristic brutality—riot police, pro-government paramilitaries on motorcycles, arbitrary arrests. Among those imprisoned was Leopoldo López, opposition leader who had surrendered publicly to dramatize the authoritarianism. Television footage showed him kissing his wife goodbye before guards took him to military prison.

The protests fizzled. López spent years in jail, then house arrest, then Spanish exile. The students' complaints—shortages, insecurity, repression—only worsened. But the image of López surrendering captured something essential: a nation where demanding accountability had become criminal, where the social contract had dissolved into raw power. Not a dictator's coup but a democracy's slow strangulation, cheered by some, endured by most, resisted by the dwindling few still believing resistance mattered. Venezuela's tragedy isn't that democracy was overthrown but that it was voted away, then held hostage to oil money and a militarized regime unwilling to relinquish control even as the nation collapsed beneath it.
