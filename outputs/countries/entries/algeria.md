# Algeria
*Empire's Unfinished Revolution*

The largest country in Africa stretches from Mediterranean beaches to Saharan vastness, a place where the weight of colonial memory shapes every political conversation and the promise of independence remains perpetually deferred. Born through one of the 20th century's bloodiest liberation wars, Algeria freed itself from France but never quite freed itself from the structures that war created: military dominance, resource dependence, and a political class that speaks revolution while practicing extraction.

## The Land

The vast majority is sand. The Sahara covers eighty percent of Algeria's 2.4 million square kilometers—larger than any African nation except Sudan—but nearly everyone lives in the thin fertile strip along the Mediterranean coast or in the valleys of the Tell Atlas mountains. The coastal cities—Algiers, Oran, Constantine—are where the country actually happens, crowded onto land that resembles southern Spain more than central Africa. 

South of the mountains, the desert begins. The Sahara here isn't romantic—it's absolute. The Hoggar Mountains rise improbably from sand. Oil and gas hide beneath. A few Tuareg and other desert peoples maintain old caravan routes, but this emptiness belongs mostly to hydrocarbons and the companies that extract them.

The Mediterranean climate supports wheat, olives, citrus. Rain comes in winter. The coast is beautiful in the classical sense—the Romans knew what they were doing when they made this North Africa's breadbasket. But Algeria's wealth no longer comes from what grows; it comes from what burns.

## The History

Indigenous Berbers—the actual original North Africans—watched Phoenicians, Romans, Vandals, Arabs, Ottomans, and finally French arrive and claim the land. The Arab conquest in the 7th century brought Islam and the Arabic language, though Berber languages and identity persisted, especially in the Kabylie mountains. The French arrived in 1830 and stayed 132 years.

Unlike other colonial projects, France didn't just exploit Algeria—it attempted to make it France. A million European settlers arrived. The best land went to *pieds-noirs*. Algerians became subjects without citizenship. Indigenous culture faced systematic erasure. Algeria wasn't a colony; it was declared three departments of France itself.

The independence war began November 1, 1954. The FLN's guerrilla campaign met French counterinsurgency torture and collective punishment. Between 300,000 and 1.5 million Algerians died. France used paratroopers in Algiers, created *harkis* (Algerian auxiliaries who would later be massacred or abandoned), and came close to military coup at home. De Gaulle negotiated independence in 1962. The *pieds-noirs* fled. The FLN took power.

The FLN never gave it up. Socialism, single-party rule, oil nationalism. In 1988, riots forced multiparty elections. In 1991, the Islamic Salvation Front was poised to win. The military canceled the election. Civil war followed—the "Black Decade"—perhaps 200,000 dead. The military won. Elections resumed but meant little. President Bouteflika ruled from 1999 until 2019, including four years after a stroke left him unable to speak. The Hirak protest movement forced him out, but the military remains the actual power.

## The Culture

French remains the language of administration and education, though officially Algeria is Arabic-speaking. Berber languages (Tamazight) gained official status in 2016 after decades of marginalization. This linguistic complexity maps onto identity tensions: Arab versus Berber, secular versus Islamist, francophone versus arabophone.

Islam is the state religion, but Algeria's Islam bears marks of both Berber tradition and French secular influence. Most Algerians are Sunni Muslim, culturally conservative but often politically skeptical of political Islam after the civil war. Women's status reflects contradictions: relatively high university enrollment, but social pressure toward traditional gender roles.

Family remains central. Elaborate weddings despite economic hardship. Young men waiting years to marry because unemployment makes it impossible to afford an apartment. The café culture, inherited from France, remains male-dominated.

Football is religion. Raï music—born in Oran, mixing Berber, Arab, French, and Spanish influences—gave the world Khaled and Cheb Mami. The novelist Kamel Daouine writes in French about the civil war. Albert Camus, born in Algiers to *pied-noir* parents, remains claimed and contested. The FLN still controls the official memory of the revolution.

## The Challenge

Oil and gas are ninety-five percent of export revenue. When prices are high, the regime can subsidize bread and buy social peace. When they fall, the state budget collapses. Unemployment hovers around twelve percent officially, higher for young people. Algeria imports most of its food despite fertile coastal land.

The military-backed regime has mastered survival without reform. Opposition is fragmented between Islamists (remembered with fear after the civil war), secularists, Berber activists, and socialists. The Hirak movement proved Algerians can mobilize peacefully en masse, but couldn't break the system. The aging generals remain in control.

The revolution's legitimacy is exhausted. Everyone under fifty was born long after independence. The FLN's nationalist rhetoric rings hollow when corruption is pervasive and opportunity requires connections. Young Algerians risk death crossing the Mediterranean in boats to reach the shores of the former colonizer.

The relationship with France remains poisoned and intimate. Algeria demands apologies for colonial crimes; France hedges. Algerians watch French TV and listen to French music while resenting French cultural dominance. The past won't pass.

## The Contribution

The Algerian Revolution inspired liberation movements worldwide. Frantz Fanon wrote *The Wretched of the Earth* while working as a psychiatrist for the FLN. The battle of Algiers became a template studied by guerrillas and counterinsurgents alike. Algeria showed that even settler colonies could be dismantled through armed struggle, though at tremendous cost.

Raï music created a North African sound that mixed everything—Berber rhythms, Arabic poetry, French chanson, Spanish guitars, electronic beats. It became the voice of Algerian youth and spread across the Mediterranean.

Algeria's anti-colonial stance shaped Third World politics in the 1960s and 1970s. It hosted revolutionary movements, championed non-alignment, and supported Palestine. Before oil money and military rule hollowed it out, Algeria represented genuine independence.

The Algerian novel in French—Kateb Yacine, Assia Djebar, Kamel Daouine—created a literature that belongs to both languages and neither, exploring what it means to write in the colonizer's language about decolonization.

## A Moment

May 2019, Algiers. Every Friday for three months, millions have filled the streets. They call it the Hirak—the Movement. They demand the president step down, but Abdelaziz Bouteflika hasn't appeared in public since his stroke in 2013. His fifth term was announced while he sat in a Swiss hospital. The protests are peaceful, organized, massive. Women and men march together. Berbers and Arabs. Old revolutionaries whose legitimacy comes from 1962 march beside young people who want jobs and dignity.

They clean up after themselves. They refuse provocation. They sing the national anthem and chant "silmiya, silmiya"—peaceful, peaceful. On April 2, Bouteflika resigns. The crowds celebrate, then return the next Friday. They understand the president was a symbol; the system remains. The chants change: "They must all go."

The marches continue through summer heat. Millions in the streets, week after week, with no violence, no chaos. It's a civics lesson the world ignores. By December, the military has installed a new president in a election hardly anyone votes in. The protests face increasing repression. COVID provides cover for a crackdown. The Hirak fades.

But for those months, Algeria showed itself capable of mass peaceful mobilization, of crossing its internal divides, of demanding accountability. The revolution the FLN promised finally appeared—only to be defeated, again, by the men in uniform who claim to represent it.
