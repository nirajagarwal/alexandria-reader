# Côte d'Ivoire
*The Cocoa Republic*

For thirty years after independence, Côte d'Ivoire was West Africa's success story—stable, prosperous, confident. Then the model broke. What was once the region's economic engine descended into civil war, ethnic division, and political crisis. The country has clawed its way back, but the fractures remain visible. This is a nation built on cocoa and French connections, still searching for a unity that transcends its constructed borders and inherited divisions.

## The Land

Côte d'Ivoire occupies a green slice of the West African coast, stretching from the Atlantic northward into savanna. The south is tropical forest—dense, hot, perpetually humid—where cocoa trees thrive in the shade. The center transitions to woodland and farmland. The north is drier, part of the Sudanese belt, more Muslim, more connected to the Sahel than to the coast.

The Ébrié Lagoon cuts behind Abidjan, the economic capital, creating a natural harbor that became the country's economic heart. Yamoussoukro, the official capital since 1983, sits inland—an assertion of presidential ego more than geographic logic. The land produces cocoa, coffee, palm oil, rubber. It remains extraordinarily fertile, the reason the French stayed and the reason migrants still come.

## The History

France claimed the territory in the 1840s but didn't fully control it until the early twentieth century. They made it the jewel of French West Africa—investing heavily, building infrastructure, creating a cocoa economy that depended on migrant labor from Burkina Faso and Mali.

Independence came in 1960 under Félix Houphouët-Boigny, who maintained such close ties to France that critics called it "Françafrique" incarnate. But Houphouët-Boigny delivered: economic growth averaged 7% annually through the 1970s. Abidjan became the Paris of West Africa. He ruled for thirty-three years, autocratic but relatively benign, holding the country's ethnic divisions in check through patronage and French backing.

His death in 1993 exposed the fragility. Economic crisis hit. Politicians began weaponizing ethnicity, particularly the concept of *Ivoirité*—Ivorian-ness—to exclude northerners and Muslims from power. In 2002, the country split: a rebellion in the north, the government in the south. A decade of crisis followed. The 2010 election descended into violence when Laurent Gbagbo refused to concede defeat to Alassane Ouattara. French troops intervened. Three thousand people died.

Ouattara has presided over recovery and reconciliation since 2011, though his own third-term power grab in 2020 raised questions about whether Côte d'Ivoire has truly escaped its pattern of strongman politics.

## The Culture

Côte d'Ivoire contains more than sixty ethnic groups, speaking dozens of languages, practicing different religions, with no dominant majority. The Akan peoples (including Baoulé) dominate the south and center. The Voltaic groups control the north. The Mandé spread across the west. French serves as lingua franca—spoken more widely here than in many former French colonies.

Islam and Christianity divide the country roughly along regional lines, though Abidjan mixes everything. The south is more Christian, more French-influenced, more cosmopolitan. The north is more Muslim, more connected to Sahelian culture, long excluded from power.

Music transcends the divisions—coupé-décalé, zouglou, reggae—with Abidjan producing sounds that dominate West African airwaves. The city remains stylish, French-speaking, fashion-conscious, even after the crisis years. But the cultural confidence is newer than it looks. *Ivoirité* wasn't ancient identity—it was invented politics, an attempt to draw boundaries around a fundamentally plural society.

## The Challenge

The country still hasn't resolved the question of who belongs. Millions of migrants and their descendants—from Burkina Faso, Mali, Guinea—built the cocoa economy but remain politically suspect. The north-south divide persists. Ouattara's third term, constitutionally dubious, suggests the old patterns haven't broken.

Economic growth has returned—5-7% annually—but it concentrates in Abidjan while rural areas stagnate. Cocoa production dominates exports, making the country vulnerable to global price swings. Most cocoa farmers live in poverty despite producing 40% of the world's supply. Land disputes continue. Former combatants remain inadequately reintegrated.

The deeper challenge is forging genuine national identity from colonial artifice. The borders are French inventions. The economy runs on French corporate interests. The language is French. What makes someone Ivorian if not French citizenship, cocoa production, and an Abidjan address?

## The Contribution

Côte d'Ivoire gave West African urbanism one of its great exemplars. Abidjan in the 1970s and 1980s was the region's cultural capital—sophisticated, multilingual, economically dynamic, architecturally ambitious. It proved that African cities could be modern without being purely imitative.

The country's model of inclusive economic growth—however dependent on France, however fragile—worked for three decades and influenced regional policy. Its music exports remain formidable. *Zouglou*, emerging from university campuses during economic crisis, became the soundtrack of West African youth frustration and joy.

More quietly, Côte d'Ivoire demonstrated something important about post-conflict recovery. After a decade of civil war and election violence, the country stabilized without descending into permanent failed-state status. Reconciliation remains incomplete, but the fact that it's happening at all matters.

## A Moment

The Basilica of Our Lady of Peace stands in Yamoussoukro, Houphouët-Boigny's hometown, built between 1985 and 1989. It's the largest church in the world, modeled on St. Peter's in Rome but bigger, its dome rising 158 meters above the savanna. Air-conditioned, marble-clad, surrounded by papal-blessed grounds, it seats 18,000 with standing room for 300,000 more.

The country was already in economic decline when Houphouët-Boigny commissioned it with his personal fortune—though "personal" meant money extracted from cocoa revenues and French corporate deals. Critics called it obscene waste in a developing country. He called it a gift to God and insisted the Pope visit to consecrate it, which John Paul II did in 1990.

The basilica still stands, mostly empty except for major holidays, air conditioning off to save costs, a monument to presidential megalomania and sincere Catholic faith simultaneously. Guides show tourists the marble imported from Italy, the stained glass from France, the acres of unused seating. Outside, Yamoussoukro remains a provincial town that happens to be the capital, its grand boulevards leading nowhere in particular, its presidential palace visible in the distance. The basilica embodies something essential about Côte d'Ivoire's post-colonial trajectory: enormous ambition, French connections, brilliant execution, questionable priorities, and the persistent question of what was being built and for whom.
