# Myanmar
*The Broken Promise*

Myanmar stands as one of Asia's great tragedies—a nation rich in resources and culture, blessed with diverse peoples and strategic location, yet cursed by military rule for most of its independent existence. What could have been Southeast Asia's jewel became instead a cautionary tale about how institutions matter more than endowments. The brief democratic opening between 2011 and 2021 offered hope; the military's brutal reassertion of power confirmed the depth of the rot. This is a country trying desperately to become itself while those with guns insist it remain their property.

## The Land

Myanmar stretches along the Bay of Bengal and Andaman Sea, sharing borders with Bangladesh, India, China, Laos, and Thailand. The Irrawaddy River flows nearly the entire length of the country—1,350 miles from the Himalayan foothills to the vast delta—creating Myanmar's agricultural heartland. The landscape varies dramatically: snow-capped mountains in the north, the fertile central plains where most Burmans live, the rugged hill country of ethnic minorities along the Thai and Chinese borders, and the tropical forests of Tanintharyi in the south. Myanmar possesses substantial natural resources—jade, rubies, teak, oil, natural gas—which have funded military rule while enriching generals and foreign corporations rather than ordinary people. The country's position between South and Southeast Asia should be strategic advantage; instead it has made Myanmar a contested space between Chinese and Western influence.

## The History

Myanmar's history is one of kingdoms, conquest, colonialism, and catastrophic governance. The Bagan Kingdom (849-1287) built thousands of Buddhist temples that still define the landscape. Successive dynasties—Ava, Hanthawaddy, Taungoo, Konbaung—ruled the Irrawaddy valley and periodically conquered neighboring peoples, creating a multi-ethnic empire. The British conquered Burma in three wars (1824-1826, 1852, 1885), making it a province of British India and exploiting its resources while fostering ethnic divisions. Independence in 1948 brought hope but quickly descended into ethnic insurgencies and parliamentary dysfunction. General Ne Win's coup in 1962 established military rule that persists in various forms to the present. His "Burmese Way to Socialism" isolated the country and impoverished its people. The 1988 pro-democracy uprising was crushed; the military junta renamed the country Myanmar (from Burma) and the capital Yangon (from Rangoon). Aung San Suu Kyi's National League for Democracy won elections in 1990 that the military ignored, keeping her under house arrest for 15 of the next 21 years. A quasi-civilian government from 2011 allowed limited reforms and her release. The 2015 elections brought her party to power—but the military retained control of security ministries and guaranteed parliamentary seats. The brief democratic experiment ended February 1, 2021, when the military staged another coup, detained Suu Kyi, and began killing protesters. The country descended into civil war.

## The Culture

Burmans constitute roughly 68% of the population, but Myanmar contains over 135 distinct ethnic groups—Shan, Karen, Rakhine, Chin, Kachin, Mon, and many others—each with its own language, culture, and often armed resistance movement. Theravada Buddhism dominates among Burmans and several other groups, shaping social values around merit-making, monastic education, and cyclical conceptions of suffering and rebirth. The sangha (monastic community) has occasionally opposed military rule—most famously in the 2007 "Saffron Revolution"—but has also fostered anti-Muslim sentiment, particularly toward the Rohingya. Traditional culture values social harmony and face-saving, which can translate into surface politeness concealing deep resentments. Gender relations are relatively egalitarian compared to neighboring South Asian countries, with women participating actively in commerce and public life. Longyi (sarongs) are worn by both men and women. The culture includes rich traditions of puppetry, classical dance, and lacquerware. But Myanmar's defining cultural reality is fragmentation: the military's Burman chauvinism versus ethnic minorities' desire for autonomy or independence; Buddhist nationalism versus religious pluralism; urban aspirations versus rural realities. No shared national culture truly exists—only Burman dominance imposed by force.

## The Challenge

Myanmar faces simultaneous crises: civil war, military dictatorship, economic collapse, and humanitarian catastrophe. Since the 2021 coup, armed resistance has spread beyond traditional ethnic insurgencies to include urban youth forming People's Defense Forces. The military—the Tatmadaw—responds with airstrikes on villages, massacres, and scorched earth tactics. Over a million people are internally displaced; thousands have been killed. The economy, already weakened by COVID-19, has contracted sharply. The kyat has collapsed. Healthcare and education systems have largely ceased functioning. China and Russia support the junta; Western sanctions have limited impact. The military believes it alone can hold the country together; in reality, it is tearing Myanmar apart. Even if the junta fell tomorrow, Myanmar would face the challenge of building a federal democracy that accommodates ethnic diversity after 75 years of centralized military rule. The Rohingya genocide (2016-2017), which forced 700,000 Muslims to flee to Bangladesh, demonstrated how deeply Buddhist nationalism has been weaponized. Trust between communities has been systematically destroyed. The challenge is existential: can Myanmar become an actual nation rather than a military holding pen?

## The Contribution

Myanmar's cultural contributions have been largely contained within its borders, but they are substantial. Bagan's temple complex rivals Angkor Wat as Southeast Asia's supreme architectural achievement—over 2,200 temples remaining from an original 10,000, built between the 11th and 13th centuries. Burmese Buddhism developed distinctive practices, including strong lay meditation movements that influenced Western Buddhism through teachers like S.N. Goenka and Mahasi Sayadaw, who systematized vipassana meditation techniques now practiced worldwide. George Orwell's "Burmese Days" drew on his experience as a colonial police officer, shaping his critique of imperialism. Myanmar's struggle for democracy, embodied in Aung San Suu Kyi before her complicity in the Rohingya genocide tarnished her reputation, inspired global human rights movements. The country's ethnic resistance movements have demonstrated extraordinary endurance—the Karen have been fighting for autonomy since 1949, the longest-running civil war in modern history. This persistence, whether tragic or heroic, testifies to something the military has never grasped: you cannot simply erase peoples' desire for self-determination by shooting them.

## A Moment

August 8, 1988—8/8/88, a date chosen for numerological significance—students and monks marched through Yangon demanding democracy. The protests spread nationwide. For weeks, Myanmar tasted freedom. People spoke openly against Ne Win's dictatorship. A generation believed change was possible. On September 18, the military opened fire. Soldiers shot protesters in the streets, hunted students through neighborhoods, bayoneted the wounded. Conservative estimates put the death toll at 3,000; the actual number is higher. Aung San Suu Kyi emerged as the movement's leader, her father Aung San having led Myanmar to independence before his assassination in 1947. But 1988 ended in blood. The junta that seized power would rule for decades, allowing limited reforms only when convenient, always ready to kill when necessary. That willingness to massacre its own citizens defines Myanmar's tragedy. The military saw three thousand dead and calculated it was worth it to maintain power. They made the same calculation in 2021. Until that calculation changes—until the cost of killing becomes higher than the cost of sharing power—Myanmar will remain broken.
