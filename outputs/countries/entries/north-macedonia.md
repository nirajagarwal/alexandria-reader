# North Macedonia
*The Name That Cost Everything*

A landlocked country the size of Vermont, wedged between Greece, Bulgaria, Serbia, Kosovo, and Albania—North Macedonia is what remains when neighbors consume your history, contest your identity, and block your future. For three decades, this small nation has fought not for territory or resources but for the right to call itself by a name its southern neighbor considers theft. The fight cost it EU membership, NATO entry, and economic development. In 2019, it finally surrendered, adding "North" to "Macedonia" in exchange for Greece's permission to join the Western club. The bitterness lingers.

## The Land

Mountains dominate—the Šar range along Kosovo, the Osogovo along Bulgaria, smaller chains cutting the interior into isolated valleys. Only the Vardar River valley, running southeast toward Greece, offers flat agricultural land and easy passage. Lake Ohrid in the southwest, shared with Albania, is among Europe's oldest and deepest—a remnant of the Tertiary period, host to endemic species found nowhere else. The climate is continental in the north, Mediterranean in the south. Skopje sits in a seismic zone; a 1963 earthquake killed over a thousand and destroyed much of the old city. Most of the population clusters in the valleys; the mountains remain sparsely inhabited.

## The History

The region's ancient past—Alexander the Great, Philip II, the kingdom of Macedon—belongs primarily to Greek history, though Slavic Macedonians claim a connection to the land if not the people. Slavs settled here in the 6th and 7th centuries. The medieval Bulgarian Empire controlled the territory; later the Serbian Empire. The Ottomans conquered it in the 14th century and held it for five hundred years, long enough to convert a significant portion of the population to Islam and establish a multiethnic pattern that persists.

The 1913 Treaty of Bucharest partitioned Macedonia among Serbia, Greece, and Bulgaria after the Balkan Wars—none asked the Macedonians. The Serbian portion became part of Yugoslavia after World War I. Tito's communist Yugoslavia recognized a Macedonian republic in 1945, partly to counterbalance Serbian dominance, partly to maintain a claim on Greek and Bulgarian Macedonia. When Yugoslavia collapsed in 1991, Macedonia declared independence peacefully—the only Yugoslav republic to do so without war.

Then came the name dispute. Greece, which has a northern region called Macedonia, objected to the new country using the name, claiming it implied territorial ambitions on Greek land and appropriated Greek history. The dispute lasted twenty-seven years, during which Greece vetoed Macedonian NATO and EU membership. The 2018 Prespa Agreement—adding "North" to the name—finally resolved it, though nationalist opposition nearly tore the country apart.

## The Culture

Macedonian Slavs comprise about two-thirds of the population; Albanians about a quarter, concentrated in the west and northwest. Smaller populations of Turks, Roma, Serbs, and Bosniaks complete the mix. Macedonian, a South Slavic language close to Bulgarian, is official; Albanian has co-official status in Albanian-majority areas. Orthodoxy dominates among Macedonians, Islam among Albanians.

The ethnic division runs deep. Albanians boycotted the 2018 referendum on the name change, seeing it as a Macedonian issue. The 2001 armed conflict between Albanian insurgents and Macedonian security forces nearly became civil war; the Ohrid Framework Agreement granted Albanians greater rights and ended the violence, but mutual suspicion remains. Intermarriage is rare. Cities segregate informally. Schools teach in separate languages.

Macedonian culture emphasizes hospitality, family loyalty, and a certain defensive pride—we've survived empires and neighbors who denied our existence. The government's "Skopje 2014" project built kitschy neoclassical facades and massive statues across the capital, including a giant warrior on horseback officially called "Warrior on a Horse" to avoid angering Greece, though everyone knew it represented Alexander. Critics called it nationalist kitsch; supporters called it reclaiming stolen history.

## The Challenge

Corruption and state capture. Every international index ranks North Macedonia poorly on rule of law, government effectiveness, and control of corruption. Political parties function as patronage networks. Public sector jobs go to the connected. Judges rule as politics demands. Businesses pay bribes or don't do business. The EU has repeatedly delayed membership talks, citing insufficient progress on judicial reform and fighting corruption.

Ethnic tensions simmer. The Albanian population is younger and growing; the Macedonian population is aging and emigrating. Albanians demand greater autonomy, more state support for the Albanian language, proportional representation in government jobs. Macedonians fear partition or absorption by a Greater Albania. The constitutional court, the police, the army—all remain sites of ethnic competition.

Emigration hollows the country. About a quarter of the population lives abroad. Young, educated Macedonians leave for Germany, Austria, Western Europe, anywhere with jobs and futures. The population has declined from over two million in the early 1990s to under two million today. Villages empty. Remittances from abroad keep many families afloat, but a nation cannot survive on nostalgia and wire transfers.

## The Contribution

North Macedonia gave the world Mother Teresa—born Anjezë Gonxhe Bojaxhiu in Skopje in 1910 when it was still Ottoman, of Albanian ethnicity, she became the face of Christian charity in the late 20th century. The country claims her; so does Albania; so does the Catholic Church. She probably wouldn't have cared about the argument.

The Ohrid Literary School, established in the 9th or 10th century, was a center of Slavic learning and manuscript production, helping standardize Church Slavonic and spread literacy among Slavic peoples. The Cyrillic alphabet—used by Russians, Ukrainians, Bulgarians, Serbs, and others—passed through Ohrid on its journey from Bulgaria to the wider Slavic world.

The survival of multiethnic coexistence, however fragile, offers a lesson to neighbors who chose ethnic cleansing. North Macedonia in the 1990s could have gone the way of Bosnia or Kosovo—mass violence, partition, permanent division. It came close in 2001. That it pulled back, however incompletely, suggests the possibility of compromise even in the Balkans.

## A Moment

August 2017: Workers begin erecting a giant statue of Alexander the Great in Skopje's main square, part of the Skopje 2014 project. Officially it's called "Warrior on a Horse" because Greece has threatened to veto EU membership if Macedonia glorifies a Greek historical figure. Everyone knows it's Alexander. The statue costs millions in a country where the average salary is €400 a month. Critics protest the kitsch, the cost, the nationalism. Supporters say: We have the right to our history. Greece has stolen everything else—our name, our symbols, our past. This statue, however ridiculous, is resistance.

A year later, the government agrees to change the country's name to North Macedonia. The statue remains, renamed, now a monument to a failed assertion of identity. Tourists take photos. Macedonians walk past it, bitter or resigned, depending on which side of the referendum they supported. The warrior on his horse rears up over a city that couldn't afford him, in a country that traded its name for permission to join a union that may never accept it anyway. The statue says: We were here. We mattered. Believe us.
