# Belgium
*The Invented Country*

Belgium exists because Europe's great powers needed a buffer state. Created in 1830 from the wreckage of the United Kingdom of the Netherlands, it has spent nearly two centuries proving that artificial nations can survive—barely. The country splits linguistically, culturally, and politically between Dutch-speaking Flanders in the north and French-speaking Wallonia in the south, with a bilingual Brussels caught between them. That it functions at all is a minor miracle of compromise and institutional complexity. That it houses both the European Union and NATO headquarters is a particularly Belgian irony: the continent's most fragile state hosting its boldest experiments in unity.

## The Land

Belgium is small—roughly the size of Maryland—and relentlessly flat across most of its territory, rising only to modest hills in the Ardennes forest of the southeast. The Scheldt River connects Antwerp to the sea, making it one of Europe's great ports despite being landlocked by geography. The land itself is fertile, intensively farmed for centuries, every hectare put to use. Rain comes often, the climate mild and gray. No natural borders define Belgium—no mountain range, no wide river separating it cleanly from France, Germany, or the Netherlands. The Low Countries earned their name here: much of Flanders sits barely above sea level, reclaimed from marshland, vulnerable to flooding. The land offers no obvious reason for Belgium to exist as a distinct entity. That distinction came from elsewhere.

## The History

The territory that became Belgium passed between empires for centuries—Spanish, Austrian, French, Dutch—before the Belgian Revolution of 1830 gave it independence. The revolt began in a Brussels opera house during a performance of *La Muette de Portici*, and within weeks the Dutch were expelled. Europe's great powers, fresh from the Congress of Vienna's reshuffling of borders, recognized Belgium as a permanently neutral buffer state between France and Germany. Leopold I, a German prince married to British royalty, became its first king.

Belgium industrialized early. By the 1850s, it had continental Europe's densest railway network and most productive coal mines. Wallonia boomed with steel mills and glass factories while Flanders remained agricultural and poor. This economic divide would later invert, reshaping the nation's politics.

Leopold II, who ruled from 1865 to 1909, claimed the Congo as his personal property and extracted rubber through a system of terror that killed millions. The wealth flowed to Brussels—monuments, museums, parks—built on Congolese corpses. Belgium took formal control of the colony in 1908 but continued the exploitation until independence in 1960.

Two world wars devastated Belgium. German armies violated its neutrality in 1914, turning Flanders into a charnel house. Ypres, Passchendaele—the names became synonyms for mechanized slaughter. In 1940, the Germans returned, and Belgium endured four years of occupation. King Leopold III's collaboration with the Nazis permanently damaged the monarchy's legitimacy.

Post-war recovery coincided with linguistic federalization. As Flanders industrialized and Wallonia's mines closed, political power shifted north. Constitutional reforms in 1970, 1980, and 1993 transformed Belgium into a federal state with separate Flemish, Walloon, and Brussels governments. The country now has six governments for eleven million people.

## The Culture

Belgium is two countries occupying one state. Flemings speak Dutch (they call it Flemish) and look north to the Netherlands and Scandinavia. Walloons speak French and orient toward France. Brussels, officially bilingual, is predominantly Francophone but surrounded by Flemish territory. A small German-speaking community exists along the eastern border, a remnant of post-WWI border adjustments.

The linguistic divide shapes everything. Flemish and Francophone children attend separate schools, watch different television, read different newspapers, vote for different political parties. National politics consists almost entirely of managing this division through endless negotiation and institutional complexity. After the 2010 election, Belgium went 541 days without a federal government—a world record—because the two communities couldn't agree on a coalition. The country functioned normally throughout.

Belgian culture excels at the small-scale and the domestic. Comic books—Tintin, the Smurfs, Lucky Luke—rank among Belgium's most successful cultural exports. The country takes food seriously: French fries (despite the name) originated here, served with mayonnaise in paper cones. Belgian beer brewing is a UNESCO-recognized cultural tradition, with hundreds of varieties from abbey Tripels to lambic fermented in open air. Chocolate, waffles, mussels—Belgium's cuisine privileges quality over innovation.

Surrealism found fertile ground in Belgium's peculiar combination of bourgeois conformity and underlying absurdity. René Magritte painted his dreamlike images in a Brussels suburb, presenting the impossible with perfect ordinariness. Paul Delvaux's empty streets and somnambulant women captured something essential about Belgian unease. The country has always been slightly uncanny—familiar but off, recognizable but strange.

## The Challenge

Belgium faces slow-motion disintegration. Flemish nationalists, who dominate politics in the north, openly discuss independence or confederation so loose it amounts to the same thing. Wallonia, economically struggling and demographically declining, depends on transfer payments from wealthier Flanders. The Flemish resent subsidizing the Walloons; the Walloons resent being treated as dependents. Brussels complicates everything—a Francophone island in Flemish territory, economically vital, politically contentious.

No shared national identity bridges the divide. Belgium has produced no unifying myth, no common story both communities accept. The king, theoretically a symbol of unity, commands little real loyalty. The national soccer team briefly united the country during successful World Cups, but the effect faded quickly. Young Flemings increasingly view themselves as Flemish first, Belgian second or not at all.

The European Union's presence in Brussels creates an additional paradox: as Belgium fragments, it hosts the institutions meant to unite Europe. The country that cannot hold itself together houses the project to transcend national divisions. Whether this is ironic or appropriate depends on your view of both Belgium and Europe.

## The Contribution

Belgium gave the world Art Nouveau. Victor Horta's Brussels townhouses, with their sinuous ironwork and organic forms, defined an entire aesthetic movement. The Atomium, built for the 1958 World's Fair, became an icon of atomic-age optimism.

The saxophone, invented by Adolphe Sax in 1840s Brussels, transformed jazz and popular music. Georges Lemaître, a Belgian priest and physicist, proposed the Big Bang theory in 1927. Gerardus Mercator created the map projection that still shapes how we imagine the globe.

Belgian detective fiction—Georges Simenon's Inspector Maigret novels—brought psychological depth to the genre. Hergé's Tintin remains one of the most translated comic series in history, read by generations worldwide.

Belgium pioneered modern international law. The Congo atrocities prompted the first international human rights investigations. Brussels became synonymous with multilateral diplomacy, hosting institutions from the EU to NATO to countless NGOs. The country's necessity for compromise and its comfort with complexity made it ideal headquarters for international cooperation.

Belgian practicality produced innovations in everyday life: asphalt roads, roller skates, body mass index. The country invented nothing world-historical but improved countless small things, making modern life marginally more efficient and pleasant.

## A Moment

Brussels, July 21, 2013—Belgian National Day. The parade passes before the royal family on Rue de la Loi. Flemish units march under the Lion of Flanders. Walloon regiments follow under the Walloon rooster. The national anthem plays twice—once in Dutch, once in French—because performing it bilingually would be too complicated.

King Albert II, visibly aging, watches with his son Philippe beside him. In ten days, Albert will abdicate, exhausted by a role that demands the impossible: embodying unity in a country that doesn't want to be unified. Philippe will inherit the crown and the task of holding Belgium together through force of will and institutional inertia.

The parade continues. Bands play, flags wave, children eat frites. Brussels itself is festive and indifferent, a capital city celebrating a nation-state that exists mainly because dismantling it would be more trouble than maintaining it. The sun breaks through—rare for Belgium—and the crowd cheers without much conviction. The beer is good. The chocolate is excellent. The country endures, as it has for 183 years, because no one has figured out a better alternative.

This is Belgium: functional despite itself, prosperous through compromise, surviving on institutional complexity and mutual exhaustion. It shouldn't work. That it does is the country's greatest achievement and its defining contradiction.
