# Georgia
*Wine, War, and the Weight of Empires*

A small country wedged between the Black Sea and the Caucasus Mountains, Georgia has survived by turning occupation into art and defiance into culture. It is one of the world's oldest Christian nations, home to an ancient alphabet and older winemaking tradition, and it has spent most of its existence as someone else's borderland—Persian, Ottoman, Russian, Soviet. Independence came late and remains incomplete: two provinces are occupied, the great power to the north casts a permanent shadow, and the question of how to be Georgian while looking West has no settled answer. Yet Georgia persists with a stubbornness that borders on the miraculous, making wine and poetry out of everything empires tried to crush.

**The Land**

Georgia sits where Europe blurs into Asia, a Texas-sized country dominated by the Caucasus range to the north and opening to the Black Sea in the west. The mountains form a natural fortress—the highest peaks exceed 16,000 feet—while the valleys below hold microclimates that shift dramatically across short distances. The Alazani and Rioni river valleys are lush, almost subtropical, famous for vineyards that have produced wine for eight millennia. The eastern plains are drier, more open to invasion. The mountains protected Georgia from complete absorption but never enough to guarantee sovereignty. The land is fertile, beautiful, and strategically cursed—everyone who matters has wanted it.

**The History**

Georgia's kingdoms emerged in antiquity, and by the fourth century had adopted Christianity, making it one of the world's first Christian nations. A golden age under Queen Tamar in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries produced Shota Rustaveli's *The Knight in the Panther's Skin*, still the national epic. Then came centuries of partition between Ottoman and Persian empires, with Georgian kingdoms playing the great powers against each other for survival. In 1801, desperate for protection, the Georgian crown accepted annexation by Russia—a devil's bargain that became a prison.

The first Georgian republic lasted three years (1918-1921) before the Red Army invaded. Soviet rule brought electrification and industrialization alongside purges, forced collectivization, and Russification. Stalin, a Georgian from Gori, embodied the twisted relationship—Georgia gave the USSR its most brutal leader, who then killed tens of thousands of his countrymen. Independence came again in 1991 amid chaos: civil war, economic collapse, and the loss of Abkhazia and South Ossetia to Russian-backed separatists. The 2008 war with Russia confirmed what Georgians already knew—their independence remains contested.

**The Culture**

Georgian culture is built on hospitality (*stumari*—the guest is a gift from God), wine, and polyphonic singing that predates Christianity. The Georgian alphabet, created in the fifth century, looks like no other script on earth. The language, unrelated to any major family, is famously difficult—Mark Twain joked that God must have created it when drunk. Georgians are intensely proud, quick to toast, slower to forget. The *supra* (feast) is a sacred institution where a *tamada* (toastmaster) presides over hours of eating, drinking, and ritual toasting that weaves together family, nation, and the dead.

Georgian Orthodoxy permeates national identity, though the church's conservatism sits uneasily with Tbilisi's growing cosmopolitanism. Gender roles remain traditional outside the capital. The country is ethnically diverse—Armenians, Azerbaijanis, Russians, and others—but Georgianness is defined more by culture and language than blood. The tension between a European future and Orthodox tradition runs through everything. Tbilisi has become a hub for digital nomads and liberal exiles from Russia, creating a cultural avant-garde the provinces don't quite recognize.

**The Challenge**

Russia. Two decades after the 2008 war, Russian forces still occupy Abkhazia and South Ossetia—roughly twenty percent of Georgia's territory. The occupation is quiet but absolute, the borders creeping forward through "borderization" that swallows Georgian villages. NATO membership remains a stated goal but a distant prospect, leaving Georgia in geopolitical limbo: too committed to the West to reconcile with Russia, too vulnerable to fully join Western institutions. Emigration drains the country—nearly a fifth of Georgians live abroad. Corruption persists despite reforms. The dream of returning to Europe (which Georgians insist they never truly left) confronts the reality of Russian power and Western ambivalence. Independence is real but incomplete, and the occupied territories are a wound that won't close.

**The Contribution**

Georgia gave the world its oldest winemaking tradition—8,000 years of fermenting grapes in clay vessels called *qvevri*, now a UNESCO heritage practice. Georgian polyphonic singing, with its complex harmonies and haunting dissonances, influenced church music across Eastern Europe. Shota Rustaveli's medieval epic influenced Persian and Turkish literature. In the twentieth century, Georgia produced filmmakers (Sergei Parajanov, Tengiz Abuladze) whose work pushed the boundaries of Soviet cinema. The alphabet itself is a work of art—flowing, elegant, unlike anything else. More abstractly, Georgia offers a model of cultural survival: how a small nation keeps its language, faith, and identity intact through centuries of occupation. The Georgian toast is a gift to conviviality everywhere.

**A Moment**

In 1978, thousands of Georgians gathered outside the parliament in Tbilisi to protest a Soviet plan to remove Georgian as the official language of the republic. It was a rare public demonstration in the USSR, and it succeeded—the Kremlin backed down. That moment of defiance, peaceful but absolute, captured something essential: Georgians will endure almost anything except the erasure of their language. The alphabet, that strange and beautiful script, is more than a writing system—it's proof of existence, the visible claim that Georgia is not a province of anywhere. To threaten it was to threaten survival itself. The protestors won because the Soviet authorities understood, however dimly, that some things cannot be taken without breaking the people entirely. Wine can be taxed, churches can be closed, borders can be redrawn, but the alphabet—that stays.
