# Eswatini
*Africa's Last Absolute Monarchy*

A landlocked kingdom squeezed between South Africa and Mozambique, Eswatini is one of the world's smallest nations and Africa's last remaining absolute monarchy. What was once Swaziland—the name changed in 2018—preserves a political system elsewhere abandoned: genuine royal rule, not ceremonial pageantry. King Mswati III governs as his ancestors did, wedding tradition to modern state apparatus. The result is a country suspended between eras, caught between the logic of monarchical authority and the pressure of democratic aspiration.

## The Land

Eswatini descends in four parallel strips from mountainous highveld in the west to lowveld savanna in the east, then rises again to the Lubombo plateau along the Mozambican border. The country is roughly the size of New Jersey, just 17,000 square kilometers of varied terrain. Rain falls generously in the highlands—thick grasslands, pine forests, waterfalls—while the lowveld grows hot and dry, suitable for sugar and citrus plantations. Rivers flow eastward: the Komati, the Usutu, the Ngwavuma. Most of Eswatini's 1.2 million people live in the middleveld between extremes, where the capital Mbabane perches in the hills and the administrative center Lobamba sits in the Ezulwini Valley. South Africa nearly surrounds the country; Mozambique provides a narrow eastern exit.

## The History

The Swazi nation emerged in the early nineteenth century when King Sobhuza I consolidated clans fleeing the Mfecane upheavals—Zulu expansion, warfare, displacement. The kingdom survived by strategic maneuvering between Boer republics and British imperialism, playing rivals against each other. Still, Britain established a protectorate in 1906, ruling through indirect control that left traditional structures intact. Unlike many African colonies, Swaziland retained its monarchy and much of its land during colonial rule. Independence came peacefully in 1968, with Sobhuza II—the longest-reigning monarch in recorded history—on the throne.

What followed was the systematic dismantling of democracy. In 1973, Sobhuza suspended the constitution, banned political parties, and assumed absolute power. When he died in 1982 after a sixty-one-year reign, succession intrigues delayed his son's ascension. Mswati III took power in 1986 at eighteen and has ruled ever since, maintaining the system his father established: no parties, no elected government with real authority, royal decree as law. Parliament exists but serves at the king's pleasure. Protests demanding democracy have been met with arrests, tear gas, and live ammunition—most recently in 2021, when security forces killed dozens. The monarchy endures not through consent but through control.

## The Culture

Swazi identity centers on the monarchy and its rituals. The annual Reed Dance—thousands of young women presenting reeds to the Queen Mother—and the Incwala ceremony, where the king eats the first fruits of harvest, bind nation to tradition. These aren't tourist performances but living institutions that organize social life. Polygamy remains royal prerogative—Mswati has fifteen wives—and cultural practice for commoners who can afford it. Christianity coexists with ancestor veneration; most Swazis navigate both worlds without contradiction.

The clan system, or sibongo, still structures identity. Marriage between clan members is taboo; knowing your clan means knowing your place in the social order. Women face legal subordination—many require male consent for basic transactions—though they also hold crucial ritual roles like brewing traditional beer and maintaining homesteads. Language unifies: nearly everyone speaks siSwati, with English as the official second tongue. Extended families, or emabudle, remain the primary social unit, with rural homesteads often housing multiple generations under one patriarch.

The gap between royal opulence and popular poverty grows more conspicuous. While the king builds palaces and maintains a luxury vehicle fleet, most Swazis farm small plots or work for wages barely above subsistence. Yet many still venerate the monarchy as the embodiment of Swazi identity, making opposition feel like cultural betrayal.

## The Challenge

Eswatini faces a democracy crisis it cannot resolve without fundamental change. The king controls the military, police, and courts. Political parties are banned. Elections choose powerless representatives. When thousands took to the streets in 2021 demanding democratic reforms, security forces responded with bullets. The government shut down the internet. Activists disappeared. Nothing changed. The monarchy has no interest in sharing power; the people have no mechanism to compel it.

Beneath this lies the HIV/AIDS catastrophe that has ravaged Eswatini more severely than almost anywhere on earth. At the pandemic's peak, life expectancy collapsed to thirty-two years. One in four adults carries the virus—the world's highest prevalence rate. The epidemic orphaned tens of thousands, devastated the workforce, and overwhelmed medical systems. Aggressive treatment campaigns have improved outcomes, but the damage endures. Meanwhile, unemployment hovers near 40 percent, youth see no future, and the economy depends on sugar exports and South African trade. The kingdom that survived colonialism by preserving tradition now finds that same tradition blocks the transformation survival demands.

## The Contribution

Eswatini has maintained African monarchy as a living institution, not museum piece. For better and worse, it shows what genuine royal authority looks like in the twenty-first century—governance without the mediating institutions democracy requires. The Reed Dance, though sometimes criticized when the king selects new wives from its participants, remains a spectacular assertion of cultural continuity. Traditional crafts—woven baskets, wooden carvings, calabashes, beadwork—are produced not for heritage centers but for daily use.

The kingdom's conservation work has created one of Africa's success stories. Through partnership with conservancies, Eswatini has reintroduced white rhinos in numbers, protected lion populations, and maintained wilderness areas despite limited territory. The country also stands among the few in Africa where most people have access to clean water and basic healthcare, however strained. Its cultural festivals, particularly the Incwala, represent one of the continent's oldest unbroken ceremonial traditions, practiced as law requires rather than tourist spectacle.

## A Moment

In September 2021, a young lawyer named Thulani Maseko stood before journalists in Mbabane, calling for democratic reforms. He had spent two years in prison for contempt of court after criticizing the judiciary's subservience to the throne. Now, amid the largest protests in Eswatini's history, he articulated demands the king refused to acknowledge: constitutional monarchy, elected government, basic freedoms. "We are not fighting against Swazi culture," he said. "We are fighting against dictatorship dressed in cultural garb."

Security forces killed more than seventy people in those protests. The internet went dark. By early 2023, Maseko was dead—shot in his home before his family. No arrests followed. The king built another palace. In January 2024, Swazis went to the polls to elect representatives to a parliament that exists at royal sufferance, can pass no law the king disapproves, and serves mainly to provide democratic window-dressing for absolute rule. Turnout was low. Those who voted knew the outcome before casting ballots: the monarch would remain, the system would continue, and speaking truth would remain dangerous.
