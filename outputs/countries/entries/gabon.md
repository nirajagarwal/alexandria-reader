# Gabon
*Oil-Rich, Forest-Covered, Sparsely Inhabited*

A country the size of Colorado with fewer people than Phoenix. Gabon sits on the equator where Central Africa meets the Atlantic, blessed with oil revenues and cursed by the paradox of prosperity: how wealth concentrates rather than spreads, how a fortunate geography can sustain both ecological wonder and political stagnation. It's France's favored client, Africa's wealthiest per capita, and a place where the forest still dominates human settlement in ways almost nowhere else on the continent can claim.

## The Land

Equatorial rainforest covers eighty-eight percent of the country—a biological treasury second in density only to the Congo Basin. The land rises from a narrow coastal plain into the Crystal Mountains and then descends into the vast interior forests drained by the Ogooué River. The coast receives four meters of rain annually; the interior slightly less but still enough to sustain closed-canopy forest where gorillas, forest elephants, and mandrills move through galleries of light and shadow. Libreville, the capital, perches where the Komo River meets the Atlantic. Most of Gabon's two million people cluster along the coast and rivers—the interior remains almost empty, a green expanse navigable mainly by water. The oil lies offshore, pumped from platforms that sustain the economy without disturbing the forest.

## The History

The Fang, Mpongwe, and other Bantu groups settled these forests over centuries, trading along river networks. Portuguese contact came in the fifteenth century; the French established a trading post at what became Libreville ("Freetown") in 1849 for freed slaves. France absorbed Gabon into French Equatorial Africa in 1910, extracting timber and eventually discovering oil in the 1930s. Independence came in 1960 under Léon M'ba, but France never really left—French troops intervened in 1964 to restore M'ba after a coup. His successor, Omar Bongo, ruled from 1967 until his death in 2009—forty-two years of single-party and then nominal multi-party rule, sustained by oil revenues and French support. His son, Ali Bongo, continued the dynasty until a military coup in 2023. The pattern is consistent: wealth, autocracy, French involvement, minimal democratic development.

## The Culture

French is the official language, but Fang and other languages persist in daily life. The forest shapes cultural memory—masks and ritual objects reflect the density of the green world, the presence of spirits in trees and rivers. Urban life centers on Libreville, where oil money has built a veneer of prosperity: French restaurants, modern apartments, universities. But the gap between elite and ordinary Gabonese is vast. Church attendance is high—Catholic and Protestant congregations coexist with traditional belief systems. Music blends Congolese rumba influences with local rhythms; artists like Pierre-Claver Akendengué have created a distinctive Gabonese sound. Family structures remain strong, but urbanization and oil wealth have strained traditional reciprocity. The elite often educate their children in France; the poor navigate informal economies. Forest communities, though small, maintain old ways of relating to the land. A country of two million contains dozens of ethnic groups; coherence comes more from shared territory than shared identity.

## The Challenge

The oil will run out. Gabon has relied on petroleum for sixty percent of government revenue, ninety percent of exports. Production peaked around 2010 and has declined since. The government knows this, has declared ambitious conservation targets—protecting forest, developing ecotourism. But economic diversification remains aspirational. Infrastructure outside Libreville is poor. Education lags. Corruption is endemic. The August 2023 coup, which ended the Bongo dynasty, came after a disputed election the international community deemed fraudulent. The military junta promised reform and transition to democracy; whether that happens or merely inaugurates another strongman remains uncertain. Meanwhile, a generation has grown up expecting oil wealth to deliver prosperity that never reaches them. Youth unemployment hovers near forty percent. The forest offers carbon credits and biodiversity hotspots, but these don't employ the urban young or fund the schools and hospitals a small population requires. Gabon has wealth but cannot translate it into development.

## The Contribution

Gabon protects rainforest at a scale few African nations can claim. Thirteen national parks cover eleven percent of the territory—Loango, where hippos swim in the ocean and elephants walk beaches; Ivindo, with waterfalls and virgin forest. These aren't empty gestures; Gabon has genuinely preserved habitat critical to species found almost nowhere else. Forest elephants, western lowland gorillas, chimpanzees, mandrills—populations that have collapsed elsewhere persist here. The country sequesters more carbon than it emits, a rare distinction. Artists like Akendengué have brought Gabonese music to global stages. Libreville hosts the African Union's Biodiversity Summit and positions itself as a green leader. The forest itself, simply by persisting, is a contribution—lungs for a continent, sanctuary for creatures that need vast territories. In literature, scholars and writers have explored what it means to be Gabonese in a post-colonial, oil-dependent world; the conversation is smaller than in Nigeria or Senegal, but it exists.

## A Moment

In 2002, President Omar Bongo announced the creation of thirteen national parks, setting aside nearly twelve percent of the country for conservation—an unprecedented commitment in Central Africa. Environmental groups celebrated; ecotourists began arriving. But walk through Libreville's markets or informal neighborhoods and you see the paradox: a country wealthy enough to protect wilderness but unable to provide basic services to its citizens. The parks became a source of national pride and international recognition. Gabon marketed itself as a green destination. Yet most Gabonese never visit the parks—they require permits, guides, money. The forest remains inaccessible to the people whose ancestors lived in it. Oil revenues funded the conservation initiative; when oil declines, will the commitment hold? Bongo, autocrat and conservationist, embodied the contradiction. He enriched his family, stifled democracy, maintained French dependency, and protected more wilderness than democratic Botswana. The parks endure, a green monument to wealth that couldn't be translated into freedom.
