# Zambia
*Copper and Water*

A landlocked plateau shaped by rivers and metals, where independence came peacefully and democracy survived when neighbors fell to tyranny. The story of modern Zambia is inseparable from copper—the red ore that funded liberation, built cities, then failed to deliver the prosperity it promised. Between the Zambezi and the Congo basin, eight million people navigate the gap between resource wealth and endemic poverty, between multiparty democracy and elite capture, between traditional authorities and urban modernity. What remains distinctive is the relative absence of ethnic violence in a region torn by it, and the survival of democratic forms in a neighborhood of strongmen.

## The Land

A high plateau between 1,000 and 1,500 meters, drained by rivers that define its borders: the Zambezi in the south (shared with Zimbabwe), the Kafue and Luangwa within. Victoria Falls—Mosi-oa-Tunya, "the smoke that thunders"—marks where the Zambezi drops 108 meters into a gorge, one of the world's great waterfalls. The Copperbelt runs along the northwestern border with the Democratic Republic of Congo, an arc of mineral deposits that extends both sides of the frontier. Three seasons: cool and dry, hot and dry, then the rains from November to April. Most people live in the central and southern provinces—Lusaka, the capital, and the industrial towns of the Copperbelt. Rural areas remain sparsely populated. Wildlife concentrations in South Luangwa and Kafue national parks rival anywhere in Africa. The land provides water, metals, and potential—but the metals dominate the economy and the imagination.

## The History

The territory took its current form under British colonial rule, carved from land controlled by various Bantu-speaking peoples—Bemba, Tonga, Lozi, Nyanja—whose kingdoms and chieftaincies the British subordinated but did not destroy. Named Northern Rhodesia after Cecil Rhodes, it became a British protectorate in 1924. Copper was discovered in the 1920s; by the 1930s the Copperbelt was producing globally significant quantities. The white settler population remained small compared to Southern Rhodesia (Zimbabwe). Independence came in 1964 under Kenneth Kaunda and his United National Independence Party, achieved through negotiation rather than armed struggle. Kaunda declared Zambia a "one-party participatory democracy" in 1973—an Orwellian formulation that lasted until economic collapse and domestic pressure forced multiparty elections in 1991. Frederick Chiluba won, marking one of Africa's early democratic transitions. Copper nationalization in the 1970s coincided with price crashes; the economy never recovered its early post-independence growth. Democracy survived—messy, imperfect, but functional. Peaceful transfers of power have occurred multiple times, most recently in 2021.

## The Culture

Seventy-three ethnic groups sharing a relatively peaceful coexistence, united more by a common colonial experience than by pre-colonial unity. Bemba dominance in the north and Copperbelt, Tonga strength in the south, Lozi distinctiveness in the west—but ethnic politics have remained muted compared to Kenya or Nigeria. English is the official language; Bemba, Nyanja, Tonga, and Lozi are widely spoken. Christianity is nearly universal, a legacy of intense missionary activity, though traditional beliefs persist beneath the surface. Urbanization rates are among Africa's highest—44% live in cities—creating a distinctive urban culture in Lusaka and the Copperbelt towns. Extended family obligations remain paramount; the nuclear family is more Western ideal than African practice. Gender norms are conservative: polygamy is legal, women's property rights are contested, traditional authorities (mostly male) hold significant power. Music blends Congolese rumba, South African influences, and local rhythms—Zamrock in the 1970s, contemporary gospel and hip-hop now. The national motto, "One Zambia, One Nation," reflects aspiration more than description, but the aspiration has mattered.

## The Challenge

Copper dependence and the failure to diversify. The economy rises and falls with global copper prices; when prices crashed in the 1970s, Zambia's GDP per capita began a decline that lasted decades. Debt accumulated, structural adjustment programs failed, poverty deepened. Recent price increases have not translated into broad prosperity—the mines are now mostly foreign-owned, profits flow out, and the benefits concentrate in elite hands. Sixty percent of the population lives below the poverty line. Corruption is endemic; public resources disappear into private accounts. The education and health systems are underfunded. Climate change threatens the agricultural sector, which employs most people despite contributing little to GDP. Political democracy survives, but economic democracy is absent. The gap between constitutional rights and lived reality is vast. Young people—70% of the population is under 25—face unemployment and limited prospects. The question is whether democracy can deliver material improvement, or whether it will be discarded as a failed experiment.

## The Contribution

Kenneth Kaunda's model of non-racial African socialism influenced liberation movements across southern Africa. Zambia hosted training camps for liberation fighters from Zimbabwe, Namibia, South Africa, Angola—a frontline state that paid economic costs for its solidarity. The peaceful transition to multiparty democracy in 1991 demonstrated that African authoritarianism was not inevitable, that opposition parties could win and incumbents could concede. Zambian music—particularly the Zamrock of the 1970s, bands like WITCH and Amanaz—created a distinctive fusion that is now being rediscovered globally. The survival of traditional authorities alongside modern democratic institutions offers a model of legal pluralism, however imperfect. The Victoria Falls tourism industry has been developed jointly with Zimbabwe, a rare example of transborder cooperation. Perhaps most significantly, Zambia proves that ethnic diversity need not produce ethnic violence—that it is possible, even in a poor country, to build a national identity that transcends tribal loyalties.

## A Moment

October 1991, Lusaka: Frederick Chiluba, a former bus conductor and trade unionist, addresses a crowd after defeating Kenneth Kaunda in Zambia's first multiparty election since independence. Kaunda has governed for twenty-seven years; he created the one-party state, nationalized the copper mines, proclaimed African humanism, supported liberation struggles. Now he has accepted defeat and will hand over power peacefully. Chiluba, short and round where Kaunda is tall and austere, promises to restore democracy and revive the economy. The crowd celebrates—not Kaunda's overthrow but democracy's arrival. It is one of Africa's landmark moments. Within years, Chiluba will prove corrupt, enriching himself and his associates, undermining the institutions he claimed to restore. The 2001 election will be marred by irregularities. But the precedent holds: in Zambia, even bad leaders must face voters. Elections matter, even when rigged. Opposition parties can win, even when the incumbents cheat. The system survives its own abuse. Standing in the Lusaka heat, watching power change hands without violence, Zambians understand they have achieved something rare—a democracy that works, however imperfectly, in a region where most have failed.
