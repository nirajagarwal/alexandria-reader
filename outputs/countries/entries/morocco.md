# Morocco
*Where Africa Meets the Arab World*

Morocco sits at the edge of everything—Africa's northwestern corner, the Atlantic's eastern shore, the Mediterranean's southern gateway. It is where the Arab world bleeds into Africa, where Berber mountains meet coastal plains, where Islam absorbed rather than erased older identities. The country has always been defined by this position: close enough to Europe to be influenced, far enough to remain distinct, African enough to be continental, Arab enough to claim a seat in the Middle East's conversations. It is a kingdom that has survived by adapting, a society that has preserved tradition by making room for modernity.

## The Land

Morocco's geography is strikingly diverse. The Rif and Atlas mountain ranges cut across the country like a spine, with the High Atlas reaching above 4,000 meters. To the north, a Mediterranean coastline; to the west, the Atlantic. Between the mountains and the Sahara lies fertile agricultural land where most Moroccans live. The south dissolves into the desert—the Western Sahara, which Morocco claims and occupies despite international dispute. Casablanca anchors the Atlantic coast, a sprawling economic hub. Marrakech sits at the foothills of the Atlas, a tourist magnet. Fez and Meknes preserve medieval cities largely intact. The mountains have historically sheltered Berber communities from coastal powers; the coast has connected Morocco to Mediterranean trade and European influence.

## The History

Morocco's indigenous Berbers met Arab conquest in the 7th century but were never entirely Arabized. The country became a center of Islamic learning—the University of al-Qarawiyyin in Fez, founded in 859, is among the world's oldest continuously operating universities. From the 11th to the 17th centuries, Moroccan dynasties—the Almoravids, Almohads, Marinids—ruled empires stretching into Spain and across North Africa. European colonialism arrived late: France and Spain divided Morocco in 1912. Resistance never stopped. Moroccan troops fought for France in both World Wars while independence movements organized at home. Independence came in 1956 under King Mohammed V, who became a national hero. His son Hassan II ruled for 38 years (1961-1999) with an iron fist, crushing dissent during the "Years of Lead." Hassan's son, Mohammed VI, has governed since 1999, liberalizing slowly while preserving monarchical power. The 2011 Arab Spring brought protests; the king responded with a new constitution and limited reforms.

## The Culture

Morocco is officially Arab but fundamentally Berber—at least 40% of the population speaks Berber languages, which gained official status in 2011. Islam is the state religion, practiced by 99% of the population, mostly Sunni with Sufi influences. Moroccan Islam has historically been tolerant, syncretistic, incorporating pre-Islamic practices. Family and hospitality are central social values. Gender roles are traditional but changing—women gained legal rights in 2004 but still face economic and social constraints. Moroccan cuisine—tagines, couscous, mint tea—is celebrated globally. The medinas (old cities) of Fez, Marrakech, and elsewhere preserve centuries-old urban patterns: winding alleys, hidden courtyards, markets organized by craft. Morocco has always been a crossroads—African, Arab, Mediterranean, European influences blend without dissolving into uniformity. French is widely spoken; modernity and tradition coexist, sometimes awkwardly, in daily life.

## The Challenge

Morocco faces the dilemma of reform without rupture. King Mohammed VI has liberalized the economy and society more than his father, but ultimate power remains with the monarchy. Corruption is endemic. Youth unemployment is high, driving emigration to Europe. The Rif protests of 2016-2017 revealed deep dissatisfaction in marginalized regions—the state responded with arrests and repression. The Western Sahara remains a frozen conflict: Morocco controls the territory, which it annexed after Spain's 1975 withdrawal, but the Polisario Front demands independence. The conflict drains resources and isolates Morocco diplomatically in Africa. Economic growth has been steady but hasn't trickled down—inequality is stark, rural poverty widespread. The country must balance Islamic identity with secular modernity, monarchical stability with democratic aspirations, regional development with centralized control. Climate change threatens agriculture and water supplies. Morocco is stable compared to its neighbors, but stability built on repression is fragile.

## The Contribution

Morocco has given the world a model of religious coexistence—Jews lived in Morocco for two millennia, and while most emigrated to Israel after 1948, Morocco still celebrates its Jewish heritage. Moroccan cities preserve Islamic architecture and urban planning unmatched in the Arab world—the medinas are not museums but living cities. The country has been a bridge between Europe and Africa, mediating migration, trade, and political relations. Moroccan literature—Tahar Ben Jelloun, Mohamed Choukri—explores post-colonial identity with unflinching honesty. The country pioneered renewable energy in Africa; the Noor Ouarzazate solar complex is the world's largest concentrated solar power plant. Morocco's diplomatic agility—maintaining ties with Israel while supporting Palestinians, partnering with Europe while asserting African identity—offers lessons in navigating competing allegiances. Moroccan music, particularly Gnawa and Chaabi, blends African, Arab, and Berber traditions into something distinctive.

## A Moment

In Fez's Chouara tannery, men still dye leather in stone vessels filled with pigeon dung, cow urine, and natural dyes—the process unchanged for a thousand years. Tourists watch from balconies above, overwhelmed by the smell. The tanners work barefoot in the vats, their legs stained permanent colors—yellow from saffron, red from poppy, brown from henna. It is backbreaking, unhealthy work. Sons inherit jobs from fathers. The leather produced is sold in the souks below, turned into bags and shoes and jackets that tourists buy, often unaware of the labor behind them. The tannery embodies Morocco's tension: it is picturesque tradition, it is tourist attraction, it is economic exploitation, it is genuine craft, it is environmental disaster (the dyes and waste pollute the river). The men dye the leather because their families need income, because tourists want "authentic" Morocco, because modernity hasn't reached this pocket of the medina. The state has tried to relocate the tanneries, but the tanners resist—this is their livelihood, their heritage. So the work continues, medieval methods in a modern city, beauty and suffering mixed in the same vat.
