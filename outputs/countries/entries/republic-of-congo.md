# Republic of the Congo
*The Other Congo*

Not to be confused with its massive neighbor across the river, the Republic of the Congo occupies a smaller but still substantial stretch of Central African rainforest. Once a French colony, it has spent its independence oscillating between Marxist-Leninist rhetoric and petro-state capitalism, between military strongmen and attempted democracy. The Congo River and its tributaries define it—both the geography and the economy. Oil money flows through Brazzaville while most Congolese remain poor. It is a country perpetually in the shadow of its namesake, struggling to forge a distinct identity from colonial legacy, resource dependence, and recurrent violence.

**The Land**

The Congo River forms the country's eastern and southern border, separating it from the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Dense equatorial rainforest covers much of the north—part of the Congo Basin, second only to the Amazon. The landscape rises from coastal plains through plateaus to the Mayombe Mountains in the southwest. Two-thirds of the territory remains forested, though logging threatens the ecosystem. Most of the 5.5 million people live in the south, concentrated in Brazzaville and Pointe-Noire, the port city on the Atlantic coast. The Sangha River in the north flows through some of Africa's most pristine wilderness. The climate is hot and humid year-round, with heavy rains. Oil lies offshore and in coastal reserves—the resource that has shaped modern Congolese politics and economics, for better and decidedly for worse.

**The History**

The Kingdom of Kongo controlled parts of the region when Portuguese explorers arrived in the late fifteenth century. The French colonized the territory in the 1880s, calling it Middle Congo, part of French Equatorial Africa. Colonial rule was brutal—forced labor on the Congo-Ocean Railway killed thousands in the 1920s and 1930s. Independence came in 1960 under Fulbert Youlou, a Catholic priest turned politician. He lasted three years before mass protests forced him out—the "Three Glorious Days" of August 1963.

What followed was decades of instability. Alphonse Massamba-Débat embraced scientific socialism. Marien Ngouabi took power in 1968, renaming the country the People's Republic of the Congo and aligning with the Soviet Union and China. He was assassinated in 1977. Denis Sassou Nguesso, a military officer from the north, took power in 1979 and maintained Marxist-Leninist orthodoxy until the Soviet collapse forced a pivot. Multiparty elections in 1992 brought Pascal Lissouba to power. Civil war erupted in 1997—militia battles in Brazzaville killed thousands. Sassou Nguesso retook power with Angolan military support and has ruled since, winning elections of questionable legitimacy. Oil revenue sustains the system.

**The Culture**

The Republic of the Congo is ethnically diverse—Kongo, Téké, Mbochi, and dozens of smaller groups, each with distinct languages and traditions. French remains the official language, the colonial inheritance. Lingala and Kituba serve as lingua francas. Christianity dominates, especially Catholicism, often blended with traditional beliefs. Kimbanguism, an African Christian movement founded in the neighboring DRC, has many followers.

Congolese rumba—soukous—emerged in Brazzaville and Kinshasa in the mid-twentieth century, blending Cuban rhythms with Congolese guitar styles. The music scene was vibrant in the 1960s and 1970s, producing stars like Franklin Boukaka, who sang in Kikongo and criticized the government before dying under mysterious circumstances in 1972. La Sape (Société des Ambianceurs et des Personnes Élégantes) is a Brazzaville and Kinshasa subculture where working-class men dress in expensive European designer clothes—a form of aesthetic resistance and dignity in contexts of poverty and political oppression.

The society remains deeply divided by region and ethnicity—northern groups tend to support Sassou Nguesso; southern groups often oppose him. Patronage networks and ethnic loyalty shape politics more than ideology. The gap between the oil-enriched elite and the poor majority defines daily life. Brazzaville feels both cosmopolitan and provincial, shadowed by its larger twin across the river.

**The Challenge**

Oil wealth has not translated into broad prosperity. Despite billions in revenue, most Congolese live in poverty, lacking access to reliable electricity, clean water, or quality healthcare. Corruption siphons off public funds—Sassou Nguesso and his family reportedly own vast properties abroad while schools crumble and hospitals lack basic supplies. The economy depends almost entirely on oil exports, making it vulnerable to price fluctuations. When oil prices collapsed in 2014-2016, the country faced severe fiscal crisis.

Political power remains concentrated in the hands of Sassou Nguesso, now in his eighties, who has ruled for most of the past forty-five years. A 2015 constitutional referendum removed presidential age and term limits, allowing him to run again. Elections are neither free nor fair. Opposition figures face harassment and exile. Civil society operates under constraint. The Pool region, south of Brazzaville, saw violent conflict as recently as 2016-2017 between government forces and Pastor Ntumi's "Ninja" militia.

Environmental degradation accelerates as logging companies, often Chinese-owned, clear rainforest. Indigenous communities lose their land. Climate change threatens the Congo Basin's stability.

**The Contribution**

The Republic of the Congo has given the world soukous music—infectious guitar-driven rhythms that influenced African popular music across the continent. The genre's energy and innovation remain a testament to Congolese creativity under difficult circumstances.

La Sape represents a unique cultural practice—men known as sapeurs invest everything in style, turning fashion into art and resistance. Photographers and documentarians have captured this phenomenon, bringing international attention to Brazzaville's aesthetic defiance.

The country harbors significant biodiversity—lowland gorillas, forest elephants, chimpanzees—in parks like Odzala-Kokoua and Nouabalé-Ndoki. Conservation efforts, though underfunded, protect ecosystems critical to global climate stability.

Emmanuel Dongala's novels, especially *Photo of the Group at the River* and *Johnny Mad Dog*, offer unflinching portrayals of African political violence and survival. Henri Lopes, a novelist and diplomat, has explored postcolonial identity and the absurdities of power in works like *The Laughing Cry*. Their writing refuses easy consolations or Western expectations.

**A Moment**

In June 1997, as militias loyal to Pascal Lissouba and Denis Sassou Nguesso fought for control of Brazzaville, residents found themselves trapped in their homes, caught between rocket fire and roving gunmen. The city's infrastructure collapsed—no electricity, no water, bodies in the streets. Families huddled in darkness, not knowing which faction controlled their neighborhood. The Cathedral of the Sacred Heart, Brazzaville's most recognizable landmark, was shelled. Thousands fled across the river to Kinshasa, itself hardly a refuge.

A journalist described a woman carrying a cooking pot and a small child, walking through abandoned streets looking for water. She passed burned-out cars and militia checkpoints, risking sniper fire for a chance to fill the pot at a public tap that might still work. When she reached it, the tap was dry. She sat down on the curb, not crying, not speaking, just sitting with the empty pot.

The war lasted four months and killed thousands. Sassou Nguesso retook power with Angolan troops and armor. The fighting destroyed much of Brazzaville's infrastructure, already fragile. The woman with the pot—no one recorded her name—represents the civilians who endure while strongmen fight for oil revenue. The postwar government rebuilt government buildings and presidential palaces. Many neighborhoods remain unreconstructed. The pot stayed empty.
