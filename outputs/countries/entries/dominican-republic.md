# Dominican Republic
*Hispaniola's Complicated Half*

The eastern two-thirds of Hispaniola, sharing the Caribbean's second-largest island with Haiti, forged an identity defined less by what it is than by what it is not. Independence came not from a European power but from Haiti itself in 1844, and that foundational severance—linguistic, racial, cultural, psychological—continues to shape the nation. It has built a successful tourism economy while wrestling with profound inequality, constructed a baseball powerhouse while exporting its best talent, and maintained a fractious democracy while never fully escaping strongman politics. The contradiction runs deep: a majority-mixed-race nation that often denies its African heritage, a Spanish-speaking culture in a largely Anglophone and Francophone Caribbean, an increasingly prosperous country shadowed by its impoverished neighbor.

## The Land

The island Columbus called La Española offers extraordinary topography for a Caribbean nation. Pico Duarte rises to 10,164 feet—the Caribbean's highest peak—anchoring the Cordillera Central that runs like a spine through the country's interior. The Cibao Valley in the north grows tobacco and cacao. The southeast holds sugarcane country and resort beaches. Four major mountain ranges create distinct climate zones—pine forests at elevation, tropical lowlands along the coasts, semi-arid scrubland in the southwest near Lake Enriquillo, the Caribbean's lowest point at 151 feet below sea level. Rivers run short but plentiful. The Atlantic coast faces hurricane paths; September 1998's Hurricane Georges killed hundreds and revealed infrastructure fragility. Most Dominicans live along the coasts or in the Cibao, leaving the mountainous interior sparsely populated. The border with Haiti follows rough terrain, contested and porous.

## The History

Columbus's second voyage in 1493 established Santo Domingo, the first European city in the Americas, which retains colonial architecture in its Zona Colonial. Spain's attention soon shifted to Mexico and Peru, leaving Hispaniola a backwater. The western third fell to French control by 1697—Haiti. The Spanish east watched nervously as Haiti's slave revolt succeeded in 1804, then briefly unified under Haitian rule from 1822 to 1844. Juan Pablo Duarte led independence, establishing the Dominican Republic on February 27, 1844. What followed was chaos: competing caudillos, brief return to Spanish rule (1861-1865), chronic debt, and U.S. military occupation from 1916 to 1924. Rafael Trujillo's dictatorship (1930-1961) brought order through terror, modernization through megalomania, and the 1937 Parsley Massacre—the murder of perhaps 20,000 Haitians and Haitian Dominicans. His assassination opened decades of instability: civil war in 1965, another U.S. invasion, then Joaquín Balaguer's twelve-year presidency (1966-1978). Democracy arrived fitfully in the 1980s and has survived since, though never cleanly.

## The Culture

Spanish colonialism blended with Taíno indigenous influence and African heritage to create Dominican culture, though the nation has long been uncomfortable acknowledging the African contribution. Most Dominicans are mixed-race—*mulato* or *indio* in local terminology—yet anti-Haitian prejudice runs deep, and darker skin carries social stigma. The Catholic Church retains influence despite growing Protestant conversion. Merengue music, with its African-derived rhythms and European instruments, became national identity during Trujillo's regime; bachata emerged from the countryside as music of heartbreak and poverty, scorned by elites until Juan Luis Guerra made it respectable in the 1990s. Baseball is passion and export industry—more than 700 Dominicans have played in Major Leagues, more per capita than anywhere. Family networks matter intensely; extended families share resources and housing. Machismo remains strong despite women's increasing economic participation. Spanish dominicano carries distinct intonation and vocabulary. The merengue típico accordion and tambora drum at rural fiestas, the colonial zona in Santo Domingo hosting evening strolls, the colmado corner stores as social centers—these texture daily life.

## The Challenge

The relationship with Haiti—geographic, economic, racial, psychological—defines the Dominican predicament. The border remains contested; migration flows steadily east. The 2013 Constitutional Court ruling retroactively stripped citizenship from children of undocumented immigrants, creating statelessness for thousands of Haitian-descended Dominicans. Anti-Haitian racism serves political purposes, deflecting attention from inequality and elite corruption. Economic growth since the 1990s, driven by tourism, remittances, and free trade zones, has lifted GDP but distributed gains unevenly. Tourism development along the east coast (Punta Cana, La Romana) creates resort enclaves disconnected from Dominican life. Remittances from the U.S. diaspora—more than two million Dominicans live abroad—prop up families but signal economic exodus. Corruption remains endemic; politicians cycle through scandals without accountability. Infrastructure lags despite growth. The education system produces graduates without skills. The country remains vulnerable to hurricanes and climate change, with limited adaptive capacity. And beneath everything runs the question the nation has never resolved: what does it mean to be Dominican rather than Haitian?

## The Contribution

Baseball talent flows from Dominican academies to major leagues—from Juan Marichal to Pedro Martínez to Vladimir Guerrero to Albert Pujols. The sport is Dominican cultural expression and economic lifeline. Juan Luis Guerra brought bachata and merengue to global audiences while using success to critique inequality. Oscar de la Renta dressed American first ladies and Hollywood stars, bringing Dominican aesthetics to fashion. Julia Alvarez's novels (*How the García Girls Lost Their Accents*, *In the Time of the Butterflies*) gave literary voice to Dominican-American experience and Trujillo-era suffering. Junot Díaz's Pulitzer Prize-winning *The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao* combined Dominican history with science fiction and diaspora identity. The country has preserved significant colonial architecture—Santo Domingo's Zona Colonial is a UNESCO World Heritage site, the oldest continuously inhabited European settlement in the Americas. Dominican adaptability in creating hybrid musical forms, from merengue to bachata to dembow, demonstrates cultural vitality. The cigar industry continues traditional craft at high quality. The country offers a case study in middle-income development's possibilities and limits.

## A Moment

September 1965, Santo Domingo: U.S. Marines land at Haina, establishing a security corridor through the city as civil war rages. The intervention—eventually involving 42,000 troops—comes after reformist forces attempt to restore Juan Bosch, the democratically elected president overthrown by military coup two years earlier. President Lyndon Johnson fears "another Cuba" and acts to prevent leftist victory. Dominican civilians watch foreign troops patrol their streets for the second time in fifty years. The intervention crushes the constitutionalist movement, installs Joaquín Balaguer, and entrenches the conservative order for another generation. But it also radicalizes many Dominicans, sending them into exile or opposition. Among them are the families who will emigrate to New York, establishing the Dominican diaspora that transforms Washington Heights into Little DR, creating the transnational identity that defines modern Dominican experience. The country learns again what it learned in 1916: sovereignty is conditional, democracy is fragile, and superpowers do as they please. The lesson shapes political culture—cynicism about promises, pragmatism about power, ambivalence about the United States despite economic dependence. In that moment of foreign troops and domestic conflict, the Dominican Republic of the late twentieth century takes shape.
