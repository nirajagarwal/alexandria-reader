# Togo
*The Sliver State*

A narrow strip pressed between Ghana and Benin, Togo extends barely sixty kilometers at its widest point but stretches over five hundred kilometers from the Atlantic to the Sahel. This geographic improbability—the result of colonial cartography at its most arbitrary—created a nation that is less a coherent territory than a corridor connecting ecosystems. The coast, the forest, the savanna: Togo contains them all in miniature, a cross-section of West Africa compressed into one of the continent's smallest countries. What it lacks in size it has never compensated for in stability.

## The Land

Togo runs north-south like a dagger thrust inland from the Gulf of Guinea. The southern coast offers lagoons and coconut palms; move north and you climb into the Togo Mountains, green highlands that give the country its only real geographic distinction. Further north still, the land flattens into savanna, growing drier as it approaches Burkina Faso. Lake Togo, despite the name, is a lagoon separated from the ocean by a sandbar. The capital, Lomé, sprawls along the coast at the western edge of the country, nearly touching Ghana. Most Togolese live in the south, where the land is more forgiving. The north, with its cotton fields and subsistence farms, feels like a different country entirely—which, before German colonization, it essentially was.

## The History

Germany claimed Togoland in 1884, establishing what it considered its model African colony—orderly, profitable, and relatively compact. World War I ended that experiment. The League of Nations split the territory between France and Britain; the British portion eventually joined Ghana, while French Togoland became the modern state. Independence came in 1960, but not stability. Sylvanus Olympio, the first president, was assassinated in 1963 in Africa's first post-independence coup—gunned down outside the American embassy gates he was trying to reach. The soldiers who killed him installed Gnassingbé Eyadéma, who seized full power in 1967 and ruled for thirty-eight years, one of Africa's longest-running dictatorships. When Eyadéma died in 2005, the military installed his son, Faure Gnassingbé, who rules still. The 2005 election that "legitimized" this succession killed hundreds in protests. Togo has known only two families in power since independence, and only the Gnassingbés since 1967.

## The Culture

The Ewe dominate the south, the Kabye the north—a division that maps perfectly onto the political fault lines, as Eyadéma and his son are Kabye ruling over a majority-Ewe population. Vodun (voodoo) originated in this region, shared with Benin, and remains a living practice despite Christianity's spread. The fetish markets of Lomé sell monkey heads and chameleon skins for rituals that blend ancestor veneration with protective magic. Women dominate market trade—the Nana Benz, named for the Mercedes-Benzes they drove, built fortunes on cloth dealing in the 1960s and '70s, becoming some of West Africa's first female business magnates. Football (soccer) unites the country more than any political institution; Emmanuel Adebayor became a national icon playing for Arsenal and Real Madrid. Family structures remain extended and collective, though urbanization and poverty strain the old patterns. French is official, but most speak Ewe or Kabye at home, and code-switching defines daily life.

## The Challenge

Togo remains what it has always been: a dictatorship with democratic window dressing. Faure Gnassingbé has won every election since 2005, each one contested, each result predetermined. Constitutional term limits were removed in 2019, ensuring the dynasty continues. Opposition parties exist but cannot win; protests are met with force; journalists who challenge the government face harassment or worse. The economy depends heavily on phosphate mining and agriculture, but corruption siphons wealth to the ruling family and their allies. Youth unemployment is endemic, driving emigration. The north-south divide persists—the Kabye minority maintains power through military control, breeding resentment in the south. Political reform efforts have stalled repeatedly; the regime offers just enough liberalization to satisfy international donors, then pulls back. The fundamental question remains unanswered: can Togo transition from dynastic rule to genuine democracy without violence?

## The Contribution

Togo gave the world vodun's philosophical foundation—the understanding that the physical and spiritual realms interpenetrate constantly, that ancestors remain active presences, that magic is not supernatural but natural. This cosmology traveled with enslaved peoples to Haiti, Brazil, and the American South, where it evolved into vodou, candomblé, and hoodoo. Architecturally, the Batammariba people in the north built the Takienta—mud tower-houses that are both fortresses and symbols of cosmic order, recognized as UNESCO World Heritage sites. In literature, Togo produced Kossi Efoui, whose plays and novels examine postcolonial identity with surgical precision. The country's traditional cloth-weaving techniques, particularly Ewe kente, influenced textile arts across the region. Musically, Togo contributed to the development of afrobeat and highlife, though it never achieved the international recognition of Nigerian or Ghanaian musicians. Small contributions, perhaps, but real ones.

## A Moment

January 24, 1963: President Sylvanus Olympio hears the gunshots and knows what they mean. He runs from his residence toward the American embassy, two hundred meters away, hoping for asylum. He doesn't make it. The soldiers—former French colonial troops angry about being denied integration into Togo's new army—shoot him down at the embassy gates. He dies in his pajamas on the street. The American ambassador, sleeping inside, hears nothing. The coup takes less than an hour. Olympio's body lies there until morning, the first African president killed by his own military. His brother-in-law will identify the corpse. The soldiers who killed him will not face justice; one of them, Étienne Eyadéma (who later changes his name to Gnassingbé), will seize power four years later and rule until 2005, when his son takes over. The dynasty that began with those bullets has never ended. The gates where Olympio fell still stand, unmarked, ordinary metal in a capital that has learned not to remember too loudly.
