# Mozambique
*Coastal Resilience*

A long sliver of southeastern Africa facing the Indian Ocean, Mozambique stretches nearly 1,800 miles along the coast—one of Africa's poorest nations, repeatedly battered by natural disasters and civil war, yet somehow still standing. Portuguese is the official language in a country where most speak Bantu languages at home. The Zambezi River cuts it nearly in half. This is a place that has endured almost everything—colonialism, Marxist revolution, devastating civil war, floods, cyclones, droughts—and refuses to disappear.

**The Land**

Mozambique is defined by its coastline and its rivers. The Indian Ocean shapes everything—trade routes, culture, climate, vulnerability. The Zambezi, Limpopo, and Save rivers run from the interior highlands to the coast, creating fertile valleys and seasonal flooding. The north is mountainous and forested; the south transitions to coastal plains and savanna. Cyclones hammer the coast with brutal regularity—Idai in 2019 killed over a thousand people and displaced hundreds of thousands. The soil is rich where water reaches it; drought plagues the interior. Most of the population clusters in the coastal lowlands and river valleys. The land provides fish, cashews, timber, natural gas deposits offshore—but it also kills, regularly, through storm and flood.

**The History**

Arab and Swahili traders established coastal settlements centuries before the Portuguese arrived in 1498. Vasco da Gama stopped at what is now Mozambique Island; Portugal claimed the entire coast and held it, brutally, for nearly five centuries. The colonial economy ran on forced labor and plantation agriculture. Portugal was the last European power to decolonize Africa—only after its own 1974 revolution did Mozambique achieve independence in 1975 under FRELIMO, led by Samora Machel. The new Marxist government nationalized property, antagonized white settlers and traditional authorities, and immediately faced RENAMO, a rebel movement backed by Rhodesia and South Africa during the Cold War. The civil war lasted from 1977 to 1992, killed perhaps a million people, displaced millions more, and destroyed most infrastructure. Peace came in 1992; multiparty elections followed. Joaquim Chissano led reconstruction; Armando Guebuza presided over economic growth fueled by foreign investment and coal discoveries. Filipe Nyusi has faced an Islamist insurgency in the north since 2017. The country has known peace for barely three decades.

**The Culture**

Mozambique contains over forty ethnic groups; the Makua in the north are the largest. Portuguese functions as the lingua franca among the educated, but most speak Makonde, Sena, Tsonga, or other Bantu languages at home. The coast reflects centuries of Indian Ocean trade—Arab and Swahili influences in architecture, food, Islam mixed with Christianity and traditional beliefs. Music blends African rhythms with Portuguese fado, Brazilian influences, marrabenta—the national sound, born in Maputo's suburbs. The writer Mia Couto crafts magical realist fiction in Portuguese infused with Mozambican idioms and invention. Family networks remain central; extended kin provide social security where the state cannot. Women work the fields while men often migrate for labor; matrilineal inheritance survives in some northern groups. Urban youth navigate between traditional expectations and global culture. The culture values endurance—you survive what comes.

**The Challenge**

Mozambique remains desperately poor—GDP per capita hovers around $500, among the lowest on Earth. Corruption siphons off foreign aid and resource revenues; a hidden debt scandal in 2016 revealed the government had secretly borrowed over $2 billion for phantom projects, triggering economic crisis. The Islamist insurgency in Cabo Delgado province, linked to ISIS, has killed thousands and displaced nearly a million since 2017, threatening the massive natural gas projects that represent the country's best economic hope. Climate change intensifies cyclones and droughts; floods destroy crops and infrastructure with increasing frequency. Half the population lacks access to clean water; malnutrition is endemic; life expectancy barely reaches sixty. The government controls the political system but struggles to deliver services or security. Mozambique has resources—gas, coal, arable land, tourism potential—but translating potential into shared prosperity has proved elusive for fifty years.

**The Contribution**

Mozambique gave Africa one of its most effective liberation movements—FRELIMO's war against Portugal inspired other colonies and helped topple the Portuguese dictatorship itself. The country has produced distinctive art: the Makonde sculptures, abstract and spiritual, carved from ebony. Mia Couto's novels reimagine what Portuguese can be when rooted in African soil and experience. Mozambican music—marrabenta, pandza—influenced regional sounds across southern Africa. The country's struggle to build peace after brutal civil war offers lessons, however incomplete, in reconciliation and reconstruction. More modestly, Mozambique reminds the world that poverty and disaster do not erase dignity or culture, that people endure what seems unendurable and still make music.

**A Moment**

In April 2019, Cyclone Idai struck Beira, Mozambique's second city, with winds exceeding 120 miles per hour. The storm destroyed ninety percent of the city—roofs ripped off, power lines down, neighborhoods submerged. Beira effectively ceased to exist as a functioning city. Then the Búzi, Pungwe, and Save rivers flooded simultaneously, inundating vast areas inland. From the air, central Mozambique looked like an inland sea dotted with treetops and roof peaks. Bodies floated in the water; survivors clung to trees for days waiting for rescue. International aid arrived slowly; the government lacked resources to respond at scale. Yet within weeks, people were rebuilding—clearing mud, salvaging materials, replanting. Markets reopened in the ruins. This is Mozambique's reality: catastrophe is regular, help is limited, you rebuild anyway because there's no choice. The cyclone revealed both the country's vulnerability and its stubborn refusal to surrender. Two years later, Cyclone Gombe hit. They're rebuilding again.
