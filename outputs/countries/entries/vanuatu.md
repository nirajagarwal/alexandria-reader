# Vanuatu
*Kastom and Cargo*

Eighty-three islands scattered across the southwest Pacific, where the world's highest linguistic diversity meets one of its newest nations. Vanuatu—the name means "Our Land Forever" in several local languages—emerged from dual colonial rule in 1980, inheriting French and English administration, Presbyterian and Catholic missions, and a bewildering political geography that still shapes its present. The archipelago stretches across 1,300 kilometers of ocean, from volcanic peaks to coral atolls, binding together Melanesian peoples who speak more than one hundred languages among 300,000 people. The challenge is holding together what geography and history have fragmented.

## The Land

A volcanic arc rising from the Pacific Ring of Fire, where active volcanoes smolder alongside beaches of black sand. Mount Yasur on Tanna has erupted continuously for centuries, its red glow visible from the sea—early European sailors called it the "Lighthouse of the Pacific." The islands range from mountainous Espiritu Santo, largest at 3,900 square kilometers, to tiny coral outcrops barely visible at high tide. Tropical rainforest covers the interior slopes. Cyclones regularly devastate the islands—Cyclone Pam in 2015 destroyed 90% of Port Vila's structures. The ocean provides: fish, trade routes, and increasingly, rising waters that threaten coastal villages. Most ni-Vanuatu still live in rural areas, practicing subsistence agriculture on volcanic soil enriched by ash.

## The History

Melanesian peoples settled these islands three thousand years ago, developing societies organized around kinship, rank, and elaborate ceremonial exchange. Europeans arrived seeking sandalwood in the 1820s, followed by missionaries who translated the Bible into dozens of local languages while dismantling traditional practices. By 1906, Britain and France established a unique "condominium"—joint colonial rule that created parallel administrations, school systems, legal codes, and languages, but no unified government. Ni-Vanuatu called it the "Pandemonium." Independence came in 1980 after a brief rebellion on Espirtu Santo, backed by French settlers and American libertarians seeking to establish a tax haven. Father Walter Lini, an Anglican priest, became the first prime minister, navigating between kastom (traditional custom) and modernity, between Francophone and Anglophone populations, between chiefly authority and parliamentary democracy. The experiment in dual-language democracy continues, fragile but functioning.

## The Culture

Over one hundred indigenous languages persist in a population smaller than most American cities—the world's highest per capita linguistic diversity. Bislama, a creole blending English, French, and local vocabularies, serves as the lingua franca: "Mi lukim yu" means "I see you"; a helicopter is a "mixmaster belong Jesus Christ." Traditional grade-taking ceremonies persist on some islands, where men earn rank through elaborate pig exchanges and ceremonial displays. Kava—the mildly narcotic root drink—remains central to social life; men gather at nakamals (kava bars) at dusk for quiet conversation. Christianity dominates outwardly, but ancestral practices endure beneath: land disputes are settled by custom, not courts; kastom marriages precede church ceremonies. The John Frum cargo cult on Tanna still awaits an American messiah figure they believe will return with material abundance—a theological response to colonial inequality. Ni-Vanuatu navigate multiple identities: island, language group, religious affiliation, and the fragile national whole.

## The Challenge

Political instability has produced fourteen prime ministers since 2004, as coalition governments collapse through no-confidence votes and defections. The Westminster parliamentary system, designed for stable two-party democracies, proves ill-suited to Vanuatu's fragmented politics, where loyalties align with islands, language groups, and personal networks rather than ideological platforms. Corruption scandals regularly unseat leaders. Climate change threatens existentially: rising seas, intensifying cyclones, coral bleaching, saltwater intrusion into freshwater sources. Some islands may become uninhabitable within decades. The economy depends precariously on tourism, offshore financial services (selling citizenship and banking secrecy), and foreign aid. Educational outcomes remain poor outside Port Vila. Young people leave for Australia and New Zealand, seeking opportunities the islands cannot provide. The tension between preserving kastom and building a modern economy remains unresolved—and perhaps unresolvable.

## The Contribution

Vanuatu pioneered the concept of "climate justice," becoming a leading voice for small island developing states facing climate catastrophe they did nothing to cause. Foreign Minister Ralph Regenvanu has articulated the moral case at the United Nations with unusual clarity: those who caused the crisis must compensate those who will drown because of it. The country banned plastic bags nationwide in 2018 and banned disposable diapers in 2019—small gestures that demonstrate seriousness. Land diving on Pentecost Island—men leaping from wooden towers with vines tied to their ankles—inspired modern bungee jumping. Linguistically, Vanuatu offers a living laboratory of language diversity, preservation, and evolution. And the kastom governance systems, though under pressure, demonstrate alternatives to Western models: consensus-based decision-making, restorative rather than punitive justice, collective rather than individual land ownership.

## A Moment

On Tanna in the 1950s, American servicemen built an airstrip during World War II, then left behind material wealth unlike anything islanders had seen—jeeps, Coca-Cola, cigarettes, radios. The John Frum movement emerged, prophesying that a messianic figure named John would return with cargo, bringing prosperity and expelling the missionaries who had banned kava, dancing, and polygamy. Followers abandoned Christianity, destroyed their money, and built symbolic airstrips from bamboo to summon the planes back. Anthropologists dismissed it as primitive misunderstanding. But the movement survives seventy years later, not because ni-Vanuatu are naive, but because it articulates a profound critique: Why should some people have abundance while others have nothing? Why must we abandon our ways to receive what others take for granted? Every February 15, on John Frum Day, devotees raise the American flag and march in military formation, awaiting cargo that won't come—but enacting a question that won't fade: What does the world owe those it has left behind?
