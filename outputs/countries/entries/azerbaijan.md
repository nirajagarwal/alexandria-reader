# Azerbaijan
*Fire and Oil*

Azerbaijan sits where empires collide—Persia and Russia, Islam and Orthodoxy, Asia and Europe. It is the land of fire, where natural gas seeps through rock and burns on hillsides, and of oil, which made Baku wealthy before petroleum enriched Texas. It is also the land of contested borders, authoritarian stability, and a diaspora that remembers Karabakh as home. For a century it has been trying to convert fossil wealth into lasting nationhood.

## The Land

The country sits on the western shore of the Caspian Sea, pinched between the Greater Caucasus Mountains to the north and the Lesser Caucasus to the west. Much of the land is semi-arid, dropping to below sea level near the Caspian. The Kura River runs through the center, creating fertile lowlands where cotton and fruit grow. But the real geography is subterranean—vast reserves of oil and natural gas that have made this small nation disproportionately wealthy and strategically important.

The climate is continental and dry. Baku, the capital, sits on a windswept peninsula where fire temples once burned with natural gas. The exclave of Nakhchivan lies separated from the rest of Azerbaijan by Armenian territory, a geographic absurdity born of Soviet cartography.

## The History

Persia ruled here for centuries, leaving the Shia Islam that still dominates. Russians arrived in the early 19th century, taking the region from Qajar Iran and industrializing around Baku's oil. By 1900, Baku produced half the world's petroleum. The Rothschilds and Nobels built fortunes here; Stalin organized strikes.

When the Russian Empire collapsed, Azerbaijan declared independence in 1918—the first Muslim-majority democracy. It lasted two years before the Red Army invaded. As a Soviet republic, Azerbaijan became the USSR's oil depot and Moscow's window on the Islamic world. The Nagorno-Karabakh region, populated by Armenians, was placed under Azerbaijani administration—a decision that would haunt both nations.

Independence came again in 1991. Within years, Armenia and Karabakh Armenians seized not just Nagorno-Karabakh but seven surrounding districts, displacing 700,000 Azerbaijanis. A ceasefire in 1994 left Armenia occupying roughly 20% of Azerbaijan's territory. The Aliyev dynasty—Heydar, then his son Ilham—has ruled since 1993, maintaining power through oil revenues, controlled elections, and suppression of dissent.

In 2020, Azerbaijan recaptured most of Nagorno-Karabakh through a six-week war, backed by Turkish drones and Russian acquiescence. By 2023, the remaining Armenian population had fled. Azerbaijan had won—at the cost of confirming its authoritarian trajectory.

## The Culture

Azerbaijanis are a Turkic people, linguistically closer to Turkey than to Persian Iran despite centuries of Iranian influence. The majority practice Shia Islam, but secularism runs deep—a Soviet legacy. Women don't veil; vodka flows; the old Zoroastrian fire worship survives in folk memory.

Mugham music, with its complex modal system and improvisation, is the high art form. Carpet-weaving remains a living tradition, patterns encoding clan and region. Baku has become a gleaming capital, hosting Formula One races and Eurovision, projecting modernity while the countryside remains more traditional.

Family ties are strong, networks decisive. Corruption is pervasive; connections matter more than merit. The Aliyev family and its circle control major sectors of the economy. There is wealth but little freedom—the bargain many oil states offer. The public space for dissent has narrowed over decades. Journalists are jailed; activists disappear; elections are theater.

## The Challenge

The resource curse has fully manifested. Oil wealth props up an authoritarian state that has failed to diversify the economy or build functional institutions. When oil prices fall, the economy contracts; when they rise, corruption deepens. The non-oil sector remains anemic. Cronyism stifles entrepreneurship. Educated young people emigrate.

The Karabakh victory solved one problem by creating another—how to integrate recaptured territories where almost no Azerbaijanis remain, and how to deal with a now-hostile Armenia. The war hardened nationalism on both sides. Azerbaijan has grown closer to Turkey, alienating Russia and Iran. It sits in a dangerous neighborhood with no reliable allies.

Demographically, the country is young, but opportunities are scarce. The government maintains control through patronage and repression, not legitimacy. What happens when oil revenues can no longer buy acquiescence? The succession question looms—dynastic rule works until it doesn't.

## The Contribution

Azerbaijan gave the world some of its earliest oil—Baku's derricks pumping before Spindletop or the Persian Gulf. It pioneered offshore drilling in the Caspian. The wealth flowed outward, financing both tsarist industrialization and Bolshevik revolution.

Culturally, mugham music influenced Persian classical tradition and Turkish makam. The 12th-century poet Nizami Ganjavi wrote in Persian but is claimed by Azerbaijan as national heritage—his Khamsa (five long poems) are masterworks of Islamic literature. The miniaturist tradition flourished here under the Safavids.

More recently, Azerbaijan has served as a bridge—between Europe and Asia, between secular modernity and Islamic tradition, between Russian and Turkish spheres. It has shown that oil wealth can build infrastructure and reduce poverty, even if it hasn't built freedom.

## A Moment

In September 2020, as the war for Karabakh raged, drones buzzed over the mountains. Turkish-made Bayraktar TB2s, piloted from hundreds of miles away, destroyed Armenian tanks and artillery with precision. The footage, released by Azerbaijan's Ministry of Defense, looked like a video game—vehicles exploding in silent infrared blooms.

This was the first drone war, the future arriving in the Caucasus. Azerbaijan had spent its oil money on Turkish and Israeli military technology while Armenia relied on aging Soviet equipment. The result was inevitable. Within six weeks, Azerbaijan reclaimed land it had lost in the 1990s.

In Baku, celebrations erupted. The defeat was avenged; the displaced could return. President Aliyev declared victory, his authority cemented. But in the ruins of Stepanakert, Armenians packed what they could carry and fled, burning their homes rather than leave them to Azerbaijan. A century of coexistence was ending. The drones had solved the military problem but not the human one. Both nations are still learning to live with the cost of victory and defeat.
