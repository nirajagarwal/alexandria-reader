# Bulgaria
*Europe's Forgotten Crossroads*

Bulgaria sits where empires collided for millennia—Byzantine, Ottoman, Russian, Soviet—leaving it perpetually between worlds. Neither quite Balkan nor fully Eastern European, neither Orthodox heartland nor Western outpost, it has absorbed conquest after conquest while maintaining a stubborn, quiet distinctiveness. This is the oldest continuous state in Europe by name, yet also among its poorest and most depopulated. The roses still bloom in the Valley of Roses, but the young have left for Berlin and London.

## The Land

The Balkan Mountains bisect the country east to west, giving it its regional name. North of the range, fertile plains roll toward the Danube and Romania. South, the Rhodope Mountains rise toward Greece and Turkey, their peaks holding ancient Thracian tombs and isolated villages where old dialects persist. The Black Sea coast in the east has become a budget resort destination, its beaches less glamorous than Croatia's but cheaper. The land is generous—wheat, sunflowers, tobacco, grapes—yet many fields now lie fallow. The Rose Valley near Kazanlak produces attar of roses for French perfumers, a delicate industry in a rough landscape. Winter in Sofia, nestled beneath Vitosha Mountain, brings sharp cold and thermal springs steaming in the snow.

## The History

The First Bulgarian Empire emerged in 681 CE when Bulgar tribes from Central Asia conquered Slavic peoples and adopted their language, creating something new. By the ninth century, Tsar Boris I had chosen Orthodox Christianity and commissioned Cyril and Methodius to create the Glagolitic alphabet, ancestor of Cyrillic. The empire reached its zenith under Simeon the Great, who briefly threatened Byzantium itself. A Second Empire rose in 1185 after Byzantine rule, flourishing until the Ottomans conquered it in 1396. Five centuries of Ottoman domination followed—not the apocalypse of nationalist mythology, but not benign either. The Bulgarian Revival of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries saw a cultural renaissance even under foreign rule.

Liberation came in 1878 via Russian armies, creating a Russian dependency that persists psychologically. Bulgaria chose the wrong side in both world wars, occupying neighboring territory and collaborating with the Nazis, though it saved its Jewish population from deportation—one of the few bright spots. The Communist coup of 1946 brought four decades of totalitarian rule under Todor Zhivkov, loyal to Moscow, grinding and gray. The transition to democracy in 1989 was peaceful but economically catastrophic. EU membership in 2007 brought subsidies but also the freedom to leave, which millions did.

## The Culture

Bulgarians shake their heads for yes and nod for no, a peculiarity that still confuses visitors. They maintain a fierce but understated pride—not the demonstrative nationalism of neighboring Balkans, but a quiet insistence on continuity despite everything. The language, South Slavic but with unique features, connects them to Russia linguistically while the Orthodox faith connects them spiritually. Folk traditions persist: martenitsi (red and white tassels) exchanged on March 1st, elaborate Easter bread, rose festivals in Kazanlak. The cuisine blends Turkish and Slavic influences—banitsa pastries, shopska salad, yogurt that predates the Greek variety by centuries. Social life revolves around family and small circles; trust beyond that is scarce after decades of informants and corruption.

Gender roles remain traditional despite Communist-era rhetoric of equality. The Romani minority, perhaps ten percent of the population, faces systematic discrimination—segregated schools, employment barriers, periodic violence. Turkish Bulgarians, another significant minority, navigate an uneasy position between Bulgarian nationalism and Turkish politics. Literature and music reflect melancholy and fatalism—the wedding songs sound like dirges. Yet there's humor too, dark and self-deprecating, a survival mechanism for a people who've seen too many empires come and go.

## The Challenge

Depopulation is eating Bulgaria alive. Since 1989, the population has dropped from nine million to under seven million—the fastest decline in the world outside war zones. The young and educated leave for Western Europe; the old and poor remain. Entire villages stand empty in the mountains, their schools and churches locked. Birth rates have collapsed, and those born here increasingly see no future worth staying for. This isn't just economic—it's existential. Who will be Bulgarian in fifty years?

Corruption pervades everything: politics, judiciary, healthcare, education. Bulgaria ranks as the EU's most corrupt member, where government contracts enrich oligarchs, journalists who investigate die in suspicious car accidents, and the rule of law functions sporadically. The mafia state captured during transition has proved nearly impossible to dislodge. EU membership brought money but little accountability. Russia retains influence through energy dependence and propaganda, while the West offers criticism without much material support. Young Bulgarians see a choice between staying in a corrupt, dying country or leaving for opportunity elsewhere. Most choose elsewhere.

## The Contribution

The Cyrillic alphabet, created for Old Church Slavonic in Bulgaria, now serves hundreds of millions across Eurasia. This alone would be legacy enough—a script born from one empire's need to evangelize, now outliving that empire by a millennium. Bulgarian yogurt, made with *Lactobacillus bulgaricus*, became a global health food, though few consumers know its origin. The country saved its entire Jewish population during World War II through a combination of Orthodox Church intervention, popular protest, and bureaucratic resistance—one of the few bright spots in that dark era. 

Contemporary Bulgaria has given the world little known internationally, but its folk music influenced composers from Bartók to Philip Glass. The distinctive asymmetric meters—7/8, 11/8—create a complexity Western ears find alien and compelling. Christo, the artist who wrapped buildings and coastlines, was Bulgarian before he was anything else, though he left and never returned. The country preserves Thracian tombs and medieval frescoes, keeping alive fragments of vanished civilizations. And it endures, which after so many conquests counts as its own achievement.

## A Moment

In 1943, when Nazi Germany demanded Bulgaria deport its 48,000 Jews to death camps, the country refused. Not through military resistance—Bulgaria was a German ally, had already enacted anti-Semitic laws, and had handed over Jews from occupied territories. But when deportation orders reached Bulgarian-held Jews, something unexpected happened. The Orthodox Church hierarchy protested. Forty-three members of parliament signed a petition. The writer Dimitar Peshev, Deputy Speaker, organized legislative opposition at personal risk. Most remarkably, ordinary Bulgarians demonstrated, hid Jews, and pressured the government.

King Boris III met with Hitler but delayed, equivocated, dispersed Jews to the provinces instead of concentrating them. He died mysteriously weeks later—possibly poisoned, possibly not. The deportations never happened. Bulgaria emerged from the war with its Jewish community intact, the only Axis country that could say so. After the war, nearly all Bulgarian Jews emigrated to Israel, but they left alive. In a continent of collaboration and complicity, this small, compromised kingdom found the will to refuse the final step. It wasn't heroism—the anti-Semitic laws remained—but it was enough. Sometimes enough is everything.
