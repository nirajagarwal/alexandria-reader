# Yemen
*Arabia's Forgotten War*

Yemen sits at the southwestern corner of the Arabian Peninsula, where ancient trade routes met monsoon winds and empires rose on frankincense. It was sophisticated when much of Arabia was desert nomadism—terraced mountains, elaborate irrigation, the Queen of Sheba. Now it endures what the UN calls the world's worst humanitarian crisis, a proxy war that has reduced one of civilization's oldest seats to rubble and starvation. The world looks away. Yemen burns.

## The Land

Mountains dominate the west, rising over 3,000 meters, their slopes terraced for coffee and qat cultivation. The highlands catch monsoon rains—the Arabia Felix of Roman geographers, "Happy Arabia," the only part of the peninsula that could sustain agriculture without modern technology. East stretches the Rub' al Khali, the Empty Quarter, one of Earth's most forbidding deserts. The coast along the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden gave Yemen control over maritime trade between the Mediterranean and Indian Ocean. Socotra Island, 350 kilometers offshore, holds plants found nowhere else—dragon's blood trees like umbrellas against the sky. The land is beautiful and harsh, capable of supporting dense highland populations but demanding constant labor.

## The History

Yemen's history predates Islam by millennia. The Sabaean kingdom built the Marib Dam around 750 BCE, an engineering marvel that irrigated the highlands. Yemen grew wealthy on frankincense and myrrh, shipped north along the Incense Route. The Queen of Sheba—whether legend or history—represents this ancient prosperity. Islam arrived in the 620s CE; Yemen became a center of Islamic scholarship, producing legal schools and Sufi traditions. The Zaidi imamate ruled the northern highlands for a thousand years—a Shia sect that rejected hereditary succession. The Ottomans claimed the north; Britain took Aden in 1839 as a coaling station. 

The 20th century brought fragmentation. North Yemen became independent in 1918; South Yemen fell to Marxist revolutionaries in 1967. The two Yemens unified in 1990 under Ali Abdullah Saleh, who ruled through patronage networks and tribal balancing for three decades. The Arab Spring reached Yemen in 2011. Saleh fell; his deputy Abd Rabbu Mansour Hadi took power. The Houthis—a Zaidi revivalist movement from the north—seized Sana'a in 2014. Saudi Arabia and the UAE intervened in 2015, fearing Iranian influence. The war has killed over 150,000 directly, perhaps 400,000 from starvation and disease. It continues.

## The Culture

Tribal identity runs deep, especially in the northern highlands where genealogy matters intensely and hospitality remains sacred duty. Qat chewing dominates afternoon social life—men gather to talk politics and poetry while the mild stimulant loosens tongues. Yemeni architecture is distinctive: tall tower houses in Sana'a with geometric white patterns on brown stone, every building a fortress. Coffee originated in Yemen—mocha comes from the port city. Poetry matters enormously; oral tradition preserves pre-Islamic verse alongside Islamic learning.

Zaidi Islam in the north differs from Saudi Wahhabism—less rigid, more scholastic. The south absorbed Shafi'i Sunni Islam and British influences; Aden was cosmopolitan until the Marxists drove out the merchants. Women's roles vary dramatically by region—urban women once worked and studied; rural women remain deeply restricted. The war has erased gains.

## The Challenge

Yemen is dying. Eight years of war have destroyed infrastructure, created famine, unleashed cholera. Saudi and Emirati airstrikes hit markets, weddings, school buses—thousands of civilian deaths. The Houthis fire missiles at Saudi cities and repress dissent. Al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula thrives in the chaos. Iranian weapons flow to the Houthis; Saudi money props up a fractured anti-Houthi coalition. 

The economy has collapsed. The currency is worthless. Most Yemenis survive on humanitarian aid—when it arrives. Blockades restrict imports to a country that imported 90% of its food. Children starve while warring parties profit from smuggling. The international community talks about peace while selling weapons to the combatants. Yemen has become a case study in how the world allows suffering when no strategic interest intervenes. The war could end tomorrow if external powers withdrew support. It doesn't.

## The Contribution

Yemen preserved Islamic scholarship when Baghdad fell to the Mongols. Yemeni architects created a building tradition uniquely suited to highland life—beauty and defense unified. Coffee cultivation began in Yemen's mountains; the world's caffeine habit traces to terraces above Sana'a. Yemeni poetry influenced Arabic literature for centuries. The frankincense trade funded ancient civilizations. 

Intellectually, Yemen mediated between Africa and Asia, absorbing influences and transforming them. Its legal schools emphasized consultation and limited authority—the Zaidi tradition rejected dynastic succession long before modern democracy. That same tradition informs the Houthis now, but the principle mattered: power must be justified, not inherited.

## A Moment

In Sana'a's Old City, before the war, you could stand in the early morning light and watch the tower houses emerge from darkness—six, seven, eight stories tall, built from stone and brick in geometric patterns that have stood for centuries. The call to prayer echoed between the buildings. Men walked to the souq past buildings that housed their families for generations. The city was a UNESCO World Heritage site, its architecture irreplaceable.

Saudi airstrikes have damaged the Old City repeatedly. Parts of it are rubble now. The buildings survived the Ottomans, the civil wars, the unification, but not this. A friend of mine from Sana'a hasn't seen his home since 2015. He doesn't know if the house where his grandfather taught him poetry still stands. "Yemen taught the world how to build cities in mountains," he said. "Now we're teaching it how to destroy them."
