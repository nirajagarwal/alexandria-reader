# Burkina Faso
*The Land of Upright People*

A landlocked nation in West Africa's Sahel, Burkina Faso takes its name from a revolutionary gesture—"Land of Upright People" in two local languages, chosen in 1984 to replace the colonial "Upper Volta." The name proclaimed dignity and self-determination. The reality has proven harder. This is a place where aspirations for justice collide with grinding poverty, where French influence lingers despite formal independence, and where the world's attention arrives only during coups or crises. Yet Burkinabè culture—particularly its vibrant cinema—insists on being seen on its own terms.

## The Land

The terrain rises gradually from south to north, from scrubby savanna to semi-arid Sahel, the region where the Sahara's edge meets farmable land. Three river basins—the Black, White, and Red Voltas—gave the country its colonial name, though these rivers flow only seasonally and often run dry. The southern regions receive enough rain for cultivation; the north struggles with drought and desertification. Ouagadougou, the capital, sits in the center where conditions are marginally better. No natural harbors, no navigable rivers, no significant mineral wealth—the land offers few easy advantages. Most people farm millet, sorghum, and cotton, depending on rains that increasingly fail to come. The Sahel is expanding southward.

## The History

The Mossi Kingdoms dominated the region for centuries, organized confederacies that resisted both Islamic conquest from the north and Ashanti expansion from the south. French colonizers arrived late in the 19th century, encountering fiercer resistance than in many West African territories. The Mossi finally submitted in 1896, and France created Upper Volta as an administrative unit—essentially a labor reserve for coastal colonies. Independence came in 1960 under Maurice Yaméogo, who was soon overthrown. What followed was a cycle of coups and military governments that seemed to confirm every pessimistic assumption about African politics. 

Then came Thomas Sankara. The young army captain took power in 1983 and launched one of Africa's most ambitious revolutionary experiments. He renamed the country, banned female genital mutilation, promoted women's rights, planted millions of trees, refused international debt and foreign aid, built schools and health clinics through local initiative. He drove a Renault 5 and turned the presidential palace into an orphanage. "He who feeds you, controls you," he said, insisting on self-sufficiency. He was assassinated in 1987 by his friend and comrade Blaise Compaoré, who ruled for the next 27 years in exactly the corrupt, dependent style Sankara had rejected. Compaoré fell to popular uprising in 2014. Since then, Burkina Faso has struggled through democratic transition while jihadist insurgency from Mali spread south, bringing violence that has displaced millions.

## The Culture

More than sixty ethnic groups—Mossi largest among them—speak different languages under the umbrella of French administration and shared national aspiration. Islam and Christianity overlay traditional religious practices without fully displacing them. Extended family networks remain the basic social unit; individual advancement is still understood as collective obligation. The culture emphasizes dignity, hospitality, and endurance—qualities needed in a harsh climate with few resources.

Burkina Faso's true cultural achievement is FESPACO, the Pan-African Film and Television Festival of Ouagadougou, held every two years since 1969. It remains Africa's largest film festival, a place where African filmmakers can show work to African audiences without Western gatekeepers. Burkinabè directors like Idrissa Ouedraogo and Gaston Kaboré built international reputations filming stories about village life, moral choices, and social change. The cinema matters here in ways it doesn't elsewhere—a space where the country insists on telling its own stories.

Music combines traditional balafon (wooden xylophone) with modern instruments, creating the Sahel sound that travels across borders. Dress blends imported textiles with local tailoring traditions, women's elaborate headwraps serving as both fashion and identity marker.

## The Challenge

The jihadist insurgency has become existential. Groups affiliated with al-Qaeda and Islamic State control large sections of the north and east, attacking villages, schools, military outposts, and gold mines. Over two million people have fled their homes—roughly one-tenth of the population displaced in a country with no resources to absorb them. The weak state—underfunded military, sparse infrastructure, minimal services—cannot effectively counter the insurgency. Each military failure triggers another coup, the latest in 2022, but changing governments doesn't create capacity.

The violence accelerates a spiral: disrupted agriculture means food insecurity, closed schools mean lost education, internal displacement strains urban services, militarization drains the budget from development. International partners offer counterterrorism assistance but no sustainable solutions. France, the former colonizer, maintains military bases and advisors—their presence resented even as their departure seems to enable more chaos. Russia's Wagner Group has appeared, offering muscular alternatives that deliver neither peace nor prosperity. Meanwhile, climate change makes the underlying conditions worse—drought, failing harvests, competition for scarce resources—creating exactly the desperation that insurgencies exploit.

## The Contribution

Thomas Sankara's four-year revolution, though it ended in assassination, provided a vision of what African self-determination might look like if taken seriously. His speeches—still widely circulated, still inspiring—articulated principles that embarrassed both Western donors and African dictators: refuse debt peonage, reject the civilizing mission, build with what you have, expect nothing from former masters. He was wrong about some particulars but right about the larger question: Why should African nations organize their economies and governance to please external creditors? His example haunts conversations about development and dependency across the continent.

FESPACO has given African cinema institutional permanence, a place where the art form can develop its own aesthetics and concerns beyond the exoticizing gaze of Western festivals. Films shown in Ouagadougou address African audiences first—a small revolution in representation.

Less celebrated but significant: Burkina Faso's resistance to genetically modified cotton, insisting on organic certification and traditional seed preservation even when it meant lower yields, preserved agricultural biodiversity and farmer autonomy.

## A Moment

October 15, 1987, afternoon. Gunfire erupts at the Conseil de l'Entente building in Ouagadougou during a meeting. Thomas Sankara, 37 years old, walks toward the shooters. "It's me they want," he tells his guards. Twelve bullets strike him. His body is quickly buried in an unmarked grave, its location kept secret for decades.

His assassination came four years into the revolution—long enough to threaten entrenched interests, not long enough to build institutions that could survive him. Blaise Compaoré, his friend since childhood, his comrade in earlier coups, ordered the killing. Compaoré would rule for 27 years, enriching himself and his associates, doing exactly what the West wanted: accepting structural adjustment, servicing debt, opening markets, maintaining stability. Sankara had planted ten million trees to fight desertification, raised literacy rates, reduced infant mortality. Compaoré let the revolution's achievements wither while offshore accounts grew fat.

In 2015, after Compaoré fled to Côte d'Ivoire, Sankara's body was finally exhumed. The forensic examination confirmed twelve bullets, death by military execution. His widow Mariam watched as the remains were removed from the pauper's cemetery. Young Burkinabè, born after the revolution, had made Sankara an icon—his face on murals, his words in rap lyrics, his red beret the symbol of uncorrupted idealism. They understood they had been robbed of something precious, some possible future murdered before it could mature. The exhumation couldn't restore what was lost, but it could at least name the loss honestly.
