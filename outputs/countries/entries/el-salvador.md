# El Salvador
*The Gang State*

The smallest country in Central America is also the most densely populated, the most violent, and now the most watched. Twenty-one thousand square kilometers wedged between Guatemala and Honduras, with a narrow Pacific coast. No Caribbean shore, no oil, no strategic ports—just coffee, remittances, and the legacy of a vicious civil war. A place where half the population once fled abroad, where gangs became a parallel state, and where a millennial president in a baseball cap is building Latin America's strangest authoritarian experiment. El Salvador has always been extreme—in its inequality, its violence, its politics. Now it offers the world an unsettling question: What if people prefer order to freedom?

**The Land**

Volcanic, mountainous, subtropical. The land rises sharply from the Pacific through coffee-growing highlands to the northern mountains. Twenty-three volcanoes, some still active. Rich volcanic soil good for coffee and sugar. Earthquakes are common—the country sits on three tectonic plates. Most people live in the central valleys and the coastal plain. San Salvador, the capital, sprawls through volcanic foothills. Deforestation is severe—only two percent of primary forest remains. The climate is tropical but the elevation keeps much of it temperate. Small enough that you can drive coast to mountains in two hours. Dense enough that nowhere feels empty. The land has been worked hard for centuries—coffee plantations, then factories, now mostly remittance-funded construction and gang territory.

**The History**

Spanish conquest in the 1520s, then three centuries as the southernmost province of the Captaincy General of Guatemala. Independence in 1821, followed by a brief annexation to Mexico and then membership in the Federal Republic of Central America until its collapse in 1840. El Salvador emerged as a coffee oligarchy—fourteen families controlled the land and the export economy. Indigenous identity was suppressed; peasants became laborers. In 1932, after a communist-led peasant uprising, the military killed thirty thousand people in a month—*La Matanza*, the slaughter. The event haunted the country for decades.

The oligarchy ruled through the military until the 1970s, when repression sparked guerrilla movements. Civil war erupted in 1980—the FMLN guerrillas against U.S.-backed government forces. Twelve years, seventy-five thousand dead, villages massacred, priests murdered. The war ended in 1992 with UN-brokered peace accords. The FMLN became a political party; democracy was supposed to begin. Instead came the gangs. Mara Salvatrucha (MS-13) and Barrio 18 formed in Los Angeles among Salvadoran refugees, then were deported back to a country they barely knew. They filled the vacuum left by war.

**The Culture**

Mestizo culture with indigenous roots mostly erased. Spanish is universal; indigenous languages are nearly extinct. Catholicism dominates but has weakened—evangelical Protestantism grows rapidly. The culture is marked by trauma—the civil war, the gangs, the migrations. Family is central, often transnational—someone is always in the United States, sending money home. Remittances account for a quarter of GDP. Pupusas (thick corn tortillas filled with cheese or beans) are the national food, claimed fiercely against Honduran rivals.

Gender norms are traditional but eroding. Machismo persists but women have fought for space—many led families through the war and migration. Gang culture warped masculinity further—tattoos, loyalty codes, territorial violence. The gangs became a state within the state, taxing buses, controlling neighborhoods, recruiting children. By the 2010s, El Salvador had the highest murder rate on earth outside active war zones. Everyone had a gang story—a brother killed, an extortion paid, a bus route avoided.

**The Challenge**

In 2019, Nayib Bukele won the presidency at age thirty-seven, breaking the two-party system. Handsome, media-savvy, authoritarian. He declared a "war on gangs" and in March 2022 imposed a state of emergency that has never been lifted. Seventy-five thousand people arrested—five percent of the adult male population. Mega-prisons built. Civil liberties suspended. Courts rubber-stamp. International observers cry foul. The murder rate collapsed. Salvadorans, exhausted by decades of violence, support him overwhelmingly.

The challenge is that the cure may be worse than the disease, just slower. Arbitrary arrests. Torture reports. Disappeared critics. Bitcoin made legal tender (a failed experiment). Democratic institutions gutted. The opposition neutered. Bukele's popularity remains above eighty percent—he offers order, however brutal. El Salvador has become a test case: Can authoritarianism deliver what democracy could not? What happens when a population chooses a strongman because the alternative was unlivable? The gang state has become an autocratic state. The violence has moved from the streets to the prisons and the courts.

**The Contribution**

Óscar Romero, the archbishop assassinated in 1980 while saying mass, became a symbol of liberation theology and moral witness against state terror—canonized in 2018. Roque Dalton, the revolutionary poet killed by his own comrades in 1975, left searing verse about violence and resistance. Claribel Alegría's poetry bore witness to war and exile. Salvadoran food—especially pupusas—has spread wherever Salvadorans migrated. The FMLN's transformation from guerrilla army to political party was studied as a model for peace processes elsewhere. More recently, El Salvador has become a cautionary tale and a test case—for what happens when gangs replace the state, when democracy fails to deliver security, when a young autocrat offers order and the people accept.

**A Moment**

On March 24, 1980, Archbishop Óscar Romero stood at the altar of the chapel of the Divine Providence cancer hospital in San Salvador, celebrating mass. He had spent the previous day's homily pleading with soldiers to disobey orders to kill. "No soldier is obliged to obey an order contrary to the law of God," he said. The government and the oligarchy wanted him dead. As he raised the chalice, a single shot rang out. The bullet struck his heart. He collapsed at the altar, blood pooling on the white marble. The assassin fled. Romero died minutes later. His funeral a week later drew a hundred thousand people; snipers opened fire, killing forty. The civil war began in earnest. Romero's murder crystallized the conflict—the church defending the poor, the state defending power through terror. Decades later, his assassination remains the country's wound, still unhealed, still defining everything that followed.
