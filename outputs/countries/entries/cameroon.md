# Cameroon
*Africa in Miniature*

Cameroon contains nearly every landscape, climate, and culture Africa offers—rainforests and savannas, mountains and coasts, Muslim north and Christian south, Francophone majority and Anglophone minority, over 250 ethnic groups speaking as many languages. This diversity makes it a microcosm of the continent's complexity. It also makes it perpetually unstable, held together by authoritarian continuity rather than genuine unity. The country's nickname—"Africa in Miniature"—is both celebration and curse.

## The Land

The geography runs from Lake Chad's semi-arid shores in the north to the dense Congo Basin rainforests in the south, from Atlantic beaches to volcanic highlands. Mount Cameroon, still active, rises 13,000 feet near the coast—the highest peak in West Africa. The climate shifts from Sahel to equatorial. The land provides cocoa, coffee, timber, and oil, though not enough of any to escape poverty. Most people live in the cities—Douala's humid port, Yaoundé's administrative highlands—or in the farming regions where subsistence agriculture still dominates. The Waza floodplains in the north draw elephants and lions; the southern forests hold gorillas and chimpanzees. Rivers flow in every direction, as if the country can't decide which ocean to choose.

## The History

Germany colonized Kamerun in 1884, establishing plantations and brutal labor systems. After World War I, France and Britain divided the territory as League of Nations mandates. French Cameroun gained independence in 1960; the following year, the southern portion of British Cameroons voted to join it, creating a bilingual federation. The northern British portion joined Nigeria. Ahmadou Ahidjo, the first president, centralized power and abolished the federation in 1972, creating a unitary state dominated by Francophones. When Paul Biya succeeded him in 1982, many expected reform. Instead, Biya entrenched himself through corruption, co-optation, and occasional violence. He has ruled for over four decades, making Cameroon one of Africa's most stable dictatorships. The Anglophone regions, feeling marginalized and exploited, erupted into separatist conflict in 2016—a crisis that continues to simmer, displacing hundreds of thousands.

## The Culture

The cultural diversity is staggering. In the Muslim north, Fulani herders and Kanuri farmers follow Islamic traditions shaped by centuries of contact with Saharan trade routes. In the Christian and animist south and west, Bamileke merchants dominate commerce, while Bassa and Douala communities hold coastal influence. Pygmy groups in the eastern forests maintain distinct ways of life, increasingly threatened by logging and land seizures. Pidgin English serves as a lingua franca in Anglophone regions; French dominates elsewhere. Makossa music—urban, electronic, guitar-driven—emerged from Douala in the 1970s, with Manu Dibango's "Soul Makossa" achieving global fame. Football obsesses the nation; the Indomitable Lions won Olympic gold in 2000 and have reached the World Cup quarterfinals. Despite authoritarianism, civil society persists in churches, mosques, traditional chieftaincies, and informal networks that hold communities together when the state fails them.

## The Challenge

The Anglophone Crisis defines contemporary Cameroon. What began as protests over linguistic and economic marginalization in 2016 has metastasized into armed separatist insurgency in the Northwest and Southwest regions. Government forces have responded with brutal repression—burning villages, detaining thousands, conducting extrajudicial killings. Separatist militias have kidnapped students, attacked schools, and terrorized civilians suspected of collaboration. Thousands have died; over a million have been displaced. The crisis exposes deeper fractures: Francophone dominance, regional inequality, authoritarian rigidity. Biya, aging and intransigent, offers no vision for reconciliation. The economy stagnates while corruption siphons oil and timber revenues. Boko Haram's insurgency spills across the northern border from Nigeria. Climate change stresses the Sahel regions. The country that contains all of Africa's diversity is discovering it also contains all of Africa's conflicts.

## The Contribution

Cameroon gave the world Manu Dibango, whose saxophone and groove influenced generations of African and global musicians. The country's literary tradition includes Mongo Beti, whose novels anatomized colonialism and post-colonial corruption with unflinching clarity, and Calixthe Beyala, whose feminist fiction challenged both African patriarchy and French condescension. Cameroon's football success—four African Cup of Nations titles, the first African team to reach a World Cup quarterfinal—demonstrated that African teams could compete at the highest level. The country has also contributed to biodiversity conservation, hosting the Dja Faunal Reserve, a UNESCO World Heritage Site protecting one of Africa's most intact rainforests. Its cultural synthesis—the blend of indigenous, French, British, and pan-African influences—represents a kind of cosmopolitanism forged through historical trauma and geographic complexity.

## A Moment

In the early morning of February 10, 2008, in the dusty northern city of Garoua, a young man named Hamadou stands in line at a petrol station. Fuel prices have just doubled. The line stretches for blocks—motorcycles, cars, women with jerry cans for cooking oil. Hamadou has been waiting since 4 AM. By noon, protests erupt across the city. Students, traders, unemployed youth flood the streets, burning tires, blocking roads, chanting against Biya, against corruption, against the Constitutional Council that just approved the elimination of presidential term limits. Police fire tear gas, then live ammunition. In Douala, over one hundred die in a week of riots—the worst unrest since independence. The government arrests opposition leaders, floods cities with soldiers, restores order through force. Biya goes on television, elderly and impassive, promising nothing. Hamadou returns to the petrol station the next week. The line is just as long. The price has not decreased. Sixteen years later, Biya still rules. Hamadou, if he survived, would be in his thirties now, still waiting for something to change.
