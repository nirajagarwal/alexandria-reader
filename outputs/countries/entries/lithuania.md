# Lithuania
*The Last Pagan Fortress*

The northernmost of the Baltic states, Lithuania spent centuries as a buffer between empires—sometimes a great power itself, more often crushed between them. It emerged from Soviet occupation with a fierce sense of national identity forged through resistance, a complex relationship with its own imperial past, and the knowledge that independence is never guaranteed. The land is flat, forested, and historically contested; the people remember what was taken and what they reclaimed.

## The Land

Lithuania occupies the southeastern shore of the Baltic Sea, mostly flat or gently rolling, covered in pine and birch forests, dotted with thousands of small lakes. The Curonian Spit—a narrow sand dune peninsula stretching nearly sixty miles—separates the Curonian Lagoon from the Baltic, a landscape so distinctive UNESCO declared it a World Heritage site. The soil is decent for farming, the climate harsh: long, dark winters, brief summers. Vilnius sits inland near the Belarusian border; Kaunas straddles the Nemunas River; Klaipėda, the port city, faces the sea. This is not dramatic geography—no mountains, no major rivers—but land that has seen armies cross it in every direction for centuries.

## The History

Lithuania was the last pagan nation in Europe, holding out against Christianity until 1387. Before that, it built an empire—the Grand Duchy of Lithuania stretched from the Baltic to the Black Sea by the 14th century, absorbing Slavic lands, challenging the Mongols. The 1569 Union of Lublin merged it with Poland into the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, which dominated Eastern Europe until the 18th century partitions erased it from the map. Russia absorbed Lithuanian territory; the identity survived underground through language, folk culture, Catholicism.

Brief independence came after World War I—1918 to 1940. The Soviets occupied it in 1940 under the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, deported tens of thousands to Siberia. The Nazis occupied it 1941-44; nearly all of Lithuania's 200,000 Jews were murdered, with significant local collaboration. Soviet re-occupation brought more deportations, forced collectivization, suppression of language and religion. Guerrilla resistance—the Forest Brothers—fought Soviet rule into the 1950s.

Lithuania declared independence on March 11, 1990, before the Soviet Union collapsed—the first Soviet republic to do so. Moscow imposed economic blockades and sent troops; thirteen civilians were killed in January 1991 defending the TV tower in Vilnius. Independence held. Lithuania joined NATO and the EU in 2004, cementing its return to the West.

## The Culture

Lithuanian is one of the most conservative Indo-European languages, preserving ancient features lost elsewhere—linguists study it for what it reveals about proto-Indo-European. The language survived as a marker of identity through occupation; today it's fiercely protected. Catholicism remained strong even through Soviet atheism, a repository of national culture.

The folk tradition runs deep—songs, dances, amber jewelry, the Hill of Crosses where pilgrims have planted hundreds of thousands of crosses since the 19th century. Basketball is a national obsession, a rare arena where a small nation can compete globally. Post-Soviet literature grapples with memory, occupation, complicity—Tomas Venclova, Ričardas Gavelis.

Social life values resilience, skepticism of authority, dark humor born of historical trauma. Emigration has hollowed out the population—hundreds of thousands left after EU accession for better wages in the West. Those who remain feel the weight of keeping the nation alive.

## The Challenge

Depopulation threatens the national project. Lithuania's population has fallen from 3.7 million in 1990 to under 2.8 million today—emigration and low birth rates combined. Young people leave for London, Dublin, Oslo; villages empty; schools close. The economy has grown impressively since independence, but wages still lag Western Europe. Will there be enough Lithuanians left to sustain Lithuania?

Russia looms—a neighbor that has never accepted the loss of its former empire. Lithuania hosts NATO troops and remains on edge about what happened to Georgia in 2008, Ukraine in 2014. The Suwalki Gap, the narrow land corridor connecting Lithuania to Poland between Belarus and Kaliningrad, is NATO's most vulnerable point. Lithuania knows it could be cut off in hours.

Reckoning with the Holocaust remains incomplete—local collaboration in the genocide was extensive, and nationalist narratives have sometimes sanitized or minimized it. The Jewish community that shaped Vilnius for centuries is gone; memory politics are contested.

## The Contribution

Lithuania kept alive languages, traditions, and folk cultures when empires tried to erase them. The Baltic Way of 1989—two million people forming a human chain across Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania to demand freedom—remains one of history's great nonviolent protests. Lithuanian independence catalyzed the Soviet Union's collapse; the courage of those who declared freedom in March 1990 changed the world.

Vilnius was once the "Jerusalem of the North," home to the Vilna Gaon and a vibrant Jewish intellectual tradition until the Holocaust destroyed it. Lithuanian literature—Antanas Škėma, Marius Katiliškis, Jurgis Kunčinas—captures the experience of occupation, exile, endurance.

Lithuania proved that small nations can reclaim sovereignty even from empires. It joined the West not as supplicant but as a nation with its own history, its own voice, its own terms.

## A Moment

On January 13, 1991, Soviet tanks and special forces moved on the Vilnius TV tower. Lithuania had declared independence ten months earlier; Gorbachev's government refused to recognize it. Thousands of unarmed Lithuanians surrounded the tower, singing, forming human chains. The soldiers opened fire. Fourteen civilians were killed, over seven hundred injured. The footage went global—people crushed by tanks, shot defending a broadcast facility, still singing.

The tower held. The Soviet Union would collapse within a year, but that night no one knew what came next. The people defending the tower knew only that they would not go back. Some were middle-aged, some teenagers, some barely adults—ordinary people who had lived under occupation their entire lives and decided they'd had enough. The tanks rolled forward; the people didn't move. That's Lithuania—small, stubborn, refusing to disappear.
