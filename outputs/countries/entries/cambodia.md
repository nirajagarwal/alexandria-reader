# Cambodia
*The Killing Fields, Still Haunted*

Few nations have suffered such complete rupture and survived to tell it. Cambodia is a civilization that killed itself and crawled back from annihilation—the temples still standing, the trauma still raw, the question of how to live after such horror still unresolved. Between the glories of Angkor and the genocide of the Khmer Rouge lies a thousand-year arc of rise, decline, colonial subjugation, and catastrophic violence. What remains is a country trying to remember and forget simultaneously, rebuilding under authoritarian rule while the bones of the dead still surface after heavy rains.

**The Land**

Cambodia is shaped by the Mekong River and Tonlé Sap lake—the great pulse that defines everything. During monsoon season the Mekong swells, reversing the flow of the Tonlé Sap River, flooding the lake to five times its dry-season size. This creates the richest inland fishery in Southeast Asia and rice paddies that fed the Angkorian empire. The country is mostly flat, hot, and green—a fertile plain surrounded by mountains at the borders with Thailand, Laos, and Vietnam. The south reaches the Gulf of Thailand through mangrove forests and new beach resorts. Phnom Penh sits at the confluence of the Mekong, Tonlé Sap, and Bassac rivers. The land is generous when it floods, harsh when it doesn't. Tropical heat and heavy rains for half the year, dry heat the other half.

**The History**

Between the 9th and 15th centuries, the Khmer Empire controlled much of mainland Southeast Asia from its capital at Angkor. The temples they built—Angkor Wat, Bayon, Ta Prohm—remain among humanity's greatest architectural achievements, manifestations of divine kingship and hydraulic engineering genius. But the empire fell, eroded by Thai invasions, environmental degradation, and the shift of trade routes. By the 19th century Cambodia was a weakened kingdom squeezed between Thailand and Vietnam.

French colonization from 1863 preserved Cambodia from total absorption but reduced it to a colonial backwater within French Indochina. Independence came in 1953 under King Norodom Sihanouk, who maneuvered skillfully through the early Cold War. But the Vietnam War engulfed Cambodia. American bombing campaigns from 1969-73 killed perhaps 150,000 civilians and destabilized the country completely.

In 1975 the Khmer Rouge seized power. Led by Pol Pot, they attempted to create an agrarian communist utopia by emptying cities, abolishing money, destroying records, and murdering anyone educated, professional, religious, or ethnic minority. In three years, eight months, and twenty days they killed roughly 1.7 million people—a quarter of the population—through execution, starvation, and forced labor. The country became a charnel house.

Vietnam invaded in 1979 and drove the Khmer Rouge to the Thai border, where they continued guerrilla war for another decade. The 1991 Paris Peace Accords brought UN administration and elections in 1993. Hun Sen, a former Khmer Rouge commander turned Vietnamese puppet turned strongman, has ruled ever since—initially as second prime minister, then prime minister from 1998 to 2023, now as senate president while his son holds the premiership. The transition was dynastic, the authoritarianism intact.

**The Culture**

Khmer culture survived genocide—barely. Nearly all monks, teachers, artists, and intellectuals were murdered. Classical dance traditions, Buddhist practices, and literary knowledge survived only because scattered survivors remembered. The work of recovery has been enormous and incomplete.

Theravada Buddhism shapes daily life—saffron-robed monks at dawn, spirit houses at businesses, temples as community centers. But traditional beliefs in neak ta (territorial spirits) and ancestral ghosts remain powerful, sometimes integrated with Buddhism, sometimes parallel to it. The genocide shattered social trust in ways still palpable—families inform on families, corruption is endemic, brutality lurks beneath surface courtesy.

Khmer language unites the country—nearly everyone speaks it, unlike the ethnic fragmentation of many Southeast Asian nations. Extended family networks remain central. Respect for elders, hierarchy, and indirect communication define social relations. But modernity frays tradition: young people flood to Phnom Penh, garment factories employ hundreds of thousands of women, Chinese investment reshapes the coast.

Gender roles remain conservative despite women's economic participation. Domestic violence rates are high. LGBTQ Cambodians exist in a space of tolerance and invisibility—not criminalized, not celebrated, not discussed.

**The Challenge**

Hun Sen's Cambodian People's Party has created a soft authoritarianism lubricated by Chinese investment and sustained by memory of worse horrors. Democracy flickered briefly in the 1990s and 2000s—the opposition CNRP won 44% of the vote in 2013 before being dissolved in 2017, its leaders jailed or exiled. The 2023 transition to Hun Manet, Hun Sen's son, was seamless and hereditary.

Corruption permeates everything. Land grabbing by connected elites displaces farmers and slum dwellers. Environmental destruction accelerates—illegal logging, sand dredging that destabilizes the Mekong, Tonlé Sap lake shrinking from upstream dams. Chinese money floods in: casinos at the border, resorts on the coast, infrastructure projects that saddle Cambodia with debt.

Justice for the genocide remains incomplete. The Khmer Rouge Tribunal convicted a handful of senior leaders after decades of delay and political interference, most dying before sentencing. Many mid-level perpetrators live openly, some in government. The country has neither fully confronted nor fully buried its past.

Inequality grows as the elite enriches itself and garment workers protest for living wages. The young majority—60% born after the genocide—wants prosperity and stability but inherits a political system offering neither genuine development nor democratic voice.

**The Contribution**

Angkor Wat stands as one of the world's supreme architectural achievements—a sandstone mountain-temple that covers 400 acres, its bas-reliefs depicting the Ramayana and Mahabharata in exquisite detail. The entire Angkor complex represents centuries of Khmer genius in engineering, astronomy, art, and religious synthesis.

Cambodia gave the world classical apsara dance, preserved through oral tradition and surviving dancers after the genocide. The movements—impossibly bent fingers, arched backs, narrative gestures—express Hindu-Buddhist cosmology through the body.

Cambodian resilience itself is a contribution. The country survived auto-genocide and rebuilt. Rithy Panh's films (The Missing Picture, S21) created new forms of documentary to address unimaginable trauma. Vann Nath painted what he witnessed in Tuol Sleng prison. Youk Chhang's Documentation Center archives the evidence, ensuring memory.

And there is the gentleness—despite everything, a capacity for warmth, humor, and generosity that the Khmer Rouge could not extinguish. Street food vendors, tuk-tuk drivers, market women carry on with a grace that does not deny suffering but refuses to be defined by it alone.

**A Moment**

Stand in the Tuol Sleng Genocide Museum—a former high school that the Khmer Rouge converted into Security Prison 21. In what were once classrooms, you see the iron beds where prisoners were shackled, the photographs of thousands who passed through—carefully documented by their executioners before being taken to Choeung Ek killing field and bludgeoned to death to save bullets.

On the walls: rules prohibiting crying during torture, painted in white letters. In the cases: shackles, whips, the tools of extraction. Outside: the playground equipment the Khmer Rouge used as a gallows. The place is unbearably specific—the names, the faces, the instruments, the meticulous record-keeping of mass murder.

Then you notice the survivor paintings by Vann Nath—one of seven prisoners to survive Tuol Sleng out of roughly 14,000 who entered. His paintings show the torture in careful detail because he was ordered to paint them for propaganda, but also because he needed to testify. He painted the truth until he died in 2011, his body broken by what was done to him.

Every day tourists walk through Tuol Sleng and Choeung Ek—necessary pilgrimage, unbearable confrontation. The killing fields are beautiful now, green and peaceful except for the bones and bits of cloth that still surface. At the center stands a Buddhist stupa filled with 5,000 skulls arranged by age and gender. This is what happened. These are the people. This is what we did to ourselves.
