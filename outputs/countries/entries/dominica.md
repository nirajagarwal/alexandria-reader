# Dominica
*The Nature Island*

The Caribbean's least touristy island nation earned its nickname honestly. Dominica is what the region looked like before resort development—volcanic peaks, rainforest cascading to black sand beaches, rivers you can still drink from. It remains poor precisely because it refused to flatten its mountains and pave its coasts. The question it poses: can a small island survive on principle when neighbors prosper by selling themselves?

## The Land

Dominica rises abruptly from the sea—365 rivers cutting through rainforest so dense it helped Kalinago people resist European conquest for two centuries. The island is a spine of volcanic peaks, steam vents, and sulfur springs. Morne Trois Pitons dominates the southern interior; the Boiling Lake, the world's second-largest hot spring, sits in its crater. There are no white sand beaches worth marketing. The coastline is dramatic cliffs and river mouths. Hurricanes Maria (2017) and David (1979) devastated infrastructure but proved the forest's resilience—it grew back. Ninety percent of rainfall supports agriculture without irrigation. The land itself dictates limitations: steep, roadless, unsuited to mass tourism or industrial agriculture.

## The History

Columbus named it for the Sunday he spotted it in 1493, but the Kalinago resisted colonization longer than almost anywhere in the Americas. The terrain favored guerrilla resistance. France and Britain battled over it, finally settling on British control in 1763, though French planters and African slaves made the population. The island never developed plantation wealth—too mountainous, too forested. Slavery existed but at smaller scale than Jamaica or Barbados. Kalinago people survived in a designated territory on the eastern coast. Independence came in 1978 under Eugenia Charles, the Caribbean's first female prime minister, who governed for fifteen years with anti-communist, pro-American conservatism. She supported the U.S. invasion of Grenada. A 1981 coup attempt backed by white supremacists and American mercenaries failed spectacularly. Recent politics have oscillated between development-focused progressives and those defending the environmental status quo.

## The Culture

English is official, but French Creole (Kwéyòl) persists in rural areas, linking the island to Martinique and St. Lucia more than to anglophone neighbors. Catholicism dominates despite British colonial rule. Villages cluster along the coast and valleys; the interior remains roadless. Kalinago territory maintains distinct cultural practices—basket weaving, cassava bread, traditional governance—though modernity has eroded much. Dominican identity centers on independence and self-sufficiency: small farmers, strong local markets, distrust of outside control. Community ties remain paramount. The island produces writers and cricketers disproportionate to its size. Carnival, called Mas Domnik, blends African and French traditions. Gender roles are conservative but women have held significant political power. Music favors zouk and bouyon, the latter invented here in the 1980s—percussive, hypnotic, distinctly Dominican.

## The Challenge

Hurricanes threaten existence itself. Maria killed sixty-five and destroyed ninety percent of structures. Climate change means stronger storms more frequently—Category 5 winds in 2017, repeated flooding. The economy cannot generate enough employment; young people emigrate to Antigua, Barbados, Britain. Poverty rates exceed thirty percent. Citizenship-by-investment programs—selling passports—now fund reconstruction, raising questions about sovereignty. Infrastructure remains fragile: one major hospital, limited roads, unreliable electricity. The choice to preserve the environment has meant forgoing tourism revenue neighbors enjoy. How do you rebuild after nature destroys you, when you've staked everything on nature being your asset? The island's principles are admirable; its economy is not viable. That tension defines every policy debate.

## The Contribution

Dominica preserved something vanishing elsewhere: a Caribbean island that still looks like itself. The boiling lake, the Morne Trois Pitons National Park (UNESCO World Heritage), the whale watching—these exist because development was refused. The Kalinago territory is the last significant indigenous community in the Eastern Caribbean. Dominica pioneered eco-tourism before the term existed, proving small economies could survive without flattening everything for resorts. It also contributed political independence: Eugenia Charles's fifteen-year tenure demonstrated Caribbean governance could be stable and uncorrupt. The island's writers—Phyllis Shand Allfrey, Jean Rhys (though she identified more with Dominica than birthplace St. Lucia in later work)—captured Caribbean complexity. In a region that sold its soul to cruise ships, Dominica kept its mountains.

## A Moment

November 2017, weeks after Hurricane Maria. Villages are still cut off; landslides block the Transinsular Road. In the Kalinago Territory, Irvince Auguiste stands in what was his home—now just a concrete foundation and scattered belongings. The roof flew to Martinique. He's sixty-seven, has rebuilt twice before (David in 1979, Omar in 2010). He tells a visiting journalist he will rebuild again, here, on the same spot, because this is where his ancestors lived before Columbus. The forest around him, stripped bare by 180 mph winds, is already showing green shoots. The reporter asks if he's considered leaving—emigrating like his children did, to Antigua or Toronto. Auguiste looks at the mountain rising behind his demolished house. "Leave to go where?" he asks. "This is Dominica. This is what it means to be here." The forest will grow back. The house will rise again. The next hurricane will come. This is the bargain the island made: to remain itself, whatever it costs.
