# Gambia
*The River Nation*

A thin sliver of land wrapped around a river, Gambia is one of Africa's smallest countries and perhaps its strangest shape—a cartographic afterthought of colonial negotiation. The British claimed the Gambia River's banks; everything else went to France. What resulted is a country 300 miles long and rarely more than 30 miles wide, a geographic absurdity that somehow functions as a nation. It exists because a river exists, and because empires once drew lines where it suited them.

## The Land

The Gambia River defines everything. It flows west from Guinea through the country's entire length, widening as it approaches the Atlantic. During rainy season it floods the lowlands; during dry season it shrinks but never dies. Mangrove swamps line the lower reaches. The land itself is unremarkable—flat, sandy, hot, prone to drought. No mountains, no dramatic vistas, just the river and its banks. Most Gambians live within a few miles of the water. The country produces groundnuts (peanuts) in quantity, but the soil exhausts easily. Tourism clusters around the coastal beaches where Europeans seek winter sun. The river remains the reason anyone is here at all.

## The History

Portuguese traders reached the river in the 15th century. The British established a fort on James Island in 1661, trading in slaves and later groundnuts. Gambia became a British colony in 1888, a peculiar enclave completely surrounded by French Senegal. Independence came peacefully in 1965 under Dawda Jawara, who governed as a Westminster-style democrat for three decades—rare stability in West Africa. In 1994, a young army officer named Yahya Jammeh seized power in a bloodless coup. What followed was 22 years of increasingly erratic dictatorship: torture, disappearances, claims of magical healing powers, wild homophobia, paranoid purges. In 2016, Jammeh lost an election, briefly accepted defeat, then refused to leave. West African troops massed at the border. He fled to Equatorial Guinea with millions stolen from state coffers. Democracy returned, fragile and cautious.

## The Culture

Ninety percent Muslim, but traditionally tolerant—interfaith marriages are common, and the Islam practiced here historically bent toward Sufi mysticism rather than strict orthodoxy. Mandinka people form the largest group, followed by Fula, Wolof, Jola, and others. English is the official language, but most speak their ethnic tongue at home. Kora music—the 21-stringed harp-lute of the griots—remains central to cultural life. Family networks matter more than formal institutions; remittances from the diaspora keep many households afloat. Gender norms are conservative: female genital cutting remains widespread despite official bans. The country feels more like a collection of river villages than a modern state. Trust in government is low after Jammeh, but trust in community endures.

## The Challenge

Economic viability. Gambia has almost no natural resources, limited arable land, and an absurd geography that makes internal cohesion difficult. It depends entirely on groundnut exports, tourism, and remittances—all vulnerable to external shocks. Youth unemployment is severe; thousands risk the Mediterranean crossing to Europe each year. The country lacks the scale to develop meaningful industry. Senegal surrounds it completely, and the two countries have discussed confederation multiple times, but Gambians fear being absorbed. Post-Jammeh political institutions remain weak; corruption is endemic; the military has too much power. How does a country this small, this poor, this strangely shaped build a sustainable economy and functioning democracy? No one has answered convincingly.

## The Contribution

Gambia gave the world Alex Haley's *Roots*. The 1977 television series brought millions of Americans to Juffure village, where Haley claimed his ancestor Kunta Kinte was enslaved and shipped across the Atlantic. Historians dispute the details, but the emotional truth resonated: Gambia became a pilgrimage site for African Americans seeking connection to ancestral origins. The country also preserved the griot tradition—oral historians who carry centuries of genealogy, proverbs, and epic poetry in memory. In recent years, Gambia led the charge against Myanmar at the International Court of Justice over the Rohingya genocide—a tiny nation invoking international law on behalf of another persecuted Muslim minority. Moral courage sometimes comes from unexpected places.

## A Moment

Banjul, December 2016. Yahya Jammeh has lost the election to Adama Barrow and initially conceded, stunning everyone. Then he changes his mind, annuls the results, declares a state of emergency. ECOWAS—the West African regional bloc—gives him an ultimatum: leave or be removed by force. Senegalese troops mass at the border. Nigerian warships appear offshore. Most observers expect violence; Jammeh has ruled through fear for 22 years and commands the security forces. 

But on January 21, 2017, as Barrow takes the oath of office in neighboring Senegal, Jammeh boards a plane to Equatorial Guinea. No final stand, no bloodbath. He leaves with a convoy of luxury cars and stolen millions, but he leaves. Regional pressure worked. African democracy, fragile as it is, enforced its own norms. Gambians celebrated in the streets—tentatively at first, then with abandon. The dictator was gone. 

The truth commission that followed documented horrors: torture centers, death squads, rape as state policy. Jammeh had ruled through terror. But he's gone, and Gambia is trying—imperfectly, uncertainly—to build something better. The river flows on.
