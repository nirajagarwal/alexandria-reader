# Sri Lanka
*The Wounded Island*

An island that should be paradise—tropical, fertile, positioned at the crossroads of ancient trade routes—but carries the weight of a bitter ethnic war that only ended in 2009. Buddhism and Hinduism coexist uneasily. Sinhalese and Tamil communities nurse old wounds. The country oscillates between democracy and authoritarianism, between embracing its diversity and enforcing ethnic hierarchy. It's a place of extraordinary natural beauty and cultural richness that keeps stumbling over the question of who belongs.

## The Land

A teardrop-shaped island in the Indian Ocean, separated from India by the shallow Palk Strait. The interior rises to cool highlands where tea plantations carpet the hillsides—the British colonial legacy that still defines the economy. The north is dry, flat, dominated by the Jaffna Peninsula. The south and southwest coast receive monsoon rains that sustain rice paddies and tropical forests. The island is small—roughly the size of Ireland—but ecologically diverse. Elephants still roam national parks. The coast offers palm-fringed beaches that tourism marketers dream about. Ancient irrigation systems built by early kingdoms still function in the dry zone. It's a land that should support abundance, and mostly does, but that abundance hasn't translated into peace.

## The History

The Sinhalese trace their presence to the 6th century BCE, building hydraulic civilizations around massive reservoirs. Tamil kingdoms established themselves in the north, with ties to South India. The island was a Buddhist stronghold even as Hinduism dominated the mainland. The Portuguese arrived in 1505, followed by the Dutch, then the British who unified the island as Ceylon in 1815. The colonial powers favored Tamils for administrative positions, breeding Sinhalese resentment. Independence came in 1948. The Sinhalese majority passed discriminatory language laws in 1956. Tamil militants formed in response. By 1983, full civil war erupted between the government and the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE). The conflict lasted 26 years, killed perhaps 100,000 people, introduced suicide bombing to modern warfare. The war ended in 2009 with government victory and credible allegations of war crimes. Reconciliation remains unfinished.

## The Culture

Buddhism defines Sinhalese identity—not just as religion but as civilization marker, with monasteries serving as cultural and political centers. The Tamils are predominantly Hindu, with Christianity significant among both communities as a colonial inheritance. Sinhala and Tamil are both official languages, but implementation of language rights remains contested. Family structure is traditional but matrilineal patterns persist in some areas—unusual for the region. Cricket obsesses the nation, one of the few arenas where ethnic divisions temporarily dissolve. The arts draw from both Indian and Southeast Asian traditions—dance forms, temple architecture, elaborate masks used in ritual drama. Food culture reflects the island's position: rice and curry as base, with coconut, chilies, and spices that once drew merchants from Rome and China. Caste still matters, especially in rural areas. Gender norms are conservative despite relatively high female literacy and political participation. The culture values education highly—literacy rates exceed 92%—but struggle to convert that education into broadly shared prosperity.

## The Challenge

Ethnic reconciliation. The civil war ended militarily, not politically. The north and east remain under heavy military presence. Tamils report land seizures, disappearances, surveillance. The government resists international investigation into war crimes. Sinhalese Buddhist nationalism has intensified, not diminished—sometimes targeting Muslims, who form about 10% of the population. The 2019 Easter bombings by Islamic extremists killed 269 people, hardening communal tensions. The economy collapsed spectacularly in 2022—foreign reserves depleted, unable to import fuel or medicine, inflation exceeding 50%. The crisis revealed deep corruption and mismanagement by the Rajapaksa political dynasty. Protests forced out the president, but structural problems persist: debt burden, brain drain, overdependence on remittances and tourism. The island needs a political solution that acknowledges historical grievances without demanding impossible forgetting, an economic model less extractive and more inclusive. It hasn't found either yet.

## The Contribution

The island gave Buddhism to Southeast Asia—monks carried the dharma from Sri Lanka to Thailand, Burma, Cambodia. The ancient chronicle Mahavamsa, written in Pali in the 6th century, remains a foundational Buddhist text. Tea cultivation perfected here in the 19th century shaped global commodity markets and British imperial economics. Arthur C. Clarke wrote his later novels from Colombo and set *The Fountains of Paradise* on a fictionalized Sri Lanka. Michael Ondaatje's *Running in the Family* and *Anil's Ghost* explore the island's beauty and violence with devastating precision. The novelist Romesh Gunesekera writes the exile's perspective. Sri Lankan cricket produced Muttiah Muralitharan, the most successful bowler in Test history, and the island has punched above its weight in international competition. Theravada Buddhism as practiced here preserves ancient meditation techniques now exported worldwide through vipassana movements. The ruins of Anuradhapura and Polonnaruwa demonstrate sophisticated urban planning from the first millennium. Sigiriya, the rock fortress palace, remains one of Asia's most stunning architectural achievements.

## A Moment

July 1983. Colombo. Thirteen soldiers killed by Tamil Tigers in Jaffna. The news reaches the Sinhalese south. What follows is called Black July—organized mobs, often with police complicity, attack Tamil homes and businesses in Colombo and other cities. They check identity cards, pull Tamil families from buses, set fire to shops with families trapped inside. Between 400 and 3,000 Tamils die in less than a week. Much of the property destroyed belonged to prosperous Tamil businessmen—the violence had economic as well as ethnic dimensions. The government imposes curfew too late, and some ministers are implicated in orchestrating the attacks. Perhaps 100,000 Tamils flee north. Tens of thousands eventually flee the island entirely, creating diaspora communities in Toronto, London, Paris. The pogrom transforms the conflict from political grievance into existential war. The LTTE gains recruits and international support. The wounds opened that week never properly closed. When the war finally ended in 2009, the military defeat of separatism didn't address the fear that prompted Black July—that Tamil lives are expendable, that Sinhalese Buddhist supremacy might turn violent at any moment. Reconciliation requires memory, but memory here is weaponized, each community nursing its traumas while denying the other's.
