# Chad
*Sahel's Broken Center*

Chad is where the Sahara meets the Sahel meets central Africa, a landlocked expanse larger than France and Peru with fewer paved roads than Luxembourg. It contains deserts, savannas, and fertile floodplains—three ecological zones that rarely constitute a single nation. Named for a lake that has shrunk to a tenth of its former size, Chad has spent most of its independent existence either fighting itself or serving as the battleground for other people's wars. It is simultaneously crucial to regional stability and perpetually unstable, a country whose poverty and violence obscure the fact that it shouldn't exist as currently configured, yet somehow does.

## The Land

Chad stretches from the Tibesti Mountains in the Sahara—volcanic peaks reaching 11,000 feet where Toubou pastoralists have lived for millennia—south through the Sahel belt to the relatively fertile Chari and Logone river basins near Lake Chad. The country divides sharply between the northern desert, thinly populated by Arab and Toubou peoples, and the more densely settled south, where Bantu-speaking farmers and Christians live. The capital N'Djamena sits in the southwest, barely in Chad at all, closer to Cameroon than to most of the country it governs. Lake Chad itself, once among Africa's largest lakes, has shrunk catastrophically due to irrigation, climate change, and drought—a slow-motion environmental collapse affecting thirty million people across four countries. Oil was discovered in the south in the 1970s but didn't flow until 2003, providing the government with revenue that has funded armies more than development.

## The History

The region knew empires—Kanem-Bornu flourished for a millennium around Lake Chad, controlling trans-Saharan trade routes. But French colonial rule (1900-1960) stitched together peoples with nothing in common except French occupation. The north resisted violently; France barely controlled the Tibesti until the 1930s and governed the region as a separate military territory. Independence in 1960 immediately collapsed into civil war. François Tombalbaye, a southerner, ruled through authoritarianism and ethnic favoritism until northern rebels killed him in 1975. What followed was fourteen years of rotating warlords, Libyan invasion, French intervention, and CIA funding—the "Toyota War" of 1987, where Chadian forces armed with machine guns on pickup trucks defeated Gaddafi's mechanized columns, became a case study in asymmetric warfare. Hissène Habré's brutal regime (1982-1990) gave way to Idriss Déby's equally brutal one, backed by France and the United States because he kept jihadists at bay and Libyan chaos from spreading. Déby ruled for thirty years until rebels killed him in 2021; his son Mahamat now runs a military junta, promising elections that never arrive.

## The Culture

Chad contains more than 200 ethnic groups speaking over 120 languages. Arabic and French are official, but most Chadians speak Sara, Kanembu, Wadai, or dozens of other languages at home. Islam dominates in the north and center—roughly 55% of the population—while Christianity and traditional religions prevail in the south. These divisions are not mere demographics; they structure politics, power, and violence. The north has controlled the military and presidency since 1979 despite being less populous. Kinship and ethnic loyalty matter more than national identity—clan networks provide security and economic support that the state never has. Music blends Saharan desert blues, central African rhythms, and Arabic influences. Chadian wrestlers are legendary; the sport transcends ethnic divisions in rare moments of shared culture. Gender norms are deeply conservative across most of the country; women's literacy is among the world's lowest at 14%. Polygamy is common. Life expectancy is 54 years. Two-thirds of the population lives in extreme poverty.

## The Challenge

Chad is trapped in a cycle where insecurity justifies military spending, which prevents development, which perpetuates insecurity. The army consumes 40% of government revenue while education gets 2%. Jihadist groups—Boko Haram around Lake Chad, ISIS affiliates in the Sahel—treat Chad as both target and battleground. The government responds with French and American support, positioning itself as indispensable to Western counterterrorism while remaining fundamentally predatory toward its own people. Corruption is systemic; oil revenues disappear into military budgets and elite pockets. Environmental collapse continues—Lake Chad's shrinkage displaces communities and intensifies competition for water and grazing land. Climate change is accelerating desertification. The political system offers no mechanism for peaceful transition; power passes through violence or dynastic succession. Youth unemployment exceeds 70% in cities. The country ranks last or nearly last on every human development metric. The north-south divide remains unresolved sixty years after independence. Chad is essential to regional stability precisely because its collapse would be catastrophic, which gives its rulers leverage to avoid accountability.

## The Contribution

Chad's military has become central Africa's most effective fighting force—not through professionalism but through necessity. Chadian soldiers have intervened in Mali, the Central African Republic, and Nigeria, often doing the fighting that better-funded militaries won't. They've pushed back Boko Haram when Nigerian forces couldn't. This is a grim contribution, but in a region where jihadist groups threaten to overrun entire countries, it matters. The Kanem-Bornu Empire's Islamic scholarship produced centuries of Arabic manuscripts and Quranic commentary that enriched West African Islamic tradition. Toubou and Zaghawa cultures have preserved nomadic lifeways that represent sophisticated adaptations to the Sahara's harshness. Chadian cinema, though little-known internationally, has produced powerful films about postcolonial trauma—Mahamat-Saleh Haroun's *A Screaming Man* (2010) won the Jury Prize at Cannes, a meditation on dignity under dictatorship. The country has also contributed a cautionary tale about state construction: you cannot force coherence across desert, Sahel, and savanna, between herders and farmers, Muslims and Christians, Arabs and Bantu speakers, and expect stability.

## A Moment

On April 20, 2021, President Idriss Déby died fighting rebels in the north—the first African head of state killed in combat in modern times. He'd ruled for thirty years, surviving coups and rebellions through French support and military ruthlessness. Hours after his death, the military dissolved parliament and installed his 37-year-old son Mahamat as "transitional president," promising elections in eighteen months. The African Union condemned it as a coup. France's ambassador attended Mahamat's inauguration. Three years later, the junta still governs. Déby's death revealed Chad's fundamental reality: power flows from armed force, legitimacy from international backing, and succession follows kinship not law. He died as he'd ruled—in the Tibesti, fighting, surrounded by soldiers. The transition wasn't to democracy but to his son, because in Chad, the state is whichever general controls N'Djamena and has France's phone number. The country continues—not as a nation in any meaningful sense, but as a military operation with borders, perpetually at war with itself and essential to everyone else's security. Nothing changed when Déby died except the name on the presidential palace.
