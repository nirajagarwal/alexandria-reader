# Saudi Arabia
*Oil, Islam, and the House of Saud*

The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia is the geographic and spiritual heart of the Islamic world, built on the twin foundations of Wahhabi Islam and the world's largest oil reserves. It is a young nation—founded only in 1932—yet it claims custodianship of Islam's holiest sites and exercises outsize influence over global energy markets and Muslim religious life. The country embodies a profound tension: between religious conservatism and modernizing ambition, between vast wealth and persistent social restrictions, between its role as protector of tradition and its need to prepare for a post-oil future.

**The Land**

Most of Saudi Arabia is desert—the Rub' al-Khali, the Empty Quarter, stretches across the south, one of the most inhospitable places on Earth. The country occupies most of the Arabian Peninsula, 830,000 square miles of sand, rock, and mountain. Along the Red Sea coast, the Hijaz mountains rise; along the Persian Gulf, oil fields sprawl beneath the sand. Rainfall is scarce; summer temperatures exceed 120°F. Most of the population clusters in cities—Riyadh in the interior, Jeddah on the coast, the holy cities of Mecca and Medina in the west. The eastern province, home to the oil industry, is where the country's Shia minority lives. Water comes from desalination plants; food is imported. The land shaped Bedouin culture—nomadic, tribal, harsh—before oil rewrote everything. The desert remains vast, empty, and unforgiving.

**The History**

Saudi Arabia is the third iteration of a state built on alliance between the House of Saud and Wahhabi religious authorities. The first Saudi emirate formed in 1744 when Muhammad ibn Saud, a local ruler, allied with Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab, a puritanical Islamic reformer. This partnership—political power backing religious authority—became the template. The first and second Saudi states collapsed; the third, founded by Abdulaziz ibn Saud, succeeded. Between 1902 and 1932, Abdulaziz unified the peninsula through conquest and tribal alliance, proclaiming the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia in 1932.

The discovery of oil in 1938 transformed everything. After World War II, Saudi Arabia became America's crucial Middle Eastern ally—oil for security. The kingdom modernized its infrastructure while maintaining strict religious conservatism. King Faisal's reforms in the 1960s and 70s brought education and development; the oil boom made the country phenomenally wealthy. The 1979 seizure of the Grand Mosque in Mecca by religious extremists shocked the kingdom into deeper conservatism. Saudi Arabia funded Wahhabi mosques and schools globally, spreading its austere interpretation of Islam.

The royal family maintained power through oil wealth, religious legitimacy, and ruthless suppression of dissent. Fifteen of the 9/11 hijackers were Saudi; Osama bin Laden was Saudi. Yet the kingdom remained America's ally. Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, who became de facto ruler in 2017, launched sweeping social reforms—allowing women to drive, opening cinemas, reducing religious police power—while simultaneously consolidating power, ordering the murder of journalist Jamal Khashoggi in 2018, and bombing Yemen. The country is attempting the most ambitious social transformation in its history while remaining an absolute monarchy.

**The Culture**

Saudi culture is tribal, conservative, and structured by strict interpretations of Islamic law. The country has no constitution beyond the Quran and Sunnah. Until recently, religious police enforced prayer times, gender segregation, and dress codes. Women required male guardians for basic activities; they gained the right to drive only in 2018. Public entertainment was banned; music was discouraged; cinemas were forbidden.

Yet beneath this austere surface, Saudi society is more complex. The royal family numbers in the thousands; their lavish private lives contrast with public piety. Satellite television and the internet have exposed Saudis to global culture. Young people—70% of the population is under thirty—increasingly chafe at restrictions. The recent reforms under MBS have opened public space: concerts, movies, sporting events, beach resorts. Women enter the workforce in growing numbers.

Arabic is the language; Islam is the faith—the country tolerates no other. Mecca and Medina draw millions of pilgrims annually; non-Muslims are forbidden entry. Extended family remains central. Hospitality is sacred. Coffee and dates ritualize welcome. Poetry, especially the ancient Bedouin forms, still matters. The country is modernizing rapidly, but Islam and tribal identity remain foundational. The tension between tradition and transformation defines daily life.

**The Challenge**

Saudi Arabia must prepare for the end of oil while maintaining political and social stability. Oil accounts for 90% of government revenue; the economy depends on foreign workers doing jobs Saudis won't take; unemployment among young Saudis is high. Vision 2030, MBS's plan to diversify the economy, aims to reduce oil dependence, build new industries, and attract foreign investment. But economic transformation requires social transformation—women in the workforce, more personal freedom, less religious control—which threatens the Wahhabi establishment that legitimizes the monarchy.

The social reforms are real but limited. Women can drive but activists who demanded that right remain in prison. Cinemas opened but political dissent is crushed. The murder of Khashoggi revealed the regime's brutality. The war in Yemen—a proxy conflict with Iran—has killed hundreds of thousands and damaged Saudi Arabia's reputation. The kingdom's relationship with the United States is strained but endures.

Crown Prince Mohammed has concentrated power ruthlessly. He arrested rivals in the royal family and prominent businessmen in 2017, holding them in the Ritz-Carlton until they paid billions in "settlements." This is modernization by dictatorship—social liberalization paired with political repression. The question is whether economic diversification can succeed without political liberalization, and whether the kingdom can transform without fracturing.

**The Contribution**

Saudi Arabia is the birthplace of Islam—Muhammad was born in Mecca, received revelation there, established the first Muslim community in Medina. The annual Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca is one of humanity's largest religious gatherings, drawing three million Muslims. The kingdom sees itself as guardian of Islam's holiest sites and protector of the faith.

Saudi oil stabilized global energy markets for decades. The kingdom's willingness to adjust production influenced world prices and economic stability. Saudi wealth funded development across the Muslim world—mosques, schools, universities, hospitals. Aramco, the national oil company, became one of the world's most valuable corporations and a leader in petroleum technology.

Saudi Arabia also exported Wahhabism, funding madrasas and mosques that spread its austere interpretation globally. This contribution is contested—critics argue it fueled extremism; defenders say it promoted Islamic renewal. The kingdom mediated conflicts, hosted peace negotiations, and balanced regional rivalries. Its influence over the Organization of Islamic Cooperation gives it voice in Muslim affairs worldwide.

**A Moment**

On September 26, 2017, Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman announced that women would be allowed to drive, ending the world's only ban on female drivers. The decree would take effect in June 2018. Saudi women posted videos of themselves dancing in celebration. International media hailed the reform as historic progress.

Yet several of the women who had campaigned for the right to drive—Loujain al-Hathloul, Eman al-Nafjan, Aziza al-Yousef—were arrested in May 2018, weeks before the ban lifted. They were accused of threatening national security. Some were reportedly tortured. They remained in prison while other women legally drove past their cells.

This is the paradox of Saudi reform: real change delivered by authoritarian decree, with dissent punished even when the dissidents were right. The crown prince wanted credit for liberalization but could not tolerate activists who had demanded it. The women in prison had won their cause and lost their freedom. Saudi Arabia was moving forward and tightening control simultaneously, modernizing and repressing, opening and closing at once. The driving ban ended; the activists remained behind bars. The moment captured both the kingdom's transformation and its limits—change permitted only from above, never demanded from below.
