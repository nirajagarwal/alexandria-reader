# Jordan
*The Hashemite Bulwark*

A small kingdom carved from the desert, Jordan exists as much through what it is not as what it is. Not oil-rich like its Gulf neighbors. Not ancient like Egypt or Iraq. Not militant like Syria or radicalized like Yemen. Instead, it has survived through calculated moderation, strategic positioning, and the improbable durability of a monarchy that was never supposed to last this long. In a region defined by extremes, Jordan has made stability itself a kind of achievement.

## The Land

The Jordan Rift Valley splits the country north to south—the Dead Sea at its lowest point, the Jordan River marking the western border with Israel and Palestine. East of the valley, the land rises into arid plateau, then dissolves into desert that merges with Saudi Arabia and Iraq. Ninety percent of Jordan is desert or semi-desert, receiving less than 200 millimeters of annual rainfall. Water is the defining scarcity: Jordan ranks among the world's most water-poor nations, its aquifers depleting faster than they replenish.

Most of the population clusters in the northwest—Amman sprawling across seven hills, Irbid and Zarqa forming an urban triangle. The rest is emptiness: the black basalt desert of the east, the red sands of Wadi Rum in the south, the limestone wadis of the center. Petra, the ancient Nabataean city carved into rose-red cliffs, stands as testament to civilizations that learned to thrive in such harshness. The modern kingdom hasn't mastered that same trick—it imports 98% of its energy and most of its food.

## The History

Jordan is an invention, and relatively recent. After the Ottoman Empire's collapse, the British created Transjordan in 1921 as a consolation prize for Emir Abdullah, whose Hashemite family had led the Arab Revolt but lost out in the power scramble. The Hashemites claimed descent from the Prophet Muhammad and had ruled Mecca for centuries until the Saudis drove them out. Abdullah got a patch of desert and British support; his brother Faisal got Iraq.

Independence came in 1946, but the real Jordan was forged in 1948. When Israel's establishment sent 700,000 Palestinians fleeing, Jordan absorbed the West Bank and East Jerusalem, along with hundreds of thousands of refugees. The population doubled overnight. King Abdullah I was assassinated at Al-Aqsa Mosque in 1951, supposedly for negotiating with Israel. His grandson Hussein inherited the throne at fifteen in 1952 and held it for forty-seven years through coups, wars, assassinations, and the constant threat of dissolution.

Hussein navigated the impossible. He fought Israel in 1967 and lost the West Bank—a military disaster that paradoxically simplified the kingdom's demographics. He crushed a Palestinian uprising in Black September 1970, turning tanks on refugee camps to prevent Jordan from becoming a PLO state. He stayed out of the 1973 war, made peace with Israel in 1994, and maintained ties with both America and Arab nationalists. When he died in 1999, his son Abdullah II inherited the same tightrope.

## The Culture

Jordan is bifurcated between East Bankers—the original Bedouin and village tribes who consider themselves "real" Jordanians—and Palestinians, who comprise at least half the population and dominate commerce and professional life. The monarchy depends on East Bank loyalty for security forces and tribal support; Palestinians provide economic dynamism but remain politically suspect. This split runs deep: intermarriage is common, but identity politics simmer beneath surface civility.

Islam provides cohesion—94% Sunni Muslim, with small Christian and Circassian minorities. The society is conservative without being extreme. Women work, drive, and attend university in significant numbers, yet honor killings persist in traditional areas. Amman has hipster coffee shops and nightlife; rural villages operate by tribal codes. The regime promotes "moderate Islam" as brand and buffer against both Saudi Wahhabism and Iranian Shi'ism.

Family remains the fundamental unit. Extended kinship networks distribute resources, secure jobs, and enforce social control. A man's reputation depends on his family's honor, which depends especially on women's conduct. This creates pressure for conformity even as urbanization and education push toward individual autonomy. The result is a culture of surfaces: public propriety masking private complexity.

## The Challenge

Jordan survives on subsidy and has no clear path to self-sufficiency. It receives billions in American and Gulf aid, hosts hundreds of thousands of Syrian refugees (adding to Palestinian and Iraqi populations), and faces youth unemployment above 40%. The rentier state model—distributing external money to maintain loyalty—works until the money stops or expectations outpace distribution.

The monarchy's legitimacy rests on delivering stability and services. But austerity measures demanded by the IMF—cutting fuel subsidies, raising taxes—sparked protests in 2018 and forced the prime minister's resignation. The social contract is fraying: tribes expect patronage, youth want jobs, refugees need support, and the treasury is empty. Abdullah II has responded with modest reforms and careful repression, jailing critics under cybercrime laws while loosening social restrictions just enough to vent pressure.

Geopolitical dependence compounds economic fragility. Jordan relies on Western support, Gulf largesse, and Israeli cooperation (water sharing, trade, security coordination against jihadists). Any regional upheaval—Saudi-Iranian war, renewed Israeli-Palestinian conflict, Syrian chaos spilling over—threatens the kingdom's careful balancing act. Jordan's greatest asset has always been others' interest in its survival. But interests change.

## The Contribution

Jordan has given the Middle East a model of how not to implode. In a region addicted to maximalism—pan-Arab nationalism, revolutionary Islam, ethnic cleansing, perpetual war—Jordan has practiced the unglamorous art of muddling through. This isn't nothing. Small states in tough neighborhoods often disappear. Jordan absorbed refugees when others closed borders, made peace when militancy was popular, and maintained pluralism when sectarianism raged.

Petra represents Jordan's gift to human heritage—not just the Treasury facade every tourist photographs, but a whole city carved from stone, demonstrating the Nabataean genius for water management and trade that once connected Arabia to the Mediterranean. The kingdom has preserved Roman ruins at Jerash, Byzantine mosaics at Madaba, and Crusader castles at Kerak. It has protected archaeological sites even when struggling to feed its people.

More quietly, Jordan has hosted millions of refugees—Palestinians since 1948, Iraqis since 2003, Syrians since 2011—often in circumstances that would break other states. The refugee camps have their own economy, their own despair, their own resilience. Jordan didn't choose this burden, but it has borne it without committing the atrocities that mark other refugee crises.

## A Moment

King Hussein, September 1970, pacing his palace in Amman as Palestinian fedayeen fighters control half his capital. They've hijacked airliners, declared their own state-within-a-state, and challenged his authority at every turn. Arab leaders warn him not to fight fellow Palestinians. The PLO assumes he won't risk it.

He orders the army to attack. For ten days, tanks shell refugee camps and artillery pounds fedayeen positions. Thousands die. Syrian tanks cross the border to support the Palestinians; Jordanian pilots bomb them back. When it ends, the PLO is broken, its fighters expelled to Lebanon. Hussein has crushed a national movement and survived.

Afterward, he visits wounded soldiers and Palestinian civilians. He cries at military funerals and negotiates compensation for destroyed homes. He knows what he's done—preserved his throne by killing people his kingdom claims as citizens. The contradiction defines Jordan: a state that exists because it repeatedly chooses survival over justice, pragmatism over principle, and has somehow convinced enough people that this isn't cowardice but wisdom. Whether it's wisdom depends on what you think states are for.
