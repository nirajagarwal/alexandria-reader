# Mexico
*Revolution's Unfinished Business*

Mexico exists in permanent tension between its spectacular past and its struggling present, between the grandeur of ancient civilizations and colonial baroque and the violence of cartels, between a culture that has shaped the hemisphere and an economy that cannot keep its people home. It is the United States' twin and opposite—bound by geography and history, divided by everything else. What Spain and indigenous empires created, what revolution promised, what NAFTA accelerated—none of it resolved. Mexico remains caught between worlds.

## The Land

Two mountain ranges—the Sierra Madre Oriental and Occidental—bracket a high central plateau where most Mexicans live. The capital sits in a drained lakebed at 7,350 feet, surrounded by volcanoes. Coastal plains on both sides: the Gulf humid and tropical, the Pacific varied from Baja's deserts to Oaxaca's beaches. The north is arid, the south is jungle. The Yucatán Peninsula juts flat and limestone into the Caribbean. The Rio Grande—called Río Bravo here—marks half the northern border; the rest is fence and wall and desert. 

The land is rich in silver, copper, oil. It produces corn, beans, peppers, tomatoes, avocados, coffee, sugarcane. The geography has always fragmented the country—mountains isolate regions, creating distinct cultures. Mexico City dominates, but regional identities persist: norteños, chilangos, jarochos, yucatecos. The land shapes everything.

## The History

Three civilizations before Spain: the Olmec, the Maya, the Aztec. Tenochtitlán, the Aztec capital, was larger than any European city when Cortés arrived in 1519. Spain conquered through disease as much as warfare, destroyed temples, built churches on their ruins, created New Spain—the richest colony in the Americas. Silver from Zacatecas and Guanajuato financed Spanish empire for three centuries. The colonial system created haciendas, peonage, a rigid caste hierarchy based on Spanish blood quantum. Mestizaje became the national reality, though whiteness retained privilege.

Independence came in 1821 after a decade of war, but the colony's inequalities persisted. Texas broke away in 1836, then the U.S.-Mexican War (1846-48) took half the national territory—California, Nevada, Utah, most of Arizona and New Mexico, parts of Colorado and Wyoming. The wound never healed. Mexico lost territory; the United States gained an empire and a guilty conscience it refuses to acknowledge.

The French briefly installed Emperor Maximilian (1864-67); Benito Juárez, a Zapotec lawyer, had him executed and restored the republic. Then came Porfirio Díaz's 35-year dictatorship (1876-1911): modernization, railroads, foreign investment, brutal repression. The Revolution exploded in 1910—Madero, Zapata, Villa, ten years of chaos and civil war. The PRI (Institutional Revolutionary Party) emerged from the wreckage in 1929 and ruled Mexico as a one-party state for 71 years, combining revolutionary rhetoric with authoritarian control.

Neoliberal reforms began in the 1980s. NAFTA in 1994 opened the economy just as the Zapatista uprising in Chiapas exposed its costs. The PRI finally lost the presidency in 2000. Democracy arrived, but cartels filled the vacuum.

## The Culture

Spanish, but transformed. Mexicans speak a distinct Spanish, mixing Nahuatl words (chocolate, tomato, coyote, avocado) with regional slang. Indigenous languages persist—Nahuatl, Maya, Mixtec, Zapotec—but Spanish dominates. Catholicism infused with pre-Hispanic elements: the Virgin of Guadalupe is Mexico's true deity, appearing brown-skinned to an indigenous peasant in 1531. Day of the Dead blends Catholic All Saints with Aztec ancestor veneration—death is familiar, intimate, mocked.

Family is central: loyalty, obligation, extended networks. Machismo persists despite feminist challenges—men prove masculinity through dominance, women through endurance. Class divisions run deep: the wealthy speak English, vacation abroad, live in fortified neighborhoods; the poor migrate north or survive informally. Race matters silently—indigenous features correlate with poverty, European features with privilege, though official ideology celebrates mestizaje.

Mexican culture has conquered the world through food: tacos, mole, tamales, pozole, the infinite regional cuisines Spain never touched. Tequila and mezcal. Mariachi and norteño music. Frida Kahlo and Diego Rivera. Telenovelas. Lucha libre. Fútbol passion approaching religion. A certain fatalism, dark humor, pride mixed with self-mockery. Beauty and violence intertwined. Life lived intensely because death is always close.

## The Challenge

The cartels. What began as drug trafficking evolved into a parallel state: the Sinaloa Cartel, CJNG (Jalisco New Generation), the fragmenting of old organizations into dozens of violent gangs. They don't just move drugs—they extort businesses, kidnap, control territory, kill journalists and mayors. The government response oscillates between militarization and accommodation. Over 350,000 dead since 2006. Mass graves. Disappeared students. Journalists murdered at a higher rate than anywhere outside war zones.

The violence stems from American demand for drugs and the weapon flow south, from corruption throughout Mexican institutions, from poverty that makes cartel wages attractive, from a justice system that prosecutes 2% of crimes. AMLO's "hugs not bullets" approach changed the rhetoric but not the reality. Entire states are contested territory. Families pay protection money. Politicians choose between bribes and bullets.

Meanwhile, emigration drains talent and labor. Over 12 million Mexican-born people live in the United States—10% of Mexico's population. Remittances flow back ($60 billion annually), but whole villages are women, children, and old men. Economic growth is anemic. Inequality persists. NAFTA created maquiladoras along the border—assembly plants with low wages, no unions, environmental damage. The country is stuck between development models, unable to escape the middle-income trap.

## The Contribution

The Revolution's ideals—land reform, worker rights, anti-imperialism, indigenismo—influenced Latin American politics for a century. Mexican muralism (Rivera, Orozco, Siqueiros) made public art political and accessible, influencing artists worldwide. Octavio Paz won the Nobel Prize for poetry that explored Mexican identity and universal human questions. Carlos Fuentes, Juan Rulfo, Elena Poniatowska shaped Latin American literature. Cinema's Golden Age produced directors like Luis Buñuel (Spanish-born, Mexican-made) and Emilio Fernández.

Pre-Columbian civilizations gave the world corn, chocolate, vanilla, tomatoes, rubber, the concept of zero (Maya mathematics), astronomical precision, massive urban planning. Mexican cuisine—designated UNESCO intangible cultural heritage—influenced global food culture. Tequila and mezcal conquered global bar culture. Lucha libre influenced professional wrestling everywhere.

And the Mexican diaspora reshaped the United States—demographically, culturally, culinarily, politically. Mexican-Americans built the Southwest's infrastructure, picked its crops, formed its largest immigrant community. They kept Spanish alive and made bilingualism normal. They changed American music through Tejano, norteño, and regional Mexican genres. They proved immigration fuels rather than drains economies.

## A Moment

1968, October 2. Students have been protesting for months—demanding democracy, denouncing the PRI's authoritarianism, embarrassing the government days before Mexico City hosts the Olympics. Thousands gather at Tlatelolco, Plaza de las Tres Culturas—named for the Aztec ruins, colonial church, and modern housing project that surround it, representing Mexico's layered history.

The army surrounds them. Snipers position on rooftops. At 6:10 p.m., flares arc across the darkening sky. Then automatic weapons fire. Soldiers shoot into the crowd. Bodies fall. People flee, trampling others, crushing against walls. The shooting continues for hours. The government claims 30 dead. Witnesses report hundreds. Bodies disappeared into military trucks. Blood washed away before dawn. The Olympics began ten days later. No one was ever prosecuted.

Tlatelolco revealed the contradiction at Mexico's heart: revolutionary rhetoric masking authoritarian violence, modernization built on repression, the state willing to massacre its children rather than share power. The students wanted what the Revolution promised. The PRI defended what it had become. Elena Poniatowska documented the massacre in *La noche de Tlatelolco*, preserving testimony the government wanted buried.

The plaza still exists, three cultures layered and haunted. Mexico still lives in that contradiction—between what it claims and what it does, between its revolutionary ideals and its violent reality.
