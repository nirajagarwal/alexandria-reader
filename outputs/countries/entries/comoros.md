# Comoros
*The Coup Islands*

Four volcanic islands scattered between Madagascar and Mozambique, Comoros is among the world's poorest and most unstable nations—a place where fragrant ylang-ylang blossoms and endemic species flourish amid chronic political upheaval. Since independence in 1975, it has endured more than twenty coups and coup attempts, an average of nearly one per year. Yet life continues: fishermen launch outriggers into the Mozambique Channel, women distill essential oils from flowers, and a complex Swahili-Arab-African culture persists despite the chaos that periodically erupts in Moroni, the capital.

## The Land

The archipelago comprises three main islands under Comorian sovereignty—Grande Comore, Anjouan, and Mohéli—plus Mayotte, which voted to remain French and is now an overseas department. Grande Comore rises around Mount Karthala, an active volcano whose eruptions have shaped the island's black lava fields and ash-enriched soil. The climate is tropical, hot and humid, with monsoon rains that sustain forests rich in endemic plants and animals found nowhere else on earth. The islands are scattered across 1,100 square miles of ocean, isolated enough to have developed unique species—lemurs, fruit bats, coelacanths in the deep waters—but connected enough to the Swahili coast to have absorbed centuries of maritime trade. Most people cluster in coastal villages. The land yields vanilla, cloves, and ylang-ylang, though production has declined with political instability.

## The History

Settled by Austronesian seafarers, Bantu migrants, and Arab traders over successive centuries, the islands became a Swahili-Muslim society, part of the Indian Ocean trading world linking East Africa, Arabia, and Asia. Sultanates ruled each island until French colonization in the late nineteenth century. France governed Comoros as part of Madagascar until 1946, then as a separate territory. In 1974, referendums showed overwhelming support for independence on three islands; Mayotte voted to stay French. Ahmed Abdallah declared independence unilaterally in 1975. Within weeks, a mercenary named Bob Denard staged the first of several coups, beginning a pattern that has never truly ended. Denard became the shadow power behind multiple regimes, returning repeatedly until 1995. Since independence, Comoros has seen presidents assassinated, overthrown, or fleeing into exile. Anjouan and Mohéli briefly seceded in 1997, reunifying only after military intervention. Constitutional changes in 2001 created a rotating presidency among islands, attempting to balance power. It hasn't stopped the coups.

## The Culture

Comoros is deeply Islamic—nearly the entire population is Sunni Muslim—yet retains pre-Islamic matrilineal traditions unusual in the Muslim world. Women inherit land and homes; men must prove their worth through the "grand marriage," an elaborate, bankruptingly expensive ceremony required for full social status. A man who hasn't completed the grand marriage remains, in traditional terms, a child regardless of age. The ceremony can cost ten years' income, driving men to work abroad—primarily in France—to afford it. The culture is Swahili-speaking, though French and Arabic are official languages. Poetry, music, and dance blend African and Arab influences. Villages remain tight-knit, organized around mosques and family compounds. The diaspora, particularly in France, often outnumbers those remaining on the islands and sends crucial remittances. Yet there is pride in Comorian identity—small as the nation is, people fiercely resist further fragmentation.

## The Challenge

Chronic instability has made development nearly impossible. Each coup disrupts what little governance exists. Infrastructure crumbles. Foreign investment vanishes. The economy depends on subsistence agriculture, declining cash crops, and remittances from the diaspora. More than forty percent of the population lives below the poverty line. The secession of Mayotte—the richest island—removed the archipelago's economic anchor; Mayotte's GDP per capita as a French department is now twenty times higher than the Comorian average. The rotating presidency, meant to prevent domination by Grande Comore, has instead created a system where ambition and grievance simmer on every island. Young men see no future except working abroad. Corruption is endemic. Political violence feels inevitable. The most recent coup attempt was in 2013; the question is not if another will come, but when.

## The Contribution

Comoros has given the world ylang-ylang, the intensely fragrant flower essential to perfume-making—the islands produce a significant share of global supply, their oils forming the heart of classic fragrances. The discovery of coelacanths in Comorian waters in 1938 revolutionized understanding of evolutionary biology; these "living fossils," fish thought extinct for 65 million years, still swim in the deep channels between islands. Comorian musicians developed twarab, a distinctive fusion of Swahili, Arab, and Indian Ocean musical traditions, poetry sung to the oud and traditional drums. The nation's unique matrilineal system within an Islamic context offers anthropologists insights into how cultures blend and adapt rather than simply replace one another. And despite its struggles, Comoros represents African persistence—a small nation refusing to disappear despite every reason to fragment.

## A Moment

In 1995, Bob Denard and a group of mercenaries staged what would be his final coup, overthrowing President Said Mohamed Djohar. For three days, the white mercenary and his men—some of whom had been involved in Comorian politics for twenty years—controlled the islands. Then French paratroopers arrived, arresting Denard and restoring some semblance of order. Denard, then in his sixties, was flown to France in handcuffs, ending his bizarre career as Africa's last white mercenary king. Photographs from those days show something surreal: a grizzled French adventurer in tropical uniform, flanked by Comorian soldiers, claiming to defend stability. He had overthrown governments, installed presidents, enriched himself, and repeatedly returned as if the islands were his personal theater. For many Comorians, the images captured a humiliating truth—their country had been so unstable, so marginalized, that one foreign mercenary could treat it as a plaything for decades. Yet even that indignity couldn't break the nation. After Denard's arrest, Comoros stumbled forward, still poor, still fractious, still somehow itself.
