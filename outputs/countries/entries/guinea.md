# Guinea
*Wealth Squandered*

Guinea sits at the crossroads of West Africa with more natural wealth than most nations could dream of—bauxite enough to supply a third of the world's aluminum, iron ore, gold, diamonds, and rivers capable of powering the entire region. Yet its people remain among the poorest on earth. This is not the story of a nation lacking resources. It is the story of how military rule, ethnic politics, and systematic corruption can transform natural abundance into collective misery.

## The Land

The Atlantic coast spreads into mangrove swamps and coastal plains before rising through the Fouta Djallon highlands—a vast plateau of red laterite hills and deep valleys where the Niger, Gambia, and Senegal rivers begin their journeys. The mountains hold iron deposits so pure they were worked in pre-colonial times. Moving east, the land descends into savanna and finally the forested Guinea Highlands along the Liberian border, where Mount Nimba rises, rich with ore. The climate shifts with elevation—coastal humidity giving way to cooler highlands, then tropical heat in the south. Beneath it all lies bauxite, massive deposits of the aluminum ore that should have funded development for generations. Instead, the contracts enriched foreign companies and local elites while ordinary Guineans saw little return.

## The History

Ancient empires—Ghana, Mali, Songhai—held sway over different parts of what would become Guinea, with the Malinke people forming powerful kingdoms in the interior. The Fouta Djallon became an Islamic theocracy in the eighteenth century, a Fulani state that raided for slaves and resisted French colonial expansion longer than its neighbors. France eventually conquered the territory in the 1890s, incorporating it into French West Africa.

Independence in 1958 came dramatically. Sékou Touré told Charles de Gaulle that Guineans preferred "poverty in freedom to riches in slavery" and became the only French African colony to vote against remaining in the French Community. De Gaulle withdrew everything—ripping out telephone lines, burning documents, taking the national treasury. Touré turned to the Soviet Union and launched twenty-six years of revolutionary dictatorship, nationalizing the economy, suppressing ethnic and religious identities, imprisoning thousands in Camp Boiro where torture and death were routine. His paranoia grew monstrous; even family members weren't safe.

Lansana Conté seized power after Touré's death in 1984, ruling for twenty-four years through a combination of ethnic favoritism, military patronage, and resource extraction deals that bled the country dry. Alpha Condé won democratic elections in 2010, promising reform, but changed the constitution to seek a third term and was overthrown by Colonel Mamady Doumbouya in 2021. The pattern holds: brief hope, military intervention, continued extraction.

## The Culture

Guinea's ethnic diversity—Fulani, Malinke, Susu, and smaller groups—defines its social structure and political tensions. The Fulani dominate the Fouta Djallon highlands, historically Islamic merchants and cattle herders. The Malinke, descended from the Mali Empire, control much of Upper Guinea and maintain traditions of oral history through griots—hereditary bards whose performances preserve centuries of genealogy and wisdom. The coastal Susu have long been traders and fishermen.

Islam predominates, practiced with variations that incorporate older spiritual traditions. Polygamy remains common; women's status varies by ethnic group and education level, but inequality persists across the board. Extended family networks provide social security in the absence of state welfare, but also create obligations that drain individual resources into collective maintenance.

Guinean music exploded onto the world stage through artists like Mory Kanté and Bembeya Jazz National. The kora, the twenty-one-string harp-lute, and the balafon, a wooden xylophone, carry Malinke musical traditions into contemporary genres. Sékou Touré, for all his brutality, promoted cultural expression as long as it served the state. After his fall, the music became freer and angrier.

Family and ethnic loyalty often trump national identity. Trust extends to kin networks; beyond them, wariness prevails. This makes collective action difficult and corruption easy—you take care of your own because the state certainly won't take care of anyone.

## The Challenge

Guinea should be wealthy. Instead, it exemplifies the resource curse—abundant minerals, persistent poverty, and political instability feeding on each other. Bauxite mining contracts historically favored foreign companies and connected elites over national development. Infrastructure crumbles; roads deteriorate; electricity reaches only a fraction of the population. Conakry, the capital, floods regularly because drainage systems built decades ago were never maintained.

Ethnic politics poison governance. Each leader has favored his ethnic group, distributing resources and positions tribally rather than nationally. This ensures that every change of power feels existential to those losing it, making democratic transitions nearly impossible. When Condé, a Malinke, won power, Fulani and Susu communities felt excluded. When he manipulated the constitution for a third term, young people rioted—not just against him, but against a system that perpetually failed them.

Corruption permeates everything from mining contracts to petty bureaucracy. Police demand bribes at checkpoints; teachers expect payment to pass students; hospital staff require extra fees for treatment. The system runs on extraction at every level.

Meanwhile, Guinea remains a potential Ebola reservoir—the 2014 outbreak that killed thousands across West Africa began in a Guinean village. Public health infrastructure barely exists. Life expectancy hovers around sixty years.

## The Contribution

Guinea gave the world Sékou Touré's defiant "no" to colonial paternalism—the first French African colony to choose complete independence regardless of cost. That moment inspired anti-colonial movements everywhere, even if what followed diminished its glory.

Guinean music contributed the kora to global consciousness and produced artists who brought Malinke traditions into conversation with jazz, funk, and electronic music. The country's oral traditions, maintained through griot families, preserve historical memory in cultures where writing came late.

Guinea's rivers literally sustain neighboring countries—the Niger, Senegal, and Gambia rivers all rise in the Fouta Djallon, making Guinea the "water tower of West Africa." Hydroelectric potential remains largely untapped, but the water itself flows to millions downstream.

The Guinean diaspora, fleeing dictatorship and poverty, has built communities across Europe and America, maintaining cultural traditions while sending remittances home that often exceed foreign aid.

## A Moment

In the Simandou Range, some of the world's richest iron ore deposits run for eighty miles through forested mountains. International mining companies have fought for decades over extraction rights—Rio Tinto, Vale, Chinese firms—signing contracts, renegotiating, being expelled, returning. Each time, Guinean officials promise development: schools, hospitals, roads, jobs. 

A village elder in the region described watching company after company arrive with promises. "They show us pictures of what will be built. We sign papers we cannot read. The foreigners become rich. Our children still walk hours to fetch water." The contracts themselves, when leaked, reveal terms that would shame any government: tax breaks, infrastructure obligations unfulfilled, royalty payments that somehow never reach the treasury.

Simandou represents both Guinea's promise and its curse. The ore is there, wealth literally lying in the hills. But the gap between geological fortune and human flourishing seems unbridgeable—not because the resources lack value, but because the institutions meant to manage them serve extraction rather than development. The iron will eventually be mined. Whether Guineans benefit remains, after decades of disappointment, an open question.
