# Belarus
*Europe's Last Dictatorship*

Belarus exists in a state of enforced suspension—a country that never quite completed its transition from Soviet republic to independent nation, now frozen under one man's authoritarian grip. Landlocked between Russia and the European Union, it has become what its neighbors might have been: a place where the Cold War never entirely ended, where Soviet aesthetics and political structures persist not as nostalgia but as governing reality. It is a nation of ten million people whose distinct identity has been systematically obscured, first by empire, then by their own government.

## The Land

Belarus is flat, forested, and watery. The Pripyat Marshes in the south form one of Europe's largest wetlands—a landscape of birch forests, peat bogs, and slow rivers that has historically made the region difficult to traverse and easier to hide in. Nearly 40% of the country remains forested, much of it mixed pine and birch that turns the landscape silver-green in summer, gray in the long winter. The land is poor in minerals and natural resources but rich in potash, used for fertilizer. The Dnieper River runs through the eastern part of the country. This is terrain that armies have crossed repeatedly but rarely held easily—open enough for invasion, dense enough for resistance. The southern reaches remain contaminated by Chernobyl's fallout, the 1986 disaster just across the Ukrainian border that poisoned a fifth of Belarusian territory.

## The History

Belarus has spent most of its history under someone else's rule. The medieval Grand Duchy of Lithuania encompassed much of modern Belarus; its Slavic population spoke a language distinct from Russian and Polish, but political power belonged to others. The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth absorbed the region until the partitions of the late 18th century delivered it to the Russian Empire. A brief moment of independence came in 1918—the Belarusian People's Republic, crushed within months by Bolsheviks and Poles. The Soviet Belorussian SSR was established, then ravaged by Stalin's purges and collectivization.

World War II destroyed the place. Nazi Germany's Operation Barbarossa swept through in 1941; Belarus became a killing ground. An estimated 2.3 million people—a quarter of the population—died. Entire villages were burned with their inhabitants inside. The Jewish population, once nearly a million, was almost entirely exterminated. Partisan resistance in the forests was fierce. The Soviets rebuilt the cities as monuments to socialist reconstruction.

Independence came in 1991 not through revolution but through the Soviet Union's collapse. For a few years, Belarus stumbled toward democracy and a distinct national identity. Then in 1994, Alexander Lukashenko was elected president. He has remained in power ever since through falsified elections, systematic repression, and alliance with Russia. Every attempted democratic movement has been crushed.

## The Culture

Belarusian culture exists in a state of suppression and resistance. The Belarusian language—related to but distinct from Russian—is spoken by perhaps a third of the population, discouraged by decades of Russification and current state policy that treats Russian as the language of modernity. The older generation often speaks a mixed form called *trasianka*. The government promotes Soviet-style cultural production: state orchestras, folk ensembles performing approved traditions, monuments to the Great Patriotic War.

But beneath this official culture, another Belarus persists. Belarusian literature has a rich tradition—the poet Yanka Kupala, the modernist Vasil Bykaŭ writing about war's moral compromises. Contemporary artists and musicians work in opposition or exile. The traditional white-red-white flag from the 1918 republic has become a symbol of resistance, banned by the state. Folk traditions survive in villages—spring festivals, harvest rituals, wooden architecture. The country is religiously mixed: Orthodox Christianity dominant, with significant Catholic and small Jewish communities. Family structures remain traditional, shaped by economic hardship and political repression.

## The Challenge

Belarus is a country held hostage by its president. Lukashenko has constructed a personalist dictatorship supported by security services, subsidies from Russia, and systematic crushing of dissent. Elections are theatrical. Independent media has been eliminated. Civil society exists underground or in exile. The 2020 presidential election, blatantly falsified, triggered the largest protests in Belarusian history—hundreds of thousands in the streets demanding change. Lukashenko responded with mass arrests, torture, and forced confessions. Thousands fled the country.

The regime survives through Russian support—Belarus has become increasingly dependent on Moscow economically and politically, hosting Russian military forces, participating in the 2022 invasion of Ukraine as a staging ground. This dependency erases what little sovereignty Belarus retained. Economically, the country operates a hybrid system: state-owned Soviet-era factories producing tractors and trucks, alongside a small private sector. Corruption is endemic. The younger generation sees no future under current conditions.

## The Contribution

Belarus gave the world Svetlana Alexievich, who won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 2015 for her oral histories that document Soviet and post-Soviet suffering with devastating precision. Her books *Voices from Chernobyl* and *The Unwomanly Face of War* preserve testimonies the state would prefer forgotten. The country produced Marc Chagall, whose dreamlike paintings drew on his childhood in Vitebsk. Vasil Bykaŭ's novels explore moral choice under totalitarianism with unflinching clarity.

Belarusian software engineers developed World of Tanks, one of the most successful video games globally, and the Viber messaging app. The country has a surprisingly strong tech sector, young people coding their way toward something beyond state factories. The democratic opposition, now largely in exile, has shown remarkable persistence and courage—ordinary people risking everything for the possibility of freedom. This resistance, though defeated for now, adds to the long European tradition of struggling against authoritarianism.

## A Moment

August 2020. Thousands gather in Minsk's Independence Square despite riot police. A woman climbs a pedestal and holds up three fingers—the symbol of resistance. The crowd roars. They are teachers, factory workers, IT specialists, pensioners. They chant "Long live Belarus!" in Belarusian, reclaiming the language. They wave the white-red-white flags their grandparents carried in 1918. Young women in white dresses form human chains, daring the police to strike them. For a few weeks, it feels like the entire country has awoken from decades of enforced sleep.

Then the security services move in. Thousands are arrested and beaten in detention centers. Protesters are given long prison sentences. Leaders flee or disappear. The dictator gives a speech holding a rifle. Russia signals its support. By autumn, the protests are suppressed. The prisoners remain. But something changed: an entire generation now knows they are not alone in wanting a different Belarus. The white-red-white flag, even outlawed, has been planted in people's minds. You cannot un-remember that the country rose up together. You cannot forget what nearly was.
