# Angola
*Oil, Diamonds, and the Long Peace*

Angola emerged from nearly three decades of civil war in 2002 as one of Africa's most resource-rich and resource-cursed nations. Vast petroleum reserves and diamond deposits have generated enormous wealth while failing to lift most Angolans from poverty. The ruling MPLA, in power since independence, has built an authoritarian petrostate where elite enrichment coexists with one of the world's highest infant mortality rates. This is a country still learning what peace means after generations knew only war.

## The Land

Angola sprawls across southwestern Africa—1.2 million square kilometers of Atlantic coastline, central highlands, and the Kalahari's northern edge. The coastal plain, where most Angolans live, is hot and arid. Inland, the plateau rises to temperate highlands, fertile for agriculture. Rivers like the Cuanza and Cunene carve through the landscape, though infrastructure decay leaves much of the interior isolated. The Cabinda enclave, separated from Angola proper by the Democratic Republic of Congo, holds some of the country's richest oil fields. Offshore petroleum deposits in the Atlantic have made Angola sub-Saharan Africa's second-largest oil producer. Diamonds come from the northeast, in provinces that saw the war's worst violence.

## The History

The Portuguese arrived in 1483, finding the Kingdom of Kongo. Unlike most of Africa, Angola's colonization began early—Luanda, founded in 1575, became the slave trade's primary African port. Millions of enslaved Angolans crossed the Atlantic, especially to Brazil. Portuguese rule was extractive and brutal, leaving almost nothing at independence.

Liberation movements formed in the 1950s. The MPLA (Marxist, urban, Mbundu), FNLA (anticommunist, Bakongo), and UNITA (Maoist-turned-anticommunist, Ovimbundu) fought Portugal, then each other. Independence came in 1975 as Portugal's dictatorship collapsed. Immediately, civil war erupted. The MPLA, backed by Cuba and the Soviet Union, held Luanda. UNITA, backed by the United States and apartheid South Africa, controlled the interior. The war lasted twenty-seven years, killed half a million, displaced millions more. Cease-fires failed repeatedly. Only in 2002, when UNITA leader Jonas Savimbi was killed in combat, did the war finally end.

## The Culture

Angola's culture bears deep Portuguese influence—the language, Catholicism, the architecture of Luanda's decaying colonial core. But beneath this lies enormous ethnic diversity. The Ovimbundu (37% of the population) dominate the highlands. The Mbundu (25%) cluster around Luanda. The Bakongo (13%) inhabit the north. Dozens of smaller groups maintain distinct languages and traditions.

Kuduro, Angola's hyperkinetic electronic dance music, has conquered Portuguese-speaking Africa and beyond—a rare cultural export from a country better known for war and extraction. Angolan literature, especially the work of José Luandino Vieira and Pepetela, chronicles colonialism and civil war with unflinching honesty. Kinship networks remain strong, crucial for survival in a country where the state provides little. In Luanda, oil wealth has created a tiny elite living in fortified compounds while the majority inhabit musseques—sprawling slums without basic services. The gap between haves and have-nots is among Africa's widest.

## The Challenge

Angola suffers from the resource curse in its most acute form. Oil revenues account for 95% of exports and fund the government, yet 40% of Angolans live below the poverty line. President João Lourenço, who took power in 2017, has pursued anticorruption efforts against his predecessor's family but has maintained authoritarian control. The MPLA has never lost an election; meaningful opposition is marginalized.

Economic diversification remains aspirational. Agriculture, once Angola's strength, collapsed during the war and has never recovered—the country imports most of its food. Infrastructure is crumbling. Outside Luanda, roads, hospitals, and schools barely function. Land mines still litter former battlefields. Infant mortality stands at 80 per 1,000 births—among the world's highest. Youth unemployment approaches 60%. When oil prices fall, the economy convulses. Angola borrowed heavily from China during the boom years; debt service now consumes massive portions of the budget. The peace dividend never arrived for ordinary Angolans.

## The Contribution

Angola gave the world proof that guerrilla movements could defeat European colonialism. The liberation struggle inspired independence movements across southern Africa. Cuban troops defending the MPLA against South African invasion in 1975-1976 and again at Cuito Cuanavale in 1987-1988 contributed to apartheid's eventual collapse—Pretoria could no longer sustain its regional wars.

Culturally, kuduro has become a genuinely global genre, Angola's most visible export beyond petroleum. Angolan literature offers some of Portuguese Africa's most powerful writing. The country's successful transition from total war to stable (if authoritarian) peace, despite the odds, provides lessons—however ambiguous—for other post-conflict societies. That Angola has not fractured despite ethnic diversity, artificial borders, and decades of externally fueled warfare represents a fragile but real achievement.

## A Moment

February 22, 2002: Jonas Savimbi, the charismatic, brutal leader who kept Angola's civil war burning for twenty-seven years, dies in a government ambush in Moxico province. He is sixty-seven, has been fighting since 1966. Within weeks, UNITA commanders, exhausted and leaderless, sign a cease-fire. The war simply stops.

Luanda erupts in celebration, but across the country, millions of displaced people face a devastating question: where is home? Villages are destroyed, families scattered, land mined. The MPLA-controlled government offers no reconciliation process, no truth commission, just silence about the war's atrocities. Many former UNITA fighters are absorbed into the military or marginalized into poverty. The dead are not counted. Mass graves go unmarked. Peace arrives not as healing but as exhaustion—everyone too broken to keep fighting. Two decades later, most Angolans under thirty have known only peace, but they inherit a country where war's consequences—inequality, corruption, authoritarian control, underdevelopment—remain unaddressed. The guns are silent. The reckoning has not come.
