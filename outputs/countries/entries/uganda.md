# Uganda
*The Pearl Cracked Open*

Churchill called it "the pearl of Africa"—and the land justifies the claim. But the nation built atop that land has cycled through violence and recovery for six decades, never quite achieving the stability its geography seems to promise. Uganda sits at the headwaters of the Nile, blessed with soil and rain, cursed by the strongmen who've ruled it. The question it poses is whether natural abundance can overcome political pathology.

## The Land

Uganda straddles the equator in East Africa's great lake region, bordered by Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and South Sudan. Lake Victoria—the Nile's source—forms its southern border. Mountains rise in the east (Mount Elgon) and west (the Rwenzori range, the fabled "Mountains of the Moon"). Between them: fertile highlands, savannas, and the lush green that made Churchill reach for superlatives. The climate is temperate despite the equatorial location—altitude moderates the heat. Rainfall is reliable. The soil grows coffee, tea, cotton, maize, bananas. Natural resources include copper, cobalt, and recently discovered oil. The land should support prosperity. Often it has supported armies instead.

## The History

British colonizers arrived in the 1890s, ruling through traditional kingdoms—especially Buganda, the dominant southern polity whose language (Luganda) and people (Baganda) gave the country its name. Independence came in 1962 under Milton Obote, who abolished the kingdoms in 1966, triggering the first of Uganda's recurring upheavals. Idi Amin seized power in 1971 and unleashed eight years of murderous chaos—perhaps 300,000 dead, the Asian merchant class expelled, the economy shattered, religious and ethnic groups targeted arbitrarily. Tanzanian forces and Ugandan exiles overthrew Amin in 1979. Obote returned, triggering civil war. Yoweri Museveni's National Resistance Army fought from the bush, taking power in 1986.

Museveni brought stability and economic growth—the model African reformer of the 1990s. But power corrupted predictably. He amended the constitution to eliminate term limits, ruled increasingly through patronage and repression, and has clung to the presidency for nearly four decades. The Lord's Resistance Army, a messianic cult led by Joseph Kony, terrorized northern Uganda from the late 1980s through the mid-2000s, abducting perhaps 60,000 children as soldiers and sex slaves before being pushed into Congo and the Central African Republic.

## The Culture

Uganda contains over 50 ethnic groups speaking more than 40 languages—Bantu peoples in the south (Baganda, Banyankole, Basoga), Nilotic groups in the north (Acholi, Langi), and others throughout. English is the official language; Swahili is widely spoken. The north-south divide matters: colonial favoritism toward southern kingdoms created lasting resentment; Amin and Obote both hailed from the north, Museveni from the west. Religion splits roughly between Christian (majority, both Catholic and Protestant), Muslim (significant minority), and traditional beliefs. Extended family networks remain central to social organization; clan and ethnic ties often matter more than national identity.

Music and dance are vibrantly diverse—each group maintains distinctive traditions. Kampala's contemporary art and music scene blends traditional forms with global influences. Ugandan literature emerged powerfully in the diaspora during the Amin years; writers like Moses Isegawa and Jackee Budesta Batanda have chronicled the violence and resilience. Gender norms remain largely patriarchal despite constitutional guarantees; women gained political representation through reserved parliamentary seats but still face significant inequality. Homosexuality is criminalized and deeply stigmatized—Parliament nearly passed a death penalty law in 2023 before international pressure forced moderation to life imprisonment.

## The Challenge

Museveni won't let go. The "no-party democracy" of the 1990s has calcified into one-man rule sustained by military patronage, electoral manipulation, and periodic violence against opposition. The 2021 election saw security forces kill dozens of protesters; opposition leader Bobi Wine (Robert Kyagulanyi), a popular musician-turned-politician, was placed under house arrest. Uganda's politics have become gerontocratic—Museveni is 80, having ruled since age 41. The army is the real power structure; political reform would require its consent, and the generals have too much to lose.

Oil revenue, expected to begin flowing from Lake Albert fields, could either fund development or deepen the curse of patronage. Corruption already permeates government; new wealth without accountability would likely entrench existing power structures rather than transform them. Youth unemployment remains high despite economic growth. Northern regions still lag behind the south, and the LRA's legacy—trauma, displacement, lost education—persists in Acholi communities.

## The Contribution

Uganda gave the world matoke—the East African banana stew that sustains millions. More substantially: it pioneered Africa's most successful response to HIV/AIDS in the 1990s, when Museveni's government broke taboos and promoted the "ABC" approach (Abstinence, Be faithful, use Condoms). Infection rates dropped from 15% to 5% when other countries still denied the crisis existed. The model influenced global public health.

Ugandan peacekeepers have served throughout Africa—Somalia, South Sudan, the Central African Republic—often showing more discipline than other regional forces. The country has hosted massive refugee populations (now over 1.5 million, one of the world's largest) with a relatively progressive approach: refugees can work, move freely, access services. This generosity, rooted partly in Uganda's own experience of displacement, has made it a model for refugee policy.

Philly Bongoley Lutaaya, a musician who publicly declared his HIV status in 1989 and toured the country raising awareness until his death in 1990, became Africa's first major public figure to fight stigma through personal disclosure. His courage shifted national consciousness.

## A Moment

July 4, 1976: Israeli commandos raid Entebbe Airport to free hostages held by Palestinian and German hijackers with Idi Amin's complicity. The raid is flawless—most hostages saved, hijackers killed, only one Israeli soldier dead (Yonatan Netanyahu, brother of the future prime minister). Uganda's humiliation is complete: the world watches Amin's forces exposed as impotent, his airport violated, his rhetoric revealed as empty.

But the raid's success obscures what happens after. Amin, enraged, murders Dora Bloch, a 75-year-old British-Israeli hostage who had been taken to a Kampala hospital before the raid. He orders the killing of hundreds of Kenyans in Uganda (Kenya had allowed the Israeli planes to refuel). The airport workers who'd been on duty are executed for "assisting the Zionists." The raid saves 102 hostages; the revenge that follows claims hundreds of Ugandan lives whose names nobody remembers. It captures something essential: Uganda's suffering has often been a footnote to other people's stories. The country that Churchill praised has spent decades as a stage for others' dramas—colonial, Cold War, humanitarian—while its own people endure what documentaries don't film and raids don't rescue.
