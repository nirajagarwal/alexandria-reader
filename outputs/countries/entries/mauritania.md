# Mauritania
*Where the Sahara Meets the Sea*

The last country in the world to formally abolish slavery, in 1981. The last to criminalize it, in 2007. And still, in the second decade of the twenty-first century, a place where somewhere between one and four percent of the population lives in hereditary bondage. This is Mauritania—a nation straddling the ancient divide between Arab North Africa and Black West Africa, between desert and ocean, between past and present, unable or unwilling to fully cross over.

## The Land

Mauritania is emptiness. Three-quarters of its million square kilometers is Sahara—dunes, stone plains, scattered oases. The Atlantic coastline stretches for 750 kilometers, cold currents meeting hot sand, creating some of the world's richest fishing grounds. Most of the country's four million people cluster along this coastal strip or near the Senegal River in the south, the only region with reliable rainfall. The north and east are nomad country—or were, until drought and urbanization drove the herders into slums around Nouakchott, the capital city that has swelled from 20,000 in 1960 to over a million today. Inland lies the Richat Structure, a vast geological bull's-eye visible from space, perfectly circular, forty kilometers across, purpose unknown. The desert holds iron ore, copper, gold. The ocean holds fish. Neither wealth has lifted the nation from profound poverty.

## The History

France conquered the region piecemeal between 1901 and 1912, fighting Moorish resistance into the 1930s. Independence came in 1960, to a country divided between Arabic-speaking Moors (themselves split into "White Moors" of Arab-Berber descent and "Black Moors" of sub-Saharan African descent) and Black Africans in the south—Pulaar, Soninke, Wolof. The new state chose Arabic as its official language, aligned itself with the Arab world, and marginalized its Black population.

In 1989, tensions exploded. Border disputes with Senegal triggered pogroms. Moorish mobs in Nouakchott killed hundreds of Black Mauritanians. The government expelled tens of thousands more, stripping them of citizenship, seizing their land along the fertile river valley. The military has ruled, directly or indirectly, ever since—a succession of coups, brief democratic openings, more coups. The current president, Mohamed Ould Ghazouani, came to power through an election in 2019, but he was the handpicked successor of the previous military ruler. Mauritania is functionally a military oligarchy with democratic decoration.

## The Culture

Mauritania is a society structured by caste and color. The traditional Moorish system recognizes noble warriors, religious scholars, artisans, griots, and slaves—categories that persist despite nominal abolition. Skin color correlates roughly with caste: lighter-skinned "White Moors" dominate government and business, darker "Black Moors" occupy intermediate positions, and Black Africans remain largely excluded from power.

The dominant culture is Moorish—Arabic language, Islamic law, nomadic traditions adapting to sedentary life. Tea drinking is ritual art: three rounds, each progressively sweeter, served with elaborate ceremony. Poetry remains central, though the oral tradition struggles against television and smartphones. Women wear the *melhfa*, a single piece of cloth wrapped around the body, and traditionally, fatness was beauty—force-feeding young girls to make them marriageable.

Islam here is conservative but quieter than Gulf Wahhabism. Nearly everyone is Sunni, Maliki school. Sufi brotherhoods remain influential. The country has largely avoided jihadist violence despite Al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb operating in the region—through brutal security measures and, some allege, quiet accommodation.

## The Challenge

Slavery. It won't go away. Despite abolition, despite criminalization, despite international pressure, the practice persists—particularly in rural areas, among the Haratine (freed slaves and their descendants) who remain bonded to their former masters through debt, tradition, psychological conditioning, lack of alternatives. Children are born into it. The government denies the problem, arrests activists who document it. When cases reach court, prosecutions are rare, sentences light.

Related but distinct is the broader system of racial hierarchy. Black Mauritanians, whether Haratine or ethnic groups from the south, face systematic discrimination in employment, education, land rights. The 1989 expulsions have never been properly addressed—some refugees have returned, but without restitution or justice. The Arabic-only education policy marginalizes communities whose first language is Pulaar or Wolof.

Then there is simple poverty. Mauritania ranks among the world's poorest nations despite its mineral and fish wealth. Corruption is endemic. Infrastructure is minimal. Youth unemployment breeds desperation—many attempt the deadly migration route through the Sahara and across the Mediterranean to Europe.

## The Contribution

Mauritania preserves libraries. In the ancient trading cities of Chinguetti, Ouadane, Tichitt, and Oualata—once stops on the trans-Saharan gold and salt routes—families guard collections of medieval Islamic manuscripts: astronomy, mathematics, medicine, law, poetry. Some libraries hold thousands of volumes, hand-copied on paper or gazelle skin, centuries old, surviving in the desert dry. These cities, now crumbling but still inhabited, are UNESCO World Heritage sites, and the manuscripts they protect represent a bridge to the medieval Islamic golden age.

The Moorish tradition of poetry—oral and written, in Arabic and Hassaniya (the local dialect)—has produced distinctive forms. The *tbara'a*, a classical ode; the *leghfa*, women's songs. Modern Mauritanian music blends Arab and African traditions—artists like Dimi Mint Abba and Malouma have brought this fusion to international audiences, though Malouma's outspoken advocacy for women's rights and against slavery has made her controversial at home.

The country also offers a case study in ecological adaptation to extremes. The Sahara is advancing, but Mauritania has pioneered community-based natural resource management, combining traditional nomadic practices with modern conservation. Success is modest, but the techniques matter for a warming world.

## A Moment

November 2016. Biram Dah Abeid, an anti-slavery activist from the Haratine community, stands trial in Nouadane. He faces charges of "inciting rebellion" and "leading unauthorized demonstrations." His crime: documenting slavery cases, demanding prosecutions, refusing to be silent. In 2013, he burned Islamic jurisprudence texts that he said justified slavery—a provocative act that landed him in prison for 18 months. Now, back in court, he faces another two years.

Supporters gather outside—Haratine, some Black Africans, a few sympathetic Moors, foreign journalists. Security forces outnumber them. The trial is brief, the verdict predetermined: guilty. Two years. As guards lead him away, Biram raises his shackled hands and shouts: "We are fighting a system that has lasted for centuries. Our struggle will outlast these chains."

He was released early, under international pressure, and ran for president in 2019, winning second place with 18 percent of the vote—a remarkable showing for someone the state calls a criminal. He was arrested again in 2021, released again. The cycle continues. The slavery he documents continues. The silence he breaks keeps trying to close over the truth.

Outside the courthouse, an old woman watches him taken away. She is Haratine, born to slaves, married to a man who still works his family's former masters' land for no pay because he knows no other life. Her daughter attends school now—Biram's activism made that possible. Her grandson might be free. That hope, fragile as it is in the Mauritanian desert, is what activists like Biram are trying to keep alive.
