# Israel
*The Impossible State*

Zionism succeeded: the Jews have a state. It sits on land where other people lived, and still live. This is the fact that shapes everything—the military might, the technological prowess, the democratic vitality, the occupation, the settlements, the wars without end. Israel is a liberal democracy and an ethnocracy, a refuge and a garrison, an innovation hub and a permanent battlefield. It cannot reconcile what it is with what it claims to be, and cannot survive without the claim. Everything else flows from this.

## The Land

A strip of the eastern Mediterranean barely larger than New Jersey—desert in the south, mountains in the north, a coastal plain where most people live. The Jordan River valley dropping below sea level to the Dead Sea. The Negev consuming half the territory. Climate: hot, dry summers; mild, wet winters. Water always scarce, now engineered with desalination. The land itself is theologically charged—every hill has three names, every valley a contested history. Gaza and the West Bank are politically separate but geographically integral, their borders the result of armistice lines drawn in 1949. The geography allows no natural borders, only political ones enforced by arms.

## The History

Zionism emerged in late 19th-century Europe as a response to antisemitism—a nationalist movement demanding Jewish sovereignty in the Jews' ancestral homeland. Jews began immigrating to Ottoman Palestine in the 1880s, buying land, building settlements. The British Mandate (1920-1948) promised both a Jewish homeland and protection of Arab rights—contradictory commitments that ended in civil war. In 1947, the UN voted to partition Palestine; the Arabs rejected it. Israel declared independence in May 1948. Five Arab armies invaded and lost. In the fighting, some 700,000 Palestinians fled or were expelled—the Nakba, catastrophe. Israel survived.

The Six-Day War (1967) gave Israel the West Bank, Gaza, Sinai, and the Golan Heights—a military triumph that became a moral trap. The peace treaty with Egypt (1979) returned Sinai. The Oslo Accords (1993) promised Palestinian statehood but delivered expanding settlements instead. A second intifada (2000-2005) hardened both sides. Israel withdrew from Gaza in 2005; Hamas took control in 2007. Wars in 2008, 2012, 2014, 2021, 2023—each devastating Gaza, none resolving anything. The West Bank remains under Israeli military control, with half a million Israeli settlers and millions of Palestinians living under different legal systems.

## The Culture

Hebrew revived from liturgy to living language—perhaps Zionism's most complete success. A fusion society: Ashkenazi founders met Mizrahi refugees from Arab countries, Russian immigrants of the 1990s, Ethiopian Jews, secular Tel Avivis and ultra-Orthodox Jerusalemites. The army as universal experience (except for Arabs and ultra-Orthodox). Kibbutzim replaced by start-ups. A thriving arts scene—literature, film, music—obsessed with identity, memory, conflict. Judaism as ethnicity, religion, and political identity—often in tension. Friday night Shabbat dinners even among the secular. A café culture that feels Mediterranean. Underneath everything: the question of what it means to be Israeli when Israeliness depends on Jewishness and Jewishness remains contested.

The Israeli Arab minority (20% of citizens) lives in legal equality and practical exclusion—citizens but not quite belonging, their Palestinianness a permanent suspicion. The ultra-Orthodox (18% and rising) reject Zionism, refuse military service, depend on state subsidies, and wield disproportionate political power. Divisions deepen: secular and religious, Ashkenazi and Mizrahi, citizen and occupied, Israeli and Palestinian.

## The Challenge

The occupation. Fifty-seven years of military rule over millions of Palestinians—no citizenship, no sovereignty, checkpoints and permits and house demolitions and settler expansion. Israel calls it temporary; the settlements make it permanent. Two states seem impossible (too many settlers, too little trust), one state unthinkable (Israel would cease to be a Jewish majority), so neither happens. The status quo persists: a democracy for Jews, a military dictatorship for Palestinians. This corrodes Israeli democracy—Netanyahu's judicial overhaul of 2023 showed how far right the country has shifted, how fragile its liberalism. Gaza remains a blockaded enclave, periodically bombed to rubble. The Palestinian Authority administers parts of the West Bank as Israeli subcontractor. Hamas launches rockets; Israel launches airstrikes. Repeat.

Demography compounds the problem: Arab birth rates higher than Jewish, ultra-Orthodox birth rates higher still. The ultra-Orthodox will be a third of the population by 2050—mostly non-working, dependent on welfare, opposed to core Zionist values. Israel faces a choice between Jewish and democratic, and keeps choosing Jewish.

## The Contribution

It proved that statelessness could be overcome—the most persecuted people in history reclaimed sovereignty. For that alone it matters. The cultural revival: Hebrew literature, Israeli cinema, a musical tradition fusing diaspora fragments. Medical technology, cybersecurity, agricultural innovation—drip irrigation was invented here. A functioning democracy in a region with few. The ingathering: Russian refuseniks, Ethiopian airlifts, French Jews fleeing antisemitism. Universal healthcare, strong public education, vigorous civil society despite the rightward drift. The moral argument: whatever Israel has become, it emerged from the ashes of the Holocaust, from statelessness and genocide. That history doesn't justify everything, but it explains the ferocity with which Israelis defend their state.

## A Moment

November 4, 1995, Kings of Israel Square in Tel Aviv. Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin had just addressed a massive peace rally—perhaps 100,000 people singing "Song for Peace," believing Oslo might actually work, that coexistence was possible. As Rabin descended the steps toward his car, a young Orthodox nationalist named Yigal Amir stepped forward and shot him three times. Rabin died an hour later. The assassin wanted to stop territorial compromise. He succeeded. The peace process never recovered. Rabin's successor, Shimon Peres, lost the next election to Netanyahu, who has dominated Israeli politics ever since, building settlements and abandoning negotiations. The rally was the high-water mark of Israeli liberalism. What came after: intifada, rightward drift, the wall, the blockade, the endless wars. Israelis still ask: what if Rabin had lived? But he didn't. And the man who killed him spoke for forces that couldn't be killed—Jewish supremacism, messianic nationalism, territorial maximalism. Those forces won.
