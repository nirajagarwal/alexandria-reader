# United States
*Empire by Accident*

The United States exists as a vast continental republic that became the world's dominant power almost despite itself—a nation founded on Enlightenment principles and Protestant work ethic, built on indigenous dispossession and enslaved labor, that stumbled into global hegemony through two world wars it didn't start. For better and worse, it has shaped the modern world more than any other single country, exporting democracy and capitalism, Hollywood and Silicon Valley, constitutional government and consumer culture. It remains the indispensable case study in whether liberal democracy can govern a continental empire, whether markets can be reconciled with equality, whether a multiethnic society can actually work.

## The Land

Three million square miles stretching from Atlantic to Pacific, Arctic to subtropical—perhaps no other nation commands such geographic abundance. The eastern forests and Appalachian ridges. The vast central plains, once tallgrass prairie, now the world's breadbasket. The Mississippi River system draining half a continent. The Rocky Mountains rising a mile high. California's Mediterranean valleys. The deserts of the Southwest. Natural harbors on both coasts. Oil, coal, iron, copper, timber, fresh water—nearly everything required for industrial civilization exists within these borders.

This geographic lottery shaped everything else. No hostile neighbors, no need for a large standing army, oceans as moats. Room to expand, to absorb immigration, to make mistakes and recover. The land itself encouraged a particular American delusion: that success was earned rather than inherited, that abundance was normal rather than exceptional.

## The History

Thirteen British colonies declared independence in 1776, won it by 1783, and drafted the Constitution—that improbable Enlightenment document—in 1787. The founders created a republic designed to limit government power while governing a continental territory, balancing federal and state authority, separating powers to prevent tyranny. It was experimental, fragile, and hypocritical: a slaveholding society proclaiming that all men are created equal.

The nineteenth century was westward expansion and its euphemisms—Manifest Destiny cloaking the systematic dispossession of indigenous peoples. The Louisiana Purchase (1803) doubled the territory. War with Mexico (1846-48) seized California and the Southwest. Meanwhile, the contradiction of slavery metastasized until it ruptured in Civil War (1861-65), which killed 600,000 and ended slavery but not white supremacy. Jim Crow segregation replaced chattel bondage.

Industrialization transformed the nation. By 1900, the U.S. was the world's largest economy—steel mills, railroads, factories, cities swelling with immigrants from Europe and Asia. The twentieth century made it a superpower almost accidentally. World War I (1917-18) was a European war America finished. The 1920s roared with prosperity until the 1929 crash and Great Depression, which Franklin Roosevelt addressed with the New Deal—Social Security, labor rights, public works, an expanded federal government.

World War II (1941-45) was the hinge. America emerged as one of two superpowers, architect of the postwar liberal order: the United Nations, NATO, the Marshall Plan, Bretton Woods institutions. The Cold War (1947-91) with the Soviet Union organized everything—massive defense spending, covert interventions, nuclear brinkmanship, the space race. Korea, Vietnam, dozens of proxy wars.

Domestically: the civil rights movement (1954-68) finally dismantled legal segregation. The Great Society expanded the welfare state. The 1960s counterculture challenged traditional authority. Women's liberation, gay rights, environmentalism. Reagan (1981-89) shifted politics rightward—tax cuts, deregulation, reduced social spending, muscular anticommunism.

The Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, leaving America as sole superpower in a "unipolar moment" that lasted about a decade. September 11, 2001 triggered wars in Afghanistan and Iraq that cost trillions and achieved little. The 2008 financial crisis exposed inequality and regulatory failure. Barack Obama's election (2008) as the first Black president suggested progress; Donald Trump's (2016) suggested backlash. The 2020s began with pandemic, political polarization, and declining faith in institutions.

## The Culture

American culture is individualism verging on atomization—the belief that people are self-made, that success reflects virtue, that government should leave you alone. This produces extraordinary innovation and entrepreneurship; it also produces indifference to collective welfare. Americans work longer hours than Europeans, take less vacation, retire later. The work ethic is Protestant in origin, capitalist in practice.

It's also deeply religious, especially compared to other wealthy democracies. Evangelical Christianity shapes politics and social values in ways secular Europeans find baffling. Church attendance remains high. Faith-based rhetoric saturates political discourse. Yet religious practice is declining among younger generations.

The culture is profoundly commercial—everything is for sale, everyone is selling. Entertainment dominates: Hollywood films, television, streaming platforms, pop music, sports as spectacle. Celebrity worship is the national religion's secular wing. Consumer choice is freedom. Brands are identities. Shopping is leisure.

It's a immigrant nation constantly negotiating what that means. English dominates but Spanish is widely spoken. White Protestant culture remains hegemonic even as demographics shift toward a multiethnic majority-minority future. Race is the unresolved question—slavery's afterlife, segregation's inheritance, ongoing disparities in wealth, health, incarceration, opportunity.

Regional differences matter: the South's conservative Christianity and racial history; the Northeast's liberalism and old money; the Midwest's industrial decline and rural values; the West's openness and innovation; California as its own civilization. Cities are diverse and liberal; rural areas are homogeneous and conservative. The divide between them is widening.

Education varies wildly. Elite universities—Harvard, MIT, Stanford—are world-class research engines. Public schools depend on local property taxes, entrenching inequality. College is increasingly unaffordable without debt. Anti-intellectualism coexists with technological sophistication.

Gun ownership is widespread, gun violence endemic. Mass shootings are routine. The Second Amendment is sacred text. This baffles outsiders but reflects a frontier mythology and distrust of government.

## The Challenge

The central question is whether American democracy can survive success. Wealth inequality approaches Gilded Age levels: billionaires accumulate obscene fortunes while the middle class stagnates and the working class despairs. Healthcare costs bankrupt families. Student debt crushes graduates. Housing is unaffordable in productive cities. Social mobility—the American Dream—has become largely mythological.

Political polarization is extreme. Republicans and Democrats inhabit different information ecosystems, different moral universes. Trust in institutions—government, media, science, each other—has collapsed. Elections are contested, norms are flouted, constitutional mechanisms are weaponized. The January 6, 2021 Capitol riot revealed how fragile democratic legitimacy has become.

Race remains unfinished business. Police killings, mass incarceration, residential segregation, wealth gaps—the patterns persist. White grievance politics competes with demands for racial justice. Immigration provokes nativist backlash.

Climate change threatens but partisan division prevents action. Infrastructure crumbles. Healthcare remains tied to employment, leaving millions uninsured. The opioid epidemic kills tens of thousands annually.

Globally, America's authority is declining. China is rising. Allies question U.S. reliability. Military interventions have failed. The model of liberal democratic capitalism looks less triumphant than it did in 1991.

The question is whether a system designed for an agrarian republic of four million can govern a post-industrial empire of 330 million—whether institutions built for compromise can function amid tribal politics, whether prosperity can be shared widely enough to sustain legitimacy, whether the center can hold.

## The Contribution

The United States invented modern democracy at scale—the constitutional republic, federalism, separation of powers, judicial review, the Bill of Rights. Flawed in practice, these ideas remain powerful worldwide.

It pioneered mass production, consumerism, the modern corporation. Fordism, Taylorism, Silicon Valley—American capitalism transformed how humans work and live. For better and worse.

Culturally: jazz, blues, rock and roll, hip-hop—American music is global vernacular. Hollywood created the film industry. Television. The internet. Social media. American entertainment dominates worldwide.

Science and technology: the transistor, the personal computer, the internet, the smartphone, mRNA vaccines. American research universities and laboratories have produced extraordinary innovation. The moon landing. The Human Genome Project. GPS. 

Literature: Melville, Whitman, Dickinson, Hemingway, Faulkner, Morrison. The American novel is world literature.

Political movements: abolitionism, women's suffrage, civil rights, environmentalism—American activists have shaped global consciousness.

The Marshall Plan rebuilt Europe. The postwar liberal order, however frayed, prevented great-power war for 75 years. American power has been imperial and often brutal, but the alternatives were arguably worse.

The idea of America—that a multiethnic democracy could work, that people could govern themselves, that merit might matter more than birth—remains powerful even when the reality disappoints.

## A Moment

July 20, 1969. Neil Armstrong steps onto the moon's surface. "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind." A quarter-million miles from Earth, he plants an American flag but speaks for humanity.

It was the culmination of a decade-long project that employed 400,000 people and cost $25 billion—the largest peacetime mobilization in history. Kennedy had promised it in 1961, partly from scientific vision, mostly from Cold War competition with the Soviets. The engineers were German rocket scientists and American farm boys. The computers were room-sized. The calculations were done partly by hand, by women called "computers" working in segregated offices.

Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin spent two and a half hours on the lunar surface. They collected rocks, planted instruments, took photographs that changed human consciousness. Meanwhile Michael Collins orbited above in the command module, alone behind the moon for 48 minutes each orbit, more isolated than any human had ever been.

They returned safely. The world watched on television—something like 600 million people, witnessing the impossible. It was American technology, American ambition, American showmanship. It was also human achievement, the fulfillment of ancient dreams.

Within three years, the program ended. Too expensive, other priorities, the war in Vietnam. The last men walked on the moon in 1972. No one has returned since.

The moment captures something essential about America: the audacity, the resources, the can-do engineering genius, the geopolitical competition, the television spectacle, the grand vision and short attention span. The capacity to do something magnificent and then wander away from it. The flags planted on the moon have been bleached white by radiation—American triumphalism literally faded, but the human accomplishment remains.
