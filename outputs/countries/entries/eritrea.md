# Eritrea
*Independence Squandered*

Eritrea won one of Africa's longest liberation struggles, then became one of its most repressive states. This narrow Red Sea nation, compressed between Ethiopia and Sudan, achieved independence in 1993 after thirty years of guerrilla warfare. Within a decade, it had transformed from beacon of self-determination into militarized autocracy. The president who led the independence movement has ruled ever since, without elections, without constitution, without dissent. What was celebrated as Africa's newest nation became one of its primary sources of refugees.

## The Land

Eritrea occupies 45,000 square miles along the Red Sea coast, its territory rising sharply from coastal desert through eastern highlands to western lowlands. The central plateau, where most people live, enjoys temperate climate and adequate rainfall. The port cities of Massawa and Assab gave Ethiopia sea access for generations—control of these ports drove much of the conflict. The Dahlak Archipelago offers coral islands and fishing grounds. Arable land is scarce; much of the country receives minimal rainfall. Strategic location has always mattered more than resources. The Italian colonial infrastructure—railways, roads, architecture—still marks the landscape, a reminder of the European occupation that first defined Eritrea as a political entity distinct from Ethiopia.

## The History

Italy colonized the region in 1890, creating "Eritrea" from diverse ethnic groups. The Italians built Africa's most extensive cable car system, linking Massawa to Asmara, and developed the port as a gateway to their East African ambitions. British administration followed Italy's defeat in World War II. In 1952, the UN federated Eritrea with Ethiopia under Emperor Haile Selassie; in 1962, Ethiopia annexed it outright. The Eritrean Liberation Front began armed resistance in 1961; the Eritrean People's Liberation Front (EPLF), led by Isaias Afwerki, emerged as the dominant force by the 1980s. The guerrillas controlled the countryside while Ethiopia held cities. When the Soviet-backed Derg regime collapsed in 1991, the EPLF took Asmara without fighting. A 1993 referendum on independence passed with 99.8% support—one of the few such votes taken seriously. The jubilation lasted five years. Border war with Ethiopia erupted in 1998 over disputed territory, killing seventy thousand before a 2000 ceasefire. The war became the excuse for indefinite emergency rule. Isaias dissolved the parliament, banned political parties, imprisoned journalists and dissidents, and instituted unlimited national service. The promised constitution never took effect.

## The Culture

Nine ethnic groups speak nine languages—Tigrinya-speaking Christians in the highlands, Tigre-speaking Muslims in the lowlands, plus Saho, Afar, Bilen, Kunama, Nara, Rashaida, and Hidareb peoples. This diversity, bridged during the liberation struggle, became problematic under authoritarian rule dominated by highland Tigrinya. Half the population is Christian (mainly Orthodox), half Muslim, with traditional religious tolerance strained by political control. The EPLF's culture of self-reliance and collective sacrifice, forged in guerrilla struggle, morphed into justification for permanent mobilization. Women fighters achieved prominence during the war—the revolution promised equality—but peacetime has seen traditional patriarchy reassert itself despite rhetoric. Italian architectural legacy gives Asmara a distinctive Art Deco character; UNESCO designated it a World Heritage site for its modernist buildings. Coffee ceremony remains central to social life. But the dominant cultural fact is absence—so many have left. The educated, the young, the ambitious flee when possible.

## The Challenge

Indefinite national service has made Eritrea unlivable for its youth. Men and women are conscripted into military or government work, often for years beyond the official eighteen-month term, paid subsistence wages, unable to build independent lives. The system functions as forced labor justified by permanent emergency. Thousands flee monthly across harsh desert into Sudan or across the Mediterranean toward Europe. The government treats departure as treason—returning refugees face prison or worse. International isolation deepens the crisis. Eritrea has antagonized every neighbor, faced UN sanctions for supporting Somali militants, and rejected international development aid as neocolonial interference. The economy barely functions; remittances from the diaspora keep families alive. President Isaias, now in his fourth decade of rule, has created a garrison state where fear silences dissent and hope drives people to risk their lives leaving. The 2018 peace with Ethiopia briefly raised expectations, but promised reforms never materialized. The question is no longer whether Eritrea can develop, but whether the nation will survive the exodus.

## The Contribution

Eritrea's independence struggle inspired liberation movements globally. The EPLF's self-reliance, strategic patience, and eventual victory against a Soviet-backed army demonstrated what disciplined resistance could achieve. Women comprised a third of fighters—the liberation front pioneered gender equality in combat that influenced feminist movements. Eritrean musical traditions, blending highland rhythms with coastal Arab influences, produced a distinctive sound; singers like Helen Meles and Yemane Barya became Pan-African voices. The preservation of Italian modernist architecture in Asmara offers a unique African urban landscape—Art Deco cinemas, rationalist government buildings, Fiat Tagliero Building with its futuristic cantilever. Eritrean long-distance runners, products of highland endurance culture, compete globally. The diaspora maintains strong cultural identity, running restaurants, organizing festivals, preserving Tigrinya and Tigre languages. Most poignantly, Eritrea demonstrated both the power and the peril of liberation—that winning independence guarantees nothing about what follows.

## A Moment

In the Dahlak Archipelago, Italian colonizers built a prison on Nocra Island in 1887. For a century, political prisoners endured this place—first Eritrean resisters to Italian rule, then opponents of successive regimes. The EPLF imprisoned Ethiopian POWs here during the liberation struggle. After independence, the new government continued the tradition. In 2001, when Isaias began his crackdown, eleven former liberation fighters—ministers, generals, heroes of the independence struggle—questioned his abandonment of constitutional rule. They were arrested and disappeared. Some reportedly ended up in island prisons like Nocra or underground facilities near Asmara. None have been seen since. No trial, no charges, no acknowledgment. Their wives and children still wait. The prison island persists under new management—the revolution's children incarcerating the revolution's heroes—a perfect emblem of independence squandered, where the apparatus of oppression simply changes masters while the cells remain occupied. The Red Sea laps against shores that have witnessed every regime, every ideology, every promise, and still the prisoners arrive.
