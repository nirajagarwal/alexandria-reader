# Somalia
*The Collapse That Won't End*

Somalia is what happens when a state disintegrates and nothing coherent replaces it. For three decades, this Horn of Africa nation has been synonymous with civil war, piracy, famine, and failed international interventions. Yet Somalia is more than its catastrophe—it is an ancient maritime culture, a pastoral society with deep clan traditions, and a people who have survived conditions that would break others. The question is not whether Somalia can function—Somalis have proven remarkably resilient. The question is whether the nation-state itself means anything here anymore.

## The Land

Somalia occupies the Horn of Africa's easternmost point, a harsh, arid landscape where the Indian Ocean meets the Gulf of Aden. The longest coastline in mainland Africa—3,300 kilometers—has made Somalis fishermen, traders, and lately, pirates. Most of the interior is semi-desert scrubland punctuated by seasonal rivers that rarely reach the sea. The Jubba and Shabelle rivers support agriculture in the south, but droughts are frequent and devastating. Mogadishu, the capital, sits on a white-sand coast that was once beautiful—before the wars turned it into rubble. Somaliland in the north is mountainous and slightly greener. This is a land that demands toughness, where water determines everything, where pastoralists move with their camels and goats following rain that may not come.

## The History

Somalis are ethnically and linguistically homogeneous—almost everyone speaks Somali, practices Sunni Islam, and traces ancestry through patrilineal clans. This unity should have made nation-building easier. Instead, clan loyalty proved stronger than national identity. The Somali coast traded with Arabs, Persians, and Indians for centuries. European colonization came late—Britain took the north, Italy the south. Independence in 1960 unified the territories under one flag. For nine years, Somalia was a parliamentary democracy. Then Siad Barre seized power in 1969, ruling as a socialist dictator who used Cold War patronage to build a military state. His catastrophic invasion of Ethiopia in 1977 failed. As Soviet support evaporated, Barre turned increasingly brutal, massacring entire clans. By 1991, warlords had overthrown him, and the country collapsed into clan warfare. It has never recovered. Multiple governments, transitional authorities, international interventions—none have restored order. The state exists on paper. On the ground, authority is fragmented.

## The Culture

Somali culture centers on poetry, clan loyalty, and Islam. Somalis are oral poets of extraordinary skill—in a society where memorization matters, poets hold social power, settling disputes and articulating grievances through verse. The clan system (*qabiil*) organizes society into lineages that provide identity, protection, and obligation. Your clan determines your allies, your feuds, your marriage prospects. Islam arrived early and runs deep—Sufism has traditionally dominated, though Salafist movements have grown. Nomadic pastoralism shaped values: independence, honor, resilience, mobility. Somali women face significant restrictions but also play crucial economic roles. The diaspora is vast—Somalis fled the wars to Minnesota, London, Nairobi, Dubai, maintaining tight-knit communities and sending remittances that keep the homeland alive. Trust is given to kin, not institutions. The nation-state never penetrated deeply enough to replace older loyalties.

## The Challenge

Somalia's challenge is existential: can any functional state emerge from three decades of collapse? The Transitional Federal Government, supported by African Union troops, controls little beyond Mogadishu—and barely that. Al-Shabaab, a jihadist insurgency linked to al-Qaeda, holds rural areas and wages bombings in the capital. Somaliland, the northwest region, declared independence in 1991 and has achieved relative stability—but no country recognizes it. Puntland in the northeast is semi-autonomous. Warlords, militias, and pirate networks fill power vacuums. Famine stalks the land whenever drought arrives. International aid feeds millions but creates dependency. The diaspora's remittances—estimated at $1.4 billion annually—exceed all other income. Every intervention has failed: UN peacekeepers withdrew after the "Black Hawk Down" battle in 1993; Ethiopian invasions backfired; current efforts make little progress. The central problem is legitimacy. The government in Mogadishu has no credible claim to represent Somali interests because clan divisions run deeper than national identity. You cannot build a state where most people see it as merely one faction among many.

## The Contribution

Despite everything, Somalia has given the world a remarkable culture of adaptation. Somali traders established networks across the Indian Ocean, linking Africa to Asia for centuries. The Somali language, with its complex poetry tradition, produced an oral literature of extraordinary sophistication—*gabay* poems can run to hundreds of lines, all memorized. Somali cuisine reflects this maritime trade: *bariis iskukaris* (spiced rice), *muqmad* (dried meat), *canjeero* (spongy flatbread), tea spiced with cardamom and cinnamon. The diaspora demonstrates resilience: Somalis have built communities worldwide, often starting with nothing and creating businesses, sending money home, preserving culture in exile. The informal *hawala* money transfer system, which allows remittances to reach even war zones without banks, is a Somali innovation now used globally. Somalis have shown what survival looks like when the state disappears—communities organize themselves, trade continues, life persists. It is a grim lesson, but a real one.

## A Moment

In 1992, after two years of civil war and famine, photographer James Nachtwey captured an image that shocked the world: a skeletal Somali child, dying of starvation, barely able to stand. The photo ran everywhere. Western nations mobilized. President George H.W. Bush launched Operation Restore Hope, sending 28,000 American troops to Somalia to secure food distribution and restore order. For a moment, it looked like salvation. By October 1993, eighteen American soldiers were dead in Mogadishu, their bodies dragged through the streets by jubilant militias while crowds cheered. The mission had transformed from humanitarian relief to counterinsurgency to disaster. America withdrew. The UN withdrew. The world moved on. Somalia kept bleeding. The intervention failed because it misunderstood the problem. This wasn't a crisis requiring temporary stabilization until legitimate authority returned. There was no legitimate authority. The collapse was the new equilibrium. Outsiders could feed people—they did—but they could not make Somalis want the same government. That photo of the starving child promised rescue. The bodies in the streets taught a different lesson: some catastrophes are not solvable by outside intervention. They must be lived through.
