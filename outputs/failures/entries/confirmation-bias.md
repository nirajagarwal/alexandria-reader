We prefer evidence that confirms what we already believe. When information aligns with our existing views, we accept it quickly, often uncritically. When it contradicts them, we scrutinize it harshly, finding reasons to dismiss or discount it. This isn't conscious deception — we genuinely believe we're being rational, weighing evidence fairly. But the scales are rigged from the start.

Psychologist Peter Wason demonstrated this in 1960 with a simple experiment. He gave subjects a sequence of three numbers — 2, 4, 6 — and told them the sequence followed a rule. Their task was to discover the rule by proposing other sequences. Subjects could test as many sequences as they wanted. Most quickly formed a hypothesis: "numbers increasing by two." They then tested sequences that would confirm this: 8, 10, 12 or 20, 22, 24. Each time, Wason said yes, these fit the rule. Confident, they announced their answer. They were wrong. The actual rule was simpler: any ascending numbers. But they'd never tested sequences that might disprove their hypothesis — 1, 2, 3 or 5, 17, 23. They'd only looked for confirmation.

This pattern repeats across domains. A manager believes a new hire is exceptional based on their pedigree. In meetings, she notices when they make good points, remembers their successes, attributes their mistakes to circumstances. Another employee without the prestigious background makes similar contributions, but she registers them as adequate rather than impressive. Both employees receive the same performance data, but she interprets it through different lenses. By year's end, her initial impression has become self-fulfilling prophecy — the favored employee gets better assignments, more mentorship, a stronger review.

The mechanism is partly evolutionary. Our ancestors lived in information-scarce environments where changing beliefs was costly. If you'd learned which plants were poisonous through hard experience, you didn't want to easily unlearn it. Better to be stubbornly consistent than dangerously flexible. We inherited brains optimized for conviction, not curiosity. Modern life inverts this: we're drowning in information, much of it contradictory, and the cost of being wrong has shifted. Clinging to outdated beliefs now often carries more risk than updating them. But the machinery remains the same.

The internet has amplified confirmation bias by solving a problem we didn't know we had. Previous generations couldn't easily find evidence for fringe beliefs. Now, whatever you believe, you can find a community that shares it, expert-sounding arguments that support it, data that seems to confirm it. Flat earthers find engineering analyses of horizon curvature. Anti-vaccine activists find doctors questioning vaccine safety. Every conspiracy theory has its evidence base. The marketplace of ideas hasn't produced consensus — it's produced niche markets where any belief can find validation.

The damage compounds. In personal life, confirmation bias maintains toxic relationships — we explain away red flags that contradict our initial attraction, notice behaviors that fit the story we want. In organizations, it produces strategic blindness — leadership teams dismiss market signals that contradict their chosen direction, rally around data that supports it. Blockbuster saw evidence that streaming was the future but found reasons why their retail model would endure. Kodak invented the digital camera but concluded film would remain dominant. Both had access to contradictory evidence. Both filtered it out.

Politically, confirmation bias transforms disagreement into tribalism. We don't just prefer policies — we consume news sources that validate our preferences, follow social media accounts that reinforce them, dismiss criticism as bias and embrace agreement as objectivity. Studies show that presenting people with fact-checks that contradict their political beliefs often strengthens those beliefs rather than weakening them. The correction itself becomes evidence of conspiracy or institutional bias.

You can recognize confirmation bias by noticing what questions you ask. When evaluating something you want to believe, do you ask "Can I believe this?" — searching for reasons it might be true? When evaluating something you don't want to believe, do you ask "Must I believe this?" — searching for reasons it might be false? The difference in those questions predicts the difference in your conclusions. Watch for the evidence you ignore, not just the evidence you accept. Notice when you find counterarguments more compelling applied to others' beliefs than to your own. Pay attention to your immediate emotional response to new information — if you feel pleased by data that supports your view but suspicious of data that challenges it, the bias is active.

The insidious aspect is that confirmation bias feels like rational thinking. When you dismiss evidence that contradicts your beliefs, you're not being irrational — you're being rationally selective, protecting your worldview from bad information. When you accept evidence that confirms your beliefs, you're not being gullible — you're recognizing truth. The person with confirmation bias doesn't feel biased. They feel clear-sighted, able to see through the noise to the signal. Which is precisely why it persists.
