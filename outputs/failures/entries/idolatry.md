# Idolatry

The successful executive builds her identity around achievement. She's the person who delivers, who exceeds targets, whose performance defines her worth. Then the company restructures. Her role disappears. And she discovers something terrifying: without the work, she doesn't know who she is. The achievement wasn't just important to her — it had become her god.

Idolatry is the elevation of something good into the ultimate thing. It's taking what should be penultimate — worthy of attention, effort, devotion — and making it ultimate, the organizing center around which everything else orbits. The problem isn't the object of devotion itself. Work matters. Love matters. Success, beauty, knowledge, justice — all genuinely valuable. Idolatry happens when we ask these finite goods to bear infinite weight, to provide what only the transcendent can provide: ultimate meaning, unconditional worth, final security.

The mechanism is subtle. It begins with appropriate valuing. You care about your work because work is meaningful. You love your children because they're precious. You pursue excellence because quality matters. But gradually, imperceptibly, the relationship shifts. The good thing becomes the necessary thing. Then the only thing. What began as one value among others becomes the lens through which all other values are evaluated. The part pretends to be the whole.

Søren Kierkegaard identified this as the fundamental human temptation: to seek infinite significance in finite things. We know we need meaning, purpose, worth. But instead of finding these in proper relationship to the transcendent, we inflate something accessible, controllable, achievable. The executive makes achievement her god because achievement feels attainable in ways that grace doesn't. The intellectual makes ideas ultimate because ideas can be mastered in ways mystery cannot. The parent makes their child's success absolute because a child's future feels more concrete than eternity.

The damage unfolds in stages. First comes the constriction. When one thing becomes ultimate, everything else becomes instrumental. The executive who idolizes achievement sees relationships as networking opportunities, rest as productivity loss, art as decoration. The parent who idolizes their child's success reduces education to credentialing, play to skill development, the child's personhood to potential. Life narrows. Everything serves the idol.

Then comes the anxiety. Finite things are fragile. They fail, fade, disappoint. When your worth depends on achievement, every project becomes existentially loaded. When your meaning depends on a relationship, every conflict threatens annihilation. When your purpose depends on a cause, every setback feels like cosmic defeat. The idol demands constant feeding — more achievement, more devotion, more proof that it can bear the weight you've placed on it.

But it cannot. The executive discovers this in the restructuring. The parent discovers it when the child chooses their own path. The activist discovers it when the cause advances but the emptiness remains. This is the moment of crisis: the idol reveals its insufficiency. What you made ultimate cannot actually be ultimate. It cannot provide unconditional worth because its value is conditional. It cannot give final meaning because it is itself contingent. The thing you elevated to godhood turns out to be a creature.

David Foster Wallace, in his Kenyon College commencement address, described this with precision: "In the day-to-day trenches of adult life, there is actually no such thing as atheism. There is no such thing as not worshipping. Everybody worships. The only choice we get is what to worship." His point wasn't religious propaganda but phenomenological observation. Humans are worshipping creatures. We organize our lives around something ultimate. The question is whether that something can bear the weight.

The classic idolatries are obvious: money, power, pleasure, status. But modern idolatry often wears more sophisticated disguises. The progressive makes justice itself ultimate, reducing every relationship and institution to its political significance. The rationalist makes reason ultimate, unable to grant legitimacy to anything that cannot be empirically verified. The romantic makes love ultimate, expecting a relationship to provide what only the infinite can provide. These are harder to recognize because the objects seem so worthy. Justice is good. Reason is necessary. Love is beautiful. But goodness is not godhood.

Martin Luther observed that the human heart is a "perpetual factory of idols." We don't choose between worship and non-worship. We choose what we worship. And whatever we choose to worship, we begin to resemble. The person who worships achievement becomes anxious, driven, hollow. The person who worships pleasure becomes enslaved to appetite, diminished by each satisfaction. The person who worships ideology becomes cruel in service of the cause. We are shaped by our ultimate commitments.

Recognition requires a kind of spiritual archaeology. What governs your decisions when values conflict? What makes you irrationally anxious? What do you defend with disproportionate emotion? What would devastate you if you lost it? These questions reveal the actual hierarchy of your devotions, which may differ significantly from your stated values. The executive says family matters most but works eighty hours a week. The academic says truth matters most but cannot tolerate challenges to their intellectual identity. The believer says God matters most but panics when religious observance is threatened. What we actually treat as ultimate becomes visible in crisis.

The spiritual traditions recognize gradations. Thomas Aquinas distinguished between veneration (appropriate honor) and worship (ultimate devotion). The first is fitting — we should honor what is genuinely good. The second is reserved for what is actually ultimate. The confusion of categories causes the breakdown. We worship what should be venerated. We treat as absolute what should be relative.

Paul Tillich called this the difference between ultimate concern and preliminary concerns. Preliminary concerns are genuine — we should care about work, relationships, justice, beauty. But only one concern can be ultimate, and that concern should be directed toward what is actually ultimate: being-itself, the ground of meaning. When we make preliminary concerns ultimate, we commit not just a category error but an existential one. We ask finite things to be infinite, and they shatter under the demand.

The recovery isn't abandonment of the good thing. The executive doesn't need to stop caring about achievement. The parent doesn't need to stop loving their child. The activist doesn't need to stop pursuing justice. What's required is reordering — returning the good thing to its proper place in the hierarchy. When achievement is penultimate rather than ultimate, you can fail without being destroyed. When love is properly ordered, you can lose without losing everything. When justice is important but not god, you can engage without becoming monstrous.

This reordering is what the spiritual traditions call worship: not religious observance but proper alignment of devotion. It's the recognition that finite goods are genuinely good, worthy of our attention and care, but cannot be god. Only what is actually ultimate can be ultimate. Everything else, no matter how beautiful or meaningful, is a creature — to be valued, served, enjoyed, but not worshipped.

The test is deprivation. What can you lose and still be yourself? What can fail and not destroy you? What can be taken and leave your core intact? The things that pass this test were never ultimate, however much you loved them. The thing that wouldn't pass it — that's where your idolatry lives.
