# Purity Spirals

In 2017, the organizers of the Oakland Women's March found themselves in an impossible position. A group within the organization accused the leadership of being insufficiently intersectional. The leadership responded by attempting to demonstrate their commitment to anti-racism, which prompted another faction to demand they address ableism as well. Then class issues. Then environmental justice. Each response opened new fronts of criticism. Within months, the march was cancelled. Not because of external opposition — because the organizing committee had fractured into factions competing to prove who held the purest position. This is the purity spiral: when a group turns its moral scrutiny inward and begins consuming itself.

A purity spiral occurs when a community's standards for moral or ideological purity ratchet upward through competitive escalation. What begins as collective commitment to shared values transforms into an arms race of virtue. Each member seeks to demonstrate greater commitment than their peers, raising the bar for acceptable belief and behavior. The group fragments into concentric circles of the increasingly pure, with each inner circle viewing the outer ones with suspicion or contempt.

The mechanism is simple: someone proposes a purer standard. Others, not wanting to appear less committed, adopt it. This becomes the new baseline. Then someone proposes an even purer standard. Those who hesitate or question are suspected of insufficient commitment — a suspicion that becomes self-confirming as people leave or are expelled. The spiral accelerates because disagreement itself becomes evidence of impurity.

Purity spirals appear across contexts. The Reign of Terror during the French Revolution: first the monarchy, then the Girondins, then the Dantonists, each faction falling to accusations of insufficient revolutionary commitment until Robespierre himself faced the guillotine. Mao's Cultural Revolution: Red Guards competing to demonstrate greater ideological purity, eventually turning on their own leadership. Online political communities fragmenting into ever-smaller factions, each viewing the others as compromised. Religious movements splitting over increasingly granular points of doctrine.

The spiral requires specific conditions. First, a strong collective identity built around moral or ideological commitments — you're not just members of a group, you're the righteous, the enlightened, the truly committed. Second, a mechanism for public performance of purity — call-outs, denunciations, loyalty tests. Third, unclear or contestable standards — if purity criteria were objective and fixed, there would be nothing to spiral. Fourth, social rewards for demonstrating purity — status, belonging, moral superiority.

The damage compounds across levels. Individuals live under constant anxiety about whether their commitment is sufficient. Friendships and working relationships fracture over subtle differences in position. The group's energy turns inward — endless internal policing replaces external work. Moderates leave first, taking with them the ballast that might have slowed the spiral. What remains becomes increasingly rigid and fragile. Eventually the group either collapses or becomes so insular it loses relevance.

The paradox is that purity spirals often begin with genuine moral concern. The Oakland organizers really did care about intersectionality. The Jacobins really did care about revolution. Online activists really do care about justice. The commitment is real. But the competitive dynamic transforms commitment into weapon. Caring about something becomes insufficient — you must care about it more purely, more consistently, more publicly than anyone else.

You recognize a purity spiral by its distinctive features. Escalating standards: what was acceptable last month is suspect this month. Competitive signaling: people perform their commitments in increasingly elaborate ways. Purity tests: litmus questions that determine who's truly committed. Expulsions: regular ejection of members who fail to keep pace. Narrowing discourse: fewer topics become safe to discuss, fewer questions acceptable to ask. Growing paranoia: members become hypervigilant about their own and others' positions.

In organizations, watch for the moment when internal alignment becomes more important than external achievement. When more time is spent on statements of values than on work. When disagreement on means gets interpreted as disagreement on ends. When people begin prefacing every contribution with credentials of commitment.

In yourself, notice when you're performing purity rather than practicing it. When you're more concerned with being seen as committed than with the commitment itself. When you find yourself policing others not because their actions cause harm but because their positions aren't pure enough. When you hesitate to ask honest questions because questions themselves might be suspect.

The Yale historian Timothy Snyder documents how Nazi and Soviet systems both relied on purity spirals within their own ranks. The SS competed to demonstrate greater ideological purity. Soviet officials denounced each other in ever-more-elaborate displays of loyalty. Both systems created environments where yesterday's orthodoxy became today's deviation. The result wasn't just political terror but organizational paralysis — no one could risk the initiative that might be tomorrow's heresy.

The spiral ends when it runs out of people to expel or when someone breaks the competitive dynamic. Sometimes an external crisis forces attention outward. Sometimes exhaustion sets in — people simply tire of the constant escalation. Sometimes a remnant recognizes what's happening and deliberately steps back from the edge. But often the spiral simply consumes the group entirely. The original mission becomes irrelevant. All that remains is the competition to prove purity in an ever-shrinking circle.

What makes purity spirals particularly insidious is that they weaponize real moral concern. The person raising the bar isn't necessarily acting in bad faith. They may genuinely believe the standard should be higher. And their belief may be correct — maybe the previous standard was inadequate. But the competitive dynamic ensures that no standard can ever be high enough. There's always a purer position, always someone willing to claim it, always an incentive to escalate.

The spiral reveals something uncomfortable about human moral psychology: our capacity to use righteousness as social currency, to convert genuine concern into competitive advantage, to fragment communities in the very name of the values that should unite them. It shows how quickly the pursuit of purity can become more important than purity itself.
