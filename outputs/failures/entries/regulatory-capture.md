When Boeing's 737 MAX received its safety certification in 2017, most of the actual inspection work wasn't done by Federal Aviation Administration engineers. It was done by Boeing employees. The FAA had deputized the company to certify its own aircraft, a practice called "delegation" that emerged from decades of the aviation industry arguing for streamlined regulation. Two years later, after two crashes killed 346 people, investigators found that Boeing's safety analysis had been inadequate and that FAA oversight had been minimal. The agency created to regulate Boeing had, in practice, become dependent on Boeing's cooperation, expertise, and framing of what regulation should look like.

This is regulatory capture — when an industry gains substantial influence over the regulatory agencies meant to oversee it. The regulator doesn't disappear. It continues to exist, write rules, conduct reviews. But its priorities gradually align with the industry's interests rather than the public's. The agency starts seeing problems through industry eyes, adopting industry assumptions about what's feasible, what's necessary, what counts as safety.

The pattern appears across sectors. Pharmaceutical companies influence FDA approval processes. Financial firms shape banking regulation. Tech platforms help write privacy laws. The mechanism varies — sometimes it's direct lobbying, sometimes industry experts joining regulatory agencies, sometimes agencies depending on industry data to write rules. But the outcome is consistent: regulation that protects incumbents more than it protects the public.

Regulatory capture happens because of asymmetries. The industry has concentrated interests — a regulation costs or benefits each company significantly. The public has diffuse interests — each person gains or loses relatively little. This means industry has strong incentive to invest in influencing regulation, while the public has weak incentive to monitor it. A pharmaceutical company will spend millions lobbying for a longer patent period. Each consumer loses a few dollars from delayed generic drugs, not enough to justify fighting back.

The industry also has information advantages. They know their own operations intimately. Regulatory agencies depend on industry data, industry expertise, industry assessments of feasibility. When the FAA needed to understand Boeing's new flight control system, they relied largely on Boeing's own analysis. When financial regulators wrote rules after 2008, they consulted extensively with banks about what regulations were practical. The regulated become the authorities on regulation.

Career incentives accelerate this. Regulators know that industry jobs pay better than government positions. The SEC lawyer might join a securities firm. The FDA reviewer might work for a pharmaceutical company. This "revolving door" doesn't require corruption — just professionals advancing their careers. But it means regulators hesitate to antagonize future employers. Industry gains allies who understand regulation from the inside.

The damage compounds over time. Captured regulation creates barriers that protect existing firms from competition. If you need millions in compliance costs to enter a market, established players with regulatory relationships have an advantage. The pharmaceutical industry's complex approval process protects major firms from generic manufacturers and newcomers. Financial regulation's Byzantine requirements favor banks that can afford compliance departments. The regulation meant to protect the public becomes a moat protecting incumbents.

Safety and innovation suffer. When Boeing essentially certified its own aircraft, corners got cut. When financial regulators adopted bank-friendly stress tests, systemic risks went undetected. When tech platforms helped write privacy laws, the rules permitted business models built on data extraction. The regulator still produces rules, but they're rules the industry can live with — often rules the industry helped write.

Public trust erodes. People sense when regulation is performative. They notice when financial firms that caused the 2008 crisis face minimal consequences. They observe that the FDA takes years to approve generic drugs while fast-tracking branded pharmaceuticals. They see that tech platforms that promised to self-regulate kept violating privacy. The agency still exists, but its legitimacy drains away. And without trust, even good regulation faces resistance.

The capture is often invisible. No formal conspiracy is required. Industry executives aren't secretly controlling agencies. Instead, regulators gradually adopt industry perspectives through normal institutional channels — hiring industry experts as consultants, using industry data for decisions, treating industry concerns as identical with public interest. The FAA believed Boeing's safety assessment because Boeing were the aviation experts. The assumption seemed reasonable. Until planes fell from the sky.

Recognition requires asking: whose interests does this regulation actually serve? When banks help write banking rules, do the rules prevent the next crisis or legitimize current practices? When pharmaceutical companies influence drug approval, does the process ensure safety or extend patent monopolies? When platforms draft privacy legislation, does it protect users or protect business models? The hardest part is noticing when regulation exists primarily to create the appearance of oversight while industry continues as before.

Economist George Stigler won the Nobel Prize partly for documenting this pattern. He showed that regulation frequently ends up serving producers rather than consumers, that industries often seek regulation because it creates barriers to entry, and that the public's diffuse interests rarely compete effectively with industry's concentrated incentives. His work suggested capture wasn't a bug but a predictable feature of regulatory systems. The question isn't whether it happens. The question is recognizing it when it does — and understanding that sometimes the presence of regulation provides less protection than the absence of it would suggest.
