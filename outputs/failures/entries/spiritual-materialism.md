# Spiritual Materialism

The meditation app on your phone tracks your streak. Forty-seven days of consecutive practice. You feel accomplished. You post about your morning ritual on social media — the cushion by the window, the carefully arranged altar, the quote from Thich Nhat Hanh. Someone comments "inspiring." You feel validated. You're collecting spiritual experiences the way you once collected professional achievements, relationships, possessions. The vocabulary has changed from "successful" to "awakened," from "status" to "consciousness," but the underlying pattern remains identical: accumulation, comparison, self-elevation. You have turned the tools meant to dissolve the self into another project of self-construction.

This is spiritual materialism — the co-option of spiritual practices, insights, and traditions into the service of ego. The term comes from Chögyam Trungpa Rinpoche, a Tibetan Buddhist teacher who watched Western students approach meditation with the same acquisitive mentality they brought to everything else. They wanted enlightenment the way they wanted graduate degrees. They collected teachings like credentials. They used mindfulness to become better performers, better competitors, more effective versions of exactly what they were trying to transcend.

The mechanism is subtle. Authentic spiritual practice aims at self-transcendence — the dissolution of ego boundaries, the recognition of interconnection, the release from constant self-reference. But ego is adaptable. It takes the very practices meant to undermine it and repurposes them as evidence of its superiority. "I meditate daily" becomes another item on the resume. "I've had profound experiences" becomes another way to establish hierarchy. The self that should be getting smaller instead gets more refined, more sophisticated, more pleased with itself.

Trungpa described three forms this takes. First, the spirituality of materialism — using spiritual practice to acquire what you wanted anyway: peace of mind to be more productive, equanimity to handle stress better, compassion to improve your relationships. The practice becomes instrumental, a tool for worldly goals repackaged in spiritual language. You meditate to close better deals. You practice loving-kindness to network more effectively.

Second, the materialism of ego: using spiritual accomplishments to build identity. You become "a meditator," "a yogi," "someone on a path." You collect experiences — a silent retreat here, a plant medicine ceremony there, a pilgrimage to India. Each one confirms your specialness. You're not like those unconscious people sleepwalking through life. You're awake, or at least waking up. You've seen through the illusions they're still trapped in. The very recognition of ego becomes proof of its transcendence. Pride in your humility. Attachment to non-attachment. Ego claiming credit for its own dissolution.

Third, the materialism of doctrine: accumulating spiritual knowledge as intellectual property. You've read the Bhagavad Gita, the Tao Te Ching, the Cloud of Unknowing. You can quote Rumi and Eckhart Tolle. You understand the difference between Vipassana and Samatha, between Kashmir Shaivism and Advaita Vedanta. The knowledge becomes another possession, another mark of distinction. You're spiritually literate in a way most people aren't. The wisdom traditions meant to point beyond concepts become concepts you possess.

The damage compounds over time. First, to yourself: you think you're progressing when you're actually constructing a more sophisticated prison. The spiritual vocabulary gives you new ways to judge yourself and others, new hierarchies to navigate, new anxieties about whether you're doing it right. You bring the same performance mentality that created your suffering to the practices meant to address it. You turn meditation into another thing to optimize, another competition to win.

Second, to authentic practice: when spirituality becomes self-improvement, it loses its transformative power. Real practice is supposed to be humiliating — the constant recognition that you're not special, that your insights are ordinary, that enlightenment keeps receding because you keep trying to possess it. But spiritual materialism cushions you from this necessary discomfort. You get to feel like you're doing spiritual work while protecting exactly what that work should threaten.

Third, to traditions themselves: when spiritual teachings are mined for techniques stripped of context, they become consumer products. Mindfulness becomes an app. Yoga becomes exercise. Sacred rituals become wellness activities. The traditions are flattened, commodified, made safe and marketable. What was meant to challenge your fundamental assumptions about reality gets repackaged as stress management.

You recognize it by a particular flavor of satisfaction. Genuine spiritual practice tends to generate what John of the Cross called "holy dissatisfaction" — a recognition of how far you are from what you glimpsed, how much you don't know, how constantly you fail. Spiritual materialism feels good. You're proud of your practice. You feel superior to less conscious people. You use spiritual language to dismiss others' concerns or establish yourself as more evolved. When someone challenges you, you respond with spiritual platitudes that position you as the teacher.

Another sign: you can't laugh at yourself. Real practice makes you funnier, lighter, less self-serious — you've seen how absurd the whole edifice of self really is. Spiritual materialism makes you earnest, careful, concerned with maintaining your spiritual image. You're very serious about your non-attachment. Very careful about your spontaneity. Very disciplined about your freedom.

In 1976, Werner Erhard — founder of est, the human potential movement that charged thousands of dollars for weekend seminars promising enlightenment — represented spiritual materialism at scale. Participants paid to be verbally abused for sixteen hours while not being allowed bathroom breaks, emerging feeling transformed, special, part of an elite group who'd "gotten it." The techniques borrowed from Zen, gestalt therapy, Scientology. The structure was pure American capitalism — transformation as commodity, consciousness as purchasable product. Est graduated 700,000 people before rebranding as Landmark Forum. The business model proved durable: package spiritual insights as purchasable transformation, make people feel special for buying in, create hierarchies of advancement that mirror corporate structures, call it all consciousness work.

The trap is that genuine spiritual insight can feed it. You actually do have a meditation experience that feels profound. You actually do recognize some pattern in your thinking. You actually do feel more spacious, more present, more connected. But then ego takes credit. "I achieved this. I've progressed. I'm further along than others." The very recognition that was supposed to dissolve boundaries becomes another way to establish them.

Trungpa's prescription was simple but devastating: give up hope of fruition. Stop trying to get somewhere. Stop collecting experiences. Stop building a spiritual resume. Stop performing enlightenment. Just practice — stupidly, consistently, without expecting it to make you special or even make you better. Expect to fail. Expect to stay neurotic. Expect the practice to disappoint you. Only when you give up trying to use spirituality for self-improvement does actual transformation become possible.

But even this can become another achievement. "I've given up spiritual materialism" becomes proof you're more advanced than those still caught in it. The game is rigged. Ego plays every hand, including the one where you think you've transcended ego. 

The recognition of this endless recursion isn't depressing — it's the beginning of humor. Of course you're using spirituality to feel special. Of course your enlightenment is just another project. Of course you can't think your way out of thinking. The cosmic joke is that you were looking for what you already are.
