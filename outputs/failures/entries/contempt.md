# Contempt

In a Seattle laboratory, psychologist John Gottman watches couples discuss a disagreement. He's recording their facial expressions, their voice patterns, the microscopic movements that betray emotion. He's looking for what he calls "the four horsemen" — four patterns that predict divorce with disturbing accuracy. The most lethal? Contempt. When one partner speaks to the other with contempt — that particular curl of the lip, that tone of moral superiority, that communication of disgust — the marriage is in profound danger. Gottman can predict divorce from these moments with over 90% accuracy. What he's witnessing isn't anger or frustration. It's something more corrosive: the conviction that the other person is beneath consideration.

Contempt is the stance of superiority from which you communicate that another person is worthless. Not wrong — worthless. Not mistaken — beneath you. It's the emotional foundation of dehumanization. When you feel contempt, you don't just disagree with someone or dislike them. You place yourself on a moral plane above them, from which their very existence seems contemptible. The psychologist Paul Rozin, who studies disgust, notes that contempt adds a dimension of moral judgment to the basic emotion of disgust. You're not just repelled. You're certain of your right to be.

The face of contempt is unmistakable: the unilateral lip curl, often accompanied by a roll of the eyes. The voice drips with mockery or goes cold with dismissal. The language shifts — you stop addressing the person and start performing your disdain for an audience. "Oh, that's rich coming from you." "As if you would understand." "How predictable." The content barely matters. What matters is the metacommunication: I am above you. You are beneath consideration.

Contempt flourishes in conditions of self-righteousness. When you're certain not just that you're correct but that being correct makes you superior, contempt becomes nearly automatic. Political polarization creates ideal conditions — the other side isn't just wrong, they're stupid, venal, beneath contempt. Online environments accelerate it. Without the immediate feedback of another person's humanity — their face registering hurt, their posture showing withdrawal — contempt flows freely. You're performing for your tribe, and contempt is the currency of in-group solidarity. Look how far beneath us they are.

The damage operates on multiple levels. In intimate relationships, contempt is relationship poison. When your partner communicates contempt, they're not just criticizing a behavior. They're communicating fundamental disrespect for who you are. Over time, this creates what Gottman calls "negative sentiment override" — you begin interpreting everything they do through a lens of negativity. A neutral comment becomes an attack. A gesture becomes a judgment. The relationship doesn't recover from occasional contempt the way it recovers from occasional anger. Contempt degrades the foundation.

At the social level, contempt enables atrocity. When one group consistently views another with contempt — when they communicate through word and gesture that these people are fundamentally inferior — dehumanization follows. The Hutus who massacred Tutsis in Rwanda had spent years being taught to view them as "cockroaches." Nazi propaganda systematically taught Germans to view Jews with contempt. Contempt isn't the same as hatred, but it's often the precondition. You don't systematically destroy people you view as equals who happen to disagree with you. You destroy people you view as vermin, as beneath consideration, as contemptible.

Within yourself, contempt creates a terrible feedback loop. When you view others with contempt, you elevate yourself. This elevation feels good — you're on the moral high ground, you're superior, you're among the enlightened few who see clearly. But this superiority requires maintaining the contempt. If you start seeing the humanity in those you've deemed contemptible, your elevation collapses. So you seek out more evidence of their unworthiness. You notice every mistake, every hypocrisy, every confirmation of your judgment. Contempt, once established, becomes self-reinforcing.

You can recognize contempt by paying attention to your internal monologue. When you're in contempt, you're not thinking "they're wrong" or even "they're frustrating." You're thinking "they're pathetic." "They're stupid." "They don't deserve my time." You might find yourself performing your contempt — telling stories about their failures, mocking them to others, finding satisfaction in their missteps. The satisfaction is the tell. When someone else's pain or failure brings you pleasure because it confirms their unworthiness, you're in contempt.

In organizations, contempt shows up in how colleagues speak about other departments, how management talks about workers, how workers talk about management. "Those idiots in marketing." "The incompetents upstairs." "The lazy ones on the floor." When contempt becomes organizational culture, cooperation becomes nearly impossible. Why collaborate with people you view as fundamentally inferior? Why listen to their ideas? Why assume good faith? Contempt destroys trust without the obvious drama of conflict. You can maintain professional courtesy while holding someone in complete contempt. But you can't build anything together.

The philosopher Martha Nussbaum points out that contempt is ultimately about refusing to see others as fully human — as beings who make mistakes, who struggle, who deserve basic consideration. When you hold someone in contempt, you've stopped seeing them as a person trying to navigate difficulty. You see them as a category, a type, an embodiment of everything you're not. This is why contempt is so hard to reverse. Changing your opinion requires engaging with someone as a person. But contempt is precisely the stance from which engagement seems beneath you.

The deepest danger of contempt is how righteous it feels. When you're angry, part of you knows the anger might be disproportionate. When you're afraid, you can recognize the fear as possibly exaggerated. But contempt feels like clear sight. You're not being emotional — you're seeing someone for what they really are. This certainty is contempt's signature and its trap. The moment you're certain that someone is beneath consideration is the moment you should question your certainty most carefully.
