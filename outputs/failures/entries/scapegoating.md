# Scapegoating

A company's revenue drops. The CFO reviews dozens of factors: market conditions, product delays, competitor moves, pricing decisions. But in the emergency board meeting, she finds herself emphasizing one element above all others: the marketing director's failed campaign. It's not that the campaign succeeded—it didn't. But neither did it cause a 20% revenue shortfall. The narrative simplifies, though. One person. One failure. One explanation. Within a month, the marketing director is gone. The company's structural problems remain.

This is scapegoating—the social process of isolating blame onto a specific individual or group to discharge collective anxiety and avoid confronting systemic failure. The term comes from ancient Hebrew ritual: the high priest would symbolically transfer the community's sins onto a goat, which was then driven into the wilderness. The mechanism endures because it works, psychologically. Complex failures create unbearable uncertainty. Multiple causes, intersecting weaknesses, shared responsibility—these are cognitively overwhelming and emotionally intolerable. A scapegoat resolves this. It converts diffuse anxiety into focused blame, transforms "we failed" into "they failed," and creates the illusion that removing the designated person will solve the problem.

René Girard spent decades studying this pattern across cultures and finding it everywhere. His research revealed that scapegoating follows a consistent structure: a crisis emerges that threatens group cohesion, the group experiences collective anxiety, suspicion coalesces around a particular member (often someone already marginal or different), accusations escalate, and the group expels or punishes the designated scapegoat. The community experiences temporary relief. The underlying crisis continues.

The psychology is sophisticated. Scapegoating isn't simple cruelty or obvious prejudice. It often involves genuine belief that the designated person is responsible. The group constructs a narrative that makes the blame seem reasonable, even inevitable. In the marketing director's case: she did run a failed campaign. The evidence is real. What shifts is the proportion—a partial cause becomes the total explanation. The group selects facts that support the narrative and discounts those that don't. Confirmation bias and motivated reasoning work in service of collective discharge.

Research on organizational behavior shows how this escalates. Groups under stress regress toward simpler explanations. When Columbia Business School researchers studied corporate failures, they found that companies experiencing crisis showed predictable patterns: initial complexity in diagnosis gave way to increasingly personalized attribution. Multiple factors narrowed to individual actors. The more severe the failure, the stronger the drive to identify specific culprits. Boards demanded accountability, which often meant not "understanding what went wrong" but "finding who was responsible." The distinction collapsed under pressure.

The damage extends in two directions. First, to the scapegoated individual. They absorb blame disproportionate to their actual contribution to failure. Their reputation suffers. Their career may be destroyed. And because scapegoating involves collective participation, they face not just institutional consequences but social abandonment—colleagues who know better stay silent or join the consensus. The isolation is profound. Second, to the group itself. The real problems persist. The revenue shortfall in the company had multiple causes: overpriced product, delayed releases, poor market positioning. Removing the marketing director addressed none of these. But by providing psychological relief, scapegoating prevented the uncomfortable work of systemic diagnosis.

You recognize scapegoating through specific patterns. The narrative simplifies rapidly—complex situations reduce to simple causation. The designated person becomes increasingly isolated as former allies distance themselves. Language shifts from "we struggled with" to "they caused." The proportion of blame seems out of sync with the proportion of responsibility. And most tellingly, removing the scapegoat doesn't solve the underlying problem, though initially it feels like it should.

The mechanism appears across scales. In families, one child may become the repository for systemic dysfunction—the "problem child" whose behavior explains all family stress while the parents' failing marriage goes unexamined. In politics, minority groups serve as scapegoats for economic anxiety—complex causes of unemployment or stagnation are attributed to immigration, trade, or cultural change. Historical examples are grimmer still. The Black Death's inexplicability led medieval communities to blame Jews, with accusations of well-poisoning providing a comprehensible enemy for an incomprehensible plague. Thousands died. The plague continued.

Philip Zimbardo's research on deindividuation and dehumanization illuminates how quickly this occurs. His work showed that groups can shift within hours toward treating designated members as less than human—not through explicit ideology but through situational pressure and role assignment. In his prison experiment, guards didn't decide to be cruel in principle. They fell into patterns that the system enabled. The prisoners designated for harsher treatment became, in the guards' minds, legitimately deserving of it. The self-justification was immediate and convincing.

What makes scapegoating particularly insidious is its self-reinforcing nature. Once someone is designated, their attempts to defend themselves can be read as evidence of guilt. They protest too much. They're deflecting. Their very insistence on examining systemic factors seems like evasion of responsibility. The narrative has enough truth—they did contribute something to the failure—that total innocence is impossible to claim. And because groups feel relief after scapegoating, the mechanism is rewarded. The tension dissipates. The group feels that action has been taken. The punishment feels proportionate because the group's psychological need was proportionate, even if the actual causation wasn't.

The test is what happens after. If removing the scapegoat doesn't solve the problem—and it rarely does—does the group then examine structural causes, or do they find another scapegoat? Often it's the latter. Companies that fire the "responsible" executive and see no improvement often fire another executive. The pattern repeats because the underlying need hasn't been met: the need to avoid confronting systemic failure, organizational weakness, or collective responsibility. It's easier to keep finding culprits than to admit the system itself is failing.

This is why scapegoating persists across millennia and cultures. It serves too many functions to abandon easily. It discharges anxiety. It preserves group cohesion through shared action against a common target. It protects the majority from having to examine their own contribution to failure. It creates the satisfying narrative arc of blame identified and justice served. And it does all this while feeling rational, justified, and necessary. The group rarely experiences itself as scapegoating. They experience themselves as holding someone accountable.
