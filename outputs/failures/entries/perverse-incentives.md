# Perverse Incentives

In colonial India, the British government grew concerned about venomous cobras in Delhi. The solution seemed straightforward: offer a bounty for every dead cobra. Initially, it worked. People killed cobras and collected payments. Then entrepreneurs began breeding cobras specifically to kill them for the reward. When the government discovered this and canceled the program, the breeders released their now-worthless snakes. Delhi ended up with more cobras than before. The incentive produced the opposite of its intended effect.

This is the perverse incentive — a reward structure that motivates behavior contrary to its purpose. The mechanism is simple: people respond to incentives by optimizing for the measure, not the underlying goal. When the measure diverges from the goal, rational actors produce irrational outcomes.

Charlie Munger called it the "iron law of incentives": show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome. But perverse incentives generate outcomes nobody wants. They emerge when designers focus on what's measurable rather than what matters, when short-term metrics override long-term goals, when gaming the system becomes more profitable than serving its purpose.

The pattern appears across domains. Schools evaluated on test scores teach to the test rather than fostering genuine learning. Police departments measured on arrest numbers pursue low-level offenses rather than serious crime. Hospitals paid per procedure perform unnecessary surgeries. Wells Fargo employees, pressured to meet account-opening quotas, created millions of fraudulent accounts without customer knowledge. The workers were responding rationally to their incentive structure. The outcome was systematic fraud.

Perverse incentives exploit a fundamental disconnect: those who design systems rarely experience them the same way as those who operate within them. The designers see the goal. The operators see the measure. When these diverge, operators optimize for survival within the system, not for the system's stated purpose.

Goodhart's Law formalizes this: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Soviet nail factories, evaluated by weight, produced heavy useless nails. When the metric changed to number of nails, they produced tiny useless ones. The measure shifted; the dysfunction remained. Workers weren't sabotaging production — they were responding rationally to how success was defined.

The damage compounds. Perverse incentives don't just fail to achieve their goals; they actively undermine them. Teachers pressured to raise test scores narrow curriculum, losing educational breadth. Doctors incentivized by patient volume spend less time per patient, degrading care quality. Executives compensated in stock options take excessive short-term risks, destabilizing companies. Each actor behaves rationally within their incentive structure while the system's purpose erodes.

What makes perverse incentives particularly insidious is that they often persist after being identified. The Wells Fargo fraud was an open secret for years before regulators acted. Soviet managers knew the nail quotas produced absurdity but couldn't change the system. The problem isn't ignorance — it's that changing incentive structures threatens those who benefit from them or requires admitting the current system is fundamentally broken.

Recognition requires asking: what behavior is this actually rewarding? Not what it claims to reward — what people must do to succeed within this system. Look for divergence between stated goals and measured outcomes. A company says it values innovation but promotes based on quarterly earnings. A university claims to prioritize teaching but grants tenure based on publications. The gap between rhetoric and reward reveals the true incentive.

Watch for optimization that serves the metric but not the mission. If call center workers are measured on call duration, they'll rush customers off the phone rather than resolve issues. If researchers are evaluated by publication count, they'll split findings into multiple papers rather than pursuing significant questions. If aid organizations are funded based on money distributed, they'll prioritize spending over impact. The measure becomes the mission.

Notice when achieving the metric requires undermining the goal. This is the diagnostic signature. If hitting your target makes the underlying problem worse — like the cobra bounty increasing cobra population — you have a perverse incentive. If meeting the standard degrades the quality it's meant to ensure — like teaching to the test reducing educational value — the incentive is working against itself.

The Soviets eventually discovered that measuring economic success by gross output led factories to produce goods nobody wanted, stored in warehouses nobody accessed, transported by logistics networks nobody needed. The entire economy optimized for an abstraction divorced from human need. Everyone met their targets. The system failed comprehensively.

Perverse incentives reveal a deeper truth about system design: you cannot improve what you cannot measure, but measuring the wrong thing guarantees you'll improve it in ways that destroy the purpose of measurement. The solution isn't abandoning metrics. It's recognizing that any single measure will be gamed, that quantification always loses nuance, that the map is never the territory. The cobra bounty failed not because bounties don't work, but because counting dead cobras couldn't capture the complexity of reducing cobra population sustainably.

The ultimate perversity is this: the more consequential the incentive, the more aggressively it will be gamed, and the faster it will stop measuring what it's meant to measure. High stakes don't improve outcomes. They accelerate the divergence between metric and meaning.
