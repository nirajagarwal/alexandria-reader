# Mission Drift

The organization started with clarity. A nonprofit founded to provide job training for formerly incarcerated people. Straightforward, measurable, urgent. Five years later, they're running a bakery, offering yoga classes, hosting art exhibitions, and applying for a grant to start a community garden. The original job training program? Still there, technically, but now competing for attention with seven other initiatives. None of them bad. None of them the mission.

This is mission drift — when an organization gradually shifts away from its core purpose, accumulating new activities, goals, and constituencies until the original mission becomes one priority among many. The bakery made sense at the time: trainees needed real work experience. The yoga was a partner suggestion. The art exhibition brought in a major donor. The community garden aligns with "holistic reintegration." Each addition carried its own logic. Together, they dissolved focus.

Mission drift operates through expansion, not abandonment. The organization doesn't repudiate its founding purpose — it adds to it. The additions feel adjacent, complementary, synergistic. We're still serving the same population. We're just serving them more completely. The original focus blurs not through rejection but through accretion. Every new program dilutes the mission slightly. Over time, the drift compounds.

Why organizations are vulnerable comes down to incentives and identity. Funders want innovation. Donors have pet interests. Board members bring their own visions. Staff see needs the current programs don't address. There's always a compelling case for expansion: unreached populations, unmet needs, unclaimed impact. And narrowness feels limiting. Shouldn't we do everything we can to help? The fear of saying no — of leaving needs unmet, resources unclaimed, opportunities unexplored — makes drift almost inevitable.

The damage is insidious because it's gradual. No single addition breaks the organization. But collectively, they fracture effectiveness. Resources spread thin. Staff expertise dilutes. The thing the organization did well — the thing it was built to do — gets no better at doing. Impact becomes harder to measure because there's no clear thing being measured. Formerly incarcerated people get some job training, some baked goods, some yoga, some art. Whether any of it actually gets them employed becomes unclear. The mission becomes everything, which means it becomes nothing specific enough to accomplish.

Early warning signs appear in language and leadership attention. When mission statements expand from crisp to comprehensive — when "job training" becomes "holistic reintegration and community empowerment" — drift is underway. When board meetings spend equal time on core programs and peripheral initiatives, focus has fractured. When staff can't quickly articulate what the organization is for, drift has advanced. When "impact" gets measured in activities (people served, programs offered) rather than outcomes (people employed, lives changed), the organization has lost sight of purpose.

The pattern appears everywhere. A community health clinic starts offering dental care, then mental health services, then job counseling, then housing assistance. A tutoring program adds sports, arts, mentorship, college counseling. A domestic violence shelter expands to general women's services, then family services, then community wellness. Each step seems natural. Together they dilute the core competency. The classic case is the March of Dimes, founded specifically to fight polio. When the polio vaccine succeeded in 1955, the organization faced a choice: declare victory and close, or find a new mission. It chose expansion, pivoting to birth defects and maternal health. Not wrong — but fundamentally different from the precise, eliminable problem it was designed to solve. The organization survived. The original mission drift was complete.

Tech companies drift similarly but faster. Google started as a search company. Now it makes phones, develops AI, runs cloud infrastructure, produces original video content, operates a venture capital arm, and manufactures autonomous vehicles. Some extensions made strategic sense — search advertising enabled everything else. But the drift from "organizing the world's information" to "everything everywhere" exemplifies how success enables mission expansion. Revenue allows exploration. Exploration becomes the mission. Focus dissolves.

Universities drift across decades. Liberal arts colleges add professional programs because students want jobs. Then they add graduate programs because faculty want research. Then they add online programs because markets demand flexibility. The original mission — undergraduate liberal education — persists but competes for resources with initiatives that generate revenue or prestige. No one decided to abandon undergraduate teaching. But when it competes equally with executive education, research institutes, and online master's programs, drift has fundamentally altered what the institution is for.

The mechanism is structural. Organizations exist in environments that reward expansion. Funders favor innovation over refinement. The grant goes to the organization proposing something new, not the one doing the same thing better. Donors want their names on buildings and programs, not on incremental improvements to existing work. Media coverage favors the novel — the organization that just launched a new initiative, not the one that just got measurably better at its core work. The incentive structure punishes focus.

Leadership transitions accelerate drift. New executives need to make their mark. Continuing what works feels insufficient. So they add, expand, pivot. The board hired them for fresh vision, after all. The fresh vision usually means new programs. Five executive transitions later, the organization has accumulated five different leaders' visions, layered imperfectly on top of each other. The original mission is buried under strata of subsequent additions.

Recognition requires comparing current reality to founding purpose. What were we created to do? What are we actually doing now? If the answers significantly diverge — if the founding purpose has become one option on a menu of initiatives — drift has occurred. The test isn't whether the additions are good or whether they help people. The test is whether they serve the original, specific reason the organization exists.

The paradox is that drift often comes from commitment, not complacency. Organizations drift because they care deeply about impact, so they add programs to increase it. They drift because they're responsive to community needs, so they expand to meet new ones. They drift because they're ambitious, so they pursue every opportunity. The very qualities that make organizations effective — adaptability, responsiveness, ambition — make them vulnerable to losing focus. Mission drift is often success mistaken for mission.
