In October 1994, radio broadcasts across Rwanda began referring to Tutsi civilians as *inyenzi* — cockroaches. Not metaphorically. Not as occasional slur. As standard nomenclature in news reports, public announcements, casual conversation. Within months, ordinary Rwandans — neighbors, teachers, shopkeepers — were killing Tutsis with machetes. Eight hundred thousand dead in a hundred days. Many killers later described their victims not as people but as things to be cleared away. This is dehumanization: the psychological process of ceasing to perceive others as fully human.

The term sounds extreme — something that happens only in genocide or slavery. But researchers have found that milder forms occur constantly, even in ordinary life. Philip Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment, where college students playing guards brutalized students playing prisoners in six days, demonstrated how quickly normal people can shift from seeing others as peers to treating them as objects. The guards didn't need training or ideology. They needed only role assignment and slight distance. Once the prisoners became categories rather than individuals, empathy collapsed.

Dehumanization operates along a gradient. At one end: systematic extermination campaigns built on propaganda machines. At the other: the small daily failures where we stop registering someone's humanity. The homeless person you step around without eye contact. The customer service representative you snap at because they're just a voice on the phone. The political opponent you describe as vermin or disease. Same mechanism, different magnitudes.

What makes us vulnerable is how perception actually works. The human brain cannot hold everyone in full moral consideration simultaneously. We have cognitive shortcuts: in-group/out-group categories, us-versus-them thinking, the tendency to see our group as individuals with complex motives and other groups as homogeneous masses with simple bad intent. These shortcuts were adaptive when "other group" meant potential raiders from the next valley. In modern society, where we encounter thousands of people from dozens of groups, these shortcuts become moral catastrophes.

The process has identifiable stages. First comes categorization: they become a type rather than individuals. Not "this person who disagrees with me" but "one of them." Then comes attribution: their group gets assigned essential negative traits. They're not just wrong, they're incapable of good faith. Next comes emotional distancing: compassion circuits disengage. Their suffering registers differently than the suffering of your group. Finally comes moral exclusion: normal ethical rules don't apply to them. What would be cruel to do to "us" becomes acceptable to do to "them."

Language accelerates everything. Animal metaphors (vermin, parasites, cockroaches) are particularly effective because they bypass the brain's human recognition systems. Disease metaphors (infection, contamination, plague) trigger disgust responses that override empathy. Even subtler linguistic shifts matter: "the homeless" versus "people experiencing homelessness," "illegals" versus "undocumented immigrants." The first formulation makes a condition into an identity. The second preserves personhood.

Social structures enable dehumanization at scale. Bureaucracies excel at it — paperwork that converts people into case numbers, processes that create distance between decision-makers and affected humans. Hannah Arendt called it "the banality of evil": ordinary people implementing procedures that destroy lives without ever confronting what they're doing. The Wehrmacht soldier loading trains didn't think he was murdering children. He was following transport schedules. The drone operator striking a target isn't bombing a wedding. He's eliminating a threat signature.

Institutions can amplify individual dehumanization into structural violence. Police trained to see certain neighborhoods as war zones. Immigration systems designed to process throughput rather than evaluate cases. Healthcare systems where doctors spend nine minutes per patient, not enough time to register personhood. Each person in the system may feel individually powerless — just following protocol, just doing their job — while collectively producing dehumanized outcomes.

The damage compounds across levels. Personally: you lose access to your own empathy, your capacity for moral perception atrophies. Relationally: you treat people as instruments or obstacles rather than ends in themselves. Socially: your community splits into us versus them, conversation becomes impossible. Politically: violence becomes thinkable because the target isn't quite human. Historically: we get genocides, slavery, systematic oppression — the worst of what humans do to each other.

Early recognition is crucial because dehumanization is progressive. You notice yourself using category labels more than names. You find yourself attributing bad motives automatically. You feel righteously angry at whole groups. You use language that diminishes rather than describes. You feel nothing when you hear about their suffering. Small signs, easy to rationalize. But they indicate the erosion of moral perception.

The hardest part of recognizing dehumanization is that it feels *right*. It doesn't feel like moral failure — it feels like moral clarity. They *are* bad. They *do* deserve contempt. The certainty is part of the trap. Genuine moral judgment acknowledges complexity even in wrongdoing. Dehumanization replaces complexity with category. When you're absolutely certain about whole groups of people, when your emotional response is uniform disgust or contempt rather than mixed, when you can't imagine any of them as someone you might respect — you're probably dehumanizing.

The psychological comfort of dehumanization makes it self-reinforcing. It simplifies moral reality. It provides enemies to unite against. It excuses behavior you'd normally find shameful. Once established, it's hard to reverse because seeing someone as fully human again means confronting what you've done to them, what you've become. Better to maintain the categorization. Better to insist they really are less than human. The alternative is unbearable recognition.
