# Status Games

In corporate America during the 1990s, Enron developed a performance review system called "rank and yank." Every six months, employees were rated on a curve. The bottom 15 percent were fired. The system was meant to cultivate excellence. What it cultivated instead was a culture where protecting your ranking mattered more than protecting the company. Traders concealed losses. Managers hoarded information that might make colleagues look good. Teams were less likely to collaborate if it meant sharing credit. By 2001, Enron had collapsed in what was then the largest corporate bankruptcy in American history. The rank-and-yank system didn't create bad people. It created a game where winning required behaviors that destroyed the organization.

Status games occur when the pursuit of relative position supersedes the pursuit of shared goals. The problem isn't ambition or competition. It's when the competition shifts from "who can contribute most" to "who can seem most valuable" — and when seeming valuable requires making others seem less so. In a status game, your success depends not on absolute achievement but on outperforming those around you. This transforms colleagues into rivals, collaboration into risk, and information into ammunition.

Research on zero-sum thinking shows how quickly this shift happens. When Yale psychologist Gordon Moskowitz studied competitive environments, he found that people automatically begin monitoring others' gains as personal losses. If you and I are competing for one promotion, your success becomes my failure — even if we're both performing well in absolute terms. The game changes from "how can we both excel" to "how can I excel more than you." Once that shift occurs, behaviors that benefit the group become strategically foolish. Helping you improve helps you beat me. Sharing what I know arms my competition.

The damage is structural, not just individual. Organizations filled with talented people begin performing worse than the sum of their parts. Information that should flow freely gets hoarded because sharing it might elevate a rival. Mistakes that should be caught early get concealed because admitting error could drop your ranking. Projects that would benefit from collaboration proceed in isolation because cooperation means sharing credit. Meanwhile, everyone spends cognitive resources on signaling value rather than creating it. The question shifts from "what's the best solution" to "what's the solution that makes me look best."

The academic world offers a natural laboratory for status games. Universities explicitly rank departments, journals rank by prestige, citations measure influence. In principle, this should drive quality — compete to publish the best research. In practice, researchers at Stanford Business School found that prestige competition often drives the opposite. Academics pursue fashionable topics over important ones because fashionable topics lead to prestigious publications. They favor clever methodologies that impress reviewers over robust ones that answer questions. They cite work by influential scholars whether or not it's relevant because citation networks determine status. The game becomes about maximizing prestige signals rather than maximizing knowledge. Meanwhile, replication studies — arguably essential for science — go unpublished because they don't confer status. The status game produces a scholarly literature that's impressive and unreliable.

You can recognize a status game by what people avoid. In a functional environment, people share credit, admit uncertainty, and help others improve because these behaviors serve shared goals. In a status game, these same behaviors become dangerous. Sharing credit dilutes your achievement. Admitting uncertainty reveals weakness. Helping others improve arms your competition. If you notice yourself calculating whether to mention a colleague's contribution, or editing your questions to avoid looking ignorant, or withholding a useful insight because it might help a rival, you're in a status game. If meetings feel like performance rather than problem-solving, you're in one. If success depends on others failing, you're in one.

The most insidious version is the game that punishes opting out. Enron's traders who refused to play status games didn't stay pure — they got fired. The academic who ignores prestige signals doesn't maintain integrity — they lose funding and influence. When status games become institutionalized, refusing to play means accepting defeat. This creates a tragedy: everyone might prefer to cooperate, but cooperation requires trust that others won't defect. Once a few people start playing for status, everyone else must follow or lose. The Nash equilibrium of a status game is mutual defection — everyone competing, everyone worse off than if they'd cooperated, no one able to unilaterally stop without penalty.

Robert Frank, economist at Cornell, documented this in his work on positional competition. When law firms compete for top graduates by offering lavish perks, no firm can stop offering them without losing recruits. When universities compete for rankings by spending on amenities, no university can redirect that money to education without dropping in the rankings. The competition locks everyone into expensive signaling that benefits no one. Frank found that Americans now spend billions on positional goods — things whose value depends on exceeding others rather than absolute quality. Bigger houses in better neighborhoods, more prestigious degrees, more visible success markers. The resources consumed in these competitions don't increase total welfare; they just redistribute status. Everyone runs faster to stay in place.

This is how organizations eat themselves. The game to look valuable crowds out the work of being valuable. The competition for relative position consumes resources that could create absolute gains. And because status is inherently scarce — there can only be so many people at the top — the competition never ends. You can win a performance goal. You can't win a status game. Someone is always higher, always competing, always threatening your position. The game continues until the organization collapses under the weight of its own internal competition.
