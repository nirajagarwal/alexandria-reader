In 1986, engineers at Morton Thiokol knew the O-rings would fail in cold weather. They had data. They had photographs of damaged seals from previous launches. They recommended postponing the Challenger mission. But in a teleconference the night before launch, under pressure from NASA managers concerned about schedule delays and public perception, the engineers reversed their recommendation. Seven crew members died seventy-three seconds after liftoff. The O-rings failed exactly as predicted.

Groupthink is what happens when the desire for group harmony overrides independent judgment. Teams that should be evaluating options critically instead converge on consensus prematurely. Dissent gets suppressed—not through explicit prohibition, but through social pressure, self-censorship, and the collective illusion that everyone agrees. The group's cohesion becomes more important than the quality of its decisions.

Yale psychologist Irving Janis coined the term in 1972 while studying foreign policy disasters. He examined the Bay of Pigs invasion, the escalation in Vietnam, Pearl Harbor—cases where intelligent people in groups made catastrophically bad decisions that individuals within those groups had private doubts about. The pattern was consistent: cohesive groups, under pressure, with directive leadership, systematically filtered out information that contradicted the emerging consensus.

The mechanism is social, not cognitive. Individual members retain their critical faculties. They notice problems, see alternatives, recognize risks. But they stay quiet. Speaking up means positioning yourself against the group. It risks social isolation, being labeled difficult or disloyal, disrupting harmony. So people silence themselves. They assume others know better. They rationalize their doubts away. Meanwhile, everyone else is doing the same calculation, creating a false consensus where no one actually believes what everyone appears to agree on.

Certain conditions make groups vulnerable. High cohesion—teams that like each other, have worked together successfully, share identity. Insulation from outside perspectives. Directive leadership that signals preferred outcomes. External stress that makes dissent feel dangerous. Structural flaws like homogeneity or lack of procedures for evaluating alternatives. The Challenger decision embodied all of these. NASA was insular, the managers signaled impatience with delays, the engineers felt pressure to be "team players," and the decision-making process lacked protocols for elevating dissenting technical judgments.

The symptoms are recognizable. The group develops an illusion of invulnerability—a conviction that things will work out despite warning signs. They collectively rationalize away information that contradicts their direction. They develop stereotyped views of outsiders or critics. Pressure gets applied to dissenters, usually subtle: "Are you saying you don't trust the team?" Members self-censor, withholding doubts. Mindguards emerge—people who protect the group from troubling information. And through it all, everyone maintains an illusion of unanimity because silence gets interpreted as agreement.

The damage compounds. In the moment, bad decisions get made—risky ventures launched, warning signs ignored, alternatives never seriously considered. The Challenger disaster killed seven people. The Bay of Pigs invasion damaged American credibility and pushed Cuba closer to the Soviet Union. But groupthink also damages the group itself. Trust erodes when people realize decisions were made on false consensus. The best members—those most willing to speak uncomfortable truths—leave or stop contributing. The group becomes progressively more detached from reality, increasingly convinced of its own rightness as it fails.

Recognition requires noticing the silence. When a team reaches consensus quickly, especially on complex decisions, that's a warning sign. When no one voices concerns about an important decision, that's suspicious—not reassuring. When critics get labeled as not being team players, that's groupthink talking. When the group interprets its track record as evidence it can't be wrong, danger. When you're in a meeting and you have doubts but assume everyone else sees something you don't—that's the moment. You're probably not alone in your doubt.

Organizations can interrupt the pattern structurally. Appoint someone to argue the opposing view—make dissent a formal role. Bring in outsiders to evaluate decisions. Split into subgroups that develop independent recommendations. Create anonymous channels for concerns. Make it safe, even rewarded, to voice doubts. But the fundamental intervention is simpler: someone has to speak up. One voice can break the illusion of unanimity, giving others permission to voice their own doubts.

The engineers at Morton Thiokol had the data. They knew. But they read the social situation and chose cohesion over correctness. It's easy to condemn that choice from outside, harder to make the different choice from inside. Groupthink is what happens when social pressure overcomes individual judgment—not through coercion, but through the basic human desire to belong, to maintain harmony, to not be the one who disrupts the group. The question isn't whether your groups are vulnerable. They are. The question is whether anyone will speak the doubt everyone is thinking.
