# Sunk Cost Fallacy

You're two hours into a movie you hate. You paid $15 for the ticket. The rational choice is obvious: leave now, spend the next hour doing something you'd actually enjoy. But you stay. You've already paid. Leaving means "wasting" the money. So you waste the time instead.

This is the sunk cost fallacy — the human tendency to continue investing in something because of what's already been invested, even when continuing makes things worse. The name comes from economics: a "sunk cost" is money, time, or effort already spent that can't be recovered. It's gone regardless of what you do next. But we treat it as if it should influence future decisions. We throw good money after bad, good years after wasted ones, because we can't bear to "waste" what we've already lost.

The fallacy shows up everywhere. You've read 300 pages of a terrible book and force yourself through the last 200. You're three years into a graduate program you've realized isn't for you, but you can't quit now — not after all this work. A company has spent $50 million developing a product that market research says will fail, so they spend another $30 million launching it anyway. Relationships persist years past their expiration date because of the time already invested. "After everything we've been through" becomes a reason to endure more.

Hal Arkes and Catherine Blumer demonstrated this in a classic experiment. They sold season tickets to a theater series, randomly assigning prices: full price, small discount, or large discount. Then they tracked attendance. People who paid full price attended significantly more shows, even though the money was spent regardless of whether they went. The ticket price — a sunk cost — was influencing their behavior months later. They were trying to "get their money's worth" by sitting through performances they didn't want to see.

The psychological mechanism runs deeper than simple loss aversion. We're terrible at admitting we were wrong. Cutting your losses means acknowledging the initial decision was a mistake. The three years in graduate school, the $50 million in development costs, the seven years in a dying relationship — these become evidence of judgment failure. So we continue. We tell ourselves the investment will eventually pay off. We reframe the situation: maybe the degree will be useful somehow, maybe the market will shift, maybe the relationship will improve. We're not just avoiding waste — we're avoiding the identity cost of being someone who made a bad choice.

Organizations are particularly vulnerable because sunk costs accumulate across decision-makers and time horizons. The manager who approved the initial $50 million isn't the same person deciding whether to spend the next $30 million. But the organization treats past investment as justification for continued investment. Canceling the project means admitting "we wasted $50 million." Launching it at least creates the possibility of recouping something. The Concorde supersonic jet program became a textbook case: Britain and France continued funding it years after economic analysis showed it would never be profitable, because stopping meant admitting the massive prior investment had been for nothing. The phenomenon became known as the "Concorde fallacy."

The damage compounds. Every dollar, hour, or year spent honoring a sunk cost is itself a new sunk cost. You stay in the movie, wasting time to honor money already wasted. That wasted time becomes a new reason to stay in the adjacent decision — you've already spent the whole afternoon, might as well see it through. The initial loss metastasizes. Relationships deteriorate further. Products fail more expensively. Careers drift years off course. And because the sunk cost fallacy makes you invest more, it makes the eventual abandonment more painful. The longer you stay in the failing relationship, the harder it becomes to leave, because now there's even more invested.

You recognize it by a specific feeling: the sense that stopping now would mean everything up to this point was wasted. When you catch yourself thinking "I've already spent so much," that's the fallacy operating. When you're continuing something you wouldn't start if given the choice fresh, that's the fallacy. Ask: if you hadn't already invested anything, would you choose to start this now? If the answer is no, the prior investment is pulling you into something your judgment says to avoid.

The cruelest aspect is that the fallacy becomes more powerful the more you've lost. Small investments are easy to write off — you can leave the bad movie after twenty minutes. But after twenty years in a career you hate, after $100 million in development costs, after a decade in a dysfunctional relationship, the thought of treating all that as simply gone becomes unbearable. So you invest more. The fallacy is strongest precisely when cutting losses matters most.

What makes this particularly insidious is that the reasoning feels virtuous. Commitment is good. Follow-through matters. Quitting feels weak. We admire people who stick with things when they get hard. But the sunk cost fallacy isn't about commitment — it's about confusing past choices with future ones. The money is spent. The years are gone. The only question is what to do now. And honoring a past mistake by making a continuing one isn't virtue. It's just compounding the loss.
