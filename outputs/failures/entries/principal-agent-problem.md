# Principal-Agent Problem

A hospital administrator designs a new compensation system. Doctors will receive bonuses based on the number of patients they see per day. Productivity increases immediately. Within six months, appointment times have shortened from twenty minutes to twelve. Diagnoses become more superficial. Referrals to specialists drop — they take time the doctor no longer has. Patient satisfaction scores decline. The administrator got exactly what he incentivized, but not what he actually wanted: good medicine. This is the principal-agent problem — what happens when the person doing the work (the agent) has different interests than the person who hired them (the principal), and the principal can't fully observe what the agent is doing.

The problem emerges from three conditions occurring simultaneously. First, goal misalignment: what the agent wants differs from what the principal wants. The doctor wants to maximize income and minimize effort; the principal wants quality care. Second, information asymmetry: the agent knows more about their work than the principal can observe. The doctor knows whether a patient needed more time; the administrator sees only throughput numbers. Third, measurement difficulty: the outcomes the principal actually cares about are harder to measure than the proxies they use. Patient health is complex and long-term; appointments per day is simple and immediate.

These three conditions create a gap where agents optimize for what's measured rather than what matters. Teachers teach to the test because test scores are measured and learning isn't. Software engineers accumulate technical debt because shipping features is visible and code quality isn't. CEOs pursue quarterly earnings because stock prices respond to quarters, not to the decade-long health of the institution. The agent isn't necessarily acting in bad faith. They're responding rationally to the incentives they face. But their rational response undermines the principal's actual interests.

The classic case is Soviet nail factory managers. When Moscow set production targets by weight, factories produced heavy, useless nails. When Moscow switched to targets by quantity, factories produced tiny, useless nails. The managers hit their numbers. The economy still needed actual nails. The agents did exactly what the measurement system rewarded. The system measured the wrong thing.

Organizations try various solutions, each generating new problems. Monitoring increases — more oversight, more reporting, more surveillance. But perfect monitoring is impossibly expensive, and monitoring itself becomes the new game to beat. As economist Bengt Holmström showed, when multiple tasks matter but only some are measured, agents shift effort toward measured tasks at the expense of unmeasured ones. Add more metrics, create more opportunities for gaming. The hospital tracks appointment length now, so doctors code more time as "administrative tasks." Every new measure creates a new optimization target that may not align with the underlying goal.

Performance pay seems like an obvious answer: align the agent's financial interest with the principal's goals. But as soon as compensation depends on a metric, that metric ceases to be a good measure of what it was meant to measure. Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Wells Fargo employees opened millions of fake accounts to hit sales targets. They were rewarded for new accounts; nobody specified the accounts had to be real. The agents responded precisely to their incentives. The principal discovered that measuring "accounts opened" was not the same as measuring "customer relationships built."

The information asymmetry runs deep. A financial advisor knows more about investment products than their client. A mechanic knows more about car repairs than the driver. A contractor knows more about construction than the homeowner. In each case, the agent can claim additional work is necessary, and the principal struggles to verify. The agent's expertise is precisely what makes them valuable and what makes them difficult to oversee. You hired them because you don't know how to do what they do. Which means you can't always tell if they're doing it well.

Some relationships handle this through reputation and repeated interaction. You trust your mechanic because you've worked with them for years, because they've had opportunities to exploit you and haven't. But reputation systems work best when there's actual repetition. For one-time transactions or distant oversight, reputation provides little protection. The financial advisor managing your retirement might not face consequences of bad advice until you retire decades later. The politician might not face consequences until they're out of office.

The problem scales. It exists in relationships: parents (principals) and teenagers (agents), where monitoring is limited and interests diverge. In organizations: shareholders and executives, where stock options try to align interests but create new games around stock price manipulation. In politics: voters and elected officials, where information about policy consequences is deeply asymmetric and electoral cycles create misaligned time horizons. In regulation: the public (principals) and agencies (agents), where agency capture by the industries being regulated is a constant risk.

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky showed how even well-intentioned agents make predictably biased decisions. Add self-interest to cognitive bias, and the principal's problem compounds. The agent isn't just making mistakes; they're making mistakes that happen to benefit them. Confirmation bias plus financial incentive means the financial advisor genuinely believes the high-fee product is best for you. They're not lying. They've convinced themselves. The measurement problem isn't just external — it's internalized.

You recognize the problem when you notice: what you're getting isn't what you asked for, but it is what you measured. When meeting targets correlates with declining quality. When adding oversight creates new forms of the same behavior. When the people doing the work develop elaborate justifications for why the simple interpretation of their job misses crucial complexity — and they're not entirely wrong, but their version of complexity happens to make their performance look better. When you can't tell if you're getting bad service or if the task is genuinely harder than you understood. When someone with more expertise than you explains why the expensive option is necessary and you can't evaluate their reasoning.

The damage accumulates slowly. Individual instances seem minor — one rushed diagnosis, one test-focused lesson plan, one quarter of short-term thinking. But systematic misalignment between agents and principals degrades institutions. Healthcare becomes processing rather than healing. Education becomes credentialing rather than learning. Business becomes quarterly performance theater. Politics becomes responsive to donor interests because donor satisfaction is more immediately observable than constituent welfare. The agents are responding to incentives. The incentives are misaligned with the stated purpose. The purpose drifts.

There's no perfect solution because the information asymmetry is often inherent. You hire experts precisely because they know things you don't. Measuring everything destroys trust and creates gaming. Measuring nothing enables exploitation. The principal-agent problem isn't a failure of virtue — it's a structural feature of any relationship where one party acts on behalf of another with interests that don't perfectly align and information that isn't perfectly shared. Which is nearly every relationship where work is delegated and outcomes are complex.
