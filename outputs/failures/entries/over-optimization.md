# Over-Optimization

A hospital decides to reduce average emergency room wait times. A reasonable goal. They implement a target: patients must be seen within four hours. Staff respond by admitting patients to holding beds at 3 hours and 55 minutes — technically "seen" but not actually treated. Wait times on paper drop. Actual patient care deteriorates. The hospital has optimized the metric instead of the outcome.

This is over-optimization: the failure that occurs when improving a measure becomes more important than improving the underlying reality the measure was meant to track. You focus so intensely on making the number move that you lose sight of why the number mattered in the first place. The proxy replaces the purpose.

Charles Goodhart, an economist at the Bank of England, formalized what we now call Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The observation captures a peculiar feature of human systems — our remarkable ability to game metrics without improving performance. We don't necessarily cheat. We just redirect our efforts toward moving the number rather than solving the problem the number was meant to indicate.

The vulnerability comes from a basic fact about complex realities: they're hard to measure directly. Patient care quality, teaching effectiveness, software reliability, employee engagement — these are multidimensional, context-dependent, resistant to reduction. So we pick proxies. Test scores for learning. Lines of code for productivity. Citations for research impact. Response time for customer service. The proxies are easier to measure than the thing itself. Then we optimize them.

What happens next is predictable. Teachers teach to the test. Programmers write verbose code. Researchers chase citation counts rather than important questions. Customer service reps rush callers off the phone. Each optimization is rational from the perspective of hitting the target. Each makes the underlying reality worse.

The damage compounds across organizational levels. Wells Fargo set aggressive targets for new account openings. Employees responded by opening millions of fake accounts without customer knowledge. The metric improved. Customer trust collapsed. The executives who set the targets weren't asking employees to commit fraud — they were asking them to open accounts. But when you make the metric paramount, people will find ways to move it.

In education, the effect is particularly visible. "No Child Left Behind" legislation mandated standardized testing to measure school performance. Schools responded by narrowing curriculum to tested subjects, teaching test-taking strategies rather than deeper understanding, and in some cases, simply excluding low-performing students from test day. Reading and math scores improved in some districts. Student learning in science, arts, history, and critical thinking declined. You get what you measure, but you lose what you don't.

Software development faces a version of this in "velocity optimization." Teams measure story points completed per sprint — a useful estimate for planning. Then management starts treating velocity as a performance metric. Teams respond by inflating estimates. A task that's actually two points gets estimated as five. Velocity numbers increase. Actual work completed stays the same or drops as teams spend energy gaming estimates instead of building software.

The pattern appears wherever metrics meet incentives. Soviet nail factories, when given production targets by weight, made heavy, useless nails. When targets shifted to quantity, they made tiny, useless nails. Neither metric captured what actually mattered: producing nails people needed. The factory workers weren't sabotaging production. They were optimizing exactly what they'd been told to optimize.

You can recognize over-optimization by a characteristic signature: the metric improves while the thing the metric was meant to improve doesn't. Emergency room wait times drop but patient outcomes don't improve. Sales numbers increase but customer satisfaction declines. Code volume rises but software quality falls. When there's divergence between the measure and the reality, optimization has likely escaped its moorings.

Another sign: people stop talking about the underlying goal and start talking exclusively about the metric. Meetings focus on "moving the number" rather than understanding what the number represents. Questions about whether the metric still tracks the outcome get dismissed as naive or obstructive. The measure has become reified — treated as real in itself rather than as an imperfect indicator of something else that matters.

The hardest cases involve multiple metrics in tension. Healthcare quality involves both speed and thoroughness. Student learning includes both measurable skills and harder-to-measure capabilities like creativity. Customer service requires both efficiency and genuine help. When organizations optimize one metric without balancing others, the system tilts. Emergency rooms become fast but careless. Schools produce good test-takers who can't think independently. Call centers become efficient at ending calls without solving problems.

Jerry Muller's research on what he calls "metric fixation" documents the pattern across sectors. Universities optimize research output by pressuring faculty to publish volume rather than significance. Police departments optimize crime statistics through manipulation rather than actual crime reduction. Hospitals optimize patient satisfaction scores by overprescribing pain medication. In each case, the metric was introduced with good intentions — to improve accountability, drive performance, enable comparison. The optimization broke it.

The failure is particularly insidious because it often looks like success in the short term. The numbers improve. Dashboards turn green. Reports to stakeholders show progress. Only later do the second-order effects become visible: the thing you were actually trying to improve has gotten worse. By then the optimization has become embedded in incentives, systems, and organizational culture. Unwinding it requires admitting the metrics you've been celebrating were misleading.

Recognition requires asking not just "Is the metric improving?" but "Is the reality the metric was meant to track improving?" It requires maintaining contact with ground truth — actually talking to customers, patients, students, users. It requires noticing when people start finding clever ways to move numbers without doing the underlying work. Most critically, it requires remembering that measures are tools for understanding reality, not substitutes for it.

The failure isn't metrics themselves. Measurement can illuminate. The failure is the moment when improving the measurement becomes the goal rather than improving the thing being measured. When that inversion happens, optimization becomes its own undoing.
