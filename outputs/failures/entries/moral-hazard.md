When insurance companies first started covering fire damage, an unexpected pattern emerged: fires increased. Not because people were careless — because they were rational. When your building is insured for more than it's worth, burning down becomes financially beneficial. The insurers had created a peculiar incentive: they would bear the losses while the insured collected the gains. This is moral hazard — when protection from consequences changes behavior in ways that make those consequences more likely.

The term comes from insurance, but the mechanism operates wherever risk and reward become separated. When banks know they're too big to fail, they take bigger risks. When executives are paid in stock options that reward short-term gains, they optimize for quarterly results rather than long-term stability. When warranty coverage expires just after the likely failure point, manufacturers have no incentive to build things that last. In each case, the party making decisions doesn't bear the full cost of those decisions.

The 2008 financial crisis was moral hazard at scale. Investment banks packaged mortgages into securities, sold them to investors, and collected fees — while bearing no risk if those mortgages defaulted. The incentive structure was clear: originate as many loans as possible, regardless of quality, because someone else would absorb the losses. Lenders approved mortgages they knew were unsound. Rating agencies, paid by the banks whose securities they rated, gave AAA ratings to toxic assets. Everyone profited from the upside while the downside was distributed across the economy.

Moral hazard thrives on information asymmetry and separation of costs from decisions. The person protected from consequences knows more about their behavior than the person bearing the risk. You know whether you're checking your smoke alarms; your insurance company doesn't. Executives know whether they're mortgaging the company's future for this quarter's numbers; shareholders find out later. The distance between decision and consequence creates space for exploitation.

But moral hazard isn't always conscious or malicious. Often it's structural — the natural result of incentives that seemed reasonable when designed. Health insurance changes how people use medical care, not because they're trying to game the system, but because cost no longer constrains decisions. Unemployment insurance affects job search intensity, not because recipients are lazy, but because the urgency of finding work decreases when basic needs are met. The behavior change is rational response to changed circumstances.

The damage compounds through hidden costs. When risks are socialized but profits privatized, resources flow toward activities that would be unprofitable if the full costs were internalized. Banks that would fail in a truly free market stay afloat through implicit guarantees, distorting capital allocation. Companies that should invest in quality instead optimize for warranty expiration. The economy produces more of what's profitable-when-subsidized and less of what's valuable-when-unsubsidized.

Society responds with monitoring and restrictions — the insurance company inspects your building, requires certain safety features, limits coverage. The bank faces capital requirements and stress tests. The insured party resents the oversight, the insurer bears the cost of enforcement, and both live with the friction. The relationship becomes adversarial: the insurer assuming bad faith, the insured looking for loopholes. Trust erodes precisely where it's most needed.

You can recognize moral hazard by asking: Who makes the decision? Who bears the consequences? If the answers are different people, moral hazard is present. The larger the gap between decision-maker and consequence-bearer, the stronger the perverse incentive. A homeowner with no insurance maintains the property carefully because they'll bear the full cost of damage. The same homeowner, fully insured, might be less vigilant — not because they're dishonest, but because the incentive structure changed.

The tragedy is that protection itself creates the need for more protection. Deposit insurance prevents bank runs, but encourages riskier banking. Bailouts prevent systemic collapse, but encourage the behaviors that make collapse more likely. The solution to moral hazard — monitoring, restrictions, risk-sharing mechanisms — never fully eliminates it, because the fundamental problem is the gap between decision and consequence. Close the gap too much and you eliminate the benefit of insurance or specialization. Leave it open and behavior distorts.

The S&L crisis of the 1980s showed this in miniature. Deposit insurance meant savings and loan associations could offer high interest rates to attract deposits without depositors caring about the institution's stability — the government guaranteed their money. The S&Ls used those deposits to make increasingly risky loans, because they kept the profits if the loans succeeded but taxpayers absorbed the losses if they failed. By the time regulators noticed, the bill was $160 billion. The protection meant to prevent small failures enabled a catastrophic one.

Moral hazard lives in the space between what we intend and what we incentivize. Every insurance policy, every guarantee, every safety net changes behavior. Sometimes that's acceptable cost. Sometimes it's catastrophic miscalculation. The question isn't whether moral hazard exists — it does, wherever risk is transferred — but whether we're honest about the behavioral changes we're creating, and whether we're prepared for the costs of the protection we're offering.
