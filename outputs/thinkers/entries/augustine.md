# Augustine of Hippo

A Roman African bishop who lived as the empire collapsed (354-430 CE), Augustine converted to Christianity at thirty-three after years pursuing philosophy and pleasure. He became the most influential theologian in Western Christianity, reshaping how believers understood sin, grace, time, memory, and the relationship between God and human will. His writings — especially *Confessions* and *The City of God* — created frameworks that dominated Christian thought for a millennium.

## The Problem

How can humans be free if God is all-knowing? How can evil exist if God is good? Augustine wrestled with these questions urgently, personally. His own past haunted him — years of sexual appetite, intellectual pride, adherence to Manichaeism's dualism. He needed to understand not just sin in general but his own incapacity to choose rightly despite knowing better. "I was held back by mere trifles," he wrote of his delayed conversion. The tension between divine sovereignty and human responsibility wasn't abstract theology but lived experience.

## The Idea

Augustine argued that humans possess free will but that will is radically damaged by original sin — the inherited consequence of Adam's rebellion. We choose freely, but our freedom is bent toward evil. We cannot save ourselves through choice alone; we need grace, God's unmerited intervention that heals the will and enables right choice. This resolved his dilemma: humans are responsible for their sins because they choose them, yet wholly dependent on God for salvation because their choosing mechanism is broken.

Time, for Augustine, exists only in consciousness. The past is memory, the future is expectation; both exist as mental states in an eternal present. God exists outside time entirely, seeing all moments simultaneously. This solved the problem of divine foreknowledge without eliminating human freedom.

He divided reality into two cities: the City of God, citizens oriented toward eternal goods, and the earthly city, oriented toward temporal power and pleasure. These cities are invisible, intermixed in actual societies, known fully only to God. The visible church contains members of both. This framework let Christians participate in earthly governance without confusing it with the kingdom of God.

## The Limits

Augustine's doctrine of original sin led him to positions he himself might have questioned: unbaptized infants condemned to hell, sexuality permissible only for procreation within marriage, the suppression of heretics justified by divine authority. His theology of predestination — that God chooses who receives grace — created centuries of debate about divine fairness. His suspicion of the body, especially sexuality, reflected Manichean influences he never fully shed. He read Genesis literally in ways that constrained later scientific inquiry. His framework privileged individual salvation over collective liberation, personal piety over structural justice.

## A Moment

In a Milan garden, 386 CE, Augustine heard children chanting "tolle lege" — take up and read. He opened Paul's epistles randomly: "Not in orgies and drunkenness, not in sexual immorality and debauchery, not in dissension and jealousy. Rather, clothe yourselves with the Lord Jesus Christ, and do not think about how to gratify the desires of the flesh." He closed the book. The long intellectual struggle ended. Monica, his mother who had prayed for his conversion for years, died shortly after, her hope fulfilled. The philosopher who questioned everything became the bishop who would answer everything.
