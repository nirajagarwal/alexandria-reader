# John Rawls

American political philosopher who rebuilt liberalism from first principles. Born 1921 in Baltimore, died 2002 in Massachusetts. Taught at Harvard for four decades. His 1971 book *A Theory of Justice* answered a question many thought settled: what makes a society just? His answer — fairness as the foundation of political order — gave progressive politics a philosophical architecture it had lacked since Marx demolished classical liberalism. He made distributive justice intellectually respectable again.

## The Problem

By the mid-twentieth century, utilitarianism dominated political philosophy: a society was just if it maximized aggregate happiness. But utilitarianism permits sacrificing some people's basic liberties for others' greater good — a conclusion that sat uneasily after fascism and totalitarianism. Meanwhile, libertarian thought defended absolute property rights but couldn't explain why someone born poor owed allegiance to that system. Rawls saw that neither framework could justify a liberal democratic society to all its members, especially the least advantaged. The question: on what grounds could free and equal citizens agree to live together under shared institutions?

## The Idea

Rawls proposed a thought experiment: imagine choosing principles of justice from behind a "veil of ignorance," not knowing your place in society — your class, race, talents, or values. What rules would you accept? You'd reject utilitarianism (you might be the one sacrificed) and pure libertarianism (you might be born poor). Rawls argued you'd choose two principles: first, equal basic liberties for all; second, economic inequalities allowed only if they benefit the least advantaged and positions are open to all. This "difference principle" revolutionized thinking about distributive justice. Unlike socialism, it permits inequality; unlike libertarianism, it requires inequality to be justified to those at the bottom. Justice isn't about maximizing wealth or enforcing equality, but about what free and equal people could rationally agree to. The veil of ignorance wasn't a historical claim but a device for moral reasoning: what makes institutions legitimate is whether they could be justified to everyone subject to them, not whether they emerged from consent or maximize utility. This "justice as fairness" became the dominant framework for progressive political philosophy.

## The Limits

Rawls assumed too much agreement. His "original position" presumes people share liberal values — that freedom and equality matter, that society is a cooperative venture. But many don't. Religious communities often prioritize truth over fairness; traditionalists reject equality; revolutionaries see cooperation as mystification. His theory can't persuade those who reject its premises. The veil of ignorance also erases what makes us particular — our histories, attachments, communal identities. Critics noted that justice for persons can't abstract from personhood entirely. His focus on ideal theory — perfectly just institutions — gave little guidance for actual politics with its compromises and injustices. And while the difference principle sounds egalitarian, it permits vast inequality if it marginally improves the worst-off position — a loophole that sits uneasily with intuitions about desert and reciprocity.

## A Moment

After publishing *A Theory of Justice*, Rawls taught a legendary seminar at Harvard where he systematically worked through objections to his own theory. Students noticed he'd accept criticisms that seemed devastating, revising positions he'd defended for years. This wasn't weakness but method: philosophy as collaborative truth-seeking, not system-defending. His later work increasingly qualified the original theory, narrowing its scope from universal justice to principles for liberal democracies specifically. Critics saw retreat; Rawls saw honesty about what philosophy could justify. In his final years, as cancer weakened him, he continued revising, trying to make the argument simpler, clearer, more defensible. The unfinished manuscript on his desk when he died was still working through an objection.

## In Their Own Words

> "Justice is the first virtue of social institutions, as truth is of systems of thought. A theory however elegant and economical must be rejected or revised if it is untrue; likewise laws and institutions no matter how efficient and well-arranged must be reformed or abolished if they are unjust."

> "Each person possesses an inviolability founded on justice that even the welfare of society as a whole cannot override. For this reason justice denies that the loss of freedom for some is made right by a greater good shared by others."

> "All social primary goods—liberty and opportunity, income and wealth, and the social bases of self-respect—are to be distributed equally unless an unequal distribution of any or all of these goods is to the advantage of the least favored."

> "The question is not what we would choose if we were in the original position, but whether we find the principles arrived at in that position acceptable on reflection and can affirm them as the basis for our political relations."

> "Philosophy is at best worldly wisdom. Its business is to articulate and make explicit the shared understandings embodied in our public life and democratic traditions, not to discover new moral truths or to found society anew."
