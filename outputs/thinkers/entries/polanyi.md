# Karl Polanyi

Karl Polanyi (1886–1964) was a Hungarian-born economic historian who argued that the modern "market economy" was not a natural outgrowth of human nature but a radical, recent, and ultimately self-destructing experiment. Writing from the wreckage of interwar Europe, he traced how attempts to treat land, labor, and money as pure commodities had torn societies apart. His central work, *The Great Transformation* (1944), remains the most penetrating critique of market fundamentalism — not from Marx, but from someone who took social embeddedness seriously.

## The Problem

Why did liberal civilization collapse into fascism? Polanyi watched the dissolution of the Habsburg Empire, fled Vienna for London, and witnessed the rise of Hitler. The question haunted him: how had the promise of progress and prosperity produced mass unemployment, social disintegration, and totalitarianism? Orthodox economists blamed insufficient markets. Marxists blamed capitalism itself. Polanyi saw something deeper — a category error built into nineteenth-century thinking. The attempt to organize society as an adjunct to self-regulating markets had pulled human life up by the roots. Society was protecting itself, but clumsily, desperately, sometimes catastrophically. Fascism was not the opposite of market liberalism but its dialectical twin.

## The Idea

Polanyi's central insight: markets are embedded in society, not the other way around. Throughout history, economic exchange served social relationships — reciprocity, redistribution, kinship, ritual. The nineteenth century attempted a reversal: to dis-embed the economy, making society serve market logic. This required treating land, labor, and money as commodities — "fictitious commodities," since none is actually produced for sale. Land is nature. Labor is human life. Money is a token of purchasing power. To subject these to supply and demand alone is to commodify the substance of existence itself.

This experiment was politically constructed — through enclosures, Poor Law reform, the gold standard — against fierce resistance. And it generated a double movement: as markets expanded, society reflexively protected itself through labor laws, social insurance, trade restrictions. Not because people were irrational or anti-progress, but because unbuffered marketization was literally unsustainable. The attempt to sustain it through force — to insist that markets must be freed from social interference — produced the crises that destroyed liberal civilization. Fascism and socialism were both responses to this original violence, though Polanyi distinguished sharply between protective movements that preserved freedom and those that extinguished it.

## The Limits

Polanyi romanticized pre-market societies, underestimating their hierarchies and cruelties. His account of non-Western economies sometimes flattened into noble-savage cliché. He was better at diagnosing commodification than prescribing alternatives; his vision of socialist planning remained vague, and history would show that state control brought its own forms of dis-embeddedness. His focus on protection sometimes downplayed the ways social movements themselves could be reactionary, xenophobic, or violent — not all resistance to markets defended freedom. And while he grasped the catastrophe of the gold standard, he underestimated markets' genuine capacity to coordinate complex activity and generate innovation. The question he couldn't fully answer: how to preserve embeddedness without stifling dynamism or freedom?

## A Moment

In 1933, unemployed and exiled in London, Polanyi began working through a problem at tutorial meetings organized by Christian socialists. He was fifty, starting over, teaching workers' education classes. From these discussions emerged the skeleton of *The Great Transformation*. He researched it while living on grants, publishing it in 1944 when he was nearly sixty. The book sold poorly and was quickly remaindered. Only decades later, as neoliberalism rediscovered the dream of self-regulating markets, did readers return to Polanyi's warning. By then he was dead, having spent his final years in Canada, still writing, still arguing that you cannot build a society on the fiction that human beings are merely factors of production.

## In Their Own Words

> "To allow the market mechanism to be sole director of the fate of human beings and their natural environment... would result in the demolition of society."

> "The road to the free market was opened and kept open by an enormous increase in continuous, centrally organized and controlled interventionism."

> "Socialism is, essentially, the tendency inherent in an industrial civilization to transcend the self-regulating market by consciously subordinating it to a democratic society."

> "The economic function is but one of many vital functions of land. It invests man's life with stability; it is the site of his habitation; it is a condition of his physical safety; it is the landscape and the seasons."

> "Nineteenth-century civilization alone was economic in a different and distinctive sense, for it chose to base itself on a motive only rarely acknowledged as valid in the history of human societies, and certainly never before raised to the level of a justification of action and behavior in everyday life, namely, gain."
