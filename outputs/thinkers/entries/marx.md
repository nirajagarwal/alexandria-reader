# Karl Marx

Karl Marx (1818–1883) was a German philosopher, economist, and revolutionary whose analysis of capitalism transformed how humans understand history, power, and social change. Exiled from Prussia and France, he spent most of his adult life in London, writing in the British Museum's reading room while his family lived in poverty. His works — especially *The Communist Manifesto* (1848) and *Capital* (1867) — inspired revolutions, founded political movements, and remain central to debates about inequality, labor, and economic systems.

## The Problem

Marx inherited a world being remade by industrial capitalism. Factories multiplied wealth while workers lived in squalor. Classical economists celebrated markets as natural and just; utopian socialists proposed communes and cooperative schemes. But no one had explained how capitalism actually worked — why it produced both abundance and misery, why crises recurred, why the system seemed both dynamic and inhuman. The question was structural: not whether individuals were greedy or kind, but what logic governed production itself. Previous critics saw injustice; Marx wanted to anatomize the system generating it.

## The Idea

Marx argued that capitalism is a historically specific mode of production driven by a fundamental contradiction: between social labor and private appropriation. Workers collectively produce value, but owners privately extract surplus value — the difference between what labor produces and what labor is paid. This isn't theft in the moral sense but the system's structural logic. The worker sells labor power as a commodity; the capitalist buys it, extracts more value than it costs, and accumulates capital. This process drives everything: technological change (machinery replaces workers to cut costs), recurring crises (overproduction and falling profit rates), and class struggle (workers and owners have irreconcilable interests). Capitalism is revolutionary — it destroys feudalism, globalizes production, generates immense wealth — but it contains its own contradictions. The concentration of capital impoverishes workers, who have nothing to lose but their chains. History moves through class struggle, and capitalism will be overthrown not by moral appeal but by its own internal tensions. Communism, for Marx, wasn't a utopian blueprint but the movement of the working class becoming conscious of its power.

## The Limits

Marx underestimated capitalism's adaptability. Unions, welfare states, and political reforms extended the system's life in ways his theory didn't predict. He expected immiseration to radicalize workers, but rising living standards in industrialized nations created reformist labor movements, not revolution. His theory of value — that labor alone creates worth — struggles to account for information, services, and financialization. He dismissed nationalism and religion as false consciousness, yet both outlasted class consciousness as mobilizing forces. Most devastatingly, every state calling itself Marxist produced authoritarianism. Marx envisioned the state withering away; his followers built gulags. Whether this betrayed or fulfilled his vision remains disputed, but the gap between theory and practice is undeniable. He analyzed capitalism brilliantly but barely sketched what would replace it, leaving a vacuum his successors filled with terror.

## A Moment

In 1849, expelled from Paris and Cologne, Marx arrived in London with his wife Jenny and their children, nearly destitute. For years they lived in two rooms in Soho, furniture seized for debt, children dying of malnutrition. Jenny, descended from aristocracy, pawned her last possessions. Marx wrote furiously, borrowing from Engels, dodging creditors, collapsing from boils and liver disease. His son Edgar died at age eight. Marx read economics for sixteen hours a day in the British Museum, constructing his critique of capitalism while trapped in its logic — brilliant analysis born from grinding poverty. When Jenny died in 1881, Marx was too sick to attend her funeral. He died fifteen months later, largely unknown. Eleven people came to his burial.
