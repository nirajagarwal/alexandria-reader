# Martin Heidegger

A German philosopher who asked what it means to exist, Martin Heidegger (1889–1976) reshaped twentieth-century thought with *Being and Time* (1927). His phenomenology of human existence — analyzing how we're already "thrown" into a world we never chose — influenced existentialism, hermeneutics, and postmodern theory. His thought remains inseparable from his Nazism: he joined the Party in 1933, served as rector under the regime, and never apologized. The silence implicates the philosophy.

## The Problem

Philosophy had spent millennia asking what beings *are* — what properties things possess, what categories they fall under — but had forgotten the prior question: what does it mean for anything *to be* at all? The question of Being itself had been buried under centuries of metaphysics that treated existence as self-evident. Heidegger saw modern life as defined by this forgetfulness: humans had become objects to be calculated, nature mere "standing reserve" for technological exploitation. Recovering the question of Being required examining the one entity that asks it — the human being, or what Heidegger called *Dasein* (being-there).

## The Idea

Heidegger's central insight: we don't first exist as isolated subjects who then relate to a world "out there." We're always already *in* the world, thrown into situations we didn't choose, absorbed in activities, using tools, caring about outcomes. Our fundamental mode isn't detached contemplation but engaged concern. A hammer isn't first an object with properties; it's equipment we use without thinking until it breaks. The world discloses itself through our involvement.

This "thrownness" includes temporality. We exist toward death — the one possibility that's absolutely our own and cannot be outrun. Authenticity means confronting this finitude rather than fleeing into the anonymous "they" (*das Man*), where we think, speak, and choose as everyone does. Most existence is inauthentic: absorbed in busyness, gossip, curiosity, evading the question of what our own existence means.

Late Heidegger shifted focus from individual existence to Being itself, approaching it through poetry and pre-Socratic fragments rather than systematic philosophy. Technology, he argued, had become our metaphysics — a way of revealing the world that reduces everything to calculable resource, including humans themselves. Liberation required a different stance: letting things be what they are rather than mastering them.

## The Limits

Heidegger's Nazism wasn't incidental. His critique of modernity, his nostalgia for authentic rootedness in soil and tradition, his contempt for liberal democracy and "Americanization" — these fed directly into his embrace of National Socialism as Germany's spiritual renewal. The *Black Notebooks*, published posthumously, reveal casual antisemitism woven into his philosophical reflections. He never recanted, never explained, maintained silence after 1945.

The philosophy itself carries authoritarian strains: the valorization of decision over deliberation, the dismissal of ethics as inauthentic moralism, the romantic elevation of fate and destiny over reason. His account of authenticity, for all its insight into human finitude, offers little ground for solidarity, justice, or criticism of power. The call to "let Being be" can justify quietism. And his readings of other thinkers — Nietzsche, the pre-Socratics — often imposed his own preoccupations onto the texts.

## A Moment

In 1933, newly installed as rector of Freiburg University, Heidegger delivered his inaugural address praising the Nazi revolution and called on students to serve the Volk. He enforced racial laws, removed Jewish colleagues, aligned the university with the regime. When his former teacher Edmund Husserl — Jewish, phenomenology's founder — died in 1938, Heidegger did not attend the funeral. After the war, he moved to a cabin in the Black Forest and granted few interviews. In 1966 he spoke to *Der Spiegel* on condition the interview be published only after his death. It appeared in 1976: still no apology, still insisting only "a god can save us" from technological nihilism.

## In Their Own Words

> "Dasein is an entity which does not just occur among other entities. Rather it is ontically distinguished by the fact that, in its very Being, that Being is an issue for it."

> "The Being of Dasein is care."

> "In anxiety one feels 'uncanny.' The indefiniteness of that which Dasein finds itself alongside in anxiety, comes together with the fact that what threatens is nowhere."

> "The woods have paths, mostly overgrown, that end abruptly where the wood is untrodden. They are called Holzwege. Each goes its separate way, though within the same forest. It often appears as if one is identical to another. But it only appears so."

> "Agriculture is now a motorized food industry, the same thing in its essence as the production of corpses in the gas chambers and the extermination camps, the same thing as blockades and the reduction of countries to famine, the same thing as the manufacture of hydrogen bombs."
