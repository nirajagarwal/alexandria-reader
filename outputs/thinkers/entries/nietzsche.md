# Friedrich Nietzsche

A German philologist turned philosopher (1844–1900), Nietzsche announced the death of God, diagnosed European nihilism, and reimagined human greatness beyond Christian morality. His vision of the Übermensch, eternal recurrence, and the will to power reshaped twentieth-century thought — inspiring existentialists, psychoanalysts, and postmodernists while being catastrophically misread by fascists. He wrote in aphorisms, parables, and lyrical outbursts, rejecting systematic philosophy for what he called "philosophizing with a hammer."

## The Problem

Christianity had won. Two millennia of ascetic ideals — self-denial, pity, humility, otherworldliness — had conquered European consciousness so thoroughly that even atheists remained Christian in their values. The Enlightenment had killed God but kept His morality. Meanwhile, science dissolved metaphysical certainties without offering new grounds for meaning. Nietzsche saw a coming catastrophe: nihilism, the devaluation of all values, when people realized their highest ideals were built on nothing. The question was whether Europe would collapse into passive nihilism or find the courage to create new values — not discovered in some transcendent realm, but made by human will.

## The Idea

Nietzsche's central project was the "revaluation of all values." He argued that what we call morality — the valorization of selflessness, pity, equality, the afterlife — was a historical invention, specifically a slave revolt led by the weak against aristocratic virtue. Ancient nobles valued strength, pride, joy in life; their "good" meant noble, powerful, beautiful. Christian morality inverted this: the meek shall inherit the earth, the last shall be first, suffering is holy. This "slave morality" was brilliant revenge by the powerless, transforming their weakness into virtue and the strong's vitality into sin.

But now God was dead — killed by our own honesty, our insistence on truth that finally turned against faith itself. Without God, Christian morality had no foundation. What remained? Nietzsche offered amor fati — love of fate, saying yes to existence exactly as it is — and the eternal recurrence: could you bear to live this exact life infinitely? The Übermensch (overman) would create meaning through self-overcoming, not by discovering cosmic purposes but by imposing form on chaos through artistic self-creation. Life was fundamentally will to power — not crude domination but the drive to expand, overcome resistance, become more than one is.

## The Limits

Nietzsche's psychology of ressentiment was often brilliant but sometimes merely diagnostic imperialism: disagree with him and you're weak, damaged, reactive. His celebration of rank and hierarchy, his contempt for democracy and socialism, his occasional misogyny — these weren't incidental flourishes but consequences of his thought. He correctly saw that not all equality is liberating, but his alternative — a vision of higher men creating values for the herd — courts authoritarianism even as he despised the state.

His madness (likely caused by a brain tumor, not syphilis as myth suggests) prevented him from developing or refining his ideas. We're left with fragments, contradictions, provocations. The eternal recurrence appears sometimes as cosmological doctrine, sometimes as psychological test, sometimes as metaphor — which did he mean? And his sister Elisabeth's editorial manipulations, fashioning him into a proto-Nazi, revealed how easily his contempt for weakness could be weaponized.

## A Moment

Turin, January 3, 1889. Nietzsche saw a coachman beating a horse in the Piazza Carlo Alberto. He threw his arms around the horse's neck, weeping, then collapsed. He never recovered lucidity. Friends found in his room the last notes — signed "Dionysus" or "The Crucified," identities merging at the edge of sanity. His sister retrieved him, controlled his legacy, falsified his archive, and presented him to the Nazis as their prophet. He died in 1900, eleven years gone from himself, while his books — ignored during his sane life — began remaking European thought.

## In Their Own Words

> "God is dead. God remains dead. And we have killed him. How shall we comfort ourselves, the murderers of all murderers? What was holiest and mightiest of all that the world has yet owned has bled to death under our knives: who will wipe this blood off us?"

> "My formula for greatness in a human being is amor fati: that one wants nothing to be different, not forward, not backward, not in all eternity. Not merely bear what is necessary, still less conceal it—all idealism is mendacity in the face of what is necessary—but love it."

> "He who fights with monsters might take care lest he thereby become a monster. And if you gaze for long into an abyss, the abyss gazes also into you."

> "What is done out of love always occurs beyond good and evil."

> "I am not a man, I am dynamite."
