# Nāgārjuna

An Indian Buddhist philosopher of the second or third century CE, Nāgārjuna founded the Madhyamaka ("Middle Way") school and transformed Mahāyāna Buddhism through his devastating analysis of essence and existence. Working within the Buddhist tradition yet pushing beyond earlier schools, he wielded logical argument as spiritual practice, demonstrating that language and thought inevitably entangle us in contradictions — and that recognizing this entanglement is itself liberation. His central text, the *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* (*Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way*), remains one of philosophy's most rigorous examinations of emptiness.

## The Problem

Early Buddhist schools had analyzed experience into fundamental constituents — dharmas, irreducible elements of reality bearing intrinsic natures. But this created a philosophical trap: if things possess inherent existence, how can they arise and cease, as Buddhist doctrine insisted? If they have fixed essences, how can they be impermanent? The Abhidharma tradition had replaced the substantial self with substantial dharmas, seemingly trading one form of essentialism for another. Nāgārjuna saw that the Buddha's teaching of dependent origination — that all phenomena arise through causes and conditions — was incompatible with any doctrine of intrinsic existence. Either things exist through themselves or through relations with others; they cannot do both.

## The Idea

Nāgārjuna's central insight is *śūnyatā* (emptiness): nothing whatsoever possesses intrinsic existence or essence. Not atoms, not minds, not even Buddhist dharmas. Everything that exists does so through dependence on causes, conditions, and conceptual designation. To exist is to exist relationally; independent existence is a contradiction in terms. But emptiness is not nihilism — it doesn't mean nothing exists. Rather, things exist conventionally, dependently, without ultimate ground. Nāgārjuna employed *prasaṅga*, reductio ad absurdum argument, to show that any claim about intrinsic nature collapses into incoherence. If fire possessed the intrinsic nature of burning, it would burn eternally and need no fuel. If the self were inherently existent, it couldn't change or act. His method was therapeutic: by demonstrating the impossibility of establishing any philosophical position absolutely, he freed practitioners from clinging to views. The "middle way" avoids both eternalism (things truly exist) and nihilism (nothing exists) — it reveals that these are false alternatives. Emptiness itself is empty: it's not a higher truth but medicine for the disease of reification. Once conceptual proliferation ceases, what remains is neither existence nor non-existence but the conventional world, newly understood.

## The Limits

Nāgārjuna's method risks rendering all distinctions meaningless. If every position collapses under analysis, what grounds ethics, practice, or even the Buddhist path? His defenders claim conventional truth suffices, but the relationship between emptiness and the conventional remains obscure. More problematically, his arguments sometimes slide between different senses of "existence" and "independence" without acknowledgment. When he refutes inherent existence, does he mean logical independence, causal independence, or conceptual independence? The ambiguity makes his arguments seem more devastating than they are. His relentless negation also risks becoming another form of dogmatism — the certainty that nothing can be certain. And while his philosophy liberated Buddhist thought from reification, it made phenomenal experience philosophically problematic: if everything is empty, how do ignorance and suffering arise in the first place?

## A Moment

According to tradition, Nāgārjuna was a Brahmin who mastered the Vedas before encountering Buddhism. As a young man, he and three friends acquired an invisibility potion and infiltrated the royal palace to seduce women of the harem. When guards began randomly striking the air with swords, his friends were killed. Nāgārjuna alone escaped — standing perfectly still, recognizing that movement creates form. The king offered him ordination as a Buddhist monk or death. Nāgārjuna chose the former. Whether historical or allegorical, the story captures his philosophy: conventional reality is dangerous when taken as ultimate, liberation comes through stillness rather than grasping, and wisdom sees through apparent solidity to dependent arising.

## In Their Own Words

> "Whatever is dependently co-arisen, that is explained to be emptiness. That, being a dependent designation, is itself the middle way."

> "There does not exist anything that is not dependently arisen. Therefore there does not exist anything that is not empty."

> "If I had any thesis, then I would have that fault. But I have no thesis, so I am faultless."

> "For whom emptiness is possible, everything is possible. For whom emptiness is not possible, nothing is possible."

> "The victorious ones have said that emptiness is the relinquishing of all views. Those who hold emptiness as a view are said to be incorrigible."
