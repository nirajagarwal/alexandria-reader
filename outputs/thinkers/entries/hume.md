# David Hume

Scottish philosopher David Hume (1711-1776) dismantled the foundations of human knowledge and emerged cheerful. Writing in the white heat of the Enlightenment, he applied radical empiricism to everything — causation, the self, morality, religion — and found habit where others had found certainty. His skepticism wasn't despair but liberation: if we can't prove what we believe, we're free to live without metaphysical delusions.

## The Problem

How do we know anything? Philosophers since Descartes had searched for secure foundations — truths that couldn't be doubted. But the more Hume examined human understanding, the less foundation he found. We believe the sun will rise tomorrow, that billiard balls cause motion when they collide, that we have continuous selves. But what justifies these beliefs? Not logic — effects don't follow necessarily from causes. Not the senses — they show us impressions, not necessary connections. If knowledge requires certainty, Hume realized, we're in trouble. The question became: what happens when you follow empiricism to its limit?

## The Idea

Hume's answer demolished traditional epistemology. All knowledge comes from experience — "impressions" of sensation and "ideas" derived from them. But experience never shows us causation, only correlation. We see one billiard ball strike another, then see the second move. We never see the "power" that makes the second ball move. Causation isn't observed; it's inferred from habit. After witnessing constant conjunction enough times, our minds expect the pattern to continue. Custom, not reason, grounds our beliefs about cause and effect.

The self fares no better. Introspect honestly, Hume argued, and you find no unified "I" — just a bundle of perceptions, one after another. Personal identity is a fiction we construct from memory. Even induction — reasoning from past to future — has no rational foundation. That the future will resemble the past is an assumption, not a proof.

Yet Hume didn't conclude we should doubt everything. Nature is stronger than philosophy. We can't help believing in causation, personal identity, and the external world. These beliefs arise from instinct, not reason — and that's fine. Philosophy's job is to describe how we actually think, not prescribe impossible standards.

## The Limits

Hume's skepticism, taken seriously, makes science incoherent. If induction is just habit, why trust physics over astrology? Hume says we can't help believing, but that dodges the question of justified belief. His demolition of causation troubled even his admirers — Kant said Hume woke him from his "dogmatic slumber" but spent the *Critique of Pure Reason* rebuilding what Hume had torn down. Hume also waved away his own skeptical arguments as impractical, retreating to "common life" when philosophy got too corrosive. This move looks like intellectual evasion. If your philosophy can't survive outside the study, something's wrong with the philosophy, not the world.

## A Moment

In his forties, Hume became librarian to the Faculty of Advocates in Edinburgh. The position paid £40 a year and gave him access to 30,000 volumes. He used the library to write his six-volume *History of England*, which became a bestseller and finally brought him the recognition his philosophical works hadn't. The man who argued we're bundles of impressions spent his final decade as a literary celebrity, hosting dinner parties and exchanging letters with admirers. When he was dying, his friends expected deathbed conversion. Hume disappointed them, dying as he'd lived — cheerful, skeptical, unafraid. Boswell visited him weeks before his death and left disturbed: the great infidel seemed genuinely untroubled by annihilation.

## In Their Own Words

> "Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions, and can never pretend to any other office than to serve and obey them."

> "If we take in our hand any volume; of divinity or school metaphysics, for instance; let us ask, Does it contain any abstract reasoning concerning quantity or number? No. Does it contain any experimental reasoning concerning matter of fact and existence? No. Commit it then to the flames: for it can contain nothing but sophistry and illusion."

> "When I enter most intimately into what I call myself, I always stumble on some particular perception or other, of heat or cold, light or shade, love or hatred, pain or pleasure. I never can catch myself at any time without a perception, and never can observe any thing but the perception."

> "Custom, then, is the great guide of human life. It is that principle alone which renders our experience useful to us, and makes us expect, for the future, a similar train of events with those which have appeared in the past."

> "Generally speaking, the errors in religion are dangerous; those in philosophy only ridiculous."
