# Frantz Fanon

Frantz Fanon (1925–1961) was a psychiatrist, revolutionary, and theorist of colonialism whose work examined the psychological violence of colonial domination and the necessity of decolonization. Born in Martinique, trained in French medicine and psychiatry, radicalized by his experience treating both colonizers and colonized in Algeria, Fanon wrote with clinical precision about how colonialism deforms human consciousness. His books — especially *Black Skin, White Masks* and *The Wretched of the Earth* — became foundational texts for anti-colonial movements worldwide and remain essential to understanding power, identity, and liberation.

## The Problem

Fanon confronted a question psychiatry couldn't answer: what happens to human psychology under colonial domination? He saw in his patients — both Algerian and French — patterns that exceeded individual pathology. The colonized internalized their own inferiority, adopting the colonizer's gaze, speaking the colonizer's language, dreaming the colonizer's dreams. The colonizer, maintaining dominance through violence, became psychologically dependent on the dehumanization required to justify it. Traditional psychiatry treated these symptoms as personal disorders; Fanon recognized them as structural. Colonialism was not just political or economic exploitation — it was an assault on consciousness itself. How could anyone achieve mental health within a system designed to fragment the self?

## The Idea

Fanon's central insight was that colonialism operates through a systematic destruction of the colonized person's humanity, replacing indigenous self-conception with an impossible aspiration toward whiteness. In *Black Skin, White Masks*, he analyzed how colonial subjects become alienated from themselves — how a Martinican child learns to see blackness as ugly, how speaking French becomes a claim to humanity, how assimilation demands the annihilation of one's own culture. This wasn't metaphor; it was observable psychiatric damage. The colonized person becomes split, performing an identity that can never be achieved, denied recognition as fully human.

In *The Wretched of the Earth*, written as Algeria fought for independence, Fanon examined decolonization itself. Liberation required more than political independence — it demanded the violent reconstruction of the colonized self. Violence, he argued controversially, had psychological necessity for the colonized: not as revenge but as the shattering of internalized submission. The colonized must become historical subjects again, agents rather than objects. Decolonization was "always a violent phenomenon" because it fundamentally reordered the human world, replacing the colonizer's manufactured reality with one where the formerly colonized claimed full humanity.

## The Limits

Fanon's celebration of revolutionary violence, while psychologically astute about colonial trauma, underestimated how violence becomes its own trap. Post-colonial states often reproduced authoritarian structures, with former liberators becoming new oppressors. His focus on colonial psychology, though groundbreaking, sometimes reduced complex political situations to psychological drama. His treatment of gender was limited — women appear in his work primarily as victims or symbols, rarely as agents of their own liberation. His universalizing tendency, born from French intellectual training, occasionally flattened the specific textures of different colonial experiences. And his early death meant he never had to reckon with the compromises, corruptions, and complications of actual post-colonial governance.

## A Moment

In 1953, Fanon resigned from his position as chief psychiatrist at Blida-Joinville psychiatric hospital in Algeria, writing to the colonial governor: "If psychiatry is the medical technique that aims to enable man no longer to be a stranger to his environment, I owe it to myself to affirm that the Arab, permanently an alien in his own country, lives in a state of absolute depersonalization." He'd tried to reform the hospital, introducing Algerian music, removing restraints, respecting Muslim patients' cultural practices. But he realized he was using psychiatry to make colonialism bearable, adjusting patients to an insane system. He joined the Algerian National Liberation Front, choosing revolution over treatment because the illness was colonialism itself.

## In Their Own Words

> "I came into the world imbued with the will to find a meaning in things, my spirit filled with the desire to attain to the source of the world, and then I found that I was an object in the midst of other objects."

> "Decolonization is the veritable creation of new men. But this creation owes nothing of its legitimacy to any supernatural power; the 'thing' which has been colonized becomes man during the same process by which it frees itself."

> "The Negro is not. Any more than the white man. Both must turn their backs on the inhuman voices which were those of their respective ancestors in order that authentic communication be possible."

> "I am not a prisoner of history. I should not seek there for the meaning of my destiny. I should constantly remind myself that the real leap consists in introducing invention into existence."

> "We revolt simply because, for many reasons, we can no longer breathe."
