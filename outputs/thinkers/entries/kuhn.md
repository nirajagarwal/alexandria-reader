# Thomas Kuhn

Thomas Kuhn (1922–1996) was an American physicist and historian of science whose *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) fundamentally altered how we understand scientific progress. Before Kuhn, most people assumed science advanced steadily through the accumulation of discoveries. Kuhn argued that science lurches through revolutions — that "normal science" operates within fixed frameworks until anomalies pile up and the framework collapses, replaced by an incommensurable new one. The book made "paradigm shift" a household phrase and forced both scientists and philosophers to reckon with science as a human, historical practice rather than a march toward objective truth.

## The Problem

How does science actually change? The standard story was cumulative: Newton added to Galileo, Einstein added to Newton, each generation building on what came before. But Kuhn, trained as a physicist and then studying historical cases, noticed something unsettling. When major theories changed, scientists didn't just add new facts — they stopped asking the old questions. Aristotelian physics didn't become Newtonian physics through discovery; it became *unintelligible*. Chemists abandoned phlogiston not because they found it but because they stopped thinking in terms that required it. Scientific revolutions didn't extend the edifice; they demolished and rebuilt it. If science progresses, Kuhn wondered, why does so much get discarded rather than incorporated?

## The Idea

Kuhn argued that science operates within paradigms — comprehensive frameworks that determine what counts as a problem, what methods are legitimate, and what solutions are acceptable. Normal science is puzzle-solving within a paradigm: chemists work out reaction rates, astronomers refine orbital calculations. Most science is normal science. But paradigms accumulate anomalies — persistent problems that resist solution. Eventually, anomalies multiply into crisis. A new framework emerges, often from younger scientists less invested in the old one, and a revolution occurs. The new paradigm isn't an extension of the old; it's *incommensurable* with it. Scientists in different paradigms see different worlds. They use the same words — "mass," "motion," "element" — but mean different things. Scientific change isn't linear accumulation but rupture and replacement.

This reframed everything. Science became recognizably historical and social. Paradigm choice couldn't be reduced to logic or observation alone; scientists persuaded each other through arguments that were partly aesthetic, partly pragmatic. "Truth" became suspect as the goal; what mattered was solving puzzles within a framework that worked. Kuhn didn't deny scientific progress, but he made it contingent, contextual, and stranger than the textbooks admitted.

## The Limits

Kuhn underestimated continuity. While paradigms do shift, much scientific knowledge survives revolutions — Newtonian mechanics still works for bridge-building even after relativity. His concept of incommensurability, taken literally, makes communication across paradigms impossible, yet scientists in fact argue across frameworks constantly. The sociology of paradigm choice — how scientists persuade each other — can sound like science is just politics by other means, though Kuhn didn't intend this. He struggled late in life to clarify: paradigms constrain but don't determine; revolutions disrupt but don't obliterate. His metaphors were so vivid they outran his nuance. The irony is that *Structure* became its own paradigm, complete with disciples who turned Kuhn's insights into dogma he had to spend decades qualifying.

## A Moment

In the preface to *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*, Kuhn described a summer in 1947 when he was asked to teach a course on historical cases in science. Preparing a lecture on Aristotelian physics, Kuhn found himself baffled. Aristotle, a genius, had written obvious nonsense about motion. Then, reading in the original Greek, something clicked. Aristotle wasn't asking Newtonian questions badly; he was asking different questions altogether. "I was sitting at my desk with the text of Aristotle's *Physics* open in front of me," Kuhn later recalled, and suddenly the text transformed. What had been absurd became coherent — not right, but *sensible within its own framework*. That experience of gestalt shift, of seeing the same words mean something wholly other, became the core of *Structure*. Kuhn spent his career trying to make that click happen for his readers.

## In Their Own Words

> "Normal science does not aim at novelties of fact or theory and, when successful, finds none."

> "The decision to reject one paradigm is always simultaneously the decision to accept another, and the judgment leading to that decision involves the comparison of both paradigms with nature and with each other."

> "Though the world does not change with a change of paradigm, the scientist afterward works in a different world."

> "In learning a paradigm the scientist acquires theory, methods, and standards together, usually in an inextricable mixture."

> "We may have to relinquish the notion, explicit or implicit, that changes of paradigm carry scientists and those who learn from them closer and closer to the truth."
