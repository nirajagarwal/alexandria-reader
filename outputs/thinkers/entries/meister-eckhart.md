# Meister Eckhart

A Dominican friar and preacher in late medieval Germany, Meister Eckhart (c. 1260-1328) taught a radical mysticism that collapsed the distance between God and the soul. His sermons in the vernacular to nuns and laypeople — not Latin treatises for scholars — argued that the ground of the soul and the ground of God are one. Condemned by papal authorities shortly before his death, his thinking sank into semi-obscurity for centuries before resurfacing as a source for German idealism, psychoanalysis, and contemporary spirituality. He remains Christianity's most paradoxical mystic: a scholastic who demolished scholasticism from within.

## The Problem

Medieval theology had made God distant. Thomas Aquinas and the scholastics constructed elaborate arguments for God's existence, mapped hierarchies of being, and analyzed divine attributes with Aristotelian precision. The result was philosophically sophisticated but experientially remote — God as supreme object of knowledge, not immediate presence. Meanwhile, mystical movements were flourishing across Europe, seeking direct encounter with the divine. Eckhart faced both: how to honor scholastic rigor while speaking to souls hungry for union with God. Could one dissolve the subject-object split at the heart of theology without lapsing into heresy? Could language point to an experience that transcends all concepts, including the concept of God?

## The Idea

Eckhart taught that the deepest ground of the soul — what he called the *Grunt* or *Seelengrund* — is identical with God. Not similar to God, not made in God's image, but *undifferentiated* from the divine ground. Union with God requires radical detachment (*Abgeschiedenheit*) — not suppressing desires but releasing all attachments, including attachment to salvation, virtue, even God as conceived. In this emptying, the soul discovers it has always been what it seeks. "The eye with which I see God is the same eye with which God sees me," he preached. God needs the soul as much as the soul needs God; neither exists fully without the other.

This required linguistic paradox. Eckhart spoke of the *Godhead* beyond God — the unknowable ground prior to the Trinity, prior to personality, prior to being itself. To reach this ground, one must "break through" God as object. His sermons used negation relentlessly: God is not good, not being, not one. Every affirmation limits; only unknowing opens. The birth of the Son in the soul happens now, eternally, or it is nothing. Time itself dissolves in the eternal present.

Unlike earlier mystics who described ascent to God, Eckhart taught discovery of what's already present. The journey is not up but in — or rather, the realization that in and up, soul and God, self and other are distinctions made by the intellect that must be unmade.

## The Limits

His radical language invited misunderstanding and warranted suspicion. By teaching that the soul's ground is God's ground, Eckhart risked obliterating precisely the Creator-creation distinction Christianity insisted upon. If the soul is divine, why bother with Christ, sacraments, or Church? His detachment from "God" as conceived could sound like atheism dressed in mystical garb. His claims about the soul's nobility — that it is "uncreated and uncreatable" — blurred lines medieval theology needed sharp.

He also wrote and preached in ways that made systematic interpretation nearly impossible. Contradictions abound; scholars still debate whether he held consistent positions or deliberately used paradox to shatter conceptual thinking. This elusiveness served his mystical purposes but left him vulnerable to charges of heresy. The papal commission condemned twenty-eight propositions from his work, though he died before formal sentencing. Whether he was a pantheist, a panentheist, or something Christian theology lacked vocabulary for remains contested. His silence on suffering, sin's grip, and incarnation's particularity — themes central to other Christian mystics — leaves gaps his system doesn't fill.

## A Moment

In the midst of the inquisition against him, already old and under papal investigation, Eckhart preached a final sermon in Cologne. He knew the authorities were compiling evidence, that his teachings were being extracted from context and listed as errors. He could have softened, clarified, retreated. Instead he told his congregation: "The authorities say that God is a being and a rational being at that. But I say that God is neither a being nor rational... God is without names, for no one can say or understand anything about God." Then he added, quietly, that if his words were wrong, he trusted God would forgive him for trying to speak truth. He died before the condemnation arrived, perhaps spared seeing his life's work officially rejected by the institution he'd served.

## In Their Own Words

> "God is not found in the soul by adding anything but by a process of subtraction."

> "The seed of God is in us. Given an intelligent and hard-working farmer, it will thrive and grow up to God, whose seed it is; and accordingly its fruits will be God-nature. Pear seeds grow into pear trees, nut seeds into nut trees, and God-seed into God."

> "Man's last and highest parting occurs when, for God's sake, he takes leave of God."

> "To be full of things is to be empty of God. To be empty of things is to be full of God."

> "If I am to know God directly, I must become completely God and God must become me."
