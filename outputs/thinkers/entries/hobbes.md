# Thomas Hobbes

English philosopher (1588–1679) who built modern political theory on the darkest premise: humans in their natural state are violent, selfish, and locked in perpetual war. His *Leviathan* (1651) argued that only absolute sovereign power could save us from ourselves. Writing amid England's civil war, Hobbes made fear the foundation of order and turned the social contract into a transaction of survival rather than justice.

## The Problem

Hobbes watched England tear itself apart. The Civil War (1642–1651) shattered the fiction that political authority was natural or God-given. When king and Parliament both claimed divine right, thousands died in the chaos. Hobbes saw the question beneath the bloodshed: What makes any government legitimate? Strip away custom, tradition, and theology — why should anyone obey anyone? Without a convincing answer, society collapses into the war of all against all. Hobbes needed to ground political obligation in something undeniable, something that didn't depend on contested scriptures or ancient privileges. He found it in fear.

## The Idea

In the state of nature — before government, before law — humans live in "continual fear and danger of violent death," and life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." This isn't because we're monsters but because we're rational. Resources are scarce; everyone wants security; no one can trust anyone else not to strike first. The result is perpetual war, not from malice but from logic.

The only escape is a covenant: everyone agrees simultaneously to surrender their natural liberty to an absolute sovereign — a person or assembly with total power to make and enforce law. This isn't tyranny but salvation. The sovereign's power must be absolute because any limitation creates a judge above the sovereign, which just restarts the war. We obey not from love or duty but rational self-interest: even the worst government is better than anarchy.

Hobbes severed political authority from divine right, ancient constitution, or natural hierarchy. Legitimacy comes from consent — not ongoing consent, but the original covenant that creates the sovereign. After that, we're bound. The Leviathan, that "Mortall God," keeps the peace by monopolizing violence and defining justice. There is no right against the sovereign because rights only exist where the sovereign's law creates them.

## The Limits

Hobbes solved the problem of legitimacy by making legitimacy almost meaningless. If any stable power deserves obedience, then successful tyranny is legitimate. His account offers no grounds to resist injustice short of immediate mortal danger. He dismissed religion as a source of civic chaos but left citizens no other vocabulary for transcendent claims against power.

His psychology is half-right: humans are often self-interested and fearful. But we're also capable of solidarity, reciprocity, and revolt for principle, not just survival. His state of nature may be a thought experiment, but he treated it as history, ignoring anthropology's evidence of cooperation without Leviathans.

Most tellingly, Hobbes couldn't explain why sovereigns themselves wouldn't abuse power. He assumed they'd act rationally to preserve the state, but history is thick with rulers who exploited, plundered, and destroyed for personal gain. Fear creates order, but it doesn't create justice or restraint.

## A Moment

In 1640, with civil war brewing, Hobbes fled England for Paris. He would stay eleven years. He tutored the exiled Prince Charles in mathematics while writing *Leviathan*, which argued for absolute monarchy but grounded it in consent rather than divine right. When Royalists read it, they were appalled — Hobbes had saved the king by killing kingship's sacred foundation. He fled Paris too when they threatened prosecution, returning to Cromwell's England and submitting to the new regime. Consistency: he'd always said you obey whoever holds the sword.

## In Their Own Words

> "Hereby it is manifest that during the time men live without a common power to keep them all in awe, they are in that condition which is called war; and such a war as is of every man against every man."

> "The condition of man is a condition of war of everyone against everyone, in which case everyone is governed by his own reason, and there is nothing he can make use of that may not be a help unto him in preserving his life against his enemies."

> "Covenants, without the sword, are but words and of no strength to secure a man at all."

> "The obligation of subjects to the sovereign is understood to last as long, and no longer, than the power lasteth by which he is able to protect them."

> "There is no such thing as perpetual tranquillity of mind while we live here; because life itself is but motion, and can never be without desire, nor without fear, no more than without sense."
