# Epictetus

A slave who became one of Rome's most influential philosophers, Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE) taught that freedom lies not in circumstances but in judgment. Lame from youth, possibly from mistreatment, he gained his freedom and established a school in Nicopolis after being exiled from Rome. He wrote nothing himself; his student Arrian recorded his teachings in the *Discourses* and the *Enchiridion*. His Stoicism stripped away metaphysical speculation to focus on a single question: what is actually up to us?

## The Problem

How do you remain free when you cannot control what happens to you? Epictetus knew bondage — literal slavery, physical disability, political exile. He watched people torment themselves over things beyond their power: the actions of others, the turn of events, the approach of death. They clung to what could be taken, raged at what couldn't be changed, and called themselves free while enslaved to circumstance. The philosophical systems of his time offered elaborate theories about the cosmos; Epictetus wanted to know what made a person genuinely unfree, and whether that condition was necessary.

## The Idea

Everything divides into two categories: what is up to us and what is not. Up to us: our judgments, desires, aversions, responses. Not up to us: our body, property, reputation, office, everything external. Freedom consists in desiring only what is actually within our power. When you want your body not to be lame, your reputation to be good, or your friends to act rightly, you've made yourself a slave to circumstance. When you want only to judge truly, respond virtuously, and assent wisely, nothing can stop you. The Stoic sage isn't someone to whom nothing bad happens; they're someone who cannot be harmed because they've withdrawn desire from everything external. This isn't resignation but a radical redefinition of what matters. Your child dies — that was never up to you. Your response, your integrity, your capacity to love what remains — that is. Epictetus collapsed ethics into a practice: examine every impression before assenting to it. Ask whether it concerns what's up to you. If not, respond: "You are nothing to me." Not callousness but precision about where your will actually operates. Freedom isn't getting what you want; it's wanting only what you can guarantee.

## The Limits

The dichotomy is too clean. Some things are partly up to us — our health through choices, our relationships through effort, our political conditions through action. Epictetus's framework risks passivity disguised as wisdom. If my reputation is "nothing to me," why speak truth to power? If my body is indifferent, why resist torture? He knew this tension — he urged students to fulfill social roles, to act in the world — but never adequately reconciled civic duty with the withdrawal of desire from outcomes. His psychology is also incomplete: desires don't simply dissolve when you recognize they concern externals. Grief, fear, longing — these aren't philosophical errors to be corrected by better judgment but human experiences that may warrant engagement rather than detachment. And his framework can shade into victim-blaming: if you're upset, you've judged wrongly; suffering is always self-inflicted through false beliefs. Some suffering isn't.

## A Moment

When his master twisted his leg, Epictetus said calmly, "You'll break it." The master continued. The leg broke. Epictetus said, "Didn't I tell you?" The story may be apocryphal — it appears in later sources, perhaps too perfect to be true. But it circulated because it captured something: the philosopher who wouldn't give his tormentor the satisfaction of fear. Whether historical or legendary, the scene shows what his teaching demanded. Not numbness but lucidity. Not freedom from pain but freedom within it. The body breaks; the will that judges the breaking need not. That distinction — between what happens to you and who you are in it — defined everything he taught.

## In Their Own Words

> "Some things are up to us and some are not up to us. Our opinions are up to us, and our impulses, desires, aversions — in short, whatever is our own doing. Our bodies are not up to us, nor are our possessions, our reputations, or our public offices, or, that is, whatever is not our own doing."

> "Don't demand that things happen as you wish, but wish that they happen as they do happen, and you will go on well."

> "It is not things themselves that disturb people, but their judgments about those things. For example, death is nothing terrible, or else it would have seemed so to Socrates; but the terror consists in our judgment that death is terrible."

> "If you want to make progress, put up with being thought foolish and stupid about externals. Don't wish to seem to know anything. And even if some people think you amount to something, distrust yourself."

> "You are a little soul carrying a corpse, as Epictetus used to say."
