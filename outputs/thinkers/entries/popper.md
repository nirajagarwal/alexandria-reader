# Karl Popper

Karl Popper (1902–1994) was an Austrian-British philosopher who transformed how we understand science, knowledge, and political freedom. In an age of totalitarian certainty — Nazism, Stalinism, psychoanalysis claiming irrefutable truth — Popper argued that genuine knowledge advances not through verification but through the attempt to prove ourselves wrong. His philosophy of falsification changed scientific method, and his defense of open society articulated why democracy depends on admitting we might be mistaken.

## The Problem

How do we distinguish science from pseudoscience? Popper confronted this in 1919 Vienna, watching Marxists, Freudians, and Adlerians claim their theories explained everything. Every event confirmed their frameworks — revolution proved Marx right, its absence proved him right differently; patients' agreement proved Freud right, their denial proved resistance. Einstein's relativity, by contrast, made risky predictions that could have been false. What separated genuine knowledge from unfalsifiable dogma? And how had the Enlightenment dream of reason produced the nightmare of twentieth-century ideology? Popper saw these questions as connected: societies claiming certain knowledge of history's laws become tyrannies.

## The Idea

Scientific theories advance not through confirmation but through attempted refutation. A theory is scientific only if it can be falsified — if it makes predictions specific enough to be proven wrong. "All swans are white" is scientific because finding one black swan would refute it. Marxism and psychoanalysis are pseudoscience because no observation could disprove them; they explain everything and therefore explain nothing. Knowledge grows through conjecture and criticism: propose bold theories, test them severely, eliminate errors, repeat. We never verify theories finally — we provisionally accept those that have survived serious attempts at refutation.

This transformed not just philosophy of science but politics. The open society, Popper argued, is one that institutionalizes fallibility. Democracy isn't rule by the wise or the people's will — it's the system for removing bad leaders without bloodshed. Totalitarianism begins with claims to possess scientific certainty about history or human nature. Utopianism is dangerous because it refuses to admit error. Freedom depends on recognizing that we might be wrong, that knowledge is provisional, that the question "Who should rule?" matters less than "How do we remove rulers peacefully?" The humble claim "I may be mistaken" is the foundation of both science and liberty.

## The Limits

Popper overstated his case. Scientists rarely discard theories after a single contrary observation — they adjust auxiliary hypotheses, question instruments, wait for more data. Falsification is cleaner in principle than in practice. His dismissal of much twentieth-century physics as "metaphysical" misjudged how metaphysical speculation guides research. His claim that Marxism and psychoanalysis are unfalsifiable mistakes complex research programs for monolithic dogmas — aspects of both have been tested and abandoned. And his political philosophy, while powerful against totalitarianism, offered little guidance for positive reform. "Piecemeal social engineering" is wise counsel, but insufficient for addressing structural injustice. His own fallibilism should have made him more charitable toward other traditions of thought.

## A Moment

Popper never held a regular academic position until age forty-five. He spent the 1930s teaching mathematics at a Vienna secondary school, writing The Logic of Scientific Discovery at night, watching colleagues flee or disappear. In 1937, with Austria collapsing toward Anschluss, he took a lectureship in New Zealand, as far as possible from Europe's center. There he wrote The Open Society and Its Enemies — a systematic critique of Plato, Hegel, and Marx as enemies of freedom — while Nazi Germany conquered the continent and Stalinist Russia claimed to represent reason's culmination. The book emerged from catastrophe: a defense of fallibility written in exile by someone who had witnessed what certainty becomes.

## In Their Own Words

> "The criterion of the scientific status of a theory is its falsifiability, or refutability, or testability."

> "We must plan for freedom, and not only for security, if for no other reason than that only freedom can make security secure."

> "True ignorance is not the absence of knowledge, but the refusal to acquire it."

> "The so-called paradox of freedom is the argument that freedom in the sense of absence of any constraining control must lead to very great restraint, since it makes the bully free to enslave the meek."

> "I may be wrong and you may be right, and by an effort, we may get nearer to the truth."
