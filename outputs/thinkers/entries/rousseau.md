# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

A Genevan watchmaker's son who never held an academic post, Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) became one of the Enlightenment's most influential and contradictory voices. His writings on political legitimacy, education, and human nature challenged both religious orthodoxy and rationalist confidence. He was celebrated and exiled, embraced by revolutionaries and condemned by philosophers. His vision of freedom through collective will shaped modern democracy — and justified its worst excesses.

## The Problem

Rousseau confronted a paradox: humans are born free, yet everywhere live in chains. Civilization promised progress but delivered corruption, inequality, and alienation. The sciences and arts refined manners while degrading morals. Private property created dependence; social hierarchy bred envy and domination. Enlightenment thinkers celebrated reason and refinement; Rousseau saw artifice and unfreedom. The question that obsessed him: how had humanity traded natural liberty for social bondage? Could legitimate political order restore freedom without abandoning society? Was there a way to be civilized without being corrupted?

## The Idea

Rousseau's answer revolutionized political thought: legitimate authority derives not from God, tradition, or force, but from the "general will" — the collective sovereignty of citizens united in common interest. In *The Social Contract*, he argued that freedom consists not in doing what you want but in obeying laws you give yourself. By entering civil society through a genuine social contract, individuals alienate all rights to the community and receive them back as citizens — transformed from subjects into sovereigns. This is freedom as self-legislation: being governed by laws you participate in creating through the general will.

But Rousseau's politics rested on a devastating anthropology. In his *Discourse on Inequality*, he traced corruption to property and comparison. Natural humans lived solitary, self-sufficient lives without pride or envy. Society awakened *amour-propre* — the need for recognition — making humans dependent on others' opinions. Inequality emerged with "This is mine," private property creating masters and slaves. Civilization refined this bondage without breaking it. Only a legitimate republic, where citizens subordinate particular interests to the common good, could reconcile freedom with social life. The general will cannot err because it seeks the good of all; those who resist it must be "forced to be free."

## The Limits

Rousseau's general will is conceptually slippery and politically dangerous. How do we distinguish the general will from the will of all, or from what a majority wants? Who determines the common good when citizens disagree — which is always? His answer — that the general will is always right because it seeks the collective interest — becomes circular or coercive. "Forced to be free" licensed revolutionary terror: if the general will is always legitimate, dissent becomes illegitimate. His idealization of ancient republics ignored their slavery and exclusion. His treatment of women was worse — he theorized education's liberation while insisting women exist for men's pleasure. He saw corruption everywhere but located redemption in a collective that erases difference. His anthropology of natural goodness couldn't explain why humans created the civilization he despised.

## A Moment

In 1762, *Emile* and *The Social Contract* were published. Both were immediately condemned and burned. A warrant was issued for Rousseau's arrest in Paris. He fled to Geneva, expecting refuge in his birthplace, only to find his books burned there too. The philosopher who preached civic virtue was rejected by every republic, welcomed only by monarchs he despised. He spent his final years in paranoid isolation, convinced former friends Diderot and Hume were conspiring against him. The man who wrote that humans are born free died stateless, fleeing from city to city, undone by the same social recognition he diagnosed as humanity's corruption. He haunted parks composing his *Confessions*, trying to explain himself to an ungrateful world.

## In Their Own Words

> "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains. One man thinks himself the master of others, but remains more of a slave than they are."

> "The first man who, having enclosed a piece of ground, bethought himself of saying 'This is mine,' and found people simple enough to believe him, was the real founder of civil society. From how many crimes, wars, and murders, from how many horrors and misfortunes might not any one have saved mankind, by pulling up the stakes, or filling up the ditch, and crying to his fellows: 'Beware of listening to this imposter.'"

> "In the social compact there is no real renunciation on the part of the individuals, and the position in which they find themselves as a result of the contract is really preferable to that in which they were before."

> "Whoever refuses to obey the general will shall be compelled to do so by the whole body. This means nothing less than that he will be forced to be free."

> "Everything is good as it leaves the hands of the Author of things; everything degenerates in the hands of man."
