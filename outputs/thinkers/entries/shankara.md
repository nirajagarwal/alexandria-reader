# Ādi Śaṅkara

An 8th-century Hindu philosopher from Kerala who traveled across India establishing monasteries and debating opponents, Śaṅkara systematized Advaita Vedānta — the philosophy of non-dualism. He argued that ultimate reality is one undivided consciousness, and that the apparent multiplicity of the world is an illusion born of ignorance. Through rigorous commentary on the Upaniṣads, Bhagavad Gītā, and Brahma Sūtras, he established a philosophical framework that dominated Hindu thought for centuries and offered a monistic alternative to Buddhist metaphysics.

## The Problem

How do we reconcile the teachings of the Upaniṣads — which describe Brahman as the one ultimate reality — with our everyday experience of a world filled with distinct objects, separate souls, and real suffering? Earlier interpreters had proposed various relationships between Brahman and the world: real transformation, partial identity, difference-in-identity. But these solutions seemed to compromise either the absolute nature of Brahman or the authority of the Upaniṣads themselves. Meanwhile, Buddhist philosophers were mounting sophisticated challenges to Hindu metaphysics, arguing that everything is empty of inherent existence. Indian philosophy needed a defender who could answer both internal tensions and external critics.

## The Idea

Śaṅkara argued for radical non-dualism: Brahman alone is real, and the individual self (ātman) is absolutely identical with Brahman — not similar to it, not a part of it, but utterly non-different from it. The apparent world of multiplicity, including our sense of individual selfhood, is māyā — not exactly illusion, but a beginningless superimposition, like mistaking a rope for a snake in dim light. The rope doesn't become a snake; the snake never existed; yet the fear was real until knowledge dispelled the error.

What we take as the world is Brahman misapprehended through ignorance (avidyā). Liberation comes not through action or devotion primarily, but through knowledge — specifically, the direct realization that "I am Brahman" (aham brahmāsmi). This isn't intellectual understanding but transformative insight that dissolves the fundamental error of taking yourself to be a limited, separate being. Śaṅkara defended this through subtle textual interpretation: passages describing creation are pedagogical, not literal; passages suggesting dualism are provisional, addressing students not yet ready for the highest teaching.

His method combined scriptural authority with rigorous logic. He showed how apparent contradictions in the Upaniṣads resolve when you distinguish between ultimate truth (paramārtha) and conventional truth (vyavahāra). At the conventional level, the world functions, karma operates, devotion matters. At the ultimate level, none of this ever truly existed apart from Brahman.

## The Limits

Śaṅkara's system faces a paradox it never fully resolves: if only Brahman is real, what is the status of māyā itself? It cannot be real (that would introduce duality) or unreal (then it wouldn't need to be overcome). His answer — that māyā is "neither real nor unreal" — satisfies as poetry but strains as metaphysics. Critics from other Hindu schools argued that Śaṅkara's position collapses into Buddhism's emptiness doctrine, merely replacing "everything is empty" with "only Brahman exists." His dismissal of the world as ultimately unreal seemed to undercut ethics and devotion, despite his insistence that these matter at the conventional level. Later Advaita thinkers spent centuries defending Śaṅkara against charges that his philosophy was "crypto-Buddhism" and couldn't account for the mechanics of illusion.

## A Moment

According to tradition, Śaṅkara encountered an untouchable on a narrow street in Varanasi. When asked to move aside, the outcaste replied: "Who should move — this body made of food, or the eternal ātman? You teach they are identical in all beings. What then is 'moving aside'?" Śaṅkara, recognizing his teacher in this challenge to his own social assumptions, prostrated before the man. The story may be legendary, but it captures the gap between Śaṅkara's metaphysical radicalism — all selves are one Self — and his acceptance of caste hierarchy in conventional life, a tension his followers would debate for centuries.

## In Their Own Words

> "Brahman is real, the world is illusory, the individual self is not different from Brahman — this is the conclusion of all Vedānta."

> "Just as one who has forgotten that he is wearing a necklace searches for it everywhere, so does the ignorant person seek the Self in the body, though the Self is his own nature."

> "The knowledge of Brahman cannot be attained by one who is without strength, or by one who is devoid of earnestness, or who lacks the indirect means of knowledge. But the man who strives by these means enters into the abode of Brahman."

> "As long as there is identification with the body, one cannot escape the experiences of pleasure and pain caused by natural tendencies, even if one has knowledge. But the man of realization, though appearing to experience these, remains unaffected like the sky by clouds."
