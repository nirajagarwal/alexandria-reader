# Susan Sontag

American essayist and cultural critic whose work from the 1960s through the 2000s dismantled received wisdom about art, illness, photography, and interpretation. Sontag (1933–2004) wrote against lazy thinking — the metaphors that obscure reality, the moralism that replaces looking, the presumption that art exists to be decoded. She defended difficulty, sensation, and the surface of things. Her essays became events: "Notes on 'Camp'" (1964) brought a subversive aesthetic into mainstream discourse; "Against Interpretation" (1966) challenged the academy's grip on meaning; *On Photography* (1977) and *Illness as Metaphor* (1978) changed how people understood images and disease.

### The Problem

How do we think about art, culture, and experience without reducing them to something else? By the 1960s, criticism had become an apparatus of translation: art meant something beyond itself, required expert decoding, existed to deliver messages. Middlebrow culture moralized everything — tuberculosis signified spiritual refinement, cancer meant repressed emotion, photographs captured truth. Avant-garde art was either ignored or domesticated through interpretation. Sontag saw thinking degraded into a search for hidden meanings, surface dismissed as superficial, sensation subordinated to significance. The urgent question: how to restore direct engagement with art and reality when criticism had become a barrier rather than an opening?

### The Idea

Sontag's central insight was that interpretation kills experience. "In place of a hermeneutics we need an erotics of art" — not what something means but how it works, what it does to perception. Art should intensify experience, not point beyond it. Photography doesn't capture reality; it aestheticizes it, creates distance, turns suffering into spectacle. We need to see this plainly rather than trust the image. Illness isn't metaphor — it's biology, and the metaphors (cancer as invasion, TB as consumption) harm patients by adding moral weight to physical suffering. Strip them away. Camp isn't trivial; it's a serious aesthetic that embraces artifice, sees style as substance, converts the earnest into play. Sontag defended difficulty in art because easy art demands nothing of us. She wrote against the American faith in simplification, the assumption that democratic culture means accessible culture. Some things should resist easy consumption. Her work was a sustained argument for paying attention — to surfaces, to form, to what's actually there rather than what we project onto it.

### The Limits

Sontag's opposition to interpretation sometimes became its own absolutism. Art does mean things; form and content aren't separable the way she sometimes implied. Her critiques could be more prescriptive than she acknowledged — telling people not to interpret is still telling them how to think. The erotics of art works better for visual arts and experimental literature than for political art or documentary photography, where meaning matters intensely. Her privileging of difficulty sometimes read as elitism: not everyone has the training, time, or cultural capital to approach "serious" art on her terms. Her dismissal of science fiction and popular forms showed the limits of her aesthetic openness. Later work on political violence and 9/11 revealed tensions between her formalism and her political commitments — she wanted art to resist instrumentalization but also to take moral stands. The stance against metaphor, while powerful for illness, can't work for all of culture; humans think through metaphor. Some interpretation enriches rather than reduces.

### A Moment

In 1975, Sontag traveled to Hanoi during the Vietnam War. She later wrote about watching a North Vietnamese film about the war — propaganda, didactic, aesthetically crude by her standards. She found herself moved. This created a problem: if she defended difficult, non-instrumental art, what could she say about effective political art that violated all her aesthetic principles? She never fully resolved it. In her journals, she wrestled with the tension between her formalist commitments and her leftist politics, between Camp's ironic distance and the earnest demands of moral witness. She wanted art to be free from politics and also wanted it to matter politically. The contradiction ran through her career. She kept thinking in public, kept revising, kept exposing the difficulty rather than smoothing it over.

### In Their Own Words

> "Interpretation is the revenge of the intellect upon art. Even more. It is the revenge of the intellect upon the world."

> "The camera makes everyone a tourist in other people's reality, and eventually in one's own."

> "Illness is not a metaphor, and the most truthful way of regarding illness — and the healthiest way of being ill — is one most purified of, most resistant to, metaphoric thinking."

> "Camp taste turns its back on the good-bad axis of ordinary aesthetic judgment. Camp doesn't reverse things. It doesn't argue that the good is bad, or the bad is good. What it does is to offer for art a different — a supplementary — set of standards."

> "The only interesting answers are those which destroy the questions."
