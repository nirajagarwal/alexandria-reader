# Adam Smith

Scottish moral philosopher and political economist, 1723–1790. Father of modern economics, theorist of the invisible hand, architect of free-market thinking. Best known for *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), but his first book, *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), explored how humans become ethical beings through sympathy and social approval. The two works form a unified project: understanding how order emerges from individual action without central direction, in both markets and morals.

## The Problem

How does complex social order arise without anyone designing it? Seventeenth-century thought had two answers: either God ordained it, or sovereigns imposed it. Neither satisfied Smith. He lived in a commercializing Scotland where strangers cooperated daily without force or divine command—bakers baked, brewers brewed, merchants traded, and somehow civilization held together. Meanwhile, moral philosophy debated whether humans were inherently selfish (Hobbes) or naturally sociable (Shaftesbury). Smith wanted to explain both economic coordination and moral behavior through the same principle: spontaneous order emerging from human nature interacting with social institutions.

## The Idea

Self-interest, when channeled through properly designed institutions, produces collective benefit without anyone intending it. In markets, the baker doesn't bake from benevolence but from self-interest—yet we get bread. Competition and property rights transform private vice into public virtue. The "invisible hand" guides resources to their most valued uses because profit signals what people actually want. Smith wasn't celebrating greed; he was describing a discovery mechanism. Markets aggregate dispersed knowledge that no planner could possess.

But—and this matters—markets require moral and legal foundations they cannot themselves create. *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* explains where these come from: through sympathy, humans internalize an "impartial spectator" that judges their actions. We want not just wealth but the approval of others, which requires behaving honorably. Justice (respecting others' rights) is the minimal moral foundation for commerce; beneficence (actively helping) goes beyond. The market requires the first, rewards the second, but depends on culture and law to maintain both.

Smith's third insight: specialization and trade, not natural resources, create wealth. The division of labor—one person making pins can produce 20 pins a day; ten people specializing in different steps produce 48,000—multiplies productivity spectacularly. Trade extends this principle across borders. Mercantilists thought wealth meant hoarding gold; Smith showed wealth means productive capacity, which grows through exchange.

## The Limits

Smith romanticized commercial society's civilizing effects while downplaying its costs. The division of labor that creates wealth also, he admitted, stupefies workers, confining them to "a few simple operations" that deform their minds. He noted this, then moved on. He underestimated how market forces could corrode the very moral sentiments he theorized—how commercial values might crowd out other forms of relationship.

His faith in harmony was overstated. Self-interest doesn't always align with collective good; market failures exist (monopolies, externalities, information asymmetries) that Smith glimpsed but couldn't systematically analyze. The invisible hand works under specific conditions, not universally.

He treated the commercial society he described as natural and universal rather than historically contingent. His stadial theory positioned Europe as advanced and other societies as primitive—a scaffolding for later imperial economics. And while he criticized colonialism's brutality, he never questioned whether European trade patterns themselves embodied domination.

## A Moment

Smith lived with his mother until her death when he was sixty-three, never married, and burned sixteen volumes of manuscripts on his deathbed, asking friends to destroy anything incomplete. The world lost whatever was there. But this fastidiousness shaped what survived: every published sentence had been tested through decades of lectures, revised through multiple editions, refined until it satisfied his exacting standard. When his mother died, six years before his own death, Smith was devastated. The architect of self-interested rationality had organized his entire adult life around duty, affection, and the judgment of one person whose approval he never stopped seeking.

## In Their Own Words

> "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest."

> "The man of system... seems to imagine that he can arrange the different members of a great society with as much ease as the hand arranges the different pieces upon a chess-board. He does not consider that the pieces upon the chess-board have no other principle of motion besides that which the hand impresses upon them; but that, in the great chess-board of human society, every single piece has a principle of motion of its own."

> "The real price of every thing, what every thing really costs to the man who wants to acquire it, is the toil and trouble of acquiring it."

> "To feel much for others and little for ourselves, that to restrain our selfish, and to indulge our benevolent affections, constitutes the perfection of human nature."

> "All for ourselves, and nothing for other people, seems, in every age of the world, to have been the vile maxim of the masters of mankind."
