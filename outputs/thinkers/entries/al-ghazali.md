# Al-Ghazālī

Abū Ḥāmid Muḥammad ibn Muḥammad al-Ghazālī (1058-1111) was a Persian theologian, jurist, philosopher, and mystic who reshaped Islamic thought at a moment of crisis. Teaching at Baghdad's Nizamiyya madrasa at the height of his intellectual powers, he suddenly abandoned his position, rejected philosophy's claim to truth, and embarked on a Sufi quest for certainty. His *Incoherence of the Philosophers* challenged rationalism's authority; his *Revival of the Religious Sciences* integrated law, theology, and mysticism into a coherent spirituality. He bridged Islam's exoteric and esoteric dimensions, making Sufism intellectually respectable while insisting that ultimate knowledge comes through direct experience, not argument.

## The Problem

Islamic civilization in the 11th century faced an intellectual crisis. Greek philosophy, transmitted through Arabic translations, promised rational certainty about God, the soul, and the cosmos. Philosophers like Avicenna claimed to demonstrate religious truths through logic alone, sometimes reaching conclusions that contradicted revelation. Meanwhile, legalistic Islam focused on external compliance; mystical Sufism pursued inner transformation but seemed to bypass law and orthodoxy. Could reason reach truth independently of revelation? Could spiritual experience be reconciled with legal obligation? Al-Ghazālī confronted a fractured tradition where philosophy, theology, law, and mysticism operated in separate spheres, each claiming authority but unable to speak to the others. He sought integration—and certainty.

## The Idea

Al-Ghazālī's central move was to distinguish between kinds of knowledge and their proper domains. In *The Incoherence of the Philosophers*, he systematically attacked philosophy's metaphysical claims—not all of reason, but reason's pretension to demonstrate what lies beyond experience. Philosophers couldn't prove the soul's immortality or God's knowledge of particulars through logic alone; causation itself, he argued, was mere habit, not necessity. Only God's will connects cause and effect. This wasn't anti-rationalism but delimiting reason's authority: logic works for mathematics and empirical observation, but metaphysical truth requires revelation.

Having cleared philosophy from theology's ground, he rebuilt religious knowledge on experience. *The Revival of the Religious Sciences* synthesized law, theology, and Sufism into a unified path. External practice (sharīʿa) was necessary but insufficient; it must be animated by inner transformation (ḥaqīqa). The heart's purification—achieved through Sufi practices of dhikr (remembrance), khalwa (retreat), and moral discipline—was not opposed to law but its completion. Knowledge of God comes through direct mystical experience (*dhawq*, "tasting"), not syllogism. He made Sufism orthodox and orthodoxy spiritual, insisting that the legal scholar who hadn't purified his heart understood only the surface of religion.

## The Limits

Al-Ghazālī's attack on philosophy devastated Islamic rationalism but may have foreclosed productive lines of inquiry. By denying necessary causation, he made nature radically contingent—God recreates the universe at every instant—which discouraged systematic natural science. His skepticism about reason's metaphysical reach was philosophically powerful but left no clear method for adjudicating theological disputes beyond authority and experience. His synthesis of law and mysticism privileged male scholarly elites who could afford years of study and retreat; ordinary believers and women were largely excluded from his path to certainty. His influence helped establish Ash'arite theology's dominance, narrowing the space for philosophical speculation in Sunni Islam. And despite his own spiritual crisis, he ultimately returned to teaching—perhaps the experiential certainty he sought remained elusive even for him.

## A Moment

In 1095, at age 37, Al-Ghazālī stood before his students at the Nizamiyya and found he could not speak. For months he had been paralyzed by doubt—how did he know anything he taught was true? His body rebelled: his tongue locked, his throat closed, he couldn't swallow. Physicians found no physical cause. He recognized it as spiritual crisis. He abandoned his prestigious position, distributed his wealth, left his family, and spent the next decade wandering as a Sufi seeker. The celebrated professor of Islamic law became a beggar seeking certainty. He found it not in another book but in practices of the heart—and when he returned to teaching, he taught differently. The breakdown wasn't failure but transformation. Certainty, he had learned, wasn't something you could argue your way into.

## In Their Own Words

> "The mystics do not acquire their knowledge by study but by ecstasy and by a change in their character. Their intoxication comes from their single concentration on God."

> "I knew with certainty that the Sufis were those who uniquely followed the way to God, that their manner of life was the best of all, their path the most direct of paths, and their character the purest."

> "Causation is not necessary. There is merely a habitual conjunction between what is customarily called cause and effect. God could, if He willed, create the effect without the cause."

> "Knowledge without action is madness, and action without knowledge is void."

> "The worldly sciences are useless and the religious sciences do not help unless there is action. Action is the fruit of knowledge, and knowledge without action is a tree that bears no fruit."
