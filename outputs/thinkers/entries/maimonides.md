# Moses Maimonides

Rabbi, physician, philosopher. Moses ben Maimon (1138–1204) lived through exile and upheaval — born in Córdoba under Muslim rule, forced to flee the Almohad invasions, eventually settling in Cairo as court physician. He became the preeminent Jewish legal authority of the medieval period and perhaps the greatest Jewish philosopher, attempting what seemed impossible: a rigorous synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and biblical faith. His work asked whether reason and revelation speak the same truth in different languages, or whether one must finally surrender to the other.

## The Problem

How can a rational person believe? By the twelfth century, Aristotelian philosophy — recovered through Arabic translation and commentary — presented a vision of the universe governed by natural law, knowable through reason. To educated Jews, this posed a crisis. Scripture speaks of God's anger, miracles that violate nature, anthropomorphic descriptions of the divine. Were these metaphors, mistakes, or truths that transcended philosophy? Could an intellectually serious person remain faithful to Torah while accepting Aristotle's cosmos? Maimonides refused the easy answers: that philosophy was Greek corruption, or that faith was primitive superstition. He believed both reason and revelation came from God, and therefore could not contradict.

## The Idea

God, Maimonides argued, is utterly unlike anything we can comprehend — no body, no passion, no change, no multiplicity. Every biblical description that suggests otherwise is metaphorical, accommodating human limitations. "God's hand" means divine power; "God's anger" means consequences flowing from natural law. This negative theology reaches its height: we cannot say what God is, only what God is not. God is not physical, not temporal, not composite. Even "exists" risks misunderstanding, since God's existence is of an entirely different order than anything in creation.

Philosophy and revelation, properly understood, converge. The universe is eternal or created — Maimonides remains agnostic, since Aristotle's proofs fail and Scripture's meaning is uncertain. What matters is that the cosmos operates through natural law, which is divine wisdom made manifest. Prophecy is not supernatural intervention but the perfection of human intellect and imagination, prepared through study and discipline. Miracles either follow hidden natural laws or were built into creation from the beginning. The commandments exist not as arbitrary divine decrees but as the optimal system for individual perfection and social order.

Yet Maimonides preserved mystery. Some truths are too profound for ordinary understanding; the masses need literal stories while philosophers grasp deeper meanings. The *Guide for the Perplexed* addresses this bifurcation directly — it is written esoterically, scattering its most radical ideas, protecting the simple faith of the unsophisticated while guiding the intellectually capable toward philosophical truth.

## The Limits

His rationalism has a coldness. Maimonides ranks human beings by intellectual achievement — the philosopher who grasps abstract truth stands higher than the merchant who merely follows law. Those incapable of philosophy must content themselves with literalism and obedience. This hierarchy, however much it reflects his Aristotelian framework, sits uncomfortably with Scripture's insistence that every person bears divine image.

His esoteric writing, while protecting orthodoxy, borders on dishonesty. If the simple believer's understanding is wrong but useful, what does this say about truth's relationship to virtue? And his confidence that philosophy and Torah must align assumes what needs proving. Later thinkers would argue he tortured Scripture to fit Aristotle, projecting philosophical conclusions onto texts that meant something quite different.

His negative theology, taken to its limit, makes God almost irrelevant to human life. If God is utterly unknowable and the universe runs by natural law, why pray? Why obey commandments? Maimonides answers, but the answer always threatens to dissolve into pure philosophy with Jewish vocabulary.

## A Moment

In Cairo, he served as physician to Saladin's vizier while leading the Jewish community and writing his philosophical masterwork at night. His daily schedule, recorded in a letter to his translator, is exhausting: treating patients from dawn, consulting until evening, collapsing into bed too tired to speak. "I can only teach Torah on the Sabbath," he wrote. The greatest Jewish intellect of his age, stealing hours for thought between medical rounds. His philosophy of human perfection through contemplation was earned in the margins of a physician's life — the gap between ideal and possible, woven into his days.
