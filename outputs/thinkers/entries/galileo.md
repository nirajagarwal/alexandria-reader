# Galileo Galilei

Galileo Galilei (1564-1642) was an Italian mathematician, astronomer, and physicist who transformed humanity's understanding of motion, observation, and the cosmos itself. He didn't invent the telescope, but he turned it skyward and saw what shattered the medieval picture of an unchanging, Earth-centered universe. More profoundly, he established that nature speaks in mathematics and that systematic observation trumps philosophical authority. His confrontation with the Catholic Church made him a martyr for science, but his real legacy is methodological: he showed how to read the book of nature.

## The Problem

For two thousand years, Aristotelian physics and Ptolemaic astronomy had explained the heavens and Earth through different principles. The cosmos was divided: perfect, unchanging celestial spheres above; corruptible, linear motion below. But this system was straining. Copernicus had proposed a sun-centered model, but it remained mathematical hypothesis, lacking physical proof or observational support. The telescope existed but was a curiosity. The deeper problem was epistemological: how do we actually know what's true about nature? By inherited authority and logical deduction? Or by looking closely and measuring carefully? Galileo faced a world where received wisdom still outweighed observation.

## The Idea

Galileo unified method and observation into a new way of knowing. He ground better lenses, pointed his telescope upward, and systematically recorded what he saw: mountains on the supposedly perfect moon, Jupiter's moons orbiting another planet, Venus showing phases like Earth's moon. These weren't interpretations—they were visible facts that demolished the Aristotelian cosmos. But observation alone wasn't enough. Galileo mathematized motion itself. Through careful experiments with inclined planes and pendulums, he formulated the law of falling bodies: objects accelerate uniformly regardless of weight, and motion continues unless impeded. This contradicted Aristotle's claim that force is needed to maintain motion. More radically, Galileo argued that mathematics is nature's language—physical reality is fundamentally quantitative, not qualitative. The book of nature, he wrote, "is written in the language of mathematics, and its characters are triangles, circles, and other geometric figures." He didn't just advocate for Copernicanism; he invented modern experimental physics: isolate variables, measure precisely, express relationships mathematically, test predictions. Truth comes from nature interrogated systematically, not texts interpreted cleverly.

## The Limits

Galileo's mathematics sometimes outran his evidence. His theory of tides—which he believed proved Earth's motion—was wrong; tides come from lunar gravity, which he rejected. He dismissed Kepler's elliptical orbits, clinging to circular perfection. His experiments with falling bodies were more thought experiment than systematic measurement; the apparatus didn't yet exist for the precision he claimed. He could be intellectually brutal, mocking opponents rather than persuading them, which made enemies unnecessarily. His confrontation with the Church was partly his own arrogance—he insisted on theological reinterpretation when tactical silence might have protected the work. And while he mathematized motion brilliantly, he never grasped momentum or inertia fully; that required Newton. He opened the door but stood in its threshold, still partly shaped by the medieval world he was demolishing.

## A Moment

In 1633, seventy years old and increasingly blind from decades of telescopic observation, Galileo knelt before the Inquisition in Rome. The *Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems* had gone too far—Copernican advocacy dressed as neutral comparison. Under threat of torture, he recanted: "I abjure, curse, and detest the aforesaid errors and heresies." Legend says he muttered afterward, "Eppur si muove"—"And yet it moves"—but he probably didn't; he was too shrewd and too frightened. He spent his final years under house arrest, blind, writing his greatest work on motion and materials. The Church could silence him. It couldn't make Jupiter's moons disappear.

## In Their Own Words

> "Philosophy is written in this grand book, the universe, which stands continually open to our gaze. But the book cannot be understood unless one first learns to comprehend the language and read the letters in which it is composed. It is written in the language of mathematics."

> "In questions of science, the authority of a thousand is not worth the humble reasoning of a single individual."

> "I do not feel obliged to believe that the same God who has endowed us with sense, reason, and intellect has intended us to forgo their use."

> "All truths are easy to understand once they are discovered; the point is to discover them."

> "Measure what is measurable, and make measurable what is not so."
