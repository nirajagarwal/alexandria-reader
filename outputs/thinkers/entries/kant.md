# Immanuel Kant

Immanuel Kant (1724-1804) never traveled more than forty miles from Königsberg, the Prussian city where he was born, taught, and died. Yet his three Critiques — of pure reason, practical reason, and judgment — traveled everywhere, restructuring how philosophy approached knowledge, ethics, and beauty. He asked what we can know before experience teaches us anything, what makes an action right regardless of consequences, and how subjective judgment can claim universal validity. His answers placed human reason at the center of reality while insisting on its limits.

### The Problem

Eighteenth-century philosophy was stuck. Rationalists like Leibniz claimed reason alone could discover truths about reality; empiricists like Hume insisted all knowledge came from experience and dissolved into habit and custom. Each side made the other impossible. Hume's skepticism was devastating — even causation became mere expectation — but rationalist metaphysics seemed to conjure knowledge from pure thought. Kant saw both were half-right and wholly wrong. The question became: how can we have genuine knowledge that goes beyond mere experience yet doesn't float free of it? What are the conditions that make experience possible in the first place?

### The Idea

Kant's answer was his "Copernican revolution": objects don't determine our knowledge; our knowledge determines objects. Space and time aren't features of reality itself but forms through which human minds structure experience. Categories like causation and substance aren't discovered in nature but are structures the mind imposes to make nature intelligible. We know certain truths necessarily — that every event has a cause, that objects persist through time — not because we've observed all events but because these are conditions of having coherent experience at all.

This solved both problems. Against rationalism: we can't know things-in-themselves, reality as it exists apart from human cognition. Metaphysics claiming knowledge of God, the soul's immortality, or the world's beginning commits reason to contradictions. Against empiricism: we have genuine a priori knowledge — knowledge independent of particular experiences — because experience itself is only possible through structures the mind brings to the encounter.

In ethics, Kant insisted morality couldn't rest on consequences or inclinations but only on reason. The categorical imperative commands: act only according to maxims you could will as universal law. Morality isn't about maximizing happiness but about respecting rational agency itself. To treat persons as means rather than ends violates what makes us moral beings. Duty, not desire, grounds ethics.

### The Limits

Kant's framework fractures reality. If we can't know things-in-themselves, only appearances structured by our minds, then we can't know whether our knowledge corresponds to reality at all. His confident pronouncements about the necessary structures of human cognition look provincial after two centuries of non-Euclidean geometry and quantum mechanics questioned whether space, time, and causation work as Kant insisted they must.

His ethics, despite its nobility, can be rigid to the point of cruelty. Kant notoriously argued lying is always wrong, even to murderers asking where their victim hides, because maximal universality matters more than particular lives. His respect for rational autonomy barely masked contempt for emotion, desire, and everything that makes humans human beyond their reasoning capacity.

His anthropology was racist, his views on women dismissive, his provinciality mistaken for universality. He treated European rationality as reason itself, his own cognitive structures as the structures of mind as such.

### A Moment

For forty years, Kant's daily walk through Königsberg was so regular that townspeople set their watches by it. Only once did he break routine — when Rousseau's *Émile* arrived and Kant couldn't stop reading. That moment of disruption matters: Rousseau taught Kant to respect "the common man," to see dignity in ordinary humanity rather than just philosophical elites. The thinker of pure reason absorbed something from the philosopher of feeling. Then he returned to his walk, his schedule, his room where he worked within sight of the prison tower, never seeing the sea.

### In Their Own Words

> "Two things fill the mind with ever new and increasing admiration and reverence, the more often and more steadily one reflects on them: the starry heavens above me and the moral law within me."

> "Enlightenment is man's emergence from his self-imposed immaturity. Immaturity is the inability to use one's understanding without guidance from another."

> "Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end."

> "Thoughts without content are empty, intuitions without concepts are blind."

> "We can know a priori of things only what we ourselves put into them."
