# Mary Wollstonecraft

English writer and philosopher who lived from 1759 to 1797. Best known for *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792), which argued that women's supposed intellectual inferiority stemmed from inadequate education, not nature. Writing during the ferment of revolutionary France, she extended Enlightenment principles of reason and equality to women — a population systematically excluded from such discourse. Her work founded modern feminism not as sentiment but as systematic critique of power.

## The Problem

Why were women treated as perpetual children? The revolutionary era proclaimed natural rights, human dignity, and rational citizenship — yet half of humanity remained confined to ornamental domesticity. Women were educated to please, not to think; trained in dependence, not autonomy. The exclusion was defended as natural, divinely ordained, biologically inevitable. Wollstonecraft saw this wasn't nature but construction: women were deliberately made weak, ignorant, and frivolous, then condemned for being weak, ignorant, and frivolous. The problem wasn't women's capacity but their systematic deprivation of the means to develop it.

## The Idea

Rational beings must be educated for reason. Wollstonecraft argued that women's apparent intellectual weakness was the effect, not cause, of their treatment. Denied serious education, discouraged from independent thought, praised for charm rather than sense, women were manufactured into the very creatures patriarchal society claimed they naturally were. She demanded education that developed judgment, not mere accomplishment; marriage partnerships based on companionship, not ownership; economic independence that made survival possible outside male protection. Her case wasn't sentimental — she despised fashionable femininity as much as male tyranny — but rigorously Enlightenment: humans become rational through education and exercise, and women are humans. Denying them education denies their humanity. She extended republican virtue to women, arguing they couldn't be proper mothers and citizens while kept ignorant and dependent. Reason required cultivation; cultivation required education; education was a right. The argument was radical not because it claimed women were different, but because it insisted they were fundamentally the same.

## The Limits

She couldn't escape class entirely. Wollstonecraft's feminism centered educated middle-class women; working women appear mainly as cautionary examples. Her contempt for feminine artifice sometimes became contempt for women who performed it — she judged harshly those who survived by the only means available. She also retained Enlightenment faith that reason would triumph over prejudice once demonstrated, underestimating how thoroughly power structures resist rational argument. Her vision of equality still operated within heterosexual marriage and motherhood as women's primary destination, reimagined but not questioned. She saw women needed economic independence but didn't fully imagine women's lives organized around something other than men.

## A Moment

She died at thirty-eight, eleven days after giving birth to her second daughter, from puerperal fever following the midwife's manual extraction of the placenta. Her husband William Godwin, grieving, published her letters and unfinished manuscripts with devastating honesty — revealing her suicide attempts, illegitimate first child, and unconventional relationships. He meant tribute; he destroyed her reputation for a century. The woman who demanded reason and dignity became a scandalous warning against female independence. Her daughter Mary would write *Frankenstein*, another story about creation, consequences, and what society does to those who transgress its boundaries. Only in the twentieth century was Wollstonecraft recovered as prophet rather than cautionary tale.

## In Their Own Words

> "I do not wish them to have power over men; but over themselves."

> "Taught from infancy that beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison."

> "Women are systematically degraded by receiving the trivial attentions which men think it manly to pay to the sex, when, in fact, men are insultingly supporting their own superiority."

> "Independence I have long considered as the grand blessing of life, the basis of every virtue; and independence I will ever secure by contracting my wants, though I were to live on a barren heath."

> "I do not believe that a private education can work the wonders which some sanguine writers have attributed to it. Men and women must be educated, in a great degree, by the opinions and manners of the society they live in."
