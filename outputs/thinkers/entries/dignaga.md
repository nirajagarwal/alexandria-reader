# Dignāga

Dignāga (c. 480–540 CE) was a Buddhist logician and philosopher who revolutionized Indian epistemology. Working in the Gupta period's intellectual ferment, he systematized Buddhist theories of knowledge and inference, creating a rigorous logical framework that shaped centuries of philosophical debate across Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain traditions. His *Pramāṇasamuccaya* (Compendium of Valid Cognition) established epistemology as a distinct discipline in Indian thought, making claims about reality inseparable from questions about how we know.

## The Problem

Buddhist philosophy faced a challenge: how to defend doctrines like impermanence and no-self against Hindu opponents while maintaining logical rigor. Earlier Buddhist thinkers had argued their positions but lacked a systematic account of valid knowledge itself. Meanwhile, the *Nyāya* school had developed sophisticated theories of inference and debate. Dignāga saw that Buddhism needed its own epistemological foundation — a theory of *pramāṇa* (valid means of knowledge) that could both ground Buddhist metaphysics and withstand dialectical scrutiny. Without this, Buddhist arguments remained vulnerable to the charge of incoherence or special pleading.

## The Idea

Dignāga argued that only two sources yield valid knowledge: perception (*pratyakṣa*) and inference (*anumāna*). Everything else — testimony, analogy, tradition — reduces to these. Perception is direct, non-conceptual awareness: pure sensory contact before the mind labels or categorizes. It grasps particulars (*svalakṣaṇa*) — momentary, unique instantiations that are ultimately real. Inference operates on universals (*sāmānyalakṣaṇa*) — mental constructions we use to organize experience. A valid inference requires three conditions: the logical mark must be present in the subject, present in similar cases, and absent in dissimilar cases. This "trirūpahetu" (three-charactered reason) made inference testable and precise.

Crucially, Dignāga argued that perception and inference have different objects. Perception touches reality directly but can't be communicated; inference operates through concepts but never reaches the particular. This creates an epistemological gap at the heart of knowledge: what we truly know, we can't say; what we say, we don't directly know. Language traffics in universals, constructed categories imposed on a flux of unique particulars. Yet inference remains valid within its domain, allowing us to navigate experience even if we can't capture reality's ultimate texture.

## The Limits

Dignāga's sharp division between perception and inference creates problems he couldn't fully resolve. If perception is entirely non-conceptual, how does it connect to thought at all? And if universals are mere constructions with no basis in reality, how does language ever successfully refer? His theory risks making knowledge impossible: if perception can't be expressed and inference doesn't touch reality, what grounds our claims? Later Buddhist thinkers like Dharmakīrti refined his system, but the perception-conception gap remained troubling. His dismissal of testimony as an independent source also sits uneasily with Buddhism's reliance on scripture and lineage. Most problematically, his account of momentariness — the doctrine that everything exists only for an instant — struggles to explain continuity, memory, and causation.

## A Moment

During a debate with Brahmin logicians, Dignāga was asked how he could trust his own perceptions if everything is momentary and there is no permanent self to perceive. He responded by turning the question back: show me this "self" that supposedly perceives. When his opponents offered arguments from scripture and authority, Dignāga rejected them outright — testimony was not an independent source of knowledge, only perception and inference counted. The Brahmins accused him of undermining all learning based on trusted sources, including the Buddha's own words. Dignāga did not flinch. If the Buddha's teachings were true, they must be verifiable through direct experience or sound reasoning, not accepted on authority alone. This was Buddhist epistemology's declaration of independence.

## In Their Own Words

> "Whatever is capable of performing a function is ultimately real; the rest is purely conceptual construction."

> "Perception is free from conceptual construction. Inference is characterized by conceptual construction."

> "A word does not express an individual, nor does it express a universal alone, nor both together. How then does a word express anything? This remains an inexplicable mystery."

> "The logical mark must be present in the subject under consideration, present in similar cases, and absent in all dissimilar cases. Without these three characteristics, inference fails."

> "Direct perception is awareness that arises from sense-object contact and is free from naming and conceptualization. It grasps only the particular, which exists as a unique point-instant."
