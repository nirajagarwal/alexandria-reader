# John Maynard Keynes

British economist, 1883-1946. Adviser to governments, architect of the Bretton Woods system, savior of capitalism through reinvention. Keynes argued that market economies don't naturally self-correct — that left alone during downturns, they spiral into prolonged unemployment and waste. His insight: governments must spend when everyone else stops spending. This idea restructured economic policy worldwide and made the modern welfare state intellectually respectable.

## The Problem

Classical economics promised equilibrium. Supply creates its own demand; unemployment corrects itself; markets clear. Then came the Great Depression — unemployment above 20 percent for years, factories idle, millions desperate, and no correction in sight. Orthodox economists counseled patience and budget balancing. Keynes saw this as nonsense. The problem wasn't workers pricing themselves out of jobs or temporary friction. It was insufficient demand throughout the entire economy. When everyone stops spending simultaneously — consumers, businesses, investors — who restarts the cycle? Classical theory had no answer. Keynes sought one.

## The Idea

Economies can get stuck. When confidence collapses, everyone hoards money. Consumers stop buying, so businesses stop investing, so workers lose jobs, so consumers stop buying — a vicious circle with no natural exit. Savings don't automatically become investment; they can simply vanish from circulation. The economy operates below capacity indefinitely.

The solution: government spending. When private actors won't spend, government must — not eventually, not after confidence returns, but immediately and massively. Run deficits during recessions; hire the unemployed; build infrastructure. This isn't charity but mechanical necessity. Government spending becomes someone's income, which becomes someone else's income — the multiplier effect. Demand returns, businesses invest, employment rises, the economy unsticks.

Conversely, during booms, governments should save — raise taxes, run surpluses, cool overheating. The idea wasn't perpetual deficits but countercyclical policy: lean against the wind. Markets are magnificent allocators but poor macroeconomic stabilizers. They need an external actor with different incentives — a government concerned with full employment rather than profit.

This reversed economic orthodoxy. Balanced budgets became foolish during crises. Unemployment became a policy choice, not an unfortunate necessity. Capitalism could be saved from itself.

## The Limits

Keynes underestimated inflation and overestimated government competence. His theory worked brilliantly for economies with slack — unemployed workers and idle factories. But governments discovered spending was politically easier than saving. Deficits ran during booms and busts alike. By the 1970s, Western economies faced stagflation — simultaneous unemployment and inflation — which Keynesian theory couldn't explain or fix.

He also dismissed supply-side constraints. If inflation rises not from excess demand but from oil shocks, productivity collapse, or demographic shifts, demand management fails. His framework was built for one problem — the Depression — and struggled with others. The multiplier effect proved smaller and more variable than expected, especially in open economies where spending leaked abroad.

Politically, Keynesianism enabled what he never intended: permanent expansion of government, entitlements without corresponding discipline, magical thinking about deficit spending. His nuanced countercyclical vision became "deficits don't matter."

## A Moment

Bretton Woods, 1944. Allied delegates designed the postwar economic order. Keynes, representing Britain, championed an ambitious vision: a global central bank, an international currency (the "bancor"), automatic mechanisms to penalize both surplus and deficit nations, preventing the imbalances that had collapsed the interwar economy. 

The Americans, led by Harry Dexter White, rejected it. America held the gold and the power; why surrender monetary sovereignty? Keynes's grander architecture gave way to the dollar-based system, which he knew was fragile. He signed anyway, exhausted and ill. When he died two years later, friends said Bretton Woods killed him — not the conference itself, but watching his vision reduced, knowing what instabilities remained.

## In Their Own Words

> "The ideas of economists and political philosophers, both when they are right and when they are wrong, are more powerful than is commonly understood. Indeed the world is ruled by little else."

> "In the long run we are all dead. Economists set themselves too easy, too useless a task if in tempestuous seasons they can only tell us that when the storm is long past the ocean is flat again."

> "The difficulty lies not so much in developing new ideas as in escaping from old ones."

> "Practical men who believe themselves to be quite exempt from any intellectual influence, are usually the slaves of some defunct economist."

> "It is better that a man should tyrannise over his bank balance than over his fellow-citizens."
