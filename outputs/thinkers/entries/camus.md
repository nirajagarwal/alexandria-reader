# Albert Camus

French-Algerian writer and philosopher (1913–1960), Camus articulated the experience of living in a world stripped of inherent meaning. Born into poverty in colonial Algeria, he became one of the most influential voices of mid-twentieth-century thought. He is remembered for confronting absurdity without surrender — for insisting that life without transcendent purpose could still be lived with dignity, revolt, and even joy.

## The Problem

How do you live when the universe offers no answers? After Nietzsche declared God dead, after two world wars shattered faith in progress, the question became urgent: if there is no cosmic meaning, no divine plan, no moral architecture built into reality — what then? Suicide? Despair? Camus saw people constructing elaborate fictions to avoid the question, retreating into ideologies that promised certainty. But he believed evasion was worse than confrontation. The problem was not whether life has meaning, but whether life without given meaning is worth living.

## The Idea

Camus named the condition: absurdity. Humans crave meaning, order, purpose. The universe offers none. Absurdity is not a property of the world or of consciousness but their collision — the divorce between our need for rational unity and the world's unreasonable silence. Most philosophy tries to resolve this tension through metaphysical systems, religious consolation, or revolutionary hope. Camus rejected every reconciliation as philosophical suicide, an evasion of the truth. His answer was revolt: conscious defiance of the absurd, living fully within it without false hope. The absurd person keeps the tension alive. Sisyphus, condemned to roll a boulder uphill forever, becomes the hero — not because the task has meaning, but because he embraces it. "One must imagine Sisyphus happy." This is not nihilism; it is the opposite. Meaning comes not from submission to cosmic order but from revolt against meaninglessness. Freedom, passion, and solidarity emerge not despite the absurd but because of it.

## The Limits

Camus wanted revolt without ideology, solidarity without dogma. But "revolt" proved harder to define than "absurd." His ethics rested on intuition more than argument — murder is wrong because it denies another's freedom to live absurdly, but why should that matter in an absurd universe? He rejected Marxist violence but offered no clear principle for when revolt justified force. His quarrel with Sartre over revolutionary terror exposed this: Camus condemned means that betrayed humane ends, but couldn't ground that condemnation in absurdist premises. His later turn toward "moderation" felt like retreat. And his blindness to colonial violence in Algeria — where he was born, where his mother lived — revealed how revolt could coexist with complicity. He saw European suffering clearly but struggled to see Algerian liberation as legitimate revolt.

## A Moment

January 4, 1960. Camus died instantly when his publisher's car struck a tree on a straight road in southern France. In his coat pocket was an unused train ticket; he had planned to take the train but accepted a ride at the last moment. In the wreckage, police found the manuscript of *The First Man*, his unfinished autobiographical novel about growing up poor in Algeria. He was forty-six. Sartre, estranged from him for years over politics, wrote that Camus's death was "an absurd death" — then caught himself, realizing the phrase was inadequate. Some deaths are simply absurd. Perhaps Camus would have appreciated that there was no lesson, no redemptive meaning, only the fact of it.

## In Their Own Words

> "There is but one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide. Judging whether life is or is not worth living amounts to answering the fundamental question of philosophy."

> "The absurd is the essential concept and the first truth."

> "I rebel — therefore we exist."

> "The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy."

> "In the depth of winter, I finally learned that within me there lay an invincible summer."
