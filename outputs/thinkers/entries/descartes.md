# René Descartes

French mathematician and philosopher (1596–1650) who rebuilt knowledge from the ground up. Developed analytic geometry. Argued that the mind is fundamentally distinct from matter. His method of systematic doubt and his insistence that philosophy must begin with certainty reshaped European thought and established the framework—both the promise and the prison—of modern philosophy.

## The Problem

Medieval philosophy had woven together Aristotle, Christian theology, and centuries of commentary into an elaborate system. But the new science—Galileo's physics, Copernican astronomy—was dissolving that synthesis. If the ancient authorities were wrong about the heavens and motion, what else had they gotten wrong? Descartes faced the question every thinker faces in an age of collapsing certainties: where can philosophy begin when all inherited truths are suspect? What foundation, if any, survives radical doubt?

## The Idea

Begin by doubting everything that can be doubted. Sense experience? Unreliable—dreams feel real while we're in them. Mathematical truths? An evil demon might be deceiving us about even arithmetic. But one thing resists doubt: the fact of doubting itself. "I think, therefore I am"—*cogito, ergo sum*. The thinking self is the unshakeable foundation. From this bedrock, Descartes reconstructs knowledge: God exists (because the idea of perfection must have a perfect source), and God wouldn't deceive us, so clear and distinct perceptions are trustworthy. Matter is pure extension—space that can be measured, divided, described geometrically. Mind is pure thinking—consciousness without physical properties. They're separate substances that somehow interact. This dualism solved one problem (how can immaterial souls exist in a mechanical universe?) while creating another (how do they touch?). His method—decompose problems into simple parts, reason from the certain to the complex—became the template for modern philosophy and science.

## The Limits

The mind-body split has proven harder to maintain than to declare. Descartes placed the soul's interaction with the body in the pineal gland, but never explained how something immaterial exerts force on matter. His proofs for God's existence convinced few even in his lifetime; without them, his system collapses into solipsism—nothing certain beyond your own thoughts. He dismissed animals as mere machines, incapable of genuine feeling, a conclusion most found repugnant then and absurd now. His method's demand for certainty narrowed what counts as knowledge, excluding most of human experience. And the cogito itself assumes what it claims to prove: if "I think" is certain, the "I" is already there. Doubting doesn't create a self; it presupposes one.

## A Moment

On the night of November 10, 1619, while stationed with the army in Bavaria, Descartes had three vivid dreams. In the last, he opened a book to find a line from Ausonius: "What path in life shall I follow?" He woke believing he'd been called to unify all knowledge through mathematics and reason. He spent the rest of his life pursuing that vision. Thirty years later, teaching philosophy to Queen Christina of Sweden in an unheated castle at five in the morning, he caught pneumonia. He died February 11, 1650. His final project had been a ballet.

## In Their Own Words

> I think, therefore I am.

> If you would be a real seeker after truth, it is necessary that at least once in your life you doubt, as far as possible, all things.

> It is not enough to have a good mind; the main thing is to use it well.

> The reading of all good books is like a conversation with the finest minds of past centuries.

> Each problem that I solved became a rule which served afterwards to solve other problems.
