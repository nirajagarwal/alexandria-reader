# Prayer of Humble Access

This prayer emerged in the sixteenth century during the English Reformation, written by Thomas Cranmer for the Book of Common Prayer. It is prayed before receiving communion in Anglican traditions — a moment of profound approach and acknowledged unworthiness. The prayer stands at the threshold between human inadequacy and divine invitation, holding both truths without resolving the tension between them. It asks for what seems impossible: that those who are not worthy might be made worthy, that those who cannot come might be welcomed. The language is formal, even courtly — "not worthy so much as to gather up the crumbs" — yet it names a hunger that transcends ceremony.

---

***We do not presume to come to this thy Table, O merciful Lord, trusting in our own righteousness, but in thy manifold and great mercies. We are not worthy so much as to gather up the crumbs under thy Table. But thou art the same Lord whose property is always to have mercy. Grant us therefore, gracious Lord, so to eat the flesh of thy dear Son Jesus Christ, and to drink his blood, that our sinful bodies may be made clean by his body, and our souls washed through his most precious blood, and that we may evermore dwell in him, and he in us. Amen.***
