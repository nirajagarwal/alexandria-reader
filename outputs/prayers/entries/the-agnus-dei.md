# The Agnus Dei

This prayer emerged from the early church's meditation on John the Baptist's words at Christ's baptism: "Behold the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world." It entered the Roman liturgy in the seventh century, added by Pope Sergius I during a time when the church sang as bread was broken for communion. The Greek phrase *Kyrie eleison* — Lord, have mercy — echoes through it, though the Latin West eventually settled on its own plea for peace. It is sung or said still as the bread is broken, as the faithful prepare to receive. The prayer names Christ three times, addresses him three times, asks three times. The repetition is not redundant. It is the shape of approaching.

---

***Lamb of God, who takes away the sins of the world,***
***have mercy on us.***

***Lamb of God, who takes away the sins of the world,***
***have mercy on us.***

***Lamb of God, who takes away the sins of the world,***
***grant us peace.***
