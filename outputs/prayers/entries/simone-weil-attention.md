# Attention as Prayer

Simone Weil, writing in occupied France during the Second World War, understood prayer not as petition but as orientation — a turning of the whole self toward truth. For Weil, who moved between Judaism, Christianity, and a solitude that belonged to neither, prayer was the practice of attention: absolute, patient, empty of agenda. She wrote that even academic study, when pursued with this quality of attention, becomes prayer. The mind that waits without grasping, that looks without seizing, that remains present without demanding — this, she believed, is the posture before what is sacred. "Attention," she wrote, "taken to its highest degree, is the same thing as prayer. It presupposes faith and love." This is not a prayer in the traditional sense, spoken or sung. It is a recognition that the quality of our looking is itself an act of devotion.

***Attention, taken to its highest degree, is the same thing as prayer.***

***It presupposes faith and love.***
