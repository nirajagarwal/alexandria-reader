# The Guest House

Jalāl al-Dīn Rūmī, the thirteenth-century Persian poet and Sufi mystic, wrote in a tradition that saw the soul as a dwelling place for the divine. His poems are teachings, invitations, prayers. This poem, translated by Coleman Barks, has become one of his most widely known in the West. It is prayed by those learning to meet what comes — joy, sorrow, the unexpected visitor — with welcome rather than resistance. The poem asks us to greet each moment as a guest sent from beyond, even when we do not understand why it has come.

---

***This being human is a guest house.***
***Every morning a new arrival.***

***A joy, a depression, a meanness,***
***some momentary awareness comes***
***as an unexpected visitor.***

***Welcome and entertain them all!***
***Even if they're a crowd of sorrows,***
***who violently sweep your house***
***empty of its furniture,***
***still, treat each guest honorably.***
***He may be clearing you out***
***for some new delight.***

***The dark thought, the shame, the malice,***
***meet them at the door laughing,***
***and invite them in.***

***Be grateful for whoever comes,***
***because each has been sent***
***as a guide from beyond.***
