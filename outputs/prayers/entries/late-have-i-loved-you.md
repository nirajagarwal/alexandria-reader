# Late Have I Loved You

Augustine of Hippo wrote this in his *Confessions*, composed around 397-400 CE as he reflected on his long wandering and late arrival at faith. It is not a formal liturgical prayer but an outpouring — addressed directly to God, written in the white heat of conversion remembered. Augustine had sought God in books, in philosophy, in pleasure, in ambition. He found God at last where God had always been: closer than his own breath, more interior than his innermost self. This passage is prayed by those who come late, who have looked everywhere else first, who recognize themselves in Augustine's long detour home.

---

***Late have I loved you, Beauty so ancient and so new, late have I loved you!***

***You were within me, but I was outside, and it was there that I searched for you.***
***In my unloveliness I plunged into the lovely things which you created.***

***You were with me, but I was not with you.***

***Created things kept me from you; yet if they had not been in you, they would not have been at all.***

***You called, you shouted, and you broke through my deafness.***
***You flashed, you shone, and you dispelled my blindness.***
***You breathed your fragrance on me; I drew in breath and now I pant for you.***
***I have tasted you, now I hunger and thirst for more.***
***You touched me, and I burned for your peace.***
