# The Wow! Signal

On August 15, 1977, at 11:16 PM Eastern Time, the Big Ear radio telescope at Ohio State University detected something that shouldn't exist according to our understanding of natural radio sources. For 72 seconds, a narrowband radio signal at 1420 MHz—the hydrogen line, a frequency that hydrogen atoms naturally emit and that interstellar civilizations might logically use for communication—peaked at more than thirty times the background noise. Astronomer Jerry Ehman, reviewing the computer printout days later, circled the alphanumeric sequence "6EQUJ5" that represented the signal's intensity and wrote in the margin: "Wow!"

That annotation gave the signal its name. It has never been detected again.

The Wow! Signal possessed several characteristics consistent with an artificial extraterrestrial transmission. Its frequency was precisely where the "water hole"—the quiet region of the electromagnetic spectrum between hydrogen and hydroxyl emissions—sits, the band SETI researchers consider most promising for interstellar communication. The signal's intensity rose and fell over the 72-second window exactly as expected from a fixed celestial source passing through Big Ear's beam as Earth rotated. It came from the direction of Sagittarius, near the Chi Sagittarii star group, roughly 2.5 degrees south of the ecliptic. And it was narrowband—far narrower than any known natural emission.

Immediately, the search for alternative explanations began. Could it have been terrestrial interference? The signal's characteristics argued against this—it matched the frequency drift expected from a non-terrestrial source, and Big Ear's design minimized interference through its double-horn configuration, which should have detected any Earth-based signal in both horns simultaneously. The Wow! Signal appeared in only one horn. Could it have been a satellite or aircraft? Analysis of satellite positions that night found none that could account for the signal. A passing aircraft would have produced different frequency characteristics.

The natural phenomena explanations prove equally unsatisfying. Comets can emit hydrogen line radiation, and in 2017, astronomer Antonio Paris suggested that comets 266P/Christensen and P/2008 Y2 (Gibbs) were in approximately the right position that night. But other researchers pointed out these comets weren't discovered until years after 1977, their positions weren't precisely aligned with the signal's origin, and comets emit continuously in broad hydrogen clouds—not in the narrow, intense burst Big Ear recorded. Pulsars and quasars emit at many frequencies, not the single narrowband frequency of Wow! Interstellar scintillation might intensify a signal, but not by the magnitude observed.

The most unsettling aspect: multiple follow-up observations of the same region, using more sensitive equipment, have detected nothing. If the source was a deliberate transmission, it was brief—perhaps a scanning beam that swept past Earth, or a one-time broadcast we happened to catch. If it was natural, it was a transient phenomenon we don't yet understand. The signal's uniqueness makes it scientifically frustrating. Science requires repeatability. One-time events, however compelling, resist analysis.

What haunts researchers is this: if the Wow! Signal was artificial, we may have detected evidence of extraterrestrial intelligence for 72 seconds and had no way to confirm it. We can't call back. We can't even prove the signal originated where it appeared to—could it have been gravitationally lensed from a source far more distant? We have one data point, one moment when something unexplained happened, and now silence.

Jerry Ehman himself remained agnostic. He thought it was probably natural, some phenomenon we don't yet understand. But he couldn't rule out the other possibility. The signal remains logged in SETI databases, a 72-second sequence that fits the profile of what we're looking for but refuses to reappear. It sits in the data like a half-heard word from the dark.
