# The Hum

Since at least the 1970s, a small percentage of people in locations worldwide have reported hearing a persistent low-frequency hum—continuous, maddening, and seemingly sourceless. The classic case is Taos, New Mexico, where in the early 1990s residents described a sound like a distant diesel engine idling, audible primarily indoors and at night. Congressional inquiries followed. Teams from the University of New Mexico, Sandia National Laboratories, and Phillips Laboratory deployed sophisticated acoustic equipment throughout the town. They found nothing. No measurable sound at the frequency described. Yet the hearers—roughly 2% of Taos residents—continued to suffer from sleeplessness, headaches, and the psychological strain of perceiving something invisible to instruments and inaudible to most neighbors.

The Taos Hum is not unique. Similar reports come from Bristol, England; Largs, Scotland; Bondi, Australia; Windsor, Ontario. The descriptions are remarkably consistent: low-frequency, often around 30-80 Hz, louder indoors, worse at night, accompanied by vibration or pressure in the ears. Sufferers are disproportionately middle-aged and older. Some report relief only when traveling away from their homes. A few have moved cities to escape it. The distress is genuine—depression, insomnia, even suicide attempts have been linked to the phenomenon.

Hypotheses proliferate. Industrial sources seem obvious—factories, power plants, ventilation systems—but acoustic surveys in affected areas typically find nothing anomalous in the claimed frequency range. Military installations and submarine communications have been proposed; extremely low frequency (ELF) radio waves can vibrate structures and potentially stimulate the inner ear directly. Yet ELF transmitters are rare and geographically specific, while Hum reports are widespread. Geological explanations—microseisms, tectonic activity—are appealing but don't explain why only some people hear it or why it's worse indoors where geological vibrations should be dampened.

The most parsimonious explanation is otoacoustic—the sound originates not in the environment but in the ear itself. Spontaneous otoacoustic emissions, sounds generated by the inner ear's hair cells, are common and usually inaudible to the person producing them. But some individuals, particularly as they age, may become aware of internal sounds—tinnitus being the most familiar example. The catch: tinnitus sufferers hear the sound everywhere, while Hum hearers consistently report that it stops or diminishes in certain locations. Some can eliminate it by moving to a different room. This suggests an external source, even if not one detectable by standard equipment.

There's also the possibility of a real but elusive environmental source operating at the threshold of human perception. The human ear can detect sounds below what microphones register, especially if the sound is delivered through bone conduction or vibration rather than air pressure alone. Infrasound from wind turbines, traffic, ocean waves, or industrial processes could theoretically be amplified by local geography or building resonances. A 2004 study by acoustician Geoff Leventhall found that some Hum hearers had heightened sensitivity to low frequencies—they could detect tones that others couldn't. This doesn't locate the source but suggests the perception is not pure hallucination.

What remains is a phenomenon with consistent phenomenology across geography and time, genuine suffering among those who experience it, and no consensus explanation despite decades of investigation. The Hum sits at the intersection of measurable reality and subjective experience. It may be environmental, physiological, or both—a real low-frequency sound that only certain ears can detect, or an otoacoustic phenomenon triggered by environmental factors we haven't yet identified. What it reveals is how much remains unknown at the edges of human perception, and how profoundly isolating it is to sense something that instruments cannot measure and neighbors cannot hear.
