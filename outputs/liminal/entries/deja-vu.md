# Déjà Vu

You round a corner into a room you've never entered, and the world stutters. The placement of furniture, the quality of light, the position of people mid-conversation—all of it feels not just familiar but *remembered*. For three seconds, maybe five, you're certain you've lived this exact moment before. Then the sensation dissolves, leaving only the unsettling residue of impossible recognition.

Déjà vu affects roughly two-thirds of people at least once in their lives, more frequently in the young and those with higher education. The term itself—French for "already seen"—was coined by psychical researcher Émile Boirac in 1876, though the experience appears in accounts reaching back to Augustine. Its universality across cultures and eras suggests something fundamental about how human consciousness constructs the experience of time.

The neurological explanations are multiple and not mutually exclusive. One theory proposes a brief misfiring in the brain's memory circuits—the hippocampus and rhinal cortex, responsible for memory encoding and recognition, fire out of sync. The present moment gets tagged as a memory before it's fully processed as perception. You experience familiarity without the actual prior experience to justify it. Supporting this: déjà vu occurs more frequently in people with temporal lobe epilepsy, and temporal lobe stimulation during surgery can trigger the sensation on demand.

Another hypothesis suggests déjà vu results from subliminal processing. You glimpse a scene peripherally or in fragmentary form before you fully attend to it. When you focus moments later, the brain has already begun encoding it, creating a false sense of prior encounter. Laboratory experiments have induced déjà vu-like sensations by showing subjects images too briefly for conscious recognition, then presenting them again fully visible. The subjects report strange familiarity without knowing why.

A third explanation involves attention lapses. Your brain processes a scene, attention wavers microscopically, then returns to the same input. The gap—milliseconds only—is enough to create two separate encoding events that feel like present and past. The sensation of repetition is real; what's false is the assumption that the first encoding happened in the distant rather than immediate past.

Yet these explanations, while compelling, don't capture the phenomenology completely. People report not just familiarity but often a sense of narrative continuation, as though they're remembering how the next few seconds will unfold. Some experience it as prophetic—confirmation that this moment was glimpsed in a dream or premonition. The feeling isn't merely "I've seen this," but "I've *been* here, in exactly this configuration of reality."

Philosopher Henri Bergson proposed in 1908 that déjà vu reveals something about how consciousness relates to time. Rather than memory malfunction, he suggested we're briefly perceiving how memory forms in real-time—watching the present being filed away as past while still experiencing it as present. The doubling isn't error but unusual access to a process normally hidden from conscious awareness.

Parapsychologists have pointed to cases where the déjà vu feeling preceded accurate predictions of subsequent events—people who knew what would be said next, or how a scene would unfold. These anecdotal accounts are difficult to verify and easily explained by confirmation bias: we remember the hits, forget the misses. But they persist in the literature of anomalous experience.

What déjà vu definitively demonstrates is that our sense of time as linear and our memories as stable are constructs, not givens. The sensation reveals the machinery normally concealed—how recognition works, how the brain timestamps experience, how thin the membrane is between now and then. Whether it's a glitch in temporal processing, a glimpse behind the curtain of consciousness, or something stranger, déjà vu reminds us that our experience of being in time is neither simple nor entirely reliable. The past can bleed into the present, the new can feel ancient, and for a few disorienting seconds, we're uncertain which moments belong to memory and which to now.
