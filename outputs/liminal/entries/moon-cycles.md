# Lunar Cycles and Behavior

For millennia, humans have attributed strange behavior to the full moon. Emergency room staff brace for chaos. Police expect more calls. The word "lunacy" itself derives from luna, the moon. Nurses will tell you, with absolute conviction, that full moon shifts are different—more births, more bleeding, more psychiatric crises. Yet when researchers examine hospital records, police reports, and birth certificates, the pattern dissolves. Study after study finds no correlation between lunar phase and human behavior. So why does the belief persist so stubbornly among those who work night shifts in hospitals and precincts?

The moon's gravitational pull creates ocean tides—this is physics, not folklore. Since human bodies are roughly sixty percent water, the reasoning goes, the moon must affect us too. But the math doesn't support it. The tidal force exerted by the moon on a human body is negligible, far weaker than the gravitational pull from nearby furniture. A mother holding her infant exerts more gravitational influence on the child than the full moon does. If tidal forces explained behavior, we'd act stranger around large buildings than during lunar phases.

Yet the anecdotal evidence is overwhelming. A 1995 survey found that forty-three percent of mental health professionals believed the full moon affects behavior. Emergency room doctors report the same pattern: full moon nights feel different. But when University of Saskatchewan researchers examined over 150 studies on lunar effects, they found that positive results couldn't be replicated. In a 2019 analysis of psychiatric emergency department visits across six years, no lunar correlation appeared. The Swiss Journal of Psychology reviewed three decades of research and concluded: "No evidence exists for the moon's influence on human behavior."

So what explains the persistent belief? Confirmation bias is the obvious culprit. We notice and remember events that confirm our expectations—the full moon night when the ER was chaotic—and forget the equally chaotic nights when the moon was new or half. Add in illusory correlation: we're evolutionarily primed to detect patterns even where none exist. The full moon is highly visible and culturally significant, so it becomes a convenient peg on which to hang memorable events.

But there's a more interesting possibility: the belief might be self-fulfilling. If you expect the full moon to bring trouble, you're primed to interpret ambiguous situations as problematic. Medical staff working under that expectation might approach patients differently, notice patterns they'd otherwise miss, or recall the shift as more intense than it actually was. The phenomenon becomes real through collective attention, even if the underlying lunar influence isn't.

Some researchers haven't given up. Chronobiologist Martin Röösli noted that while major effects are debunked, subtle influences on sleep architecture appear in some studies. A 2013 paper in Current Biology found sleep duration decreased by twenty minutes during full moons, though replication attempts have been mixed. Whether this represents genuine lunar influence or statistical noise in small samples remains unclear.

The moon affects ocean tides, animal reproduction cycles, coral spawning, and possibly plant growth through light exposure. It's not unreasonable to suspect we're influenced too. But humans are complex systems with internal clocks, artificial light, and cultural conditioning that may swamp any weak lunar signal. Perhaps we once responded more directly to lunar cycles, before electric lights and climate-controlled environments disrupted our connection to natural rhythms.

What persists is the feeling that the full moon matters—and that feeling itself is data. Not evidence of gravitational effects, but evidence that humans need stories to make sense of chaos. The full moon provides narrative structure for the inexplicable intensity of certain nights. Whether or not it's physically real, it's psychologically useful. The lunacy isn't in the moon's pull. It's in our need to believe we can predict the unpredictable.
