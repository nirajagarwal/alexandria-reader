# Confirmation Bias

We see what we expect to see. A believer in UFOs scans the night sky and spots an unusual light—proof of extraterrestrial visitation. A skeptic sees the same light and immediately thinks aircraft or satellite. Both are responding to identical stimulus, but their prior beliefs shape what they perceive. This is confirmation bias: the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms what we already think, while discounting evidence that contradicts it.

The phenomenon is not a character flaw but a feature of cognition. Our brains are prediction machines, constantly generating hypotheses about reality and testing them against incoming data. This is efficient—we can't evaluate every perception from scratch. But it creates a feedback loop. Once we form a belief, we unconsciously filter evidence through it. Studies show that people presented with mixed evidence on controversial topics—gun control, climate change, vaccine safety—become *more* polarized, not less. Each side finds support for their view in the same data set.

Peter Wason demonstrated this elegantly in 1960 with his 2-4-6 task. Participants were told that this sequence followed a rule and asked to generate other sequences to discover it. Most formed a hypothesis—"ascending even numbers"—and then tested only sequences that confirmed it: 8-10-12, 14-16-18. They announced confidence in their answer. The actual rule was simply "ascending numbers." By testing only confirming cases, they never discovered they were wrong. The few who succeeded were those who actively sought disconfirmation: 1-2-3, 5-4-3, 7-15-200.

Confirmation bias doesn't just shape abstract beliefs—it alters memory itself. Elizabeth Loftus's research on eyewitness testimony showed that leading questions after an event can change what people recall seeing. Witnesses who were asked about "the broken headlight" later remembered a broken headlight that never existed. Their memory conformed to the suggestion. In paranormal investigation, this is devastating. A researcher who believes in ghosts may subtly prime witnesses with expectant questions, then cite their "independent" testimony as corroboration.

The bias operates in scientific research too, despite methodological safeguards. Researchers who believe in a hypothesis are more likely to notice results supporting it, to continue experiments until they get the "right" outcome, to interpret ambiguous data favorably, and to remember confirming trials more vividly than disconfirming ones. This is why pre-registration of studies and blinding protocols exist—to prevent unconscious bias from contaminating results. The replication crisis in psychology has revealed how often positive findings disappear when researcher expectations are controlled.

What makes confirmation bias insidious is that it feels like objectivity. We don't experience ourselves as cherry-picking evidence. The supportive data simply seems more credible, more relevant, more memorable. Contradictory evidence feels flawed or irrelevant—easy to dismiss. A believer encountering a failed psychic prediction explains it away: the psychic was off that day, or skeptical energy interfered, or the prediction was metaphorical. A skeptic encountering an accurate prediction dismisses it: cold reading, lucky guess, post-hoc interpretation. Both feel they are thinking clearly.

The phenomenon reveals something unsettling about knowledge itself. We imagine forming beliefs by objectively weighing evidence, but often we form beliefs first—through culture, emotion, identity, random exposure—and then marshal evidence to support them. The process is backwards. This doesn't mean truth is impossible, but it suggests that reaching it requires actively fighting our cognitive defaults. We must seek disconfirmation, not confirmation. Steel-man opposing views. Notice when evidence *should* change our minds but doesn't.

The irony is that recognizing confirmation bias doesn't immunize us against it. Knowing about it may make us better at spotting it in others while remaining blind to it in ourselves—a meta-level confirmation bias. The solution isn't perfect objectivity, which may be neurologically impossible, but intellectual humility: holding beliefs provisionally, privileging disconfirming evidence, and remembering that certainty often signals bias rather than truth. In the liminal space between belief and skepticism, productive doubt becomes a tool for seeing more clearly.
