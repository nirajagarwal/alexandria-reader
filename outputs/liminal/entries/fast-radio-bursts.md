# Fast Radio Bursts

In 2007, astronomer Duncan Lorimer was digging through archival data from the Parkes radio telescope in Australia when he found something that shouldn't exist: a pulse of radio energy lasting just five milliseconds, bright enough to briefly outshine every star in the galaxy it came from. The signal had traveled 3 billion light-years to reach Earth, which meant the source released as much energy in a few thousandths of a second as the Sun emits in a month. Then it vanished.

For years, Lorimer's burst stood alone—a cosmic anomaly that could have been instrumental error, terrestrial interference, even a microwave oven in the facility kitchen (a real problem at Parkes, it turned out). But as telescopes improved and astronomers knew what to look for, more appeared. Dozens, then hundreds. They came from random directions across the sky. Most happened once and never repeated. A few sources flashed again, irregularly, unpredictably.

The evidence is unambiguous: Fast Radio Bursts are real, extragalactic, and genuinely bizarre. They originate from cosmological distances—billions of light-years away—yet release extraordinary energy in milliseconds. The radio waves are dispersed in ways consistent with traveling through intergalactic plasma, ruling out local sources. Advanced telescopes have pinpointed a handful to specific galaxies, even to particular regions within those galaxies. One repeating source was traced to a magnetar—a neutron star with a magnetic field a quadrillion times stronger than Earth's—which currently leads as the best natural explanation.

But magnetars don't fully solve the mystery. Most FRBs don't repeat, suggesting different mechanisms or sources. Some bursts show patterns—periodicity, clustering, correlations with other cosmic events—that magnetar models struggle to explain. The energetics remain staggering. A magnetar "starquake" or magnetic reconnection event could theoretically produce such a burst, but the physics pushes known processes to their limits. And new observations keep complicating the picture: some bursts arrive with strange polarization, others show multiple peaks, some come from unexpected galaxy types.

The mystery attracts speculation. Could FRBs be signals from advanced civilizations—perhaps energy beams from massive power sources designed for interstellar travel or communication? The idea isn't as fringe as it sounds. The Breakthrough Listen project, backed by serious astronomers, includes FRBs in its SETI observations. If you wanted to send an attention-getting signal across galactic distances, a brief, intense radio pulse would work. The counterargument: we've detected thousands of bursts, from hundreds of locations, with no discernible information content. Signals, perhaps, but noise to us. Or just physics.

What makes FRBs liminal is not that they're paranormal—they're thoroughly natural phenomena, whatever their source—but that they expose how much of the universe operates beyond our current understanding. We've been listening to the cosmos with radio telescopes for decades, confident we understood the inventory of possible sources. Then these showed up. They're too energetic for ordinary processes, too brief for most exotic ones, too common to be freakish accidents.

The phenomenon reveals something about knowledge itself: that consensus reality contains more mystery than we admit. Somewhere out there, billions of light-years away, something is releasing unfathomable energy in eyeblinks. We can detect it, measure it, catalog it. We can even guess at causes. But we don't really know. The universe keeps more secrets than we give it credit for, and sometimes those secrets announce themselves louder than anything else in the sky.
