# Gary Schwartz

Gary Schwartz's laboratory at the University of Arizona tests mediums under controlled conditions—readings given with no prior information, statements recorded and scored for accuracy, statistical analysis applied to results that consistently exceed chance. A Harvard-trained psychologist who once studied psychophysiology and biofeedback, Schwartz now investigates whether consciousness survives death. His career trajectory illustrates what happens when a credentialed scientist applies rigorous methods to a forbidden question.

The experiments follow a protocol designed to eliminate fraud and cold reading. The medium sits in one room, the bereaved client in another. No visual or auditory contact. The medium provides specific statements—names, causes of death, personality traits, shared memories. After the session, transcripts are scored by blinded raters who determine which statements match which deceased person. Meta-analysis of Schwartz's studies shows hit rates significantly above chance, with some mediums achieving accuracy that would occur randomly less than one time in millions.

Critics focus on methodology. They note that even "blinded" raters might unconsciously match readings to the most obvious deceased person. They point out that vague statements ("I'm getting something about heart trouble") apply to many elderly deaths. They argue that selection bias—only successful mediums get tested, only impressive results get published—inflates the apparent effect. The Windbridge Research Center, which employs similar protocols, has produced comparable results, but skeptics maintain that all medium studies suffer from the same fundamental flaws: the impossibility of truly blind conditions when the reading contains identifying details, and the human tendency to find meaning in ambiguous information.

Schwartz addresses these concerns methodically. Later studies use even stricter controls: proxy sitters who know nothing about the deceased, readings given for people not yet chosen, accuracy compared against readings for wrong deceased persons. The more controls he adds, the more the effect persists. Either mediums have genuine access to information they shouldn't possess, or the constellation of cognitive biases required to produce these results is so specific and consistent that it demands its own explanation.

The professional cost has been predictable. Schwartz holds an academic position and continues to publish, but he exists outside mainstream psychology. His work appears in parapsychology journals, not in the *American Psychologist*. He's invited to consciousness conferences but not to American Psychological Association symposia. The implicit message: you can investigate consciousness surviving death, but you forfeit your place in the conversation about consciousness generally.

What Schwartz demonstrates—whether or not his interpretation is correct—is that after-death communication can be studied experimentally. Consciousness, survival, and mediumship aren't inherently beyond scientific investigation; they're simply topics that most scientists refuse to investigate. The taboo isn't methodological but ontological. Mainstream psychology has accepted that consciousness emerges from brain activity and will not seriously entertain alternatives, regardless of data.

If Schwartz is wrong, the explanation matters. How are mediums consistently providing accurate information under controlled conditions? Telepathy with the living? Exceptional cold reading that defeats experimental controls? Statistical artifacts not yet identified? Any of these would be fascinating in its own right. If he's correct, the implications are more profound: consciousness is not produced by the brain but mediated by it, survival is not wishful thinking but observable fact, and the materialist paradigm requires fundamental revision.

The mystery isn't whether Gary Schwartz is credentialed—his training and position are impeccable. The mystery is why his research generates controversy rather than curiosity. Science is supposed to follow evidence wherever it leads, but some destinations are off limits.
