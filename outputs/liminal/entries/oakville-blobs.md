# The Oakville Blobs

On August 7, 1994, during a rainstorm in Oakville, Washington, something other than water fell from the sky. Residents reported translucent, gelatinous blobs the size of rice grains covering an area of about twenty square miles. The blobs were sticky, difficult to remove, and according to multiple accounts, made people sick. Dotty Hearn, who touched the substance, developed severe flu-like symptoms and was hospitalized. Her daughter, also exposed, fell ill. Others reported dizziness, nausea, and respiratory problems. The family cat died after coming into contact with the material.

Samples were sent to the Washington State Department of Health. Microbiologist Mike McDowell found human white blood cells in the substance. A second analysis confirmed the presence of cells but couldn't definitively identify them as human. The blobs contained bacteria—specifically, two types commonly found in the digestive tract. Beyond that, the tests raised more questions than answers. What was this organic, cellular material? Why was it falling from the sky? And why did it seem to make people sick?

The explanations proposed were each stranger than the last. One theory suggested military aircraft had jettisoned human waste, which dispersed into gelatinous rain. But no flights were confirmed in the area during the storm, and the material didn't match the expected composition of waste tank contents. Another hypothesis involved jellyfish or other marine organisms sucked up by waterspouts and deposited inland. Oakville is sixty miles from the Pacific, and jellyfish tissue doesn't typically contain human white blood cells or digestive bacteria. Some suggested the blobs were residue from illegal dumping, but the wide dispersal pattern and timing with the storm argued against it.

The most persistent theory involved military bombing runs over the ocean. The Navy occasionally conducts exercises off the Washington coast, and the theory proposed that explosions pulverized jellyfish or marine biomass into an aerosol that formed gelatinous rain. It's mechanically plausible—blast waves can atomize organic matter, and wind patterns could theoretically carry material inland. But the presence of human-associated cells and bacteria remains difficult to reconcile. And while the Navy confirmed exercises in the region, they occurred days before the rainfall, not during it.

The Oakville blobs have precedent. Star jelly—variously called astromyxin, astral jelly, or pwdre sêr in Welsh folklore—has been reported for centuries. A gelatinous substance appearing after meteor showers or unexplained events, sometimes described as the remnants of fallen stars. Modern accounts typically identify star jelly as slime mold, frog spawn, or regurgitated material from birds. But the Oakville case involved laboratory analysis showing cellular structure and bacteria. It wasn't misidentified frog eggs.

What makes Oakville compelling is the documentation. We have hospital records, lab results, and multiple independent witnesses reporting the same phenomenon. This wasn't mass hysteria or folklore. Something gelatinous and organic fell from the sky over a twenty-square-mile area. People touched it and became ill. A cat died. The substance contained cells and bacteria. These are facts without a consensus explanation.

Perhaps most unsettling is how quickly the case faded from investigation. After initial tests, no further analysis was pursued. Samples disappeared or degraded. The Department of Health offered no final report. The residents of Oakville were left with an experience that defied explanation and a lingering sense that something profoundly strange had happened—something no one seemed willing or able to explain.

The blobs haven't returned. Oakville moved on. But the questions remain suspended in the same liminal space as the substance itself—neither fully explained nor entirely mysterious, documented but unresolved, disturbing precisely because it happened and we still don't know why.
