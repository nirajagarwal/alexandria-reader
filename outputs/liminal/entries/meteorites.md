# Meteorites

For most of human history, rocks falling from the sky were considered absurd. Peasants might claim to have seen stones plummet from clear air, but learned men knew better. The heavens were perfect and unchanging. Stones belonged to the earth. The idea that the two domains could intersect—that celestial matter could crash into terrestrial mud—violated the cosmic order Aristotle had described and the Church had sanctified.

The resistance wasn't mere superstition. It was rigorous, scientific skepticism. When a meteor shower pelted the French town of L'Aigle in April 1803, dropping thousands of stones across the countryside, the French Academy of Sciences dispatched physicist Jean-Baptiste Biot to investigate. Local witnesses described rocks falling from a cloud during a thunderstorm. They produced fragments, still warm. Biot documented impact craters, analyzed the stones' unusual composition—high in nickel, unlike terrestrial rocks—and interviewed dozens of credible witnesses whose accounts aligned.

His report forced a paradigm shift. The Academy, which had spent decades dismissing meteorite claims as folklore or fraud, accepted that stones did indeed fall from space. Thomas Jefferson, hearing the news, allegedly quipped that he would sooner believe two Yankee professors would lie than that stones fell from heaven—but even Jefferson's skepticism couldn't withstand the weight of evidence.

What made meteorites so hard to accept? Partly, it was prior probability. Rocks falling from the sky violated everything natural philosophy understood about celestial mechanics. No mechanism explained how stones could exist in space, much less rain down on earth. The few specimens brought to museums—like the 56-pound Ensisheim meteorite that fell in Alsace in 1492—were dismissed as lightning-fused terrestrial rocks or outright hoaxes. Scientists had theories; peasants had stories. Theories won.

But there was a deeper problem: meteorite falls were witnessed mainly by farmers, laborers, and provincials—people whose testimony the educated elite discounted. When stones fell in Lucé, France in 1768, the local municipality swore affidavits. A dozen witnesses described the fireball and the smoking rock it left behind. Antoine Lavoisier, father of modern chemistry, investigated and concluded the stone had been struck by lightning. The possibility that uneducated villagers might observe something natural philosophers had missed was apparently more absurd than the alternative explanations.

The irony is that ancient peoples took meteorites seriously. They were worshipped as gifts from the gods, forged into sacred blades, enshrined in temples. The Kaaba in Mecca contains a black stone Muslims believe fell from heaven. Indigenous Australians and Native Americans incorporated meteorites into creation myths. Pre-scientific cultures, lacking theories about celestial perfection, simply accepted what they observed: sometimes, rocks fall from the sky.

Modern meteorite science has revealed how right the ancients were—and how much more there is to the story. We now know Earth is constantly bombarded by extraterrestrial material, most of it vaporizing in the atmosphere. The few fragments that survive carry information about the solar system's formation, the composition of asteroids, even the possibility of panspermia—life seeded across the cosmos on cosmic debris. Meteorites aren't just rocks. They're time capsules from the birth of worlds.

What meteorites teach us isn't just astronomy. It's epistemology. For two centuries, the scientific establishment denied a phenomenon because it contradicted theory, because witnesses lacked credentials, because the implications were unsettling. They weren't being unscientific—they were being *too* scientific, trusting models over observation, authority over experience. It took overwhelming evidence and institutional courage to admit the impossible was real.

The lesson endures: certainty is expensive. What seems absurd today—because it violates our theories, because the wrong people report it, because accepting it would overturn too much—may tomorrow be obvious. The sky, it turns out, does fall. Sometimes you just have to wait for it to hit a French village before anyone believes you.
