# Schrödinger's Cat

In 1935, physicist Erwin Schrödinger proposed a thought experiment that has haunted physics classrooms and pop culture ever since: a cat sealed in a box with a flask of poison, a radioactive atom, and a Geiger counter. If the atom decays, the counter triggers, the flask shatters, the cat dies. According to quantum mechanics, until we open the box and observe, the atom exists in superposition—simultaneously decayed and not decayed. Which means, Schrödinger argued with barely concealed horror, the cat must be both alive and dead.

He meant it as a reductio ad absurdum. Quantum superposition works fine for electrons and photons, but apply it to a living cat and the absurdity becomes undeniable. The Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics held that particles exist in all possible states until measurement collapses the wavefunction into one outcome. Schrödinger was pointing out that this makes no sense at the macro scale. You can't have half-dead cats.

Except quantum mechanics kept working. Every prediction panned out. Superposition, entanglement, wave-particle duality—all confirmed experimentally. The math was unassailable. The philosophy remained nightmarish.

The standard resolution is decoherence: the cat, the box, the air molecules—these interact with the environment so constantly that superposition collapses nearly instantaneously. You don't need a conscious observer. The universe observes itself. By the time we open the box, the cat is definitively alive or dead, and has been for microseconds. Crisis averted.

But this just pushes the mystery back a step. At what point does superposition collapse? A single photon can be in two places at once. An electron can spin both up and down. Experiments have shown superposition in molecules of hundreds of atoms. In 2010, researchers put a tiny mechanical resonator—visible to the naked eye—into quantum superposition. Where exactly does the quantum world end and the classical world begin?

Some interpretations deny there's a boundary at all. The Many-Worlds interpretation says the wavefunction never collapses—every quantum possibility happens, each in its own branching universe. Open the box and you don't determine whether the cat is alive or dead; you determine which universe you're in. There's another you, opening another box, finding the other outcome. Both cats exist. Both yous exist. The superposition never resolves—it just splits reality.

Other interpretations give consciousness a role. The von Neumann–Wigner interpretation suggests observation by a conscious mind is what collapses the wavefunction. Physicist Eugene Wigner extended this: if consciousness causes collapse, then the cat, being conscious, would collapse its own wavefunction. There'd be no superposition. Unless consciousness requires a certain neural complexity? Unless cats aren't conscious enough?

Or perhaps, as QBism proposes, quantum states aren't properties of the world but states of our knowledge. The wavefunction represents what we know, not what is. Before you open the box, your knowledge allows for both possibilities. Opening it updates your information. Nothing physical "collapses"—you just learn something new.

The strangest part: this isn't idle speculation. This is accepted physics, confirmed daily in laboratories worldwide. Quantum computers exploit superposition. Cryptography relies on entanglement. These phenomena are technologically harnessed even as they philosophically baffle us.

Schrödinger wanted to show that quantum mechanics, taken seriously, leads to nonsense. Instead, he revealed how little we understand about observation, measurement, consciousness, and reality itself. The cat isn't the thought experiment's subject—we are. The real question isn't whether the cat is alive or dead before we look. It's whether we understand what "before we look" even means.

The box remains sealed on that question. And perhaps it always will.
