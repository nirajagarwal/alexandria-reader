# The File Drawer Problem

In 1979, psychologist Robert Rosenthal calculated that for every published study showing telepathy worked, there would need to be forty-six unpublished studies showing it didn't—languishing in file drawers—to explain away the positive results as mere statistical noise. He called this the "file drawer problem," and while he was making a point about parapsychology, he'd identified something far more troubling: publication bias affects all of science, not just the fringe.

The mechanism is simple. Journals prefer novel, positive findings. "We tested this drug and it worked" gets published. "We tested this drug and nothing happened" goes in a drawer. Graduate students need publications to graduate. Researchers need publications to get tenure. Pharmaceutical companies need positive results to sell drugs. Everyone has incentive to publish successes and quietly shelve failures.

The result is a systematic distortion of the scientific record. If twenty labs test whether meditation improves memory, and one finds a positive result by chance, that's the study we read. The nineteen null results stay hidden. We think meditation improves memory when the actual evidence, if we could see all of it, shows nothing.

This isn't fraud. Individual studies might be perfectly conducted. But the collection of published studies doesn't represent the truth—it represents what people wanted or needed to publish. Meta-analyses try to correct for this by combining all available studies, but if unpublished studies are literally unavailable, the meta-analysis is poisoned at the source.

The problem becomes especially acute at the edges of science. When studying controversial phenomena—psi effects, alternative medicine, anything that challenges orthodoxy—publication bias works both ways. Believers have incentive to publish positive results. Skeptics have incentive to publish null results. Both communities have journal homes that will accept their work. But mainstream journals, where careers are made, tend to reject both, creating separate parallel literatures that don't talk to each other.

Consider the Ganzfeld experiments in parapsychology. Dozens of studies testing whether people can receive information telepathically under sensory deprivation. Meta-analyses showed small but consistent positive effects. Skeptics argued the file drawer problem explained it all—surely there were hundreds of unpublished failures. Parapsychologists responded by pre-registering studies, publicly declaring methods before collecting data, making it harder to shelve inconvenient results. The effects persisted.

Meanwhile, in mainstream psychology, the "replication crisis" revealed that many celebrated findings couldn't be reproduced. The effect of power poses on confidence? Mostly gone. Ego depletion? Questionable. Priming effects? Dramatically overstated. These weren't fringe claims—they were textbook psychology, cited thousands of times, accepted as fact. How many failures had been quietly shelved while positive results got published and celebrated?

Pharmaceutical research shows the problem with money behind it. Industry-funded trials are more likely to show positive results than independent ones. Not because companies commit fraud, but because negative trials are less likely to be published. Antidepressants look dramatically more effective in the published literature than when someone forces companies to release all their trial data, including the failures.

The irony is sharp. The file drawer problem was identified while studying telepathy, used to argue that psi effects were artifacts of publication bias. But publication bias turned out to affect cancer research, nutrition science, economics, neuroimaging—every field where careers depend on novel findings and null results are boring.

Some fields now require pre-registration. Researchers publicly declare their hypotheses and methods before collecting data. If the study fails, at least there's a public record it was attempted. The Cochrane Collaboration maintains a registry of clinical trials. Open science advocates push for publishing null results. But the incentive structure remains: positive findings get you noticed, null results make you look like you wasted your time.

The question becomes: what do we actually know? If the published literature is systematically biased toward positive findings, how do we judge what's real? This isn't just about telepathy or power poses. It's about the reliability of scientific knowledge itself. Rosenthal's calculation—forty-six unpublished studies needed to explain away the published ones—was meant to show how implausible it was that psi effects were pure publication bias. But it also showed how fragile published findings are when we can't see what's been hidden.

The file drawer problem doesn't make science useless. But it makes certainty harder to come by than we'd like. Every confident claim in a psychology textbook or medical guideline sits atop unknown numbers of contradicting studies that never saw print. We're reading the highlights, not the full record. And at the edges—where belief is controversial and careers are on the line—we might be reading a curated fiction.
