# Review of the Day

People who deliberately examine their day before sleeping often describe a shift — not just what happened, but how they moved through it. This practice appears across contemplative traditions under different names: examen, evening review, daily accounting. The commitment is simple: set aside time to look back over the hours just lived, noticing patterns, choices, moments where life revealed something.

## What It Is

Review of the day is the structured practice of mentally revisiting your day's events in sequence, typically before sleep, with attention to interior movements — emotional responses, choices made, moments of connection or disconnection. Unlike journal writing, which produces a record, this is primarily interior work. You trace the day chronologically from waking, noticing where you felt alive or deadened, where you acted from your values or against them, where grace or difficulty appeared. The review isn't problem-solving or planning but witnessing — seeing what actually happened rather than what you intended or feared.

## Where It Comes From

Ignatius of Loyola formalized the Examen in the 16th century as the centerpiece of Jesuit spiritual practice, meant to be done twice daily. But the impulse is ancient: Pythagorean communities examined their days in the 6th century BCE, and evening examination appears in Stoic philosophy as part of ethical formation. The Desert Fathers practiced remembrance of daily temptations and graces. Contemporary adaptations often secularize the practice, dropping theological language while keeping the core discipline: structured attention to lived experience as a path to self-knowledge and moral clarity.

## How It's Done

Set aside ten to fifteen minutes before sleep. Begin with a moment of settling — breathing, awareness of presence. Then recall the day chronologically, from waking forward, as if watching a film. Notice moments that carry emotional charge — positive or negative. Where did you feel most alive? Most depleted? When did you act with integrity? When did you drift from your values? "The Examen produces within us the priceless grace of self-knowledge," writes Jim Manney in A Simple, Life-Changing Prayer. Some practitioners use prompts: Where did I notice beauty? Where did I resist what was real? End with brief gratitude for what the day held and rest in that awareness.

## What It Cultivates

The practice builds continuity of consciousness — awareness of yourself as an agent moving through time rather than a passenger buffeted by circumstances. Regular review strengthens pattern recognition: you begin noticing what consistently drains or energizes you, where your choices diverge from your intentions, what triggers reactivity. Research on self-reflection shows it enhances emotional regulation and decision-making; a 2017 study in *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes* found that fifteen minutes of daily reflection improved performance by 23% over two weeks. The practice trains you to treat your life as significant, worth examining, capable of revealing truth.

## The Dangers

Review can become morbid self-scrutiny, an anxious cataloging of failures. The practice degenerates when it's weaponized for self-criticism, turning into an evening tribunal where you prosecute yourself. It can also become mechanical ritual — checking a box without genuine attention. Some practitioners develop a rehearsed narrative, reviewing the story they tell about their day rather than the day itself. The practice fails when it produces shame spirals rather than clarity, or when it replaces action with endless reflection. The antidote is gentleness: you're noticing, not judging; witnessing, not fixing.

## A Practitioner

Anthony Bloom, Orthodox Archbishop and physician, practiced evening review for decades and wrote about it with characteristic precision. He described reviewing his day not as spiritual bookkeeping but as "standing before reality." Each evening, he would recall the day's events, but with attention to where he had been genuinely present versus where he had been absent — physically there but inwardly elsewhere. Bloom emphasized that the practice wasn't about cataloging sins but about waking up: "If you have lived through the day without God, the day is wasted." His review wasn't self-flagellation but radical honesty about presence and absence, love and fear, the moments he had met life directly and the moments he had not.
