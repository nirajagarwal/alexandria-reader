# Examination of Conscience

A daily practice of reviewing one's actions, motives, and moral choices against a standard — traditionally divine law, but also personal values, virtues, or commitments. Not mere recall but judgment: where did I fail? Where did I choose well? What needs amendment?

## What It Is

Examination of conscience is the deliberate, typically daily, review of one's conduct through a moral lens. The practitioner recalls specific moments — conversations, decisions, impulses — and evaluates them: Did I act with integrity? Where did I fall short? What patterns emerge? It differs from general reflection or journaling by its focus on moral culpability and the intention to change. The examination seeks not just self-knowledge but self-correction. In religious forms, it prepares for confession or prayer. In secular adaptations, it serves conscience formation and ethical accountability. Psychologist Roy Baumeister notes that "self-monitoring is one of the key processes by which people regulate their behavior." The practice makes that monitoring systematic and moral.

## Where It Comes From

The practice has roots in multiple traditions. Ancient Stoics — Seneca, Marcus Aurelius — examined their days against virtue. Early Christian monastics formalized it as a discipline of conscience, notably through John Cassian's examination of thoughts. Ignatius of Loyola gave it structure in the 16th century: his "particular examen" directed attention to specific faults and graces. The Spiritual Exercises systematize the practice for both ordained and lay practitioners. Jewish tradition includes *cheshbon hanefesh* (accounting of the soul), particularly before Yom Kippur. Secular forms emerged through therapeutic and ethical movements in the 20th century, decoupling conscience examination from religious confession while maintaining its moral rigor.

## How It's Done

Set a regular time, typically evening. Some practitioners prefer midday and evening — Ignatius recommended both. Review the day chronologically or thematically. Ask structured questions: Where did I act according to my values? Where did I fail them? What prompted those failures — fear, pride, anger, laziness? What grace or strength did I receive? Some use prompts: the Ten Commandments, Ignatian questions about consolation and desolation, the cardinal virtues, or personal commitments. Write or reflect silently. Be specific — not "I was impatient" but "I snapped at my colleague when she asked about the deadline." End with gratitude for what went well and resolve for amendment. Duration: 10-20 minutes. The key is regularity and honesty, not length.

## What It Cultivates

The practice builds moral awareness — the capacity to notice not just what happened but what you chose and why. It interrupts habituation, where repeated minor failings become invisible. Research on self-monitoring shows it strengthens self-regulation: people who track their behavior change it. The examination cultivates humility through repeated encounters with actual failure, not theoretical sin. It also reveals patterns: the same fault recurring signals deeper work needed. Over time, practitioners report increased moral sensitivity — catching lapses in the moment rather than only in retrospect. It develops what philosopher Charles Taylor calls "the inner gaze," the ability to see oneself truthfully. Where practiced in community or with a spiritual director, it builds accountability and prevents self-deception.

## The Dangers

The examination can spiral into scrupulosity — obsessive guilt over minor infractions, paralyzing self-judgment. Some practitioners become fixated on cataloging failures rather than cultivating virtue. Psychologist Edward Welch warns that examination without grace becomes "morbid introspection." The practice can also become performative: examining conscience to maintain an identity as "someone who examines conscience." It can replace action with analysis — confessing the same fault repeatedly without changing behavior. In some hands, it becomes a tool of control: religious authorities demanding detailed accounts of thoughts and desires, violating psychological boundaries. Without proportion, it distorts: minor irritations receive the same scrutiny as genuine moral failures. The practice requires both honesty and mercy, both judgment and forgiveness.

## A Practitioner

Dorothy Day, founder of the Catholic Worker movement, practiced daily examination throughout her life. Her diaries reveal both rigorous self-judgment and profound gratitude. She examined not just personal failings — impatience with guests, pride in accomplishments — but structural participation in injustice: "We are all guilty, we have all contributed to this disorder." Her examination held together radical social activism and intimate moral accountability. She confessed specific moments: harsh words, petty resentments, failure to see Christ in the difficult guest. But she also noted consolations: unexpected kindness, strength to continue, small victories of love over irritation. Her examination wasn't morbid but clarifying. It kept her honest about her own flaws while sustaining decades of difficult work. She wrote, "We cannot love God unless we love each other," and used the examination to test whether her days reflected that conviction.
