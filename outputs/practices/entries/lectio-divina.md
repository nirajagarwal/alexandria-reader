# Lectio Divina

For fifteen hundred years, Christians have practiced a way of reading meant not to extract information but to be changed. Lectio divina — "divine reading" — approaches sacred text as a site of encounter, where words become an invitation to listen, reflect, and respond. What began in monasteries as the foundation of contemplative life has traveled into parishes, prisons, and living rooms, carried by those who discovered that reading can be a form of prayer.

## What It Is

Lectio divina is a method of prayerful reading that moves through four stages: *lectio* (reading), *meditatio* (meditation), *oratio* (prayer), and *contemplatio* (contemplation). Unlike study, which seeks to understand a text, lectio divina seeks to receive it. The practitioner reads a brief passage slowly, multiple times, attending to words or phrases that arrest attention. These become material for reflection, conversation with God, and finally silent resting in presence. The text functions less as information to be mastered than as a threshold to cross. "We read under the eye of God," writes Mariano Magrassi, "until the heart is touched and leaps to flame."

## Where It Comes From

The practice emerged from the monastic tradition of the Desert Fathers and Mothers in the third and fourth centuries, formalized by Benedict of Nursia in the sixth century as central to Benedictine life. Guigo II, a twelfth-century Carthusian monk, articulated the four stages in *The Ladder of Monks*. For centuries it remained primarily monastic — the work of communities organized around prayer. Vatican II's call to return to Scripture renewed interest beyond monasteries. Today it's practiced ecumenically, in spiritual direction, recovery groups, and interfaith contexts, adapted but recognizably continuous with its monastic origins.

## How It's Done

Choose a short passage — often Gospel narratives, Psalms, or Epistles. Read it slowly aloud or silently (*lectio*). Notice what word or phrase draws you; read again, letting it surface. Sit with this word, turning it over, exploring what it stirs (*meditatio*). Let reflection become conversation — speak to God about what's emerging, what you need, what you notice (*oratio*). Finally, release words altogether and rest in silence, simply being present (*contemplatio*). The movement isn't rigid; you may circle back, linger in one stage. Sessions typically last fifteen to thirty minutes. Some practice daily, returning to the same passage for a week.

## What It Cultivates

Lectio divina trains attention toward depth rather than speed, intimacy rather than mastery. It develops contemplative capacity — the ability to be present without grasping. Practitioners report growing sensitivity to inner movements, increased comfort with silence, and a sense of Scripture as living rather than fixed. Research on contemplative reading practices shows benefits for emotional regulation and reduced anxiety. The practice cultivates what might be called receptive agency — active engagement that's simultaneously open, willing to be surprised. Over time, many find the boundary between reading and prayer dissolving, the text becoming less object and more companion.

## The Dangers

Lectio divina can become mechanical — four steps dutifully executed, spirituality as checklist. The practice risks self-absorption when meditation curls inward, using Scripture to confirm existing opinions rather than allowing challenge. Some practitioners avoid difficult texts, cherry-picking comfort. Others mistake emotional response for genuine encounter, confusing feeling moved with being changed. Mariano Magrassi warns against "turning lectio into an occasion for learned research." In groups, it can become performative — sharing insights to impress rather than speaking from actual prayer. The practice loses its center when reading becomes about having an experience rather than meeting the One the text mediates.

## A Practitioner

Henri Nouwen practiced lectio divina throughout his life, particularly during his years at L'Arche Daybreak community. He would sit in his small apartment before dawn, reading the same Gospel passage each day of the week. His journals record not theological insights but lived questions, arguments with God, moments of unexpected consolation. When he struggled with loneliness and exhaustion, he returned to the parable of the Prodigal Son, reading it hundreds of times until, as he wrote, "I saw that the hands of the father were my true home." This practice shaped his teaching and writing — not biblical scholarship but testimony to encounter. His life demonstrates lectio's fruit: knowledge that passes through the heart.
