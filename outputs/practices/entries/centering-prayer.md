# Centering Prayer

A practice of receptive silence before God, marked not by speaking or thinking but by consenting. The practitioner chooses a sacred word — "peace," "mercy," "Jesus" — not as mantra but as intention, gently returning to it when thoughts arise. Twenty minutes, twice daily, sitting in willingness rather than effort. Not meditation on God but resting in God's presence, letting contemplation happen rather than making it happen.

## What It Is

Centering Prayer is a method of silent prayer that prepares the practitioner for the gift of contemplative union with God. The practitioner sits in silence, using a sacred word to express consent to God's presence and action within. When thoughts, feelings, or perceptions arise — and they always do — the word is gently employed to return to intention. This is not concentration, not visualization, not problem-solving. Father Thomas Keating, who developed the contemporary form, writes: "Centering Prayer is not so much the absence of thoughts as detachment from them." The sacred word functions not as object of focus but as symbol of consent, a gesture of openness repeated whenever awareness drifts into engagement with mental content.

## Where It Comes From

The practice draws on the Christian contemplative tradition, particularly the Desert Fathers, the anonymous fourteenth-century *Cloud of Unknowing*, and Teresa of Avila's stages of prayer. In the 1970s, three Trappist monks — Thomas Keating, William Meninger, and Basil Pennington — developed Centering Prayer as a method accessible to laypeople, responding to interest in Eastern meditation practices. They sought to recover the West's own contemplative heritage, dormant in many Christian communities. The name references Thomas Merton's description of contemplation as discovering one's "true self" in God. While rooted in Catholic monasticism, the practice has spread across denominations and been adapted by those who maintain its Christian orientation while softening its theological specificity.

## How It's Done

Choose a sacred word of one or two syllables — "God," "Jesus," "love," "stillness" — that expresses your intention to consent to God's presence. Sit comfortably with eyes closed. Settle briefly, then introduce the word silently. When you notice you're engaged with thoughts — planning, remembering, analyzing, even spiritual reflections — gently return to the word. Not forcefully. Not as punishment for distraction. As reminder of intention. The word may become vague, fade to mere impulse. That's fine. It's doing its work. Practice for twenty minutes, twice daily if possible. Use a timer so you're not watching the clock. At the end, remain in silence with eyes closed for a couple of minutes, letting the prayer extend into daily awareness.

## What It Cultivates

The practice develops what Keating calls "divine therapy" — the healing of the unconscious through regular exposure to God's presence. Research by David Vago and others suggests contemplative practices reduce default mode network activity, quieting the self-referential thinking that generates much psychological suffering. Practitioners report decreased reactivity, greater equanimity, and a felt sense of being grounded in something larger than the anxious ego. The practice doesn't eliminate thoughts but changes relationship to them — you notice mental content without being captured by it. Over time, this transforms not just prayer periods but daily life. The consent practiced in silence extends to consent amid activity: traffic, conflict, disappointment. You learn to rest in God's presence not only in stillness but in chaos.

## The Dangers

Centering Prayer can become spiritual bypassing — using "detachment from thoughts" to avoid necessary psychological work or difficult emotions that demand attention. Some practitioners grow passive, mistaking quietism for surrender, neglecting discernment and action for endless receptivity. Others turn the practice into achievement: better prayers, deeper silence, more complete emptiness. This misses the point entirely — Centering Prayer is about consent, not attainment. There's also risk of decontextualizing the practice from its theological roots, treating it as mere stress reduction. Keating warned against this: without the Christian framework of kenosis (self-emptying), grace, and union with Christ, the practice loses its transformative orientation. It becomes relaxation technique rather than participation in divine life.

## A Practitioner

Thomas Keating (1923-2018) lived what he taught. A Trappist monk for sixty years, he could have remained in the cloister but instead traveled widely to teach Centering Prayer, believing contemplative practice belonged to everyone, not only monastics. At St. Benedict's Monastery in Snowmass, Colorado, he maintained his own practice: rising at 3 a.m. for vigils, then sitting in Centering Prayer before dawn. Visitors reported his quality of presence — he listened with complete attention, unhurried even in brief encounters. In his eighties, dealing with Parkinson's disease, he continued teaching, his physical diminishment somehow deepening his authority. He spoke of suffering as participation in Christ's kenosis. His final years demonstrated what decades of consent had formed: not transcendence of the body but transparency to grace within limitation.
