# Spiritual Direction

Two people meet regularly. One speaks of their inner life — what troubles, what calls, what confuses them about their relationship with the divine or with meaning itself. The other listens with a particular kind of attention, helping them notice where God or grace or truth might already be moving. This is spiritual direction: guided attention to the soul's landscape.

## What It Is

Spiritual direction is a relationship where one person (the director) helps another (the directee) notice and respond to the movement of the sacred in their life. Unlike therapy, which focuses on psychological health, or coaching, which pursues goals, direction attends specifically to the spiritual dimension — prayer, meaning, vocation, the sense of being called or held by something larger. The director asks questions more than offering answers, creates space more than filling it. Thomas Merton wrote that the director "has no ready-made solutions to offer, but rather helps the other discover what the Spirit is already teaching." The sessions are typically monthly, structured around what the directee brings — a dream, a struggle in prayer, a decision that won't resolve, a persistent restlessness. The director helps them look more carefully at what's actually happening in their soul.

## Where It Comes From

The practice emerges from Christian monasticism, particularly the desert tradition of the fourth century. Early monks sought out elders — abbas and ammas — not for instruction but for help discerning the spirits, naming what was of God and what was not. Ignatius of Loyola formalized this in the sixteenth century through his Spiritual Exercises, training directors to guide others through movements of consolation and desolation. The practice remained largely Catholic until the twentieth century, when Protestant and ecumenical communities reclaimed it. Today, spiritual direction exists across traditions — Christian, Jewish, Buddhist — and in secular forms that attend to meaning without specific religious content. What persists across variations is the conviction that the spiritual life needs companionship, that we can't see our own souls clearly without help.

## How It's Done

Director and directee meet regularly, usually monthly, for sixty to ninety minutes. Research by Sandra Schneiders suggests this rhythm allows integration between sessions without losing continuity. The directee speaks first, bringing what's alive in their inner life. The director listens with what Ignatius called "holy indifference" — present but not directive, attentive to both what's said and what's avoided. They ask questions: What was happening in your body when that occurred? Where was God in that moment? What are you being invited to notice? The space is confidential, unhurried. Directors are trained — often through programs lasting two to three years — in listening, discernment, psychology, and their own spiritual maturity. They themselves receive direction; you can't guide what you don't inhabit. Between sessions, the directee practices attention: praying, journaling, noticing what consolates and what desolates.

## What It Cultivates

Direction cultivates discernment — the capacity to distinguish what's truly calling from what merely clamors. It trains attention toward the inner life, helping people notice patterns they'd otherwise miss. Studies in pastoral psychology show that regular direction correlates with increased sense of meaning, decreased anxiety about religious questions, and greater clarity in decision-making. The practice builds honesty: directees learn to name their actual experience rather than their preferred self-image. It also cultivates patience, the willingness to live with uncertainty while meaning clarifies. For many, direction provides the only space where their spiritual life receives serious, sustained attention — where doubts aren't resolved but honored, where silence isn't broken but entered. Kenneth Leech noted that direction "creates space for God to work," not by adding something but by removing interference, helping directees hear what they couldn't hear alone.

## The Dangers

Direction can become dependency, with directees looking to directors for answers rather than developing their own capacity for discernment. Directors can exercise subtle control, steering directees toward the director's own preferences or theology. The relationship can become pseudo-therapeutic, addressing psychological issues that need professional help. There's also spiritual bypassing — using direction to avoid practical responsibilities, floating in the inner life while neglecting actual obligations. Bad direction offers premature certainty, smoothing over real tensions with easy answers. It can become performance, where directees present polished versions of their spiritual lives rather than actual struggles. The practice requires maturity on both sides: directors who know their limits, directees who take responsibility for their own discernment. As Margaret Guenther warns, direction can unconsciously replicate old patterns of authority and submission, becoming spiritual colonization rather than companionship.

## A Practitioner

Howard Thurman, the African American theologian and mystic, practiced spiritual direction throughout his life and trained others in it. As dean of chapel at Boston University in the 1950s, he met individually with students of all backgrounds — including Martin Luther King Jr., who sought Thurman's guidance during the Montgomery bus boycott. Thurman's direction was rooted in silence and in the conviction that God spoke through the particulars of each person's experience, especially their suffering. He listened for what he called "the sound of the genuine" in each person, refusing to impose predetermined paths. His own director was Rufus Jones, the Quaker mystic, from whom Thurman learned that direction meant helping people trust what they already knew but couldn't yet name. In his work, spiritual and social concerns were inseparable: he directed people toward their vocation in both the inner life and the world's needs, seeing these as one movement.
