# Fasting

People fast to remember what it feels like to be hungry. The practice — deliberately abstaining from food for a set period — creates space between desire and fulfillment. In that space, practitioners report discovering what they're really seeking, what their bodies actually need, and how much of their daily rhythm revolves around eating. The hunger becomes a teacher.

## What It Is

Fasting is the voluntary abstention from food, and sometimes drink, for a defined period. It differs from starvation (involuntary deprivation) and dieting (restriction for weight loss). The practitioner chooses when to begin, how long to continue, and what to permit or exclude. Some fasts allow water, others permit liquids, still others are complete abstentions. Duration ranges from sunup to sundown, to multiple days, to weeks under medical supervision. The defining feature is intentionality — hunger becomes a tool for attention rather than a problem to solve immediately.

## Where It Comes From

Fasting appears across religious traditions spanning millennia. Jews fast on Yom Kippur, Muslims during Ramadan's daylight hours, Christians during Lent and Advent. Hindu practitioners observe ekadashi, Buddhist monks eat nothing after noon. The Desert Fathers fasted in the Egyptian wilderness; Sufi mystics used hunger to heighten spiritual awareness. Indigenous traditions incorporate fasting in vision quests and rites of passage. In recent decades, medicine has examined intermittent fasting protocols — 16:8, 5:2, alternate-day patterns — stripping the practice of religious context while preserving its metabolic effects.

## How It's Done

The practitioner sets boundaries: duration, what's excluded, what breaks the fast. A Muslim observing Ramadan abstains from food and drink from dawn to sunset for a lunar month. An intermittent faster might eat only between noon and 8 PM daily. A multi-day faster under supervision might take only water, electrolytes, and medical monitoring. Some traditions prescribe the fast; others leave it to individual discernment. The practice includes both the abstention and the breaking — how one ends the fast matters as much as how one maintains it. Many begin gradually, shortening eating windows before attempting longer fasts.

## What It Cultivates

Fasting builds metabolic flexibility — the body's capacity to switch from glucose to stored fat for fuel. Research shows improved insulin sensitivity, cellular autophagy (the breakdown of damaged proteins), and reduced inflammation markers. Psychologist Valter Longo's work demonstrates that periodic fasting may extend lifespan and improve immune function. But practitioners describe subtler gains: clarity of mind when freed from digestion's demands, awareness of emotional versus physical hunger, recognition of how much eating structures time. The practice creates distance from compulsion. You discover whether you're hungry or merely bored, anxious, or habituated to snacking. Thomas Merton wrote, "We become contemplatives when God discovers Himself in us."

## The Dangers

Fasting becomes dangerous when it masks disordered eating, when hunger transforms from teacher to punishment. The practice can slide into orthorexia or anorexia, where virtue becomes privation and control becomes compulsion. Those with eating disorder histories should approach carefully or not at all. Fasting can also become performative — a way to signal discipline or spiritual advancement rather than a private encounter with hunger. Extended fasts without medical supervision risk electrolyte imbalances, muscle loss, and metabolic damage. The diabetic who fasts without adjusting medication courts hypoglycemia. Fasting while pregnant or nursing endangers two bodies, not one.

## A Practitioner

Gandhi used fasting as both spiritual discipline and political weapon. His fasts for communal harmony, against British rule, and for Indian unity lasted days or weeks. In September 1932, while imprisoned, he began a "fast unto death" against British plans to segregate "untouchables" into separate electorates — a move he believed would calcify caste divisions. His declining health pressured all parties to negotiate. They reached the Poona Pact within six days. Gandhi wasn't performing purity or self-punishment; he was leveraging his own body's vulnerability to force moral reckoning. His fasting combined personal discipline with political theater, showing how the practice bridges inner work and public witness. The hunger was real; so were the stakes.
