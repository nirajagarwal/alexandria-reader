# Tithing

For millennia, people have given away a fixed portion of what they earn or produce — not from surplus, but from the whole. The practice transforms both giver and community, creating a rhythm where resources flow beyond accumulation, where security is found not in hoarding but in release.

## What It Is

Tithing is the practice of giving away a predetermined percentage of income or production before spending on anything else. Traditionally ten percent, though the proportion varies. What distinguishes tithing from charitable giving is its priority and automaticity: the commitment precedes the need, the amount is fixed regardless of feelings or circumstances. You don't decide each month whether to give or how much — you've already decided. The practice removes giving from the realm of impulse or convenience and makes it structural, a recurring relinquishment that shapes how you relate to everything else you have.

## Where It Comes From

The practice appears across ancient Near Eastern cultures, formalized in Torah as a contribution to the Levites and the poor. Early Christians debated whether the tithe remained binding; many Church Fathers urged even more generous giving. In medieval Europe, the tithe became institutionalized church tax. Islam established *zakat*, a 2.5% wealth tax, as one of the Five Pillars. The practice persisted among dissenting Protestant groups, particularly Methodists and Baptists, as voluntary commitment rather than compulsory levy. Contemporary secular tithing emerges from this tradition but directs resources toward any mission the giver values — the structure survives even when the theological framework doesn't.

## How It's Done

Calculate your gross income — what arrives before taxes and deductions. Determine your percentage, typically ten percent but adjusted to circumstance. Give that amount first, before rent or groceries or retirement savings. Traditional tithers write the check when the paycheck arrives; modern practitioners set up automatic transfers. The key is priority: tithe before you pay yourself, before you see what's "left over." Many practitioners split their tithe across multiple recipients — religious community, poverty relief, education, arts. Some give monthly, some per paycheck, some annually. The rhythm matters less than the commitment: it comes off the top, it happens whether you feel generous or not.

## What It Cultivates

Tithing trains the intuition that security comes from flow rather than accumulation. As writer Lauren Winner observes, "Tithing is not primarily about raising money for the church. It is about forming disciples who are not possessed by their possessions." The practice builds trust — that you'll have enough, that others' needs matter as much as your wants. Regular givers report decreased financial anxiety even while giving away substantial sums; behavioral economics research shows that committed giving activates reward circuits more strongly than impulsive charity. The practice cultivates gratitude, attention to others' needs, and immunity to consumerism's endless appetite. It makes generosity structural rather than sentimental, reliable rather than romantic.

## The Dangers

Tithing becomes spiritual performance when the practice becomes visible — when givers calculate their righteousness by their donations or judge others by theirs. It distorts into legalism when the percentage becomes more important than the heart, when you tithe while ignoring direct obligations to family or employees. The practice can subsidize institutional dysfunction, perpetuating organizations that waste resources or cause harm. Some communities weaponize tithing, manipulating guilt or promising prosperity to coerce donations. Psychologist Sonja Lyubomirsky notes that giving loses its benefits when it's purely obligatory without any sense of agency: "People need to feel they are giving freely, not out of duty or pressure." The danger is making an external rule out of what should remain an internal commitment — or using the commitment to avoid thinking carefully about where the resources actually go.

## A Practitioner

Dorothy Day, co-founder of the Catholic Worker movement, practiced a radicalized version of tithing throughout her adult life. Living in voluntary poverty, she gave away not ten percent but nearly everything, keeping only what she needed for bare subsistence. When donations arrived for the Worker houses, they went immediately to whoever needed them — rent for evicted families, food for the breadlines, bail for the arrested. She dressed in clothing from donation piles, owned almost nothing personal. This wasn't theatrical asceticism but structural commitment: money was for circulation, not keeping. When she received a $2,500 award from Notre Dame, she gave it away within days. "The mystery of poverty," she wrote, "is that by sharing in it, making ourselves poor in giving to others, we increase our knowledge and belief in love."
