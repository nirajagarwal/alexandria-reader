# Prayer

People have been speaking to what they cannot see for as long as they've been speaking. Prayer is the practice of addressing the transcendent — God, gods, the universe, the ground of being — with words, silence, or gesture. Whether understood as conversation, contemplation, or communion, prayer has shaped human consciousness across every culture and century.

## What It Is

Prayer is intentional communication with the divine or transcendent. It takes countless forms: petition (asking for help), intercession (praying for others), confession (acknowledging wrongdoing), thanksgiving (expressing gratitude), adoration (offering praise), contemplation (resting in presence). Some prayers use prescribed words — the Lord's Prayer, the Shema, the Fatihah. Others arise spontaneously. Some involve bodily postures: kneeling, prostration, hands raised or folded. Still others are silent, wordless attention. What unites these varied forms is the intention to relate to something or someone beyond the self, to acknowledge dependence, connection, or reverence toward a reality greater than our own concerns.

## Where It Comes From

Prayer appears in humanity's earliest records — Sumerian hymns, Egyptian invocations, Vedic chants. The Hebrew psalms shaped Jewish prayer life 3,000 years ago and still do. Jesus taught his disciples a particular prayer and modeled a life saturated with it. Islamic practice structures five daily prayers (salat) as pillars of faith. Buddhist traditions developed elaborate prayer forms despite philosophical debates about whether prayer to deities fits their metaphysics. Even secular modernity hasn't eliminated prayer — it's adapted into meditation, intention-setting, or simply talking to the dead. The instinct to speak into the mystery persists across belief systems. As the neurologist Andrew Newberg notes, "The brain appears to be built in such a way that prayer and meditation can profoundly reshape it."

## How It's Done

Prayer practices vary by tradition. In Christian monasticism, the Liturgy of the Hours structures prayer throughout the day — fixed psalms and readings at set times. Muslims perform salat five times daily, facing Mecca, with prescribed words and movements. Jewish prayer includes communal recitation of the Amidah while standing, individual petition, and blessing practices throughout daily life. Contemplative prayer strips away words entirely — practitioners sit in silent attention to God's presence, as in centering prayer or Christian meditation. Intercessory prayer focuses sustained attention on others' needs. Prayer can be scripted or spontaneous, solitary or communal, vocal or silent, a discipline of minutes or hours. The method matters less than the regularity and attention brought to it.

## What It Cultivates

Prayer trains attention away from self-absorption. Regular practice builds what philosophers call "ultimate concern" — the habit of relating moment-by-moment life to larger meaning and purpose. Research shows contemplative prayer reduces anxiety and increases emotional regulation, likely through mechanisms similar to mindfulness meditation. But prayer's fruit extends beyond measurable outcomes. It cultivates humility (acknowledging what we cannot control), gratitude (naming gifts received), compassion (holding others' suffering), and hope (trusting in possibilities beyond current circumstances). For believers, prayer deepens relationship with the divine. For skeptics, it can still function as a practice of radical honesty — speaking aloud our fears, longings, and dependencies without pretense.

## The Dangers

Prayer becomes toxic when it bypasses responsibility. "Thoughts and prayers" without action mocks the practice. Prayer can also become performance — public displays of piety that serve self-regard more than devotion, exactly what Jesus criticized in Matthew 6. It becomes magical thinking when treated as a transaction: proper technique guaranteeing desired results. Some use prayer to avoid difficult decisions or justify predetermined conclusions ("I prayed about it, and God told me..."). Obsessive prayer can signal anxiety disorder rather than faith. Prayer can also become spiritual narcissism — endless focus on one's own interior states and "personal relationship with God" while ignoring injustice and neighbor. The practice fails when it increases rather than decreases self-centeredness.

## A Practitioner

Thomas Merton, the Trappist monk and writer, restructured his life around prayer after entering Gethsemani Abbey in 1941. He rose at 2 a.m. for vigils, prayed the Liturgy of the Hours seven times daily, and spent hours in contemplative silence. But Merton's prayer didn't isolate him from the world — it drove him deeper into it. His contemplation generated fierce engagement with civil rights and opposition to war. "Prayer and love are really learned in the hour when prayer becomes impossible and the heart has turned to stone," he wrote in *New Seeds of Contemplation*. In his hermitage years, Merton's prayer stripped down to pure attention, influenced by Zen practice. He died electrocuted in Bangkok, having traveled to build bridges between contemplative traditions. His life demonstrated that authentic prayer doesn't withdraw from the world's suffering but equips sustained engagement with it.
