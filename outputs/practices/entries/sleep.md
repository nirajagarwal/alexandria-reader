# Sleep

We spend a third of our lives doing it, yet treat it as something to minimize, overcome, or "catch up on" later. But sleep isn't merely rest from waking life — it's a practice of biological renewal that makes flourishing possible. Every night, if we let it happen properly, our bodies and minds undergo restoration that no other activity can provide.

## What It Is

Sleep is the recurring state of reduced consciousness and metabolic activity during which the body consolidates memory, repairs tissue, regulates hormones, and processes emotion. It cycles through distinct stages — light sleep, deep sleep, and REM — each serving specific restorative functions. Unlike simple rest or lying down, sleep involves active neural processes: the brain sorting and storing information, the glymphatic system clearing metabolic waste, the immune system strengthening defenses. A practice of sleep means creating conditions for this biological imperative to unfold naturally, in adequate duration and quality, aligned with circadian rhythms rather than imposed schedules or artificial stimulation.

## Where It Comes From

Sleep predates human consciousness — it's ancient as complex nervous systems themselves. Every culture has developed rituals around it: the Jewish practice of bedtime Shema prayers, Buddhist evening contemplations, the afternoon siesta common across Mediterranean and Latin American societies. Indigenous Australian cultures spoke of the Dreamtime, recognizing sleep as access to sacred knowledge. Yet industrialization and artificial light transformed sleep from a natural rhythm into something negotiable. Thomas Edison declared sleep "a waste of time." By the late twentieth century, chronic sleep deprivation became so normalized that Matthew Walker, neuroscientist, writes that "the shorter your sleep, the shorter your life" — a statement that would have puzzled our ancestors as obviously true.

## How It's Done

The practice begins hours before bed: dimming lights as evening approaches, avoiding screens, establishing a consistent schedule. The bedroom serves as sanctuary — cool (around 65-68°F), dark, quiet. No television, no phone charging beside the pillow. Many practitioners maintain a wind-down routine: light reading, gentle stretching, warm bath. The goal is seven to nine hours of actual sleep (not just time in bed) at consistent times, even on weekends. This regularity trains the circadian system. Sleep is not forced but invited — creating conditions and then allowing the body's wisdom to take over. The practice includes morning elements too: exposure to natural light within an hour of waking, which anchors the biological clock for the following night.

## What It Cultivates

Adequate sleep strengthens every dimension of flourishing. Cognitively: enhanced learning, memory consolidation, creative problem-solving, executive function. Emotionally: improved mood regulation, reduced anxiety, better stress resilience. Research shows that even modest sleep restriction impairs emotional control as much as alcohol intoxication. Physically: strengthened immune function, tissue repair, athletic performance, metabolic health. Relationally: sleep-deprived people show reduced empathy and increased interpersonal conflict. As neuroscientist Allan Rechtschaffen observed, "If sleep does not serve an absolutely vital function, then it is the biggest mistake the evolutionary process has ever made." The evidence suggests it serves dozens of vital functions, each supporting our capacity to engage life fully.

## The Dangers

The practice corrupts when it becomes anxious monitoring rather than natural rest. Sleep tracking devices can create "orthosomnia" — obsessive pursuit of perfect sleep data that paradoxically disrupts actual sleep. The practice also fails when treated as optional during busy seasons, creating sleep debt that accumulates genuine health consequences. Some use sleep medications or alcohol as shortcuts, which produce sedation but not true restorative sleep. The opposite distortion treats excessive sleep as virtue, when sleeping beyond natural need may signal depression or other illness. There's also the trap of rigid sleep hygiene that creates performance anxiety — lying awake worried about not sleeping becomes its own insomnia. The practice requires balance: taking sleep seriously without making it another thing to achieve.

## A Practitioner

Arianna Huffington collapsed from exhaustion in 2007, breaking her cheekbone on her desk. The media mogul had treated sleep as weakness, boasting about her stamina. The collapse forced a reckoning. She began studying sleep science, ultimately writing *The Sleep Revolution*. She installed nap rooms at HuffPost offices, stopped charging her phone outside her bedroom, established a rigid digital sunset. But the transformation went deeper than hygiene tips: "We think, mistakenly, that success is the result of the amount of time we put in at work, instead of the quality of time we put in." Her practice involved not just sleeping more but reconceiving what productivity means — recognizing that rest makes everything else possible, not the reverse.
