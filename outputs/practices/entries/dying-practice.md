# Dying Practice

Most spiritual traditions treat dying not as something that happens to us, but as something we can practice. Like any art, dying well requires preparation. The practice of dying — contemplating mortality, rehearsing letting go, befriending the inevitable — transforms death from catastrophe into culmination. Those who practice dying report living with less fear, greater presence, and clearer priorities. What seems morbid proves generative.

## What It Is

Dying practice involves deliberately confronting mortality through contemplation, visualization, or ritual. Medieval monks kept skulls on their desks (*memento mori*). Buddhists meditate on corpses in graveyards. Modern practitioners write ethical wills, practice advance directives, or simply sit with the reality that they will die. The practice isn't morbid rumination but conscious relationship with death — treating it as teacher rather than enemy. It differs from grief work (processing losses that have occurred) and from crisis preparation (practical planning for emergencies). This is rehearsal for the one certainty, cultivation of what the Stoics called *praemeditatio malorum* — the premeditation of adversity.

## Where It Comes From

Buddhism has formal corpse contemplation (*maraṇasati*) dating to the Pali Canon, where monks studied decomposition in charnel grounds. Medieval Christianity developed the *ars moriendi* — manuals for dying well, treating death as an art requiring lifelong study. The Stoics practiced *premeditatio malorum*, daily imagining loss and death. "Let us prepare our minds as if we'd come to the very end of life," Seneca wrote. "Let us postpone nothing. Let us balance life's books each day." Mexican *Día de los Muertos* makes death familiar through annual celebration. Modern death cafés, mortality salons, and death doula training secularize the practice while preserving its essential work: befriending the unavoidable.

## How It's Done

The simplest form: regular contemplation, perhaps five minutes daily, of the fact that you will die. Not imagining how, but sitting with the certainty that this life ends. Some write their own eulogies or obituaries. Others practice *parting meditation* — lying down as if dead, releasing attachments mentally. Advance directive work becomes practice when done contemplatively rather than bureaucratically. Some visit graveyards, not morbidly but curiously. Others keep physical reminders — a skull, a photograph, a memento mori object. The Tibetan *phowa* practice involves visualizing consciousness leaving the body at death. Christian traditions use annual Ash Wednesday's "Remember that you are dust." Frequency matters more than method — the practice requires repetition to work past denial into genuine befriending.

## What It Cultivates

Regular dying practice generates what researchers call "mortality salience" — awareness of death that, paradoxically, enhances life. Studies show contemplating mortality increases generosity, reduces materialism, and strengthens relationships. Practitioners report increased present-moment awareness: when time is finite, attention sharpens. Priorities clarify. Petty concerns shrink. The practice builds what Irvin Yalom calls "constructive death anxiety" — not paralysis but motivation. It cultivates gratitude: each day becomes gift rather than entitlement. Perhaps most importantly, it diminishes fear through familiarity. Death, repeatedly contemplated, loses some of its terror. The practice doesn't make dying easy, but it makes living more honest.

## The Dangers

The practice can curdle into morbid obsession, particularly for those prone to depression or anxiety. What should liberate becomes paralyzing fixation. Some practitioners develop thanatophobia — pathological death fear — rather than healthy acceptance. Others use the practice to escape present responsibilities: "We all die anyway" becomes excuse for disengagement. There's spiritual bypassing too — using death contemplation to avoid grief or suppress legitimate fear. Some traditions, improperly taught, traumatize rather than prepare. The practice can also become performance: competitive mortality awareness, Instagram-worthy memento mori, death-positivity as brand. The point isn't to love death or romanticize it, but to include it honestly in life's accounting.

## A Practitioner

Paul Kalanithi, neurosurgeon dying of cancer at thirty-seven, documented his dying practice in *When Breath Becomes Air*. Trained in both medicine and literature, he approached his terminal diagnosis with the question: "What makes life worth living in the face of death?" Rather than denial or despair, he practiced what he called "present-tense living" — full engagement with remaining time. He continued surgery as long as able, wrote daily, became a father. "The fact of death is unsettling," he wrote. "Yet there is no other way to live." His practice wasn't resignation but fierce attention: reading poetry with his infant daughter, choosing words carefully for the book he'd never see published, insisting on both honesty and hope. He died having practiced what he believed: that facing death directly makes whatever life remains more precious, more deliberate, more fully lived.
