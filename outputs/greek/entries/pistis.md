# Pistis (Πίστις)
*Faith*

When Greek philosophers spoke of *pistis*, they meant intellectual assent — the confidence you place in a logical argument or the trust you give to credible testimony. It occupied the lower rungs of Plato's hierarchy of knowledge, inferior to true understanding. But when New Testament writers seized this word, they transformed it into something far more radical: not merely believing *that* something is true, but trusting *in* someone so completely that you stake your life on it. Faith became not the conclusion of an argument but the beginning of a relationship.

Paul makes this transformation explicit in Romans when he writes that Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness — not because Abraham possessed perfect knowledge, but because he trusted a promise he couldn't yet see fulfilled. The apostle unpacks *pistis* across his letters as active trust that manifests in allegiance. When he declares that we are justified by faith apart from works of law, he's not proposing mere mental agreement with doctrine. He's describing a transfer of trust — from confidence in our own moral achievements to complete reliance on Christ's finished work. This is why James can write that faith without works is dead: true *pistis* necessarily produces action because genuine trust always changes behavior.

The Gospels reveal Jesus constantly confronting people with questions about their *pistis*. When he heals the woman with the flow of blood, he tells her that her faith has made her well — her trust compelled her to reach for his garment despite ritual law forbidding her touch. When he calms the storm, he asks his terrified disciples where their faith is — not their intellectual belief in his identity, but their gut-level trust when circumstances seem to contradict his goodness. The centurion whose servant Jesus heals from a distance receives the highest praise: "I have not found such great faith even in Israel." What distinguished this Roman soldier? He understood authority so well that he trusted Jesus's word alone, without needing to see the healing happen.

The writer of Hebrews provides the most comprehensive meditation on *pistis* in Scripture, defining it as confidence in what we hope for and assurance about what we do not see. But this isn't wishful thinking — the entire eleventh chapter catalogs people who took concrete action based on unseen realities. Abel offered a better sacrifice. Noah built an ark on dry ground. Abraham left home for a destination he didn't know. Moses chose affliction with God's people over Egyptian luxury. Faith transformed speculation into obedience, hope into movement. The chapter culminates with Jesus himself as the founder and perfecter of our faith — the one who not only calls us to trust but demonstrates what perfect trust looks like by enduring the cross for the joy set before him.

What neuroscience now reveals about trust illuminates why biblical writers made *pistis* so central. The brain processes intellectual agreement primarily through the neocortex — our reasoning center. But deep trust activates the limbic system and releases oxytocin, fundamentally altering how we perceive threat and opportunity. When you truly trust someone, you're not merely intellectually convinced of their reliability; your nervous system recalibrates around their trustworthiness. This is why Paul can write that faith comes by hearing — not just processing information, but receiving a message from someone you're learning to trust. The Greek philosophers were right that *pistis* is distinct from pure knowledge, but wrong about its value. Trust doesn't replace understanding; it enables a different kind of knowing — the kind that comes through relationship rather than mere observation. This is why John writes that whoever believes in the Son has eternal life: not because correct belief earns salvation, but because faith itself is the relationship of trust that connects us to the source of life.

**Key Scriptures:** Romans 3:28, Romans 4:3, Matthew 8:10, Matthew 9:22, Hebrews 11:1, Hebrews 12:2, James 2:17, John 3:36
