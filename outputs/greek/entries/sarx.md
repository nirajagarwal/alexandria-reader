# Sarx (Σάρξ)
*Flesh*

In classical Greek, *sarx* meant simply the physical substance of the body — meat, tissue, the stuff of mortality. Homer used it to describe warriors' wounds; physicians used it in medical texts. It was neutral, biological, unremarkable. But when biblical writers adopted this ordinary word, they transformed it into one of the New Testament's most theologically loaded terms, a concept that would define Christian anthropology and soteriology for two millennia.

The New Testament uses *sarx* in multiple, overlapping ways that trace an arc from the physical to the spiritual. At its most basic, it still means bodily tissue — Jesus tells his startled disciples after the resurrection to touch him and see, for a spirit does not have flesh and bones. John's gospel declares that the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, emphasizing the radical physicality of the incarnation. Paul speaks of a thorn in his flesh, some unnamed bodily affliction. This physical meaning never disappears, but it becomes a launching point for deeper theological reflection.

Yet *sarx* quickly expands beyond mere biology. It comes to represent human existence in its earthly, mortal dimension — what we might call "the human condition." Paul writes of being absent from the body but present with the Lord, using *sarx* to denote the temporary, created aspect of human life. The gospels record Jesus saying that flesh gives birth to flesh, but the Spirit gives birth to spirit, distinguishing between natural human generation and divine regeneration. This isn't dualism — the physical isn't inherently evil — but recognition that human nature, left to itself, remains bounded by mortality and limitation.

The word reaches its full theological weight in Paul's letters, where *sarx* becomes a technical term for human nature oriented away from God. He writes that those who live according to the flesh set their minds on the things of the flesh, while those who live according to the Spirit set their minds on the things of the Spirit. The works of the flesh — sexual immorality, enmity, strife, jealousy — aren't merely bodily sins but represent the entire trajectory of human life turned inward, self-centered, resistant to divine transformation. Paul declares that while we once knew Christ according to the flesh, we know him that way no longer, marking the shift from natural human perspective to spiritual understanding.

This creates apparent paradoxes that Paul embraces rather than resolves. Believers still live *in* the flesh but are commanded not to live *according* to the flesh. The law was weakened by the flesh, yet Christ came in the likeness of sinful flesh to condemn sin in the flesh. For Paul, *sarx* represents not the body as such but the whole human being — mind, will, affections — organized around self rather than God. It's the unredeemed human project, the native trajectory of fallen creation. Romans eight captures this tension: the mind set on the flesh is hostile to God and cannot please him, yet those who are in Christ Jesus are not in the flesh but in the Spirit.

What's striking is how modern neuroscience has begun to confirm Paul's intuition about embodied patterns of thought and behavior. Researchers have discovered that repeated actions — whether generous or selfish, disciplined or indulgent — literally reshape neural pathways, creating what scientists call "neuroplasticity." The brain physically reorganizes itself around habitual patterns of thinking and acting. Paul's language about being transformed by the renewal of the mind, about putting off the old self and putting on the new, describes something neuroscience now maps at the synaptic level. The "works of the flesh" aren't just moral failures but deeply grooved patterns of neural response that require conscious, repeated intervention to rewire. The Christian journey from *sarx* to *pneuma* (spirit) involves not just theological understanding but literally retraining the embodied mind — which is why Paul speaks of discipline, training, and the ongoing work of transformation rather than instant perfection.

**Key Scriptures:** Luke 24:39, John 1:14, John 3:6, Romans 7:5, Romans 8:3-9, Romans 8:12-13, 2 Corinthians 5:16, Galatians 5:19-21
