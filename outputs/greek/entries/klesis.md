# Klēsis (Κλῆσις)
*Calling*

In classical Greek, *klēsis* meant a summons—the act of calling someone to court, to assembly, or to witness. It carried legal weight, implying both invitation and obligation. When New Testament writers adopted this term, they transformed it from a civic summons into something far more profound: God's personal invitation into relationship and purpose. This wasn't mere religious language borrowed from the courts; it was the vocabulary of divine initiative, describing how the Creator reaches across the gulf between heaven and earth to name and claim his people.

Paul makes *klēsis* central to his theology of grace. In his letter to the Romans, he writes of those who are called according to God's purpose, emphasizing that this calling originates not in human merit but in divine sovereignty. The calling precedes human response—God calls while we are still sinners, still enemies, still dead in trespasses. Paul's letters to the Corinthians reveal the scandalous nature of this calling: God chooses the foolish things of the world to shame the wise, the weak things to shame the strong. The calling comes to slaves and free, Jews and Greeks, the educated and the illiterate. Its universality demolishes every human hierarchy while its particularity makes each recipient unique.

The writer of Hebrews develops *klēsis* as both positional and directional. Believers share in a heavenly calling, but this calling also propels them forward—toward maturity, toward the promised rest, toward the city with foundations whose architect and builder is God. Peter writes of a holy calling, one that demands separation from former ignorance and conformity to the character of the one who called them out of darkness into marvelous light. This calling isn't static status; it's dynamic transformation. James speaks of those called to inherit the kingdom, while John's Revelation describes the called, chosen, and faithful who accompany the Lamb.

The ethical implications are staggering. Paul tells the Ephesians to walk worthy of the calling they have received, with all humility and gentleness, bearing with one another in love. The calling creates both identity and responsibility. To the Thessalonians, he writes that God calls believers into his own kingdom and glory—the calling is eschatological, pointing toward ultimate destiny while shaping present conduct. The Philippian believers are told to let their manner of life be worthy of the gospel, standing firm in one spirit. The calling unifies diverse individuals into a single body while simultaneously giving each member unique purpose. Paul's metaphor of spiritual gifts emerges from this understanding: each person called, each calling specific.

Here's what makes *klēsis* particularly striking: modern sociology recognizes what Paul intuited—identity shapes behavior more powerfully than rules ever could. When researchers study behavioral change, they find that people who see themselves differently act differently. The most effective interventions don't focus on modifying actions but on transforming self-concept. Paul understood this two millennia before social psychology articulated it. By framing Christian life as response to calling rather than mere adherence to law, he tapped into something fundamental about human nature. We don't first decide to act rightly and then become righteous; we're called righteous and begin to live into that identity. The calling precedes and enables the transformation. This is why Paul can write with such confidence about being convinced that he who began a good work will be faithful to complete it—the calling guarantees the outcome because the one who calls also empowers the called. The summons itself contains the resources for answering.

**Key Scriptures:** Romans 8:28, 1 Corinthians 1:26-27, Ephesians 4:1-4, 1 Thessalonians 2:12, Hebrews 3:1, 1 Peter 2:9, Philippians 3:14, 2 Timothy 1:9
