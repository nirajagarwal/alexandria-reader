# Anastasis (Ἀνάστασις)
*Resurrection*

The Greeks knew *anastasis* as a general term for standing up or rising — a person getting out of bed, a nation rising from defeat, a building being erected. The word combined *ana* (up) and *stasis* (standing), suggesting simple vertical movement. But when the Gospel writers reached for language to describe what happened to Jesus on the third day, they transformed this mundane verb into the hinge of human history. What had meant merely "to stand up" became the declaration that death itself had been stood up against and defeated.

The Septuagint had prepared the ground. When the translators needed Greek words for Hebrew concepts of resurrection, they chose *anastasis* and its verb form *anistēmi* to render passages like Job's confident assertion that his Redeemer lives and that he would stand upon the earth at the last. Isaiah's promise that the dead would live and bodies would rise found expression in this same vocabulary. By the first century, *anastasis* carried freight that no classical Greek writer could have imagined — the hope that death was not the final word, that God would one day raise the righteous to renewed life.

Jesus made the concept personal and immediate. When Martha told him that she knew her brother Lazarus would rise again in the resurrection at the last day, Jesus responded not with theological correction but with identification: "I am the resurrection and the life." Not "I will bring about resurrection" or "I believe in resurrection," but "I am anastasis." The abstract hope became embodied. Then he demonstrated what he meant by calling Lazarus forth from the tomb, though this was only a preview, a temporary revival that pointed toward something greater.

Paul built his entire theological framework on *anastasis*. His letters return to resurrection again and again, not as one doctrine among many but as the foundation on which everything else stands. Writing to the Corinthians, he declared that if Christ has not been raised, Christian faith is futile and believers are still in their sins. The resurrection of Jesus was not merely a wonder to marvel at but the firstfruits of a harvest that would include all who belong to him. Paul's conversion on the Damascus road hinged on encountering the risen Christ, and his subsequent ministry proclaimed *anastasis* as the vindication of Jesus's claims and the guarantee of believers' future hope.

The New Testament writers distinguished between different types of rising. Lazarus experienced *anastasis*, but he died again. Jairus's daughter was raised, but she would one day return to the grave. Jesus's resurrection was qualitatively different — not resuscitation but transformation into a glorified, imperishable body. Paul labored to explain this mystery: the resurrection body is sown in dishonor but raised in glory, sown in weakness but raised in power. It is a physical body — Jesus ate fish and invited Thomas to touch his wounds — yet it is also transformed, capable of appearing in locked rooms and ascending to heaven. The Greek word that had once meant simply standing up now had to stretch to contain this paradox: absolute continuity with the crucified body (the same Jesus, wounds and all) and absolute transformation (a body that transcends the limitations of mortality).

Here is what few recognize: the concept of bodily resurrection was so foreign to Greek philosophy that Paul's Athenian audience in Acts mocked him when he mentioned *anastasis*. Platonists believed in the immortality of the soul but considered the body a prison to escape, not a vessel to redeem. The Epicureans and Stoics had no category for resurrection at all. By anchoring Christian hope in *anastasis* — physical, bodily resurrection rather than mere spiritual continuation — the apostles were making a claim that struck Greek ears as absurd. They were insisting that matter matters, that God created physical reality good and intends to restore it, not discard it. This is why Paul could speak of groaning inwardly as we wait for the redemption of our bodies, and why John's Apocalypse envisions not souls floating in heaven but a New Jerusalem descending to a renewed earth where resurrected believers will reign. Christianity's most scandalous claim to the ancient world was not that God exists or that souls survive death, but that *anastasis* — the standing up again of actual bodies — was the destiny God had prepared for his people.

**Key Scriptures:** Job 19:25-26, Isaiah 26:19, John 11:23-25, Acts 17:32, Romans 6:5, 1 Corinthians 15:12-22, 1 Corinthians 15:35-44, Philippians 3:10-11, Revelation 21:1-4
