# Apokalypsis (Ἀποκάλυψις)
*Revelation*

The Greek word *apokalypsis* literally means "unveiling" or "disclosure" — the removal of a covering to reveal what was hidden. In classical Greek, it described the simple act of uncovering something concealed, whether a physical object or hidden knowledge. When early Christians adopted this word, they transformed it into something far more profound: the divine act of making known what human wisdom cannot discover. Unlike Greek philosophy's emphasis on rational ascent to truth, biblical revelation presents truth descending — God pulling back the veil between heaven and earth to disclose his purposes, his character, and his plan for creation.

The New Testament uses *apokalypsis* to describe both the process and content of divine disclosure. Paul speaks of receiving the gospel "not from any human source, but through a revelation of Jesus Christ" — emphasizing that salvific truth cannot be reasoned out but must be revealed. In Ephesians, he writes that the mystery of God's will has been made known through revelation, the astonishing truth that Jews and Gentiles together form one body in Christ. This wasn't philosophical speculation but divine unveiling of what had been hidden for ages. The word appears throughout Paul's letters to describe how God makes known his redemptive plan: to the Galatians, revealing his Son; to the Corinthians, disclosing spiritual wisdom that confounds human wisdom; to the Romans, showing his wrath against ungodliness and his righteousness through faith. Each instance emphasizes that what God reveals, humanity could never discover through investigation or intellect alone.

The eschatological dimension of *apokalypsis* runs throughout Scripture. Peter writes of the grace to be brought at the revelation of Jesus Christ, connecting present salvation with future disclosure. Paul tells the Thessalonians that the Lord Jesus will be revealed from heaven in blazing fire, making visible what is currently hidden. This future-oriented revelation doesn't contradict present disclosure but completes it — what God has partially made known in Christ's first coming will be fully unveiled at his return. The tension between already-revealed and not-yet-fully-disclosed runs through biblical theology: believers have received revelation sufficient for faith and practice, yet await the ultimate apocalypse when every hidden thing becomes manifest. First Corinthians captures this paradox: we know in part and prophesy in part, seeing dimly as in a mirror, but one day we will see face to face, will know fully even as we are fully known.

The most famous use of *apokalypsis* titles the final book of Scripture: "The Revelation of Jesus Christ, which God gave him to show his servants what must soon take place." Here the word encompasses both content (what is revealed) and process (the act of revealing). John's vision on Patmos unveils cosmic warfare, divine judgment, the ultimate triumph of the Lamb, and the restoration of all things — truths that exist whether or not they are disclosed, but which humans cannot perceive without God's initiative to make them visible. The book's bizarre imagery and cryptic symbolism actually underscore revelation's nature: these are not truths humans could invent but divine disclosures requiring interpretation, Spirit-illumination, and humble reception. Apocalyptic literature doesn't conceal truth but reveals it in forms that require faith and wisdom to comprehend.

Modern usage has twisted *apocalypse* to mean catastrophic destruction, losing the word's essential meaning of disclosure. Yet neuroscience and cognitive psychology increasingly recognize what Scripture long taught: humans are remarkably blind to truths right before them, requiring external perspective to see clearly. We operate within cognitive frameworks that filter reality, often missing what doesn't fit our expectations. The biblical concept of revelation acknowledges this human limitation while offering something deeper than perspective-shifting — divine initiative that discloses what we lack not just the framework but the capacity to perceive. Where Greek philosophy promised wisdom through rational inquiry, Scripture insists that the most vital truths require God to pull back the curtain. The gospel itself is *apokalypsis* — not discovered but disclosed, not achieved but received, making visible what remains hidden to those who consider themselves wise.

**Key Scriptures:** Galatians 1:12, Ephesians 1:17, 3:3, Romans 1:17-18, 1 Corinthians 13:12, 1 Peter 1:7, 2 Thessalonians 1:7, Revelation 1:1
