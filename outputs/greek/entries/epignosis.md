# Epignōsis (Ἐπίγνωσις)
*Full Knowledge*

When Paul prays for believers to grow in *epignōsis*, he reaches for a word that classical Greek writers used to distinguish between casual acquaintance and genuine understanding. The prefix *epi-* intensifies the root *gnōsis* (knowledge), suggesting not merely information about something, but experiential recognition, personal encounter, discernment that transforms. In secular Greek literature, *epignōsis* appeared in legal contexts to describe a judge's thorough examination of evidence, in philosophical texts to denote the soul's direct apprehension of truth, and in medical writings to indicate a physician's conclusive diagnosis. The New Testament writers seized this rich term to describe a knowledge of God that far surpasses intellectual assent—a recognition that reshapes identity and purpose.

Paul's letters particularly emphasize *epignōsis* as the goal of Christian maturity. Writing to the Colossians, he prays they might be filled with the knowledge of God's will through all spiritual wisdom and understanding. This isn't abstract theology but practical wisdom that bears fruit in every good work. To the Ephesians, he asks that God would give them a spirit of wisdom and revelation in the knowledge of Christ, that the eyes of their hearts would be enlightened. The repetition is deliberate: this knowledge engages the whole person, illuminating not just the mind but the deepest affections and motivations. When Paul describes his own dramatic conversion, he speaks of being apprehended by Christ Jesus—the verb suggesting he was seized, possessed, known completely before he could claim to know. The apostle's subsequent lifelong pursuit was to know Christ and the power of his resurrection, counting all previous achievements as loss compared to the surpassing worth of this knowledge.

Peter echoes this emphasis in his second letter, opening with grace and peace multiplied through the knowledge of God and of Jesus our Lord. He warns against those who have escaped the defilements of the world through the knowledge of the Lord and Savior Jesus Christ, only to become entangled again—suggesting that true *epignōsis* produces lasting transformation, not temporary religious experience. The writer of Hebrews uses the term more sparingly but pointedly, warning that it's impossible for those who have once been enlightened and have tasted the goodness of God's word to be restored again to repentance if they fall away. This sobering passage underscores that *epignōsis* carries moral weight—to know God fully is to be accountable to that knowledge.

This full knowledge fundamentally differs from mere information accumulation. The Old Testament background is crucial here: the Hebrew *yada* (to know) encompassed intimate relationship, covenantal bond, even sexual union between spouses. When the Septuagint translators rendered passages about knowing God, they chose words from the *gnōsis* family but understood them through Hebraic categories of relationship and commitment. Paul inherits this rich tradition, insisting that knowledge of God must produce love, since knowledge alone puffs up while love builds up. The one who thinks he knows something does not yet know as he ought to know, but whoever loves God is known by him. The direction matters: God's prior knowledge of us makes possible and shapes our knowledge of him. We don't discover God through intellectual achievement but recognize him because he has first revealed himself and claimed us as his own.

Contemporary neuroscience offers a fascinating parallel to this biblical concept. Research distinguishes between semantic memory (facts about the world) and episodic memory (personal experiences), but more significantly between explicit knowledge (consciously accessible information) and implicit knowledge (embodied understanding that shapes behavior without conscious awareness). Studies of expert performers—master chess players, veteran surgeons, accomplished musicians—reveal that genuine mastery involves something beyond memorized rules. Their knowledge is "compiled" into intuitive recognition, pattern detection that operates faster than conscious reasoning. Biblical *epignōsis* suggests something similar but far more profound: knowledge of God that becomes so integral to one's identity that it guides perception, shapes desires, and directs action at levels deeper than deliberate decision-making. This is why Paul can speak of being transformed by the renewal of the mind, able to discern God's will not through laborious analysis but through Spirit-formed intuition. The Christian life aims toward this full knowledge—not information about God, but recognition of his presence, character, and purposes woven into the very fabric of our consciousness.

**Key Scriptures:** Colossians 1:9-10, Ephesians 1:17-18, Philippians 3:8-10, 2 Peter 1:2-3, 2 Peter 2:20, Hebrews 10:26, 1 Corinthians 8:1-3, Romans 12:2
