# Chronos (Χρόνος)
*Time*

In classical Greek, *chronos* meant simply "time" — the ticking of the clock, the passage of hours and days, the chronological sequence that governs mortal existence. The Greeks had another word for time, *kairos*, which spoke of opportune moments and qualitative timing, but *chronos* marked duration itself: measurable, relentless, the medium in which human life unfolds. When biblical writers adopted this word, they preserved its quantitative sense — time as duration, as the container of events — but they also set it within a theological framework where time itself has an origin, purpose, and end.

The New Testament uses *chronos* primarily for periods and durations. Luke dates Jesus' ministry with chronological precision: in the fifteenth year of Tiberius Caesar's reign, John the Baptist appears in the wilderness. Paul speaks of times and seasons when discussing Christ's return, measuring the intervals that shape history. The writer of Hebrews notes that God spoke to the ancestors at many times and in various ways, marking the progressive unfolding of revelation across centuries. In Revelation, John describes angels declaring that there will be no more delay — the Greek literally reads "no more *chronos*," suggesting not merely the cessation of waiting but the end of time as we know it.

What distinguishes biblical *chronos* from its classical counterpart is its theological boundaries. Time is not eternal or cyclical in Scripture; it has a beginning and moves toward consummation. Paul writes that before the ages began, God chose us in Christ and destined us for adoption. The phrase "before time eternal" appears in Titus, suggesting that God's purposes transcend the chronological framework that governs creation. Yet within this framework, time matters profoundly. Christ came in the fullness of time, Paul tells the Galatians — not just at an opportune moment, but when the chronological preparation of history reached its appointed completion. Peter writes that with the Lord, a day is like a thousand years and a thousand years like a day, not to suggest God's indifference to time but to emphasize his sovereignty over it.

The early Christians lived with acute awareness of time's theological significance. They marked time by Jesus' death and resurrection, creating a new calendar of meaning. Paul urged believers to redeem the time, to purchase back the days because the times were evil. James warned that life itself is but a mist appearing briefly then vanishing — a chronological humility that should shape how we spend our hours. The New Testament presents time as a stewardship, a finite resource given for eternal purposes. We live within *chronos* but with eyes toward eternity, making our days count because they are numbered.

Modern physics reveals something ancient theology intuited: time is not absolute but relative, woven into the fabric of space itself. Einstein demonstrated that time passes differently depending on velocity and gravity — that the "now" we experience is local, not universal. Near the speed of light or in intense gravitational fields, time literally slows down. This means the biblical claim that God exists outside time is not merely poetic but potentially descriptive of reality's structure. If time is part of creation rather than its container, then the Creator necessarily transcends it, viewing all moments simultaneously. The biblical writers lacked relativity theory, yet they grasped that God relates to time differently than we do — that what for us is sequential duration may be, for him, an eternal present. When John writes that with God there is no shadow cast by turning, he suggests a stability that transcends temporal succession. The ancient Greek philosophers wrestled with time's nature, some arguing it was merely the soul's measurement of motion. Scripture offers a different resolution: time is real but not ultimate, created but meaningful, finite but pregnant with eternal consequence.

**Key Scriptures:** Luke 3:1, 1 Thessalonians 5:1, Hebrews 1:1-2, Revelation 10:6, Galatians 4:4, 2 Peter 3:8, Ephesians 5:16, James 4:14
