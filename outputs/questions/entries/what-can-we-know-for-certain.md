# What can we know for certain?

The search for certainty has driven philosophy since its beginning—not because philosophers are anxious, but because knowledge seems to require it. If we can't know anything for certain, what distinguishes knowledge from lucky guesses? Yet every attempt to find unshakable foundations runs into the same problem: the criteria we use to establish certainty must themselves be certain, launching an infinite regress.

## Why It Matters

Certainty isn't just a philosopher's obsession—it's the foundation of how we trust anything. Science builds on repeatable results. Courts demand proof beyond reasonable doubt. We make life-altering decisions based on what we think we know. If nothing is certain, then medicine is guesswork, history is fiction, and the difference between knowledge and delusion collapses. Conversely, if we demand too much certainty, we may know nothing at all—trapped in skepticism while life demands decisions. What we can know for certain determines what we can know at all.

## Three Approaches

**Descartes** sought absolute certainty through systematic doubt. In his *Meditations* (1641), he questioned everything: the senses deceive, dreams feel real, perhaps an evil demon manipulates all experience. But one thing survived: *cogito, ergo sum*—I think, therefore I am. Even if deceived about everything else, the fact of doubting proves a doubter exists. From this bedrock, Descartes rebuilt knowledge: a benevolent God wouldn't systematically deceive, so clear and distinct perceptions are trustworthy. Mathematics regained certainty, then physics, then the external world.

**Hume** demolished these foundations. In *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748), he argued that all knowledge divides into two kinds: relations of ideas (like mathematics, true by definition) and matters of fact (like "the sun will rise tomorrow"). We can be certain about relations of ideas—2+2=4 necessarily. But matters of fact? Never. We believe the sun will rise because it always has, but past experience can't logically guarantee future events. Induction—reasoning from observed cases to unobserved ones—has no rational justification. We can't even be certain that causes produce effects; we only observe constant conjunction and infer causation out of habit. Knowledge beyond tautologies rests on custom, not certainty.

**Wittgenstein's** later work reframes the question entirely. In *On Certainty* (written 1950-51), he argues that certainty isn't a super-strong kind of knowledge but a different category altogether. Some propositions ("I have hands," "the Earth existed before I was born") function as hinges—they're not things we know but the framework that makes knowledge possible. We don't check if we have hands before trusting our perception; the assumption is built into our language games. Doubt requires grounds, and grounds require a stable framework. Ultimate certainty isn't achievable because it's not even coherent—what would it mean to doubt everything simultaneously? Some things must be held fast for inquiry to begin.

## The Method

Descartes used radical doubt as a philosophical tool—methodically questioning until hitting bedrock. Hume employed conceptual analysis, dividing knowledge into types and examining whether certainty was possible for each. Wittgenstein practiced ordinary language philosophy, showing how "certainty" actually functions in our lives rather than constructing abstract epistemological systems. These different methods reveal different kinds of answers: foundationalism finds a starting point, skepticism finds limits, and therapeutic philosophy dissolves the problem.

## Where It Breaks

Descartes's God feels like a cheat—using certainty to prove what would guarantee certainty. Hume's skepticism about induction undermines itself: why trust that experience teaches us anything, even that induction fails? Wittgenstein's hinges seem arbitrary—why these frameworks and not others? If certainty is relative to a language game, does that make all frameworks equally valid? Each approach either demands too much (absolute foundations) or settles for too little (pragmatic acceptance).

## Living Consequences

Live as a Cartesian and you trust reason above all—mathematics as the model for truth, clear thinking as the path to certainty. Live as a Humean skeptic and you acknowledge that most belief is custom, that certainty is rare, that we act on probabilities not proofs—pragmatic but never dogmatic. Live with Wittgenstein's hinges and you accept that some things can't be doubted without losing the ability to doubt at all—you inhabit your framework rather than trying to transcend it. Each stance changes what counts as intellectual honesty.

## The Question Now

Contemporary epistemology has largely abandoned the search for absolute certainty. Fallibilism—the view that knowledge doesn't require certainty—dominates. We can know things while admitting we might be wrong. But the question mutates: if not certainty, what threshold makes belief count as knowledge? The Gettier problems (1963) showed that even justified true belief isn't sufficient. Current debates focus on epistemic justification, the nature of evidence, and whether knowledge is contextual. The quest for certainty may be over, but the question of what makes knowledge knowledge remains wide open.
