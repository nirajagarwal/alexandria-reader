# What is justice?

The question sounds simple—treating people fairly, giving them what they deserve—until you ask what "fair" means or who decides what people deserve. Every answer creates new problems. Justice might be about equality, but equal outcomes or equal opportunities? It might demand we respect rights, but which rights take priority when they conflict? The question persists because getting it wrong means building societies on foundations that don't hold.

## Why It Matters

How we answer determines everything from tax policy to criminal sentencing to international law. If justice means maximizing happiness, we might justify sacrificing individual rights for collective benefit. If it means protecting property, we'll defend vast inequalities as morally legitimate. If it requires equal respect for persons, we'll dismantle hierarchies others find natural. The question isn't academic—it's the ground beneath debates about healthcare, immigration, punishment, and war. Every political philosophy is ultimately an answer to what justice demands.

## Three Approaches

**Plato** rooted justice in the soul's harmony. In *Republic*, justice isn't primarily about external distribution but internal order—reason ruling spirit and appetite, each part performing its proper function. A just city mirrors this structure: rulers who love wisdom governing, guardians who love honor protecting, producers who love gain creating. Injustice is chaos, one part usurping another's role. The tyrant appears powerful but is enslaved by appetites; the philosopher-king appears to sacrifice but achieves psychic health. Justice isn't a constraint on self-interest—it *is* self-interest rightly understood. We flourish only when properly ordered.

**Rawls** began not with the soul but with fairness. His thought experiment: imagine designing society behind a "veil of ignorance"—you don't know your race, class, abilities, or conception of the good. What principles would rational people choose? First, equal basic liberties for all. Second, social and economic inequalities arranged so they benefit the least advantaged and attach to positions open to all. This "justice as fairness" rejects both utilitarian sacrifice of individuals and libertarian acceptance of luck-based inequality. The principles aren't discovered in human nature but constructed through a fair procedure. Justice is what free and equal persons would agree to under conditions that don't favor any particular interests.

**Nozick** rejected the entire project of distributing goods according to patterns. Justice isn't about end-states but respect for rights. If you acquire property justly (through labor or voluntary exchange) and transfer it justly (no fraud or coercion), the resulting distribution is just—period. Taxation for redistribution is forced labor, taking the fruits of your time without consent. Rawls's difference principle sacrifices liberty for equality. But people aren't resources for collective use. Justice means respecting their separateness. A society might be unequal yet just; another might be equal but unjust if equality came through rights violations. The question isn't "Who should get what?" but "Who may do what to whom?"

## The Method

Plato used dialectic—questioning conventional answers until deeper understanding emerged through conversation. Rawls employed reflective equilibrium—testing principles against our considered judgments, revising both until they cohere. Nozick worked through thought experiments exposing the costs of patterned theories: if everyone loves Wilt Chamberlain's basketball and freely pays to watch, the resulting inequality can't be unjust. Different methods reveal different dimensions—psychology, social contract, rights—but all three demanded consistency between our theories and what we can actually defend under scrutiny.

## Where It Breaks

Plato's organic vision ignores that harmonious souls might still disagree about justice—whose reason rules? Rawls's veil of ignorance seems to build in liberal assumptions; communitarian critics note real people aren't detached from their communities. His principles remain indeterminate in application—how much inequality benefits the least advantaged? Nozick's framework struggles with unchosen inequalities: children born into poverty didn't consent to their starting position. If initial acquisition was unjust (stolen land, slavery), the whole edifice collapses. Each theory handles some cases brilliantly while leaving others unresolved.

## Living Consequences

Believing Plato means working on self-mastery before reforming society—external justice follows internal order. Believing Rawls means judging institutions by how they treat the vulnerable, supporting redistribution as a requirement of fairness. Believing Nozick means resisting paternalism and taxation, defending people's right to keep what they earn even if others suffer. The differences aren't just political—they're about what you owe others, what constitutes a well-lived life, whether inequality itself can be a moral wrong. Your answer shapes whether you see poverty as injustice or misfortune.

## The Question Now

Not settled. Contemporary philosophy splits between those refining Rawlsian frameworks (capabilities approach, luck egalitarianism) and those defending alternatives (libertarianism, care ethics, recognition theory). Global justice adds complications: do we owe the same to distant strangers? Climate change raises questions about intergenerational justice Rawls barely touched. The debate now includes voices arguing justice requires not just distribution but also recognition, participation, and decolonization. The question has grown more complex, not simpler, as we recognize how deeply injustice runs.
