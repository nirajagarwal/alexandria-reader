# Is morality objective?

When we call torture wrong, are we stating a fact about the universe—like saying water freezes at 32°F—or expressing a preference, like saying chocolate tastes better than vanilla? The question of moral objectivity asks whether ethical truths exist independently of what anyone thinks or feels about them. Your answer determines whether moral disagreement is like disputing scientific evidence or arguing about favorite foods.

## Why It Matters

If morality is objective, then moral progress is possible—abolishing slavery wasn't just a change in preferences but a discovery of what was always true. We can genuinely be mistaken about ethics, and cross-cultural moral disagreements have right answers. But if morality is subjective, then no one can claim their values are truer than anyone else's. The Nazi and the saint aren't disagreeing about facts—they're expressing incompatible preferences, and neither is objectively correct. The stakes touch everything from how we justify laws to whether we can condemn atrocities in other cultures.

## Three Approaches

**Plato** anchored morality in the eternal Forms—abstract, perfect ideals that exist beyond the physical world. The Form of the Good is the ultimate reality, more real than any material thing, and moral truths are as objective as mathematical ones. We don't invent justice; we discover it through reason, imperfectly grasping what exists timelessly. A just society mirrors the Form of Justice just as a perfect circle on paper approximates the Form of Circularity. The cave allegory makes this vivid: prisoners mistake shadows for reality, but truth exists in the sunlit world above, waiting to be seen by those who ascend.

**David Hume** demolished this picture. Morality, he argued, comes from sentiment, not reason. Examine murder all you want—you'll find motives, actions, consequences, but no objective property of "wrongness" in the facts themselves. The wrongness is your response, your feeling of disapprobation. "Vice and virtue may be compar'd to sounds, colours, heat and cold, which... are not qualities in objects, but perceptions in the mind." Reason is powerless to motivate action; only passions move us. Moral judgments are expressions of feeling, not descriptions of reality. We say "murder is wrong" like we say "ouch"—reporting our reaction, not discovering a fact.

**Contemporary moral realists** like Derek Parfit push back: moral facts can be objective without existing like physical objects. Mathematical truths are objective—2+2=4 regardless of opinion—yet numbers aren't physical. Similarly, that torture causes unnecessary suffering makes it wrong, and this normative fact holds independent of anyone's beliefs. The objectivity comes from rational relations, not mystical Forms or empirical properties. When moral intuitions converge across cultures—prohibitions on arbitrary killing, duties to children—this suggests we're tracking something real, even if we're often mistaken about its contours.

## The Method

Plato used dialectic—rigorous questioning to reveal contradictions and ascend toward Forms. Hume employed empiricism, asking what we actually observe versus what we project. Contemporary realists use moral phenomenology (examining the character of ethical experience) and argument from convergence (moral agreement suggests shared discovery). Each method reflects its metaphysics: Plato seeks transcendent truths, Hume restricts himself to observable phenomena, realists analyze normative concepts.

## Where It Breaks

Plato can't explain moral progress if Forms are eternal—shouldn't we always have known slavery was wrong? Hume can't account for moral error in a deep sense—if morality is just feeling, how can whole cultures be wrong about slavery rather than just different? Realists face the ontological puzzle: if moral facts aren't physical, where do they exist and how do we access them? And moral disagreement persists: if morality is objective, why don't we converge like we do in science?

## Living Consequences

Believing in objective morality authorizes moral criticism across cultures and eras—you can say slavery was wrong even when societies embraced it. It grounds moral education as teaching truths, not just socializing preferences. But it risks dogmatism: certainty about objective values has fueled persecution. Moral subjectivism makes tolerance easier—who are you to impose your values?—but struggles to condemn atrocities. If morality is just preference, "I prefer not to be tortured" carries no more weight than "I prefer vanilla." Most people live as if morality matters objectively while acknowledging the philosophical difficulty of proving it.

## The Question Now

Still fiercely contested. Moral realism has regained ground against mid-20th-century emotivism, with philosophers like Thomas Nagel and Russ Shafer-Landau defending objective values. But metaethical debate continues: error theorists argue moral statements purport to be objective but are all false; constructivists locate objectivity in idealized procedures rather than mind-independent facts. Neuroscience complicates things—if moral judgments emerge from evolved emotional responses, does that vindicate Hume or just explain how we access objective truths? The question remains open because no position escapes serious objections.
