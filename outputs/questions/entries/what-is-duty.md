# What is duty?

Why do we sometimes act against our desires, our interests, even our happiness—and feel we *should*? Duty names this strange compulsion: the sense that some actions are required of us regardless of what we want or what benefits us. It's the pull of obligation when inclination points elsewhere, the voice that says "you must" when everything else says "you needn't."

## Why It Matters

The question of duty cuts to the core of moral agency. If morality is just enlightened self-interest or social conditioning, duty dissolves into calculation or habit. But if duty is real—if some acts are genuinely required independent of consequences or desires—then we face harder questions: What grounds this obligation? How do we know what duty demands? And why should we obey it when every incentive points the other way? Your answer determines whether morality has authority or is merely useful fiction.

## Three Approaches

**Kant** makes duty the foundation of morality itself. In the *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*, he argues that only actions done from duty—not from inclination, sympathy, or self-interest—have genuine moral worth. A shopkeeper who's honest because honesty pays acts prudently, not morally. A friend who helps from affection acts kindly, but not from duty. Duty emerges when we act from respect for the moral law itself: the categorical imperative that commands universally, unconditionally. "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law." Duty isn't external imposition but self-legislation—practical reason binding itself. We're autonomous precisely when we obey the law we give ourselves. The tension isn't between duty and freedom, but between duty and heteronomy: being determined by forces outside reason.

**Confucius** locates duty in role relationships, not abstract law. In the *Analects*, moral life centers on *li* (ritual propriety) and *ren* (humaneness) expressed through specific relational obligations. You have duties as child to parent, subject to ruler, friend to friend—five cardinal relationships that structure social existence. But these aren't arbitrary rules. Duty emerges from cultivating virtue within relationships: filial piety (*xiao*), loyalty, reciprocity. The junzi (exemplary person) fulfills duty not from grim obligation but from developed character that finds fulfillment in proper conduct. Duty is inseparable from who you are in relation to others.

**Mill** challenges whether duty should be divorced from consequences. In *Utilitarianism*, he argues that duty ultimately derives from the principle of utility: maximizing happiness for the greatest number. We recognize duties—promise-keeping, honesty, justice—because social happiness depends on them. These "secondary principles" are heuristics pointing toward utility, not competing authorities. When duties conflict (tell the truth or save a life?), utility decides. Mill preserves duty's practical force while grounding it in something comprehensible: human welfare. Duty isn't mysterious obligation but practical necessity for flourishing.

## The Method

Kant proceeds through pure practical reason, seeking what must be true for morality to be possible. His is transcendental argument: if morality binds, it must be categorical, not hypothetical; if categorical, it must come from reason itself. Confucius works through exemplars, analogies, and situational wisdom—showing how virtue develops through practice and observation. Mill employs consequentialist analysis, examining what actually produces human happiness and deriving duties as means to that end. The methods reveal their assumptions: Kant trusts reason to generate content, Confucius trusts tradition and cultivation, Mill trusts empirical observation of human welfare.

## Where It Breaks

Kant's abstract universalism struggles with concrete obligations. Can you derive specific duties from the categorical imperative alone? Critics note it either generates trivial results or smuggles in content from elsewhere. The focus on motive over outcome seems to make morality irrelevant to actual human welfare. Confucius faces the opposite problem: role duties can ossify into oppression. When the relationship itself is unjust (slavery, patriarchy), cultivating virtue within it perpetuates wrong. And what happens when roles conflict? Mill's utilitarianism turns duty into calculation, but this seems to miss something—the sense that we owe specific people specific things regardless of aggregate welfare, that obligations aren't fungible.

## Living Consequences

Believing in Kantian duty means treating moral requirements as non-negotiable, even when inconvenient or costly. It elevates consistency and principle over consequences and relationships. Confucian duty embeds you in a web of specific obligations that give life structure and meaning, but may subordinate individual autonomy to social harmony. Millian duty makes you responsible for outcomes, turning every choice into a calculation about human welfare—potentially paralyzing or liberating, depending on how you bear that weight. Each answer shapes what you feel bound by and what you can excuse.

## The Question Now

Contemporary ethics fragments Kant's unified duty into plural, sometimes competing obligations: deontological duties, role responsibilities, care ethics, situational demands. Virtue ethics revives Confucian insights about character and practice. Consequentialism refines Mill's calculus with expected utility and decision theory. But the core tension persists: Is duty something we discover or create? Does it transcend cultural context or emerge from it? Should we trust the feeling of obligation when it conflicts with reason, welfare, or authenticity? We still haven't settled whether duty is bedrock or construction—whether it grounds morality or requires grounding itself.
