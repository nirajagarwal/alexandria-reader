# Does God exist?

No question has generated more arguments, split more philosophers, or spawned more positions than this one. Not because it's vague—the question is clear enough—but because everything depends on what you count as an answer. Proof? Experience? Faith? And what counts as "God"—a person, a principle, the ground of being itself?

## Why It Matters

This isn't just theoretical. Your answer shapes whether the universe has purpose or is indifferent, whether morality has cosmic grounding or is human invention, whether death is transition or annihilation, whether suffering has meaning or is simply what happens. The question cuts to whether we live in a cosmos or a chaos, whether our longing for transcendence points toward something real or is just neurons misfiring. Every other philosophical question—meaning, morality, knowledge, freedom—takes a different form depending on how you answer this one.

## Three Approaches

**Thomas Aquinas** thought the question could be answered by reason alone. His Five Ways—arguments from motion, causation, contingency, gradation, and design—all follow the same pattern: trace the world's features back to what must ground them. Things move, so there must be an unmoved mover. Things are caused, so there must be an uncaused cause. Things exist contingently, so there must be a necessary being. For Aquinas, "God" names whatever plays these explanatory roles—pure actuality, the condition for everything else's possibility. This isn't the God of prayer and worship extracted from revelation; it's the God that natural theology discovers at the end of arguments. The God philosophy can reach.

**David Hume** dismantled natural theology from the inside. His Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion doesn't deny God—it denies we can know anything about God through reason. The design argument? Our only experience of design comes from human artifacts, and extrapolating from pocket watches to universes is absurd. We've never seen universes being made. The cosmological arguments? They assume that infinite regress needs stopping, but why? And even if it does, why must the stopper be God rather than matter or chance? Every argument for God, Hume shows, depends on analogies that don't hold or assumptions we can't justify. The question isn't answered—it's exposed as unanswerable by philosophical means.

**Søren Kierkegaard** rejected the whole premise that God's existence is a philosophical question. For Kierkegaard, treating God as a hypothesis to be proved or disproved misses what "God" means. God isn't an object you discover at the end of an argument—God is the infinite qualitative difference you encounter in existence itself. The question isn't "Does God exist?" but "Will you commit?" Faith isn't the conclusion of reasoning; it's a leap beyond reason, a subjective passion in the face of objective uncertainty. Proofs don't produce faith—they produce intellectual assent, which is precisely what faith is not. The real question is whether you'll stake your life on the absurd, not whether you can demonstrate the probable.

## The Method

Aquinas uses metaphysical analysis—finding what must be true to explain what we observe. Hume employs skeptical examination—exposing hidden assumptions and unjustified leaps in reasoning. Kierkegaard uses existential phenomenology—describing the actual lived experience of confronting the divine. The disagreement isn't just about the answer; it's about what kind of question this is. Is it metaphysical (about what exists), epistemological (about what we can know), or existential (about how we live)?

## Where It Breaks

Aquinas's arguments assume that explanatory chains need stopping and that whatever stops them must be God—but neither assumption is self-evident. Hume's skepticism, taken seriously, destroys not just theology but science too—if you can't reason from observed patterns to causes, empirical knowledge collapses. Kierkegaard's leap sidesteps the question entirely: if faith is beyond reason, there's nothing to debate, but also no way to distinguish genuine encounter from self-deception. Each position solves problems by creating others.

## Living Consequences

Aquinas's approach makes the universe intelligible—everything has reasons, reality is ordered, and philosophy can reach truth about ultimate things. But it risks making God distant, a metaphysical principle rather than a living presence. Hume's skepticism frees you from dogma and false certainty but leaves you unable to affirm anything beyond immediate experience. Kierkegaard's faith is passionately personal but isolated—if God is encountered only in subjective inwardness, you can't give reasons or share the ground of your most important commitment.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy has mostly moved on. Analytic philosophers debate fine-grained versions of classical arguments (modal ontological arguments, fine-tuning, consciousness as evidence) but remain divided along predictable lines. Continental philosophy treats "God" as a question about meaning-making, not existence. The real action is elsewhere: in philosophy of religion exploring what it would mean for God to exist, in natural theology updated with contemporary physics, in negative theology that says what God is not. The question hasn't been answered. It's fragmented into more specific questions, each of which generates its own debates.
