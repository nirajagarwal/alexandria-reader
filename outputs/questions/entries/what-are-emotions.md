# What are emotions?

Are emotions things that happen to us—storms that sweep through and leave—or are they judgments we make about the world? Do they cloud reason or constitute a form of understanding? Whether emotions are rational or irrational, cognitive or merely physiological, shapes everything from how we trust ourselves to whether we should suppress what we feel or listen to it.

## Why It Matters

How we understand emotions determines whether we see them as reliable guides or obstacles to overcome. If emotions are merely bodily disturbances—adrenaline, heart rate, trembling—then managing them means controlling physical responses. But if they're judgments about what matters, then feeling angry at injustice or afraid of real danger is a form of knowing. This question cuts to whether emotions belong in ethics, whether they can be educated, and whether a life of pure reason is even coherent or desirable.

## Three Approaches

**Stoicism's Epictetus** held that emotions are judgments—and specifically, false ones. When you feel fear, you're judging that something bad is about to happen to you. When you feel grief, you're judging that you've lost something good. But for the Stoics, only virtue is truly good and only vice truly bad. External events—death, loss, insult—are "indifferents." Emotions arise from mistaken valuations of indifferents as if they were genuinely good or bad. In the *Discourses*, Epictetus argues that when Medea kills her children, she doesn't act against reason but from a false judgment: that revenge matters more than virtue. Emotions aren't separate from reason; they are reason gone wrong. The solution isn't suppression but correction—training yourself to judge rightly, to care only about what's actually within your control. Get the philosophy right, and the emotions follow.

**William James** reversed the common-sense view entirely. In "What is an Emotion?" (1884), he argued that we don't cry because we're sad or run because we're afraid. We're sad *because* we cry, afraid *because* we run. Emotions are our awareness of bodily changes: increased heart rate, muscle tension, trembling, tears. See a bear, and your body responds instantly—adrenaline floods your system, you run. The feeling of fear is your perception of these physiological changes. Emotions aren't separate mental events that cause physical responses; they *are* the perception of physical responses. This explained why emotions feel so visceral, so beyond our rational control. We can't simply decide to stop being afraid any more than we can decide to stop our heart from racing.

**Martha Nussbaum**, in *Upheavals of Thought* (2001), argues emotions are intelligent judgments about what we value. When you grieve your friend's death, you're not making a Stoic error—you're recognizing their genuine importance to your flourishing. When you feel compassion for a stranger's suffering, you're perceiving their humanity and vulnerability. Emotions have intentional content: they're *about* something, directed at objects, involving beliefs and appraisals. Fear judges something dangerous, anger judges a wrong, love recognizes value. But unlike Epictetus, Nussbaum sees many emotional judgments as correct. The mother who grieves her child isn't deluded about what matters. Emotions can be educated, refined, appropriate or inappropriate—which means they're cognitive achievements, not mere feelings.

## The Method

Epictetus worked through dialectic and practical exercises—examining beliefs, testing judgments, training responses through hypothetical scenarios. James used introspection and physiological evidence, asking what remains if you subtract all bodily sensations from an emotion. Nussbaum employs conceptual analysis and literary examples, showing how emotions involve complex narratives about value and vulnerability. The clash of methods reveals the question's depth: Is this fundamentally about logic (Stoics), biology (James), or meaning (Nussbaum)?

## Where It Breaks

The Stoics struggle with emotions that seem correct: Is compassion for suffering really a mistake? James can't explain why the same bodily state (arousal) yields different emotions depending on context—why racing heart plus dark alley equals fear but racing heart plus attractive person equals desire. Nussbaum must account for emotions that persist despite intellectual recognition they're mistaken—knowing your plane flight is safe but still feeling terrified, or grieving someone you believe is in a better place.

## Living Consequences

If Epictetus is right, emotional training is intellectual training—philosophy as therapy, reason as the path to peace. If James is right, managing emotions means managing bodies: exercise, breath control, physical posture shape what you feel. If Nussbaum is right, emotions demand attention as forms of moral perception—listening to your anger might reveal injustice you've been rationalizing away. The stakes are whether you trust your emotional life or see it as something to overcome.

## The Question Now

Contemporary affective neuroscience shows emotions involve both physiological responses and cognitive appraisals—neither James nor Nussbaum had it completely right. Lisa Feldman Barrett's theory of constructed emotion suggests the brain predicts and interprets bodily states based on context and past experience. But the normative question persists: Are emotions reliable guides to what matters, or sophisticated instruments that require careful calibration? The debate has shifted from "what are emotions?" to "which emotions should we trust, and when?"
