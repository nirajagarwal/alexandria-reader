# What is the state?

Every government claims authority to tax, regulate, punish, and kill. But what makes a state different from an armed gang that controls territory? Is it consent, force, reason, or something else? The question matters because how we answer determines whether obedience is rational, whether revolution is justified, and what legitimacy even means.

## Why It Matters

The state is the only institution we accept as having the right to use violence. It can imprison, execute, wage war—and we call this legitimate rather than criminal. But legitimacy is not self-evident. Millions have died in conflicts over what makes authority rightful: divine right, popular consent, historical destiny, pure force. The question cuts to power's deepest nerve: when is obedience justified, and when is it submission to organized coercion? Political philosophy lives or dies on this question.

## Three Approaches

**Hobbes's answer:** The state is an artificial person created by covenant to escape the war of all against all. Without a sovereign power—a "Leviathan"—life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." We authorize the state by surrendering our natural right to self-preservation to one absolute authority. The sovereign's legitimacy comes not from being just or wise but from being powerful enough to keep peace. Any state, however tyrannical, is better than civil war. The social contract is not historical fact but logical necessity: we need the state to avoid mutual destruction.

**Rousseau's answer:** The state is legitimate only when it embodies the general will—the common good that citizens as a collective body would choose. The social contract transforms subjects into citizens who are both sovereign and subject, ruling and ruled simultaneously. Laws are legitimate when they express what we would will for ourselves as equal members of the body politic, not what a king or parliament imposes. Rousseau's state isn't Hobbes's peace-keeper but moral transformation: it makes freedom possible by replacing individual self-interest with civic virtue. "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains"—the state can either forge new chains or make freedom real.

**Weber's answer:** The state is the organization that successfully claims the monopoly on legitimate violence within a territory. Legitimacy comes in three forms: traditional (custom, divine right), charismatic (extraordinary leaders), and legal-rational (bureaucratic rules). But all legitimacy is belief—the state exists because enough people believe it has the right to command. Strip away ideology and the state is simply organized coercion that people accept as rightful. Weber doesn't ask if states should exist, only how they actually maintain authority. This is sociology, not justification.

## The Method

Hobbes uses thought experiments—imagine a state of nature—to reveal what the state prevents. Rousseau employs normative political theory: what would a legitimate state look like? Weber practices empirical sociology: observe what states actually do and how authority functions. The difference matters: Hobbes and Rousseau ask what makes states legitimate; Weber asks how states maintain the belief in legitimacy. One is philosophical justification, the other social explanation.

## Where It Breaks

Hobbes's absolute sovereignty justifies tyranny—any government is better than none. Critics ask: how does escaping one threat (civil war) justify submitting to another (despotism)? Rousseau's general will is dangerously vague—how do we know what it is? Robespierre claimed to embody it during the Terror. Weber's definition is purely descriptive—it tells us nothing about whether we should obey. If legitimacy is just successful belief, then effective propaganda makes right. None of these answers satisfactorily distinguishes the state from sophisticated gangsterism.

## Living Consequences

If Hobbes is right, revolution is almost never justified—stability trumps justice. If Rousseau is right, you're only free when you participate in self-rule; representative democracy might be a contradiction. If Weber is right, legitimacy is whatever people can be made to believe—citizenship becomes a kind of Stockholm syndrome. Your answer determines whether you vote, resist, emigrate, or submit. It shapes whether you see authority as protection, participation, or pacification.

## The Question Now

Anarchists still argue that the state is unjustified coercion, whatever its form. Liberals defend limited government through consent and rights. Communitarians claim the state embodies collective identity. Meanwhile, globalization challenges Weber's territorial monopoly—who is sovereign in a world of international law, corporations more powerful than nations, and stateless terrorism? The question persists because the state's power grows while its legitimacy becomes harder to ground. We still haven't agreed on what makes commands into authority rather than threats.
