# What makes actions right or wrong?

We know we *should* help someone in danger. We feel stealing is *wrong*, even when no one would catch us. But what grounds these judgments? Are actions right because of their consequences, the principles they follow, or the virtues they express? This question isn't academic—how we answer determines what we owe each other.

### Why It Matters

Every ethical decision assumes an answer. A doctor choosing between treatments, a company deciding whom to hire, someone keeping or breaking a promise—the framework matters. Consequentialists and deontologists can reach opposite conclusions about the same action. The utilitarian calculates suffering prevented; the Kantian asks whether the maxim is universalizable. Get the foundation wrong and your entire moral architecture collapses. The stakes are concrete: this question shapes law, medical ethics, business practices, and every moment you choose between competing duties.

### Three Approaches

**Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill** grounded morality in consequences. An action is right if it produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number, wrong if it increases suffering. Bentham's hedonic calculus tried to quantify pleasure and pain; Mill refined it, distinguishing higher and lower pleasures. Their radical insight: the rightness of an action has nothing to do with intentions or rules—only outcomes matter. Torture the innocent to save thousands? The utilitarian calculates. This coldly practical framework revolutionized ethics by making it measurable, but the willingness to sacrifice individuals for aggregate welfare troubles many.

**Immanuel Kant** rejected consequences entirely. Actions are right when they follow universal moral law—duties we can will everyone to follow. His categorical imperative: act only on maxims you could universalize. Lying is wrong not because it produces bad outcomes but because a world where everyone lies is logically incoherent—lying depends on truth-telling to work. Kant's ethics protects human dignity absolutely: treat people as ends, never merely as means. You cannot torture one to save thousands because persons are not instruments. The elegance is formal: morality emerges from reason itself, binding regardless of desire or circumstance.

**Aristotle** looked to character, not calculation or duty. Right actions express virtue—the settled disposition to feel and act appropriately. The courageous person acts bravely not from calculation or duty but because courage has become their nature. Virtue is the mean between excess and deficiency: courage between cowardice and recklessness, generosity between stinginess and wastefulness. Phronesis—practical wisdom—navigates context: when to be generous, how much courage this situation demands. Right action flows from good character developed through habituation and guided by the excellent person as exemplar.

### The Method

Utilitarians employ empirical calculation—measure outcomes, compare states of affairs, maximize welfare. Kant uses pure reason—test maxims for logical consistency and respect for rational agency. Aristotle observes human flourishing—what traits enable the good life? These methodological differences explain their incompatibility. The utilitarian asks "What works?"; the Kantian "What's consistent?"; the virtue ethicist "What would the excellent person do?" Same moral questions, radically different tools.

### Where It Breaks

Utilitarianism struggles with justice—why not frame the innocent if it prevents riots? The math says yes; intuition screams no. Kant's formalism seems disconnected from actual human life—duties conflict, and pure reason offers no ranking. His rigorism makes lying to murderers asking for victims wrong, which seems absurd. Virtue ethics lacks action-guidance—"be virtuous" doesn't tell you what to do when virtues conflict. Who counts as the virtuous exemplar varies by culture, threatening relativism. Each theory illuminates something vital while leaving dark gaps.

### Living Consequences

Your ethical framework shapes every choice. The utilitarian accepts painful tradeoffs—close the failing school to fund better ones elsewhere. The Kantian refuses to lie even when truth causes harm, because integrity isn't negotiable. The virtue ethicist cultivates character through practice, knowing right action requires wisdom, not just rules. In medicine, these frameworks clash: maximize quality-adjusted life years (utilitarian), respect patient autonomy absolutely (Kantian), or exercise clinical judgment considering the whole person (virtue). The theory you hold determines what you can live with.

### The Question Now

Contemporary ethics often combines approaches. Rossian pluralism acknowledges multiple irreducible duties. Parfit's *On What Matters* argues convergence between consequentialism and Kantianism when properly understood. Care ethics emphasizes relationships over abstract principles. Experimental philosophy tests moral intuitions empirically. But the fundamental divide persists: Do consequences, principles, or character ground rightness? Hybrid theories proliferate, suggesting the question may resist single answers. We need multiple lenses—though that admission may itself answer the question.
