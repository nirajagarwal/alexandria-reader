# What is existence?

To ask "What is existence?" is not to ask what exists—the inventory of being—but what it means for anything to exist at all. Before we can list entities or debate their reality, we must understand the bare fact that they are rather than are not. This question probes the most basic distinction in philosophy: the difference between being and non-being.

## Why It Matters

Every claim we make—scientific, ethical, religious—presupposes that something exists. But existence itself eludes direct observation. We see trees, not their existence. We think thoughts, not the thinking's being. If we can't grasp what existence means, our theories about what exists rest on an unexamined foundation. Different accounts of existence yield different ontologies: sparse worlds with few fundamental entities, or crowded ones with abstract objects, possible worlds, and numbers. The question shapes how we think about God, consciousness, mathematical truth, and whether the future is real.

## Three Approaches

**Parmenides** (5th century BCE) identified existence with being itself—eternal, unchanging, indivisible. His radical claim: what exists must exist necessarily. Change, plurality, and non-being are illusions because genuine existence cannot come into being or cease to be. To think of non-existence is incoherent—you cannot think nothing, therefore nothing is not. This collapses existence into logical necessity, making the empirical world a deception. **Aristotle** rejected this, distinguishing existence as actuality from mere potentiality. A thing exists when it actualizes its form in matter. Existence is not a single concept but ranges across categories—substances exist primarily, properties secondarily through substances. Critically, existence is not a property added to essence. A horse that exists and a horse that doesn't share the same essence; existence is the realization of that essence.

**Kant** demolished the traditional proofs of God by showing existence is not a real predicate—not a property that makes something more complete. "A hundred real thalers" contain no more marks than "a hundred possible thalers." Existence adds nothing to the concept; it asserts the concept's instantiation in reality. This redirected ontology from analyzing existence as a property to understanding existential claims as acts of judgment about what the world contains.

## The Method

Parmenides used logical deduction from the principle of non-contradiction: being is, non-being is not. Aristotle employed conceptual analysis of substance, distinguishing categories of being and modes of existence (actual vs. potential, necessary vs. contingent). Kant turned to transcendental critique, examining not what exists but how we judge that something exists—shifting from metaphysics to epistemology. Each method reflects its philosopher's view: for Parmenides, logic reveals reality; for Aristotle, analyzing our concepts of things reveals being's structure; for Kant, examining our cognitive capacities shows existence is a commitment, not a discovery.

## Where It Breaks

Parmenides explains nothing—if change is illusion, why the illusion? If plurality is false, what generates the appearance? Aristotle's substance ontology struggles with modern physics, where "things" dissolve into fields and processes without clear substantial cores. His distinction between essence and existence works for manufactured objects (the concept of a chair vs. actual chairs) but falters for fundamental particles or abstract entities. Kant's critique clarifies what existence isn't but offers little positive account of what it is. If existence isn't a property, what makes existential claims true or false? And quantum mechanics suggests existence may be observer-dependent, undermining the classical distinction between potential and actual being.

## Living Consequences

If Parmenides is right, change and death are illusions—a consoling but practically inert metaphysics that severs philosophy from life. Aristotle's actualization grounds purpose: to exist well is to realize your nature, giving ethical weight to human flourishing. Kant's view makes existence a stance we take toward the world, not an intrinsic feature—we construct our ontology through judgment, accepting responsibility for what we count as real. This empowers but burdens: if existence is our commitment, we must justify why we populate our world with nations, obligations, meanings. The existentialists radicalized this—for Sartre, human existence precedes essence, making us radically free and responsible for creating meaning.

## The Question Now

Contemporary metaphysics distinguishes existence from being: perhaps everything "is" (fictional characters, numbers, the past) but not everything "exists" (has concrete reality). Debates rage about which things exist fundamentally—particles, fields, properties, relations, structures. Quantum ontology challenges classical notions: do particles exist between measurements? Meinong's jungle of non-existent objects gets rehabilitated in possible worlds semantics. Yet the basic puzzle persists: saying "God exists" or "numbers exist" makes a claim, but about what? The question fragments into specialized disputes while the original mystery—that anything is at all rather than nothing—remains philosophy's most fundamental perplexity.
