# What is a valid argument?

Logic promises to separate good reasoning from bad—to show us when conclusions actually follow from premises and when they just seem to. But what makes an argument work? When can you trust that if the reasons are true, the conclusion must be too?

## Why It Matters

Validity is logic's foundational concept. It's what lets us identify fallacies, construct proofs, and distinguish between persuasive rhetoric and genuine reasoning. Every field that makes claims—science, law, mathematics, everyday debate—depends on knowing when conclusions are forced by their premises versus merely suggested. Get validity wrong and you can't tell sound thinking from skilled manipulation. The difference isn't just academic: invalid arguments kill people. They justify wars, deny medical treatments, convict the innocent. Understanding validity is understanding when inference is trustworthy.

## Aristotle's Discovery

Aristotle invented formal logic by recognizing that arguments have *forms* independent of their content. "All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore Socrates is mortal" works for the same reason "All planets orbit stars; Earth is a planet; therefore Earth orbits a star" works. The form—"All A are B; x is A; therefore x is B"—guarantees the conclusion.

A valid argument, Aristotle showed, is one where the conclusion follows *necessarily* from the premises. Not probably, not usually—*necessarily*. If the premises are true, the conclusion *cannot* be false. This is preservation of truth through logical form. His syllogistic logic catalogued the valid forms: Barbara, Celarent, Darii. Learn the forms, recognize them in real arguments, and you can evaluate reasoning without being distracted by content, emotion, or authority.

## Frege's Revolution

Gottlob Frege rebuilt logic from scratch in the late 19th century, revealing Aristotle's system as far too limited. Real mathematical and scientific reasoning couldn't be captured by syllogisms. Frege's *Begriffsschrift* (1879) introduced modern symbolic logic—quantifiers, variables, complex predicates—that could handle "Every number has a successor" or "There exists a prime between n and 2n."

Frege defined validity formally: an argument is valid when there is no possible interpretation that makes all premises true and the conclusion false. This semantic definition—focused on truth-preservation across all possible worlds rather than Aristotle's forms—became standard. Validity isn't about persuasiveness or even soundness (having true premises). It's about logical structure guaranteeing that truth flows from premises to conclusion. An argument can be valid with false premises: "All cats are immortal; Socrates is a cat; therefore Socrates is immortal" is perfectly valid—just unsound.

## The Method

Both approaches use formalization: translating ordinary language into symbols that make logical structure visible. Aristotle's syllogistic was his notation; Frege's was vastly more powerful. The test for validity differs: Aristotle catalogued valid forms (if your argument matches Barbara, it's valid); Frege asked whether any interpretation could make premises true and conclusion false (if none can, it's valid).

Modern logic adds proof theory: validity can be demonstrated through formal derivations following mechanical rules. Natural deduction, sequent calculus, and other systems let you *construct* valid arguments step by step, each inference licensed by a rule. Soundness and completeness theorems prove these methods work: what's provable matches what's valid.

## Where It Breaks

Validity tells you nothing about whether you should believe the conclusion. Valid arguments can have false conclusions (when premises are false). Sound arguments—valid with true premises—guarantee truth, but determining which premises are actually true falls outside logic.

Ordinary language resists formalization. Does "No student reads every assignment" mean "For all students, there exists some assignment they don't read" or "There exists some assignment that no student reads"? The logical form matters crucially for validity, but natural language is ambiguous. Translation introduces interpretation—and error.

Validity also faces the problem of relevance. "It's raining; therefore 2+2=4" is valid (the conclusion can't be false), but the premise is completely irrelevant. Classical logic permits this; relevance logics attempt to fix it by requiring premises to actually connect to conclusions.

## Living Consequences

Understanding validity transforms how you evaluate claims. Politicians, advertisers, friends—everyone makes arguments. Most are invalid, relying on unstated assumptions, emotional appeals, or confusion between correlation and causation. Recognizing validity means you can't be fooled by arguments that *feel* convincing but don't actually work.

It also reveals the limits of logic. Valid reasoning from false premises yields nothing. Valid reasoning from true premises still requires knowing those premises are true—an empirical, not logical, question. Logic is necessary for good thinking but never sufficient. You need both valid forms and true content.

## The Question Now

Modern logic has expanded far beyond validity. Modal logic handles necessity and possibility; conditional logic addresses "if-then" reasoning; quantum logic questions whether the law of excluded middle always holds. Yet validity remains central: the question of when conclusions genuinely follow from premises.

Contemporary debate centers on alternative logics. Classical logic's validity principles—like explosion (from a contradiction, anything follows)—seem wrong for real reasoning. Paraconsistent logics reject explosion; intuitionistic logic rejects excluded middle; fuzzy logic handles degrees of truth. Each proposes different validity standards. The question isn't settled: we're still discovering what "follows from" really means.
