# What is logic?

Logic is the study of valid reasoning—the principles that separate good arguments from bad ones. It asks: When does a conclusion actually follow from its premises? When are we entitled to say "therefore"? Every time we claim one thing implies another, we're making a logical claim. Get it right, and thought becomes reliable. Get it wrong, and even true premises lead nowhere.

## Why It Matters

Logic is the infrastructure of rational thought. Mathematics depends on it—without logical validity, proofs collapse. Science requires it—experimental reasoning is logical reasoning. Law invokes it—legal arguments stand or fall on logical structure. Even everyday decisions rest on logical inferences: If the train is late and I have a meeting, then I need to call ahead. The difference between "I think this follows" and "this actually follows" is everything. Bad logic doesn't just produce wrong answers—it makes it impossible to recognize when you're wrong.

## Three Approaches

**Aristotle invented formal logic** in the 4th century BCE with his theory of the syllogism. He identified valid argument forms—patterns where true premises guarantee true conclusions. "All humans are mortal; Socrates is human; therefore Socrates is mortal" works not because of its content but its form: All A are B, X is A, therefore X is B. Aristotle catalogued which patterns preserve truth and which don't, creating the first systematic account of deductive validity. His *Prior Analytics* treats logic as a science of demonstration—the method by which we move from what we know to what we can know. For two thousand years, "logic" meant Aristotelian logic.

**Frege revolutionized logic** in 1879 by creating predicate calculus, a symbolic language that could express arguments Aristotle's system couldn't handle. He showed that mathematical reasoning requires a more powerful logic—one that can represent relations, quantify over multiple variables, and nest logical operations. Where Aristotle had "All S are P," Frege had ∀x(S(x) → P(x))—for all x, if x is S, then x is P. His *Begriffsschrift* (concept-script) made logic mathematical, precise, and capable of grounding mathematics itself. Modern logic descends from Frege.

**Brouwer challenged the assumption that logic is universal or timeless** through his intuitionism. He argued that classical logic—with its law of excluded middle (every statement is either true or false)—doesn't apply to mathematics. For infinite collections, we can't assert "either there exists an X or there doesn't" without a constructive proof. Logic, for Brouwer, isn't discovered but constructed by the mathematical mind. Different domains might require different logics. His "The Unreliability of Logical Principles" (1908) opened the possibility that logic itself might be plural, context-dependent, interest-relative.

## The Method

Logic proceeds through formalization—translating ordinary arguments into symbolic structures where validity becomes visible. Aristotle used schematic letters to show that "All A are B" has the same form regardless of content. Frege went further, creating an artificial language where ambiguity disappears and inferential relationships become mechanical. Modern logicians construct formal systems, define syntax and semantics, prove theorems about what the system can and cannot do. The method is mathematical: give precise rules, follow them rigorously, see what follows.

## Where It Breaks

Every logical system faces limits. Gödel proved that any system rich enough to express arithmetic contains true statements it cannot prove—completeness is impossible. The choice of logical system isn't itself settled by logic; we need extra-logical reasons to prefer classical over intuitionist logic, or vice versa. Logic can't determine its own scope. And formal validity is empty—a valid argument with false premises proves nothing about the world. Logic tells you what follows from what, not what's actually true.

## Living Consequences

Take logic seriously and you become harder to fool—you recognize invalid arguments, spot hidden assumptions, demand that conclusions actually follow. But you also face a choice: Is logic discovered (built into reality) or invented (a useful tool we created)? The first makes it absolute, the second makes it revisable. Programmers encode logic in machines. Lawyers deploy it in courts. Scientists rely on it for inference. Anyone who thinks at all is using logic, well or badly. The question is whether you're doing it consciously.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy explores non-classical logics—modal logic for necessity and possibility, relevance logic that rejects irrelevant premises, paraconsistent logic that tolerates contradiction. Cognitive scientists study how humans actually reason (badly, by formal standards). Computer scientists implement logical systems in automated theorem provers. But the foundational question persists: Is there one true logic, or many legitimate ones? Is logic prescriptive (how we should reason) or descriptive (how we do reason)? The field has expanded from Aristotle's 14 valid syllogisms to hundreds of logical systems. Whether that's progress or fragmentation depends on what you think logic is for.
