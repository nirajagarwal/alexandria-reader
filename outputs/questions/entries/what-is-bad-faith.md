# What is bad faith?

To live in bad faith is to lie to yourself about your freedom—to pretend you have no choice when you do, or to claim you're simply following your nature, your role, your circumstances. It's the waiter who becomes nothing but waiter, the coward who insists he was born timid, the anti-Semite who treats his hatred as a fact of nature rather than a choice. Bad faith is how we flee from the terrifying truth that we are radically free.

## Why It Matters

Bad faith isn't simply self-deception—it's a fundamental mode of human existence. How we answer "What is bad faith?" determines whether we can take responsibility for our lives or remain trapped in the comforting lie that our choices were inevitable. It shapes how we judge others: Is the collaborator genuinely helpless, or choosing cowardice? Is the conformist simply following their nature, or fleeing their freedom? The question cuts through our rationalizations to ask: Are you living honestly, or hiding from yourself?

## Sartre's Answer

Jean-Paul Sartre made bad faith central to existentialism in *Being and Nothingness* (1943). His insight: human consciousness is "condemned to be free"—we have no fixed essence, no predetermined nature. Unlike a stone or a tree, we must continually choose what we are. This freedom is absolute and terrifying, and bad faith is the lie we tell to escape it.

Sartre's famous example: the café waiter who plays at being a waiter too perfectly. His movements are too mechanical, his voice too eager, his attentiveness too complete. He's trying to *be* a waiter the way an inkwell *is* an inkwell—a thing with a fixed nature. But he cannot succeed. Consciousness is always "for-itself" (pour-soi), aware of itself, free to negate what it is. The waiter knows he's playing a role, which means he's not simply identical to it. His very performance reveals the gap between what he is and what he pretends to be.

Bad faith requires a peculiar double-consciousness. You must both know and not-know the truth simultaneously. The woman on a date who leaves her hand in her companion's grasp while pretending to be absorbed in intellectual conversation—she knows what's happening, yet refuses to acknowledge it. She treats her hand as a passive object, as if it belongs to someone else. She is fleeing from the moment of decision: to accept or refuse the advance requires admitting her freedom.

The structure of bad faith is always the same: denying either your *facticity* (your situation, body, past) or your *transcendence* (your freedom to negate and choose). The coward who says "I cannot help being afraid" denies his transcendence—he treats his fear as a fixed fact rather than something he could overcome. The person who claims "I am completely free of all constraints" denies facticity—ignoring that freedom always operates within a situation.

## The Method

Sartre employs phenomenological analysis—examining how consciousness experiences itself. He describes concrete situations in minute detail, showing the internal contradictions in how we relate to ourselves. His method is to catch consciousness in the act of fleeing itself, to make visible the subtle moves by which we hide our freedom. He's not arguing from abstract principles but describing what we actually do: the gestures, the internal dialogue, the split attention that characterizes self-deception.

## Where It Breaks

Sartre's account faces a serious problem: How is bad faith possible? If I know the truth (that I'm free), how can I simultaneously hide it from myself? Traditional lying requires a liar who knows the truth and a dupe who doesn't—but in bad faith, I am both. Sartre tried to solve this by arguing consciousness is "pre-reflective"—we can believe contradictory things on different levels of awareness. But this remains philosophically murky.

Critics also question whether Sartre's examples really demonstrate what he claims. Perhaps the waiter is simply doing his job well, not fleeing his freedom. Maybe we can genuinely have fixed character traits without bad faith. Sartre's insistence on radical, unlimited freedom seems to ignore how genuinely constrained people often are—does the poor person show bad faith in not imagining themselves a millionaire?

## Living Consequences

If Sartre is right, most of what we call "character" or "nature" is bad faith. You cannot honestly say "I'm just not a risk-taker" or "I've always been anxious"—these are choices you continue to make. You must take responsibility for every aspect of your life, including your emotional reactions, your values, your identity. This is exhausting and terrifying, which is precisely why we flee into bad faith.

But it's also liberating. The abused person who realizes they're choosing to stay, the employee who sees they're choosing to obey, the citizen who understands they're choosing complicity—all gain the possibility of choosing differently. Bad faith is comfortable; authenticity requires courage to face your freedom and the responsibility it entails.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy has largely moved away from Sartre's stark formulation. Philosophers of mind question whether his model of consciousness is accurate. Social theorists argue he underestimated how structures genuinely constrain choice. But the question refuses to die: How much of what we tell ourselves is self-deception? When we say "I had no choice," are we ever fully honest?

The language has changed—we speak of rationalization, cognitive dissonance, motivated reasoning—but the problem remains. Are you living in bad faith right now, reading this? The question cuts too deep to dismiss.
