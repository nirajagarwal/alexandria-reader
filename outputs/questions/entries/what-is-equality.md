# What is equality?

The claim that all humans are equal sounds obvious until you ask: equal in what respect? Equal before the law? In worth? In abilities? In what they deserve? Every answer reshapes society—who gets what, who decides, what justice means. The question has produced revolutions and founded nations, but its answer remains elusive.

## Why It Matters

How we define equality determines the structure of our societies. Declare people equal in natural rights and you get liberal democracy. Define equality as equal concern and respect and you might justify redistribution. Insist on equality of opportunity rather than outcome and you get meritocracy. Or argue, as some have, that meaningful equality is impossible given natural differences, and hierarchy becomes defensible. The stakes are civilization itself: what we owe each other, what the state owes citizens, whether existing inequalities are injustices or facts of nature.

## Three Approaches

**Rousseau** made equality foundational but complicated. In the *Second Discourse*, he distinguished natural inequality (differences in strength, intelligence, health) from moral or political inequality (differences in privilege, power, wealth). Natural inequality is inevitable and mostly harmless. Moral inequality becomes pernicious when it loses connection to merit and instead stems from social conventions that allow some to dominate others. His radical move: humans in the state of nature were essentially equal because they lacked the social institutions that create meaningful inequality. Private property, he argued, introduced "mine" and "thine" and with them, inequality that compounds across generations. True equality isn't about making everyone identical—it's about preventing inequalities that destroy freedom.

**Rawls** reimagined equality through a thought experiment. In *A Theory of Justice*, he asked: what principles would people choose behind a "veil of ignorance"—not knowing their own talents, social position, race, gender, or conception of the good life? His answer: they'd choose equal basic liberties for all, and they'd accept economic inequalities only if they benefit the least advantaged (the "difference principle"). This is equality as fairness: not sameness, but a system where inequalities must be justified to those worst off. Rawls transformed the question from "are people equal?" to "what inequalities can free and equal persons reasonably accept?"

**Dworkin** pushed equality toward a different dimension: equal concern and respect. In *Sovereign Virtue*, he argued the state must treat citizens as equals—not necessarily giving them equal resources, but showing equal concern for their lives going well. This generates two principles: people should have equal resources to start, but they're responsible for their choices thereafter. If you prefer expensive wine over beer, you pay the difference—that's your choice. But if you're born with a disability requiring expensive care, society compensates—that's bad luck, not choice. Equality means neutralizing brute luck while respecting choice.

## The Method

Rousseau used historical-hypothetical analysis: imagining humanity's development from natural to civil states to identify where inequality originates. Rawls deployed the social contract tradition through a thought experiment designed to model impartiality. Dworkin used conceptual analysis, distinguishing types of equality and kinds of luck to clarify our intuitions. All three assumed equality is normatively basic—the default that requires no justification—while inequality needs defending. The method matters: different tools for making equality precise produce different equalities.

## Where It Breaks

Rousseau never explained how to achieve equality without totalitarianism—his ideal society in *The Social Contract* seems to require forcing people to be free. Rawls's veil of ignorance assumes people can reason neutrally about justice, but critics note that our deepest values shape what we'd choose even in ignorance. The difference principle might justify vast inequalities if they slightly benefit the worst off—is that really equality? Dworkin's luck-choice distinction collapses under scrutiny: most "choices" are shaped by circumstances beyond our control. Moreover, none of them adequately address whether equality should be local (within societies) or global (between them)—a question that becomes urgent in an interconnected world.

## Living Consequences

Believing in equality commits you to something, but what depends on which equality you believe in. Rousseau's equality suggests suspicion of wealth accumulation and inherited privilege. Rawls's fairness means supporting redistribution that benefits the worst off, even if it constrains your own advantage. Dworkin's equal concern implies compensating for unchosen disadvantage but not subsidizing expensive tastes. Or you might conclude, with critics from the right, that enforced equality violates freedom—that people are entitled to what they earn or inherit, period. The question "what is equality?" becomes "what do I owe strangers?" Your answer shapes your politics, your voting, your charitable giving, your career choices.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy has largely abandoned the idea of finding *the* correct conception of equality. Instead, philosophers like Elizabeth Anderson argue for "democratic equality"—not equal distribution of resources but equal standing as citizens, freedom from oppression and humiliation. Others, like G.A. Cohen, insist any adequate conception must be sensitive to both choice and circumstance. The debate has shifted from "what is equality?" to "which inequalities are compatible with relating as equals?" Meanwhile, global inequality, algorithmic discrimination, and climate injustice raise questions these traditional frameworks struggle to address. The question persists not because we lack answers but because every answer generates new questions about what treating people as equals actually requires.
