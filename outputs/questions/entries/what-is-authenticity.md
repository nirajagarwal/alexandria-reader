# What is authenticity?

To live authentically is to own your existence—to make choices that are genuinely yours rather than borrowed from others, tradition, or social expectation. But what does it mean for a choice to be "yours"? Can you ever fully escape influence? And if authenticity means being true to yourself, how do you know which self is the true one?

## Why It Matters

The question cuts to the core of modern identity. If you're just playing roles—dutiful child, successful professional, good citizen—are you living *your* life or someone else's script? The answer determines whether freedom means anything beyond an abstract possibility. It shapes how we understand responsibility: if your choices aren't really yours, can you be held accountable for them? Authenticity became urgent when traditional sources of meaning—God, king, cosmic order—lost their grip, leaving each person to author their own existence. Get it wrong, and you've wasted the only life you have.

## Three Approaches

**Heidegger** saw inauthenticity (*Uneigentlichkeit*) as the default human condition. In *Being and Time* (1927), he argued we're "thrown" into a world already structured by "the They" (*das Man*)—public opinion, conventional wisdom, inherited norms. The They dictates what "one does": one goes to college, one gets married, one fears death. Authentic existence (*Eigentlichkeit*) emerges only when you confront your own mortality through *Angst*—not fear of something specific, but existential anxiety that reveals you as a finite being facing your "ownmost possibility." Death individualizes you; it's the one thing no one else can do for you. Authenticity means resolutely choosing your possibilities while acknowledging this finitude, rather than fleeing into the comforting anonymity of the They.

**Sartre** radicalized this in *Being and Nothingness* (1943). For him, humans are "condemned to be free"—consciousness creates a gap between what we are and what we do. Bad faith (*mauvaise foi*) is lying to yourself about this freedom. The waiter who over-performs being a waiter, identifying completely with his role, denies his freedom to be otherwise. The woman on a date who treats her companion's advances as inevitable natural forces rather than choices she's complicit in—bad faith again. Authenticity requires acknowledging that you're never identical to any role or essence. You're always choosing, always free, and always responsible. No excuses.

**Kierkegaard** (predating both) framed it through stages of existence in *Either/Or* (1843) and *Fear and Trembling* (1843). The aesthetic life pursues pleasure and avoids commitment—authentic in its passion but ultimately empty. The ethical life accepts universal moral duties—marriage, career, citizenship—but risks losing individuality in conformity. Authentic existence reaches its height in the religious stage, where you stand in absolute relation to the Absolute. Think of Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac: he suspends universal ethics for a singular, non-generalizable relationship with God. This is the "teleological suspension of the ethical"—you become a "knight of faith" by making choices that can't be justified to anyone else, only to yourself before God.

## The Method

Phenomenological analysis of lived experience. Heidegger examines how we actually exist day-to-day: mostly distracted, absorbed in tasks, fleeing anxiety. Sartre uses vivid concrete examples—the waiter, the woman, the homosexual denying his identity—to reveal the structures of self-deception. Kierkegaard employs indirect communication through pseudonymous voices, forcing you to make the choice yourself rather than accepting an argument. None of them prove authenticity is possible; they describe what it would look like and what gets in its way.

## Where It Breaks

The critics ask: Isn't authenticity just another role you're performing? Theodor Adorno called it "jargon"—a pretentious way of privileging your own choices over others'. If you're shaped by language, culture, and history all the way down, where's the "true self" you're supposed to be authentic to? Feminists point out that authenticity theory often assumes an autonomous individual, ignoring how identity is relational, especially for women taught to define themselves through care for others. And Sartre's absolute freedom looks absurd: you're not free to choose your genetic inheritance, your childhood trauma, your economic circumstances. Making those disappear through "radical freedom" is its own form of bad faith.

## Living Consequences

Believing in authenticity makes you suspicious of comfort. Following the crowd becomes a kind of betrayal. It intensifies the burden of choice—you can't blame tradition, authority, or circumstance for who you've become. Relationships get complicated: can you love authentically while also compromising, accommodating, playing the roles that intimacy requires? Professionally, it creates tension between making a living and living truly. Politically, it can lead either to radical individualism (only I can determine my values) or to solidarity with others who've also faced their existential situation honestly. Either way, it makes complacency intolerable.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy has largely moved away from authenticity's existentialist formulation, suspicious of the autonomous self it presumes. Identity is now seen as intersectional, performed, socially constructed—which doesn't eliminate the question but reframes it. You still choose which constructions to inhabit and resist. The ethics of authenticity persists in debates about identity politics: is transitioning an act of becoming your true self, or is that framing itself problematic? Cultural appropriation controversies turn on who has the authority to perform which identities "authentically." The question hasn't been answered; it's been pluralized. Whose authenticity? Authentic to what?
