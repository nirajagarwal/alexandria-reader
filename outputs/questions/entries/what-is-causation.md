# What is causation?

When one billiard ball strikes another and sends it rolling, we say the first caused the second to move. But what makes this *causation* rather than just sequence? What is the metaphysical cement binding cause to effect—or is there any cement at all? This question cuts to the heart of how we understand change, explanation, and our ability to intervene in the world.

## Why It Matters

Our entire framework for understanding reality depends on causation. Science seeks causal laws. We hold people morally responsible because their actions *cause* harm or good. We make predictions, form expectations, design experiments—all presupposing that causes produce effects with some necessity or regularity. If causation is merely habit or convention, the foundations of science and ethics become precarious. If it's real metaphysical glue, we need to explain what it is and how we know it. The stakes include the rationality of induction, the possibility of free will, and whether the universe is fundamentally deterministic or open.

## Three Approaches

**Hume's skeptical regularity:** David Hume demolished the idea that we perceive causation directly. In *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748), he argues we see only constant conjunction—one type of event regularly following another—never the "necessary connection" itself. We observe billiard balls colliding repeatedly and our minds form a habit of expectation, projecting this psychological compulsion onto nature as if it were an objective power. Causation, for Hume, is custom masquerading as metaphysics. The causing event, the effect, and nothing more—no hidden force binding them. This doesn't deny causation's utility; we can still reason about regularities. But it relocates causation from the world to our mental habits, making induction rationally unjustifiable even while psychologically unavoidable.

**Kant's conceptual necessity:** Immanuel Kant accepted Hume's critique of empirical observation but rejected his conclusion. In the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781), causation becomes a category of understanding—not something we discover in experience but something we impose on experience to make it intelligible. The mind structures raw sensation into a temporal sequence where events necessarily follow rules. Causation isn't psychological habit; it's a precondition for having coherent experience at all. We can't perceive a world without causation any more than we can perceive without time. This salvages scientific reasoning but at a cost: causation describes how we must think about phenomena, not how things are in themselves. The cement is real but epistemological, not metaphysical.

**Contemporary counterfactual dependence:** David Lewis's *Counterfactuals* (1973) offers a different strategy: causation as counterfactual dependence across possible worlds. Event C causes E if, had C not occurred, E would not have occurred. The struck billiard ball moves *because* it was struck—in nearby possible worlds where the cue ball misses, the target ball stays put. This preserves Hume's empiricism (no mysterious necessary connections) while recovering causation's objectivity. The causal relation supervenes on patterns of similarity between possible worlds. Critics note problems with overdetermination, preemption, and the metaphysical cost of possible worlds realism. But it captures intuitions about causation as difference-making without appealing to unobservable powers.

## The Method

Hume employed radical empiricism: examine experience to see what's actually there versus what we project. Kant used transcendental argument: identify the conditions necessary for experience itself. Lewis deployed modal logic and possible worlds semantics. These methodological differences reveal why philosophers reach incompatible conclusions—they're not just disagreeing about data but about what counts as philosophical explanation. Empiricists demand observable evidence. Rationalists seek conceptual necessities. Modal metaphysicians construct formal frameworks. The question "what is causation?" becomes partly a question about philosophical method itself.

## Where It Breaks

Hume's regularity view struggles with spurious correlations—night regularly follows day without causing it. Kant's transcendental approach leaves causation's status in the mind-independent world mysterious. Lewis's counterfactuals face technical problems: simultaneous causation, probabilistic causes, causal asymmetry. No account fully explains causal direction (why causes precede effects), causal relevance (which regularities matter), or the felt difference between mere correlation and genuine causation. The question fractures into subquestions about determinism, laws of nature, time's arrow, and whether fundamentally quantum reality even permits classical causation.

## Living Consequences

If Hume is right, your confidence that striking a match causes fire is rationally groundless habit—which doesn't stop you lighting candles but might induce philosophical humility about induction. If Kant is right, causation structures your experience so fundamentally you can't think without it, making science possible but metaphysics limited. If Lewis is right, causation is real but consists in patterns across possible worlds, making your causal judgments depend on similarity relations you intuit. For moral responsibility, the difference matters: are you causally responsible because your action was the difference-maker, because it fits a regular pattern, or because the category of cause-and-effect structures all moral thinking?

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy of causation has exploded into specialized debates: interventionist accounts (Judea Pearl, James Woodward) defining causation through experimental manipulation; process theories (Phil Dowe, Wesley Salmon) seeking causal mechanisms; primitivist views accepting causation as unanalyzable; and eliminativism questioning whether fundamental physics needs causation at all. Quantum mechanics and relativistic constraints complicate matters. The question remains open, but the field has shifted from asking "what is causation?" to "which concept(s) of causation serve which explanatory purposes?" The cement, if there is any, may be plural.
