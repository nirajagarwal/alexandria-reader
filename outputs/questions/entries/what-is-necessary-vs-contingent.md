# What is necessary vs contingent?

Some truths couldn't be otherwise—triangles must have three sides, contradictions must be false. Others just happen to be true—you're reading this now, water freezes at 0°C at sea level. Philosophy calls the first kind necessary, the second contingent. The distinction seems obvious until you try to draw the line: Is it necessary that the laws of physics are what they are? That you exist? That anything exists at all?

## Why It Matters

Everything turns on where you place this boundary. If moral truths are necessary, they bind everyone regardless of culture or opinion. If contingent, morality becomes a human construction. If the laws of nature are necessary, science discovers eternal truths; if contingent, they're just regularities that happen to hold in our universe. The question shapes what counts as genuinely knowable versus what's just local fact. It determines whether philosophy can achieve certainty or must settle for describing how things happen to be.

## Three Approaches

**Leibniz** made the distinction foundational. Necessary truths are those whose denial involves contradiction—they hold "in all possible worlds." Contingent truths are those that could have been false—they're true in our world but false in others. God, being omniscient, knows all possibilities and chose to actualize this world from among them. Why this one? Leibniz's answer: it's the best possible world, where "best" means maximizing variety while maintaining order. Every contingent fact has a sufficient reason—nothing is truly arbitrary—but contingency means alternatives were genuinely possible before God's choice. The laws of physics? Contingent—God could have made different ones. That 2+2=4? Necessary—not even God could make it otherwise without contradiction.

**Kripke** revolutionized the debate by pulling the distinction apart from how we know things. Something can be necessary but knowable only through experience (*a posteriori*), contingent but knowable without experience (*a priori*). His examples: that water is H₂O is necessary—water couldn't be anything else and still be water—but we had to discover it empirically. That the standard meter bar in Paris is one meter long is knowable *a priori* (we defined it that way) but contingent (the bar could have been a different length). Kripke argued necessity concerns metaphysics—how things must be—not epistemology—how we know them. Identity statements about natural kinds ("water is H₂O") and proper names ("Hesperus is Phosphorus") turned out to be necessarily true if true at all, even when discoverable only by investigation.

**Quine** attacked the entire distinction. He argued there's no principled way to separate "true by meaning" from "true by fact." Even logical truths like the law of non-contradiction are confirmed by experience—we keep them because they work, not because they're sacred. We could revise them if the empirical cost became high enough (quantum logic suggested as much). What looks necessary is just what we're most unwilling to give up, and that's a measure of how central it is to our web of belief, not its metaphysical status. The distinction between necessary and contingent, for Quine, is a difference in degree of entrenchment, not kind of truth.

## The Method

Leibniz used possible worlds—asking what must be true across all conceivable scenarios versus what varies between them. Kripke refined this with modal logic and linguistic analysis, showing how reference works for terms that pick out necessary features. Quine took a naturalistic approach, treating all knowledge claims as part of one empirical web, examining how we actually revise beliefs under pressure. The methods divide over whether modal concepts (possibility, necessity) are primitive or reducible to something else—meanings, conventions, degrees of belief.

## Where It Breaks

Leibniz's framework requires accepting possible worlds as real enough to ground truth—but what are they? Mathematical structures? Divine ideas? Kripke's necessary *a posteriori* truths seem right about specific cases but don't explain why certain identities (water = H₂O) are necessary while others (the morning star = Venus) feel contingent even when both are rigid designations. Quine's holism makes revision theoretically possible for any statement but can't explain why revising "2+2=4" seems different in kind from revising "water boils at 100°C"—not just harder, but incoherent in a way physical revision isn't.

## Living Consequences

If you believe some truths are necessary, you believe reality has a fixed structure—there are facts about what must be that no evidence could overturn. This supports moral realism, mathematical platonism, confidence that science discovers rather than constructs. If everything's contingent, you live with radical openness—what seems immutable might just be entrenched habit. Ethics becomes negotiable, logical laws become revisable, nothing is sacred. The difference shows up when you're asked to tolerate moral practices that violate your core commitments: Do you appeal to necessary moral truths or acknowledge they're just your contingent values?

## The Question Now

Contemporary metaphysics still uses possible worlds but debates their nature—are they concrete universes (David Lewis) or abstract representations (Stalnaker)? Philosophy of science treats laws of nature as a live question: governing necessity or cosmic accident? Modal epistemology asks how we know what's possible or necessary—is modal knowledge a priori intuition or informed by science? The distinction itself remains contested—Quine's heirs still push deflationary accounts while modal realists defend its indispensability. We can't do without talking as if some things must be true, but we can't agree on what grounds that "must."
