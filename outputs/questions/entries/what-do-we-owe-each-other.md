# What do we owe each other?

The question cuts to the foundation of social life. Every interaction—from passing a stranger to raising a child—contains implicit claims about obligation. We navigate these claims constantly, but rarely examine what grounds them. The question persists because getting it wrong means either moral tyranny (demanding too much) or moral vacancy (demanding too little).

## Why It Matters

How we answer shapes everything from tax policy to friendship. If we owe each other only non-interference, vast inequalities require no justification. If we owe mutual aid, indifference to suffering becomes culpable. The question underlies debates about healthcare, immigration, wealth redistribution, and the boundaries of personal responsibility. What makes it philosophically urgent is that our intuitions conflict: we feel both that we must help others and that our lives are our own. The tension is not just political but existential—it determines whether we live as isolated atoms or interconnected beings.

## Three Approaches

**Scanlon's contractualism** offers the most systematic modern answer: we owe each other what no one could reasonably reject. His 1998 *What We Owe to Each Other* grounds morality not in consequences or divine commands, but in mutual recognition. A principle is wrong if someone could reasonably reject it as a basis for mutual life. The standard isn't actual agreement but *reasonable* agreement—what matters is whether the rejection itself would be reasonable, given equal concern for all.

This gives specific content to obligations. You can't reasonably reject a principle against lying in important matters—such lies make cooperation impossible. But you *could* reasonably reject a principle requiring you to sacrifice your life for any stranger who would benefit more from living. Contractualism threads between demanding too much and too little by asking what principles people with equal standing could share.

**Levinas's ethics** makes the claim more radical: we owe everything. His 1961 *Totality and Infinity* argues that the other person's face—their vulnerability and mortality—issues an infinite demand. Ethics begins not with autonomy or contracts but with asymmetrical responsibility. You are hostage to the other before any reciprocity, any calculation of desert or fairness. The question isn't what principles we can justify but what the other's existence demands of us.

This isn't contractual exchange but exposure. The other interrupts my freedom, my projects, my self-sufficiency. I am *for* the other before I am for myself. Levinas radicalizes obligation: you cannot limit your responsibility to what seems reasonable or what others will reciprocate. The face of the suffering other accuses you of your enjoyment, your comfort, your being-at-home-in-the-world.

**Confucian role ethics** locates obligation in relationships themselves, not abstract principles. What you owe depends on who you are *to* someone: parent, child, friend, neighbor, stranger. *Ren* (humanity/humaneness) is cultivated through fulfilling these roles properly. The *Analects* emphasize graded obligations—stronger to family than strangers—not as selfishness but as how virtue actually develops. You learn care by caring well for those near you.

This avoids both contractualism's abstraction and Levinas's infinitude. Obligations are concrete, embodied in practices. Filial piety, friendship, ritual propriety—these aren't derived from universal principles but constitute the ethical life. What we owe isn't the same to everyone because relationships aren't the same. The question isn't "what do autonomous individuals owe?" but "what does this relationship require?"

## The Method

Scanlon employs reflective equilibrium—testing principles against cases, revising both until they cohere. His method is *constructivist*: moral truths are built from reason's architecture, not discovered in nature or decreed by authority.

Levinas works phenomenologically, describing ethics as it appears in lived experience. His method is *anti-systematic*—ethics cannot be captured in principles because the other always exceeds what concepts can contain.

Confucian ethics proceeds through exemplars and rituals. Study the sage, practice proper conduct, and understanding emerges through embodied action rather than theoretical deduction.

## Where It Breaks

Scanlon's reasonableness risks circularity: what counts as reasonable depends on background moral assumptions. When does self-interest make rejection reasonable versus selfish? The framework needs content it cannot fully provide from within.

Levinas's infinite obligation becomes paralyzing. If you're responsible for everyone's suffering, you're responsible for nothing effectively. His account also struggles with conflicting obligations—what when two faces make incompatible demands?

Confucian ethics faces the partiality problem: privileging those near you seems to violate equality. What about strangers with greater need? And oppressive relationships can be preserved through appeal to role obligations.

## Living Consequences

Believing Scanlon means constantly checking whether you could justify your choices to others on equal terms. Buying luxuries while others starve becomes harder to defend—what principle allows it that they couldn't reasonably reject?

Believing Levinas transforms suffering into accusation. You cannot look away from homelessness, poverty, injustice without feeling the weight of your response-ability. Comfort becomes suspect.

Believing Confucius means perfectionism in relationships. Your obligations are immediate, embodied, unequal by nature. Excellence in role fulfillment—being a good parent, friend, citizen—becomes the ethical center.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy remains split. Contractualists refine principles of fair cooperation. Care ethicists emphasize relationships over rules. Effective altruists calculate maximum obligation. The question hasn't unified but fragmented into specialized debates.

What persists is the tension: we're neither fully separate nor fully responsible for each other. Every answer captures something essential while missing something crucial. Perhaps that's the question's real lesson—obligation is irreducibly plural, resistant to single principles, worked out in the friction between what we owe and what we are.
