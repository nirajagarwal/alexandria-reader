# What is power?

Power shapes every human relationship—parent to child, citizen to state, individual to society. Yet we struggle to say what it is. Is it the ability to make others obey? The capacity to achieve what you want? The right to command? Or something more subtle—the invisible force that makes certain actions thinkable and others impossible? How we answer determines whether power is something to be seized, shared, resisted, or dissolved.

## Why It Matters

Every political system, every ethics, every vision of human flourishing rests on assumptions about power's nature. If power is domination, liberation means escape or reversal. If power is productive—creating subjects, desires, truths—resistance becomes far more complex. The question cuts to how we understand freedom, justice, and human agency. Misidentify power and you misidentify what's wrong with the world and how to change it. Get it right and you can see who has it, how it moves, and where it might be challenged.

## Three Approaches

**Weber: Capacity Against Resistance**
Max Weber defined power (*Macht*) as "the probability that one actor within a social relationship will be in a position to carry out his own will despite resistance." Power is the ability to make things happen even when others oppose you. It's relational but asymmetrical—someone has power *over* someone else. Weber distinguished this from authority (*Herrschaft*)—legitimate power that people obey willingly. A mugger has power; a judge has authority. This framework dominated political science: power as a zero-sum resource you either possess or lack. It's measurable: count the votes, the armies, the money.

**Arendt: Collective Action**
Hannah Arendt rejected Weber's equation of power with domination. True power, she argued, emerges only through collective action—people acting together toward shared ends. Violence is the opposite of power, not its expression; when power collapses, violence appears. "Power corresponds to the human ability not just to act but to act in concert." A tyrant ruling through fear has violence, not power. The Polish workers of Solidarity had power—their coordinated withdrawal of cooperation brought down a regime. For Arendt, power is never property but potentiality: it exists only as long as the group remains together. This makes power inherently fragile and participatory. Revolution isn't seizing power but creating it anew.

**Foucault: Productive Relations**
Michel Foucault saw power not as something possessed but as relations that produce subjects, knowledge, and reality itself. Power operates through discourse, institutions, and practices that make certain ways of being seem natural. The prison doesn't just punish; it creates the "criminal." The clinic doesn't just treat illness; it produces medical subjects. Power is everywhere—not because it includes everything, but because it comes from everywhere. It's productive, not merely repressive: "Power produces; it produces reality; it produces domains of objects and rituals of truth." Resistance exists wherever power operates, but neither side stands outside the relationship. Foucault's genealogies trace how power creates the very categories we use to think about freedom.

## The Method

Weber used ideal types and comparative sociology—analyzing bureaucracies, states, religious movements to isolate power's structural features. Arendt turned to political phenomenology and historical examples (Greek *polis*, American Revolution, Hungarian uprising) to show power's appearance in action. Foucault employed genealogy—historical investigation revealing how present power relations emerged contingently, not inevitably. Where Weber abstracts, Arendt describes lived experience, and Foucault excavates buried processes. Each method reveals different aspects: Weber captures institutional mechanics, Arendt the existential dimension, Foucault the subtle disciplining of bodies and souls.

## Where It Breaks

Weber's definition can't explain why people obey when no one forces them—ideology, hegemony, internalized norms disappear. Arendt's idealization of collective action struggles with how groups oppress their own members; her sharp power/violence distinction blurs in practice (aren't armies instruments of collective power?). Foucault's omnipresent power risks explanatory emptiness—if power is everywhere, what isn't power? His account makes strategic action difficult to theorize: who can resist if subjects are power's products? Each framework blinds itself to what the others see.

## Living Consequences

Believe Weber and you focus on institutions—laws, elections, hierarchies. Change means capturing the state. Believe Arendt and you organize—solidarity, councils, movements. Power must be performed continuously. Believe Foucault and you examine your own formation—what desires, truths, identities power has produced in you. Resistance starts with questioning what seems natural. These aren't just theoretical differences. Weber's view breeds realism about institutions. Arendt's inspires participatory democracy. Foucault's enables critique of normalization. Your conception of power shapes what struggles you see as possible.

## The Question Now

Contemporary theory tries to integrate these insights. Judith Butler combines Foucault's productive power with Arendt's collective action, asking how subjects formed by power can still resist. Postcolonial thinkers expose how Eurocentric frameworks miss power's operation across imperial difference. Critical race theory reveals power's operation through seemingly neutral categories. The empirical turn in political science attempts to measure power's diffuse operation—but whether you can quantify what Foucault described remains contested. The question isn't settled: power remains elusive precisely because it shapes how we ask about it.
