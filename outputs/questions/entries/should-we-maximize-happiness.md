# Should we maximize happiness?

If all that matters is the sum total of happiness in the world, then killing one person to harvest their organs and save five others is morally required. This conclusion horrifies us—but why, if the math checks out? The question of whether we should maximize happiness pits our deepest moral intuitions against each other: the pull toward reducing suffering versus the conviction that some things matter beyond the calculus.

## Why It Matters

How you answer determines whether morality is a calculation or something else entirely. If maximizing happiness is the sole aim, then individual rights, personal relationships, and the separateness of persons become obstacles to overcome rather than values to respect. But if happiness isn't everything, what else matters—and why? This question cuts through abstract ethics to concrete dilemmas: should you lie to make someone happy, redistribute wealth to maximize total welfare, or sacrifice one life to save many?

## Three Approaches

**Jeremy Bentham** founded utilitarianism on a simple premise: pleasure is good, pain is bad, and we should maximize the former while minimizing the latter. His "hedonic calculus" measured actions by their tendency to produce happiness for all affected parties. "The greatest happiness for the greatest number" wasn't just a nice idea—it was the foundation of all morality and legislation. Each person counts for one and no more than one; intensity and duration of pleasure matter, but whose pleasure it is doesn't. This democratic, quantitative approach to ethics seemed to offer objective answers where religion and tradition gave only competing dogmas.

**John Stuart Mill**, Bentham's godson, recognized the crude inadequacy of treating all pleasures as equal. Some satisfactions—intellectual, aesthetic, moral—are qualitatively superior to mere bodily pleasure. "It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." Mill's revision tried to preserve utilitarian calculation while acknowledging the complexity of human flourishing. But his distinction between higher and lower pleasures threatened to smuggle in exactly the kind of non-utilitarian values Bentham had eliminated.

**Bernard Williams** exposed utilitarianism's deepest problem through thought experiments. If happiness-maximization requires you to kill an innocent person (threatening to kill twenty if you don't), utilitarianism demands you do it. But this treats people—including yourself—as mere vessels for happiness rather than as agents with integrity. Your deepest commitments, relationships, and projects matter morally, not just instrumentally. The separateness of persons isn't a computational inconvenience; it's fundamental to ethics. Williams showed how utilitarianism's impartial benevolence could be a kind of moral blindness.

## The Method

Bentham used calculation—literal arithmetic to weigh pleasures and pains. Mill employed empirical observation about what competent judges prefer. Williams used thought experiments to reveal the conflict between utilitarian conclusions and our considered moral judgments, then analyzed what that conflict revealed about the structure of ethical thought. Each method reflects deeper commitments: whether morality is primarily about outcomes, psychology, or the integrity of moral agents.

## Where It Breaks

Utilitarianism struggles with what philosophers call "the separateness of persons." It treats the boundary between individuals as morally arbitrary—pain in one person can be outweighed by pleasure in another, as if we were all cells in a single organism. This generates absurd conclusions: killing healthy people for organ transplants, punishing the innocent to prevent riots, demanding you sacrifice your deepest relationships if doing so would slightly increase total happiness. Even Mill's higher pleasures can't solve this; they just hide the calculation behind fuzzier language. And whose happiness counts? Animals? Future generations? The calculations explode into uncertainty.

## Living Consequences

Believing you should maximize happiness can justify terrible acts if the numbers work out. It can also paralyze you with impossible calculations—should you really spend money on dinner when it could save lives elsewhere? More subtly, it can corrupt your relationships. Love your child for utilitarian reasons and you've already failed to love them properly. The utilitarian must treat their own deepest commitments as provisionally held, always subject to the calculus. Williams called this a kind of "moral self-indulgence"—you get to feel morally pure while treating actual people as means to happiness-production.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophers have refined utilitarianism in countless ways—preference satisfaction instead of pleasure, rule consequentialism, sophisticated aggregation methods. But Williams's challenge remains: can any form of maximizing account for agent-relative reasons, personal projects, and the integrity of persons? The debate has shifted from whether to maximize to whether morality is fundamentally about outcomes at all. Virtue ethics and deontology offer alternatives, but utilitarian thinking dominates policy analysis and public health. The question isn't settled—it's revealed a deeper divide about whether ethics is essentially calculative or whether some things resist calculation by nature.
