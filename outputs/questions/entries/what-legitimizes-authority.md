# What legitimizes authority?

When you obey a law, follow an order, or accept someone's right to command, what makes that authority legitimate rather than mere coercion? Every government claims the right to rule; every institution demands compliance. But power alone—the ability to force obedience—doesn't answer the deeper question: what gives anyone the *right* to exercise that power over others?

## Why It Matters

The difference between legitimate authority and tyranny shapes everything from revolutions to daily compliance. If authority requires only force, then any successful oppressor is justified. If it requires consent, then most historical governments were illegitimate. If it springs from divine right, tradition, or rational agreement, then different political orders follow. This question determines when obedience is a duty and when it's complicity—when revolution is justified and when it's criminal. Every political crisis is ultimately a crisis of legitimacy.

## Three Approaches

**Weber's three types of legitimacy** distinguish authority by its source. Traditional authority rests on custom and heredity—the king rules because kings have always ruled. Charismatic authority flows from extraordinary personal qualities—the prophet or revolutionary leader whose followers believe in their mission. Legal-rational authority derives from impersonal rules and procedures—the bureaucrat whose power comes from office, not person. Weber wasn't endorsing any type; he was explaining what makes people *believe* authority is legitimate. Legitimacy is always a social fact, not a metaphysical one. The question isn't whether authority is truly legitimate, but what makes populations accept it as such.

**Locke's social contract** grounds legitimacy in consent. In the state of nature, everyone has natural rights to life, liberty, and property. Government becomes legitimate when individuals voluntarily surrender some freedoms to a collective authority that better protects those rights. Crucially, this consent must be ongoing—a government that violates natural rights dissolves its own legitimacy. For Locke, writing after the English Civil War, this meant limited government, separation of powers, and the right to revolution when authority becomes tyrannical. Legitimacy isn't inherited or seized; it's conditionally granted by the governed.

**Foucault's genealogy** dissolves the question itself. Authority doesn't become legitimate through consent, tradition, or charisma—these are stories power tells about itself. What we call "legitimacy" is the effect of power relations, not their justification. Modern authority works not through overt coercion but through normalization: schools, hospitals, prisons, and bureaucracies that produce "normal" subjects who internalize discipline. We don't consent to this power; we're constituted by it. Asking what legitimizes authority is like asking what legitimizes language—you're already inside it, shaped by it, before you can pose the question. The relevant question isn't legitimacy but genealogy: how did these particular power arrangements become naturalized as legitimate?

## The Method

Weber uses comparative historical sociology, examining how different societies actually justify authority. Locke employs a thought experiment—the state of nature—to derive principles of legitimate government from natural rights. Foucault traces genealogies, showing how concepts like "legitimacy" emerge from historical struggles and techniques of power. These methods reflect different assumptions: Weber assumes legitimacy is whatever populations believe it to be; Locke assumes it's whatever reason and natural law demand; Foucault assumes it's a mystification concealing power's operations.

## Where It Breaks

Weber's descriptive approach can't distinguish legitimate authority from successful propaganda—if people believe, that's enough. But this makes legitimacy whatever authority can make people believe, rendering the concept morally vacuous. Locke's consent theory faces the tacit consent problem: most of us never actually consented to our government. Saying we consent by staying put or accepting benefits reduces consent to fiction. Foucault's genealogy explains how we come to accept authority but can't explain why we should resist some forms and accept others—his critique of legitimacy leaves no ground for political judgment.

## Living Consequences

If Weber is right, legitimacy is performance—authority maintains itself by maintaining belief. Politics becomes theater and persuasion. If Locke is right, you have a duty to obey only governments that protect natural rights, and you retain the right to withdraw consent. Every unjust law raises the question of civil disobedience. If Foucault is right, the question of legitimacy is a trap—you can't step outside power relations to judge them. Resistance happens not by appealing to legitimacy but by creating new forms of life and relation. Your stance on legitimacy determines whether you see political obligation as contract, custom, or illusion.

## The Question Now

Contemporary political philosophy still wrestles with democratic legitimacy: does democracy require actual consent or just hypothetical consent under fair conditions? Do human rights impose legitimacy constraints transcending consent? The rise of global governance—international law, transnational corporations, algorithmic control—strains all traditional theories. What legitimizes authority that operates beyond national borders, beyond democratic accountability, beyond visibility? When an algorithm determines your credit score or social media feed, what makes that exercise of power legitimate? The question persists because authority always needs justification beyond force, yet every justification can be unmasked as another form of force.
