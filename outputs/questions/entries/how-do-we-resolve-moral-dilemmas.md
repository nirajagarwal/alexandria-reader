# How do we resolve moral dilemmas?

A trolley hurtles toward five people. You can pull a lever, diverting it to a track where it will kill one person instead. Do you pull it? The answer feels obvious—until you change the scenario slightly. What if you had to push someone off a bridge to stop the trolley? Same numbers, different intuition. Moral dilemmas expose the mechanics of our ethical reasoning: the principles we think we follow, the emotions that actually guide us, and the gaps between the two.

## Why It Matters

How we resolve moral dilemmas reveals what we actually value when values conflict. Abstract principles—maximize happiness, respect autonomy, preserve life—look clear until they collide in real cases. The trolley problem, abortion, wartime decisions, resource allocation in pandemics: these aren't thought experiments divorced from life. They're the structure of hard choices we make or delegate to others. Whether we follow rules, calculate consequences, or listen to emotional responses determines not just individual decisions but the shape of our institutions, laws, and relationships. Getting this wrong doesn't just mean philosophical error—it means preventable suffering or injustice.

## Three Approaches

**Kant says follow the rule that could be universal law.** Moral dilemmas arise when we confuse what we want with what we ought to do. His categorical imperative demands we act only on principles we could will everyone to follow. Don't lie, even to save a life—because a world where lying is permitted when convenient is a world where trust collapses and communication becomes impossible. The rule "protect innocent life" doesn't require you to push someone off a bridge; you're not obligated to prevent every death, only to avoid causing death through using someone as mere means. The dilemma dissolves when you recognize that pulling a lever redirects a threat, while pushing someone makes them the instrument of your plan. For Kant, moral clarity comes from rigorous application of universal principles, not from weighing outcomes or consulting intuitions.

**Mill says calculate which action produces the most happiness for the most people.** Moral dilemmas are real because consequences genuinely conflict—there's no action available that avoids all harm. Utilitarianism makes the math explicit: count up pleasure and pain, choose the option that maximizes overall welfare. Pull the lever, push the person—if five lives outweigh one, the arithmetic is clear. Our squeamishness about the bridge scenario reveals emotional bias, not moral truth. Mill refines Bentham's crude hedonism by distinguishing higher and lower pleasures, but the core method remains: moral problems are optimization problems. The difficulty isn't philosophical; it's practical—gathering accurate information, predicting consequences, weighing incommensurable goods. When resources are scarce and every choice means someone suffers, consequentialism at least makes the trade-offs explicit rather than hiding behind absolute rules that simply refuse to choose.

**Gilligan says attend to the relationships and context rather than applying abstract principles.** The whole setup of moral dilemmas as problems requiring a single right answer reflects a masculine, justice-oriented approach to ethics. Care ethics notices what's been abstracted away: Who are these people? What relationships exist between them? What responsibilities have we already undertaken? The trolley problem presents strangers and hypothetical certainty precisely to strip away what makes moral life real—ongoing relationships, partial knowledge, responsibilities that emerge from care rather than universal rules. Moral maturity isn't about perfecting our calculations or hardening our principles. It's about developing sensitivity to the web of relationships and responsibilities in which we're embedded, making decisions that maintain and repair those connections rather than treating people as interchangeable units in a moral equation.

## The Method

Kant employs conceptual analysis and logical consistency—testing maxims for internal contradiction and universalizability. Mill uses empirical observation and rational calculation—treating ethics as applied science, measuring utility across populations. Gilligan draws on developmental psychology and narrative inquiry—studying how people actually reason about real moral conflicts rather than constructing thought experiments. The methods matter: Kant's produces exceptionless rules, Mill's yields context-dependent optimizations, Gilligan's emphasizes irreducible particularity. They're not examining different data—they're asking different questions. Is morality fundamentally about rational consistency, aggregate welfare, or relational integrity?

## Where It Breaks

Kant's system generates genuine dilemmas when universal rules conflict (tell the truth vs. protect the innocent) with no higher principle to adjudicate. His solutions often feel like cheating—redefining "lying" to exclude misleading statements, insisting that perfect duty to avoid causing harm doesn't extend to preventing harm. Mill's utilitarianism struggles with distribution (is it better to give moderate pleasure to many or intense pleasure to few?) and with how to weigh different kinds of goods. It also potentially justifies horrifying acts—frame an innocent person to prevent riots?—unless heavily qualified. Gilligan's care ethics risks endorsing parochialism and providing no guidance when care relationships themselves conflict. What if maintaining one relationship requires betraying another? Care without justice can mean preserving oppressive relationships; attention to context without principles offers no basis for challenging structural injustice.

## Living Consequences

Kantians approach hard choices by seeking bright-line rules they can follow with a clear conscience, even when outcomes seem terrible. This produces moral certainty but can feel coldly indifferent to suffering. Consequentialists accept the burden of optimization—they own the results of their calculations but agonize over what they might have missed. This produces pragmatic flexibility but moral exhaustion from responsibility for everything their actions cause. Care ethicists resist the whole framing of moral life as a series of dilemmas requiring decisions and instead cultivate practices of attention, empathy, and relational repair. This produces moral sensitivity but potential paralysis when structures force choices between people we care about.

## The Question Now

Contemporary moral psychology suggests our intuitions about dilemmas engage multiple, sometimes conflicting systems—emotion-driven responses and conscious reasoning don't always align. Philosophers debate whether this shows we need a unified theory or whether moral pluralism better fits human experience. Trolley problem research reveals that physical contact, intentionality, and personal involvement dramatically shift intuitions in ways none of the traditional theories fully predict. The question becomes: are we trying to discover moral truth, or are we trying to understand and potentially reform how humans actually make moral judgments? The dilemmas themselves may be the wrong focus—attention shifts to moral development, institutional design, and how to construct situations that minimize tragic choices rather than how to resolve them once they arise.
