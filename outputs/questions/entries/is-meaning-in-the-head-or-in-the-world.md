# Is meaning in the head or in the world?

When you think about water, are you thinking about H₂O—or could you be thinking about XYZ, a chemically different substance that looks and tastes identical? The question seems absurd until you realize it cuts to whether meaning lives inside minds or gets anchored by reality itself.

## Why It Matters

If meaning is purely mental—a matter of concepts, intentions, or mental representations—then perfect duplicates would think identical thoughts regardless of their environments. But if meaning depends on actual things in the world, then internal mental states aren't enough to determine what we mean. This matters for everything from translation (can we really understand ancient texts?) to artificial intelligence (could a computer mean anything?) to skepticism (might we be systematically wrong about what we're even thinking about?). It determines whether mind and world are fundamentally separate or essentially connected.

## Three Approaches

**Hilary Putnam's externalism** argues meaning "ain't in the head" through his Twin Earth thought experiment. Imagine Earth and Twin Earth, molecule-for-molecule identical except that Twin Earth's oceans contain XYZ instead of H₂O, though XYZ is indistinguishable from water to ordinary observation. When you say "water," you mean H₂O. When your Twin Earth duplicate says "water," they mean XYZ. Same brain states, different meanings—because meaning is determined by what's actually in the environment, not what's in the head. The contribution of the environment fixes reference.

**Jerry Fodor's internalism** counters that mental content must supervene on brain states. His Language of Thought hypothesis posits that thinking involves computational operations over mental representations—symbols in an internal code. What matters for psychology is how these symbols function within the cognitive system, not what they happen to pick out in the world. Twin Earth duplicates have identical thoughts because they have identical computational architectures. The external stuff might determine reference, but it doesn't determine meaning as the mind experiences it. Narrow content—the meaning internal to psychology—is what explains behavior.

**Tyler Burge's social externalism** extends the argument beyond physical environment to linguistic community. In his arthritis thought experiment, someone believes they have arthritis in their thigh. They're wrong—arthritis is specifically a joint inflammation—but their belief is still *about* arthritis, not some other condition. What determines the content of "arthritis" in their thought isn't their individual conception but how their linguistic community uses the term. Meaning depends on social practices, not just individual minds or physical reference.

## The Method

These philosophers employ thought experiments—carefully constructed scenarios that isolate variables to test intuitions about meaning. Twin Earth holds brain states constant while varying environment. The arthritis case holds environment constant while varying social-linguistic context. The method reveals what we implicitly think fixes meaning by showing cases where we'd judge thoughts to differ despite internal sameness, or to stay the same despite external differences. It's conceptual analysis through imaginative variation.

## Where It Breaks

Externalism struggles with how we can have privileged access to our own thoughts—if meaning depends on external facts I might not know, how do I know what I'm thinking? It makes systematic error too easy: pre-scientific thinkers who said "water" couldn't have meant H₂O since they didn't know chemistry, suggesting meaning shifts whenever we learn new facts. Internalism faces the opposite problem: it makes meaning too independent of world, unable to explain how thoughts connect to reality or why we care about getting reference right. And both struggle with fiction—when you think about Sherlock Holmes, what determines the content of your thought if there's nothing in the world for it to be about?

## Living Consequences

If externalism is right, you cannot know what you mean without knowing about the world. Your authority over your own mind is limited—neuroscience, chemistry, and sociology partially determine your mental content. Self-knowledge requires world-knowledge. If internalism is right, you have complete epistemic access to your thoughts, but they might not connect to reality as you assume. You could be a brain in a vat thinking coherent thoughts about a non-existent world. The debate shapes how we understand expertise (do you need to know physics to think about physics?), mental illness (is delusion defined internally or by mismatch with reality?), and artificial intelligence (could a computer mean anything if it has no causal connection to what its symbols supposedly represent?).

## The Question Now

Most philosophers accept some form of externalism for reference—what your words pick out depends on environment and community. The debate has shifted to whether there's also narrow content that's purely in the head and sufficient for psychology, or whether externalism goes all the way down. Recent work explores active externalism—the extended mind thesis—arguing that meaning depends not just on passive environment but on active coupling between brain, body, and world. The question increasingly connects to consciousness studies: if meaning is external, what makes it feel internal? If it's internal, how does it reach outward? The debate continues because both positions capture something true: meaning feels intimately ours yet seems to depend on something beyond us.
