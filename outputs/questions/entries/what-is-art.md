# What is art?

We recognize art when we encounter it—or do we? A urinal in a museum, a silent concert, a rotting shark in formaldehyde. The question "What is art?" became urgent precisely when artists started making things that looked nothing like what art was supposed to be.

## Why It Matters

How we define art determines what deserves museum space, public funding, critical attention, legal protection. It shapes what counts as valuable human expression versus mere craft, decoration, or provocation. The stakes are institutional and personal: if art is whatever moves us emotionally, then greeting cards are art. If art requires technical mastery, Pollock was a fraud. If art is whatever experts say it is, the concept becomes circular. The question isn't academic—it's about which human activities we take seriously and why.

## Three Approaches

**Leo Tolstoy** made art entirely about emotional infection. In *What Is Art?* (1897), he argued that art transmits feelings from artist to audience—not just any feelings, but ones that unite people in shared experience. A peasant's song that moves other peasants is genuine art; technically sophisticated opera that leaves audiences cold is not. This led him to condemn most of what his culture called great art, including his own novels. Real art, for Tolstoy, promotes universal brotherhood through sincere emotional communication.

**Arthur Danto** approached from the opposite direction: art is whatever the artworld says it is. His encounter with Warhol's *Brillo Boxes* (1964) crystallized the problem—they looked exactly like grocery store boxes, yet one was art and one wasn't. In *The Transfiguration of the Commonplace*, Danto argued that art requires an "atmosphere of artistic theory"—a context of ideas, institutions, and interpretive practices that transform ordinary objects into artworks. The urinal becomes *Fountain* when framed by art discourse. Meaning, not appearance or technique, distinguishes art from non-art.

**Clive Bell** proposed that art possesses "significant form"—arrangements of lines, colors, and shapes that provoke a distinctive aesthetic emotion. In *Art* (1914), he claimed this formal quality exists independently of representation, emotional expression, or institutional validation. A Byzantine mosaic and a Cézanne landscape both have it; a photograph of your grandmother does not. Bell's formalism made art about pure visual relationships, stripping away subject matter, historical context, and artist intention as irrelevant.

## The Method

Tolstoy used moral philosophy—what serves human unity is good, what divides is bad. Danto employed analytical philosophy, examining the logical conditions that make something count as art rather than a mere thing. Bell trusted direct aesthetic experience, identifying a sui generis feeling that art alone produces. These methods aren't competing interpretations of the same phenomenon—they're asking different questions about different aspects of what we call art.

## Where It Breaks

Tolstoy's sincerity criterion rules out too much: irony, formal experimentation, art that challenges rather than unites. Danto's institutional theory seems circular—art is what the artworld calls art, but the artworld is defined by its dealing with art. It also makes art entirely dependent on contingent social practices rather than intrinsic qualities. Bell's significant form can't be defined without circular reference to aesthetic emotion, and his examples reveal taste masquerading as theory—why does the grandmother photo lack it? These aren't minor bugs; they're fundamental problems each approach generates.

## Living Consequences

If Tolstoy is right, you should ask whether art makes you more compassionate, not more sophisticated. Demanding entertainment has as much claim to artistic value as difficult modernism. If Danto is right, understanding context becomes essential—you can't experience art without knowing the conversations it's entering. Museums and critics aren't gatekeepers but necessary conditions for art to exist at all. If Bell is right, formal education in visual perception matters more than historical knowledge or emotional sensitivity. Each answer reorganizes how you spend time with artworks and what you're looking for in them.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy has largely abandoned seeking necessary and sufficient conditions for art. Instead, thinkers explore family resemblances (Weittgenstein's influence), historical narratives (Danto's later work), or pragmatic functions. The institutional theory has been refined but not displaced—art seems irreducibly social even if that's unsatisfying. The real action has moved to adjacent questions: What is art for? What makes art good? Can anything be art? The definitional question persists, but mainly as a tool for exposing assumptions rather than as a problem expecting solution.
