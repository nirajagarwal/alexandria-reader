# Can we speak of God?

Every attempt to talk about the divine runs into the same problem: language was built for tables and trees and human intentions, not infinite being. But religions make claims, mystics report experiences, and theologians fill libraries. Either they're all engaged in elaborate nonsense, or meaning can stretch beyond the familiar. The question of whether we can speak of God is really a question about the limits of language itself.

## Why It Matters

If God-talk is literally meaningless—mere emotional noise dressed up as propositions—then theology collapses into poetry, religious authority loses its cognitive claims, and millions of believers are making a category mistake about what they're doing. But if we can speak meaningfully about God, we need to explain how finite language reaches infinite reality. The answer shapes whether religious statements can be true or false, whether interfaith dialogue is possible, and what kind of knowledge theology might provide.

## Three Approaches

**Maimonides** insisted we can only say what God is *not*. Positive attributes—"God is good," "God knows"—are either metaphors or lead to incoherence. How can an eternal, simple being literally possess qualities that in creatures are temporal and composite? His via negativa removes false conceptions: God is not corporeal, not changing, not multiplied, not potential. We approach truth about God by eliminating errors, creating negative space around what cannot be positively conceived. "The Torah speaks in the language of men," he wrote in *Guide for the Perplexed*, but this concession to human understanding shouldn't be mistaken for literal truth.

**Thomas Aquinas** developed the doctrine of analogy. We can speak truly about God, but not univocally (same meaning) or equivocally (completely different meaning). When we say "God is good," we mean something related to but infinitely exceeding human goodness. The analogy works because creatures participate in divine perfections—goodness exists in us derivatively, in God essentially. This "analogia entis" in the *Summa Theologica* preserves both divine transcendence and the meaningfulness of theological language. We're not just making noise, but we're not describing God as we'd describe anything else.

**Wittgenstein** suggested religious language operates in its own grammar. Statements like "God loves you" aren't empirical hypotheses to be verified or falsified—they're moves in a particular language game. To call them meaningless because they don't function like scientific claims is to misunderstand their use. Religious language shows itself in how believers live, pray, and orient their existence. "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent," he wrote in the *Tractatus*, but silence here isn't empty—it's recognition that some realities require different modes of expression than propositional speech.

## The Method

Maimonides proceeds through rigorous conceptual analysis, showing contradictions in positive divine predication. Aquinas builds systematic metaphysics, grounding theological language in a theory of participation and causation. Wittgenstein performs grammatical investigation, examining how religious statements actually function in practice rather than imposing external criteria of meaning. Each method reflects a different assumption about what counts as explanation.

## Where It Breaks

The via negativa seems to end in agnosticism—if we can only say what God isn't, do we know anything at all? Analogy threatens to become equivocation in disguise: if "good" means something fundamentally different when applied to God, how do we know we're talking about goodness? Wittgenstein's language games risk relativism: if religious grammar is self-contained, how do we adjudicate between competing theological claims or challenge religious assertions that seem morally repugnant?

## Living Consequences

Accepting Maimonides means embracing intellectual humility before the divine—your confident theological claims may be sophisticated idolatry. Following Aquinas allows robust theological discourse while maintaining reverence—you can do systematic theology without pretending to comprehend God. Adopting Wittgenstein's approach means recognizing that religious language is primarily performative—what matters is not metaphysical accuracy but existential engagement.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy of religion remains divided. Logical positivists declared God-talk meaningless; their influence waned but the challenge persists. Reformed epistemologists like Alvin Plantinga argue religious beliefs can be properly basic, not requiring inference from other claims. Postmodern theology embraces paradox and apophasis. Meanwhile, new atheists and their critics still talk past each other, unable to agree what kind of speech is even occurring. The question hasn't been settled—it's become a question about whether religious and secular worldviews share enough common ground to even disagree productively.
