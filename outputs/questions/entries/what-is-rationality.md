# What is rationality?

Rationality is supposed to be the crown jewel of human cognition—what separates us from animals, what grounds science and ethics, what makes civilization possible. But philosophers can't agree on what it actually is. Is it following logical rules? Maximizing expected utility? Being responsive to reasons? The question matters because how we define rationality determines who counts as rational, which beliefs are justified, and what it means to think well.

## Why It Matters

The stakes are existential and political. If rationality means formal logic, then most human reasoning looks defective—we're bundles of cognitive biases failing to live up to an ideal. If it means practical success, then superstition might count as rational. The definition shapes education, policy, AI development, and our self-understanding. It determines whether emotions obstruct rationality or constitute it, whether cultural differences reflect irrationality or different rationalities, whether we're fundamentally reasonable creatures or sophisticated rationalizers. Get this wrong and you misunderstand what humans are.

## Three Approaches

**Classical rationality: Following logical rules.** From Aristotle through the Enlightenment, rationality meant conformity to logical principles—deductive validity, consistency, non-contradiction. Descartes sought a method of pure reason that would yield certainty. Kant argued that rationality requires acting on maxims that could be universal laws. This view treats rationality as rule-governed: you're rational if your beliefs follow from evidence via valid inference and your actions follow from principles you can universalize. The standard is formal correctness, independent of outcomes.

**Instrumental rationality: Maximizing expected utility.** Economists and decision theorists flipped the script: rationality isn't about following rules but achieving goals. You're rational if you hold consistent preferences, assign probabilities correctly, and choose actions that maximize expected utility. This doesn't care whether your goals are "reasonable"—if you prefer torture to Mozart, instrumental rationality just asks whether you're effectively pursuing torture. Philosophers like Donald Davidson argued that rationality is fundamentally about making sense of agents' behavior by attributing beliefs and desires in a way that maximizes their overall coherence and success.

**Responsive rationality: Answering to reasons.** A third tradition, from Aristotle through contemporary epistemologists like John McDowell, says rationality means responding appropriately to reasons. You're rational not because you follow algorithms or maximize utility, but because you grasp what counts as a reason in a given context and respond accordingly. This is thicker than formal logic but richer than instrumental success—it requires judgment about what actually matters. Emotions can be rational if they track genuine reasons for care or concern. Cultural practices can embody rationality if they represent appropriate responses to shared circumstances.

## The Method

Each approach employs different methods. Logical rationality uses formal analysis—validity, consistency, proof. Instrumental rationality uses decision theory and probability calculus—expected utility calculations, revealed preference, Dutch book arguments. Reason-responsiveness uses conceptual analysis and phenomenology—what does it mean to grasp a reason? How do we recognize appropriate responses? This methodological divergence matters: you can't test reason-responsiveness with decision theory, or formal logic with ethnography. The question "what is rationality?" resists empirical resolution because each approach determines what would count as evidence.

## Where It Breaks

Classical rationality makes most human thinking look irrational—we violate logical principles constantly yet function well. Instrumental rationality makes terrible goals rational and can't distinguish between someone maximizing utility and someone in the grip of addiction. Reason-responsiveness struggles to specify what counts as "appropriate" without cultural relativism or circularity—who decides what reasons matter? None handles the rationality of emotions well. All struggle with akrasia (weakness of will)—the classical view can't explain how you can know what's right and do otherwise, the instrumental view makes it impossible, the reasons view makes it mysterious. And all face the hard question: is there one rationality or many?

## Living Consequences

If you believe rationality is formal logic, you'll distrust intuition and emotion, value STEM over humanities, and see most disagreement as error. If it's instrumental, you'll focus on efficiency, treat values as given, and see conflicts as coordination problems. If it's reason-responsiveness, you'll cultivate judgment, see education as learning to recognize reasons, and treat moral disagreement as potentially substantive rather than just error or preference. Your view shapes whether you see AI as potentially rational, whether you trust gut feelings, how you handle cultural difference, and what you think education should cultivate. It determines whether you see yourself as a logic machine that malfunctions, an optimizer that sometimes fails, or a creature learning to respond well to a complex world.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy has fractured the question. Cognitive scientists study "bounded rationality"—how real humans reason under constraints. Feminist epistemologists question whose reasoning counts as the standard. Cross-cultural philosophers ask whether rationality is culturally universal or local. AI researchers debate whether machine learning constitutes rationality or mere pattern-matching. The consensus is that there isn't one thing called rationality—there are multiple norms (logical, instrumental, epistemic, practical) that sometimes conflict. The cutting edge questions: Can rationality be naturalized—explained by evolutionary or cognitive science? Is collective rationality reducible to individual rationality? What makes some reasons better than others? The question remains open because it's entangled with everything else we care about—truth, value, agency, meaning.
