# What is truth?

When Pilate asked Jesus "What is truth?" he was not being rhetorical—he was voicing philosophy's oldest anxiety. Every claim we make, from "the sky is blue" to "democracy is just," stakes itself on truth. But what makes something true? Is truth out there in the world, waiting to be discovered? Does it depend on coherence with other beliefs? Or is it simply what works? How we answer determines what we can know, what we can argue about, and whether reality has a structure independent of our minds.

## Why It Matters

The question isn't academic nitpicking—it's foundational to everything else. If truth is correspondence to reality, then there's a fact of the matter about ethics, politics, science, independent of what we believe. If truth is coherence within a system, then conflicting but internally consistent worldviews might all be "true." If truth is what's useful, then belief becomes instrumental—justified by results, not reality. These aren't equivalent positions dressed up differently. They fracture into incompatible views about objectivity, disagreement, and whether we can be genuinely wrong. Every other philosophical question—justice, knowledge, meaning—pivots on which theory of truth we accept.

## Three Approaches

**Aristotle's correspondence theory** remains the default: "To say of what is that it is not, or of what is not that it is, is false, while to say of what is that it is, and of what is not that it is not, is true" (*Metaphysics* 1011b25). Truth is alignment between proposition and fact. "Snow is white" is true if and only if snow is white. Simple, intuitive—and subtly radical. It assumes a mind-independent reality with a determinate structure, claims that stand in relations to facts, and the possibility of that mysterious alignment we call correspondence. Tarski's semantic theory formalized this in the 20th century, but the core insight is ancient: truth is about getting reality right.

**Coherence theory** emerged from idealists like Bradley and persists in revised forms. A belief is true if it coheres with the system of beliefs we hold. Truth isn't a relation to external facts—it's internal consistency within a web of propositions. This seems to avoid correspondence theory's mysteries about how mind touches world. But it creates new problems: can't a false belief system be perfectly coherent? The answer: there's ultimately one maximally coherent system (Bradley's Absolute), and contradictions are signs we haven't reached it yet. Neurath's image: we're rebuilding the ship at sea—no dry dock of raw facts, just making our beliefs hang together.

**Pragmatist theories** from Peirce through James to Rorty reject the correspondence question as misguided. James: "Truth is what works." A belief is true if acting on it produces successful results, if it "pays its way" in experience. Peirce's more subtle version: truth is what inquiry would converge on in the ideal limit. The pragmatist move sidesteps metaphysical worries—we can't step outside our practices to compare beliefs with bare reality anyway. Truth becomes epistemic: not transcendent correspondence but immanent warranted assertibility. Rorty radicalized this: drop the God's-eye view altogether. Truth is just a compliment we pay to beliefs that serve our purposes.

## The Method

Three distinct strategies, three different anxieties. Correspondence theorists do metaphysics first—what must the world be like for truth to work? Then epistemology—how do we access these truth-making facts? Coherentists reverse the order: start with the structure of justification, infer the nature of truth from how beliefs relate. They're doing epistemology all the way down. Pragmatists do neither—or both dissolved into practice. They examine what we're actually doing when we call something true, what difference it makes, what problems it solves. The methods reveal commitments: correspondence assumes realism, coherence allows idealism, pragmatism embraces instrumentalism.

## Where It Breaks

Correspondence can't explain the relation it needs. What is this "correspondence" between proposition and fact? Not similarity (propositions aren't little pictures). Not causation (true beliefs don't cause facts). The relation remains stubbornly primitive, perhaps inexplicable. Coherence faces the isolation objection: coherent fantasy is still fantasy. A perfectly consistent delusion doesn't become true through internal harmony alone. And pragmatism's "works" threatens relativism—works for whom? For what purposes? If truth is instrumental, can we say the Nazis were wrong or just that their beliefs didn't serve our purposes? Pragmatists insist this misunderstands—we can criticize from within practice—but the worry persists.

## Living Consequences

Choose correspondence, and you're committed to mind-independent reality, to genuine error, to the possibility of being deeply wrong despite coherent justification. Humility becomes mandatory—the world might not bend to our beliefs. Choose coherence, and truth becomes a matter of systematic thinking, of finding contradictions and resolving them. Knowledge is architecture, not archaeology. Choose pragmatism, and truth loses its transcendence—no beliefs are sacred, all are tools, revision is always possible. You give up certainty but gain flexibility. These aren't merely theoretical. They determine whether you think science discovers or constructs, whether moral disagreement is about facts or frameworks, whether consensus or correspondence should guide belief.

## The Question Now

The debate has fractured. Deflationists like Horwich argue truth isn't a substantive property at all—"p is true" just means p. The deflationary move: stop asking what truth *is* and notice what the truth predicate *does* (generalizing, asserting, disquoting). Meanwhile, pluralists suggest different domains need different truth theories—correspondence for science, coherence for mathematics, pragmatism for ethics. And some neo-pragmatists embrace Rorty's radical conclusion: the question was a mistake from the start, a pseudo-problem generated by bad metaphors. Truth-talk is useful, but there's no theory of Truth to discover. The question isn't settled—it's multiply answered or multiply dissolved, depending on who you ask.
