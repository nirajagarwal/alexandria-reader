# What is the scientific method?

The question seems simple—it's what scientists do, right? But philosophers have discovered that explaining how science actually works, why it works, and what distinguishes it from non-science is vastly more difficult than the textbook story of "hypothesize, test, repeat." What makes a theory scientific? How do we move from observations to laws? When should we trust scientific conclusions? These questions reveal that the method of science is itself a profound philosophical problem.

## Why It Matters

Our civilization runs on scientific knowledge—medicine, technology, climate predictions, everything we trust about the physical world. But pseudoscience, conspiracy theories, and ideological distortions of evidence proliferate by mimicking scientific language. Without understanding what actually makes science work, we can't distinguish genuine knowledge from sophisticated fraud. More deeply: if we can't explain the method that produced our most reliable knowledge, we don't really understand what makes anything count as knowledge at all.

## Three Approaches

**Karl Popper** argued that science isn't about verification but *falsification*. What makes a theory scientific isn't that you can prove it right, but that you can prove it wrong. "All swans are white" is scientific because finding one black swan destroys it. Freudian psychoanalysis isn't science because it explains everything—therefore predicts nothing that could disprove it. A theory that can't be falsified isn't making genuine contact with reality. Science advances not by accumulating confirmations but by surviving serious attempts at refutation. The method is: propose bold conjectures, then try ruthlessly to destroy them. The theories that survive are the ones we tentatively accept.

**Thomas Kuhn** shattered this clean picture. Real science, he showed, operates within *paradigms*—comprehensive frameworks of assumptions, methods, and exemplary problems. Normal science isn't testing fundamental theories; it's solving puzzles within an accepted framework. Anomalies accumulate, get explained away, until suddenly the paradigm breaks and science undergoes revolutionary restructuring. Newton to Einstein wasn't gradual improvement—it was incommensurable worldviews. There's no neutral method that determines when to abandon one paradigm for another. The choice involves judgment, aesthetics, even social dynamics. Science works, but not because scientists follow an algorithm.

**W.V.O. Quine** went further: no statement faces the tribunal of experience alone. Scientific theories form interconnected webs. When observations conflict with predictions, we can adjust peripheral hypotheses, revise our interpretation of the data, or—in extreme cases—even modify logic or mathematics. There's no sharp line between "theoretical" and "observational," no foundation of pure empirical fact. The scientific method is more like maintaining coherence across an entire system of beliefs while staying accountable to experience. Some beliefs are more central, harder to revise, but nothing is immune to revision if the whole structure demands it.

## The Method

Popper used logical analysis—what structure must theories have to be genuinely informative? Kuhn did historical investigation—what have scientists actually done during conceptual revolutions? Quine employed holistic epistemology—how do beliefs relate to each other and to experience as a system? Each method revealed different aspects: Popper saw logical demarcation, Kuhn saw historical contingency, Quine saw systematic interdependence. Their methods determined what they could see about the scientific method itself.

## Where It Breaks

Popper can't explain why scientists often rationally stick with theories despite anomalies—his physics would have been abandoned too quickly. Kuhn can't fully account for why the new paradigm is better, not just different—it risks making scientific progress arbitrary. Quine's holism makes everything revisable but gives little guidance about what to revise when—if anything goes, science becomes unmoored from reality. None explains why science works so spectacularly well at prediction and technology if there's no straightforward method capturing reality's structure.

## Living Consequences

If Popper is right, intellectual honesty means seeking your view's destruction, not its confirmation—a psychologically brutal demand. If Kuhn is right, scientific authority rests partly on social consensus, not just evidence—you must judge the community, not just the claims. If Quine is right, you can't isolate beliefs from their context—changing your mind about anything means potentially changing your mind about everything. All three suggest that certainty is an illusion and that living scientifically means living with permanent provisionality.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy of science has largely moved beyond seeking *the* method. Instead: how do different sciences actually work? What role do models, simulations, data-driven approaches play? How does social structure affect knowledge production? The question hasn't been answered so much as dissolved into more specific questions about scientific practice. But in an age of "doing your own research" and "alternative facts," the original question—what makes science reliable?—has never been more urgent or more unsettled.
