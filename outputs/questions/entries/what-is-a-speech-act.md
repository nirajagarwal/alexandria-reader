# What is a speech act?

When you say "I promise to help you move," you're not just describing something—you're doing something. The utterance itself creates the promise. This is a speech act: language as action, words that change the world by being spoken. The question seems simple until you try to explain how sounds from your mouth can generate obligations, marriages, or declarations of war.

## Why It Matters

If words can do things, then understanding language requires more than grammar and semantics—we need a theory of linguistic action. What distinguishes words that merely describe from words that perform? How do utterances create social realities, bind us to commitments, or alter institutional facts? The answer shapes everything from legal theory (when is a contract binding?) to social philosophy (what makes something official?) to ethics (what obligations flow from our words?). Speech act theory revealed that much of language's power lies not in representing the world but in transforming it.

## J.L. Austin's Discovery

Austin noticed that some utterances are fundamentally different from statements. When a judge says "I pronounce you guilty," she's not reporting on guilt—she's creating it. When you say "I bet you five dollars," you're not describing a bet, you're making one. Austin called these **performatives**, distinguishing them from **constatives** (statements that can be true or false).

But the distinction collapsed. Even "constatives" perform actions—asserting, claiming, informing. Austin reconceived all utterances as acts with three dimensions: the **locutionary act** (producing meaningful sounds), the **illocutionary act** (what you're doing in saying it: promising, warning, questioning), and the **perlocutionary act** (what effects you produce by saying it: convincing, frightening, amusing).

The illocutionary act is what makes something a speech act proper. It succeeds or fails not by being true or false, but by meeting **felicity conditions**: the right person, in the right circumstances, following the right conventions. "I now pronounce you married" works from an officiant at a wedding, not from a stranger on the street.

## Searle's Systematization

John Searle built Austin's insights into a systematic theory. He identified five basic types of speech acts:
- **Assertives** commit you to something being true (asserting, concluding, claiming)
- **Directives** try to get others to do things (requesting, commanding, asking)
- **Commissives** commit you to future action (promising, vowing, offering)
- **Expressives** express psychological states (thanking, apologizing, congratulating)
- **Declarations** bring about institutional changes by being performed (resigning, firing, christening)

Searle argued that speech acts have **constitutive rules**—rules that don't just regulate behavior but create the possibility of that behavior. "Checkmate" only exists because chess rules constitute it. Similarly, promising only exists because social conventions make certain utterances count as commitments.

His key insight: institutional reality—money, governments, property, marriage—is built entirely from collective recognition of speech acts. When we treat certain utterances as creating obligations or rights, we generate a layer of reality that transcends physical facts.

## The Method

Both philosophers used **ordinary language analysis**: examining how words actually function in everyday use rather than constructing ideal languages. Austin catalogued hundreds of performative verbs. Searle formalized the conditions that make speech acts succeed. The method assumes that natural language already contains sophisticated theories of action, commitment, and social reality—philosophy's job is to make them explicit.

This required attention to context, convention, and speaker intention. A speech act isn't just sounds plus meaning—it's an action embedded in social practices that give it force.

## Where It Breaks

Speech act theory struggles with non-literal language. What's the illocutionary force of metaphor, irony, or fiction? Saying "It's cold in here" might be asserting temperature or requesting someone close the window—how do we determine which?

**Critical theorists** argue the framework ignores power. Who gets to perform which speech acts is deeply political. Men interrupt women, judges silence defendants, colonial languages suppress indigenous ones. Speech act theory treats conventions as neutral when they're actually shaped by domination.

The theory also has trouble with **uptake**: what if your speech act isn't recognized? If you apologize but I don't accept it, did you successfully apologize? The dependence on social recognition can make the theory circular.

## Living Consequences

Understanding speech acts changes how you use language. You realize words commit you, that promises aren't just expressions of intent but bindings, that apologies require more than saying "sorry." You become aware of how much social reality depends on collective agreement to treat utterances as creating obligations and rights.

It also reveals manipulation. Politicians use declarative force without authority. Advertisers disguise directives as assertions. Recognizing the speech act helps you resist the performance.

In relationships, it matters enormously whether "I love you" is an expressive, a commissive, or just noise.

## The Question Now

Speech act theory dominates philosophy of language, but current work focuses on **implicit speech acts**—what you do without overtly performing it. Racist slurs subordinate by being spoken, regardless of speaker intent. Pornography, some argue, doesn't just depict subordination but enacts it.

**Digital communication** raises new questions: what's the illocutionary force of a like, a retweet, an emoji? When does online speech cross into action? The question now isn't whether speech acts exist, but how to map them in a world where language and action blur completely.
