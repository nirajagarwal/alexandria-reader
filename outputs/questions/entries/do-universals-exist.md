# Do universals exist?

When you see three red things—a rose, a stop sign, blood—are they red because they share in one thing called "redness"? Or is "red" just a word we use for similar-looking objects? This question splits philosophy into competing metaphysical camps, determines what counts as real, and shapes how we think language connects to the world.

## Why It Matters

If universals exist independently of particular things, then reality has an abstract structure beyond what we perceive—mathematical truths, moral facts, and properties exist whether or not any concrete thing instantiates them. If universals are merely mental constructs or linguistic conventions, then the furniture of the universe consists only of particular objects, and abstraction is something minds do, not something they discover. The answer determines whether science discovers pre-existing structures or invents useful fictions, whether moral realism is even possible, and what truth itself consists in.

## Three Approaches

**Plato's realism:** Universals exist independently in a realm of Forms. The particular red things participate in or imitate the Form of Redness itself. In the *Republic* and *Phaedo*, Plato argues that particular things are imperfect, changing copies of eternal, perfect Forms. We recognize a rose as red because our souls remember the Form of Redness from before birth. Mathematical and moral truths hold necessarily because they describe these unchanging Forms. The Form of the Good stands above all others, making both knowledge and reality possible.

**Aristotle's immanent realism:** Universals exist, but only *in* particular things, not in a separate realm. In the *Metaphysics*, Aristotle rejects Plato's transcendent Forms as explanatorily useless—saying the particular rose participates in Redness doesn't explain its redness. Instead, universals are the forms or essences instantiated in substances. Redness exists, but only as embodied in actual red things. There's no uninstantiated Redness hovering in a Platonic heaven. This grounds abstraction in the world while avoiding Plato's metaphysical extravagance.

**Ockham's nominalism:** Only particular things exist. "Redness" is just a word (*nomen*) we apply to similar particulars. In his *Summa Logicae*, William of Ockham wields his famous razor: don't multiply entities beyond necessity. We don't need abstract objects to explain language or thought—mental concepts and linguistic terms suffice. Three red things aren't red because they instantiate a universal; they're red because they resemble each other and we've learned to group them linguistically. Universals are in our heads and words, not in the world.

## The Method

Plato used dialectic and myth—the *Divided Line* and *Cave* allegory map the ascent from particular to universal, from opinion to knowledge. Aristotle employed empirical observation and logical analysis—if Forms existed separately, how could they explain particular things? Ockham's nominalism relied on logical economy and semantic analysis—what's the minimal ontology needed to explain predication? The debate reveals philosophy's perennial tension: do we trust rational insight into abstract structures (Plato), ground abstraction in observable particulars (Aristotle), or eliminate abstraction from our ontology entirely (Ockham)?

## Where It Breaks

Plato's Forms face the "Third Man" regress: if particular men resemble the Form of Man, don't they and the Form share a further Form, infinitely? Aristotle struggles to explain how we grasp universals if they're always embedded in particulars—abstraction looks suspiciously Platonic. Nominalism makes mathematics and modality mysterious: if no universals exist, what makes "2+2=4" true? How do different particulars genuinely resemble each other rather than just being called by the same name? Each position either multiplies metaphysical entities, struggles with epistemology, or renders abstract knowledge unintelligible.

## Living Consequences

Platonism makes you a discoverer, not an inventor—mathematics, morality, and beauty exist independently, awaiting recognition. It grounds objectivity but risks making the abstract more real than the concrete. Aristotelianism keeps you anchored in the physical world while preserving objective structure. Nominalism makes you radically empirical—only the particular is real, abstraction is human artifice. This shapes whether you think moral disagreements can have right answers (realism) or are matters of convention (nominalism), whether scientific laws describe reality's structure or our cognitive habits.

## The Question Now

Contemporary metaphysics has splintered the debate. Some defend "trope theory"—abstract particulars like *this* redness. Others embrace "structural realism"—only structures, not objects or properties, exist. Modal realists like David Lewis argue possible worlds are as real as actual ones, making modality concrete. Quine's criterion—"to be is to be the value of a variable"—grounds ontology in our best scientific theories. The question persists because it asks what existence itself means: do only spatiotemporal particulars exist, or does reality include abstract structure? We still don't agree.
