# What is the social contract?

We live under laws we never explicitly agreed to. The state claims authority over us. It can tax, punish, even conscript. What gives it this right? The social contract is philosophy's answer: political authority is legitimate only if it can be understood as arising from an agreement—real or hypothetical—among free people. It's the question of why we should obey when we never signed anything.

## Why It Matters

Every political system claims legitimacy. Monarchs invoked divine right. Fascists proclaimed natural hierarchy. Democracies appeal to popular sovereignty. The social contract tradition says legitimacy requires consent—that political authority must be justifiable to those who live under it. This matters because it makes power accountable. If authority rests on agreement, we can ask: what terms would rational people accept? What would we never consent to? The answers determine whether revolution is justified, what rights are inalienable, and whether obligations to obey are absolute or conditional.

## Three Approaches

**Hobbes's covenant of survival.** In *Leviathan* (1651), life without government is the state of nature: "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Everyone has unlimited natural right but constant insecurity. Rational self-interest drives us to contract: we surrender our natural liberty to a sovereign—whether monarch or assembly—in exchange for protection. The contract is absolute. Once established, the sovereign's authority is unlimited because any constraint risks sliding back into chaos. You consent by remaining in the territory. Rebellion is almost never justified because even bad government beats anarchy.

**Locke's conditional trust.** In the *Second Treatise of Government* (1689), the state of nature has natural law—reason teaches us not to harm others' life, liberty, or property. But enforcement is uncertain. We contract to create government as a neutral judge and protector. Crucially, this is a limited grant. The sovereign holds power in trust; if it violates natural rights (especially property rights), the contract breaks. Revolution becomes legitimate when government betrays its purpose. Authority is conditional, not absolute. This grounds liberal constitutionalism: government by consent with structural limits.

**Rawls's veil of ignorance.** In *A Theory of Justice* (1971), Rawls modernizes the contract through a thought experiment. Imagine choosing principles of justice behind a "veil of ignorance"—not knowing your race, class, gender, talents, or conception of the good. What would you agree to? Rawls argues you'd choose equal basic liberties and inequality only when it benefits the least advantaged. This isn't a historical contract but a device for justification. A just society is one its principles could be agreed to under fair conditions. The method makes justice independent of accidents of birth or power.

## The Method

The social contract is a thought experiment, not history. No actual society began with people gathering to sign an agreement. Instead, it's a normative tool: what terms would rational, free people accept? Hobbes uses it to justify absolutism by showing what fear of death would make us consent to. Locke deploys it to limit power by showing what we could never rationally surrender. Rawls uses it to derive principles of justice by eliminating morally arbitrary factors from the decision. The method reveals what we could justify to each other as equals, not what we'd choose selfishly.

## Where It Breaks

The obvious problem: we never actually consented. Hume demolished the myth of historical contracts—most people are born into political systems they never chose. "Tacit consent" (you stayed, so you agreed) is circular. Feminist critics note the contract tradition assumes autonomous individuals, ignoring that we're born dependent and embedded in relationships. The method often smuggles in the theorist's preferred outcome—is Rawls describing neutral rationality or liberal values? Anarchists ask: if the contract is hypothetical, why does actual coercion follow? And non-Western traditions often begin not with individuals contracting but with organic communities or cosmic order.

## Living Consequences

Believe in the social contract and political obligation becomes reciprocal. You obey because the state protects rights; when it doesn't, your obligation weakens. This grounds civil disobedience—refusing unjust laws because they violate terms no one could rationally accept. It makes you scrutinize authority: would I consent to this if I didn't know my position in society? It turns political argument into a kind of justification: can you give me reasons I could accept as a free and equal person? The cost: you might realize much of what you obey lacks justification.

## The Question Now

Contemporary political philosophy still uses contractual reasoning but with modifications. Rawls's method remains influential but faces communitarian critics who argue we can't abstract from our identities. Feminist care ethics challenges the individualist starting point. Questions persist: Does consent require explicit agreement or only fair procedures? Can hypothetical contracts justify actual obligations? How do we include future generations, children, animals—those who can't contract? The question has shifted from "did we consent?" to "what principles could we justify to each other as equals?" The search for legitimacy continues.
