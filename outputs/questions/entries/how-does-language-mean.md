# How does language mean?

Words point to things, but how? A dog hears "sit" and acts. A child learns "red" and suddenly the world organizes itself into colors. We read "justice" and argue for centuries about what it names. Language connects sounds or marks to meanings, but that connection—how it works, what makes it possible—is philosophy's most practical mystery.

## Why It Matters

Everything humans do together depends on shared meaning. Laws, love letters, scientific papers, this sentence—all assume words successfully carry thought from one mind to another. But when meaning fails—in translation, in court, in arguments where both sides use the same words differently—we realize how strange this assumption is. How does a pattern of sound or ink become *about* something? The answer determines whether meaning is in our heads, in the world, in social practices, or nowhere stable at all.

## Three Approaches

**Frege's compositional semantics** (1892): Meaning has architecture. Words have *sense* (the mode of presentation) and *reference* (what they pick out). "The morning star" and "the evening star" refer to Venus but present it differently—hence ancient astronomers' surprise they were the same. Sentences build meaning systematically: understanding "the cat sat on the mat" requires knowing what cats, sitting, and mats are, plus rules for combining them. Mathematics works because its compositional structure is perfect; natural language gets messy but follows similar principles. Meaning isn't psychological—it's objective, shareable, structured. We can say things we've never heard because we grasp the system.

**Wittgenstein's language games** (1953): Meaning is *use* in a practice. The word "game" has no essence—board games, ball games, ring-around-the-rosy share only family resemblances. Similarly, words mean by what we *do* with them in particular contexts. "Know" works differently in "I know I have hands" versus "I know quantum mechanics." There's no single thing words do called "meaning"—there are countless language games, each with its own rules. The picture theory of meaning (words as labels stuck on things) misleads us into thinking meaning is reference. But "ouch!" means without referring. "I promise" doesn't describe—it acts. Grammar shows us what makes sense in a practice; philosophy's job is describing these grammars, not building theories above them.

**Quine's indeterminacy thesis** (1960): Meaning is radically underdetermined. Imagine translating an unknown language. A native says "gavagai" when a rabbit appears. Does it mean "rabbit," "undetached rabbit part," "rabbit-stage," or "lo, rabbithood again"? No possible evidence settles this—all translations fit the behavioral data equally. If meaning were determinate, one translation would be correct. But the "fact of the matter" doesn't exist even in principle. What we call meaning is a projection of our translation scheme onto opaque behavioral dispositions. Meaning isn't in heads or the world—it's an artifact of interpretation that could always go otherwise.

## The Method

Frege used logical analysis: formalize natural language to reveal its underlying structure. Wittgenstein practiced grammatical description: collect examples of actual use until patterns emerge. Quine employed thought experiments: show what observations could and couldn't establish to reveal meaning's nature. The difference isn't trivial—formal semantics seeks universal principles, use-theory watches particular practices, indeterminacy arguments question whether there's anything stable to study.

## Where It Breaks

Frege's compositionality struggles with metaphor, poetry, irony—cases where meaning exceeds systematic combination. Wittgenstein's use-theory can't explain how we understand novel sentences or why some uses feel wrong rather than just different. Quine's indeterminacy, if true, makes translation miraculous—yet we translate successfully all the time. None explains how meaning emerged in the first place or why certain sounds latched onto certain concepts. And all three largely ignore non-linguistic meaning: pictures, music, facial expressions that clearly mean without being language games.

## Living Consequences

If Frege is right, clarity is possible—we can build better language through better logic. Legal precision, scientific communication, philosophical rigor become achievable goals. If Wittgenstein is right, many disputes are pseudo-problems born of misunderstanding language's diversity. We stop asking "what is truth really?" and start asking how "true" functions in different contexts. If Quine is right, humility about our interpretations matters—other cultures aren't making mistakes about meaning, just playing unparaphrasable games. Your view of meaning shapes whether you think communication can be perfected, or must be negotiated, or is fundamentally approximate.

## The Question Now

Contemporary semantics largely follows Frege while accommodating Wittgenstein's insights about context-dependence. Formal pragmatics studies how use shapes compositional meaning. But Quine's challenge persists: neuroscience reveals no "meaning" in brains, just patterns that somehow become about things. The symbol-grounding problem remains unsolved. AI language models predict word sequences without (apparently) meaning anything, yet seem to communicate. Whether meaning requires minds, or can emerge from pure pattern, is newly urgent.
