# What is substance?

When you say "this table exists," what exactly is doing the existing? The wood? The atoms? The form? The idea? Philosophers have asked what underlies all the properties and changes we observe—what persists when everything else shifts. This is the question of substance: whether there's something fundamental that bears all qualities without itself being a quality of anything else.

## Why It Matters

The substance question determines what counts as fundamentally real. If only minds are substance (Berkeley), matter becomes illusion. If only matter is substance (Hobbes), consciousness becomes a puzzle. If nothing persists (Buddhism), change itself becomes primary. Your metaphysics of substance shapes your physics, your theology, your ethics. It determines whether you're a thing that has experiences or a flow of experiences with no underlying experiencer. Whether the self is real or conventional. Whether God could exist. Whether science seeks ultimate reality or useful descriptions.

## Three Approaches

**Aristotle** made substance (*ousia*) the cornerstone of metaphysics. Primary substance is the individual thing—this horse, this human. It exists independently and bears properties without being a property of anything else. The horse is brown and fast, but "brown" and "fast" need the horse to exist in—they're not free-floating. Secondary substances are the species and genera—Horse, Animal—that tell us what the primary substance fundamentally is. But Aristotle's substance isn't mere matter. It's form-in-matter, the inseparable unity of what something is made of and what makes it the kind of thing it is. The bronze isn't the statue; the shape isn't the statue; the substance is this-bronze-in-this-shape persisting through time.

**Spinoza** collapsed substance into one. God or Nature is the only substance—everything else is merely a mode or modification of it. Where Aristotle saw countless substances (each horse, each human), Spinoza saw one infinite substance with infinite attributes, of which we know two: thought and extension. Your mind and body aren't separate substances (Descartes) or matter and form (Aristotle), but the same modification of substance expressed through different attributes. Nothing exists independently. Everything is a wave in the one ocean, a temporary pattern in the one substance. This is why Spinoza's ethics begins with metaphysics—understanding yourself as a mode of God transforms how you live.

**Buddhist philosophers**, particularly in the Abhidhamma tradition, denied persistent substance entirely. What we call a "person" or a "thing" is a conventional label for a stream of *dharmas*—momentary events arising and ceasing in dependence on conditions. There's seeing, but no seer. Pain, but no one in pain. The experience of continuity is real; the substance supposedly having that experience is not. Later Madhyamaka philosophers like Nagarjuna radicalized this: even dharmas lack substance. Everything is *sunyata* (empty) of intrinsic existence. What appears solid is process. What seems independent is interdependent. Substance is the fundamental illusion.

## The Method

Aristotle: logical analysis of predication. What can be said of what? What can exist on its own? The grammar of subject and property reveals metaphysical structure.

Spinoza: geometric demonstration from definitions and axioms. Substance must be self-caused, infinite, indivisible. Logic itself dictates reality's structure.

Buddhism: meditation and phenomenological analysis. Observe experience directly. Where is this "self" when you look? All you find is the looking.

## Where It Breaks

**Aristotle's** account struggles with change. If substance is what persists, how does substantial change happen? When the caterpillar becomes butterfly, did the substance change or just its properties? If it changed, it's not substance. If it didn't, what explains the transformation?

**Spinoza's** one-substance metaphysics makes individual agency mysterious. If everything is a mode of God unfolding necessarily, where is freedom? Why does anything seem to choose, to fail, to improve? Spinoza has an answer (adequate vs. inadequate ideas), but it pushes the problem to a different level.

**Buddhism's** no-substance doctrine faces the problem it admits: conventional reality functions as if things persist. Language, memory, ethics, karma—all require some continuity. Saying "there's continuity without substance" doesn't dissolve the puzzle; it relocates it.

## Living Consequences

Believe in substance and you're committed to something permanent in flux. The self might persist through change; death might not be total annihilation; your choices might belong to a real you. You seek what's essential beneath the accidental.

Deny substance and everything becomes relational, empty of intrinsic being. Attachment to self becomes confusion. Clinging to permanence causes suffering. Ethics shifts from what things are to how they relate. The goal isn't to preserve the self but to see through it.

Spinoza's single substance makes acceptance primary—this is how God necessarily is. Resistance is confusion. Understanding is freedom.

## The Question Now

Contemporary metaphysics still wrestles with substrate. Process philosophy (Whitehead) says events are fundamental, not things. Physics suggests fields, not particles, might be basic. Mereology asks whether wholes are substances over and above their parts.

But the question persists because the intuition persists: something must be doing the existing. Whether that's matter, mind, process, or nothing at all determines what kind of world this is—and what kind of thing you are in it.
