# What is intention?

When you reach for a glass of water, vote in an election, or lie to spare someone's feelings, something guides the action beyond mere muscle movement. That something is intention—the directedness of mind that transforms bodily motion into meaningful action. Philosophy asks: What makes intention different from mere desire or prediction? How does a thought become a deed? And can we ever fully know our own intentions, or do we deceive ourselves about why we act?

## Why It Matters

The concept of intention separates accidents from actions, murder from manslaughter, moral responsibility from mere causation. Legal systems turn on it: did she intend to harm, or was it negligence? Ethical theories depend on it: do our intentions matter more than consequences, or are good intentions worthless if they lead to harm? And intention shapes how we understand ourselves—whether we're authors of our lives or passengers interpreting our behavior after the fact. If we can't trust our own account of why we act, the gap between what we think we're doing and what we're actually doing threatens our very sense of agency.

## Three Approaches

**Elizabeth Anscombe** revolutionized philosophy of action in *Intention* (1957) by asking a deceptively simple question: when is an action intentional? Her answer: when it makes sense to ask "why?" and the answer gives a reason rather than a cause. If you're writing your name and someone asks why, you might say "to sign the check"—that's intentional. But if they ask why your hand is trembling, you give a cause (too much coffee), not a reason. Intention, for Anscombe, is this special kind of knowledge we have of our own actions—not by observation but by being their author. We know what we're doing "without observation," a practical knowledge that guides the action itself.

**Donald Davidson**, in "Actions, Reasons, and Causes" (1963), argued against Anscombe that reasons *are* causes—but a special kind. When you intend to do something, your beliefs and desires together cause the action. You believe opening the window will cool the room; you desire a cooler room; these jointly cause you to open the window intentionally. This causal account preserves intention's explanatory power while grounding it in the physical world. Davidson insisted we don't need mysterious "acts of will"—ordinary causation explains how mental states produce bodily movements.

**Michael Bratman**, in *Intention, Plans, and Practical Reason* (1987), showed that intention is more than momentary desire-plus-belief. It's commitment across time. When you intend to finish a book, you're not just predicting your behavior or expressing a desire—you're *planning*. Intentions structure future action, coordinate with other plans, and constrain what you'll consider doing. This planning theory explains why intentions feel different from mere wishes: they settle matters, closing off deliberation and organizing how you'll act tomorrow, next week, next year.

## The Method

Anscombe employed conceptual analysis and ordinary language philosophy—examining how we actually talk about actions and reasons. Davidson used philosophical logic to formalize the relationship between reasons and causes. Bratman drew on both philosophy and cognitive science, treating intentions as functional states in a planning system. All three avoid introspection alone—they examine intention through its role in explanation, its logical structure, its practical consequences. The question isn't "what does intention feel like?" but "what work does the concept do?"

## Where It Breaks

Anscombe's "knowledge without observation" remains mysterious: how exactly do we know what we're doing without watching ourselves do it? Davidson faces the problem of deviant causal chains—your desire to scare someone makes you so nervous that you accidentally drop a tray, scaring them. Your mental states caused the outcome, but you didn't intend it. And Bratman's planning theory struggles with impulsive acts: sometimes we intend things in the moment without prior plans. More fundamentally, all three face the challenge of self-deception: we often don't know why we act. The racist who insists she's "just asking questions," the addict who claims "I can stop anytime"—are they lying or genuinely confused about their own intentions?

## Living Consequences

How you understand intention changes how you judge yourself and others. If intentions are settled plans (Bratman), then weakness of will is a failure of commitment, not just poor self-control. If reasons are causes (Davidson), then therapy might work by changing the causal structure of your beliefs and desires, not by uncovering hidden motives. If we know our intentions without observation (Anscombe), then gaslighting—being told you didn't mean what you meant—is a peculiar violence against your practical knowledge. And if we're often wrong about our intentions, humility becomes a moral requirement: perhaps you're not as principled as you think.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy of action grapples with neuroscience findings that brain activity predicts "voluntary" actions before conscious awareness. Does this mean intentions are post-hoc rationalizations? Meanwhile, AI ethics faces intention's puzzle: can systems that mimic goal-directed behavior truly intend anything, or is intention necessarily bound to consciousness? The feminist philosopher Jennifer Hornsby has extended Anscombe's work, showing how social power shapes what counts as intentional action—marginalized people's intentions are often denied or reinterpreted by others. The question of intention, it turns out, is also a question about who gets credited with authoring their own lives.
