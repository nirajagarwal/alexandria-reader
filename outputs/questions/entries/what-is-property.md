# What is property?

When Proudhon declared "property is theft," he wasn't being paradoxical—he was identifying a tension at the heart of ownership. Property seems natural, almost self-evident: this is mine, that is yours. But the philosophical question cuts deeper. What transforms mere possession into legitimate ownership? What justifies excluding others from what nature or labor or chance has put in your hands? The answers determine who gets what, who works for whom, and what counts as justice.

## Why It Matters

Property isn't just about things—it's about power, freedom, and the structure of society. Your answer determines whether private ownership of land is legitimate or inherently unjust, whether intellectual property makes sense, whether inheritance perpetuates or undermines equality. It shapes tax policy, labor relations, environmental law, and the basic terms of social cooperation. Get property wrong, and you either sanctify exploitation or make stable cooperation impossible. The stakes are who gets to exclude whom from what, and on what grounds.

## Three Approaches

**Locke's Labor Theory**: Property begins with self-ownership. You own your body, therefore you own your labor, therefore you own what you mix your labor with. Clear the forest, plant the field—it becomes yours. The limit: you can only claim what you can use, leaving "enough and as good" for others. This naturalizes property—it precedes society, grounds it in individual effort, makes ownership a function of work rather than power. Private property isn't theft; it's the reward for productive labor and the foundation of liberty. The state exists to protect what you've already earned through your own exertion.

**Hegel's Recognition**: Property is how self-consciousness becomes concrete in the world. Through appropriation, you embody your will in external objects—this land, this house becomes an extension of your personality. But it's more than individual psychology. Property requires mutual recognition: others must acknowledge your right for it to exist. Ownership is fundamentally social, a relationship between persons mediated through things. This explains why property feels so personal—violating it isn't just taking stuff, it's denying someone's existence as a free will. But it also shows property isn't natural—it's achieved through intersubjective recognition, a collective creation rather than a pre-social fact.

**Proudhon's Contradiction**: "Property is theft" because private property in the means of production—land, factories, capital—allows owners to extract value from workers' labor without working themselves. The landlord contributes nothing yet collects rent. The factory owner pays less than the value workers create. This isn't Locke's farmer mixing labor with soil—it's systemic exploitation enabled by legal fiction. But Proudhon distinguished this from possession: your house, your tools, what you use—that's legitimate. The problem is absentee ownership, the right to profit from what others work. Property-as-domination needs abolishing; possession-as-use doesn't.

## The Method

Locke argues from natural rights and thought experiments—the state of nature, labor making value. Hegel proceeds dialectically, showing how property emerges from the internal logic of self-consciousness seeking objectivity. Proudhon combines economic analysis (showing how property enables exploitation) with moral critique (exposing the contradiction between liberty and ownership). These aren't just different conclusions—they're different ways of thinking about what property *is*. A right? A relationship? A system? The method determines what counts as evidence.

## Where It Breaks

Locke's labor theory collapses under scrutiny. How much labor creates ownership—a fence around land, or cultivating it? What counts as "enough and as good" when all desirable land is claimed? Why doesn't your labor give you ownership of the company you work for? Hegel's recognition theory explains why we *feel* property matters but not why any particular distribution is just—why should your will be embodied in this mansion while others starve? Proudhon's critique of exploitation is powerful but his possession/property distinction proves unstable—where exactly is the line between legitimate use and illegitimate accumulation?

## Living Consequences

Believe Locke: private property is sacred, taxation is taking what people earned, inequality reflects different levels of industry and merit. Build a society around individual property rights, minimal state interference, free markets. Believe Hegel: property is essential to personhood, but only legitimate when mutually recognized within a rational social order. Accept some redistribution, regulation, collective ownership where individual property conflicts with community recognition. Believe Proudhon: abolish property-for-profit, organize production cooperatively, ensure everyone has use-rights to what they need. Three different worlds, three different answers to "who deserves what."

## The Question Now

Contemporary debate has fractured. Libertarians defend strong property rights with Lockean arguments. Rawlsians accept private property but constrain it through principles of fairness. Socialists still point to property's role in exploitation, updated for platform capitalism and algorithmic ownership. New questions emerge: can you own data, genes, attention? Is intellectual property coherent or just monopoly by another name? Climate change raises Locke's proviso urgently—is there enough and as good left for future generations? The question hasn't been settled. It's mutated into battles over digital commons, land trusts, and whether ownership itself is sustainable on a finite planet.
