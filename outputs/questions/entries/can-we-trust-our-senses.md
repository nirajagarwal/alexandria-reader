# Can we trust our senses?

Everything you know about the world comes filtered through eyes, ears, fingertips. But optical illusions fool you. Dreams feel real while you're in them. The stick looks bent in water, though you know it's straight. If your senses can deceive you sometimes, how do you know they're not deceiving you right now?

## Why It Matters

The reliability of sense perception grounds everything: science, law, daily navigation, the reality of the external world itself. If senses can't be trusted, empirical knowledge collapses—no amount of observation can tell you what's true. But if we reject sense experience entirely, we're left with pure reason operating in a vacuum, cut off from the world it's supposed to explain. The question isn't just epistemological; it's existential. What connects mind to world?

## Three Approaches

**Descartes's radical doubt** pushed the question to its limit. In *Meditations on First Philosophy* (1641), he systematically demolished sense perception's authority. Dreams are indistinguishable from waking life while you're in them—so how do you know you're not dreaming now? More radically: perhaps an evil demon manipulates all your perceptions, feeding you a completely false reality. Descartes concludes you can't trust your senses at all. The only certainty is that you exist as a thinking thing (*cogito ergo sum*), because even deception requires a mind being deceived. He rebuilds from there—God's benevolence guarantees that careful, clear perception can be trusted—but the initial skeptical move haunts everything after.

**Hume flipped the question.** He didn't doubt that we trust our senses; he asked what justifies that trust and found nothing. In *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748), he argues that all knowledge comes from sense impressions—we have no other source. But sense impressions only give us *this* appeared *thus* at *this moment*. We can never perceive causation itself, only constant conjunction—the billiard ball always follows the cue ball's strike, but we don't see the "making happen." Our belief in an external world persisting beyond perception? A habit of mind, not rational justification. Hume accepts we'll keep trusting our senses—we have no choice—but insists there's no philosophical ground for doing so. Custom, not reason, governs belief.

**The Buddhist Yogācāra school** argues the question itself is backwards. In texts like Vasubandhu's *Twenty Verses* (4th century CE), they claim there is no "external world" that our senses might accurately or inaccurately represent. Everything is *vijñapti-mātra*—consciousness-only. What we call "sense experience" is mental events arising from karmic seeds stored in the "storehouse consciousness" (*ālayavijñāna*). Dreams prove the point: vivid experiences with no external objects. Why posit external objects for waking experience when consciousness alone explains everything? The question "can we trust our senses?" dissolves when you realize senses aren't representing something external—they're just consciousness manifesting in various forms.

## The Method

Descartes used systematic skepticism—doubt everything doubtable until you hit bedrock. Hume employed empirical analysis of how beliefs actually form, showing the gap between psychological compulsion and rational justification. Yogācāra used meditative introspection combined with logical argument: examine experience closely enough and the subject-object duality collapses. The methods reveal different assumptions: Descartes seeks foundations, Hume accepts groundlessness, Yogācāra denies the framework entirely.

## Where It Breaks

Descartes's solution—God guarantees clear perception—satisfies few today, and his demon is just dream skepticism intensified, not a new problem. Hume's position is honest but corrosive: if custom alone governs belief, why privilege any beliefs over others? His naturalism (we're built to trust senses) sidesteps rather than solves the question. Yogācāra creates new puzzles: if everything is consciousness, whose consciousness? And it struggles with shared experience—why do our individual consciousness-streams coordinate so precisely? Idealism must explain intersubjectivity.

## Living Consequences

If Descartes is right, radical doubt becomes the path to certainty, but you live in permanent philosophical exile from the ordinary world. If Hume is right, you act on habit while knowing there's no justification—a kind of cheerful philosophical resignation. If Yogācāra is right, you stop seeking reality "out there" and turn attention inward, recognizing that purifying consciousness matters more than correcting perception. The skeptic becomes paralyzed, the Humean pragmatic, the idealist meditative.

## The Question Now

Contemporary epistemology mostly accepts that basic sense perception is "innocent until proven guilty"—we're justified in trusting senses unless we have specific reasons not to (the defeater approach). Neuroscience reveals how much perception is construction, not passive reception, but this makes the question richer rather than settled. We know the brain actively models reality from fragmentary sensory data. Does that vindicate skepticism (perception is always interpretation) or Yogācāra (consciousness is doing the work)? Virtual reality and AI-generated content give Descartes's demon a technological upgrade. The question persists because perception remains the unavoidable starting point for knowledge, and we still can't fully explain the gap—or connection—between sensory appearance and reality.
