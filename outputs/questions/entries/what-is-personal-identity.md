# What is personal identity?

You wake up in a hospital after an accident, memories scattered. The person in the mirror looks like you—same face, same body. But if you'd lost all memory of who you were, would you still be you? What if your brain were transplanted into another body? What if, cell by cell, you were gradually replaced until none of the original remained? The question of personal identity asks: what makes you the same person over time?

## Why It Matters

Every legal system, moral framework, and human relationship assumes continuity of identity. We hold people responsible for past actions, make promises binding on our future selves, care about what happens to "us" tomorrow. But this assumes there's a stable "you" connecting past, present, and future. If personal identity is an illusion, our entire ethical architecture collapses. Why should I keep your promise if I'm not really the same person who made it? Why punish someone today for what a different person did yesterday? The question determines whether the self is a fact or a useful fiction.

## Three Approaches

**Locke's psychological continuity** grounds identity in memory and consciousness. In his *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689), Locke argued that a person persists as long as consciousness extends backward to past actions and experiences. The same body doesn't make you the same person—what matters is the continuous chain of psychological states. His thought experiment: a prince's mind transferred to a cobbler's body would be the prince, not the cobbler. Memory constitutes the self. But Locke's critics immediately spotted the problem: memory is fallible. If you forget something you did, did you not do it? Does an amnesiac become a different person?

**Parfit's reductionism** takes Locke's insight to its radical conclusion. In *Reasons and Persons* (1984), Parfit argues personal identity isn't what matters—psychological continuity is, but it doesn't require a deep further fact of identity. Through thought experiments involving spectrum cases and fission (splitting one person into two), he shows identity is not all-or-nothing but admits of degrees. If your brain hemispheres were separated and transplanted into two bodies, both resulting persons would be psychologically continuous with you, but they can't both be identical to you (they're not identical to each other). Parfit concludes: what matters in survival isn't identity but psychological connectedness and continuity. The self is a convention, not a metaphysical fact.

**Buddha's anatta doctrine** denies there's anything to be identical with in the first place. In the *Milindapañha* (Questions of King Milinda), the monk Nagasena uses the chariot analogy: just as a chariot is nothing beyond wheels, axle, and frame assembled, a person is nothing beyond the five aggregates (form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness). There's no underlying self that "has" these aggregates—they arise and pass in causal sequence. What we call identity is just one moment's aggregates causing the next in a continuous but impermanent stream. The question "Are you the same person?" is like asking "Is this the same river?"—it assumes a stability that close examination reveals doesn't exist.

## The Method

Locke used conceptual analysis and thought experiments to isolate what we really mean by "same person." Parfit employed rigorous logical analysis of identity claims and imaginative fission cases to show the concept breaks down. Buddha taught direct meditative observation of the aggregates, watching thought and sensation arise and pass without any experiencer behind them. These different methods reveal different aspects of the problem: Locke shows identity tracks psychological continuity, Parfit shows this continuity doesn't require metaphysical identity, Buddha shows both identity and continuity are conventional descriptions of impermanent processes.

## Where It Breaks

Locke can't handle memory loss or false memories without fracturing identity. Parfit's fission cases are science fiction—do they tell us about actual persons or just conceptual possibilities? And if identity doesn't matter, why do we care so fiercely about our future selves? Buddha's no-self doctrine explains impermanence but struggles to account for moral responsibility—if there's no self, who accumulates karma, who gets enlightened? All three approaches face the circularity problem: explaining personal identity requires referring to psychological states, but psychological states seem to belong to persons. Which comes first?

## Living Consequences

Believe Locke: you are your memories and experiences. Alzheimer's becomes a kind of death; radical personality change (brain injury, dementia) means you might lose someone while their body survives. Believe Parfit: stop fearing death so intensely—the connection between you-now and you-tomorrow is no stronger than the connection you already accept between you-yesterday and you-today. Care about what happens to future people with Parfitian impartiality. Believe Buddha: drop the exhausting project of maintaining a consistent self. Stop defending a reputation that belongs to someone who no longer exists. Watch your life like weather—impersonal, arising, passing.

## The Question Now

Contemporary philosophy has largely accepted Parfit's reductionism—there's no deep metaphysical self, just biological and psychological continuity. Neuroscience reveals memory as reconstructive, the unified self as an illusion generated by distributed brain processes. But the debate continues over what follows from this. Does eliminativism about the self undermine ethics, or can we ground responsibility in causal continuity without identity? Can personal identity be conventional and still matter? The question persists: not whether you're the same person, but what you're asking when you ask.
