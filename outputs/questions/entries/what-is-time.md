# What is time?

Time seems obvious until you try to explain it. We measure it, plan around it, feel it passing—yet the closer we look, the stranger it becomes. Is it a container events happen in, or just the pattern of change itself? Does it flow, or do we? And why can we remember the past but not the future?

## Why It Matters

How we understand time shapes how we understand everything else. If time is an illusion, our sense of self as continuous beings dissolves. If it's fundamental, then becoming is as real as being. The question determines whether free will is possible, whether the past still exists, whether the universe has a direction. Physics treats time as a dimension like space; experience treats it as radically different. Reconciling these views—or choosing between them—changes what we think reality ultimately is.

## Three Approaches

**Augustine** saw the problem: measure time and it vanishes under scrutiny. The past no longer exists, the future doesn't exist yet, and the present has no duration. So what are we measuring? His answer: time exists in the mind—as memory (past), attention (present), and expectation (future). The soul is distended across these modes. "What, then, is time?" he asked in *Confessions*. "If no one asks me, I know; if I want to explain it, I do not know." This makes time real but subjective, grounded in consciousness rather than physics.

**McTaggart** proved—or thought he did—that time is unreal. In his 1908 paper "The Unreality of Time," he distinguished two ways of ordering events: the A-series (past, present, future—constantly changing) and the B-series (earlier than, simultaneous with, later than—fixed relations). The A-series seems essential to time—we need genuine becoming, not just static relations. But it generates contradictions: every event must be past, present, and future, which is impossible. Meanwhile, the B-series, which is consistent, isn't really temporal—it's just ordered positions. Either way, time fails to be what we think it is.

**Bergson** argued we've been asking the wrong question. Clock time—*temps*—the physicist's dimension, is a spatialized abstraction useful for prediction but alien to lived experience. Real time is *durée*, duration: the continuous flow of consciousness where past and present interpenetrate, where each moment carries all previous moments within it. Memory isn't retrieval from storage; it's the past surviving in the present. In *Time and Free Will*, he showed how thinking of time as a line of discrete moments falsifies experience. Duration is qualitative, indivisible, and known only from within.

## The Method

Augustine used introspection and conceptual analysis—examining how temporal language works, what we can and can't coherently say. McTaggart employed pure logic, deriving contradictions from what time would have to be. Bergson insisted on phenomenology—describing immediate experience before theory distorts it. The methods differ radically: Augustine probes the limits of understanding, McTaggart builds formal arguments, Bergson tries to strip away intellectual habits and see clearly. Each method reveals different aspects of the problem, but they barely speak to each other.

## Where It Breaks

Augustine makes time dependent on minds, which seems wrong—did time not exist before consciousness? McTaggart's argument turns on whether the A-series is truly contradictory or just perspectival; many philosophers reject his proof while admitting the problem runs deep. Bergson's duration is rich but doesn't explain coordination—how do separate durations line up enough for us to meet for coffee? And none adequately addresses physics. Einstein showed time is relative to reference frames, dilates near mass, and is inseparable from space. Any theory of time ignoring relativity is incomplete.

## Living Consequences

If Augustine is right, time is always interpreted, always shaped by how we attend. Living well means managing that attention—memory, presence, hope. If McTaggart is right and time is unreal, our anxiety about death and loss is confused; nothing truly passes away. If Bergson is right, clock-watching betrays experience; we should resist reducing life to measurable intervals and recover the sense of duration. Contemporary physics suggests the "now" is local and observer-dependent—there is no universal present moment. That changes what it means to say someone exists right now.

## The Question Now

Physics has complicated the question enormously. Block universe theories suggest past, present, and future are equally real—time is just a dimension we travel through perspectively. Growing block theories allow genuine becoming. Presentism insists only the present exists. Meanwhile, phenomenology and cognitive science explore temporal consciousness—how brains construct the experience of time from timeless neural processes. The question has split: there's time as physics describes it, and time as we experience it. Whether these are the same thing, and how to bridge them, remains open.
