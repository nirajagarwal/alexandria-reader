# What is colonialism?

Not the historical fact of empires expanding, but the philosophical problem: can one people's domination of another ever be justified? How do we think about power, civilization, and moral standing when cultures collide? Colonialism forces philosophy to confront its own complicity—the ideas that rationalized conquest and the concepts needed to condemn it.

## Why It Matters

Every major Enlightenment value—reason, progress, universalism, rights—has been weaponized to justify domination while simultaneously providing tools for liberation. The question isn't just about historical injustice. It's about whether philosophical categories themselves (civilization/barbarism, rational/primitive, developed/backward) function as instruments of power. How we answer shapes contemporary debates about reparations, cultural appropriation, international intervention, and whether "universal" values are universal or just European.

## Three Approaches

**Frantz Fanon** saw colonialism as a systematic dehumanization requiring violent response. In *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961), he argued that colonial power isn't just political—it's ontological. The colonizer creates the "native" as a category, transforming human beings into zones of non-being. The colonized exists in a Manichean world divided absolutely between settler and native, human and subhuman. Violence isn't regrettable but necessary: "Decolonization is always a violent phenomenon" because only violence can shatter the colonial world's fundamental structure. The colonized doesn't petition for humanity—they seize it. Fanon's psychiatric training showed him how colonization penetrates consciousness itself, creating internalized racism and psychological splitting. Liberation requires not just political independence but psychological rebirth.

**Edward Said** exposed how knowledge itself colonizes. *Orientalism* (1978) demonstrated that Western scholarship about "the East" wasn't objective study but power disguised as knowledge. The Orient exists as Europe's inferior opposite—irrational where Europe is rational, despotic where Europe is free, stagnant where Europe progresses. This isn't accidental bias but systematic: colonial power requires producing the colonized as knowable, manageable, inherently subordinate. The scholar's archive and the administrator's policy work together. Said showed how Enlightenment universalism and colonial domination weren't contradictions but partners—the claim to understand all cultures rationally justified controlling them. Cultural representation is political domination by other means.

**Gayatri Spivak** asked whether the colonized can speak within structures of colonial knowledge. Her essay "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988) argued that even anti-colonial resistance gets ventriloquized through Western categories. When Western feminists "give voice" to third-world women, they often reimpose the very structures they claim to dismantle. The subaltern—those completely outside power—cannot speak not because they lack voice but because the discursive systems available can't hear them except as objects. Spivak demanded that intellectuals recognize complicity: even critique may reinscribe domination. The solution isn't simple inclusion but recognizing how knowledge production itself remains a colonial scene.

## The Method

Fanon: psychoanalytic phenomenology applied to colonial experience. Analyzing how power shapes consciousness and how violence reconstructs it.

Said: genealogical reading of texts, institutions, and discourses. Tracking how power operates through representation and knowledge.

Spivak: deconstructive reading attending to what discourse excludes. Interrogating the position from which intellectuals speak about oppression.

All three refuse philosophy's dream of neutral ground. Method and politics are inseparable.

## Where It Breaks

Fanon's celebration of violence troubles many—does revolutionary violence risk becoming what it opposes? His gendered metaphors (the nation as woman to be liberated) replicate patriarchal structures.

Said's Orientalism risks essentialism—if "the West" is monolithic in its colonial gaze, haven't we created a new binary? Some argue he underestimates resistance and agency within colonial contexts.

Spivak's position verges on paralysis—if speaking for the subaltern reinscribes domination, what political action remains possible? Critics ask whether her theoretical sophistication serves political struggle or replaces it.

## Living Consequences

These thinkers make comfortable liberal positions untenable. You can't celebrate "Western values" while ignoring their colonial deployment. You can't advocate for others without examining whether you're speaking for them or over them. Reading a museum placard about "primitive art," watching humanitarian intervention justified by civilization's burden, hearing "we're all one human family"—suddenly the colonial present becomes visible. The question becomes: how do we think, write, and act without reproducing the very structures we oppose? There may be no clean answer.

## The Question Now

Post-colonial theory has entered the mainstream—decolonizing curricula, reckoning with monuments, land acknowledgments. But the fundamental question persists: can former colonizers and colonized share philosophical ground, or does that ground itself bear colonial architecture? Contemporary debates about cultural appropriation, reparations, and "decolonizing" institutions return to Spivak's question: who speaks, for whom, and in whose language? The rise of "decolonial" philosophy from Latin America (Dussel, Mignolo) challenges even post-colonial theory as too embedded in European frameworks. Colonialism isn't just history—it's the condition under which we still think.
