# Promethium

***Element Born from Human Ingenuity***

> **Atomic Number:** 61 | **Symbol:** Pm | **Category:** Lanthanide

Promethium stands alone among the rare earth elements. Every other lanthanide occurs naturally in Earth's crust. Promethium does not. Its longest-lived isotope, promethium-145, has a half-life of 17.7 years. Any primordial promethium decayed billions of years ago. What exists today comes from nuclear reactors or particle accelerators, a purely human creation named for the Titan who stole fire from the gods.

The search for element 61 consumed decades. Spectroscopic lines suggested its presence in minerals, but extraction always failed. The reason became clear in 1945 when Oak Ridge National Laboratory isolated promethium-147 from uranium fission products. The element did not hide in nature. It simply was not there. All promethium in use today descends from that first artificial synthesis. A few kilograms exist worldwide at any moment, nearly all of it in specialized applications where its radioactive decay provides precisely calibrated energy. The element that does not occur naturally has found narrow but essential roles in human technology.

## Stolen Fire

In Greek mythology, Prometheus defied Zeus to bring fire to humanity and suffered eternal punishment for the theft. The naming of element 61 in 1949 echoed that act of transgression. Here was an element that nature had not preserved, that humans had to create for themselves.

The name came from Charles Coryell, Grace Mary Coryell, and Jacob Marinsky at Oak Ridge. They had isolated the element from nuclear waste, a byproduct of the Manhattan Project. The choice carried deliberate symbolism. Nuclear fission was humanity's stolen fire, a power drawn from the atom itself, dangerous and transformative.

Earlier claimants to the discovery had proposed names like florentium and illinium. None held. The element belonged to the nuclear age, and its name acknowledged the ambiguous gift of that era.

## Decades of False Starts

The periodic table showed a gap at atomic number 61. Between neodymium and samarium, something should exist. Chemists found it repeatedly, then lost it.

In 1902, Bohuslav Brauner detected spectral lines in a cerium sample. He called the element czechium. The lines vanished upon further purification. In 1926, Italian researchers Luigi Rolla and Lorenzo Fernandes claimed isolation from Brazilian monazite sand and named it florentium. Their evidence did not hold up to scrutiny. That same year, American chemists at the University of Illinois, B. Smith Hopkins and colleagues, reported extraction from the same mineral and proposed illinium. Chemical analysis seemed to confirm the presence of a new element.

All were chasing traces of decay products or misidentifying contaminants. The mineral samples contained no stable promethium because no stable promethium exists. The successful isolation had to wait for a source that produced the element continuously: a nuclear reactor.

## Nuclear Orphan

Promethium has no stable isotopes. Every atomic configuration decays. The most stable, promethium-145, breaks down through electron capture, converting a proton to a neutron and transforming into neodymium. Promethium-147, the most commonly used isotope, undergoes beta decay with a half-life of 2.62 years, becoming samarium.

This instability explains its absence from Earth's crust. Any promethium present at planetary formation decayed within a few million years. Trace amounts form continuously through spontaneous fission of uranium-238 in ore deposits, but concentrations remain far below detection thresholds for conventional mining.

Stars produce promethium briefly during nucleosynthesis, and spectral analysis has detected it in the atmospheres of certain red giants. There it serves as an indicator of recent nuclear burning. Its presence confirms ongoing fusion processes because its short half-life means any observed promethium formed recently in stellar terms.

For practical purposes, promethium exists only where nuclear reactions create it: reactors, accelerators, or the hearts of stars.

## Fission's Byproduct

Modern promethium comes from uranium fission products in nuclear reactors. When uranium-235 splits, the fragments include promethium-147 at yields around 2.6 percent. The element concentrates in spent fuel rods alongside dozens of other radioisotopes.

Oak Ridge National Laboratory developed the first separation techniques, using ion exchange chromatography to isolate promethium from the complex mixture. The process remains demanding. Promethium sits chemically between other lanthanides, making separation slow and expensive.

Annual world production amounts to a few hundred grams. Russia and the United States maintain the primary production capability. The element appears as a pale yellowish metal when freshly prepared, though sustained observation proves difficult. Promethium's own radioactivity damages its crystal structure and produces enough heat to warm samples noticeably.

Few applications justify the cost and complexity. Those that exist exploit the element's radioactive properties rather than its chemistry.

## Atomic Batteries

Promethium-147 powers radioisotope thermoelectric generators in specific niches. The element emits low-energy beta particles that can be converted to electricity or simply used as a heat source. Unlike stronger radioactive materials, promethium's emissions stop at thin shielding, simplifying safety concerns.

Soviet spacecraft used promethium batteries during the 1960s. The Lunokhod rovers that explored the Moon carried promethium heat sources to survive the lunar night. Early cardiac pacemakers, before lithium batteries matured, employed tiny promethium power cells. The patient's body shielded the low-energy radiation while the device ran for years without replacement.

Thickness gauges in industry use promethium sources. A detector measures how much radiation passes through material like paper or sheet metal. Changes in transmission indicate variations in thickness. The setup requires minimal shielding and produces no external hazard.

Most of these applications have declined. Better alternatives exist for nearly every use. Promethium remains where its specific decay characteristics match requirements exactly.

## Luminous Paint Without Radium

Promethium found brief use in luminous paints as a safer replacement for radium. Mixed with phosphors, promethium-147 provides a continuous glow without external charging. The low-energy beta emissions excite the phosphor but penetrate less than radium's gamma rays.

Watch dials, instrument panels, and exit signs incorporated promethium paint through the 1960s and 1970s. The advantage over radium was clear. No gamma radiation meant no long-term health risk to manufacturers or users. The paint required no external light to charge, unlike photoluminescent alternatives.

Tritium eventually displaced promethium in these applications. Tritium's longer half-life (12.3 years versus 2.6 years) and lower energy beta emissions made it cheaper and safer still. Promethium paint faded relatively quickly as the isotope decayed, while tritium maintained brightness longer.

Some aerospace and military equipment still uses promethium markers where its characteristics suit the application. Production remains limited and specialized.

## The Lanthanide That Isn't There

Promethium occupies position 61 in the periodic table, embedded in the lanthanide series. Its chemistry follows lanthanide patterns precisely. It forms a +3 oxidation state, coordinates with similar ligands, and separates from its neighbors with difficulty.

Researchers study promethium primarily to complete understanding of lanthanide behavior. The element fills a gap in the series, allowing comparison of properties across the full sequence. Spectroscopic studies examine how its electronic structure fits theoretical predictions.

Handling remains challenging. Samples are always radioactive, always decaying, always warm from their own emissions. Crystal structures damage themselves through internal radiation. Long-term experiments become impossible as the sample transforms into samarium.

The element serves as a reminder that the periodic table contains spaces nature chose not to fill with stable matter. Promethium exists because humans create it, a temporary resident of a permanent address.
