# Tennessine

***Born in a Particle Beam***

> **Atomic Number:** 117 | **Symbol:** Ts | **Category:** Halogen

Tennessine exists because humans willed it into being. No natural reserves. No cosmic abundance. This element emerges only when berkelium-249 targets face calcium-48 projectiles in particle accelerators, colliding at velocities that overcome electromagnetic repulsion and fuse atomic cores. The first atoms appeared in 2010 at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia. They persisted for less than a hundred milliseconds before decaying through alpha emission chains. Only a few dozen atoms have ever existed. Tennessine occupies the penultimate position among halogens, that column of reactive elements descending from fluorine through chlorine, bromine, iodine, and astatine. Theory predicts it shares their chemical hunger for electrons, though relativistic effects at such high atomic numbers may alter its behavior in ways fluorine cannot anticipate. No bulk sample will ever exist. No properties can be measured directly. This element is known entirely through inference, decay signatures, and quantum calculations.

## The Dubna Synthesis

Creating tennessine required a target that does not exist in nature. Berkelium-249, already a synthetic element, had to be produced in milligram quantities at Oak Ridge National Laboratory in Tennessee. The material traveled to Russia under international scientific collaboration. A calcium-48 beam, itself enriched and accelerated, bombarded this target for months. Detectors watched for the characteristic decay pattern: an atom of tennessine-294 splitting into moscovium, then nihonium, then roentgenium, each step releasing an alpha particle with specific energy. Six such events confirmed the element in the initial experiments. Later runs at Oak Ridge and Riken in Japan added to the tally. The entire global inventory of tennessine atoms that have ever existed would fit on a pinhead with room to spare, if they had not already decayed.

## The Halogen Question

Periodic trends suggest tennessine should behave as a halogen. These elements sit one electron short of a complete outer shell and react aggressively to fill that gap. Fluorine strips electrons from nearly anything. Chlorine forms table salt. Bromine burns skin on contact. Iodine colors antiseptics brown. Astatine, the element above tennessine, is so rare and short-lived that its chemistry remains largely theoretical. Tennessine stands even further out. Relativistic effects, where electrons move at appreciable fractions of light speed near such massive nuclei, distort electron orbitals and may suppress halogen reactivity. Some calculations suggest tennessine might behave more like a noble gas or a metalloid. The question will remain theoretical. No experiment can isolate enough atoms long enough to test reactivity. The element's chemistry exists as probability distributions in supercomputer simulations.

## The Tennessee Connection

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry approved the name tennessine in 2016, honoring Oak Ridge National Laboratory and the state surrounding it. Tennessee appears in an element name because that state hosts facilities capable of breeding rare transcurium isotopes. The High Flux Isotope Reactor and Radiochemical Engineering Development Center at Oak Ridge produced the berkelium target essential to the discovery. This marks the second element named for a U.S. state, after californium. The naming follows tradition: elements often honor places where discovery work occurred. Dubna appears in dubnium. Berkeley in berkelium. The names encode a geography of nuclear science, mapping the handful of sites worldwide with accelerators powerful enough and targets exotic enough to push the periodic table to its current limits.

## The Superheavy Frontier

Tennessine belongs to the seventh period of the periodic table, the row of superheavy elements extending from francium through oganesson. Every member is synthetic and radioactive. Many exist for only fractions of a second. Their creation requires sophisticated accelerator facilities, international collaboration, and years of beam time. The work proceeds atom by atom, event by event. Detectors wait months for a single decay chain. Theorists predict an "island of stability" somewhere in this superheavy region, isotopes with unusually long half-lives, perhaps seconds or minutes rather than milliseconds. Tennessine-294 is not on that island. Its half-life measures about 50 milliseconds. Heavier isotopes might last longer, but synthesizing them would require even more exotic target-projectile combinations, materials harder to produce and collisions less likely to succeed.

## Limits of Matter

The periodic table does not extend infinitely. Somewhere beyond oganesson, atomic nuclei become impossible to create or too unstable to detect. The electromagnetic repulsion between protons overwhelms the strong nuclear force binding them together. Electrons in the innermost orbitals spiral at relativistic speeds, creating quantum effects that undermine conventional chemistry. Element 119 and 120 are theoretical targets for the next generation of experiments, but each step requires more beam intensity, longer run times, rarer targets. Tennessine marks a way station in this progression. Creating it demonstrated that elements could be synthesized in the 117 position despite the enormous technical barriers. The knowledge gained informs attempts to push further. Each new element refines understanding of nuclear structure at the edge of stability, where matter barely holds together long enough to be called matter.

## The Theoretical Element

No one will ever hold tennessine. No compound incorporating it will ever be synthesized. Its electron configuration, chemical bonds, crystal structure, melting point, density, and color remain matters of quantum mechanical prediction. Studies of tennessine mean analyzing decay products, measuring alpha particle energies, timing disintegration chains. The element exists as data points, confirmation that certain nuclear arrangements can briefly persist. This abstraction does not diminish its place in the periodic table. The table arranges elements by atomic number, the count of protons in the nucleus. Tennessine has 117 protons. Whether those protons bind together for a minute or a millisecond, whether in bulk the material would shine or corrode or sublime, the element occupies its position. The periodic table describes possibility as much as substance. Tennessine is possible, barely, at the cost of enormous effort. That suffices.
