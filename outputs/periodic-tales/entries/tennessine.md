# Tennessine

***Synthetic Element, Fleeting Existence***

> **Atomic Number:** 117 | **Symbol:** Ts | **Category:** Halogen

Tennessine does not exist in nature. It comes into being only through deliberate collision, atoms of berkelium struck by calcium-48 nuclei in a particle accelerator. The resulting atoms last milliseconds before decay. In those brief moments, this element occupies a place on the periodic table predicted decades before its creation, the second-heaviest halogen, positioned below iodine and astatine in a family known for reactivity. Its synthesis required years of preparation and international collaboration. The element represents the outer edge of the known periodic table, where matter itself becomes increasingly unstable, where the question is not what an element does but whether it can exist at all.

The creation of tennessine began with a problem of supply. Berkelium-249, one of the target materials required for synthesis, exists in quantities measured in milligrams. Only one facility in the United States produces it: Oak Ridge National Laboratory in Tennessee. Russian scientists at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna needed this material. American scientists needed access to Dubna's accelerator technology. The collaboration that followed brought together teams separated by eight time zones and decades of Cold War isolation. In 2010, the first atoms appeared. Six atoms total across the initial experiments, each lasting less than a tenth of a second before fragmenting into lighter elements.

The name honors Tennessee, where the berkelium originated, and the Oak Ridge laboratory's role in nuclear science extending back to the Manhattan Project. It follows a tradition of naming elements after places, but here the place names a state rather than a nation or city, a recognition of regional contribution to global science. The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry approved the name in 2016. Tennessine became one of four new elements announced that year, filling gaps in the seventh row of the periodic table.

Tennessine sits in Group 17, the halogens, elements known for forming salts and for high reactivity. Fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine, astatine—each lighter than the last, each less reactive as atomic mass increases. Theoretical calculations suggest tennessine might break this pattern. Relativistic effects, the behavior of electrons moving at speeds approaching light around such a heavy nucleus, could alter its chemistry fundamentally. It might act less like a halogen and more like a metal. It might form compounds unlike those of any element above it. These predictions remain untested. No sample has ever been large enough or stable enough for chemical experiments.

The isotope tennessine-294, the most stable yet produced, lasts approximately 80 milliseconds. In that interval, it undergoes alpha decay, ejecting a helium nucleus and transforming into moscovium-290. That isotope decays in turn. The chain continues through nihonium, roentgenium, meitnerium, bohrium, dubnium, lawrencium, and mendelevium before reaching fermium-253, which persists for about three days. Each atom of tennessine thus initiates a cascade, a sequence of transformations ending in something comparatively stable. Scientists track these decay chains to confirm tennessine's creation, watching for the characteristic energy signatures of each step.

The element occupies a theoretical frontier. The periodic table extends only so far. Beyond a certain atomic number, elements become too unstable to persist. But theory predicts islands of stability, regions where particular combinations of protons and neutrons create nuclei that last minutes, hours, perhaps longer. Tennessine lies near one predicted island. Its mere existence, however brief, suggests that superheavy elements can be synthesized. Laboratories in Germany, Japan, and China now join the pursuit, pushing toward element 119, element 120, testing whether the periodic table has a natural limit or whether patience and energy can extend it indefinitely.

Applications remain distant, perhaps nonexistent. Elements that decay in milliseconds serve no practical function. The synthesis of a single atom costs millions of dollars in accelerator time and target preparation. But utility was never the point. Tennessine answers questions about nuclear structure, about how many protons and neutrons can coexist, about where chemistry ends and physics alone governs. It tests models of the atomic nucleus, confirms or refutes predictions about shell closures and binding energies. Each atom produced adds a data point to humanity's map of possible matter.

The element will never be isolated in bulk, never be held or weighed or photographed. It exists as traces in detector output, decay signals recorded and analyzed. Yet it is as real as iron or carbon, governed by the same laws, built from the same fundamental particles. Its place on the periodic table is permanent, even if its atoms are not.
