# Californium

***Born in Berkeley's Cyclotron***

> **Atomic Number:** 98 | **Symbol:** Cf | **Category:** Actinide

Californium does not exist on Earth except where humans make it. No stellar process produces it in quantity. No mineral contains it. It appears only in nuclear reactors and particle accelerators, forged atom by atom through deliberate bombardment of lighter elements.

The element emerged in 1950 at the University of California, Berkeley, when researchers struck curium-242 with alpha particles. They named it for the state and university where it came to be. Only micrograms exist at any moment. A gram of californium-252 costs tens of millions of dollars.

Despite this scarcity, californium has found use. Its isotope californium-252 undergoes spontaneous fission, releasing neutrons at prodigious rates. A single microgram emits 170 million neutrons per second. This makes it invaluable for neutron radiography, well logging in oil exploration, and as a neutron startup source in nuclear reactors. Medicine employs it in cancer treatment, directing neutron beams at tumors resistant to conventional radiation.

The element decays rapidly. Californium-252, the most useful isotope, has a half-life of 2.6 years. Production is continuous, expensive, and limited to a handful of facilities worldwide.

## The Berkeley Synthesis

Glenn Seaborg, Albert Ghiorso, Kenneth Street Jr., and Stanley Thompson created element 98 by bombarding microgram quantities of curium-242 with 35 MeV alpha particles in Berkeley's 60-inch cyclotron. They detected californium atoms through their decay signatures, working with samples too small to see. The announcement came in March 1950. The team had already discovered americium, curium, berkelium, and would go on to discover five more transuranium elements. Their systematic approach to synthesizing superheavy elements transformed nuclear chemistry.

## Neutron Fountains

Californium-252 spontaneously fissions without external bombardment, splitting into lighter elements and releasing neutrons. One microgram produces more neutrons per second than most small laboratory sources. Industries use these neutron emissions to peer inside sealed containers, analyze coal for mineral content, detect explosives, and map water tables in oil fields. The neutrons penetrate where X-rays and gamma rays cannot, revealing composition through the way different elements slow or capture neutrons. Portable californium sources bring nuclear analysis to field sites.

## Medical Isotopes

Neutron brachytherapy employs californium-252 to treat certain cancers, particularly those of the cervix and brain. Physicians place sealed californium sources near or within tumors. The intense neutron flux damages cancer cell DNA more effectively than photon radiation for some tumor types. The treatment remains rare due to californium's cost and limited availability. A few specialized facilities maintain californium inventories. Each source requires careful handling, precise calibration, and eventual return for disposal.

## Oak Ridge Production

The United States produces californium-252 at Oak Ridge National Laboratory in Tennessee through a multi-year process. Plutonium-239 undergoes neutron bombardment in the High Flux Isotope Reactor, gradually transforming through successive neutron captures into heavier actinides. The target material moves through americium, curium, berkelium, and finally californium. After years of irradiation, technicians chemically separate the californium from other products. The annual production measures tens of milligrams. Russia maintains the only other significant production capability.

## Half-Life Economics

Californium's decay imposes harsh economic realities. A californium-252 source loses half its strength every 2.6 years through radioactive decay. Users must purchase new sources regularly or accept declining performance. Storage facilities monitor inventory constantly, knowing their stock diminishes daily. This decay also generates heat and radiation, requiring specialized shielding and cooling. The element exists in a perpetual race between production and disappearance. Every californium atom made will be gone within decades, regardless of how carefully stored.

## Transuranium Chemistry

Californium helped establish the chemistry of the actinide series. Its behavior confirmed that elements 89 through 103 form a distinct series analogous to the lanthanides. Californium typically exists in the +3 oxidation state in solution, though +2 and +4 states occur under specific conditions. These chemical properties guide separation techniques used to isolate californium from production targets. The element forms compounds with oxygen, halogens, and other elements, most of which glow from self-heating caused by radioactive decay.

## The Weight Barrier

Californium represents one of the last elements produced in weighable quantities. Beyond californium, elements exist only as individual atoms created momentarily in accelerators. Einsteinium and fermium have been made in nanogram amounts. Elements 100 and higher remain in the realm of single atoms observed through decay chains. Californium sits at the edge of bulk matter, the heaviest element for which chemistry can be conducted on visible samples. Past this point, the periodic table becomes a collection of names attached to fleeting atomic events.
