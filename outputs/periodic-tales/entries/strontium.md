# Strontium

***Fireworks and Fallout***

> **Atomic Number:** 38 | **Symbol:** Sr | **Category:** Alkaline Earth Metal

Strontium burns crimson. This single property, discovered in the eighteenth century, made it the signature of celebration and the marker of catastrophe. A soft silvery metal that oxidizes instantly in air, it exists in nature only in compounds, primarily celestite and strontianite. The element concentrates in certain minerals and, fatefully, in bone. Its isotopes divide cleanly: most are stable and benign, one is radioactive and persistent. The same chemistry that produces brilliant red flares also drew strontium-90 into the food chain after atmospheric nuclear tests. What began as a curiosity from Scottish mines became first a pyrotechnic standard, then a biological tracer, then a Cold War anxiety, and finally a tool for dating both geological formations and criminal remains.

In 1787, Adair Crawford examined a mineral from lead mines near Strontian, Scotland. The substance differed from known compounds. William Cruickshank confirmed a new earth in 1792. Humphry Davy isolated the metal itself in 1808 through electrolysis, the same year he extracted barium, calcium, and magnesium. Strontium joined its family, the alkaline earth metals, each reactive but not explosive, each forming characteristic compounds, each claiming a distinct color when heated.

The crimson flame became strontium's calling card. Strontium salts, particularly strontium nitrate and strontium carbonate, produce the purest red in pyrotechnics. No other element delivers this wavelength with such intensity and clarity. The fireworks industry adopted strontium compounds as standard by the mid-nineteenth century. Emergency flares rely on the same chemistry. The red glow that marks distress or celebration comes from electrons in strontium atoms dropping to lower energy states, releasing photons at 650 nanometers. The color is not merely red but a specific scarlet, unmistakable and consistent.

Strontium occurs throughout Earth's crust at about 370 parts per million, more abundant than carbon but less than zinc. It concentrates in celestite, strontium sulfate, which forms sedimentary deposits, and strontianite, strontium carbonate, found in hydrothermal veins. The element follows calcium through geological and biological systems. Their similar ionic radii and charges mean strontium substitutes for calcium in many processes. This chemical mimicry would prove consequential.

During the 1950s, atmospheric nuclear weapons tests released strontium-90 into the stratosphere. This isotope, a fission product of uranium and plutonium, has a half-life of 28.8 years. Fallout descended globally. Strontium-90 entered soil, was taken up by plants, consumed by cattle, concentrated in milk, and deposited in human bone. Children, whose bones were actively growing, accumulated the highest levels. The isotope decays by beta emission, irradiating bone marrow continuously. Studies of baby teeth collected during the 1950s and 1960s documented the spike in strontium-90 levels. The measurements helped establish the biological cost of atmospheric testing and contributed to the 1963 Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty. Levels have declined steadily since.

Stable strontium serves medicine and industry. Strontium-89, with a half-life of 50.5 days, treats bone cancer. Administered intravenously, it targets metastases in bone, delivering targeted radiation. Strontium ranelate, a pharmaceutical compound, was prescribed for osteoporosis until cardiovascular risks emerged. Strontium titanate crystals, with their high refractive index, once substituted for diamond before cubic zirconia dominated that market. Ferrite magnets incorporate strontium to enhance magnetic properties. Zinc refining uses strontium to remove lead impurities.

Forensic science employs strontium isotope ratios to trace geographic origins. The ratio of strontium-87 to strontium-86 varies by geology because rubidium-87 decays slowly to strontium-87. This ratio, absorbed through diet, records in teeth and bone. Analyzing these ratios reveals where a person lived during tooth formation or years before death. The method has identified victims, tracked migrations, and authenticated historical remains. The same technique dates geological formations and provides evidence in cases of food fraud, verifying the claimed origins of agricultural products.

The element poses minimal toxicity in its stable forms. Strontium substitutes for calcium without the severe disruption caused by lead or cadmium. Exposure to stable strontium, even at elevated levels, produces no significant poisoning. The hazard is radiological, not chemical. Strontium-90 remains a concern at nuclear facilities and contaminated sites. Its presence in weapons fallout and reactor accidents like Chernobyl required long-term monitoring of food supplies and agricultural land. The isotope's residence in bone and its decay characteristics make internal contamination particularly problematic.

Production of strontium involves reduction of strontium oxide with aluminum in a vacuum at high temperature. China produces most of the world's supply, primarily for ferrite magnets. Annual production runs to several hundred thousand tons of strontium compounds. The market is stable, not expanding. Substitutes exist for most applications, but pyrotechnics continue to rely on strontium for red coloration. No alternative matches its performance for this purpose. The demand remains steady, driven by celebrations worldwide and military requirements for signaling devices.
