# Platinum

***The Incorruptible Metal***

> **Atomic Number:** 78 | **Symbol:** Pt | **Category:** Transition Metal

Platinum formed in the violent collapse of massive stars and the collisions of neutron stars, processes that forge elements heavier than iron. The metal reaches Earth primarily through meteorite impacts and deep volcanic activity that brings it from the mantle. It resists corrosion absolutely. No single acid dissolves it. Air leaves it unchanged. Time does not tarnish it. This permanence, combined with rarity—one gram extracted for every ton of ore processed in most deposits—establishes its value. The metal catalyzes reactions without being consumed by them. Automobile exhaust passes through platinum-coated ceramic honeycombs that convert carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, and unburned hydrocarbons into less harmful compounds. Cancer drugs built on platinum frameworks attack tumor cells. The metal defines the kilogram, literally. A cylinder of platinum-iridium alloy in Paris served as the international prototype until 2019, when measurement shifted to fundamental constants. Where permanence matters, platinum appears.

---

Spanish conquistadors encountered platinum in Colombian rivers during the sixteenth century. They called it platina, little silver, considering it an inferior metal, an impediment to gold extraction. The miners threw it back into the rivers to ripen, believing it was unfinished gold. European scientists received samples in the eighteenth century and recognized something different: a metal denser than gold that resisted the standard methods of melting and working. William Hyde Wollaston developed a technique to render platinum malleable in 1805, making it practical for laboratory equipment and precise instruments.

The metal's chemical inertness made it essential for high-temperature work. Platinum crucibles could contain molten glass and corrosive chemicals without contamination. Standards of length and mass required materials that would not corrode or change dimension over centuries. By the late nineteenth century, platinum had become the substrate for scientific precision itself.

Catalytic properties emerged as the next frontier. Platinum speeds reactions involving hydrogen and oxygen without being consumed. The metal's surface provides a stage where molecules break apart and recombine, then release, leaving the platinum unchanged. This property drives the catalytic converter, mandatory in most automobiles since the 1970s. As exhaust gases pass through the device, platinum and its sister metals—palladium and rhodium—convert pollutants at temperatures above 400 degrees Celsius. A single catalytic converter contains roughly three to seven grams of platinum group metals.

Medical applications followed a chance observation. Barnett Rosenberg noticed in 1965 that platinum electrodes inhibited bacterial cell division. Investigation revealed that platinum complexes interfered with DNA replication. Cisplatin, approved for medical use in 1978, became a cornerstone of chemotherapy. The compound binds to DNA in cancer cells, preventing division and triggering cell death. Tumors of the testes, ovaries, bladder, and lungs respond to platinum-based treatment. The therapy is harsh—platinum affects healthy cells as well—but effective enough to remain in use decades later.

Supply concentrates dramatically. South Africa holds roughly eighty percent of known reserves, with Russia accounting for most of the remainder. The Bushveld Igneous Complex in South Africa contains the largest deposits, a geological formation 2 billion years old where platinum group metals crystallized in layers during magma cooling. Zimbabwe and Canada produce modest amounts. This geographic concentration creates vulnerability in industrial supply chains dependent on catalytic converters and other applications.

Recycling recovers roughly a quarter of annual platinum supply. Spent catalytic converters return to refineries where the metals are extracted and purified. Electronic components, laboratory equipment, and jewelry contribute smaller flows. The recycling process itself requires platinum group metals as catalysts, a closed loop of the incorruptible metal facilitating its own recovery.

Hydrogen fuel cells represent a potential expansion of demand. These devices generate electricity by combining hydrogen and oxygen, producing only water as exhaust. Platinum catalyzes both the splitting of hydrogen molecules and the reduction of oxygen. Each fuel cell requires platinum coatings on its electrodes. Automotive and stationary power applications remain limited by infrastructure and cost, but the chemistry works, constrained primarily by the metal's scarcity and price.

The metal endures in its applications as in its character. Once placed in a catalytic converter or fashioned into jewelry, platinum remains essentially unchanged by its service. The wedding ring passed through generations shows no corrosion. The catalyst in an industrial reactor performs identically after a decade. This permanence, born in stellar catastrophe and refined by geological chance, defines platinum's place in human systems where change must be managed but the manager must not change.
