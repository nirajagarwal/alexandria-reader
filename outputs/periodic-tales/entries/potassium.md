# Potassium

***Essential Electrolyte, Explosive Metal***

> **Atomic Number:** 19 | **Symbol:** K | **Category:** Alkali Metal

Potassium burns with a violet flame and explodes in water, yet every heartbeat depends on it. The seventh most abundant element in Earth's crust and the eighth most abundant in the human body, it exists as the soft silvery metal only under oil or inert atmosphere. In nature it occurs exclusively in compounds, leached from rocks by water, concentrated in ocean brine and mineral deposits, cycled endlessly through soil and living tissue. Humphry Davy isolated it in 1807 by passing electric current through molten potash, the first metal won by electrolysis. The metal itself sees little use. The compounds sustain agriculture and regulate the electrical potential across every cell membrane in every organism with a nervous system.

Davy named it after potash, the wood-ash residue from which he extracted it, though the symbol K derives from kalium, the Neo-Latin term rooted in the Arabic word for alkali. The discovery came during his systematic assault on the chemical elements using the voltaic pile. When current flowed through caustic potash, globules of metal formed at the negative wire and immediately burst into flame. Davy captured some under naphtha. The metal was lighter than water, soft as wax, and tarnished within seconds in air. Its violent reaction with water both fascinated and terrified early chemists.

The human body contains about 140 grams of potassium, most of it inside cells. Sodium predominates outside cells. This asymmetric distribution creates the electrical gradient that enables nerve impulses and muscle contractions. Sodium-potassium pumps embedded in cell membranes use energy to maintain this imbalance, ejecting three sodium ions for every two potassium ions brought in. When a nerve fires, channels open briefly and the ions rush toward equilibrium. The resulting wave of depolarization constitutes the nerve signal. Heart rhythm depends on the precise cycling of these channels. Too much or too little potassium in blood serum disrupts the cycle and can stop the heart.

Plants require potassium in quantities nearly as large as nitrogen and phosphorus. It activates enzymes, regulates water uptake, strengthens cell walls. Deficiency shows first in older leaves, which yellow at the edges. Potassium fertilizer, the third leg of the NPK triad that sustains industrial agriculture, comes mainly from ancient marine deposits. Sylvite and carnallite ores, remnants of evaporated seas, are mined in Saskatchewan, Russia, Belarus. Global production exceeds 40 million tons annually. Nearly all goes into fertilizer. The price affects food costs worldwide.

The metal finds niche applications. Alloyed with sodium in certain nuclear reactors as a liquid coolant. Used in specialized heat transfer systems. Its compounds have broader reach. Potassium hydroxide goes into batteries, soap, biodiesel production. Potassium nitrate serves as oxidizer in gunpowder and fireworks. Potassium chloride replaces sodium chloride for those limiting sodium intake, though the bitter metallic aftertaste marks the substitution. The same compound, injected in concentrated form, constitutes the third drug in the lethal injection protocol, stopping the heart by overwhelming its electrical system.

The geochemical cycle moves potassium from granite to ocean and back across hundreds of millions of years. Feldspar weathering releases potassium ions into groundwater. Rivers carry them to the sea. Marine organisms extract some. The rest remains dissolved until tectonic processes lift seafloor sediments, restarting the cycle. Human agriculture short-circuits this natural tempo, removing potassium with every harvest. Without replacement, soils exhaust within years. The fertilizer industry exists largely to service this deficit, mining ancient marine reserves to sustain current production levels on a timeline that cannot be sustained indefinitely.
