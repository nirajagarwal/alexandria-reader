# Tin

***Metal of Boxes and Solder***

> **Atomic Number:** 50 | **Symbol:** Sn | **Category:** Post-transition metal

Tin forms in the explosive deaths of massive stars, scattered through supernovae and gathered into planets during solar system formation. In Earth's crust it appears rarely, concentrated in cassiterite ore deposits where geological processes have worked for millions of years. The element exists in two distinct forms: white tin, the malleable metal of commerce, and gray tin, a brittle powder that emerges in extreme cold. This transformation, called tin pest, once rotted the buttons from Napoleon's soldiers in the Russian winter. Humans have worked tin for five thousand years, first in bronze and later as a coating for iron and steel. The metal resists corrosion, bends without breaking, and melts at temperatures achievable with wood fires. It remains essential to electronics, joining components in circuits that run on solder that is mostly tin.

## Bronze Age Foundation

Copper alone makes tools too soft for sustained labor. Mixed with tin—typically one part in ten—the alloy becomes bronze: harder, more durable, capable of holding an edge. This discovery, made independently across Eurasia around 3000 BCE, defined an era. Tin deposits were scarce. Ancient traders carried it from Cornwall, from Bohemia, from the mountains of Afghanistan. Control of tin sources meant control of bronze production, which meant military advantage. Empires rose along tin trade routes. The metal's scarcity shaped the geopolitics of the ancient world as surely as oil shapes the modern.

## The Tin Can

In 1810, Peter Durand patented the tin canister for preserved food. The innovation was simple: thin-rolled iron or steel, coated with a layer of tin to prevent rust, formed into sealed containers. Tin's resistance to corrosion and non-toxicity made food storage possible for months or years. Napoleon's armies adopted canned rations. British naval expeditions carried them to the Arctic. Early cans required hammer and chisel to open; the can opener was invented decades later. The technology transformed food distribution. Urban populations grew. Ships traveled farther. The tin can, more than refrigeration, enabled the modern global food system.

## Pewter and Plate

For centuries, pewter—an alloy of tin with small amounts of copper, antimony, or bismuth—served as the common person's silver. Plates, cups, spoons, candlesticks: functional objects made from a metal that didn't tarnish like silver or break like ceramic. European pewtersmiths formed guilds, developed standards, marked their work. By the 18th century, the wealthy coated copper vessels with tin to create affordable imitations of silver serving ware. Tin plating became an industry. The practice declined only when stainless steel and aluminum offered cheaper alternatives, but pewter persists in decorative objects, valued now for tradition rather than necessity.

## Solder's Domain

Electronic circuits require connections: components joined to boards, wires attached to terminals, chips mounted to substrates. Solder provides these joints. For decades, the standard was an alloy of 63% tin and 37% lead, which melts at 183 degrees Celsius and flows smoothly into tight spaces. The mixture cools to form strong electrical and mechanical bonds. When lead's toxicity became untenable, the electronics industry shifted to lead-free solders, typically 96-99% tin with small additions of silver and copper. These require higher temperatures and different techniques, but tin remains the essential element. Every circuit board in every device contains hundreds or thousands of tin joints holding the assembly together.

## Cassiterite and Conflict

Tin ore concentrates in weathered granite, washed into river gravels, mined by dredging or panning. The richest modern deposits lie in Southeast Asia, Central Africa, and South America. In the Democratic Republic of Congo, tin mining funds armed groups that control territory through violence. The metal enters supply chains that feed global electronics manufacturers. International regulations attempt to track ore sources and certify conflict-free tin, but enforcement is inconsistent. Artisanal miners work dangerous conditions for minimal pay. The element that makes modern technology possible carries a burden of exploitation that the industry has not resolved.

## Tin Whiskers

Thin filaments of tin, called whiskers, spontaneously grow from pure tin surfaces. They emerge slowly, sometimes reaching millimeters in length, and they conduct electricity. In electronic assemblies, whiskers can bridge adjacent circuits, causing short circuits and failures. The phenomenon destroyed satellites, disrupted communications systems, shut down nuclear power plant controls. Lead-tin solder rarely grows whiskers; pure tin does so reliably. The electronics industry's shift to lead-free solder reintroduced a problem that had been solved. Engineers now apply conformal coatings, control humidity, and avoid pure tin finishes where reliability matters. The whiskers grow anyway, unpredictable and persistent.

## Organotin Compounds

Tin bonded to carbon forms compounds with remarkable properties and concerning toxicity. Tributyltin, widely used as a marine antifouling paint, prevented barnacles and algae from growing on ship hulls. It worked efficiently. It also poisoned oysters, caused deformities in marine snails, accumulated in food chains. By the 1980s, tributyltin contamination had damaged coastal ecosystems worldwide. International treaties banned its use on small vessels in 1989 and on all ships by 2008. Other organotin compounds serve as stabilizers in PVC plastic and catalysts in silicone production. They remain in use, regulated but present, their long-term environmental effects still accumulating data.

## The Whistle and Cry

Bend a bar of tin and it screams. The sound, called tin cry, comes from crystal structures sliding against each other under stress. The noise is distinctive enough that metalworkers once used it to verify tin quality. Pure tin forms large, tetragonal crystals in its white form. Under stress, these crystals deform, producing acoustic emissions audible to human ears. The phenomenon serves no industrial purpose. It is simply a property, a reminder that materials have behaviors beyond their engineering specifications, that the elements retain characteristics that surprise even after millennia of use.
