# Cadmium

***Toxic Twin of Zinc***

> **Atomic Number:** 48 | **Symbol:** Cd | **Category:** Transition Metal

Cadmium forms as a minor byproduct when stars forge zinc through neutron capture. In Earth's crust, it appears wherever zinc concentrates, bound in the same sulfide ores at roughly one part cadmium to every hundred parts zinc. This geological kinship stems from nearly identical chemical behavior. Both metals form +2 ions of similar size. Both seek the same minerals. The soft, silvery-white metal melts at 321°C, low enough that ancient metallurgists inadvertently released it from zinc ores without recognizing a separate element. Friedrich Stromeyer isolated it in 1817 while investigating contaminated zinc carbonate. He named it for cadmia, the Latin term for zinc ore. Today, virtually all cadmium comes not from dedicated mining but as an unavoidable companion to zinc production. The world extracts roughly 25,000 tons annually. Most enters nickel-cadmium batteries. The rest colors pigments yellow, coats steel against corrosion, or absorbs neutrons in nuclear reactors. The metal accumulates in kidneys and never leaves.

## The Yellow Pigment

Artists embraced cadmium yellow and cadmium red in the mid-1800s. The pigments offered brilliance and lightfastness that organic dyes could not match. Claude Monet used cadmium yellow extensively in his later work. The color appears in Van Gogh's sunflowers. Unlike earlier yellows derived from lead or arsenic compounds, cadmium pigments resist fading in sunlight for centuries. The color comes from cadmium sulfide crystals. Particle size determines hue. Fine particles produce pale lemon yellow. Larger crystals shift toward orange and deep red. By 1910, cadmium pigments dominated professional paint manufacturing. They remain standard in artist-grade materials despite toxicity concerns and high cost. A single tube of cadmium red can cost five times more than synthetic alternatives. Modern environmental regulations restrict cadmium pigments in commercial products, but artists' materials retain exemptions. The color persists because no substitute matches its intensity.

## Bone Damage

The human body mistakes cadmium for zinc and calcium. Once absorbed, cadmium travels to the kidneys, where it concentrates in tubule cells. The biological half-life exceeds 20 years. Chronic exposure leads to kidney failure. The metal also leaches calcium from bones, causing severe pain and fractures. Japanese farmers first documented this toxicity in the 1950s. Zinc mines upstream contaminated rice paddies. Women who ate the rice developed itai-itai disease, "it hurts-it hurts" disease. Their bones became so brittle that coughing fractured ribs. The kidneys failed. More than 200 people died before authorities traced the source. The mining company fought liability for decades. Courts finally ordered compensation in 1972. Today, rice from that region remains monitored. Cadmium also enters food through phosphate fertilizers, which concentrate trace metals from rock deposits. Smokers accumulate twice as much cadmium as non-smokers because tobacco plants absorb the metal efficiently from soil.

## Battery Chemistry

Nickel-cadmium batteries powered portable electronics for decades. The technology emerged in 1899 but became practical only in the 1950s when sealed cell designs prevented leakage. The batteries could withstand deep discharge cycles that destroyed lead-acid and early lithium cells. They performed in extreme temperatures. They recharged quickly. By 1990, NiCd batteries dominated cordless tools, emergency lighting, and aviation. The aviation industry still relies on them. Commercial aircraft carry NiCd batteries for emergency power. They remain certified because their behavior under all conditions is thoroughly understood. Lithium batteries, despite higher energy density, occasionally catch fire. NiCd batteries do not. The European Union banned consumer NiCd batteries in 2009 due to cadmium toxicity. Exemptions persist for medical devices and professional equipment. Proper recycling recovers 95% of the cadmium, which returns to battery manufacturing. The metal, once extracted, circulates through industrial systems indefinitely if captured.

## Nuclear Control

Cadmium absorbs neutrons more effectively than almost any stable element. When a neutron strikes a cadmium-113 nucleus, the nucleus captures it and releases gamma radiation instead of triggering fission. This property makes cadmium essential in nuclear reactor control rods. The rods contain cadmium metal or cadmium compounds mixed with other neutron absorbers. Operators lower the rods into the reactor core to slow the chain reaction. Full insertion stops fission entirely. Early reactor designs used pure cadmium rods. Modern reactors combine cadmium with silver and indium for better mechanical properties at high temperatures. The metal also shields spent fuel. Cadmium plates line storage pools and transport casks. Each atom that absorbs a neutron becomes cadmium-114, stable and harmless. The process gradually depletes the cadmium, requiring eventual replacement. Naval reactors use cadmium extensively because compact size demands efficient neutron control. No other material matches cadmium's combination of absorption cross-section and material properties.

## Solar Cells

Cadmium telluride emerged as a viable photovoltaic material in the 1980s. Thin-film CdTe solar cells convert sunlight to electricity at 22% efficiency, approaching silicon's performance. Manufacturing costs run significantly lower. The cells require 100 times less semiconductor material than crystalline silicon panels. They perform better in high temperatures and low light. By 2020, CdTe panels generated 5% of global solar power. The technology concentrates in large utility-scale installations. First Solar, the primary manufacturer, operates gigawatt-scale factories. Each panel contains roughly 10 grams of cadmium, sealed between glass plates. Critics highlight the toxicity. Manufacturers respond that coal combustion releases far more cadmium into the environment per kilowatt-hour generated. CdTe panels also offset their cadmium content within months of operation. The company maintains recycling facilities that recover 95% of materials from spent panels. Whether CdTe technology expands depends partly on tellurium supply. Tellurium is rarer than gold.

## Coatings and Alloys

Electroplated cadmium protects steel fasteners in marine and aviation applications. A thin cadmium layer, typically 5-25 micrometers, prevents corrosion more effectively than zinc plating. The coating self-heals. When scratched, cadmium corrodes sacrificially, protecting the underlying steel. It also provides lubricity, preventing fasteners from seizing during installation or removal. The aerospace industry consumes most electroplating-grade cadmium. Aircraft specifications require cadmium-plated fasteners for landing gear, engine mounts, and structural bolts. Military standards mandate it for hardware exposed to saltwater. The plating process generates hazardous waste. Workers require extensive protective equipment. Environmental regulations have eliminated cadmium plating from consumer products and most industrial applications. Aviation retains approval because no alternative meets performance requirements across all conditions. Cadmium also enters low-melting alloys. Wood's metal and similar compositions use cadmium to lower melting points below 100°C. These alloys serve in fire sprinkler systems and specialized casting applications.

## Occupational Exposure

Welding and soldering fumes contain cadmium when working with plated or cadmium-bearing materials. The metal vaporizes at temperatures common in metalworking. Inhaled cadmium oxide particles lodge in the lungs and enter the bloodstream. Acute exposure causes chemical pneumonia. Survivors often develop chronic lung disease. Occupational safety standards set airborne limits at 5 micrograms per cubic meter. Battery manufacturing workers face highest exposure. Recycling operations that handle electronic waste also pose risk. Regulations require air monitoring, ventilation systems, and respirators. Japan maintains a national registry of cadmium exposure cases. The registry documents that average body burden has declined 30% since industrial controls tightened in 1990. Workers who handled cadmium before modern protections often developed kidney disease decades later. The latency period obscured the connection for years. Current exposure limits assume no safe threshold. Any cadmium accumulation carries risk.
