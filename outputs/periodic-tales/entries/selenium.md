# Selenium

***Light Turned to Current***

> **Atomic Number:** 34 | **Symbol:** Se | **Category:** Nonmetal

Selenium exists at the boundary between metal and nonmetal, conductor and insulator, poison and necessity. Its electrical resistance changes with light. In darkness, it insulates. Under illumination, current flows. This property, discovered in 1873, made the first photoelectric cells possible and opened the path to solar power, television cameras, and the conversion of electromagnetic radiation into electricity.

The element appears in Earth's crust at three parts per hundred million, neither rare nor common. It concentrates in volcanic regions, in certain shales, in the tissues of organisms that have learned to use it. The human body requires trace amounts for enzyme function. Too little causes heart disease and immune failure. Too much destroys liver and nerve. The margin between essential and toxic spans less than an order of magnitude.

---

Jöns Jacob Berzelius found selenium in 1817 while investigating a red sediment from sulfuric acid production at a factory in Gripsholm, Sweden. He named it for the moon, Selene, because it resembled tellurium, which had been named for Earth. The pairing seemed appropriate. Both elements sit in the same chemical family, both exhibit similar metallic luster in certain forms, both resist simple classification.

For half a century, selenium remained a curiosity. Then Willoughby Smith, testing underwater telegraph cables in 1873, noticed that selenium's resistance dropped when light struck it. This photoconductivity had no precedent. Other materials conducted or they didn't. Selenium responded to illumination like a living thing responding to stimulus.

The first practical application came within five years. Alexander Graham Bell built the photophone in 1880, a device that transmitted voice on a beam of light using selenium's light sensitivity to modulate an electrical signal. The technology worked but remained impractical. Copper wire proved more reliable than light beams for carrying conversation. Yet the principle endured. When television emerged in the 1920s, the first cameras used selenium cells to convert images into electrical signals. The element saw what the eye saw and translated vision into current.

The biological role went unrecognized until 1957. Before that year, selenium was considered purely toxic, an industrial hazard to be avoided. Klaus Schwarz and Calvin Foltz demonstrated that rats fed selenium-deficient diets developed liver disease. The element wasn't poison. It was essential, required in parts per billion for the function of selenoproteins, enzymes that protect cells from oxidative damage. Humans carry 25 genes for selenium-dependent proteins. Without them, immune systems fail, thyroid function collapses, heart muscle degenerates.

The margin of safety remains narrow. Selenium deficiency appears below 40 micrograms daily. Toxicity begins above 400 micrograms. In regions where soil concentrations run high, livestock develop "blind staggers" and sloughing hooves. In Enshi County, China, naturally high selenium in coal caused endemic poisoning until dietary sources were controlled. The element accumulates in certain plants—locoweed, milk vetch, prince's plume—which concentrate it to levels that poison grazing animals. These accumulator species thrive in selenium-rich soils where other plants cannot survive, using the element as a chemical defense against herbivores.

Industry consumes most mined selenium for glassmaking and electronics. Added to glass, it cancels the green tint that iron impurities create, producing clear colorless panes. It colors other glass red or pink. In photocopy machines, selenium-coated drums hold electrostatic charge in darkness and release it where light strikes, allowing toner to adhere in patterns that reproduce text and image. Though newer machines use organic photoconductors, millions of older copiers still depend on selenium's light-sensitive surface.

Modern solar cells use selenium only rarely. Cadmium telluride and silicon proved more efficient at converting sunlight to electricity. But copper indium gallium selenide, CIGS, forms one of the most efficient thin-film photovoltaic materials. The element that first demonstrated photoconductivity continues to turn light into power, now at scales that were unimaginable when Smith noticed his telegraph cable behaving strangely in sunlight.

Mining selenium produces a byproduct of copper refining. The element concentrates in the anode slimes that settle during electrolytic purification. These residues, rich in selenium and tellurium and precious metals, undergo roasting and leaching to separate the constituents. Production amounts to about 3,000 tons annually, most from Japan, Belgium, and the Philippines. Price fluctuates with copper production and electronics demand.

The element exists in multiple forms. Gray metallic selenium conducts electricity modestly. Red amorphous selenium insulates. Black vitreous selenium forms when the molten element cools rapidly. These allotropes demonstrate selenium's position at the border between metal and nonmetal, its refusal to commit entirely to one category or the other. In chemical behavior it resembles sulfur, forming similar compounds, occupying the same position in organic molecules. But unlike sulfur, selenium responds to light. That single property, discovered in a cable factory, changed how humans capture and convert energy, how they reproduce images, how they understand the spectrum between essential and toxic.
