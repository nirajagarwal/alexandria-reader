# Selenium

***Between Essential and Toxic***

> **Atomic Number:** 34 | **Symbol:** Se | **Category:** Nonmetal

Selenium occupies a narrow zone. Too little causes disease. Too much causes disease. The margin is thin.

The element forms in massive stars during supernova nucleosynthesis. On Earth it appears in sulfide ores, concentrated where volcanic activity or ancient seabeds deposited mineral-rich sediments. It exists in six stable isotopes. At room temperature it takes several allotropic forms: red powder, gray metallic crystals, black vitreous solid.

Jöns Jacob Berzelius identified it in 1817 while examining residue from sulfuric acid production. He named it for the moon, following the celestial pattern he had established with tellurium, named for Earth. The element showed semiconductor properties before the concept existed.

Modern electronics depend on selenium's response to light. Biology demands precise amounts in specific proteins. Agriculture manages soil concentrations to prevent livestock poisoning while ensuring adequate intake. The element marks the boundary where necessity becomes danger.

## The Berzelius Residue

Berzelius noticed something wrong with the sulfuric acid produced at a Swedish chemical works. The process used pyrite from Falun's copper mines, and the acid carried an unexpected radish-like odor. He investigated the red sediment accumulating in the acid chambers.

The residue resembled tellurium but behaved differently. Berzelius conducted systematic tests through 1817 and 1818. The substance formed compounds with oxygen and hydrogen. It produced a distinctive smell when heated. He isolated the pure element and published his findings.

Others had encountered selenium without recognizing it. Johann Gottlieb Gahn had noted the Falun deposits' peculiarity. Antoine Lavoisier may have detected it in analyses of pyrites. Berzelius possessed the chemical expertise and persistence to complete the identification.

His naming convention placed selenium beside tellurium in what would become the chalcogen group.

## Photoelectric Dawn

Willoughby Smith discovered selenium's light sensitivity in 1873 while testing cables for underwater telegraphy. The element's electrical resistance dropped dramatically under illumination. This property, unexpected and reproducible, opened new technological territory.

The selenium photocell arrived within two decades. Early versions powered light meters for photography. Television experimentation in the 1920s employed selenium to convert images into electrical signals. German engineer Paul Nipkow built mechanical scanning systems using selenium cells as detectors.

The technology had limitations. Selenium cells responded slowly and produced weak signals. Silicon and other semiconductors eventually displaced them in most applications. But selenium photocells established the principle: light could be translated directly into electricity, making distant seeing possible.

The element still appears in photocopiers, where selenium-coated drums hold electrostatic charges in darkness and release them in light.

## The Selenoproteins

Selenium functions in biology as part of selenocysteine, the twenty-first amino acid. The element substitutes for sulfur in cysteine, creating proteins with unique catalytic properties. Mammals produce twenty-five selenoproteins. Each requires selenium; none function without it.

Glutathione peroxidases use selenocysteine at their active sites to neutralize hydrogen peroxide and lipid peroxides. These enzymes protect cell membranes from oxidative damage. Thioredoxin reductases, also selenium-dependent, regulate cellular redox states. Iodothyronine deiodinases control thyroid hormone activation; without adequate selenium, thyroid function fails.

The body maintains selenium homeostasis within narrow limits. Recommended intake ranges from 55 to 70 micrograms daily for adults. Below 20 micrograms, deficiency diseases emerge. Above 400 micrograms, toxicity begins. This ten-fold window distinguishes selenium from most essential nutrients.

Evolution selected selenium for specific biochemical tasks where sulfur proves inadequate.

## Keshan and Kashin-Beck

Selenium deficiency produces distinct pathologies. Keshan disease, named for the Chinese county where it was first documented, causes cardiomyopathy primarily in children and women of childbearing age. The heart muscle degenerates. Cardiac failure follows. The disease appeared in a belt across northeastern China where soil selenium concentrations measured less than 0.1 parts per million.

Kashin-Beck disease, identified in eastern Siberia and northern China, affects cartilage and bone. Joints enlarge. Growth plates degenerate. Children develop shortened fingers and limbs. The condition appears in the same selenium-deficient regions as Keshan disease.

Supplementation programs begun in the 1970s reduced both diseases dramatically. China added sodium selenite to table salt in affected areas. Residents received selenium tablets. Keshan disease cases dropped by more than 90 percent.

The geographic restriction of these diseases revealed soil chemistry's influence on human health. Selenium content varies by geology; populations living on deficient soils suffer consequences no dietary adjustment can overcome without supplementation.

## Alkali Flats

Selenium accumulates in alkaline, poorly drained soils of the western United States. Certain plants—locoweed, prince's plume, woody aster—hyperaccumulate the element, concentrating it to levels that poison grazing livestock. Cattle and sheep develop "alkali disease": hair loss, hoof deformation, joint stiffness, emaciation.

The San Joaquin Valley in California faces selenium contamination from agricultural drainage. Irrigation water leaches selenium from marine shale deposits, carrying it into evaporation ponds and wetlands. At Kesterson Reservoir in the 1980s, waterbirds showed grotesque deformities: missing eyes, twisted beaks, malformed wings. The reservoir held drainage water containing selenium at 300 parts per billion, sixty times higher than levels considered safe.

Managing selenium in irrigated agriculture requires controlling water movement. Drainage must not concentrate the element in wildlife habitat. Fields need careful water application to minimize leaching. Some regions now use drainage water treatment systems that capture selenium before it reaches wetlands.

The same element required for health becomes a potent teratogen at slightly elevated concentrations.

## Glassmaking Clarity

Selenium removes color from glass. Iron impurities in silica sand produce a green tint in ordinary glass. Adding selenium—typically as sodium selenite—neutralizes this coloration. The element acts as a decolorizer by complementary color cancellation, producing clear glass for windows and containers.

In controlled amounts, selenium creates specific colors. Pink and red glasses contain colloidal selenium particles. The element distributes through the glass matrix during cooling. Ruby glass manufactured with selenium offers durability superior to gold-based alternatives.

Photochromic lenses use selenium compounds alongside silver halides. Ultraviolet light triggers reactions that darken the glass. Visible light reverses the process. The element enables reversible light-responsive materials.

Glass production consumes modest selenium quantities compared to other industrial uses, but the application remains economically significant for specialty products.

## Semiconductor Heritage

Selenium was the first material used to convert alternating current to direct current. Selenium rectifiers, developed in the 1930s, consisted of thin selenium films on metal plates. They powered radio receivers and battery chargers before silicon rectifiers displaced them.

The element's photovoltaic effect, though weaker than silicon's, saw application in light meters and solar cells. Early space satellites carried selenium photovoltaic cells. The material's response to light validated the concept of solid-state power generation from sunlight.

Modern electronics have largely abandoned selenium for more efficient semiconductors. But the element retains niche applications where its specific properties prove advantageous. X-ray detectors use amorphous selenium for medical and security imaging. The material converts X-rays directly to electrical charge with high spatial resolution.

Selenium bridged vacuum tube and silicon eras, proving that solid materials could perform electronic functions previously requiring heated filaments and evacuated envelopes.

## Threshold Element

Selenium exists at the boundary between necessity and harm. Biology requires it for oxidative defense and thyroid regulation. The environment concentrates it in ways that poison wildlife. Industry exploits its semiconductor and optical properties. Agriculture monitors it in soil and feed.

No element better illustrates biochemistry's dependence on precise dosage. A few dozen micrograms separate deficiency from adequacy from toxicity. The margin allows no carelessness.
