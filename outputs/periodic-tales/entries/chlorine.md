# Chlorine

***The Element That Purifies and Destroys***

> **Atomic Number:** 17 | **Symbol:** Cl | **Category:** Halogen

Chlorine exists as a yellow-green gas at room temperature, the only halogen visible in its elemental form under ordinary conditions. Its name derives from the Greek *chloros*, meaning greenish-yellow, though its discoverer initially called it "dephlogisticated marine acid." The element forms when salt deposits interact with sulfuric acid, releasing vapors that corrode metal and sear living tissue. Its reactivity makes free chlorine rare in nature, but combined with sodium it forms the most abundant dissolved substance in Earth's oceans. Human civilization transformed chlorine from chemical curiosity to industrial necessity within a century, harnessing its destructive power to purify water, bleach textiles, and synthesize thousands of compounds. A single element, capable of saving millions of lives through sanitation and claiming them through weaponized gas.

---

Carl Wilhelm Scheele isolated chlorine in 1774 by treating pyrolusite with hydrochloric acid in his Swedish laboratory. He documented the gas's ability to bleach flowers and corrode metals but never recognized it as elemental. For three decades, chemists believed chlorine contained oxygen, calling it "oxymuriatic acid." Humphry Davy settled the matter in 1810, demonstrating that the substance could not be decomposed further and naming it for its distinctive color. The element became industrially significant almost immediately. By 1820, factories in Britain and France produced chlorine-based bleach, revolutionizing textile manufacturing and paper production.

The element's biological effects proved both beneficial and catastrophic. In 1854, physician John Snow traced a London cholera outbreak to contaminated water from the Broad Street pump. Though he did not employ chlorine himself, his work established the link between water and disease that chlorine treatment would break. Jersey City became the first American city to chlorinate its water supply continuously in 1908. Typhoid deaths dropped precipitously wherever the practice spread. Within two decades, waterborne disease mortality in developed nations fell by orders of magnitude.

The same oxidizing power that kills bacteria in water makes chlorine lethal to human tissue. German forces released 168 tons of chlorine gas at Ypres in April 1915, creating a yellow-green cloud that rolled across Allied trenches. Soldiers who inhaled the gas drowned in their own fluids as chlorine reacted with the moisture in their lungs, forming hydrochloric acid. Five thousand died. The attack initiated a chemical arms race that would claim nearly 100,000 lives before the war ended. Chlorine's use as a weapon demonstrated that the line between industrial chemical and instrument of mass death could be crossed in an afternoon.

Modern industry consumes 70 million tons of chlorine annually. Polyvinyl chloride production accounts for a third of this demand. PVC appears in pipes, siding, medical tubing, credit cards, and countless other applications. The manufacture of propylene oxide, epichlorohydrin, and other organic intermediates requires substantial chlorine inputs. Water treatment still represents a major use, though the element's role has grown more complex. Free chlorine reacts with organic matter in water to form trihalomethanes and other disinfection byproducts, some carcinogenic. Utilities balance the imperative to kill pathogens against the risk of creating harmful compounds.

The chemistry that makes chlorine industrially valuable creates environmental persistence. Chlorinated organic compounds resist degradation. DDT and PCBs, once celebrated for their stability and effectiveness, accumulated in food chains and persisted in soil and water for decades after their use was restricted. The Montreal Protocol targeted chlorofluorocarbons after scientists demonstrated their role in stratospheric ozone depletion. A single chlorine atom, liberated from a CFC molecule by ultraviolet light, can catalyze the destruction of 100,000 ozone molecules before being removed from the stratosphere.

Chlorine remains essential to human metabolism despite its toxicity as a free element. Chloride ions maintain osmotic balance in cells, regulate fluid volume, and enable nerve impulse transmission. Gastric acid, hydrochloric acid produced in the stomach, depends on chloride for its formation. The human body contains roughly 95 grams of chlorine, nearly all of it as chloride. Dietary deficiency is rare; excess sodium chloride intake presents the more common health concern.

The element's future involves managing its paradoxes. No substitute for chlorine in water treatment combines equivalent effectiveness, cost, and ease of application. Yet the byproducts of chlorination remain problematic. Research continues into alternative disinfectants and methods to minimize trihalomethane formation. The chemical industry explores routes to valuable compounds that avoid chlorine entirely, driven by both environmental concerns and the element's corrosive nature. Chlorine will remain indispensable for decades, even as its applications evolve to address the hazards its use creates.
