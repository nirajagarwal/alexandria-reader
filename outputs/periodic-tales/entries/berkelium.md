# Berkelium

***Made in Berkeley, Scarce Everywhere***

> **Atomic Number:** 97 | **Symbol:** Bk | **Category:** Actinide

Berkelium does not occur in nature. Every atom of this element has been made by human hands, synthesized in nuclear reactors or particle accelerators. Named for the city where it was first created, berkelium occupies a position deep in the actinide series where elements become increasingly unstable and short-lived. Its most stable isotope, berkelium-247, has a half-life of 1,380 years—long by the standards of synthetic elements, but ephemeral on geological timescales. The element serves primarily as a stepping stone to heavier elements, particularly californium, which has more practical applications. Berkelium exists in quantities measured in milligrams at most, produced deliberately for research purposes. It remains one of the rarest materials on Earth, present only where scientists have summoned it into being.

---

On December 19, 1949, Glenn Seaborg, Stanley Thompson, and Albert Ghiorso bombarded americium-241 with alpha particles in the 60-inch cyclotron at the University of California, Berkeley. The collision produced a new element, atomic number 97, which they named for the city where it emerged. The discovery came during the golden age of synthetic element creation, when Berkeley's Lawrence Radiation Laboratory was systematically extending the periodic table beyond uranium. Berkelium was the fifth transuranium element discovered there in less than a decade.

The element has no stable isotopes. Thirteen isotopes have been identified, all radioactive, with half-lives ranging from milliseconds to centuries. Berkelium-247, the longest-lived, decays through alpha emission to americium-243. This relative longevity makes berkelium-247 the isotope of choice for research, though producing even minute quantities requires months of neutron bombardment in specialized reactors. The Oak Ridge National Laboratory maintains one of the few facilities capable of manufacturing berkelium in usable amounts.

Berkelium's chemistry mirrors that of other actinides. It exists most commonly in the +3 oxidation state, forming compounds similar to those of the lanthanides. In solution, berkelium ions produce a greenish color. The element has been studied in various chemical forms—oxides, halides, nitrates—but always in microscopic quantities. Researchers have measured its properties by working with samples containing millions of atoms but weighing almost nothing by conventional standards.

The primary use of berkelium is as target material for synthesizing still heavier elements. Bombardment of berkelium-249 with calcium-48 ions has produced tennessine, element 117, one of the newest additions to the periodic table. This synthesis required 22 milligrams of berkelium-249, representing several months of reactor operation and one of the largest berkelium samples ever assembled. The californium isotope 249 is also produced through berkelium's decay chain, and californium has medical and industrial applications that berkelium itself lacks.

The element poses significant radiological hazards. Berkelium-249, with its 330-day half-life, presents risks through both alpha and beta radiation. Alpha particles do not penetrate skin but are dangerous if the material is inhaled or ingested. Beta radiation penetrates tissue more deeply. Workers handling berkelium must use glove boxes, special ventilation systems, and continuous radiation monitoring. The quantities involved are small enough that catastrophic accidents are unlikely, but chronic exposure remains a concern for researchers.

Global berkelium production amounts to less than one gram per year, concentrated in a handful of research facilities. The United States and Russia maintain the only significant production capabilities. The element has no commercial applications and appears in no consumer products. Its existence remains confined to nuclear research laboratories and the interior of nuclear reactors, where neutron flux drives the transmutation reactions that create it.

The cost of berkelium exceeds that of any precious metal or gemstone. Estimates suggest berkelium-249 costs approximately $27 million per gram, though actual pricing is meaningless given that it cannot be purchased on any market. The expense reflects not the material itself but the reactor time, specialized equipment, and chemical separation processes required to isolate it. Each milligram represents months of neutron bombardment followed by elaborate purification procedures to separate berkelium from other actinides produced simultaneously.
