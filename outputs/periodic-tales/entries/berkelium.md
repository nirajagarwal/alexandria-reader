# Berkelium

***Born in the Cyclotron's Fire***

> **Atomic Number:** 97 | **Symbol:** Bk | **Category:** Actinide

Berkelium does not exist in nature. Every atom of this element has been made by human hands, synthesized in particle accelerators by bombarding lighter elements with charged particles. Stanley Thompson, Albert Ghiorso, and Glenn Seaborg created it in December 1945 at the University of California, Berkeley, striking americium with helium ions. The element took its name from the city where it first appeared. It belongs to the actinide series, heavy elements that sit below the main body of the periodic table, all radioactive, most synthetic. The most stable isotope, berkelium-247, has a half-life of 1,380 years. In weighable quantities it glows with its own heat. Production requires months of irradiation in nuclear reactors, followed by elaborate chemical separation. A few grams exist worldwide at any moment. Its primary use: as feedstock for creating still heavier elements, stepping stones toward the edge of the periodic table.

## The Manhattan Project's Offspring

The cyclotron at Berkeley that produced berkelium was built on wartime knowledge. During the Manhattan Project, researchers learned to transmute elements, to push nuclei beyond uranium into the transuranics. After the war, the same teams turned their expertise toward pure discovery. The 60-inch cyclotron became an atomic forge. They created neptunium, plutonium, americium, curium—elements 93 through 96. Berkelium came next. The sample was invisible, measured in micrograms, detected only by its radioactive signature. But it was real. A new element had entered the catalog.

## Difficult Chemistry

Berkelium occupies a position where quantum mechanics produces unusual behavior. It typically exists in the +3 oxidation state, like other late actinides, but also forms a +4 state under certain conditions. This dual nature makes its chemistry complex, interesting to nuclear chemists studying actinide behavior. Separation from other elements requires precise control of acidity, reducing agents, ion exchange resins. The element must be handled remotely due to radiation. Single atoms have been studied using microchemical techniques. Understanding berkelium helps predict the behavior of heavier elements that exist only briefly.

## The Californium Bridge

Berkelium serves as precursor to californium-249, produced by neutron bombardment of berkelium-249 in high-flux reactors. This process takes months. The berkelium captures neutrons, undergoes beta decay, transforms. Californium-249 then becomes the target for synthesizing even heavier elements. Without berkelium, access to the upper reaches of the periodic table would be far more difficult. It is infrastructure for the superheavy elements, the ones that exist for fractions of a second. Element 117, tennessine, was discovered by bombarding berkelium with calcium ions.

## Radiation and Heat

Berkelium-249, the isotope used in research, decays by emitting beta particles and gamma rays. The radiation is intense enough to require thick shielding. Larger samples generate heat from radioactive decay. A gram of berkelium-249 produces about 10 watts of thermal power, enough to feel warm. This property limits handling and storage. The element must be kept in specialized containers designed to dissipate heat and contain radiation. Workers use remote manipulators and hot cells. The glow is not metaphorical.

## Weighable Quantities

In 1967, researchers at Oak Ridge National Laboratory produced 1.7 grams of berkelium-249, the largest sample ever assembled. It took two years of continuous irradiation in the High Flux Isotope Reactor. This quantity allowed the first measurements of berkelium's bulk properties: melting point, crystal structure, metallic appearance. The sample was silvery. It demonstrated paramagnetic behavior. Most elements can be studied in quantity. Berkelium remains scarce enough that much of its physical chemistry derives from these few occasions when enough atoms gathered in one place to behave like ordinary matter.

## Naming Convention

The element honors a place, following the tradition of ytterbium (Ytterby, Sweden), europium (Europe), americium (the Americas). Berkeley, California, had become the center of heavy element research. Seven elements would eventually be discovered there or by Berkeley-affiliated scientists. The name was fitting but also marked a departure. Earlier elements often honored people or properties. The synthetic elements increasingly honored institutions, laboratories, nations. They were products of organized science, of cyclotrons and reactors, of government funding and international collaboration. The name acknowledges this.

## The Production Limit

Global production of berkelium amounts to less than a gram per year, almost entirely at Oak Ridge and the Research Institute of Atomic Reactors in Dimitrovgrad, Russia. Demand exceeds supply. Experiments requiring berkelium targets wait months or years for material. The bottleneck is reactor time and the availability of curium-244, which must be irradiated to produce berkelium. There are proposals for dedicated facilities, but costs are high. The element remains exotic not because of theoretical barriers but because of practical constraints: time, money, reactor capacity. It is a substance that exists at the margin of what industrial civilization can produce.
