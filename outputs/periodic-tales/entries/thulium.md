# Thulium

***The Rarest Rare Earth***

> **Atomic Number:** 69 | **Symbol:** Tm | **Category:** Lanthanide

Thulium occupies an extreme position even among the rare earth elements. It is the least abundant lanthanide in Earth's crust, present at concentrations around 0.5 parts per million. Discovered in 1879 by Swedish chemist Per Teodor Cleve, who isolated it from the mineral erbia, thulium took its name from Thule, the ancient designation for Scandinavia. For a century after its identification, the element remained a laboratory curiosity, separated in tiny quantities and studied for its properties without practical application. Its scarcity and the difficulty of obtaining pure samples kept it at the margins of chemistry. The twentieth century's end brought a shift. Thulium-170, a radioactive isotope, found use in portable X-ray devices. Thulium-doped lasers emerged as surgical tools. The element that had no function became specialized, employed in circumstances where its unique characteristics justified the cost of extraction.

Cleve worked methodically through the mineral yttria, a substance that earlier chemists had believed to be a single element. He found it contained multiple rare earths, components so chemically similar that separating them required countless cycles of precipitation and crystallization. From erbium oxide, itself recently isolated, Cleve extracted two new elements in 1879: holmium and thulium. The quantities were minute. The work consumed months. Thulium appeared as a pale green oxide that turned white on heating, distinguishable from its neighbors primarily through careful spectroscopic analysis. Cleve published his findings in Uppsala. The element joined the growing roster of lanthanides, occupying position 69 in the periodic table.

The rarity of thulium stems from nucleosynthesis. Formed through the slow neutron capture process in aging stars, thulium atoms require specific conditions that produce few of them compared to their lanthanide cousins. When Earth condensed from the solar nebula, thulium was present in only trace amounts. It concentrates in monazite and xenotime, the principal rare earth ores, but even there it constitutes less than fifty parts per million. Extracting it requires processing tons of ore through ion exchange or solvent extraction procedures that separate elements differing by fractions of an atomic radius. The industrial scale needed to produce kilogram quantities emerged only in the late twentieth century.

Thulium-170, with a half-life of 128 days, decays by emitting X-rays and gamma rays. These properties found application in portable X-ray sources used for industrial radiography. A small pellet of thulium-170, encased in shielding, generates penetrating radiation suitable for examining welds, detecting cracks in metal, and inspecting structures where conventional X-ray machines cannot reach. Oil pipelines, aircraft components, and construction sites became workplaces for devices powered by this isotope. The sources require no electricity. They decay predictably. When spent, they are returned for disposal or reprocessing.

In 1962, researchers demonstrated that thulium could be used to dope crystals for laser applications. Thulium-doped yttrium aluminum garnet lasers, operating at a wavelength near two micrometers, interact strongly with water. This characteristic made them valuable in surgery. The laser energy is absorbed within a shallow depth, vaporizing tissue with minimal damage to surrounding areas. Urology adopted thulium lasers for treating kidney stones and prostate conditions. The precision cuts, the reduced bleeding, the faster healing times justified the expense of the rare element embedded in the laser crystal.

Thulium also enters fiber optic amplifiers. Doped into fiber, it amplifies signals in the wavelength range used for telecommunications. As data demands grew and existing infrastructure strained, thulium-doped fiber amplifiers offered a way to extend capacity without replacing entire networks. The element's electronic structure allows it to absorb and emit photons at frequencies that complement other amplification technologies, filling gaps in the spectrum.

The element presents no significant biological role. Human bodies contain no measurable thulium. It is not known to be essential to any organism. In soluble salts, thulium shows low to moderate toxicity, similar to other rare earths. Industrial handling follows standard precautions for metal dusts and chemical compounds. The quantities used remain small enough that environmental accumulation is not a widespread concern. Miners and refiners processing rare earth ores face greater exposure, but thulium itself is one hazard among many in those operations.

Global production of thulium amounts to only a few tens of tons per year. China dominates rare earth extraction and processing, controlling access to thulium as it does the other lanthanides. Prices fluctuate with geopolitical tensions and supply chain disruptions. Alternative sources exist in Australia, the United States, and other countries with rare earth deposits, but bringing them online requires substantial investment and years of development. Recycling of thulium from spent lasers and other devices remains minimal. The element's applications are too specialized, the quantities too small, for recovery to be economical under current conditions.

Research continues into new uses. Thulium compounds are tested as catalysts, as magnetic materials, as components in advanced alloys. The element's position at the heavy end of the lanthanide series gives it properties distinct from lighter rare earths. Whether these properties will find broader application depends on cost, availability, and the emergence of technologies that require precisely what thulium offers. For now, it remains what it has always been: the rarest of a rare group, pulled from the earth in small amounts for purposes that could not have been imagined when Cleve first isolated it in a Swedish laboratory more than a century ago.
