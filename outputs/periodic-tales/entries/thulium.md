# Thulium

***The Rarest Stable Lanthanide***

> **Atomic Number:** 69 | **Symbol:** Tm | **Category:** Lanthanide

Thulium formed in the slow neutron capture of stellar nucleosynthesis, an accumulation process that builds heavy elements atom by atom over cosmic time. Of all the stable rare earth elements, thulium is the least abundant. The Earth's crust holds roughly 0.5 parts per million. It concentrates in the mineral monazite alongside other lanthanides, from which separation requires painstaking chemical extraction. Swedish chemist Per Teodor Cleve isolated it in 1879 from the oxide erbia, naming it for Thule, the ancient designation for Scandinavia. For nearly a century, thulium remained a laboratory curiosity. Its scarcity and the difficulty of producing pure metal kept it from practical use. The development of ion exchange techniques in the mid-twentieth century made isolation feasible, though never routine. Today thulium finds application in specialized domains where its unique properties justify the cost of extraction.

## Last of the Lanthanides

Per Teodor Cleve worked methodically through rare earth oxides at Uppsala University. In 1879, he found two new elements where others had seen one. Holmium came first, then thulium emerged from the remaining material. The work required patience. Fractional crystallization separated elements that behaved almost identically in solution. Cleve's thulium oxide was brown, distinct from the other rare earth oxides he knew. Pure metallic thulium would not be prepared until 1911, when Charles James at the University of New Hampshire produced the first samples. The metal is soft, silvery, and slowly tarnishes in air. It remains difficult to obtain. Thulium mining does not exist as an independent operation. The element arrives as a minor fraction when processors extract more abundant rare earths.

## Portable X-Rays

Thulium-170, a radioactive isotope, emits gamma rays suitable for radiography. The isotope has a half-life of 128 days, long enough for practical deployment but short enough to produce intense radiation. Portable X-ray devices use thulium-170 sources to examine welds, castings, and structural components in the field. The equipment weighs less than conventional X-ray generators and requires no electrical power. Industrial inspectors carry these devices to construction sites, pipelines, and manufacturing facilities where permanent equipment cannot go. The sources decay to stable ytterbium-170, producing no long-lived waste. Regulatory oversight is strict. The gamma rays penetrate tissue as readily as steel. Operators work behind shielding or at distance. Despite constraints, thulium-170 sources inspect thousands of critical structures annually, finding flaws that would otherwise remain hidden until failure.

## Surgical Precision

Thulium-doped lasers emit at 2,013 nanometers, a wavelength strongly absorbed by water. This makes them effective for cutting tissue with minimal thermal damage to surrounding material. Urologists use thulium fiber lasers to treat kidney stones and enlarged prostates. The laser vaporizes tissue layer by layer, allowing precise removal while blood vessels seal as the beam passes. The procedure requires less energy than earlier laser systems and causes less postoperative discomfort. The wavelength works through thin optical fibers, enabling minimally invasive surgery. Surgeons guide the fiber through a scope inserted into the urinary tract. The tissue responds predictably. There is little charring, less bleeding, faster healing. Thousands of procedures occur annually in hospitals that can justify the equipment cost. The technology has not displaced all alternatives but has established a permanent place in surgical practice.

## The Efficiency Problem

Solid-state lasers sometimes use thulium as the active medium in host crystals. These lasers find use in military rangefinders, materials processing, and research applications. The emission wavelength falls in a range where atmospheric transmission is favorable and eye safety margins are better than for nearer-infrared lasers. Efficiency remains an issue. Thulium lasers require significant pump power to achieve useful output. The energy levels involved mean that much of the input converts to heat rather than coherent light. Cooling systems add weight and complexity. Alternative rare earth dopants often perform better for general applications. Thulium lasers persist where their specific wavelength or other characteristics solve problems that alternatives cannot. Military systems accept the inefficiency for tactical advantages. Research laboratories use them when investigating phenomena at 2-micron wavelengths. The technology is mature but constrained to niches.

## Nuclear Medicine

Thulium-170 can be used to produce ytterbium-169, which decays to thulium-169. This isotope emits low-energy gamma rays suitable for medical imaging. Researchers have investigated its potential for diagnostic procedures where traditional technetium-99m performs inadequately. The applications remain experimental. Production requires a nuclear reactor and chemical processing to separate the desired isotope from other activation products. The energy spectrum presents challenges for conventional gamma cameras. Image quality and radiation dose must balance carefully. Clinical trials have been limited. The element's rarity means that scaling production would be difficult even if medical advantages were proven conclusively. Thulium-169 remains in the category of isotopes that work in principle, have been tested in controlled settings, and await either a compelling advantage over existing agents or a breakthrough in production economics.

## Market Realities

Global thulium production measures in tens of tons annually, mostly as oxide. China supplies the majority. The price fluctuates with rare earth market dynamics, typically ranging from hundreds to over a thousand dollars per kilogram for oxide, far more for pure metal. No applications use thulium in bulk. The economics of extraction mean that thulium appears only when processors target other rare earths. Mining operations assess deposits for their content of neodymium, dysprosium, and other more valuable elements. Thulium comes along, a minor fraction of the concentrate. Some processors discard it rather than bear separation costs. Others stockpile oxide against future demand. The element's scarcity protects it from overuse but also prevents development of applications that might require larger quantities. Researchers work within constraints, designing systems that need grams rather than kilograms.
