# Magnesium

***Lightness and Fire Combined***

> **Atomic Number:** 12 | **Symbol:** Mg | **Category:** Alkaline Earth Metal

Magnesium formed in dying stars through successive helium captures, making it the eighth most abundant element in the universe. On Earth, it ranks as the fourth most common element in the planet's mass, concentrated in the mantle and dissolved throughout the oceans at 1,300 parts per million. The pure metal never occurs naturally. Oxygen and carbon claim it immediately, binding it into minerals: dolomite, magnesite, carnallite. The element's name descends from Magnesia, a district in Thessaly rich in its ores. Sir Humphry Davy isolated the metal in 1808 through electrolysis, though he could produce only small amounts. The metal that emerged was silver-white, startlingly light, and hungry for oxygen. This combination of properties—two-thirds the density of aluminum, yet strong when alloyed—would make magnesium essential to flight. Every living cell requires it. Chlorophyll cannot function without magnesium at its core. The human body contains twenty-five grams, most locked in bone, the rest enabling hundreds of enzymatic reactions. Industry extracts six million tons annually, most from seawater and brine.

## Green Blood

Chlorophyll places a magnesium ion at the exact center of its molecular architecture. Four nitrogen atoms grip the metal in a flat ring, holding it steady while surrounding carbon chains capture photons. Without this specific arrangement, the molecule cannot harvest light. The magnesium atom sits where iron sits in hemoglobin, performing an opposite function: hemoglobin transports oxygen already made; chlorophyll uses light to split water and release it. Every green thing on Earth—every leaf, every blade of grass, every photosynthesizing bacterium—depends on this metal. Plants pull magnesium from soil through their roots. Deficiency shows quickly: chlorophyll production fails, older leaves yellow between the veins while veins stay green, a condition called chlorosis. Agricultural soils often lack sufficient magnesium, particularly in high-rainfall regions where it leaches away. Farmers apply dolomitic limestone or Epsom salts. The global photosynthetic apparatus, the machinery that feeds nearly all life, cannot operate without magnesium occupying that central position in billions upon billions of chlorophyll molecules.

## Bones and Signals

The human skeleton stores sixty percent of the body's magnesium, incorporated into the mineral matrix alongside calcium and phosphate. This reserve serves structural purposes and acts as a buffer, releasing magnesium into blood when dietary intake falls short. The remaining magnesium distributes through soft tissue and fluid, where it enables critical processes. More than three hundred enzymes require magnesium as a cofactor. DNA synthesis cannot proceed without it. Protein assembly stops. Muscle contraction depends on magnesium regulating calcium flow across cell membranes. Nerve impulses travel because magnesium helps maintain electrical gradients. The heart's rhythm requires steady magnesium levels; deficiency can trigger arrhythmias. Adult bodies need 310 to 420 milligrams daily, obtained from green vegetables, nuts, whole grains, legumes. Modern diets often fall short. Refined foods lose magnesium during processing. Chronic deficiency links to hypertension, type 2 diabetes, osteoporosis. Excess rarely occurs from food; the kidneys excrete surplus efficiently. Intravenous magnesium sulfate treats eclampsia and certain cardiac emergencies.

## Incendiary Metal

Magnesium burns with intense white light, releasing 24.7 megajoules per kilogram, nearly matching gasoline. Once ignited, the metal reaches 3,100 degrees Celsius, hot enough to decompose water into hydrogen and oxygen, sustaining combustion even underwater. Attempts to extinguish magnesium fires with water intensify them violently. Carbon dioxide and standard fire extinguishers prove useless; burning magnesium strips oxygen from CO₂ and continues. Only smothering with dry sand or specialized Class D extinguishing powders works. These properties made magnesium valuable for incendiary weapons. World War II bombers dropped hundreds of thousands of magnesium bombs on German and Japanese cities. The metal's low density allowed aircraft to carry large quantities. Each bomb scattered burning fragments that ignited wood, paper, fabric, starting firestorms that consumed whole districts. The same brilliant combustion found peaceful use in photography. Powdered magnesium mixed with oxidizers produced the photoflash that illuminated portraits and news photographs before electronic strobes. Flares and fireworks still use magnesium for white-hot light visible over long distances.

## Seawater Harvest

Extracting magnesium from the ocean represents industrial chemistry at scale. Each cubic meter of seawater contains 1.3 kilograms of dissolved magnesium chloride. The Dow Chemical Company pioneered the process in 1941, building a plant in Freeport, Texas that pulled metal from the Gulf of Mexico. The method begins with precipitation. Operators mix seawater with lime made from crushed and heated oyster shells. Magnesium hydroxide settles out as a white solid. Hydrochloric acid converts this to magnesium chloride, which is dried and melted. Electrolysis splits the molten salt, liberating magnesium metal at the cathode and chlorine gas at the anode. The chlorine becomes hydrochloric acid, recycling back into the process. Modern plants produce 100,000 tons yearly using this method. Mining provides the alternative source. Magnesite and dolomite ores undergo thermal reduction, heated with ferrosilicon in vacuum furnaces. Chinese production dominates global supply, drawing from vast inland deposits. The metal emerges as crystalline ingots, ready for alloying.

## Structural Alloy

Pure magnesium lacks sufficient strength for demanding applications. Alloyed with aluminum, zinc, manganese, or rare earths, it becomes structural material. Magnesium-aluminum alloys combine low density—1.8 grams per cubic centimeter against steel's 7.8—with respectable tensile strength and excellent machinability. These properties made magnesium essential to aviation. Aircraft manufacturers use magnesium alloys for gearbox housings, engine mounts, helicopter transmission cases, and interior seat frames. Every kilogram saved from aircraft structure translates to increased payload or range. The automotive industry adopted magnesium for similar reasons. Steering wheels, instrument panels, and seat frames switched to magnesium alloys in high-performance vehicles during the 1960s. Modern cars contain 5 to 15 kilograms, mostly in transmission cases, steering columns, and dashboard supports. Laptop computers and smartphones employ magnesium alloy chassis for lightness and electromagnetic shielding. Corrosion limits broader use. Magnesium oxidizes readily in salt or humid environments unless protected by coatings or alloy modifications.

## Grignard Chemistry

Victor Grignard discovered in 1900 that magnesium metal reacts with organic halides in dry ether to form organometallic compounds of extraordinary synthetic utility. These Grignard reagents, as they became known, enabled chemists to forge carbon-carbon bonds with precision and versatility. The magnesium atom inserts itself between carbon and halogen, creating a species where carbon carries partial negative character, primed to attack electrophilic carbons in ketones, aldehydes, esters, carbon dioxide. This reactivity opened pathways to alcohols, carboxylic acids, and complex molecular architectures previously difficult or impossible to construct. Pharmaceutical synthesis depends heavily on Grignard reactions. The process requires rigorous exclusion of water and air, which destroy the reagents instantly. Industrial applications run to hundreds of tons annually, producing everything from fragrance molecules to polymer precursors. Grignard received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1912. His discovery transformed organic synthesis from empirical trial to systematic construction, magnesium serving as the key that unlocked controlled reactivity.

## Desulfurization Agent

Steelmaking consumes significant magnesium in a role invisible to end users. Molten iron emerging from blast furnaces contains sulfur absorbed from coke, typically 0.03 to 0.1 percent by weight. Sulfur makes steel brittle and prone to cracking during hot rolling. Removing it requires a reagent that binds sulfur more strongly than iron does. Magnesium serves this purpose. Foundries inject powdered magnesium or magnesium-rich alloys into ladles of molten iron. The magnesium reacts violently with dissolved sulfur, forming magnesium sulfide that floats to the surface as slag. The process reduces sulfur content to 0.001 percent or less within minutes. Steel quality improves markedly. Ductility increases. Weldability improves. This application accounts for substantial magnesium consumption despite leaving no magnesium in the final product. The metal sacrifices itself, binding impurity, enabling the iron to become proper steel. Modern foundries dose each ladle precisely, balancing sulfur removal against magnesium's tendency to also react with oxygen and other elements.

## Desert Abundance

Magnesium silicate minerals compose much of Earth's mantle and dominate certain surface formations. Olivine, a magnesium iron silicate, colors ultramafic rocks green and weathers to serpentinite. Vast outcrops exist in Oman, Cyprus, and coastal California. These rocks represent pieces of oceanic crust and upper mantle thrust onto continents during ancient collisions. When exposed to air and water, olivine absorbs carbon dioxide, converting it to stable magnesium carbonate. This natural carbon sequestration proceeds slowly under normal conditions but accelerates with heat and pressure. Scientists propose mining olivine-rich rock, crushing it, and spreading it on beaches or fields to capture atmospheric CO₂. The chemistry is simple; the scale is daunting. Converting enough carbon dioxide to meaningfully affect climate would require processing gigatons of rock annually. Magnesium's abundance makes the scheme theoretically possible. Economic and logistical barriers remain formidable. Meanwhile, the natural process continues in ophiolite massifs worldwide, drawing carbon from air into stone at geological pace.
