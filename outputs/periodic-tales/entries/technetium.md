# Technetium

***First Element Made by Hands***

> **Atomic Number:** 43 | **Symbol:** Tc | **Category:** Transition Metal

The element with atomic number 43 exists nowhere naturally on Earth in measurable quantities. Every atom of technetium present today was either created in a laboratory or generated inside a nuclear reactor. Its discovery in 1937 filled the last gap in the periodic table between hydrogen and uranium, but this achievement revealed something unsettling: an element that decays faster than geological time can preserve it. The name derives from the Greek *technetos*, meaning artificial, though this term misleads. Technetium forms continuously in the hearts of red giant stars and appears in their spectra. On Earth, however, its longest-lived isotope has a half-life of 4.2 million years—a blink against the planet's 4.5 billion year age. Any primordial technetium vanished long before the first bacteria appeared. What remains must be made.

---

Dmitri Mendeleev predicted element 43 in 1869, calling it eka-manganese and forecasting its properties from the gap in his table. For decades, chemists announced its discovery, then retracted their claims. The element proved elusive because it wasn't hiding—it simply wasn't there. In 1937, Italian physicist Emilio Segrè received a discarded molybdenum strip from a cyclotron at the University of California, Berkeley. The strip had been used as part of the machine's deflector. When Segrè analyzed it in Palermo, he found element 43 among the transmutation products. The first element synthesized by humans had been created accidentally, as industrial waste.

This absence from nature makes technetium's most common use possible. Technetium-99m, a metastable nuclear isomer with a six-hour half-life, appears in roughly 40 million medical procedures annually. Injected into patients, it concentrates in specific organs or tumors and emits gamma rays detectable by cameras outside the body. The short half-life means radiation exposure remains minimal. The patient receives a diagnostic image; the isotope decays to nearly nothing within two days. Hospitals generate technetium-99m from molybdenum-99, itself a fission product from nuclear reactors. A global supply chain moves these precursors from a handful of aging reactors to medical facilities worldwide. When reactors shut down for maintenance, shortages ripple through diagnostic medicine.

The chemistry of technetium resembles rhenium, the element directly below it on the periodic table, more than it resembles manganese above. It forms compounds in oxidation states from -1 to +7. Pertechnetate ion, TcO₄⁻, remains stable in solution and resists reduction, similar to perchlorate. This stability makes environmental contamination persistent. Nuclear reprocessing plants have released technetium into the Irish Sea and the English Channel. The element concentrates in seaweed and shellfish. It migrates slowly through soil. Its long half-life means contaminated sites remain so for millions of years—long enough for continents to drift measurably.

Beyond medicine, technetium finds limited use as a corrosion inhibitor for steel. Minute quantities prevent rust more effectively than other treatments. The U.S. considered using it to protect underground steel in its nuclear waste repositories, but regulations forbid adding more radioactive material to waste intended for isolation. The element's scarcity and radioactivity restrict most applications to research. Scientists study its chemistry to understand how it might move through groundwater near waste storage sites, or how to separate it from spent reactor fuel.

Technetium occupies a peculiar position: the lightest element with no stable isotopes, yet common enough in cosmic terms that astronomers can observe it in stars. Red giants fuse lighter elements and produce technetium as a byproduct of slow neutron capture. The element's presence in stellar spectra serves as evidence for ongoing nucleosynthesis—these stars are factories still in operation. On Earth, every sample ever held required a reactor, a cyclotron, or a particle accelerator. The element exists as proof that nature and artifice meet at the level of the nucleus. What stars make continuously in their depths, humans make in bursts, isolate, and watch decay.
