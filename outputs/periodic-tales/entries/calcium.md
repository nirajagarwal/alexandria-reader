# Calcium

***Builder of Shells and Bones***

> **Atomic Number:** 20 | **Symbol:** Ca | **Category:** Alkaline Earth Metal

Calcium ranks fifth among the elements in crustal abundance. It forms no pure deposits. Water dissolves it from limestone and chalk, ancient accumulations of marine shells. Rivers carry it to the sea, where organisms extract it again, building new shells, maintaining the cycle that has run for half a billion years.

The metal itself, soft enough to cut with a knife, reacts vigorously with water. It burns in air with a brick-red flame. No organism uses elemental calcium. All use its compounds, especially calcium phosphate and calcium carbonate. Vertebrate skeletons, mollusk shells, coral reefs, egg casings, the very structure of multicellular life on land and in water depends on this element's ability to form stable, insoluble minerals within living tissue.

Human bodies contain more calcium than any other mineral. Industry uses it to make cement, steel, glass, paper. The element moves perpetually between rock, water, living tissue, and atmosphere, the largest cycle of any element after carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen.

### Limestone and Chalk

Calcium carbonate deposits blanket continental shelves and form entire mountain ranges. The White Cliffs of Dover consist of coccoliths, calcium carbonate plates secreted by marine algae, accumulated over millions of years. The Alps contain thick beds of limestone, once tropical reefs. Underground, water charged with carbon dioxide slowly dissolves limestone, creating caves, sinkholes, the karst topography that covers twenty percent of Earth's ice-free land surface. The dissolved calcium eventually reaches the ocean, where the cycle resumes.

### The First Isolation

Humphry Davy isolated calcium metal in 1808 through electrolysis of lime mixed with mercury oxide. The pure metal proved too reactive for practical use. Earlier experimenters had studied calcium compounds without recognizing a distinct element. Lime, known since antiquity, served for mortar, plaster, agriculture. Its chemical nature remained obscure until Lavoisier classified it as an oxide. Davy's isolation confirmed calcium as an element, but the metal remained a laboratory curiosity. Industrial chemistry continued to use calcium oxide and hydroxide, not the pure metal.

### Bones and Teeth

Hydroxyapatite, a crystalline calcium phosphate, comprises sixty-five percent of bone mass and ninety-six percent of tooth enamel. Bone serves as calcium's reservoir. When blood calcium drops, parathyroid hormone triggers bone resorption, releasing calcium into circulation. When blood calcium rises, calcitonin promotes bone deposition. This regulation maintains constant blood calcium levels, essential for nerve transmission, muscle contraction, blood clotting. Vitamin D controls calcium absorption in the intestine. Without adequate dietary calcium, bone density decreases. The skeleton surrenders its substance to maintain the blood concentration that keeps nerves and muscles functioning.

### Cement and Concrete

Portland cement, a mixture of calcium silicates produced by heating limestone and clay to 1450 degrees Celsius, transforms when mixed with water. Calcium hydroxide forms, then slowly reacts with carbon dioxide from air, gradually hardening into calcium carbonate again. Concrete, cement mixed with sand and aggregate, becomes the world's most used manufactured material. Humanity produces four billion tons annually. The chemical reaction that binds it mirrors the geological processes that formed limestone, a metamorphosis of calcium compounds running in reverse, slowly returning to stone.

### Shells and Reefs

Mollusks precipitate calcium carbonate in two crystal forms: calcite and aragonite. Nacre, the iridescent lining of abalone shells, consists of aragonite platelets separated by organic matrix, achieving exceptional strength through microscopic architecture. Corals secrete calcium carbonate skeletons, building structures visible from space. Reef formation requires warm water saturated with calcium carbonate. Ocean acidification, caused by increased atmospheric carbon dioxide, reduces carbonate ion concentration. Corals struggle to build. Some shells thin. The calcium cycle, stable for epochs, adjusts to new chemistry.

### Dietary Source

Dairy products, leafy greens, fish with edible bones supply calcium. Recommended intake ranges from 1000 to 1300 milligrams daily, depending on age. Deficiency leads to weakened bones, tetany, impaired growth. Excess calcium contributes to kidney stones and vascular calcification. Most calcium remains in bone. A small fraction, tightly regulated, circulates in blood and tissue, enabling muscle contraction and nerve function. The body maintains this fraction even when dietary intake falls short, drawing from bone if necessary, prioritizing immediate physiological need over long-term skeletal integrity.

### The Red Flame

Calcium burns with a distinctive brick-red flame, used in fireworks and signal flares. The color originates from electron transitions in excited calcium atoms. Steelmakers add calcium to molten iron to remove sulfur and oxygen, improving steel quality. Calcium metal serves as a reducing agent in the production of other metals, including uranium and thorium. These applications consume thousands of tons annually, though calcium's reactivity makes handling difficult. The metal must be stored under inert atmosphere or mineral oil to prevent oxidation.

### Blood and Heartbeat

Cardiac muscle contraction depends on calcium ions flowing into cells. The ion channels that admit calcium determine heart rhythm. Too much calcium causes excessive contraction, too little prevents it. Calcium channel blockers, medications that reduce calcium entry into cells, treat high blood pressure and arrhythmias. The element's role extends beyond structure. It carries signals within cells, triggering enzyme activation, neurotransmitter release, muscle contraction. Cellular calcium concentration, maintained at extremely low levels, rises rapidly when cells receive certain signals, then falls again through active transport, a switch that controls countless biological processes.
