# Chromium

***Metal of Color and Hardness***

> **Atomic Number:** 24 | **Symbol:** Cr | **Category:** Transition Metal

Chromium forms in the supernova deaths of massive stars, where neutron capture builds atoms heavier than iron. On Earth it concentrates in chromite ore, the only commercially viable source, found in layered igneous intrusions and ophiolite complexes. The pure metal resists corrosion through a microscopically thin oxide layer that reforms instantly when damaged. This passivation makes chromium essential for stainless steel, discovered when early twentieth-century metallurgists learned that adding chromium to iron prevented rust. The element's name comes from the Greek for color, given by Louis Vauquelin in 1797 after he isolated it from Siberian red lead ore and noted the brilliant hues of its compounds. Every oxidation state produces a different color: green chromium oxide, orange dichromate, yellow chromate. Industrial civilization runs on chromium-hardened steel. The metal's biological role remains uncertain, though trivalent chromium appears in trace amounts in human metabolism.

## Discovery in Siberia

Louis Vauquelin received samples of Siberian red lead ore in 1797. The mineral, now called crocoite, contained an unknown element. Vauquelin dissolved the ore in acid, precipitated lead, and found a residue that produced green, yellow, and orange solutions depending on treatment. He isolated the metal itself by heating chromium oxide with charcoal. The pure element appeared as gray crystals with unusual hardness.

Nicolas-Louis Vauquelin taught chemistry at the École Polytechnique and worked as assayer at the Paris Mint. His isolation of chromium came two years after his discovery of beryllium. He named the new element for the range of colors its compounds displayed, from the vivid reds and oranges of lead chromate to the deep greens of chromium oxide.

## The Stainless Revolution

Harry Brearley discovered stainless steel in Sheffield in 1913 while searching for a rifle barrel alloy resistant to erosion. He added chromium to molten iron, let the samples cool, and found one with 12.8 percent chromium remained bright after exposure to acid and air. The chromium formed a protective oxide layer so thin it appeared invisible, yet impervious to further oxidation.

Steel containing at least 10.5 percent chromium qualifies as stainless. The chromium oxide layer measures 1 to 5 nanometers thick. It forms in milliseconds when fresh steel meets oxygen. Scratch the surface and the layer regenerates instantly. This passivation made possible surgical instruments, food processing equipment, architectural panels, chemical reactors. Modern stainless steel production exceeds 50 million tons annually.

## Industrial Hardening

Chromium plating deposits a layer of pure metal onto steel, copper, aluminum, or plastic. The process uses hexavalent chromium in sulfuric acid solution. An electric current drives chromium ions to the cathode, where they form a coating 2 to 250 micrometers thick. The plated surface resists wear and corrosion.

Decorative chrome appeared on automobile bumpers and trim in the 1920s. Hard chrome plating, developed for industrial applications, creates surfaces harder than most tool steels. Aircraft landing gear, hydraulic cylinder rods, printing press rollers, diesel engine components receive chrome plating to extend service life. The characteristic mirror finish comes from careful polishing before plating. Beneath that reflective surface lies the functional reality: a metal that withstands friction and environment longer than almost any alternative.

## Pigment and Dye

Chromium compounds color paint, glass, ceramics, and textiles. Lead chromate produces chrome yellow, the pigment van Gogh used for sunflowers and wheat fields. Chromium oxide creates the stable green in currency notes and military camouflage. Chromate and dichromate salts yield oranges and reds for warning signs and construction equipment.

The textile industry fixes chromium mordants to bind dyes permanently to wool, silk, and cotton. Chrome tanning converts animal hide to leather, replacing vegetable tannins that require months with a chromium sulfate process completed in hours. Most shoe leather, upholstery, and industrial leather undergoes chrome tanning. The process produces blue-green leather in the wet state, neutralized to the final color during finishing.

## Hexavalent Hazard

Chromium exists in two stable oxidation states with opposite biological effects. Trivalent chromium, Cr(III), appears in trace amounts in metabolism and may play a role in glucose regulation. Hexavalent chromium, Cr(VI), ranks among the most toxic industrial chemicals.

Cr(VI) penetrates cell membranes readily. Inside cells it reduces to Cr(III), generating free radicals that damage DNA. Inhalation causes lung cancer. Skin contact produces ulcers that heal slowly. Industrial exposure occurs in chrome plating, stainless steel welding, leather tanning, pigment production. The Occupational Safety and Health Administration lowered the permissible exposure limit in 2006. Water contamination from chromium plating facilities, documented in Hinkley, California and other sites, required extensive remediation. The gap between chromium's industrial utility and hexavalent chromium's toxicity demands continuous management.

## Refractory Oxide

Chromium oxide, Cr₂O₃, melts at 2,435 degrees Celsius. This refractory ceramic lines furnaces, kilns, and crucibles for glass and steel production. The oxide resists chemical attack and thermal shock. Ground to fine particles, it serves as polishing compound for precision optics and metal surfaces.

Ruby gains its red color from chromium impurities in aluminum oxide. Emerald's green comes from chromium in beryllium aluminum silicate. Synthetic ruby, grown by the Verneuil process since 1902, uses aluminum oxide powder doped with chromium oxide. These crystals served as laser gain media in early solid-state lasers and continue in industrial cutting and medical applications.

## Planetary Composition

Chromium appears in meteorites at concentrations reflecting solar system abundances. Isotope ratios help date the formation of planetary bodies and trace their differentiation. Earth's mantle contains approximately 2,500 parts per million chromium. The metal concentrates in mafic and ultramafic rocks.

Chromite deposits form where dense chromium-rich minerals crystallize early from cooling magma and settle into layers. The Bushveld Complex in South Africa contains the world's largest chromite reserves, formed 2 billion years ago. These layered intrusions supply 70 percent of global chromium production. Zimbabwe, Kazakhstan, India, and Turkey mine significant deposits. No substitute exists for chromium in stainless steel. Strategic metal stockpiles in consuming nations reflect this dependency.
