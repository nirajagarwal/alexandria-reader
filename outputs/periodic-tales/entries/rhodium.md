# Rhodium

***The Rarest Catalyst***

> **Atomic Number:** 45 | **Symbol:** Rh | **Category:** Transition Metal

Rhodium exists in concentrations of one part per 200 million in Earth's crust, making it among the scarcest stable elements. It formed in supernova nucleosynthesis, scattered thinly through the cosmos. On Earth, it concentrates almost exclusively alongside platinum in South African ores, with smaller deposits in Russia and Canada. The metal resists corrosion absolutely. It reflects light with unusual efficiency. At room temperature it shows no tendency to oxidize, tarnish, or degrade. These properties, combined with its catalytic power, have made this rare element indispensable to industrial civilization. Nearly all rhodium extracted serves a single purpose: converting automotive exhaust into less harmful gases. The price has reached $30,000 per troy ounce, driven by supply constraints and regulatory demand. Eighty percent of global production comes from a single geological formation in South Africa.

## Discovery in Residue

William Hyde Wollaston isolated rhodium in 1803 from crude platinum ore. He had dissolved the material in aqua regia, precipitated the platinum, and found a rose-red residue remaining. The color gave the element its name, from the Greek *rhodon*, meaning rose. Wollaston kept the discovery quiet for a year while he worked to purify the metal and understand its properties. He had discovered palladium the previous year using similar methods. Both elements existed as trace contaminants in platinum ore, their presence previously unrecognized. The isolation required meticulous chemistry. Modern extraction follows the same principle but with industrial scale and efficiency. The ore undergoes months of processing to yield grams of rhodium from tons of rock.

## The Catalytic Triangle

Three platinum-group metals form the core of automotive catalytic converters: platinum, palladium, and rhodium. Each performs a distinct function. Rhodium specializes in nitrogen oxide reduction, breaking N-O bonds and releasing harmless nitrogen gas. The other metals cannot match this efficiency. A typical converter contains 1-2 grams of rhodium coating a ceramic honeycomb. Exhaust gases pass through at 400-800 degrees Celsius. The rhodium surface facilitates reactions without being consumed. Regulations mandating catalytic converters took effect in the United States in 1975. Global adoption followed. Annual automotive demand now exceeds total mining production. Recycling from scrapped vehicles has become essential to supply. Theft of catalytic converters has emerged as a significant crime category, driven by rhodium's value.

## Reflective Brilliance

Rhodium reflects 80 percent of visible light, more than silver or aluminum. The jewelry industry exploits this property through rhodium plating. White gold receives a thin rhodium layer, typically 0.1-1.0 microns, to enhance whiteness and provide scratch resistance. The plating wears over time, requiring reapplication every few years. Mirrors for specialized optical instruments use rhodium coatings for their reflectivity and durability. Searchlights once used rhodium-plated reflectors. The metal's reflectivity extends into ultraviolet wavelengths, valuable for certain scientific applications. Electroplating deposits rhodium from sulfate or phosphate baths. The process requires careful control; the metal's rarity makes waste expensive. A single gram plates hundreds of jewelry pieces.

## Supply Concentration

The Bushveld Complex in South Africa contains 80 percent of the world's rhodium reserves. This geological formation, 2 billion years old, holds concentrated platinum-group metals in layered intrusions. Three companies control most production. Mining occurs at depths exceeding 1,500 meters. Labor disputes, power outages, and safety concerns regularly disrupt output. Russia's Norilsk deposits provide most of the remaining supply. No significant new deposits have been discovered in decades. Recycling provides 20-30 percent of annual supply, constrained by the lag time between vehicle sale and scrapping. The concentration creates price volatility. Between 2019 and 2021, rhodium prices increased tenfold. Industrial users have no substitute for the metal's catalytic properties in nitrogen oxide reduction. Research into alternative converter technologies continues but has not yet displaced rhodium.

## Chemical Resilience

Rhodium resists attack by all single acids, including aqua regia at room temperature. Only at high temperatures, or in molten alkalis, does the metal react significantly. It forms compounds in oxidation states from +1 to +6, though +3 dominates. Rhodium chloride serves as a precursor for most rhodium chemistry. The metal crystallizes in a face-centered cubic structure, typical of noble metals. Its melting point of 1,964 degrees Celsius exceeds platinum's. Rhodium is hard, brittle, and difficult to work. It cannot be drawn into wire or rolled into sheets without special techniques. These properties limit its use to applications where its unique catalytic or optical characteristics justify the difficulty and expense of fabrication.

## Beyond Catalysis

Nuclear reactors use rhodium as a detector material. Rhodium-103, the only stable isotope, absorbs neutrons efficiently, making it useful for measuring neutron flux. The pharmaceutical industry employs rhodium complexes in certain catalytic reactions, particularly in asymmetric hydrogenation to create chiral molecules. Laboratory equipment manufacturers coat glass instruments with rhodium to create durable, chemically resistant surfaces for handling aggressive substances. Some high-performance spark plugs use rhodium-tipped electrodes for longevity. The metal appears in specialized electrical contacts where its resistance to corrosion and low contact resistance matter. These applications consume a small fraction of production but demonstrate the metal's versatility. Research into rhodium-based catalysts for hydrogen fuel cells continues, though no commercial application has emerged.
