# Antimony

***Ancient Cosmetic, Modern Flame Retardant***

> **Atomic Number:** 51 | **Symbol:** Sb | **Category:** Metalloid

Antimony occupies the border between metal and nonmetal, a metalloid with properties of both. The universe created it through stellar nucleosynthesis, primarily in supernovae, scattering it thinly across the cosmos. On Earth, it concentrates in sulfide ores, most commonly as stibnite, a silvery-gray mineral that humans have extracted for five millennia. The element forms brittle crystals, conducts electricity poorly, and resists corrosion. Its compounds found use in ancient cosmetics, medieval alchemy, and modern flame retardants. Today it appears in batteries, semiconductors, and plastics, though toxicity limits direct handling. China produces most of the world's supply. Antimony sits at atomic number 51, below arsenic in the periodic table, sharing its metalloid character and its capacity for both utility and harm.

---

Egyptian women ground stibnite into kohl, darkening their eyelids with antimony sulfide. The practice spread across ancient civilizations. Pliny the Elder documented both cosmetic and medicinal applications, noting the mineral's ability to cauterize wounds. The element's name likely derives from the Greek *anti-monos*, "not alone," referring to its frequent occurrence with other elements. Medieval alchemists assigned it esoteric significance, calling it the "wolf of metals" for its tendency to combine with and alter other substances during smelting.

The pure element resisted isolation until the seventeenth century. Metallurgists found it expanded upon solidification, unlike most metals, making it valuable for casting type metal in printing presses. This property allowed sharp, durable characters. For three centuries, antimony alloys formed the backbone of movable type, enabling the mass production of books. The metal's brittleness prevented structural use, but its addition to lead created harder, more precisely cast objects.

In living systems, antimony serves no known biological function. The body treats it as a toxin, though one less immediately lethal than arsenic. Antimony compounds, particularly tartar emetic, served as medicines for centuries despite causing violent purging. Nineteenth-century physicians prescribed antimony to induce vomiting, reduce fever, and treat parasitic diseases. Some formulations actually worked against certain tropical parasites, though the therapeutic window remained narrow. Modern medicine uses pentavalent antimony compounds as first-line treatment for leishmaniasis in regions where the parasitic disease persists, carefully balancing efficacy against toxicity.

The twentieth century found antimony's most widespread application in flame retardants. Antimony trioxide, combined with halogenated compounds, suppresses combustion in plastics, textiles, and electronic components. The mechanism involves interference with the radical chain reactions that sustain flames. This use grew exponentially after World War II as synthetic materials proliferated. By the early twenty-first century, flame retardants accounted for more than half of antimony consumption globally. Concern over environmental persistence and potential health effects of some formulations has prompted research into alternatives, though antimony compounds remain standard in many applications.

Modern electronics employ antimony in semiconductor doping, where small quantities alter silicon's electrical properties. The element also appears in lead-acid batteries as a hardening agent, though calcium increasingly replaces it in maintenance-free designs. Antimony improves the performance of certain photovoltaic cells and appears in some infrared detectors. These high-tech applications consume far less material than flame retardants but represent growth areas as older uses face restriction.

China dominates antimony production, controlling roughly ninety percent of global supply. Extraction concentrates in Hunan Province, where mining dates back centuries. The geopolitical implications of such concentration have drawn attention as electronic devices proliferate and renewable energy systems expand. Primary ore grades have declined, making extraction more difficult and expensive. Recycling recovers some antimony from lead-acid batteries and electronic waste, but most remains unrecovered in dispersed products.

The element accumulates in soils near smelters and mines, persisting for decades. Elevated antimony in drinking water correlates with areas of natural mineralization and industrial activity. Regulatory agencies have set limits, though standards vary internationally. Workers in antimony processing face occupational exposure risks, primarily through inhalation of dust containing antimony compounds. Chronic exposure can damage the heart and lungs.

Antimony's future depends on balancing utility against environmental and health concerns. Its role in flame safety remains significant, particularly in regions with stringent fire codes. The search for substitutes continues, driven by both toxicity concerns and supply constraints. Some applications will likely persist indefinitely, while others face phase-out. The element remains what it has always been: useful within limits, hazardous beyond them, and thoroughly embedded in the material infrastructure of industrial civilization.
