# Nihonium

***Forged in Collision, Born in Milliseconds***

> **Atomic Number:** 113 | **Symbol:** Nh | **Category:** Post-transition metal

Nihonium exists only because physicists refuse to accept the periodic table's natural limits. No star forged it. No supernova scattered it. Earth holds none. The element appears when zinc atoms, accelerated to tremendous speeds, collide with bismuth targets in particle accelerators. The impacts fuse nuclei briefly, creating atoms that decay in milliseconds. Between 2003 and 2012, researchers at RIKEN in Japan produced fewer than ten confirmed atoms. In 2016, IUPAC granted naming rights to the Japanese team. They chose Nihonium, from Nihon, Japan's name for itself. It became the first element named for an Asian nation. The achievement marked not discovery in the classical sense but creation through brute technological force, a demonstration that human ingenuity could extend the periodic table beyond nature's own production.

---

The story begins at RIKEN's Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science in Wako, Saitama Prefecture. A linear accelerator, RILAC, propelled zinc-70 ions to roughly ten percent the speed of light. These projectiles struck a rotating bismuth-209 target, itself an unusual choice as bismuth is the heaviest stable element. The collision, when successful, fused the nuclei momentarily. Most attempts failed. The zinc and bismuth simply scattered. But in exceedingly rare instances, the nuclei merged and held together long enough to be called an atom of element 113.

The numbers convey the challenge. RIKEN's team ran experiments continuously from September 2003 to August 2012. They recorded three unambiguous decay chains attributable to nihonium-278, the isotope produced when zinc-70 meets bismuth-209. Three atoms across nine years. Each atom existed for roughly two milliseconds before decaying through alpha emission to roentgenium-274, which itself decayed onward through a cascade of short-lived superheavy elements. The researchers identified nihonium not by observing it directly but by tracking the characteristic decay signatures of its daughter products, a chain of atomic fingerprints leading backward to the original creation.

A parallel effort unfolded at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia, in collaboration with Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory. This team pursued a different synthesis pathway, bombarding americium-243 with calcium-48. Their experiments, conducted between 2003 and 2005, produced possible nihonium atoms through the decay of moscovium-288. The evidence remained less definitive. IUPAC's review in 2015 credited both teams but emphasized RIKEN's reproducible results. The Japanese claim held.

The naming became significant beyond mere chemistry. When Kosuke Morita and his colleagues proposed Nihonium, they broke new ground in periodic table geography. Elements had been named for places before: francium, germanium, polonium, berkelium, californium, tennessine. But these honored Western Europe and North America. Nihonium represented Asia's first presence on the table. The name itself carries weight. Nihon combines the characters for sun and origin, commonly translated as "the land of the rising sun." An element born from collision, named for a nation rebuilt from devastation, earned through decades of disciplined experimental physics.

Nihonium's properties remain largely theoretical. No sample exists long enough to measure density, melting point, or reactivity. Calculations suggest it would be a relatively dense metal, perhaps with some unexpected chemical behavior due to relativistic effects on its electron configuration. At atomic number 113, electrons move at velocities approaching light speed in their innermost orbitals. This relativistic contraction alters how outer electrons behave, potentially making superheavy elements chemically distinct from their lighter periodic table neighbors. But these remain predictions. Nihonium's chemistry is a thought experiment conducted with quantum mechanics rather than test tubes.

The element's existence raises questions about the periodic table's boundaries. Element 113 sits well beyond uranium, the heaviest naturally occurring element in significant quantities. It resides in what physicists call the island of instability, a region where nuclei hold together only briefly before radioactive decay tears them apart. Theory predicts an island of stability farther out, perhaps near element 120 or 126, where certain magic numbers of protons and neutrons might confer relative longevity. Nihonium sits in the waters before that theoretical shore. Its millisecond lifespan represents not stability but a brief pause in inevitable collapse.

The cost of creating three atoms defies easy calculation. RIKEN's heavy-ion accelerator, the staff years, the electricity, the target materials, the detector systems that captured the decay chains—all expenditure for a handful of nuclei that flashed into and out of existence faster than a camera shutter clicks. Yet the achievement stands. Nihonium confirms that the periodic table extends beyond nature's own workshop, that human beings can, with sufficient energy and precision, summon elements that stars and supernovae never produced in abundance. Whether this represents progress or hubris depends on how one values knowledge that has no application beyond its own existence.
