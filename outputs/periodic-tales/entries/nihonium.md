# Nihonium

***Born in Collision, Gone in Seconds***

> **Atomic Number:** 113 | **Symbol:** Nh | **Category:** Post-transition metal

Nihonium exists only because humans force it into being. It does not occur in nature. No star makes it. No geological process concentrates it. Scientists create individual atoms by smashing zinc nuclei into bismuth targets at extraordinary energies, fusing them into something that has never existed before and will never exist for long. The element lives for fractions of a second before decaying into lighter elements. Its properties remain largely theoretical. Its appearance is unknown. Its chemistry is inferred from position on the periodic table and computational models. Yet it holds a permanent place in the roster of elements, the first to be discovered in East Asia, named for Nihon, the Japanese word for Japan.

## The Beam and the Target

A team at RIKEN's Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science in Saitama, Japan, began attempts to synthesize element 113 in September 2003. They accelerated zinc-70 nuclei to roughly 10 percent the speed of light and fired them at a thin bismuth-209 target. Most collisions produced nothing useful. The nuclei either bounced apart or fused briefly before splitting. The researchers were searching for the rare event where 30 protons from zinc and 83 from bismuth would combine and hold together long enough to be detected. In July 2004, they observed their first confirmed atom of nihonium-278. It lasted 0.34 milliseconds. Two more atoms appeared in 2005 and 2012. Three atoms in nine years of continuous experimentation.

## The Naming Rights

Russia's Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna and the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory in California also claimed synthesis of element 113 through different reaction pathways in the early 2000s. The competing claims required adjudication. The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry reviewed all evidence and in 2015 awarded priority to the RIKEN team. This gave Japan the right to name the element. RIKEN proposed nihonium in 2016, marking the first element named for an Asian nation. The name was approved that same year. Elements 114 through 118, synthesized by Russian and American teams, were named simultaneously. Nihonium joined moscovium, tennessine, and oganesson in completing the periodic table's seventh row.

## Superheavy Territory

Nihonium sits among the superheavy elements, those with atomic numbers exceeding 104. At these extremes, nuclear forces barely overcome the electromagnetic repulsion between protons packed into an impossibly small space. The nucleus exists in a state of profound instability. Nihonium-286, the longest-lived known isotope, survives perhaps 20 seconds before decaying through alpha emission. Theoretical predictions suggest an island of stability might exist around atomic number 120, where certain configurations of protons and neutrons could produce isotopes lasting days or years. Nihonium lies short of this island. Current understanding suggests no isotope will prove stable enough for bulk chemistry. The element will remain accessible only as individual short-lived atoms.

## Theoretical Chemistry

What nihonium would do if enough existed to observe remains speculation grounded in quantum mechanics. Position suggests it should behave as a post-transition metal, similar to thallium or indium. Relativistic effects—the influence of extreme nuclear charge on electron behavior—become pronounced in superheavy elements. Calculations indicate nihonium's outer electrons might behave unexpectedly, possibly making the element more noble gas-like than its position predicts. No one has isolated enough atoms simultaneously to test melting point, reactivity, or structure. The chemistry exists in computer models and educated extrapolation. The gap between what is known and what is inferred grows wider as atomic number increases.

## The Question of Purpose

Nihonium serves no practical function. It cannot be manufactured in quantities useful for any application. Its study yields knowledge about nuclear structure, the limits of the periodic table, the behavior of matter under extreme conditions. The research requires enormous resources: particle accelerators, detection equipment, years of beam time, international collaboration. Critics note the expense and ask what justifies creating elements that exist only momentarily. Proponents argue that pushing the boundaries of nuclear physics reveals fundamental truths about how matter is constructed, what holds it together, where the periodic table ends. The debate continues. The experiments proceed.

## Detection in Decay

Nihonium cannot be observed directly. Researchers detect it through its decay products. When a nihonium nucleus forms, it almost immediately emits an alpha particle—a helium nucleus—becoming roentgenium. Roentgenium decays to meitnerium. Meitnerium decays to bohrium. Each step releases energy in a characteristic pattern. Detectors measure these energies and the time intervals between them, producing a decay chain that serves as a signature. If the chain matches predictions for nihonium, the atom is considered confirmed. The entire sequence unfolds in milliseconds. The element's existence is recorded not as a substance but as a cascade of radiation. This is how discovery happens in the superheavy realm.

## The Seventh Row Complete

Nihonium filled one of the last gaps in the periodic table's seventh row. By 2016, all elements from hydrogen to oganesson had been either discovered in nature or synthesized in laboratories. The table, first sketched by Mendeleev in 1869 with gaps where unknown elements should be, stood complete. Whether row eight will be possible remains unknown. Theoretical limits on nuclear binding suggest that beyond atomic number 120 or so, no nucleus can hold together long enough to be detected. The periodic table may have a natural boundary. Nihonium marks a point near that boundary, where human ingenuity touches the edge of what matter can do.
