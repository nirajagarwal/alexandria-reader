# Aluminum

***Earth's Most Abundant Metal***

> **Atomic Number:** 13 | **Symbol:** Al | **Category:** Post-transition metal

Aluminum composes eight percent of Earth's crust by weight, more than any other metal. It never appears pure in nature. Oxygen binds it tightly in clays and minerals, holding it so firmly that humans encountered the metal itself only two centuries ago. When finally isolated, it proved lighter than iron, stronger than copper when alloyed, and resistant to corrosion despite its chemical eagerness to react. This combination of properties, once refined at scale, remade aviation, construction, and packaging. The element's abundance promised plenty. Its extraction demanded electricity. The story of aluminum is inseparable from the story of industrial power.

Humphry Davy predicted its existence in 1808, naming it from alum, the astringent compound known since antiquity. He never isolated it. Hans Christian Ørsted produced a few impure droplets in 1825. Friedrich Wöhler improved the process in 1845, obtaining powder and small beads. Henri Sainte-Claire Deville achieved the first industrial production in the 1850s using sodium reduction of aluminum chloride. The method yielded enough for Emperor Napoleon III to commission aluminum cutlery for state dinners while lesser guests used gold. For three decades, aluminum remained more precious than silver, a curiosity metal shaped into jewelry and display pieces.

The transformation came in 1886, when two men working independently on opposite sides of the Atlantic discovered the same process within months of each other. Charles Martin Hall in Ohio and Paul Héroult in France were both twenty-two years old. Both realized that dissolving aluminum oxide in molten cryolite and passing electric current through the mixture would deposit pure aluminum at the cathode. The Hall-Héroult process still governs production worldwide. Within a decade, the price of aluminum fell by ninety-eight percent.

The metal's resistance to corrosion stems from an instant reaction with air. A coherent oxide layer, mere nanometers thick, forms on any exposed surface and protects the metal beneath. This passivation makes aluminum suitable for structures exposed to weather and chemicals. The same oxide, however, makes aluminum extraordinarily difficult to extract. Bauxite ore, composed of aluminum hydroxides, must be refined through the Bayer process into pure alumina, then subjected to electrolysis at nine hundred and sixty degrees Celsius. Smelting one kilogram of aluminum requires thirteen to seventeen kilowatt-hours of electricity, more than any other common metal. Approximately three percent of global electricity consumption goes to aluminum production.

Aviation adopted aluminum early. The Wright brothers used an aluminum engine block to save weight. As aircraft grew more ambitious, they grew more aluminum. Duralumin, an alloy hardened by copper additions and heat treatment, combined lightness with strength. By the Second World War, military aircraft were flying aluminum skeletons wrapped in aluminum skin. The metal enabled the jet age. Modern commercial aircraft are sixty to eighty percent aluminum by weight, despite increasing competition from composites.

On Earth, aluminum occurs primarily bound in feldspars and micas, scattered throughout igneous rocks. Weathering concentrates it in clays. Bauxite forms in tropical conditions where heavy rainfall leaches away silicon, leaving aluminum hydroxides behind. Major deposits lie in Guinea, Australia, Brazil, and Jamaica. China produces and consumes more aluminum than any other nation, running smelters near hydroelectric dams where power is cheap. Iceland and Norway export their hydroelectric capacity as embedded electricity in aluminum ingots.

The element appears throughout the human body at low concentrations, between fifty and one hundred and fifty milligrams total, accumulated from food, water, and cookware. No biological function has been established. The body excretes most ingested aluminum through the kidneys. Occupational exposure to aluminum dust can cause pulmonary fibrosis. High doses affect the nervous system. Some research has examined potential links between aluminum exposure and neurological conditions, but no definitive mechanism has been established. Aluminum-based antiperspirants and antacids deliver regular exposure to millions of people. The metal's ubiquity in modern life makes complete avoidance impossible.

Recycling aluminum requires five percent of the energy needed for primary production from ore. The metal can be remelted indefinitely without degradation. Beverage cans, the most visible aluminum product in wealthy nations, achieve recycling rates exceeding seventy percent in some countries. The economic incentive is powerful enough that aluminum recycling developed quickly after mass production began. Still, primary smelting continues to expand, driven by demand from emerging economies and applications where recycled material cannot yet substitute for virgin metal.

The element's abundance ensures no shortage for centuries. The constraint is energy. As electricity generation shifts toward renewable sources, aluminum production must follow or remain tethered to fossil fuels and hydroelectric sites. Some smelters have closed when power prices rose. Others locate themselves beside power plants. The metal's future depends less on ore deposits than on how humanity chooses to generate and price electricity.
