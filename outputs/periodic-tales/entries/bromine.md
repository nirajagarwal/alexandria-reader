# Bromine

***The Only Liquid Nonmetal***

> **Atomic Number:** 35 | **Symbol:** Br | **Category:** Halogen

Bromine stands alone among the nonmetals as the only one liquid at room temperature. A heavy, dark red-brown substance that fumes in air, it exists naturally in salt deposits and seawater, concentrated over geological time as lighter elements evaporated. The name comes from the Greek *bromos*, meaning stench—the vapor reeks and burns the throat. Antoine-Jérôme Balard isolated it in 1826 from the salt marshes of Montpellier, beating Carl Löwig by months, though Löwig had kept his sample in a cupboard while finishing his doctorate. Chemically aggressive like its halogen siblings chlorine and iodine, bromine readily forms compounds with most elements. For a century it found use in sedatives, photographic film, and leaded gasoline additives. Industrial extraction continues from brine wells in Arkansas, China, and the Dead Sea—the Earth's saltiest body of water and its richest bromine source.

## Salt and Sea

Bromine never occurs free in nature. Too reactive, it binds immediately with other elements, primarily sodium, potassium, and magnesium. Seawater contains roughly 65 parts per million, a concentration low enough that early chemists dismissed it as unrecoverable. Salt lakes and underground brines contain far more. The Dead Sea holds 5,000 parts per million. When seawater evaporates, bromine concentrates in the remaining brine. Ancient salt deposits preserve this concentration. Modern extraction pumps deep brines to the surface or processes seawater directly, using chlorine gas to oxidize dissolved bromide ions into elemental bromine, which separates as a dense liquid layer.

## Sedation and Dependence

Potassium bromide entered medicine in 1857 as the first effective treatment for epileptic seizures. It calmed neural activity by replacing some chloride ions in the nervous system. Physicians prescribed it widely—for epilepsy, yes, but also for anxiety, insomnia, and what Victorian doctors termed excessive nervous excitement. By the early 1900s, bromide sedatives saturated the market. Patent medicines contained it. Soldiers received it to suppress sexual urges. Chronic use caused bromism: mental dullness, skin eruptions, tremors, psychosis. The symptoms mimicked mental illness. Patients committed to asylums recovered when the drug cleared their systems. Phenobarbital and other barbiturates replaced bromides by the 1920s, though bromide sedatives persisted in over-the-counter preparations until the 1970s in some countries.

## Flame Without Fire

Bromine compounds suppress combustion. When heated, they decompose and release bromine radicals that interrupt the chain reactions sustaining flames. This property made brominated flame retardants ubiquitous in consumer goods—furniture foam, carpet backing, electronics housings, children's pajamas. The chemicals migrated out of products into house dust, then into human bodies. Studies detected them in blood samples worldwide, including newborns. Some compounds persisted in the environment and accumulated up food chains. Concerns about toxicity and bioaccumulation led to phase-outs beginning in the 2000s. Manufacturers shifted to alternative retardants. Bromine-based systems remain in specific applications where performance requirements leave few substitutes: aircraft interiors, oil drilling fluids, certain industrial processes.

## Leaded Gasoline's Partner

Tetraethyl lead boosted octane ratings in gasoline starting in the 1920s, preventing engine knock. But lead deposited on engine parts and exhaust valves, causing damage. Ethylene dibromide solved this problem. Added alongside the lead, it reacted at high temperatures to form lead bromide, which passed out the exhaust as a gas. From the 1930s through the 1990s, vehicles burning leaded gasoline released both lead and bromine compounds into the atmosphere. Urban air contained measurable bromine. Roadside soils accumulated it. The phase-out of leaded gasoline—completed in most countries by 2000, globally by 2021—ended this dispersal. Bromine concentrations in ice cores and sediment layers mark the leaded gasoline era like a stratigraphic signature.

## Fumigation and Ozone

Methyl bromide proved devastatingly effective against agricultural pests, pathogens, and weeds. Applied to soil before planting or used to fumigate stored grain and shipping containers, it penetrated everywhere the air reached and killed nearly everything living. Global use peaked in the 1980s. Then scientists determined that methyl bromide depleted stratospheric ozone—each molecule could destroy hundreds of ozone molecules through catalytic cycles. The Montreal Protocol amendments listed it as a controlled substance in 1992. Most uses faced phase-out by 2005 in developed nations, 2015 in developing ones. Critical use exemptions persisted for strawberry cultivation and certain quarantine treatments. Alternative fumigants and cultural practices slowly filled the gap. Atmospheric methyl bromide concentrations declined but remained detectable years after restrictions took effect.

## Photography's Medium

Silver bromide crystals formed the light-sensitive layer of photographic film for over a century. Suspended in gelatin on celluloid or polyester, the tiny crystals underwent a subtle change when struck by photons—a few silver atoms formed in each affected crystal, creating a latent image invisible to the eye. Chemical developers amplified this image, reducing the affected crystals to metallic silver while leaving unexposed crystals unchanged. The result: a negative. Color film used three layers of differently sensitized silver bromide emulsions, each responding to a different wavelength range. The transition to digital imaging eliminated most photographic bromine use by the 2010s. Billions of film rolls entered landfills, their silver bromide content largely unrecovered.

## Red Liquid, Burned Skin

Elemental bromine destroys tissue on contact. The liquid penetrates skin rapidly, causing painful chemical burns that heal slowly. Vapor irritates eyes, nose, throat, and lungs at low concentrations. Higher exposures cause pulmonary edema—fluid filling the lungs—which can prove fatal. Laboratory workers handle it under fume hoods, often chilled to reduce fuming. The liquid must be stored in glass—it attacks many plastics—in secondary containment. Spills require careful neutralization with sodium thiosulfate solution. Industrial bromine facilities maintain emergency response protocols. Transport regulations classify it as corrosive and toxic. Accidental releases have caused evacuations. Yet controlled properly, the hazards become manageable. Chemists work with it routinely, respecting its properties, never casual.
