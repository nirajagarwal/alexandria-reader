# Bromine

***The Red Liquid Metal***

> **Atomic Number:** 35 | **Symbol:** Br | **Category:** Halogen

Bromine stands alone among nonmetals as the only one liquid at room temperature. A dense, corrosive red-brown substance that fumes readily into toxic vapor, it exists naturally only in combined form, most abundantly dissolved in seawater and concentrated in salt deposits left by ancient oceans. Its name derives from the Greek *bromos*, meaning stench, a reference to its choking, acrid odor. Discovered independently in 1825 and 1826 by two chemists working with brine samples, bromine quickly found industrial application despite its hazardous nature. Today it appears in flame retardants, agricultural fumigants, and pharmaceutical compounds, though environmental concerns have constrained some traditional uses. The element's reactivity places it among the halogens, one step less aggressive than chlorine but considerably more so than iodine.

Antoine-Jérôme Balard, a young French chemist working in Montpellier, isolated bromine from Mediterranean seawater in 1825. He heated the concentrated brine with chlorine, which displaced the bromine, then extracted the element through repeated distillation. A year later, Carl Jacob Löwig, a German chemistry student, independently extracted bromine from mineral water near his hometown. Balard published first, securing credit for the discovery. Both men had worked with material that burned the skin on contact and produced vapors that inflamed the eyes and throat. The element's violence announced itself immediately.

The ocean contains roughly 65 parts per million of bromide ion, making seawater the planet's primary bromine reservoir. This works out to approximately 100 trillion kilograms dissolved in Earth's waters. Concentrated deposits also exist in underground brines and salt lakes, particularly in Arkansas, Michigan, and the Dead Sea, where evaporation has left bromide levels hundreds of times higher than in open ocean. Commercial extraction pumps these brines to the surface, treating them with chlorine gas to oxidize bromide to elemental bromine, which is then separated by distillation. The process recovers a fuming red liquid that must be transported in lead-lined containers or special glass vessels.

Bromine's chemistry makes it useful for disrupting biological and chemical processes. For most of the twentieth century, ethylene dibromide served as an additive in leaded gasoline, scavenging lead deposits from engine cylinders. Agricultural fumigants containing bromine sterilized soil before planting, killing nematodes, fungi, and weed seeds. Methyl bromide dominated this application until its phasing out under the Montreal Protocol, which identified it as an ozone-depleting substance. The compound's effectiveness came from the same property that made it dangerous: high reactivity and good penetration through soil and organic matter.

Flame retardants represent bromine's largest current market. Brominated compounds, added to plastics, textiles, and foam, interfere with combustion by releasing bromine radicals that interrupt the chemical chain reactions sustaining fire. These additives appear in electronics, furniture, building materials, and vehicles. Their effectiveness is clear—fire deaths have declined in regions mandating their use. Yet concerns about persistence and bioaccumulation have grown. Some brominated flame retardants build up in human tissue and wildlife, appearing in breast milk, household dust, and Arctic animals far from any manufacturing source. Regulatory pressure has pushed development toward less persistent alternatives.

Pharmaceutical chemistry employs bromine in synthesis, though the element rarely appears in final drug molecules. Brominated intermediates facilitate reactions that build complex organic structures, then the bromine gets replaced or removed before the medicine reaches patients. A few drugs do retain bromine: certain sedatives and anticonvulsants rely on its presence for activity. The element also appears in photographic chemistry, where silver bromide's sensitivity to light made it fundamental to film emulsions for more than a century.

Direct exposure to bromine liquid causes severe chemical burns. The vapor irritates mucous membranes at concentrations well below lethal levels, producing coughing, difficulty breathing, and fluid accumulation in the lungs. Industrial accidents involving bromine typically involve transport or storage failures. The liquid's density exceeds that of water, so spills flow along the ground, pooling in low areas while releasing choking fumes. Cleanup requires neutralization with sodium thiosulfate or other reducing agents before the contaminated area becomes safe. Workers handling bromine wear full protective equipment: respirators, face shields, neoprene suits, and boots.

The element's atomic structure positions it between two other halogens in reactivity. Chlorine above it on the periodic table displaces bromine from bromide compounds, while bromine displaces iodine from iodides. This hierarchy reflects the tightness with which each element holds electrons. Bromine's outer electron shell seeks one additional electron to achieve stability, making it aggressive toward metals, organic molecules, and most other chemical partners. Only fluorine and chlorine exceed its appetite for electrons. The liquid state at room temperature results from weak attractions between bromine molecules, strong enough to condense the element but not to solidify it.
