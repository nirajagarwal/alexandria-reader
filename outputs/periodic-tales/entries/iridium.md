# Iridium

***Rarest Metal on Earth's Surface***

> **Atomic Number:** 77 | **Symbol:** Ir | **Category:** Transition Metal

Iridium arrived at Earth's surface in two waves separated by billions of years. The first came with planetary formation, most of it sinking into the core as molten iron drew dense metals downward. The second came 66 million years ago when an asteroid struck the Yucatán Peninsula, vaporizing on impact and depositing a thin layer of iridium-rich dust worldwide. This boundary line in the geological record marks the extinction of the dinosaurs. The element takes its name from the rainbow-colored compounds it forms, though the metal itself is white, hard, and exceptionally resistant to corrosion. At room temperature, nothing attacks it. It sits in the platinum group, sharing properties with its neighbors but distinguished by being the densest element under standard conditions and the most corrosion-resistant substance known. Annual production amounts to only three metric tons globally.

---

The Spanish conquistadors first encountered iridium unknowingly in the sixteenth century, finding it as an impurity in platinum ore they considered worthless. They called the mixture platina del Pinto, "little silver of the Pinto River," and discarded it. Two centuries passed before chemists isolated platinum itself. Another generation passed before Smithson Tennant, working in London in 1803, dissolved crude platinum in aqua regia and found residues that would not dissolve. From these residues he extracted two new elements. He named one osmium for its penetrating odor. The other he named iridium, iris being Greek for rainbow, after observing the striking colors of its salts.

The metal resists every common reagent. Acids, even aqua regia, leave it untouched at room temperature. Alkalis have no effect. It withstands oxidation at any temperature short of white heat. This resistance stems from its electronic structure and the tightness with which its atoms pack together. The density exceeds that of osmium when measured carefully at standard temperature and pressure, making iridium the heaviest substance in existence by volume. A cube of iridium one foot on each side would weigh nearly 1,400 pounds.

These properties made iridium the material of choice for objects requiring permanence. In 1889, the International Bureau of Weights and Measures cast the international prototype kilogram from an alloy of 90 percent platinum and 10 percent iridium. This cylinder, stored in a vault outside Paris, defined the kilogram for 130 years. The iridium hardened the platinum and prevented wear. In 2019, the definition shifted to fundamental constants, but the prototype remains, its surface still pristine. Fountain pen nibs also employ iridium-tipped points, though modern nibs often substitute cheaper platinum-group alloys while retaining the traditional name.

The asteroid impact that ended the Cretaceous Period left iridium concentrations in sedimentary layers worldwide at levels thirty times higher than background. The physicist Luis Alvarez and his son Walter, a geologist, identified this anomaly in 1980 while studying rock strata near Gubbio, Italy. They proposed that an extraterrestrial impact had delivered the iridium and triggered the mass extinction. The hypothesis faced skepticism until the discovery of the Chicxulub crater a decade later provided physical evidence. The iridium layer now serves as a chronological marker, a thin dark line visible in cliff faces across continents, testament to a day when the sky filled with debris and the world changed in an instant.

Industrial applications remain limited by scarcity and price. The metal costs more per ounce than gold or platinum. Where extreme conditions demand materials that will not fail, iridium finds use. Spark plugs in high-performance engines employ iridium electrodes that resist erosion far better than conventional materials. Crucibles for growing synthetic crystals at extreme temperatures use iridium to contain melts that would attack any other metal. The meter bar that once defined length, like the kilogram prototype, was cast from platinum-iridium alloy for dimensional stability.

Iridium satellites, launched in the 1990s to provide global communications, took their name from the element because the original design called for 77 satellites matching iridium's atomic number. The final constellation used 66 satellites, which would have made it a dysprosium constellation, but the name stuck. When these satellites tumble, their flat antennae catch sunlight and produce bright flares visible from Earth, sometimes mistaken for UFOs. The metal itself plays no role in their construction.

The biological role of iridium is negligible. It appears in human tissue at concentrations measured in parts per trillion, likely from dietary intake of trace amounts in food grown in iridium-bearing soils. Some research has examined iridium compounds for cancer treatment, exploiting their ability to interact with DNA, but no approved medical applications exist. The element's rarity and inertness keep it removed from biological systems. In nature, iridium remains mostly locked in Earth's core, brought to the surface only by volcanic activity or cosmic delivery.
