# Americium

***Wartime Element, Peaceful Purpose***

> **Atomic Number:** 95 | **Symbol:** Am | **Category:** Actinide

Americium does not exist in nature. It is made by human hands, born in nuclear reactors from the transformation of plutonium. Discovered in 1944 at the University of Chicago as part of the Manhattan Project, it was the fourth transuranium element isolated, named for the Americas as europium was named for Europe. Most americium comes into being as a byproduct of plutonium production or the operation of nuclear reactors. It decays by emitting alpha particles, with a half-life of 432 years for its most common isotope, americium-241. This same isotope, produced by the ton as nuclear waste, found an unexpected domestic application. Nearly every smoke detector in the developed world contains a small amount of americium-241, making it the most widely distributed radioactive element in human environments.

The creation of americium required sustained neutron bombardment. Glenn Seaborg, Ralph James, Leon Morgan, and Albert Ghiorso synthesized it by irradiating plutonium-239 in a nuclear reactor. The plutonium absorbed neutrons and transformed through a series of nuclear reactions into americium-241. The team identified the new element by its distinctive radioactive signature. They completed the work in 1944 but could not publish until 1945, after the war's end lifted security restrictions.

The element's discovery extended the actinide series and confirmed theoretical predictions about the behavior of heavy elements. Americium sits between plutonium and curium in the periodic table, part of a family of elements that exist primarily because humans learned to manipulate atomic nuclei. In metallic form, it is silvery-white and tarnishes slowly in air. It exhibits multiple oxidation states, most commonly +3, mirroring the chemistry of the rare earth elements above it in the table.

Industrial-scale production of americium-241 occurs unintentionally. Every operating nuclear reactor converts some uranium fuel into plutonium-241, which decays with a 14-year half-life into americium-241. The world's stockpile of separated plutonium, much of it held in storage, continuously generates americium as it ages. Extracting this americium from spent nuclear fuel or aged plutonium requires complex chemical separation processes in heavily shielded facilities. The United States, Russia, and France maintain facilities capable of these operations.

The alpha particles that americium emits made it useful in ionization smoke detectors. Inside the detector, americium-241 ionizes the air in a small chamber, creating a measurable electric current. Smoke particles disrupt this current, triggering the alarm. The amount used is small, typically 0.9 micrograms, equivalent to about 37,000 becquerels of activity. The source is sealed behind gold foil and mounted in a metal chamber. Under normal conditions, virtually no radiation escapes the device. A person receives more radiation exposure from sleeping next to another human than from living with an americium smoke detector. Tens of millions of these devices enter homes each year.

This domestic presence creates a diffuse stream of low-level radioactive waste. When smoke detectors reach end of life, most enter landfills. The americium remains radioactive for millennia. Some jurisdictions mandate return programs, but compliance is low. The alpha radiation poses no environmental hazard unless the source is disturbed and the material becomes airborne. Inhalation or ingestion of americium particles presents serious health risks, as the alpha radiation damages tissue at close range and the element concentrates in bone and liver.

Beyond smoke detection, americium serves as a neutron source when mixed with beryllium. The alpha particles from americium decay strike beryllium nuclei, ejecting neutrons. These americium-beryllium sources appear in industrial thickness gauges, well-logging equipment, and some research instruments. The neutrons probe material composition without destroying the sample. Oil field service companies use such sources extensively to map underground geology and locate petroleum deposits. The sources must be licensed, tracked, and eventually recovered, though some are lost or abandoned in the field.

Research applications explore americium's potential as reactor fuel. Mixed with plutonium in certain fast reactor designs, americium could be transmuted into shorter-lived or stable isotopes, reducing the long-term radioactive burden of nuclear waste. France and Russia have conducted experiments with americium-bearing fuel. The chemistry is challenging and the economics uncertain. Most americium continues accumulating in storage pools and underground repositories, a byproduct awaiting a use that may never materialize at scale.

The element remains primarily a creature of weapons programs and their aftermath. Its existence testifies to the power humans gained in the 1940s to create elements that the universe had not provided. Unlike the naturally occurring elements, which arrived with Earth or formed in its crust, americium represents a new category: elements that persist only through continuous human activity. When the last reactor shuts down and the last weapons dismantled, the americium already created will endure for tens of thousands of years, slowly decaying in smoke detectors, waste repositories, and scattered locations known and unknown.
