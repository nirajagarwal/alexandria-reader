# Plutonium

***Forged by Humans, Not Stars***

> **Atomic Number:** 94 | **Symbol:** Pu | **Category:** Actinide

Plutonium does not exist in nature in meaningful quantities. The universe did not make it. Humans did, in 1940, by bombarding uranium with deuterons in a Berkeley cyclotron. Glenn Seaborg and his team created element 94, naming it for Pluto, the planet beyond Neptune, following the sequence that had given neptunium its name. Within five years, plutonium would destroy a city. Within decades, it would power spacecraft beyond the solar system. The element exists almost entirely because of human intention, and nearly every gram serves a purpose humanity chose: weapons, reactors, exploration. Its half-life of 24,000 years means that what we have made will outlast civilizations. Plutonium is both an achievement and a burden, a substance that reveals our capacity to reshape matter at the most fundamental level and our responsibility for what we create.

## The Manhattan Project

Seaborg's discovery arrived at a moment of urgency. By 1942, physicists understood that plutonium-239, like uranium-235, could sustain a chain reaction. Unlike uranium-235, it could be separated from uranium-238 by chemical means rather than the agonizingly difficult isotopic separation. The Manhattan Project built reactors at Hanford, Washington, to breed plutonium in uranium fuel. The reactors ran. The plutonium accumulated. Chemists extracted it from intensely radioactive fuel rods in remote concrete canyons. On July 16, 1945, the Trinity test in New Mexico proved the plutonium implosion design. Three weeks later, "Fat Man" destroyed Nagasaki. Six kilograms of plutonium released energy equivalent to 21,000 tons of TNT. The age of plutonium had arrived with a flash visible 200 miles away.

## Alpha Decay

Plutonium-239 decays by emitting alpha particles: helium nuclei, two protons and two neutrons, traveling at five percent the speed of light. The alpha particle cannot penetrate skin. A sheet of paper stops it. This makes plutonium relatively safe to handle in sealed containers. It makes plutonium extraordinarily dangerous to inhale or ingest. Once inside the body, alpha emitters deliver intense radiation to surrounding tissue. Plutonium concentrates in bone and liver. A microgram in the lungs can cause cancer. The element generates heat from its own decay, warmth you can feel through a container. Larger masses grow hot enough to glow. This thermal output powered the radioisotope thermoelectric generators that kept Voyager spacecraft alive as they crossed into interstellar space.

## The Breeder Promise

Plutonium offered a solution to uranium scarcity. Fast breeder reactors could produce more plutonium than they consumed, converting abundant uranium-238 into fissile plutonium-239. France, Russia, Japan, and others built commercial breeders. The United States operated the Experimental Breeder Reactor in Idaho, which in 1951 became the first reactor to generate electricity. The economics never aligned. Uranium remained cheap. Breeders proved complex and expensive. The plutonium fuel cycle raised proliferation concerns. Most breeder programs shut down. Russia's BN-800 reactor still operates. The promise of inexhaustible nuclear fuel receded, but the technical achievement remained: humanity had learned to breed its own nuclear material, to turn the inert into the fissile.

## Weapons Stockpiles

The United States produced 111 tons of weapons-grade plutonium during the Cold War. Russia produced more, though exact figures remain classified. Britain, France, and China built their own stockpiles. Weapons-grade plutonium is at least 93 percent plutonium-239, with minimal plutonium-240, which can cause pre-detonation. Each warhead requires several kilograms. At the Cold War's peak, global stockpiles contained enough plutonium for tens of thousands of weapons. After the Soviet Union fell, the United States bought 500 tons of Russian highly enriched uranium, blending it down for reactor fuel. Plutonium proved harder to dispose of. Mixed oxide fuel programs converted some to reactor fuel. Most remains in storage, monitored, guarded, waiting for decisions about what to do with a substance that remains dangerous for millennia.

## Space Exploration

When sunlight grows too dim for solar panels, plutonium provides power. Radioisotope thermoelectric generators convert the heat of plutonium-238 decay into electricity. The isotope emits no significant neutron or gamma radiation, only alpha particles easily shielded. RTGs powered the Apollo lunar surface experiments, the Viking Mars landers, the Voyager probes, Cassini, Curiosity, Perseverance. New Horizons carried plutonium past Pluto, the planet for which the element was named. The United States produced plutonium-238 as a byproduct of weapons production until 1988. Stocks dwindled. NASA rationed the supply. In 2015, Oak Ridge National Laboratory resumed production, making new fuel for future missions. The element that destroyed one city now powers our eyes in the outer darkness.

## The Hanford Legacy

The Hanford Site in Washington State produced two-thirds of America's weapons plutonium. Nine reactors ran for decades, breeding plutonium in uranium fuel, discharging radioactive water into the Columbia River, storing waste in underground tanks. Production ceased in the 1980s. The cleanup began. It continues. Fifty-six million gallons of radioactive and chemical waste sit in 177 underground tanks, some leaking. The soil contains plutonium, strontium, cesium. The groundwater is contaminated. The Department of Energy has spent over $100 billion on cleanup. The work will continue for decades more. Hanford is the most contaminated site in the Western Hemisphere, a monument to the hidden costs of plutonium production, to the long tail of decisions made in haste during war.

## Critical Assembly

Plutonium metal is dense, silver-white when freshly cut, tarnishing quickly in air. It exists in six crystal structures at normal pressure, more than any other element. The phase transitions complicate metallurgy. A sphere of alpha-phase plutonium ten centimeters across contains enough mass to sustain a chain reaction: the critical mass, roughly 10 kilograms. Bring two subcritical masses together quickly enough and plutonium fissions, releasing neutrons that cause more fissions in a cascade. The physics are straightforward. The engineering is not. Implosion weapons use conventional explosives to compress a plutonium core to supercritical density. The margins are tight. The calculations are precise. The knowledge is restricted. The element exists at the intersection of physics and politics, science and secrecy.

## The Long Count

Plutonium-239's half-life of 24,110 years means that plutonium released in 1945 will not decay to half its original activity until the year 26,055. Plutonium from weapons testing, reactor accidents, and waste storage will remain hazardous deep into the future. The Waste Isolation Pilot Plant in New Mexico stores plutonium-contaminated waste in salt formations 2,000 feet underground, designed to isolate it for 10,000 years. Warning markers describe the danger in six languages and pictograms, attempting to communicate across a timespan longer than recorded history. The plutonium we have made is now part of Earth's inventory, a new element in the planetary composition, placed here by choice, our gift and our problem for the civilizations that follow.
