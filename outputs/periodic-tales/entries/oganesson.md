# Oganesson

***The Element That Barely Exists***

> **Atomic Number:** 118 | **Symbol:** Og | **Category:** Noble Gas

Oganesson occupies the final position in the seventh period of the periodic table, the heaviest element yet synthesized. Created atom by atom in particle accelerators, it exists for less than a millisecond before decaying. Only a handful of atoms have ever been produced. No one has observed its properties directly. Everything known about it comes from theory and inference. It may not behave as a noble gas at all, despite its position in that column. Relativistic effects at such extreme atomic numbers warp electron behavior, potentially making this "inert" gas reactive. The element bears the name of Yuri Oganessian, the physicist who spent decades pursuing superheavy nuclei at Russia's Joint Institute for Nuclear Research. Its discovery in 2002 and confirmation in 2006 marked the completion of the periodic table's seventh row, a boundary that may represent the practical limit of element synthesis.

The creation of oganesson required accelerating calcium-48 nuclei into a target of californium-249. The collision fused nuclei briefly, forming oganesson-294 before it split apart. After five months of continuous bombardment in 2002, three atoms appeared in the detector at Dubna, Russia. Each decayed in less than a millisecond through a cascade of alpha emissions. A 2005 experiment at Dubna produced a single additional atom. Independent confirmation came in 2015 when a team at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory detected five more atoms over 150 days of beam time. The isotope oganesson-294 has a half-life of approximately 0.7 milliseconds.

The physics underlying its synthesis pushes against fundamental constraints. Creating nuclei this heavy requires finding combinations where protons and neutrons achieve temporary stability despite the enormous electrostatic repulsion trying to tear them apart. Calcium-48 works as a projectile because its neutron-rich composition helps balance the neutron-to-proton ratio in the compound nucleus. Californium-249 provides the necessary target mass. Even so, the cross-section for fusion is measured in picobarns, meaning trillions of projectiles must strike the target to produce a single atom. The entire experimental apparatus—accelerator, target, detector—exists to witness events that occur perhaps once per week.

Theory predicts oganesson may violate the expectations its position suggests. Noble gases are inert because their outer electron shells are filled, providing no incentive for chemical bonding. But at atomic number 118, relativistic effects become extreme. Electrons in the innermost shells orbit so close to the nucleus they approach a significant fraction of light speed. This increases their mass and contracts their orbits, which in turn affects the behavior of outer electrons through screening effects. Calculations suggest oganesson's outermost electrons may not form a stable closed shell. The element might be a semiconductor rather than a gas at room temperature, and it could form chemical compounds more readily than xenon or radon.

No experiment has confirmed these predictions. The half-life is too short and the atom count too low for chemical studies. Even measuring basic properties like ionization energy or electron affinity remains beyond current capability. What exists is theoretical oganesson, a structure predicted by quantum mechanical calculations calibrated against lighter elements and extrapolated into a regime where those models become uncertain. The element serves as a test case for theoretical chemistry at the limits of the periodic table, a place where familiar patterns may break down entirely.

The naming followed decades of work by Yuri Oganessian and his collaborators at Dubna. Between 1965 and 2006, teams under his direction discovered five superheavy elements, more than any other research group. The name oganesson, approved by IUPAC in 2016, makes Oganessian the second person honored with an element name during their lifetime, after Glenn Seaborg. The choice recognizes not just the discovery of element 118 but a research program that mapped the upper reaches of the periodic table. Dubna's experiments, often conducted in collaboration with American national laboratories, extended nuclear physics into regions where theoretical predictions about nuclear stability remained uncertain.

Whether elements significantly heavier than oganesson can exist remains an open question. Theory suggests an "island of stability" around atomic numbers 120 to 126, where certain neutron-rich isotopes might persist for minutes or longer rather than microseconds. Reaching this island would require synthesizing elements 119 and 120 first, using even more exotic projectile-target combinations. Experiments searching for element 119 have been underway at Dubna, RIKEN in Japan, and GSI in Germany for years without success. The cross-sections may be too small, the beam intensities insufficient, or the island may not exist as predicted. Oganesson may represent not just the end of the seventh period but the practical boundary of the periodic table itself.
