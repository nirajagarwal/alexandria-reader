# Silicon

***The Valley's Namesake***

> **Atomic Number:** 14 | **Symbol:** Si | **Category:** Metalloid

Silicon forms approximately 28 percent of Earth's crust by mass. Only oxygen exceeds it. The element does not exist free in nature. It binds tightly to oxygen, forming silica and silicates, the minerals that compose most rocks, sand, and clay. Silicon crystallizes in the same structure as diamond. Pure silicon is a gray solid with metallic luster. It conducts electricity better than insulators but worse than metals, a property that made the modern world possible. Jöns Jakob Berzelius isolated silicon in 1824, heating potassium metal with silicon tetrafluoride. He named it for *silex*, the Latin word for flint. For a century silicon remained a laboratory curiosity. Then came the transistor. Silicon's semiconducting properties, its abundance, and the ease of growing pure crystals converged. Every integrated circuit, every microprocessor, every digital device rests on silicon's peculiar relationship with electrons. The element gave its name to a valley, and the valley changed civilization.

## Stone and Sand

Quartz is silicon dioxide. Feldspar, mica, and most clay minerals are silicon compounds. Granite contains roughly 70 percent silica. Sandstone is nearly pure silicon dioxide, grains of quartz eroded from older rocks. Beach sand consists mostly of quartz, with local additions. The deserts of the world are silicon repositories. Obsidian, volcanic glass, forms when lava rich in silica cools too quickly to crystallize. Silicon chemistry shapes landscapes. When silicate rocks weather, silicon washes to the sea, where diatoms and other organisms extract it to build shells. These shells accumulate on the ocean floor, forming deposits that lithify into chert and diatomite. The silicon cycle moves the element from rock to water to organism to sediment to rock again, a loop operating across geological time.

## The Diatom's Armor

Diatoms construct intricate shells from hydrated silicon dioxide. These microscopic algae extract dissolved silica from water, polymerizing it into complex geometrical structures, each species with its characteristic pattern. The shells, called frustules, fit together like tiny boxes with lids. When diatoms die, their silica shells drift to the seafloor. Over time, vast deposits accumulate. Diatomaceous earth, mined from ancient seabeds, consists of countless fossilized frustules. Industry uses it as a filtration medium, an abrasive, and an insulating material. Other organisms employ silicon: horsetails, certain sponges, some grasses. Rice plants deposit silica in their stems and leaves, strengthening them. The human requirement for silicon remains unclear. Some evidence suggests it aids bone formation and connective tissue health, but no deficiency disease has been definitively established. Dietary silicon comes mainly from grains, vegetables, and drinking water.

## Fire and Furnace

Producing pure silicon requires extremes. The industrial process begins with quartzite, nearly pure silicon dioxide. In an electric arc furnace, carbon reduces the silica to silicon at temperatures exceeding 1,900 degrees Celsius. The product, metallurgical-grade silicon, is 98 to 99 percent pure. For electronics, this is insufficient. The semiconductor industry demands silicon purer than any other industrial material, with fewer than one foreign atom per billion silicon atoms. Achieving this requires further refinement. Silicon reacts with hydrochloric acid to form trichlorosilane, a liquid that can be distilled to extreme purity. Heating the purified trichlorosilane causes it to decompose, depositing silicon. The Czochralski process grows single crystals. A seed crystal touches the surface of molten silicon. As the seed withdraws, silicon crystallizes onto it, forming a cylindrical ingot. The ingot, sliced into thin wafers, becomes the substrate for integrated circuits.

## The Transistor's Foundation

Silicon revolutionized electronics not through inherent superiority but through a convergence of favorable properties. Its energy bandgap suits room-temperature operation. Its oxide, silicon dioxide, forms naturally and is electrically insulating, chemically stable, and mechanically robust. This oxide enables the fabrication techniques that produce billions of transistors on a single chip. Germanium, used in early transistors, cannot form such an oxide. Silicon's abundance keeps costs manageable. The planar process, developed at Fairchild Semiconductor in 1959, allowed multiple transistors to be fabricated simultaneously on a silicon wafer. Photolithography patterns circuits onto the surface. Doping introduces precise amounts of phosphorus or boron, creating regions with excess or deficient electrons. These regions, arranged in specific geometries, control current flow. Moore's Law, predicting the doubling of transistors per chip every two years, held for decades. The dimensions of silicon transistors now approach atomic scales.

## Glass and Ceramics

Fused quartz, pure silicon dioxide melted and cooled, transmits ultraviolet light and withstands thermal shock. Telescopes, semiconductor equipment, and laboratory apparatus use fused quartz. Common glass is a silicate mixture: silicon dioxide with sodium and calcium oxides. Varying the proportions and adding other elements produces different properties. Borosilicate glass, containing boron, resists thermal expansion. Lead crystal contains lead oxide, increasing refractive index. Fiberglass consists of silicon dioxide fibers, used for insulation and reinforcement. Optical fiber, ultra-pure glass drawn into thin strands, carries light signals across continents and oceans. Total internal reflection confines light within the fiber. Data transmission through fiber optics relies on silicon dioxide's transparency at specific wavelengths. Ceramic materials often contain silicon. Silicon carbide, formed by heating silicon dioxide and carbon, ranks among the hardest materials. It serves as an abrasive and a refractory.

## The Silicone Family

Silicones are synthetic polymers with silicon-oxygen backbones and organic side groups. Eugene Rochow developed the direct process for producing silicones in 1940, reacting silicon with methyl chloride. The resulting polymers resist heat, cold, water, and chemical attack. Silicone fluids lubricate. Silicone rubbers seal. Silicone resins coat and protect. Medical devices use silicones for their biocompatibility. Breast implants, catheters, and tubing employ silicone elastomers. Cosmetics and personal care products contain silicones for texture and water resistance. Construction relies on silicone sealants. The polymers' inertness makes them persistent in the environment. They degrade slowly, raising concerns about accumulation. Despite the name, silicones are not silicon. The term comes from their empirical formula, which once suggested a similarity to ketones. The chemistry is entirely different. Silicon's ability to form stable bonds with oxygen and carbon enables this vast family of materials.

## The Solar Harvest

Photovoltaic cells convert sunlight to electricity through the photoelectric effect. Most solar panels use crystalline silicon wafers. When photons strike the silicon, they excite electrons, creating current. The efficiency of conversion depends on silicon purity and crystal structure. Monocrystalline silicon, cut from single ingots, achieves the highest efficiency. Polycrystalline silicon, formed from multiple crystals, costs less to produce but converts less efficiently. Thin-film technologies use amorphous silicon or other materials, reducing material costs further. The solar industry consumes substantial silicon. Producing photovoltaic-grade silicon requires less purity than semiconductor-grade but still demands significant energy. The energy payback time, the duration a panel must operate to generate the energy used in its manufacture, has dropped to under two years. Silicon solar cells now provide a meaningful fraction of global electricity. Perovskite materials may eventually supplement or replace silicon, but silicon's dominance continues.
