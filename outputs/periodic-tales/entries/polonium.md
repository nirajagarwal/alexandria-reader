# Polonium

***Radioactive Rarity, Twice Discovered***

> **Atomic Number:** 84 | **Symbol:** Po | **Category:** Metalloid

Polonium exists almost nowhere. Created in minute quantities when uranium and thorium decay, it appears briefly before disintegrating. The most common isotope, polonium-210, survives 138 days. Natural concentrations remain so low that extracting a gram requires processing tons of uranium ore. This scarcity masks extraordinary properties: polonium emits alpha particles with such intensity that a speck glows blue in darkness and grows hot enough to boil water. Marie Curie isolated it in 1898 from pitchblende residue, naming it for her occupied homeland. The element she discovered would later kill her—chronic exposure to polonium and radium caused the aplastic anemia that ended her life in 1934. Today polonium appears in specialized instruments, nuclear batteries for spacecraft, and the occasional assassination. Its rarity and lethality have made it both indispensable for certain technologies and notorious as an untraceable poison.

---

Marie Curie identified polonium before radium, working through tons of pitchblende from Bohemian mines. The ore arrived as waste from uranium extraction. She and Pierre processed it in a converted shed, boiling and precipitating, following traces of radioactivity through chemical separation. Polonium revealed itself first, four months before radium, through its powerful emissions. The Curies never saw it—concentrations were too low, decay too rapid. They characterized it by what it did, not what it looked like.

Fifty years later, Manhattan Project scientists encountered polonium again. They needed a neutron source to initiate the plutonium bomb. Polonium-210 paired with beryllium released neutrons when alpha particles struck beryllium nuclei. Oak Ridge and Dayton produced it in quantity, though "quantity" meant grams, not kilograms. Workers called it "Po." Some died from it. The initiator they built, a device the size of a ping pong ball, contained enough polonium to kill thousands if dispersed. It instead triggered the first nuclear explosion.

The same properties that make polonium deadly make it useful. Alpha particles carry enormous energy but travel mere centimeters in air and stop at skin. A polonium source sealed in metal poses little external hazard. It heats itself without moving parts, converting radioactive decay directly to thermal energy. Soviet spacecraft carried polonium-210 batteries to keep instruments warm in the void. American lunar probes used them similarly. The half-life proved ideal—long enough for a mission, short enough to decay substantially within years.

Industry finds minor uses. Static eliminators employ tiny polonium sources to ionize air and discharge static electricity around sensitive equipment or film. These devices contain micrograms, just enough to strip electrons from nearby molecules. Photography relied on them before electronic alternatives emerged. Some manufacturers still use them where electronics might fail.

Biological systems exclude polonium completely. No organism uses it, incorporates it, or tolerates it. When inhaled or ingested, it concentrates in soft tissues and irradiates cells from within. The liver and kidneys accumulate it. Alpha particles, stopped by dead skin from outside, devastate living cells from inside. A microgram delivered internally can kill. Cigarette smoke contains traces from polonium in tobacco—plants absorb it from soil and fertilizer. The dose remains small but measurable, contributing to smoking's cancer risk.

This internal lethality made polonium an assassination tool. Alexander Litvinenko, former Russian intelligence officer, died in London in 2006 after drinking tea laced with polonium-210. The autopsy revealed massive contamination. Investigators traced polonium to meetings, airplanes, hotel rooms. The amount required—less than a microgram—left radioactive traces everywhere the poisoners went. The isotope's rarity pointed to state-level production. Russia operates the only facilities that produce polonium-210 in requisite quantities, though it denied involvement. Litvinenko took three weeks to die, organs failing as alpha radiation destroyed tissue throughout his body.

Extracting polonium requires nuclear reactors or particle accelerators. Bismuth-209, bombarded with neutrons, transmutes to polonium-210. The process yields milligrams from months of irradiation. The United States ceased production decades ago. Russia maintains capacity, supplying polonium for spacecraft batteries and research. No commercial market exists. The amounts needed for legitimate use remain microscopic. A global total of perhaps a hundred grams exists at any moment, scattered across laboratories and devices.

The element vanishes as it forms. Natural polonium in uranium ore decays faster than geological processes create it. Human-made polonium disappears within years. Nothing accumulates. Nothing persists. Polonium exists in the present tense only, arriving and departing, leaving radiation and heat as evidence it was ever there.
