# Polonium

***Radiation's Invisible Killer***

> **Atomic Number:** 84 | **Symbol:** Po | **Category:** Metalloid

Polonium exists as pure radioactivity given physical form. Every isotope decays, most within minutes or days, releasing alpha particles with such intensity that a speck glows blue in darkness from ionized air. The longest-lived isotope, polonium-210, persists for 138 days before surrendering half its atoms to decay. A single gram generates 140 watts of heat. The element appears in uranium ores at concentrations measured in parts per trillion, the final stop before lead in one decay chain. Its scarcity matches its lethality. One microgram constitutes a fatal dose. Marie Curie isolated it in 1898 from pitchblende residue, naming it for her homeland. She handled it with bare hands, measuring its properties while it measured out her life in accumulated dose. Polonium finds limited application, each use exploiting its fierce radioactivity: static eliminators in industry, heat sources in spacecraft, neutron triggers in early nuclear weapons. It kills efficiently, leaving little trace, a fact demonstrated in several high-profile poisonings. The element has no stable existence, only a brief passage from heavier elements to lead, releasing invisible energy that tears through living tissue.

## Pitchblende Mountains

Marie Curie processed tons of uranium ore residue to isolate polonium. After uranium extraction, the remaining material showed radioactivity stronger than uranium itself. She and Pierre Curie worked through eight tons of pitchblende in a converted shed, boiling, crystallizing, repeating. Four years yielded one-tenth of a gram. The element announced itself through its rays before revealing its physical form. Curie measured radioactivity 400 times greater than uranium in her fractions, tracking an invisible substance by its invisible emissions. She published the discovery in July 1898, six months before isolating radium from the same ore. The name honored Poland, partitioned and erased from maps, Curie's assertion of a homeland that officially did not exist. The element proved too unstable for conventional characterization. Its atomic weight remained uncertain for years. Every sample decayed as she studied it, heat from its disintegration warming the laboratory air.

## Alpha Heat

Polonium-210 releases alpha particles with 5.3 million electron volts of energy. These particles travel less than four centimeters in air, stopped by paper, blocked by skin. They pose minimal external threat. Internally, they destroy. Each alpha strike damages nearby cells, the particle's mass and charge concentrating energy into a microscopic radius. One microgram delivers a fatal dose, though death arrives slowly, weeks after exposure. The body absorbs polonium readily, mistaking it chemically for tellurium or bismuth. It distributes to organs, concentrating in liver, kidneys, bone marrow, spleen. There it decays, alpha particles ravaging cells at point-blank range. Red blood cells die. Immune function collapses. Organs fail in sequence. No treatment reverses this internal bombardment. The element generates heat sufficient to warm itself above surrounding temperature. Samples glow blue-white in darkness from air ionization. NASA used this property in radioisotope thermoelectric generators, polonium-210 heating thermocouples to power satellites and lunar experiments.

## Urchin Triggers

The first nuclear weapons carried polonium at their core. The Fat Man device dropped on Nagasaki contained an "urchin initiator": a polonium-210 sphere coated with beryllium, compressed by conventional explosives at the moment of detonation. The compression mixed polonium and beryllium, alpha particles from polonium striking beryllium nuclei to release neutrons. These neutrons initiated the plutonium chain reaction. Polonium's 138-day half-life created operational pressure. Each initiator lost effectiveness as it aged, decaying toward insufficient neutron flux. Manhattan Project scientists synthesized polonium-210 by neutron bombardment of bismuth-209 in reactors. Dayton, Ohio hosted a secret facility where hundreds of workers processed polonium, many contaminated during production. The Mound Laboratory operated until 1988, leaving radioactive legacy requiring decades of cleanup. Later weapon designs abandoned polonium initiators for systems using tritium and deuterium. The element's brief half-life made it unsuitable for arsenals meant to persist for years.

## Litvinenko's Tea

Alexander Litvinenko drank tea laced with polonium-210 in a London hotel on November 1, 2006. The former Russian security officer fell ill within hours. His hair fell out. Blood counts plummeted. Organs failed. He died three weeks later, body devastated by internal radiation. Investigators found polonium traces in the teapot, in Litvinenko's urine, along the trail his killers traveled through London and back to Moscow. The choice of poison suggested state sponsorship. Polonium-210 requires reactor production, available only to nations with nuclear programs. Russia's Avangard facility produced polonium for civilian purposes, including sale to other nations. The isotope's alpha emissions pass undetected through conventional radiation monitors. Only specialized equipment registers its presence. Litvinenko's case revealed it post-mortem, doctors initially baffled by symptoms resembling radiation sickness without external source. The assassination demonstrated polonium's properties: lethal in minute quantities, difficult to detect, requiring sophisticated production. A message delivered through physics.

## Industrial Static

Static electricity disrupts manufacturing. Film processing, paper production, electronics assembly all suffer from unwanted charge accumulation. Polonium-210 strips away static through ionization. A polonium source emits alpha particles that ionize surrounding air molecules, creating free electrons and positive ions. These charges neutralize static buildup on moving materials. Devices called static eliminators house polonium in sealed containers with thin windows, allowing alpha particles to escape while containing the source. Industrial versions operate in printing plants and textile mills. The sources require periodic replacement as polonium decays. Handling regulations mandate precautions. A breached source disperses radioactive material. Alternative technologies using high voltage or radioactive americium have largely displaced polonium in new installations. Existing polonium sources remain in service, regulated as radioactive materials requiring licensing. The element's unique decay characteristics once made it preferred for this application. Its hazards now make it obsolete.

## Invisible Contamination

Polonium-210 enters the environment through uranium decay chains. Tobacco plants absorb it from soil and fertilizer, concentrating the isotope in leaves. Cigarette smoke delivers polonium to lung tissue, alpha radiation contributing to cancer risk beyond that from chemical carcinogens. A pack-a-day smoker accumulates radiation dose equivalent to several chest X-rays annually. The polonium remains in lungs, decaying slowly. Seafood contains trace polonium absorbed from water. Arctic regions show elevated levels from atmospheric weapons testing decades past, the element cycling through food chains. Industrial facilities using polonium require monitoring for contamination. Mound Laboratory cleanup revealed widespread polonium deposition in buildings and soil, requiring removal and disposal as radioactive waste. Workers who ingested or inhaled polonium during production developed cancers years later. The element's alpha emissions create detection challenges. Swipe tests using scintillation counters identify surface contamination. Whole-body counting detects internal deposition. Each measurement technique requires specialized equipment, polonium's radiation signature distinct from other common radioisotopes.

## Thermoelectric Space

Spacecraft traveling beyond Mars orbit require power independent of solar panels. Radioisotope thermoelectric generators convert decay heat to electricity. Early designs used polonium-210, its high power density generating substantial heat from small mass. The Soviet Union flew polonium generators on Luna landers and Lunokhod rovers in the 1970s. American space programs favored plutonium-238, with its 88-year half-life providing decades of operation. Polonium's 138-day half-life limited mission duration, the heat source decaying to half power before spacecraft reached distant targets. The isotope's intense alpha emission also created shielding challenges. Engineers balanced power output against radiation protection mass. Polonium generators operated in vacuum, preventing air ionization and heat loss. The technology demonstrated feasibility but proved impractical for long missions. Modern radioisotope generators use plutonium-238 or americium-241. Polonium remains a theoretical option for brief, high-power applications. None currently fly. The element's contribution to space exploration ended with Soviet lunar programs.

## Chain Terminus

Uranium-238 decays through eighteen steps spanning 4.5 billion years, polonium-210 appearing near the end. The chain proceeds: uranium to thorium to radium, each step releasing alpha or beta radiation, transmuting elements. Polonium-210 emerges from lead-210 through two beta decays. It decays to stable lead-206, the chain's final product. Natural polonium exists wherever uranium persists: granite, phosphate rock, uranium ore. Its concentration remains vanishingly small, equilibrium between production and decay yielding steady-state amounts. This natural occurrence provides no practical source. Industrial polonium requires artificial production, bismuth-209 bombarded with neutrons in nuclear reactors. The bismuth absorbs a neutron, becoming bismuth-210, which beta decays to polonium-210. Target irradiation lasts months. Chemical separation follows, isolating polonium from bismuth matrix. The product begins decaying immediately, half-life clock started. All polonium handled today originated in reactors. None remains from Earth's formation. The element exists only in transient amounts, continuously created, continuously destroyed.
