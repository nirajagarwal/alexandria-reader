# Uranium

***The Element That Split the Atom***

> **Atomic Number:** 92 | **Symbol:** U | **Category:** Actinide

Uranium formed in the death throes of massive stars, forged when neutron star collisions scattered heavy elements into space. It concentrates in Earth's crust at about two parts per million, more abundant than gold or silver, typically bound in oxide minerals like uraninite. The element exists as three isotopes in nature: uranium-238 comprises 99.3 percent, uranium-235 makes up 0.7 percent, and uranium-234 appears in traces. All three are radioactive, decaying over timescales measured in hundreds of millions or billions of years. Before 1938, uranium was a curiosity used to color glass yellow and glaze ceramics orange. After Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann split its atom, uranium became the most politically consequential element on Earth. The rare isotope U-235, when struck by a neutron, undergoes fission, releasing energy and more neutrons that can sustain a chain reaction. This property enabled both nuclear weapons and nuclear power. Uranium now fuels roughly ten percent of global electricity generation and defines geopolitical relationships between nations.

## Yellow Glass and Ceramic Glaze

Martin Klaproth isolated uranium oxide from pitchblende ore in 1789, eight years after William Herschel discovered the planet Uranus. He named the new element in honor of that discovery. For the next 150 years, uranium served decorative purposes. Glassmakers added uranium compounds to produce a brilliant yellow-green glass that fluoresced under ultraviolet light. Ceramic manufacturers used uranium oxide to create bright orange and red glazes, particularly on Fiestaware dishes popular in American homes. These applications consumed small quantities. The element remained a minor industrial material, radioactive but not dangerous in the dilute forms used in consumer goods. Museums now house collections of uranium glass that still glow faintly under UV lamps. The last uranium-glazed Fiestaware rolled off production lines in 1972.

## The Discovery of Fission

In December 1938, Hahn and Strassmann bombarded uranium with neutrons in their Berlin laboratory. Chemical analysis revealed barium among the products, an element with roughly half the atomic mass of uranium. The atom had split. Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch, working in exile, provided the theoretical explanation in January 1939. When a neutron strikes a U-235 nucleus, the nucleus becomes unstable and breaks apart, releasing energy according to Einstein's mass-energy equivalence. The discovery reached Allied and Axis scientists simultaneously. Both sides recognized the implications. A sustained fission chain reaction could release energy on an unprecedented scale. The race to build an atomic weapon began within months. Physics crossed into history, and uranium became the material around which global strategy would revolve for the next eight decades.

## The Manhattan Project

The United States assembled scientists at Los Alamos to design an atomic bomb. The challenge was isolating U-235 from the vastly more abundant U-238. The isotopes are chemically identical. Separation required exploiting the slight mass difference. Engineers built gaseous diffusion plants at Oak Ridge, Tennessee, where uranium hexafluoride gas passed through thousands of porous barriers. The lighter U-235 molecules moved slightly faster, gradually concentrating. The process consumed enormous amounts of electricity and industrial capacity. By 1945, enough weapons-grade uranium had accumulated for one bomb. "Little Boy" contained 64 kilograms of uranium enriched to 80 percent U-235. It detonated over Hiroshima on August 6, 1945, releasing energy equivalent to 15,000 tons of TNT. The weapon killed an estimated 70,000 people immediately and tens of thousands more from radiation effects. Uranium had demonstrated its capacity for destruction.

## Nuclear Reactors

Enrico Fermi achieved the first controlled chain reaction in December 1942 beneath the stands of Stagg Field at the University of Chicago. His reactor used natural uranium and graphite blocks arranged in a precise geometry. Control rods made of neutron-absorbing cadmium regulated the reaction. The pile generated half a watt of power and proved that sustained, controlled fission was possible. Modern nuclear reactors operate on the same principle but use uranium enriched to 3-5 percent U-235. Water serves as both coolant and neutron moderator. The fission process heats water to produce steam that drives turbines. A 1,000-megawatt reactor consumes approximately 27 tons of enriched uranium annually and produces about 27 tons of spent fuel containing fission products and newly created elements like plutonium. As of 2024, roughly 440 nuclear reactors operate in 32 countries, generating about 10 percent of global electricity with no direct carbon emissions.

## Mining and Enrichment

Kazakhstan produces 43 percent of the world's uranium, followed by Canada and Australia. Most modern extraction uses in-situ leaching: operators inject solutions into underground ore bodies and pump out uranium-bearing liquid. The uranium concentrates into yellowcake, a uranium oxide powder, at processing facilities. Conversion plants turn yellowcake into uranium hexafluoride gas. Enrichment facilities, using centrifuges that spin at 100,000 rotations per minute, separate U-235 from U-238. Natural uranium becomes two streams: enriched uranium for reactors and depleted uranium containing mostly U-238. Depleted uranium, 40 percent less radioactive than natural uranium, finds use in armor-piercing ammunition and radiation shielding. Global uranium production amounts to about 47,000 tons annually. Identified resources total roughly 6 million tons, enough for current reactor designs for at least a century. Seawater contains an estimated 4 billion tons, though extraction remains uneconomical.

## Radiation and Health

Uranium emits alpha particles, helium nuclei that travel only a few centimeters in air and cannot penetrate skin. The primary hazard comes from inhalation or ingestion, which places radioactive material inside the body where alpha particles can damage cells directly. Uranium also acts as a toxic heavy metal, affecting kidney function. Miners working underground face elevated risks of lung cancer from radon gas, a radioactive decay product of uranium that accumulates in enclosed spaces. The danger scales with concentration and exposure duration. Natural uranium in ore form presents minimal risk with ordinary handling. Enriched uranium requires controlled environments. Spent fuel from reactors contains highly radioactive fission products and must be stored in water pools for years, then in dry casks for decades or centuries. No permanent disposal site for high-level nuclear waste operates anywhere in the world. The material remains hazardous for timescales that exceed the span of human civilizations.

## Nuclear Weapons Proliferation

Nine nations possess nuclear weapons: the United States, Russia, United Kingdom, France, China, India, Pakistan, Israel, and North Korea. The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, in force since 1970, attempts to prevent additional countries from acquiring the capability. The treaty's effectiveness depends on monitoring enrichment activities. Uranium enriched to 20 percent qualifies as highly enriched. At 90 percent, it becomes weapons-grade. A nation that masters uranium enrichment for reactor fuel can, with further processing, produce weapons material. Iran's nuclear program has repeatedly approached this threshold. The difference between civilian and military nuclear capability is technical, not fundamental. Every reactor-operating nation stands months or years from a weapons program if it chooses that path. This proximity creates persistent international tension. The International Atomic Energy Agency inspects facilities and verifies that civilian uranium remains civilian. The system relies on transparency and cooperation. It has prevented some proliferation but not all.

## Thorium and Future Reactors

Uranium-based reactors represent one nuclear path, not the only one. Thorium-232, three times more abundant than uranium, can sustain a fission chain reaction with different reactor designs. Thorium reactors would produce less long-lived waste and present greater proliferation resistance. Research programs in China and India are developing thorium technology, but no thorium reactor operates commercially. Fast breeder reactors can convert non-fissile U-238 into fissile plutonium-239, effectively multiplying fuel supplies by a factor of sixty. France operated the Superphénix breeder reactor from 1985 to 1998 before technical and political challenges forced closure. Small modular reactors, factory-built units of 300 megawatts or less, may reduce construction costs and improve safety. Fusion power, which requires no uranium, remains decades from commercial viability. For the foreseeable future, existing uranium-fueled light water reactors will continue to dominate nuclear energy production, carrying the tension between carbon-free electricity and radioactive waste into the next century.
