# Uranium

***The Element That Split History***

> **Atomic Number:** 92 | **Symbol:** U | **Category:** Actinide

Uranium formed in the violent collapse of dying stars, scattered through space, condensed into Earth's crust at roughly two parts per million. For four billion years it decayed, each atom counting down through a chain of daughter isotopes, releasing heat that keeps the planet's core molten. The element exists primarily as uranium-238, stable enough to persist since Earth's formation, with a smaller fraction of uranium-235, the isotope that would change everything. Miners extracted it as an ore coloring agent before anyone understood its nature. Martin Klaproth isolated it in 1789, naming it for the newly discovered planet Uranus, unaware he held the heaviest naturally occurring element. For a century and a half it remained a curiosity, a dense metal used to tint glass yellow and glaze ceramics orange. Then in 1938, Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann split its nucleus, and uranium became the hinge on which the twentieth century turned.

The fission of uranium-235 releases more neutrons than it consumes. One split atom triggers two more, those four trigger eight. The reaction multiplies exponentially if enough uranium concentrates in one place, if the geometry is right, if neutrons are slowed to the correct speed. Natural uranium contains only 0.7 percent of the fissile isotope, the rest uranium-238, nearly identical in chemistry but useless for chain reactions. Enrichment separates them, exploiting the tiny mass difference through thousands of repetitions in cascading centrifuges or gaseous diffusion plants. At 3 to 5 percent uranium-235, the fuel runs nuclear reactors. At 90 percent or higher, it makes weapons.

The first controlled chain reaction occurred beneath the stands of Staples Field in Chicago on December 2, 1942. Enrico Fermi's pile of graphite blocks and uranium achieved criticality for twenty-eight minutes, proving that sustained fission was possible, that the energy binding atomic nuclei could be systematically released. Three years later, a uranium bomb destroyed Hiroshima. The blast came from roughly sixty kilograms of uranium-235, though only about one kilogram actually fissioned before the assembly blew itself apart. That single kilogram converted mass to energy according to Einstein's equation, releasing force equivalent to fifteen thousand tons of TNT.

The same physics that enables weapons also generates electricity. Commercial reactors hold the chain reaction just below the threshold of exponential growth, extracting heat to drive turbines. A single uranium pellet, the size of a fingertip, contains energy equivalent to a ton of coal. France derives 70 percent of its electricity from uranium. The United States operates nearly one hundred reactors. Global uranium mining extracts roughly fifty thousand tons annually, primarily from Kazakhstan, Canada, and Australia. The ore grades vary. High-grade deposits hold several percent uranium oxide. Most commercial mines work ore containing a fraction of a percent, grinding millions of tons of rock to extract the element.

Uranium's radioactivity makes it persistently toxic. Alpha particles from decay cannot penetrate skin but devastate tissue if inhaled or ingested. The element concentrates in bone, substituting for calcium, irradiating marrow for years. Depleted uranium, the uranium-238 left after enrichment, sees military use in armor-piercing projectiles. Its density, 1.7 times that of lead, and its tendency to self-sharpen as it penetrates make it effective against tanks. The munitions leave contamination. Studies from Iraq and the Balkans document elevated uranium in soil and water near strike sites, though linking this definitively to health effects remains contested.

Spent reactor fuel contains uranium-235 not yet consumed, along with plutonium-239 bred from uranium-238 during operation, and a spectrum of fission products. Some countries reprocess this fuel, chemically separating the remaining usable isotopes from waste. Others treat the entire assemblage as waste, destined for geological repositories that must contain it for hundreds of thousands of years, longer than human civilization has existed. Finland's Onkalo facility tunnels into bedrock four hundred meters deep, designed to hold spent fuel beyond the reach of future ice ages and whatever societies might follow ours.

The element's concentration determines its character. Dispersed in granite at a few parts per million, uranium is simply part of the crust, decaying quietly, contributing background radiation. Refined to weapons grade and assembled into critical mass, it becomes a force that can erase cities. Between these extremes lies nuclear power, humanity's most concentrated energy source and its most intractable waste problem, both consequences of the same nuclear property. Every path forward requires managing uranium: as fuel, as waste, as proliferation risk. The element that split history continues to divide opinion on what comes next.
