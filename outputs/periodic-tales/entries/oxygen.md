# Oxygen

***The Breath Between Stars and Cells***

> **Atomic Number:** 8 | **Symbol:** O | **Category:** Nonmetal

Oxygen forms in the cores of massive stars through helium fusion, each nucleus built from the collision of helium atoms under temperatures exceeding 100 million degrees. When these stars die, they scatter oxygen across space. It constitutes roughly 21 percent of Earth's atmosphere and nearly half the mass of the planet's crust, bound into silicates and oxides. Most terrestrial oxygen exists locked in rock. The free gas that animals breathe appeared only after photosynthetic organisms spent two billion years splitting water molecules and releasing oxygen as waste. This atmospheric oxygen makes possible all animal life, yet the element itself is not gentle. It corrodes, combusts, and oxidizes. Every breath sustains and every breath damages. Oxygen enables the metabolism that powers complex life while simultaneously producing the free radicals that age cells. Industry consumes it for steel production, chemical synthesis, and medical support. Space programs must carry it. Deep-sea divers breathe altered mixtures. The element that built the modern atmosphere now requires careful management in contexts where humans venture beyond breathable air.

---

Joseph Priestley isolated oxygen in 1774 by heating mercuric oxide, though Carl Wilhelm Scheele had performed similar experiments earlier. Neither understood what they had found. Antoine Lavoisier named it in 1777, incorrectly believing it essential to all acids—the name means "acid-former" in Greek. He recognized its role in combustion, dismantling the phlogiston theory that had dominated chemistry. The discovery reordered scientific understanding of burning, breathing, and rusting, revealing these as variations of the same chemical process.

The element exists primarily as O₂, a diatomic molecule, though a small fraction forms ozone—O₃—in the upper atmosphere. Ozone absorbs ultraviolet radiation that would otherwise sterilize Earth's surface. In the 1980s, chlorofluorocarbons were discovered to be destroying this shield. The resulting Montreal Protocol marked one of the few successful global responses to an environmental threat. The ozone layer has since begun recovering, though the process will take decades.

Oxygen's reactivity makes it central to energy production in living systems. Cellular respiration burns glucose with oxygen, extracting energy by controlled oxidation. The process generates water and carbon dioxide. Mitochondria, the organelles that perform this work, likely originated as free-living bacteria that formed a symbiotic relationship with early complex cells. Every animal depends on this ancient partnership. Without oxygen, only small, sluggish organisms could exist. The Cambrian explosion of complex life forms 540 million years ago coincided with rising atmospheric oxygen levels.

Industrially, oxygen enables processes requiring intense heat. Steel furnaces inject pure oxygen to burn away carbon and impurities. Rocket engines combine liquid oxygen with fuel to produce thrust in the vacuum of space. The element constitutes 89 percent of water by mass, and industrial electrolysis splits water to produce both oxygen and hydrogen. Chemical plants use oxygen for synthesizing everything from plastics to explosives. The Haber-Bosch process, which feeds billions by fixing nitrogen into fertilizer, requires oxygen to burn methane and generate the heat and hydrogen the reaction demands.

Medical oxygen supports patients whose lungs cannot extract sufficient gas from air. Premature infants, trauma victims, and those with respiratory disease depend on concentrated oxygen. Yet high concentrations damage tissue. Oxygen toxicity causes seizures in divers breathing pure oxygen at depth. Premature infants given excessive oxygen suffer retinal damage. The concentration that sustains life sits within narrow bounds.

Oxygen marks the boundary between two Earths. The planet's first two billion years passed under an atmosphere of nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and methane. Cyanobacteria began photosynthesizing, splitting water and releasing oxygen. For hundreds of millions of years, this oxygen reacted with dissolved iron in the oceans, precipitating out as rust and forming the banded iron formations that now supply much of the world's steel. Only after the oceans' iron was exhausted did oxygen begin accumulating in the atmosphere. This Great Oxidation Event, around 2.4 billion years ago, poisoned most existing life. The organisms that survived learned to harness oxygen's reactive power. Their descendants inherited the Earth.

Combustion remains humanity's primary energy source. Coal, oil, natural gas—all burn by combining with oxygen. The carbon dioxide produced now alters the atmosphere's composition faster than at any point in human history. The same oxygen that enabled complex life and industrial civilization now participates in the chemistry of climate change. Oxygen levels themselves remain stable; the atmosphere's composition shifts in other elements. Yet the balance that allows oxygen to remain free rather than locked in rock or water depends on the continued existence of the photosynthetic organisms that produce it. Forests, phytoplankton, and grasslands maintain the breath of the world.

The element sits at the center of every conversation about habitability. Exoplanet searches look for oxygen signatures in distant atmospheres. Its presence suggests either life or unusual geology. The choice between those interpretations depends on other factors—atmospheric composition, stellar radiation, planetary temperature. Oxygen alone does not prove biology, but its sustained presence in an atmosphere rich with gases it readily reacts with suggests something continuously replenishing the supply. On Earth, that something is life itself, the vast engine of photosynthesis that has run for billions of years, splitting water and exhaling the gas that animals breathe.
