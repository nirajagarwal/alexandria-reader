# Lanthanum

***The First of the Rare Earths***

> **Atomic Number:** 57 | **Symbol:** La | **Category:** Lanthanide

Lanthanum marks the beginning of the lanthanides, fifteen elements that cluster together in atomic structure and chemical behavior. Swedish chemist Carl Gustaf Mosander isolated it in 1839 from cerium nitrate, naming it from the Greek *lanthanein*, "to lie hidden." The metal proved difficult to separate from its neighbors, a characteristic of the entire series. Lanthanum belongs to the rare earth elements, though this designation misleads. The element is more abundant in Earth's crust than lead. Its importance lies not in scarcity but in the precision required to extract and refine it. Modern applications depend on compounds that alter the properties of glass, store hydrogen, and enable the chemistry of petroleum refinement.

The discovery followed a pattern common to the rare earths. Mosander, working with what he thought was pure cerium oxide, detected spectroscopic anomalies. He succeeded in separating a new oxide, lanthana. The work required repeated crystallizations, each step concentrating one element over another by slight differences in solubility. Complete separation of lanthanum from neighboring elements would not become routine until ion exchange methods emerged in the twentieth century.

Lanthanum's chemistry resembles that of calcium and the other rare earths. It oxidizes readily in air, tarnishing to a white oxide. Water reacts with it vigorously. The metal itself has limited direct use, but its compounds found early application in gas mantles. When heated, lanthanum oxide produces brilliant white light. These mantles, soaked in solutions of rare earth salts, glowed over camp stoves and street lamps before electric lighting displaced them.

The element's largest modern application lies in petroleum refining. Lanthanum-based catalysts crack heavy hydrocarbon molecules into lighter fractions, particularly gasoline. Refineries worldwide depend on fluid catalytic cracking units where finely powdered zeolites, their crystal structures doped with lanthanum, break carbon-carbon bonds at temperatures above five hundred degrees. The lanthanum stabilizes the zeolite structure against repeated heating cycles and improves selectivity for desired products.

Glass containing lanthanum oxide exhibits a high refractive index and low dispersion. Camera lenses, microscope objectives, and telescope optics incorporate these special glasses. The lanthanum increases the glass's ability to bend light while reducing chromatic aberration. A modern camera lens may contain several elements of lanthanum glass, each shaped and positioned to correct specific optical defects. The improvement is measurable in resolution tests and visible in photographs.

Lanthanum also stores hydrogen. The metal absorbs hydrogen gas to form lanthanum hydride, releasing it again when heated. Nickel-metal hydride batteries, which powered hybrid vehicles before lithium batteries displaced them, use an alloy based on lanthanum and nickel for the negative electrode. One kilogram of lanthanum alloy can reversibly store roughly twenty liters of hydrogen at atmospheric pressure. This property made practical the rechargeable batteries that bridged the gap between lead-acid cells and modern lithium systems.

The element concentrates in certain minerals, particularly monazite and bastnäsite. China dominates production, controlling most of the world's supply of separated rare earth elements. Processing these ores requires careful chemical separation. Environmental concerns follow the extraction. Monazite contains thorium, a mildly radioactive element that concentrates in processing residues. Sites where rare earths were separated decades ago still require monitoring.

Lanthanum compounds find use in medicine. Lanthanum carbonate binds phosphate in the digestive system. Patients with kidney disease, unable to regulate blood phosphate levels, take the compound to prevent phosphate absorption. The lanthanum passes through the body unabsorbed, carrying phosphate with it. This application exploits the element's tendency to form stable phosphate compounds at digestive pH levels. Thousands of patients depend on this chemistry to maintain mineral balance.
