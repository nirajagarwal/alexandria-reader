# The Marathon

At mile twenty, glycogen stores approach depletion and the body begins serious negotiations with its fat reserves. The conversation is inefficient. Fat burns slowly, requires more oxygen, produces less immediate energy than the now-exhausted carbohydrate pathways. Runners call this the wall. Physiologists call it substrate depletion. Either way, it feels like trying to sprint through deep water.

The marathon distance—26.2 miles, or 42.195 kilometers—wasn't chosen for its round numbers. It commemorates Pheidippides's run from Marathon to Athens in 490 BCE, though the historical Pheidippides likely ran from Athens to Sparta first, covering 140 miles in two days before his famous final sprint. The modern distance was standardized at the 1908 London Olympics to accommodate the royal family's viewing schedule, extending the race to finish at Windsor Castle. Arbitrary origins, but the distance found its own logic: it's almost exactly the point where human metabolism breaks down interestingly.

The body stores roughly 2,000 calories of glycogen—enough to run about twenty miles at marathon pace. After that, the aerobic system must increasingly rely on fat oxidation, which at racing speeds produces energy about 30% slower than carbohydrate metabolism. Elite marathoners run at about 85% of VO2 max, right at the edge where fat can contribute meaningfully to fuel demands. Any faster and the system can't sustain; any slower and you're not racing.

Training for a marathon is training the body to be more efficient in its desperation. Long runs teach muscles to store more glycogen, mitochondria to process fat more effectively, capillary networks to deliver oxygen more precisely. The metabolic adaptations are measurable: trained marathoners can oxidize fat at rates 30-40% higher than untrained runners. But even with perfect preparation, the math catches up. Eliud Kipchoge, who broke two hours for 26.2 miles in 2019, still had to manage fuel depletion. He consumed carbohydrate gel every five kilometers, maintaining blood glucose while his muscles burned through their reserves.

The mental wall tends to arrive with the physical one. Brain imaging studies show the prefrontal cortex—responsible for executive function and willpower—activating more intensely as glycogen depletes. The brain runs on glucose exclusively, and when blood sugar drops, cognitive function falters. Decision-making becomes harder. Pain perception intensifies. The urge to stop strengthens. What feels like mental weakness is partly neurological shortage.

Yet runners finish. They finish on heavy legs and compromised brains, on fat metabolism and force of will, on crowds and silence, on whatever remains when the easy fuel is gone. The marathon found the exact distance where the body's preferred systems fail and something else must take over. Not quite far enough to be impossible, but far enough that finishing changes you.
