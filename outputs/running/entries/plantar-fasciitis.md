# Plantar Fasciitis

The pain arrives in the morning. You swing your legs out of bed, put weight on your foot, and the bottom of your heel lights up—a sharp, stabbing sensation that makes you catch your breath. The first few steps are the worst. Then, strangely, it eases. You walk it off. By mid-run, you've almost forgotten it. Until tomorrow morning, when it returns.

Plantar fasciitis is inflammation of the plantar fascia, the thick band of connective tissue running along the sole of your foot from heel to toes. Think of it as a bowstring that supports your arch and absorbs shock with every footstrike. When you run, this tissue stretches and contracts thousands of times. In plantar fasciitis, microscopic tears develop in the fascia, typically where it attaches to the heel bone. The body tries to repair these tears, but the cycle of damage and attempted healing creates chronic inflammation.

The overnight pain has a specific cause: while you sleep, your foot relaxes into a plantarflexed position—toes pointing slightly downward. The plantar fascia shortens. When you stand, you force that contracted tissue to stretch suddenly, reopening the micro-tears. That first morning step is a daily re-injury.

Risk factors cluster around mechanics and load. Tight calves pull the heel upward, increasing tension on the plantar fascia. Weak intrinsic foot muscles—the small muscles in the foot itself—mean the fascia bears more impact load. High arches or flat feet alter the distribution of force. Rapid increases in mileage overwhelm the tissue's capacity to adapt. Hard surfaces compound the problem. Running shoes with inadequate support or excessive wear change how force transmits through the foot.

The injury tends to persist because runners continue running. Unlike a muscle strain that announces itself during activity, plantar fasciitis often feels manageable mid-run. The tissue warms up, inflammation temporarily decreases, endorphins dull the edge. You convince yourself it's improving. But every footstrike is still loading damaged tissue. The cumulative stress prevents healing.

Treatment requires addressing both inflammation and mechanics. Ice and anti-inflammatories reduce swelling. Rolling the foot on a frozen water bottle combines cold therapy with tissue mobilization. Stretching the plantar fascia and calves—particularly a wall stretch holding the affected foot back, knee straight—lengthens the tissue and reduces morning tearing. Strengthening the foot's intrinsic muscles through towel scrunches or marble pickups distributes load away from the fascia.

Night splints keep the foot in a neutral or slightly dorsiflexed position during sleep, preventing that painful morning shortening. Physical therapy can identify gait issues or muscle imbalances. In stubborn cases, custom orthotics redistribute pressure away from the inflamed attachment point.

The hardest part is backing off. Complete rest usually isn't necessary, but the training load must drop below the threshold that perpetuates injury. Swap runs for swimming or cycling. Shorten distances. Run on softer surfaces. The tissue needs sub-injury-threshold stress to rebuild—enough load to stimulate healing, not so much that you keep tearing it.

Most cases resolve in six to twelve months with conservative treatment. Some linger longer. The morning pain becomes a barometer: when you can walk those first steps without wincing, you're healing. When they're painless, you're almost there.
