# Courtney Dauwalter

Courtney Dauwalter runs through Death Valley in July wearing a dress, no watch, and a smile that suggests she's accessing something the rest of us can't reach. She wins the Moab 240—240 miles through Utah desert—by ten hours. Not ten minutes. Ten hours. At the Western States 100 in 2023, she finishes second overall, beating all but one man in a field of the world's best ultrarunners. Then she wins the Hardrock 100 three weeks later. Then the UTMB—106 miles through the Alps—four weeks after that. Three of ultrarunning's most prestigious races, each brutal enough to end seasons, in a single summer.

The most remarkable thing isn't the winning. It's how she does it.

While other elite ultrarunners monitor heart rate, pace, power output, Dauwalter runs by feel. She calls it "going to the pain cave"—a mental space she's learned to inhabit, even decorate. Where most runners encounter suffering and try to manage or minimize it, Dauwalter seems curious about it. When her quads are destroyed at mile 180, when her feet are blistered through, when her stomach won't hold food, she describes the experience with anthropological interest, as if observing someone else's body breaking down.

Research on pain perception shows individual variation in pain tolerance involves both physiological and psychological components. Some athletes have different configurations of pain receptors. Some produce more endogenous opioids. But what separates Dauwalter appears to be her relationship with suffering itself. Where pain typically signals threat—the brain's way of saying *stop*—Dauwalter has recalibrated that signal. The pain still registers. She just doesn't interpret it as a reason to quit.

Her training reflects this. She runs by feel rather than prescribed workouts, sometimes covering huge miles, sometimes taking weeks almost entirely off. She eats Cheetos and drinks beer. She started ultrarunning at 30, relatively late, without the typical build-up through shorter distances. None of this should work at the elite level. But ultrarunning isn't about optimizing a single system or perfecting one variable. It's about the interaction between all of them—cardiovascular capacity, muscular endurance, fueling strategy, heat tolerance, sleep deprivation, and the ability to keep moving when every system is screaming to stop.

That last variable might matter most, and it's where Dauwalter excels. The longer the race, the more dominant she becomes. In events over 200 miles, she doesn't just win the women's race—she competes for the overall victory. The gap between her and the field often *increases* as the distance extends, suggesting she accesses some reserve that deepens with duration.

What she's demonstrated is that ultrarunning success at the highest level isn't just about physical optimization. It's about the capacity to continue in states of extreme discomfort, to remain functional when the body is falling apart, to find something sustainable in the unsustainable. Dauwalter runs into the pain cave and makes it home.
