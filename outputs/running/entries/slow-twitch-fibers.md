# Slow-Twitch Fibers

The first mile feels easy because your slow-twitch muscle fibers are doing exactly what they were built for: contracting steadily, burning oxygen, producing minimal waste. These are the marathon fibers, the all-day workers, the engine of endurance. They're smaller than their fast-twitch counterparts, packed with mitochondria and myoglobin, threaded with dense capillary networks that deliver oxygen like a municipal water system. When physiologists biopsy the gastrocnemius of elite distance runners, they sometimes find slow-twitch percentages above 80%. The rest of us carry closer to 50-50, though genetics set the basic ratio before we ever lace up shoes.

Slow-twitch fibers—technically Type I fibers—generate force through aerobic metabolism. They pull ATP from fat and carbohydrate in the presence of oxygen, a process that's efficient but not particularly powerful. A single slow-twitch fiber produces about half the force of a fast-twitch fiber, contracts more slowly, and fatigues reluctantly. This is why distance runners rarely have the explosive acceleration of sprinters. But what slow-twitch fibers lack in power, they make up for in persistence. They can fire for hours without accumulating the lactate and hydrogen ions that eventually shut down fast-twitch fibers.

The color difference is visible under a microscope. Slow-twitch fibers are dark red, almost maroon, stained by myoglobin—the protein that stores oxygen in muscle tissue. Fast-twitch fibers are pale, nearly white. The color tells the functional story: slow-twitch fibers are oxygen-dependent, built for steady-state work. A cross-section of muscle tissue from a 10K specialist looks like a patchwork of dark and light. A cross-section from a 100-meter sprinter tilts heavily toward white.

Training can't fundamentally change fiber-type distribution—you can't convert slow-twitch to fast-twitch or vice versa, at least not in any meaningful percentage. But training does transform the fibers you have. Endurance training increases mitochondrial density in slow-twitch fibers, sometimes doubling the number of these cellular power plants. Capillary networks expand, improving oxygen delivery. The fibers become more efficient at fat oxidation, sparing glycogen for when you really need it.

Eliud Kipchoge's slow-twitch fibers likely differ from yours primarily in their density of mitochondria and capillaries, refined through decades of systematic training. The genetic baseline matters—his slow-twitch percentage was probably favorable from birth. But the adaptation is what makes him capable of sub-2-hour marathon pace, his slow-twitch fibers firing in synchrony, extracting oxygen from blood, converting fat and carbohydrate to motion with extraordinary economy. Mile after mile, the steady workers doing what they do best: contracting, recovering, contracting again, sustainable as tide.
