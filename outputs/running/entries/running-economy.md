# Running Economy

At the 1968 Olympics in Mexico City, Jim Ryun ran 1:44.3 for 800 meters and failed to medal. Four years later, Dave Wottle ran 1:45.9—a full second and a half slower—and won gold. The difference wasn't fitness. Wottle had simply figured out how to waste less energy at race pace.

Running economy measures the oxygen cost of maintaining a given speed. Two runners at identical VO2 max—the same engine capacity—can perform very differently based on how efficiently they convert oxygen into forward motion. One burns premium fuel, the other leaks energy with every stride. Economy is why some runners float along at 6:00 pace while others grind. It's the body's fuel efficiency rating.

The factors are biomechanical and metabolic. Ground contact time matters—the elite spend roughly 180 milliseconds on the ground per step at marathon pace, recreational runners closer to 250. Those extra milliseconds mean energy hemorrhaging into the pavement instead of propelling forward. Vertical oscillation matters too. Bounce six inches with each stride instead of four and you're lifting your body weight an extra mile over the course of a marathon. That's not fitness—it's physics.

Muscle fiber composition plays a role. Slow-twitch fibers generate force with less oxygen than fast-twitch. A runner with 80% slow-twitch in their legs has an efficiency advantage before they take a step. Tendon stiffness contributes as well—stiffer Achilles tendons store and release more elastic energy during the stretch-shortening cycle. Studies show that Kenyan runners, who dominate distance racing, have tendons that function like better springs.

But economy improves with training in ways VO2 max doesn't. After years of running, mitochondrial density increases, capillary networks expand, the heart's stroke volume rises—these changes make oxygen delivery more efficient at the cellular level. The body learns to recruit muscle fibers in better sequence, to maintain form under fatigue, to waste less energy on extraneous movement. Form gets cleaner. The engine gets the same size, but it sips fuel instead of guzzling it.

Runners obsess over VO2 max because it's measurable and impressive. But economy is what determines who wins when the fittest runners line up together. It's the reason a marathoner can run 26.2 miles at a pace that would destroy them after two miles a decade earlier, even though their max heart rate hasn't budged. The difference is learning to run without bleeding speed into wasted motion—every stride doing exactly what it needs to, nothing more.
