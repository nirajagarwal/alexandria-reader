**The Running Gait**

Your left foot leaves the ground. For forty milliseconds—less time than it takes to blink—you're airborne, both feet off the pavement. Then your right foot lands, absorbs roughly three times your body weight in a tenth of a second, and launches you forward again. This happens ninety times a minute if you're running easy, more if you're pushing. You don't think about any of it. But your body is executing one of the most complex motor patterns humans can perform.

The running gait breaks into two phases: stance and swing. Stance is when your foot contacts the ground—it lasts about 200 milliseconds at an easy pace, less at speed. This is where the physics happens. Your foot strikes, your leg compresses like a spring, your body passes over it, and you push off. The entire kinetic chain—ankle, knee, hip—coordinates to absorb impact and generate propulsion. Meanwhile, your other leg swings through the air in a calculated arc, preparing to land just ahead of your center of mass.

Researchers at Harvard mapped this pattern in extreme slow motion, filming runners at 1,000 frames per second. What they found: the gait cycle is remarkably consistent across runners, but the details—where you land on your foot, how much your knee flexes, the angle of your shin at contact—vary wildly. Some runners land on their heels, some on their midfoot, some on their forefoot. Elite Kenyan runners tend toward midfoot strikes with minimal ground contact time. Recreational runners show more variation. Both groups run efficiently within their own patterns.

The gait isn't fixed. It changes with speed. At a jog, you spend more time on the ground, your legs cycle slowly, your flight phase barely exists. As you accelerate, ground contact time drops, your knees drive higher, your stride opens up. Sprinters at full speed spend only sixty milliseconds on the ground per step—just long enough to redirect force. The gait adapts to terrain too. On trails, your stride shortens, your foot placement becomes more deliberate, your cadence might increase to maintain balance.

Daniel Lieberman, the evolutionary biologist, argues that humans evolved to run this way—persistence hunters on the African savanna, chasing prey to exhaustion over miles. The gait we use now is essentially unchanged from that ancient pattern. What has changed is the surface we run on, the shoes we wear, the speeds we attempt. But the fundamental choreography—stance, swing, flight, repeat—remains the same. Efficient, adaptable, unconscious. You don't learn to run so much as remember how.
