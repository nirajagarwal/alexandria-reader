# The Running Gait

Your foot hits the ground for maybe 200 milliseconds. In that fifth of a second, forces equal to two or three times your body weight surge through bones and tendons. The impact begins at the outer heel—for most runners—then the foot rolls inward as the arch compresses, spreading the load across the metatarsals before the big toe finally pushes off. Contact, load, propulsion. Twenty milliseconds to brake, eighty to support, a hundred to push. Then you're airborne again.

This is the running gait, and it happens so automatically that most runners never think about it. But biomechanists have spent decades measuring every angle and force, building models from motion capture data and force plates. They've learned that running isn't just faster walking—it's a fundamentally different movement pattern, defined by that flight phase where both feet leave the ground.

The gait cycle divides into stance and swing. Stance is when your foot contacts the ground—that 200-millisecond window of impact, loading, and toe-off. Swing is the foot in the air, recovering forward for the next strike. At easy running pace, you spend about 60% of the cycle in stance, 40% in swing. As you speed up, stance time compresses. Elite sprinters touch down for barely 80 milliseconds. The faster you want to go, the less time you can afford on the ground.

Peter Weyand's research at Southern Methodist University revealed why: running speed equals stride length times stride rate, but what really matters is how much force you can apply in the time available. Sprinters don't necessarily push harder—they push faster. The limiting factor isn't how much force your muscles can generate, but how quickly they can generate it during ground contact.

This is why Eliud Kipchoge and other elite marathoners seem to float—their cadence hovers around 180 steps per minute, minimizing ground contact time while maximizing efficiency. Quick, light touches rather than long, heavy ones. The optimal cadence varies by runner and speed, but the principle holds: less time on the ground means less braking force, less energy wasted, more speed available.

Meanwhile, the foot itself is a marvel of mechanical engineering. Twenty-six bones, thirty-three joints, over a hundred muscles and tendons working in concert. The arch acts as a spring, storing elastic energy during impact and releasing it at toe-off. Studies using ultrasound imaging show the Achilles tendon stretching and recoiling with each stride, returning about 35% of the energy needed for the next push-off. Free power, courtesy of physics.

The running gait is efficient precisely because it's a controlled fall, a continuous conversion of potential energy to kinetic and back again. You're not so much propelling yourself forward as catching yourself from falling—over and over, 170 to 180 times per minute, for as long as you keep running.
