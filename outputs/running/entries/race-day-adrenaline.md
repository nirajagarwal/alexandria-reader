# Race-Day Adrenaline

The gun goes off and your heart rate spikes thirty beats before you've taken a step. Your hands are cold, your breathing shallow, and there's a metallic taste coating your tongue. You're not scared exactly—you've trained for this—but your body has activated its oldest survival program anyway. Welcome to the race-day adrenaline dump.

Adrenaline, or epinephrine, floods your bloodstream the moment your brain registers the race as a threat. This happens whether you're a high schooler toeing the line at your first 5K or an elite marathoner at the Olympic trials. The amygdala detects a challenge that matters—something with stakes, uncertainty, the possibility of failure—and triggers the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. Within seconds, your adrenal glands release adrenaline into your blood.

The effects are immediate and comprehensive. Your heart rate accelerates, pumping more blood to your muscles. Airways dilate to increase oxygen intake. Blood vessels constrict in your digestive system and skin—this is why your hands go cold and your stomach feels tight—while dilating in your working muscles. Your liver dumps glucose into your bloodstream. Your pupils dilate. Your pain threshold rises. You are, physiologically, prepared to run for your life.

The problem is that modern racing rarely requires the explosive, short-term response adrenaline evolved to provide. A 10K or marathon demands sustained effort, not acute escape. That initial surge can leave you gasping in the first mile, burning matches you'll need later. Experienced runners learn to manage this: controlled breathing in the corral, mental routines that quiet the amygdala, strategic warming up that burns off excess nervous energy before the gun.

But adrenaline isn't purely detrimental. Research by psychologist Jeremy Jamieson at the University of Rochester has shown that reappraising physiological arousal—interpreting your racing heart and shallow breathing as your body preparing to perform rather than panicking—improves performance. The runners who view pre-race nerves as functional rather than problematic run faster. The adrenaline is the same; the interpretation changes everything.

The truly skilled learn to ride the wave. They use the heightened arousal to sharpen focus and access deeper reserves when it matters. Late in a race, when glycogen is depleted and every step hurts, another surge of adrenaline can override fatigue signals, letting you close the gap or hold the pace. This is the system working as designed—not at the gun, but in the moment when everything depends on not stopping.

Your body can't distinguish between running from a predator and racing a 5K. But you can teach it the difference.
