# Glycogen and Fat Metabolism

Your muscles store about 2,000 calories worth of glycogen—enough to run hard for roughly two hours. After that, the tank is empty. Your fat stores, by contrast, hold around 100,000 calories, enough energy to run a hundred marathons. The problem is accessing it. At marathon pace, your body burns primarily glycogen. The faster you run, the more you rely on this limited fuel. Go too hard too early, and you'll deplete it miles before the finish.

This is why the marathon wall exists at mile twenty. You've exhausted your glycogen stores, and while fat burning continues, it can't produce energy fast enough to maintain your pace. The sensation is distinct—not gradual fatigue but a sudden, total depletion. Runners describe it as hitting concrete, as though their muscles have simply stopped responding. What's happening is metabolic: without glycogen, the Krebs cycle can't function efficiently. Fat oxidation alone can't maintain the ATP production rate needed for marathon pace.

Training reshapes this equation. Long runs teach the body to spare glycogen by increasing fat oxidation at a given pace. Studies show that well-trained runners burn proportionally more fat at marathon pace than novices, preserving glycogen for later miles. This adaptation happens through increased mitochondrial density and enhanced fat-transport proteins. Your muscles literally develop more machinery for burning fat.

Carbohydrate ingestion during the race provides external glycogen in real time. The gut can absorb about sixty grams of carbohydrate per hour—roughly 240 calories. This extends glycogen availability but doesn't eliminate the wall entirely. Elite marathoners consume carbs strategically throughout the race, maintaining blood glucose levels that spare muscle glycogen stores. Eliud Kipchoge's sub-two-hour marathon attempt involved precision-timed carbohydrate delivery every five kilometers.

There's also glycogen supercompensation—the practice of depleting stores through hard training, then loading carbohydrates in the days before a race. The muscles respond by storing extra glycogen, sometimes up to fifty percent more than baseline. This gives you a deeper tank, though it comes with added water weight, since glycogen binds water at a three-to-one ratio.

The fat-adapted movement argues for training in a glycogen-depleted state to force greater fat oxidation. Morning fasted runs or back-to-back long runs without refueling between. Some ultrarunners swear by it. The research is mixed—fat adaptation increases fat burning but doesn't necessarily improve race performance, since top-end speed still requires glycogen. You become better at running slowly on fat but may lose your ability to burn carbs efficiently when you need them.

The ideal is metabolic flexibility: a body that can shift seamlessly between fuel sources, burning fat to spare glycogen until the final miles demand everything you've got left.
