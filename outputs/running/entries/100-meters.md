# The 100 Meters

The gun fires and the race is already half over. Not in time—in decisions. By five seconds in, the winner is usually determined. By eight, it's done. The remaining two seconds are just the body catching up to what's already been decided by acceleration, by the thirty or so steps where power matters more than anything else will matter again.

A hundred meters is ten seconds of nearly perfect coordination. The foot strikes the ground for less than a tenth of a second—ninety milliseconds on average for elite sprinters. In that fraction, the body generates forces three to five times body weight, directed backward and down through a stiff ankle and a knee that barely bends. There's no time for the stretch-shortening cycle that makes distance running efficient. This is pure power: the firing of fast-twitch muscle fibers, the immediate recruitment of Type IIx fibers that contract violently and fatigue within seconds.

The start is an explosion from stillness. Usain Bolt, the fastest human ever timed, generated over 2,600 watts of power in his first step—more than three horsepower from one leg. His reaction time in Berlin 2009 was 0.146 seconds, slow by sprint standards, but it didn't matter. He reached peak velocity around sixty meters, still accelerating when most sprinters are already slowing down. His top speed: 27.8 miles per hour, sustained for about twenty meters before the inevitable deceleration that comes when the central nervous system can't fire any faster.

The physics are unforgiving. Air resistance increases with the square of velocity. At sprint speeds, a sprinter fights roughly nine pounds of drag—small, but enough to matter when races are won by hundredths of a second. This is why sprinters are muscled and compact, why they lean forward, why Bolt's height was considered a disadvantage until he proved otherwise.

Unlike distance running, which can be strategized and paced, the 100 meters is a maximal effort from start to finish. There's no conservation, no tactics beyond the start and the drive phase. The aerobic system contributes almost nothing. This is phosphocreatine and stored ATP, fuel that lasts six to eight seconds before the anaerobic system takes over for the final stretch. By the finish line, lactate is flooding the muscles, but the race is over before it becomes the limiting factor.

What the 100 meters measures isn't endurance or mental fortitude or pain tolerance. It measures power output, neuromuscular coordination, and the genetic gift of fast-twitch muscle fibers. It's the only running event where thinking is a disadvantage, where the race is won by the body's ability to move faster than conscious control.

Ten seconds. One hundred meters. The simplest question in running: how fast can you go when nothing else matters?
