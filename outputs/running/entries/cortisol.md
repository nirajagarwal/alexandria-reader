# Cortisol

Your heart rate spikes twenty minutes before the starting gun. Not from warming up—you're still standing in the corral, bouncing lightly, trying to stay loose. The tension comes from somewhere else. Your adrenal glands have already begun flooding your bloodstream with cortisol, the hormone that mobilizes energy and sharpens focus. By the time you cross the start line, cortisol levels will be 50-75% higher than baseline. Your body is preparing for a controlled emergency.

Cortisol gets maligned in wellness culture as the "stress hormone," something to be suppressed through meditation and adaptogens. But for runners, cortisol is essential machinery. It breaks down glycogen stores in the liver and muscles, releasing glucose into the bloodstream. It increases the availability of fatty acids for fuel. It suppresses inflammation temporarily, allowing you to keep running even as micro-damage accumulates in muscle fibers. During a hard effort, cortisol levels can reach three to five times normal—not a malfunction, but a feature.

The problem isn't cortisol itself but chronic elevation. A study from the University of North Carolina found that runners who consistently logged high weekly mileage without adequate recovery showed persistently elevated morning cortisol levels. Their bodies couldn't distinguish between training stimulus and genuine threat. The result: suppressed immune function, disrupted sleep, loss of bone density, impaired glycogen storage. The very hormone that enables peak performance can, when sustained, systematically dismantle it.

What determines whether cortisol helps or harms is context and duration. During a race or hard workout, the spike is purposeful—fuel released, pain blunted, attention focused. Afterward, cortisol should drop as the parasympathetic nervous system takes over. But stack hard efforts too closely, add insufficient sleep and chronic life stress, and cortisol remains elevated. The body stays in a state of mobilization with nowhere to go.

Researchers at the University of Colorado measured cortisol in collegiate runners during a competitive season. The athletes with the best performances showed sharp cortisol spikes before races but equally sharp drops in recovery periods. The struggling athletes—those getting injured, sick, or mysteriously slower—showed blunted responses: less cortisol mobilization when needed, less recovery suppression when it wasn't.

You can feel the difference. After a good race, cortisol clears within hours—appetite returns, exhaustion becomes clean tiredness, sleep comes easily. After weeks of accumulating stress, even easy runs feel harder than they should. You wake wired and tired at once. The hormone that should be a tool has become ambient weather.

The wisdom isn't to eliminate cortisol but to create the conditions for its natural rhythm: intense effort followed by genuine rest. The spike and the recovery. Running isn't the problem. Running without pausing is.
