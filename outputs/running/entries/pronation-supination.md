# Pronation and Supination

Your foot hits the ground heel-first, and for the next 200 milliseconds, the arch collapses inward. The ankle rolls medially, the tibia rotates, and the foot spreads wider against the pavement. This is pronation—the natural shock absorption system that disperses impact forces up through the kinetic chain. Then, milliseconds before toe-off, the process reverses: the arch springs back, the foot becomes a rigid lever, and you push off with stored elastic energy. This is supination—the foot transforming from shock absorber to propulsion device.

Every runner pronates. It's not a problem to fix but a mechanism to understand. The arch functions like a loaded spring. As your body weight drives the foot inward, connective tissues stretch and bones shift. The talus bone tilts, the calcaneus everts, the midfoot unlocks. Energy gets stored in the plantar fascia, the posterior tibial tendon, the spring ligament. Researchers measuring ground reaction forces have found that pronation reduces peak impact by distributing force across a larger surface area and over a longer time period. Without it, every footstrike would hammer straight up through rigid bones.

The spectrum runs from underpronation (supination throughout the gait cycle) to neutral pronation to overpronation (excessive inward roll). Most runners fall somewhere in the middle, pronating 15-20 degrees. Problems emerge at the extremes. Underpronators—often high-arched, rigid-footed runners—absorb less shock through the foot, transferring more force to bones. They're more prone to stress fractures and IT band issues. Overpronators—typically flat-footed or with weak posterior tibialis muscles—let the arch collapse too far or stay collapsed too long. The excessive rotation can torque the knee, strain the Achilles, or cause medial shin splints.

What confuses runners is that pronation isn't visible from above. You can't always tell by looking at your feet during a run. Sports medicine researchers use high-speed cameras and force plates to measure it precisely—tracking calcaneal eversion angles, arch height changes, tibial rotation. The running shoe industry built an entire category system around pronation control: neutral shoes, stability shoes, motion control shoes. The evidence for whether this matching system prevents injury remains mixed. A 2016 study in the British Journal of Sports Medicine found no difference in injury rates between runners assigned shoes based on pronation type versus those given neutral shoes randomly.

Your foot pronates because it needs to. The question isn't whether to pronate but whether your particular pattern creates forces your body can't handle. That depends on strength, flexibility, training load, running surface, and how abruptly your arch collapses. The foot isn't failing when it rolls inward. It's doing exactly what evolution designed it to do—turning bone and soft tissue into a spring that loads and releases with every stride.
