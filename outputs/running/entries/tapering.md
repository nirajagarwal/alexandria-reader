# Tapering

The first time you taper for a race, it feels wrong. You've been running seventy miles a week, your legs heavy with accumulated fatigue, and now two weeks out you're told to cut the volume in half. Your body still wants to push. The restlessness builds. You feel soft, undertrained, like you're losing everything you've built.

But taper is not rest. It's strategic reduction designed to preserve fitness while clearing the debt of training stress. The principle is simple: fitness built over months doesn't disappear in days, but fatigue does. The goal is to arrive on race day with your aerobic engine intact and your muscles ready to fire.

The physiology is precise. Hard training creates microscopic muscle damage, depletes glycogen stores, and suppresses immune function. Recovery from a single hard workout takes forty-eight to seventy-two hours. Recovery from months of accumulated training takes longer—typically ten to fourteen days. During taper, muscle glycogen increases fifteen to twenty percent. Muscle damage repairs. Inflammation markers drop. Blood volume expands. Mitochondrial density holds steady.

Research by David Costill in the 1980s established the framework: reduce volume by forty to sixty percent while maintaining intensity. The key is keeping the nervous system sharp with short, fast efforts while giving the cardiovascular system time to recover. A typical two-week taper might look like this: week one cuts mileage by thirty percent with two moderate workouts, week two cuts another thirty percent with one short, sharp session five days out.

The mental challenge is real. Runners report anxiety, irritability, phantom aches. The body, adapted to high mileage, suddenly has surplus energy with nowhere to go. Sleep patterns shift. You notice twinges you'd normally run through. This is normal. The body is recalibrating, sharpening its edge.

The temptation is to test yourself—to check if the fitness is still there with an unexpected hard run. Don't. Trust the process. Studies show that maintaining training volume right up to race day can leave runners three to six percent slower. That's the difference between a breakthrough and a disappointment.

Done right, taper feels like waking up. The heaviness lifts. Legs that felt sluggish suddenly feel springy. The race-day morning warmup feels different—not labored but eager, the body coiled and ready. The fitness you built over months is still there. You've just cleared away the fatigue that was masking it.
