**The Stretch-Shortening Cycle**

Watch a runner's calf muscle in slow motion. Just before the foot leaves the ground, the muscle lengthens under load—stretching like a rubber band pulled taut. Then it snaps back, releasing that stored energy into forward propulsion. This is the stretch-shortening cycle, and it's happening dozens of times per minute in nearly every muscle involved in running.

The cycle has three phases, each measured in milliseconds. First comes the eccentric phase—the muscle lengthens while contracting, like your quadriceps when your foot strikes the ground. The muscle fibers stretch, but so do the tendons attached to them, particularly the Achilles and patellar tendons. These tendons are essentially springs, and stretching them stores elastic energy.

Then the amortization phase—the briefest moment between stretching and shortening, typically 50-100 milliseconds in running. This is the critical window. The longer the amortization, the more stored elastic energy dissipates as heat. Elite runners minimize this phase, spending as little time as possible on the ground. When Moses Kipsiro or Hellen Obiri run, their ground contact time often drops below 150 milliseconds. They're not muscling through each stride—they're bouncing.

Finally, the concentric phase—the muscle shortens, the tendon recoils, and the stored elastic energy releases. Done efficiently, this means you get propulsion without the full metabolic cost. Your muscles don't have to generate all the force; your tendons do some of the work for free.

This is why running economy improves with training even when VO2 max plateaus. Your body learns to exploit the stretch-shortening cycle more effectively. Tendons stiffen slightly—not from damage but adaptation—becoming better springs. The nervous system learns to time the phases precisely. What once required full muscular effort becomes partially passive, like running on a trampoline instead of concrete.

Plyometric training—box jumps, bounding, hopping drills—enhances this cycle. These exercises teach the body to handle and release elastic energy rapidly. Runners who incorporate plyometrics don't just build strength; they build springiness. The muscle-tendon unit becomes a better energy storage and release system.

But the cycle can fail. Land too heavily, spend too long on the ground, or run when the tendons are cold or fatigued, and the elastic advantage disappears. The energy dissipates. The muscles have to generate all the force chemically, burning more glycogen, producing more heat, fatiguing faster. Running becomes grinding instead of fluid.

This is partly why cadence matters, why form matters, why stiffness—in the mechanical sense—matters. The stretch-shortening cycle rewards quick, light ground contact. It rewards the runner who moves like a stone skipping across water rather than a foot stomping through it.

Every footfall is a tiny test of elastic physics. Stretch, hold, release. The body as a collection of springs, giving back what you put in—if you put it in correctly.
