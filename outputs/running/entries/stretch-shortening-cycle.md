# The Stretch-Shortening Cycle

Your foot lands and the achilles tendon lengthens under load, stretching like a rubber band pulled taut. For a fraction of a second—maybe 200 milliseconds—elastic energy loads into the tendon and the muscle fibers connected to it. Then you push off and it all snaps back, releasing that stored tension into forward motion. You didn't have to generate all that force with muscular contraction alone. The tissue itself became a spring.

This is the stretch-shortening cycle, and it's happening with every stride. The eccentric phase: muscle lengthens under tension as you land. The amortization phase: that brief moment of transition at the bottom of the stride. The concentric phase: muscle shortens forcefully as you push off. But it's not just muscular. Tendons, particularly the achilles, store and return elastic energy with remarkable efficiency—up to 90% recovery in some estimates. This makes running more economical than it would be if muscles alone had to generate every bit of propulsive force.

The cycle works best when ground contact time is short. Land, transition, spring off. The longer you spend on the ground, the more that stored elastic energy dissipates as heat instead of motion. Elite distance runners often have ground contact times under 200 milliseconds. They're not muscling through each stride—they're loading and releasing springs in rapid succession. This is partly why cadence matters: faster turnover means less time to lose that elastic recoil.

Training amplifies this mechanism. Plyometric exercises—bounding, box jumps, single-leg hops—teach the neuromuscular system to handle the eccentric load and convert it rapidly into concentric power. Tendon stiffness adapts over time, becoming more responsive to loading. Hill repeats and track intervals condition the legs to cycle through this pattern at intensity. The body learns to be a better spring.

But there's a limit. Fatigue degrades the cycle. Late in a race, ground contact time lengthens, the amortization phase stretches out, and you lose that elastic snap. What felt like bouncing earlier now feels like slogging. The springs are still there, but the nervous system can't coordinate the rapid transition. Strength endurance—the ability to maintain power output under fatigue—determines how long you can preserve the cycle's efficiency.

Watch a fresh runner at mile two and the same runner at mile twenty. The biomechanics tell the story. Early: quick ground contact, minimal vertical oscillation, smooth elastic recoil. Late: longer contact, more muscular effort, less spring. The stretch-shortening cycle hasn't disappeared. It's just breaking down under accumulated load, ground contact by ground contact, until the body that once bounced now pushes.
