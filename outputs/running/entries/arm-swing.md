**Arm Swing**

Your arms pump faster and your legs follow. This isn't metaphor—it's biomechanics. The arms and legs are coupled through the nervous system's central pattern generators, neural circuits in the spinal cord that coordinate rhythmic movement. When your arms accelerate, signals cascade down to the legs, pulling cadence upward. Try it: mid-run, quicken your arm swing without consciously changing your leg turnover. Your stride rate rises automatically, almost before you notice.

But arm swing does more than set tempo. It provides balance against the rotational forces generated by the legs. Each time your right leg swings forward, it creates torque that would twist your torso counterclockwise. Your left arm swings forward simultaneously, creating equal and opposite torque. The result: your upper body stays stable while your lower body churns. Runners who let their arms cross the body's midline—swinging laterally instead of front-to-back—add unwanted rotation, wasting energy fighting against themselves.

The optimal position is roughly 90 degrees at the elbow, though this opens slightly during the backswing and closes during the forward drive. Hands should pass somewhere between the hip and lower ribs—not reaching toward the chest, not dropping below the waist. Shoulders stay relaxed, not hunched toward the ears. The motion comes from the shoulder joint, not from tensing the neck and upper trapezius muscles that fatigue quickly and contribute nothing to forward motion.

Watch elite distance runners and you'll see compact, economical arm action—minimal vertical movement, hands tracing a narrow arc close to the body. Sprinters drive their arms more aggressively, with greater range of motion and more forceful backswing, generating the power their explosive stride demands. The physics remain the same; the amplitude changes.

Some runners let their arms go loose and floppy, thinking relaxation means abandoning structure. But relaxation isn't the same as passivity. The arms should move with controlled purpose, muscles engaged enough to maintain position and rhythm without gripping tight. Clench your fists and tension radiates up through the forearms and shoulders. Cup your hands lightly—as if holding a potato chip you don't want to crush—and the arms stay nimble.

Research on arm swing's metabolic cost offers a counterintuitive finding: deliberately holding your arms still while running increases energy expenditure by about 3%. The body has to work harder to stabilize the trunk and maintain balance. Free arm swing, far from being wasted motion, actually improves running economy. Those pumping arms aren't along for the ride—they're reducing the total energy bill, keeping you upright, and setting the rhythm that carries you forward.
