# Runner's Knee

The pain starts as a whisper on the outside of the kneecap. A slight ache during mile three. By mile five it's speaking clearly—a sharp, grinding sensation with every footfall. By mile seven, the knee feels like it might buckle. This is patellofemoral pain syndrome, what runners simply call runner's knee, and it accounts for nearly a quarter of all running injuries.

The patella—the kneecap—is a sesamoid bone embedded in the quadriceps tendon. It glides through a groove in the femur with each stride, multiplying the mechanical advantage of the quad muscles. When this tracking goes wrong, when the patella pulls slightly lateral with each knee bend, the cartilage underneath begins to wear. The pain isn't in the bone itself but in the richly innervated tissue surrounding it.

What causes the misalignment is rarely the knee itself. The culprit usually sits upstream: weak hip abductors, particularly the gluteus medius. When this muscle fails to stabilize the pelvis during single-leg stance, the femur rotates inward, the knee collapses medially, and the patella gets yanked off track. It's called dynamic knee valgus, visible in slow-motion video as that slight inward collapse at foot strike. The knee pays for what the hip can't control.

Biomechanist Irene Davis found that runners with patellofemoral pain consistently showed weaker hip abduction strength—about 26% less than healthy runners. The fix isn't stretching the IT band with a foam roller, though that's become ritual. It's strengthening the posterior chain: clamshells, single-leg bridges, side planks with hip abduction. Boring work that happens off the road.

Overstriding compounds the problem. Landing with the foot too far ahead of the body's center of mass increases the braking force on the knee and amplifies the stress on the patellofemoral joint. Shortening stride length by just five percent can reduce knee loading by up to 20%. The correction feels almost trivial—quicker turnover, lighter contact—but it redistributes force across the entire kinetic chain.

Recovery means backing off before complete rest becomes necessary. Reducing mileage by 30-50% while maintaining frequency keeps aerobic fitness without grinding cartilage. Swimming and cycling maintain cardiovascular capacity while the knee heals. Most runner's knee resolves in six to eight weeks with proper strength work, but only if you catch it early. Ignore it and the cartilage degrades, the pain deepens, and what was a training interruption becomes a season-ending condition.

The knee doesn't fail on its own. It fails because something else—the hip, the foot, the stride—stopped doing its job.
