# Runner's Knee

**Runner's Knee**

The pain arrives as a dull ache behind the kneecap, worse going downhill, worse on stairs. It feels like pressure, like something grinding that shouldn't grind. You can run through it at first—it loosens after a mile or two—but eventually it starts earlier, stays longer, turns sharp. The medical term is patellofemoral pain syndrome, but everyone calls it runner's knee because runners get it more than anyone else.

The patella—the kneecap—is a floating bone embedded in the quadriceps tendon. When you bend and straighten your leg, it tracks up and down a groove in the femur called the trochlear groove. In runner's knee, the patella isn't tracking cleanly. It tilts, shifts laterally, compresses unevenly against the cartilage beneath. The result is irritation, inflammation, pain that announces itself with every step.

The causes are mechanical and multiple. Weak hip abductors—the gluteus medius especially—fail to stabilize the femur, letting it rotate inward with each footfall. The knee follows, and the patella gets pulled out of alignment. Tight IT bands, tight hip flexors, overdeveloped outer quads relative to the vastus medialis oblique (the teardrop-shaped muscle just above the inside of the knee)—all can tug the kneecap off its ideal path. Sometimes it's as simple as ramping up mileage too fast, the cumulative stress outpacing the body's adaptation.

Or it's the downhills. Eccentric loading—the quadriceps lengthening under tension as you brake with each step—puts enormous pressure on the patellofemoral joint. Trail runners coming off mountains know this pain. So do marathoners who train on hills without building the strength to handle the descent.

The fix isn't rest alone, though backing off helps. Runner's knee responds to correction: strengthening the glutes and the VMO, improving hip stability, addressing muscle imbalances. Single-leg squats, clamshells, step-downs with controlled descent. The boring work. Foam rolling the IT band doesn't fix the tracking issue, but loosening tight structures around the hip and thigh can reduce the lateral pull on the patella.

Physical therapists will assess your gait, looking for the inward collapse of the knee, the drop of the hip, the compensations that cascade down the kinetic chain. Sometimes a slight adjustment in cadence—quicker, shorter steps—reduces the impact load. Sometimes new shoes help, though often it's the training error, not the footwear.

What's maddening is that runner's knee can linger. It's not a clean break that heals in six weeks. It's a pattern that needs interrupting, a habit the body has learned that must be unlearned. You can run through mild cases if you're patient and strategic—easy miles, flat routes, consistent strengthening. But push too hard and it flares, and you're back where you started.

The good news: runner's knee is almost never serious damage. No torn ligaments, no structural failure. It's misalignment, not destruction. Fix the mechanics, build the strength, give it time, and the kneecap finds its groove again. The pain fades. The stairs stop announcing themselves. You run downhill without wincing, and the knee, for now at least, remembers how to track true.
