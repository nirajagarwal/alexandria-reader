# Endorphins

The story about endorphins and running is mostly wrong.

For decades, runners have credited endorphins—the brain's morphine-like molecules—with the runner's high. The logic seemed clean: running hurts, endorphins kill pain, therefore running produces endorphins, therefore euphoria. Scientists measured elevated endorphin levels in runners' blood after hard efforts. The case appeared closed.

Except endorphins are large molecules. They can't cross the blood-brain barrier. The endorphins flooding a runner's bloodstream after a hard workout are stuck outside the brain, doing their work in the periphery—reducing pain signals from muscles and joints, yes, but not creating the mental state that runners chase. The euphoria comes from somewhere else.

What actually produces the runner's high is likely a different system entirely: endocannabinoids, the brain's own cannabis-like molecules. These are small enough to enter the brain, and they spike during sustained aerobic exercise. When German researchers gave runners either a drug that blocks endorphins or one that blocks endocannabinoids, only the endocannabinoid blocker eliminated the mood boost and euphoria. The endorphin blocker did nothing to the high—though it did make running hurt more.

But endorphins aren't useless. They're just doing something different than advertised.

They work at the level of the body, not the mind. During a long run, as muscles accumulate microdamage and joints absorb thousands of impacts, endorphins dampen the pain signals before they reach consciousness. They don't make you high—they make it possible to keep running when your body has reasons to stop. They're part of the body's remarkable tolerance for self-inflicted stress, the system that allows distance running to feel sustainable even when you're systematically breaking yourself down.

The runner's high requires endocannabinoids. But endorphins are what let you run long enough to get there.

This matters because it reveals something about how running works on the brain and body simultaneously. The peripheral pain management system—endorphins—enables the central reward system—endocannabinoids and dopamine—to kick in. You need both. The body has to be convinced to tolerate the punishment. The brain has to be rewarded for inflicting it.

The confusion between endorphins and the runner's high isn't just semantic. It's a window into how poorly we understood running's effects on the brain until recently. We knew runners felt good. We measured molecules. We drew a line between them and called it science. The actual mechanism is more complex, more interesting, and still being mapped. The feeling that pulls runners back out the door comes from a neurochemical cascade we're only beginning to understand—and endorphins are just the opening act.
