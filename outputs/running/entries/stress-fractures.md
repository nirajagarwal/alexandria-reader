# Stress Fractures

**Stress Fractures**

The pain arrives as a whisper before it becomes a scream. First, a vague ache during the run that fades afterward. Then it lingers into the evening. Then it greets you with the first step out of bed. By the time a stress fracture announces itself clearly, the bone has already been asking for rest for weeks.

A stress fracture is a hairline crack in bone, caused not by a single impact but by accumulated load. Running delivers roughly 2.5 times body weight through the bones with each footstrike. The tibia, metatarsals, and femoral neck absorb this force thousands of times per run. Bone responds to stress by breaking down and rebuilding—a process called remodeling. But remodeling requires rest periods between runs to complete. Push too hard, too often, and breakdown outpaces repair. The microscopic cracks don't heal. They deepen.

The most common sites tell the story of running's repeated forces. Metatarsals—the long bones of the midfoot—account for up to 20% of stress fractures in runners, concentrated especially in the second and third. The tibia, absorbing massive vertical loads with every stride, represents another 20-35%. The femoral neck, where the thigh bone meets the hip, is less common but more dangerous—a complete fracture there can compromise blood flow to the femoral head, risking permanent damage.

Women face higher risk, particularly those with irregular or absent menstrual cycles. Low estrogen reduces bone density, weakening the skeleton's tolerance for impact. This is part of the Female Athlete Triad—the intersection of low energy availability, menstrual dysfunction, and bone loss. Male runners aren't immune, but the combination of lower bone density baseline and hormonal disruption makes stress fractures roughly twice as common in women.

The treatment is simple and agonizing: stop running. Complete rest for 6-8 weeks minimum, sometimes longer depending on location and severity. The femoral neck can require 12 weeks or more. High-risk fractures may need surgery—a screw to stabilize the bone while it heals. During that time, the fracture line slowly fills with new bone, invisible on X-ray for weeks, gradually appearing on MRI as the body does what it does: repairs what's broken if you give it the chance.

Prevention is load management. Increase weekly mileage by no more than 10% per week. Vary surfaces—pavement punishes bone more than trails or tracks. Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D intake, the raw materials of bone formation. For women, regular menstrual cycles are a sign that hormones are balanced enough to support bone health. Ignoring absent periods is ignoring a warning light.

Stress fractures don't announce themselves dramatically. They arrive quietly, a small hurt that grows. The body speaks softly before it shouts, and a stress fracture is what happens when the soft voice gets ignored too long.
