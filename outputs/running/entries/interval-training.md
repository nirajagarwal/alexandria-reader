**Interval Training**

The stopwatch starts and the body shifts gears—30 seconds, maybe two minutes, maybe five, as hard as sustainable, then the blessed slowdown, the recovery jog that never feels quite long enough before the next one begins. Interval training is structured suffering: periods of high-intensity running punctuated by rest or easy running, repeated until the workout becomes a kind of metabolic algebra. It's where runners get faster.

The principle is simple: you can run at intensities that would destroy you in continuous form if you break them into manageable chunks. Run for three minutes at 5K race pace and you've barely started. Run for three minutes at 5K pace, recover for two, and repeat eight times, and you've accumulated 24 minutes at a pace you couldn't hold for half that long otherwise. The recovery intervals allow partial restoration—heart rate drops, lactate clears partially, ATP-CP stores replenish—enough to go hard again, but not enough to eliminate the stress. That incomplete recovery is the point.

The body adapts to this specific demand. Stroke volume increases as the heart learns to pump more blood per beat. Mitochondrial density rises in the muscle cells. The lactate threshold—the intensity at which lactate begins accumulating faster than it can be cleared—creeps upward. The cardiovascular system becomes more efficient at delivering oxygen. The muscles become better at using it. Interval training doesn't just make you faster; it rewrites the physiological ceiling.

Czech runner Emil Zátopek pioneered modern interval work in the 1940s, running 100-meter repeats until he could barely stand, sometimes 50 or 60 in a single session. His competitors thought he was insane. He won three gold medals at the 1952 Olympics—the 5,000, the 10,000, and the marathon, which he'd never run before. His methods were extreme, but the insight was sound: controlled intensity, repeated, transforms capacity.

The variations are endless. Short intervals—200 to 400 meters at nearly all-out pace—stress the anaerobic system and develop speed. Medium intervals—800 to 1,600 meters—target VO2 max, the maximum rate of oxygen consumption, expanding aerobic power. Longer intervals—3 to 5 minutes—hover near lactate threshold, teaching the body to sustain discomfort. The recovery can be active or passive, short or long, depending on what's being trained.

The watch beeps. Heart rate climbing. Breath quickening. The fourth repeat always feels harder than the third, the seventh harder than the sixth. That's the workout working—accumulating fatigue that forces adaptation. Between intervals, there's time to think, which is both blessing and curse. You know exactly how many remain. The stopwatch doesn't lie, and neither does the body's response. Interval training is running distilled to its essence: go hard, recover incompletely, repeat until changed.
