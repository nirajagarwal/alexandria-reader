# Interval Training

The track is quiet at 5:30 a.m., just you and the painted lines and the knowledge that in three minutes you'll be running faster than feels sustainable. That's the point. Interval training—repeated bursts of hard effort separated by recovery—is controlled metabolic stress, the body pushed past its comfort zone in measured doses.

In 1952, Emil Zátopek won three gold medals at the Helsinki Olympics running a schedule so punishing it became legend: sets of 400-meter repeats, sometimes a hundred in a session, interspersed with 200-meter jogs. He wasn't just training harder; he was training differently, stressing specific energy systems in ways continuous running couldn't reach.

The physiology is straightforward. When you run an interval at threshold pace or faster, you accumulate lactate, increase oxygen demand, and push against the limits of your cardiovascular system. Then you recover—incompletely. The next interval starts before your heart rate returns to baseline, before lactate fully clears. This accumulation is the stimulus. Your body adapts by improving lactate clearance, increasing stroke volume, enhancing mitochondrial density, making the VO2 max ceiling a little higher.

Different intervals target different systems. Short, fast repeats—200s at mile pace—train speed and running economy, teaching your legs to turn over quickly while maintaining form. Longer intervals at threshold pace—1000-meter repeats, three to five minutes of controlled discomfort—expand your anaerobic capacity and raise your lactate threshold, the speed you can sustain before fatigue compounds faster than you can clear it. The rest periods matter as much as the work: too long and the training stimulus diminishes; too short and you can't complete the session.

The mental component is distinct from other running. Intervals require precision—hitting splits, managing effort, staying present through discomfort. You can't drift into flow state. Each repeat is a negotiation: how hard to start, when to push, whether you have another gear for the final interval. The clock removes ambiguity. Either you ran 3:20 or you didn't.

Recovery intervals teach their own lesson. The jog between hard efforts isn't rest—it's active recovery, keeping blood flowing, clearing metabolic waste, preparing for the next surge. Elite runners often run their recovery laps faster than recreational runners' tempo pace. But the principle scales: the recovery should be easy enough to let your breathing settle, hard enough to keep the workout's momentum.

What Zátopek understood is that the body adapts to what you ask of it. Long, steady runs build aerobic base, but they don't teach your cardiovascular system to respond to sudden demands or train your muscles to handle lactate accumulation. Intervals do. They're uncomfortable by design—that discomfort is the signal telling your body to get stronger, faster, more efficient.

The track at 5:30 a.m., three minutes into your first interval, lungs working, legs turning over, the stopwatch ticking: this is where aerobic capacity expands. Not in theory. In real time, one controlled surge after another.
