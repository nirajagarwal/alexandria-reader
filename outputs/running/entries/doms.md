**DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness)**

You don't feel it during the run. Not even in the first hour after. But the next morning, when you swing your legs out of bed, the staircase might as well be Everest. Your quads scream with each descent. Sitting down requires strategy. This is DOMS—delayed onset muscle soreness—the body's protest that arrives fashionably late to the injury party.

The delay is the strange part. Tear a muscle badly enough and you know immediately. But DOMS operates on a different timeline. The pain peaks 24 to 72 hours after the insult, which makes it feel disconnected from the run that caused it. The mechanism involves eccentric muscle contractions—when the muscle lengthens under load, like the quadriceps controlling your descent down a steep hill. These contractions create microscopic tears in the muscle fibers, tiny structural disruptions that don't register as pain until the inflammatory response kicks in.

What happens in those 24 hours is a cascade. The damaged muscle fibers leak their contents—creatine kinase, myoglobin, proteins that don't belong floating free. The immune system detects the debris and sends in the cleanup crew: macrophages, neutrophils, inflammatory mediators. They flood the damaged tissue, causing swelling and sensitizing the pain receptors. The soreness you feel isn't the original damage—it's inflammation, which is the body's repair process making itself known.

Runners encounter DOMS most reliably after downhill runs, speed work, or any deviation from routine. Ultra-marathoner Courtney Dauwalter, after winning the Moab 240-mile race, could barely walk for days. Her quads, pounded by 62,000 feet of elevation change, had accumulated damage that wouldn't show its full invoice until she crossed the finish line. First-time marathoners often find week one of training relatively painless, then wake up after their first long run unable to lift their arms to shampoo their hair. The body adapts to familiar stresses but responds to novelty with soreness.

The good news: DOMS is temporary and, paradoxically, protective. The repeated bout effect means that once you've suffered through it, subsequent similar efforts produce less soreness. The muscle fibers repair themselves slightly thicker, stronger, more resistant to damage. The body learns. Research by Kazunori Nosaka shows that even a single bout of eccentric exercise can protect against soreness for up to six months.

Treatment is mostly patience. Ice, compression, and anti-inflammatories might dull the pain but could actually slow recovery—inflammation is the repair mechanism, not just an inconvenience. Light movement helps more than complete rest; easy running increases blood flow without adding damage. The muscle needs to heal on its own timeline.

What DOMS is not: dangerous. The pain feels severe, but it's different from acute injury. You're not causing permanent damage by moving through it. The distinction matters—DOMS is adaptation, injury is breakdown. One makes you stronger, the other requires rest. The trick is learning to read the difference between muscles rebuilding themselves and muscles asking you to stop.
