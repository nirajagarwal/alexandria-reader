# DOMS

The soreness arrives the next morning, or more often, the morning after that. You wake and swing your legs out of bed and the quads protest—a deep, diffuse ache that makes stairs an ordeal and sitting down a controlled fall. This is delayed onset muscle soreness, DOMS, the price of running harder or longer than your body was ready for.

For decades, the explanation seemed simple: lactic acid buildup. Except lactate clears from muscles within an hour of finishing a run, and DOMS peaks 24 to 72 hours later. The real culprit is microscopic damage to the muscle fibers themselves.

During eccentric contractions—when muscles lengthen under load—the sarcomeres that make up muscle fibers get overstretched and tear. In running, this happens most dramatically during downhill running, when the quads work to control your descent. Each footstrike forces the muscle to lengthen while simultaneously contracting to absorb impact. The Z-lines that anchor the sarcomeres fracture. Calcium leaks into the damaged fibers, triggering inflammation. White blood cells arrive to clean up the damage, releasing prostaglandins and other inflammatory compounds. It's this inflammatory cascade, not the initial damage itself, that creates the deep, tender ache.

The soreness is a sign of adaptation in progress. When the muscle repairs, it rebuilds stronger—adding sarcomeres in series, strengthening connective tissue, improving the coordination between muscle fibers. George Ebbeling's research at the University of Massachusetts showed that a single bout of eccentric exercise provides protection for weeks. Run the same downhill route that left you hobbling for three days, and two weeks later you'll handle it with minimal soreness. The body learns.

But severe OMS slows you down. Performance drops—not just from the discomfort but from actual muscle weakness. Damaged fibers can't generate full force. Range of motion decreases. The inflammation that's part of healing also temporarily impairs muscle function. Studies show strength can drop 50 percent immediately after damaging exercise and take a week to fully recover.

The morning after a hard effort, the soreness is almost reassuring—evidence of work done. But it's not a training goal. The adaptations happen regardless of whether you can feel them, and constantly chasing DOMS means constantly recovering from damage rather than building fitness. The experienced runner learns to distinguish productive effort from destructive excess, to build gradually enough that the muscles adapt without breaking down.

You descend the stairs sideways, one step at a time. Tomorrow it will hurt less. Next week, the same run won't hurt at all.
