# The Half Marathon

Thirteen point one miles. The distance sits at an odd intersection—too long to run on pure speed, too short to be a true test of endurance. And yet that tension is precisely what makes it the most instructive distance in running.

The half marathon lives in a physiological sweet spot. You're running at roughly 85-90% of your maximum heart rate for the duration, just below the lactate threshold where breathing becomes labored and legs start to burn. It's fast enough to hurt, sustained enough to require serious aerobic capacity, but short enough that the body doesn't fully deplete its glycogen stores. You finish with something left in the tank, which means you were always negotiating between going faster and holding back.

Runners use the half to predict marathon times—double the half marathon time and add ten minutes is the old formula. But the relationship isn't linear. The marathon multiplies the half's demands: greater glycogen depletion, more muscular damage, deeper mental fatigue. The half marathon teaches you to run hard while managing effort. The marathon teaches you to keep running when everything says stop.

What makes the half distinctive is its pace. It's the fastest you can run while still being primarily aerobic. During a half marathon, roughly 98% of your energy comes from aerobic metabolism. In a 5K, that drops to about 90%. In a marathon, it's 99%, but you're running significantly slower. The half asks: how fast can you go while staying just under the threshold where lactate accumulates faster than you can clear it?

Elite runners cover it in under an hour. Jacob Kiplimo ran 57:31 in Lisbon in 2021. Letesenbet Gidey ran 1:02:52 in Valencia the same year. At that pace—sub-4:30 miles for Kiplimo, sub-5:00 for Gidey—they're operating at the edge of sustainable speed. For recreational runners finishing between 1:30 and 2:30, the effort is the same: finding the pace that hurts but doesn't break you.

The half marathon emerged as a standard distance in the 1990s, becoming the bridge event for marathoners and the destination race for runners who wanted something more than a 10K but less daunting than 26.2 miles. It's now the most popular race distance in the United States, drawing more participants than marathons. People can train for it without surrendering their lives to long runs. They can race it hard without risking the body breakdown that marathons invite.

At thirteen miles, you're always doing math. You reach halfway and realize you're only at mile 6.5. You hit mile ten and know three more miles at this pace might be impossible. The half marathon teaches you that pace is a negotiation, not a setting.
