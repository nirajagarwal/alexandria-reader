# Shin Splints

The pain starts as a dull ache along the inside edge of your shinbone, maybe three inches above the ankle. You notice it a mile into your run, ignore it for another mile, then spend the rest of the week feeling it every time you walk downstairs. Welcome to medial tibial stress syndrome—what everyone calls shin splints, though the name tells you almost nothing about what's actually happening.

The tibia doesn't hurt because something's wrong with the bone itself. It hurts because the muscles that attach to it—primarily the posterior tibialis and soleus—are pulling on the periosteum, the thin membrane that wraps the bone like shrink wrap. Every footstrike loads these muscles, and when they're overworked, they tug relentlessly at their attachment points. The periosteum responds with inflammation. The inflammation feels like your shin is on fire.

Shin splints almost always trace back to two interrelated problems: doing too much too soon, and doing it on legs that aren't prepared for the load. The classic scenario is a new runner jumping into daily mileage, or an experienced runner ramping up volume too quickly after time off. The posterior tibialis, which controls pronation and supports the arch, gets overwhelmed. It fatigues, loses its ability to absorb shock efficiently, and starts yanking on the bone with every stride. A 2018 study in the *British Journal of Sports Medicine* found that runners who increased weekly mileage by more than 30% had a fourfold increase in shin splint risk.

Biomechanics matter too. Overpronation—the inward roll of the foot after landing—forces the posterior tibialis to work overtime. Tight calves shift more stress forward onto the shin. Running on hard surfaces, or in worn-out shoes with collapsed midsoles, amplifies every impact. The shin muscles don't get stronger from this; they get strained.

What makes shin splints insidious is that they don't announce themselves dramatically. A stress fracture forces you to stop immediately. Shin splints let you keep running through moderate pain, which is precisely the wrong thing to do. The inflammation gets worse. The pain spreads. What could have been resolved with a week of rest and some calf stretching becomes a months-long problem that ruins a training cycle.

Recovery means backing off mileage, icing the shins after runs, and addressing the underlying mechanics—strengthening the posterior tibialis through toe raises, stretching the calves, possibly getting shoes with more arch support. The frustrating part is how preventable it all is. Gradual mileage increases, strength work, attention to form. But that's the shin splint paradox: it only teaches you to respect those things after it's already too late.
