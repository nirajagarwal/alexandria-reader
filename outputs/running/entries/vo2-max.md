# VO2 Max

The number arrives like a verdict. You're hooked up to a mask, breathing hard on a treadmill that keeps getting faster, and somewhere a machine is measuring how much oxygen your body can use. The test administrator watches the screen. The number climbs, then plateaus. That's it—your VO2 max, measured in milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute. Elite marathoners hit the low 80s. Recreational runners land in the 40s or 50s. The number feels both abstract and definitive, a ceiling written in your cells.

What it actually measures is the maximum rate at which your cardiovascular system can deliver oxygen to working muscles, and the muscles' capacity to use it. It's a measurement of your entire aerobic engine—heart, lungs, blood vessels, mitochondria, all working at full capacity. When exercise physiologist Per-Olof Åstrand began measuring VO2 max in the 1950s, he found it was the single best predictor of endurance performance. It still is. You can't run a fast marathon without a high VO2 max, though having one doesn't guarantee speed. It's necessary but not sufficient.

The physiology is straightforward. Your heart pumps oxygenated blood. Your muscles extract that oxygen and use it to produce ATP, the energy currency that powers muscle contractions. VO2 max is reached when these systems hit their limit—when your heart can't pump any faster, your blood can't carry any more oxygen, or your muscles can't extract it any more efficiently. For most people, the limiting factor is cardiac output: how much blood the heart can move. Elite endurance athletes have hearts that can pump 35 to 40 liters of blood per minute. Untrained individuals max out around 20.

About half of your VO2 max is genetic. The other half responds to training. Start running consistently and your VO2 max will climb—new capillaries branch into muscle tissue, mitochondria multiply, stroke volume increases. The heart adapts like any muscle, growing larger and more efficient. After six months of steady training, most people see gains of 15 to 25 percent. Then the curve flattens. You can nudge it higher with intervals and tempo runs, but eventually you approach your physiological ceiling.

But here's what the number doesn't tell you: running economy, lactate threshold, mental toughness, race tactics. Two runners with identical VO2 max scores can have vastly different race times. One might use oxygen more efficiently at race pace, maintaining speed with less effort. The other might have trained their body to tolerate more lactate, pushing harder before slowing. VO2 max is the size of the engine. What matters more is how you drive it.
