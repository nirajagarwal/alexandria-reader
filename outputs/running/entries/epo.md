# EPO

The first sign is usually the night sweats. Riders wake soaked, hearts hammering, blood thick as syrup moving through narrowed vessels. The hematocrit—the percentage of blood volume made of red cells—has climbed too high. Forty-seven, forty-eight, forty-nine percent. The heart strains to push what's become more sludge than liquid. Some keep an alarm set for 3 AM to get up and ride the trainer, get everything moving again. Others just hope they wake up.

Erythropoietin, EPO, is a hormone the kidneys produce when they sense low oxygen in the blood. It signals bone marrow to make more red blood cells. More red cells mean more hemoglobin, more oxygen-carrying capacity, more fuel delivered to working muscles. For people with kidney disease or undergoing chemotherapy, synthetic EPO is lifesaving medicine. For endurance athletes, it became the most effective drug in the sport's history.

The effect is straightforward: increase red blood cell count by 10 to 15 percent, and VO2 max—the maximum amount of oxygen the body can use—increases by roughly the same margin. A runner with a VO2 max of 70 might push it to 75 or 76. That's the difference between good and elite, between making the team and winning the race.

EPO entered cycling in the early 1990s and spread like wildfire. Within a few years, nearly the entire professional peloton was using it. Average speeds increased. Climbing times on iconic Tour de France ascents dropped by minutes. Between 1987 and 2003, at least eighteen professional cyclists died of heart attacks in their sleep, most under the age of thirty-five. Bjarne Riis won the 1996 Tour de France with a hematocrit of 56.3 percent—admitted later, along with everything else.

Running remained cleaner longer, or at least more quietly corrupt. But the same logic applied. Distance running is oxygen delivery. Any edge in oxygen-carrying capacity translates directly to performance. When Chinese runners shattered world records in the early 1990s, speculation centered on EPO, though nothing was proven at the time. No test existed yet—athletes were injecting a substance the body produces naturally.

The test, when it finally arrived in 2000, looked for synthetic EPO's slightly different molecular structure. Suddenly what had been undetectable became catchable. Lance Armstrong's 1999 Tour samples, retested years later, showed EPO positive. Rita Jeptoo, two-time Boston and Chicago Marathon winner, tested positive in 2014. The list grew.

But EPO's legacy isn't just the positives. It's the recalibrated expectations, the performances from that era that can't be trusted, the records that may never be believed. It revealed how much endurance sport is constrained by oxygen, and how badly some want to break that constraint. The sweating cyclist, heart pounding in the dark, chose risk over limitation. The sport is still calculating the cost.
