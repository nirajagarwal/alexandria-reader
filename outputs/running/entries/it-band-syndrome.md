# IT Band Syndrome

The pain starts around mile eight, a sharp tightness on the outside of your knee that turns every footfall into a wince. By mile ten, you're running with a hitch in your stride, trying to find an angle that doesn't aggravate it. By mile eleven, you're walking. The iliotibial band—that thick strip of fascia running from hip to knee—has gone from structural support to active saboteur.

The IT band itself doesn't actually get inflamed, despite the name. It's too tough for that—more like a taut cable than living tissue. What happens instead is friction. The band slides back and forth over the lateral femoral epicondyle, a bony prominence on the outside of the knee, with every bend and straighten of the leg. When something goes wrong—weak glutes, tight hip flexors, sudden mileage increase—that sliding becomes rubbing. The bursa beneath gets irritated. The pain blooms.

Biomechanist Irene Davis at Harvard's Spaulding National Running Center found that runners with IT band syndrome showed a distinct pattern: their hips dropped significantly on the stance leg, creating an inward collapse of the knee. This adduction increases the tension and friction at the exact spot where the band crosses the knee. The IT band itself is just doing its job. The problem lives upstream, in hip stability.

Most runners' first instinct is to stretch the IT band—standing cross-legged, pulling the hip sideways, feeling that familiar deep ache. But you can't really stretch it. Studies using cadavers and force measurement show you'd need roughly 2,000 pounds of pressure to elongate the band even one percent. That roadside stretch is doing something—maybe mobilizing the hip joint, maybe providing temporary relief through neurological signaling—but it's not lengthening the band.

What does work: strengthening the gluteus medius, the hip abductor that keeps the pelvis level during single-leg stance. Clamshells, side-lying leg lifts, single-leg deadlifts. Physical therapist Jay Dicharry found that six weeks of targeted hip strengthening resolved IT band pain in 92% of runners, compared to stretching and rest alone. The band stops screaming when the hip does its job.

The cruel thing is that IT band syndrome often strikes when you're getting fit—when you've added miles, increased intensity, started racing. Your cardiovascular system is ready, your legs feel strong, and then this one spot derails everything. But that's the nature of kinetic chain injuries. Running doesn't just stress individual parts; it reveals where the system breaks down. The knee pain is the symptom. The weak hip is the story.
