# IT Band Syndrome

The iliotibial band isn't a muscle—it's a thick strap of fascia running from your hip to just below your knee, and when it decides to hurt, it hurts with a precision that feels almost personal. A sharp, burning pain on the outer knee, usually around mile three or four, building with every step until you can't ignore it anymore. Not a dull ache you can run through. A hot wire.

The classic explanation: friction. The IT band rubs over the lateral femoral epicondyle—the bony knob on the outside of your knee—with each foot strike. Repeat that rubbing ten thousand times per run and you get inflammation, irritation, pain. For years, runners were told the band was too tight, that it needed stretching, foam rolling, massage to loosen it up. Stretch it out, roll it out, break up the adhesions.

Except the IT band doesn't really stretch. It's tensile strength approaches that of soft steel. You can't loosen it with a foam roller any more than you could stretch a car's seatbelt by lying on it. The pain isn't coming from the band being tight—it's coming from the band being overloaded.

Recent research suggests IT band syndrome is actually a compression injury. The band doesn't slide over the bone—it compresses against it. Beneath the band sits a layer of highly innervated fat, and when the knee bends around 30 degrees—exactly where you are in mid-stance while running—that fat gets squeezed between band and bone. Do it enough times with poor biomechanics and the fat becomes inflamed. The pain is the fat screaming.

The biomechanics that cause the overload usually start upstream. Weak hip abductors—particularly the gluteus medius—allow the pelvis to drop on the swing-leg side, a movement called Trendelenburg gait. The hip drops, the knee caves inward, the IT band pulls tighter across the outside of the knee. Every step compounds the problem. By the time you feel it in your knee, the issue has been building in your hip for miles.

Runners with IT band syndrome often have the same profile: high mileage, weak glutes, a tendency to run the same routes with the same camber. Roads are crowned for drainage, so if you always run on the left side facing traffic, your left leg is perpetually dealing with a slight downhill slope. The outside leg works harder to stabilize. The IT band takes the load.

Treatment isn't about the band itself—it's about the chain. Strengthen the hip abductors. Fix the Trendelenburg. Glute bridges, clamshells, single-leg work. Reduce mileage temporarily. Vary your terrain. If you must run on cambered roads, switch sides periodically. The pain will resist at first—inflammation has its own timeline—but as the hip stabilizers get stronger, the knee gets a reprieve.

Some runners never get it. Others battle it for years. The difference is rarely the IT band itself but everything around it: training volume ramped too quickly, muscle imbalances ignored too long, the same biomechanical fault repeated until the body finds the weakest link and breaks it. The IT band is just the messenger, delivering news the hip didn't want to hear.
