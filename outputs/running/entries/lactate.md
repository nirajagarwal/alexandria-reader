# Lactate

Your legs are burning. Mile three of a 5K and the effort has shifted from sustainable to something sharper, a sensation that seems to radiate from deep in the muscle fibers themselves. You've been taught to blame lactate—lactic acid, the enemy, the toxic byproduct that makes your muscles scream. But that burning isn't what you think it is. And lactate isn't the villain.

For decades, lactate was cast as metabolic waste, the stuff that accumulates when you push too hard, when your muscles can't get enough oxygen and resort to anaerobic glycolysis, breaking down glucose without oxygen's help. The story went: hard effort produces lactate, lactate causes fatigue, lactate makes you slow down. It was intuitive. It was wrong.

George Brooks at Berkeley spent thirty years dismantling this myth. His research revealed that lactate isn't a waste product—it's a fuel. When your muscles produce lactate during intense effort, it doesn't just sit there poisoning the system. It travels. Your heart takes it up and burns it. Your brain uses it. Other muscles, less active in the moment, convert it back to glucose. Lactate is your body's way of redistributing energy during exercise, a shuttle system for fuel when demand is high.

The burning sensation isn't lactate itself—it's hydrogen ions, byproducts of the same metabolic process that produces lactate. They accumulate in the muscle, lower the pH, interfere with contraction. Lactate actually helps buffer this acidity, shuttling hydrogen ions out of the muscle. So that sensation you've been blaming on lactate? Lactate is trying to solve it.

More surprising: you're producing lactate right now, sitting still. It's not an emergency metabolite. It's constantly being made and used, even at rest, even with plenty of oxygen available. When you start running easy, lactate production increases slightly, but so does lactate clearance. The system stays balanced. Push harder—threshold pace, intervals—and production outpaces clearance. Lactate accumulates. But it accumulates because you're producing energy faster than your body can redistribute it, not because your muscles are oxygen-starved and desperate.

Training raises your lactate threshold—the pace at which lactate starts to accumulate faster than you can clear it. Elite marathoners can run at 85-90% of their VO2 max while staying below threshold. Recreational runners might hit threshold at 70%. The difference isn't just fitness—it's adaptation. Your muscles build more mitochondria, improve blood flow, get better at using lactate as fuel. You're not training your body to produce less lactate. You're training it to handle more.

That burn at mile three? It's not toxic buildup. It's your body working at the edge of its current capacity to generate and redistribute energy. The limit isn't chemical—it's systemic. And it moves. Keep training, and next month that same pace won't burn the same way. Not because you're producing less lactate, but because you've gotten better at using it.
