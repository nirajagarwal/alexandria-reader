# The Aging Brain

The prefrontal cortex shrinks with age. Executive function declines. Working memory weakens. Processing speed slows. These are the typical trajectories of the aging brain—but runners chart a different course.

A 2012 study at the University of Illinois scanned the brains of older adults who'd been running for decades and compared them to sedentary age-matched controls. The runners' brains looked younger. Their white matter—the neural wiring that connects different brain regions—showed less degradation. The bundles of nerve fibers remained intact where, in non-runners, they had frayed. Information still traveled efficiently between regions. The communication networks that typically deteriorate with age had been preserved.

This isn't about staving off the inevitable. It's about maintaining function longer. The runner who's 65 doesn't have the brain of someone 25, but they maintain capacities that their sedentary peers have lost. Executive function—the ability to plan, focus, switch between tasks—remains sharper. Processing speed stays higher. The hippocampus, the brain's memory center that normally shrinks about one percent per year after age 55, actually grows in runners.

The mechanism is cardiovascular first. Running increases blood flow to the brain, delivering more oxygen and glucose. It triggers the release of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), a protein that supports the survival of existing neurons and encourages the growth of new ones. It reduces inflammation, which accelerates cognitive decline. It improves insulin sensitivity, protecting against the metabolic dysfunction that damages brain tissue.

But there's also the cognitive demand of running itself. Navigating terrain, maintaining form when fatigued, making split-second adjustments to uneven ground—these aren't automatic processes. They require constant communication between sensory input, motor planning, and execution. The brain stays engaged, plastic, adaptive.

A 70-year-old runner lacing up for a morning run is doing something the research suggests their brain desperately needs. Not jogging slowly to nowhere in particular, though that helps too. Running with intention—trails that demand attention, hills that require management, workouts that need pacing. The brain responds to challenge, to novelty, to the specific stresses running provides.

The paradox is that we often stop running precisely when we need it most. The knees ache, the pace slows, the effort feels harder. But the aging brain doesn't need you to run fast. It needs you to keep running—to maintain the flow, the growth factors, the neural engagement. What feels like preservation is actually protection, not against death but against the slow erosion of the self.
