# Ketones

Mile eighteen of a marathon and the glycogen is gone. The legs feel wooden, the pace slowing despite every mental command to maintain it. The body has burned through its primary fuel—glucose stored in muscles and liver—and now it shifts. Fat oxidation increases, and with it comes the production of ketone bodies: acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetone. These molecules, synthesized in the liver from fatty acids, cross the blood-brain barrier and begin fueling the brain when glucose runs low. This is the metabolic pivot that can make or break an endurance effort.

Ketones are the body's backup generator. When carbohydrate stores deplete—through prolonged exercise, fasting, or very low carbohydrate intake—the liver converts fat into these small, water-soluble molecules. Unlike fatty acids, which can't cross into the brain, ketones slip through easily. The brain, which normally runs almost entirely on glucose, can derive up to seventy percent of its energy from ketones during extended fuel scarcity. For endurance runners, this matters: the wall isn't just about muscles running out of glycogen, it's about the brain losing its preferred fuel and the temporary cognitive fog that accompanies the switch.

The ketogenic diet—extremely low in carbohydrates, high in fat—forces the body into chronic ketone production, a state called nutritional ketosis. Some ultrarunners and endurance athletes have adopted this approach, training their bodies to become "fat adapted," relying more on fat oxidation and ketones even during moderate-intensity effort. The theory: unlimited fat stores versus limited glycogen means sustained energy without the bonk. Research shows mixed results. A 2016 study by Louise Burke found that elite race walkers on ketogenic diets showed decreased economy—they burned more oxygen to maintain the same pace compared to high-carb counterparts. The metabolic efficiency of fat and ketones comes at a cost: they're slower to convert to ATP than glucose, particularly at higher intensities.

But ketones aren't just fuel. Beta-hydroxybutyrate acts as a signaling molecule, influencing gene expression, reducing inflammation, and potentially protecting against oxidative stress. Some research suggests ketones may enhance recovery, preserve muscle glycogen during low-intensity efforts, and even improve mental clarity once the adaptation period passes. Endurance events lasting many hours—hundred-mile races, multi-day stage races—may benefit from the body's ability to sustain effort when glucose is scarce.

The body produces small amounts of ketones even on a regular diet during overnight fasts or after long runs. That faint acetone smell on the breath after a hard morning run—that's ketosis beginning, the metabolic machinery shifting into its secondary mode. For most runners, this is transient. The next meal replenishes glycogen, glucose returns, and ketones drop back to baseline. The body is always balancing its fuel sources: glucose for intensity, fat for duration, ketones as the bridge when supplies run thin.
