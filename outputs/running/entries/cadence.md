# Cadence

Your feet hit the ground 180 times per minute. Not because someone told you to count them, but because that's roughly where most distance runners land when they settle into their natural rhythm. Some fall a bit lower, some push past 185, but the clustering is real: for reasons the body seems to understand before the mind does, 180 steps per minute emerges as a sweet spot in the mechanics of human running.

Jack Daniels—the exercise physiologist, not the distiller—first documented this in the 1984 Olympics. He stood trackside with a stopwatch, counting foot strikes of elite distance runners across different races and paces. The number kept appearing: 180 steps per minute, give or take a few beats. Later analysis of recreational runners showed a wider spread—many shuffle along at 160 or lower—but among efficient runners, the convergence holds.

The reason lives in ground contact time. Each step is a negotiation between forward momentum and the ground's resistance. Land, load, push off. The longer your foot stays planted, the more you're braking against your own motion, absorbing impact vertically rather than redirecting it horizontally. Quick cadence means quick feet, and quick feet mean less time to decelerate with each strike. At 160 steps per minute, you're on the ground roughly 250 milliseconds per step. At 180, you're closer to 200 milliseconds. Those saved 50 milliseconds per step compound: less braking, less vertical oscillation, less load absorbed by joints.

Stride length adjusts to pace, but cadence stays relatively constant. Run faster and you don't spin your legs quicker—you push harder, extend farther, cover more ground per step. Run slower and your stride shortens, but the rhythm holds. This stability matters because cadence is partly hardwired—determined by leg length, muscle fiber composition, neuromuscular patterns grooved over thousands of miles. Trying to drastically increase cadence without changing anything else often backfires, creating a choppy, effortful stride that fights the body's natural spring.

But many runners do benefit from nudging cadence upward. Overstriding—landing with the foot too far ahead of the body's center of mass—increases impact forces and inefficiency. A slightly quicker cadence naturally shortens stride length, pulling foot strike closer to center. The result isn't faster running immediately, but smoother, more economical motion. The body stops fighting itself.

The rhythm becomes automatic. After enough miles at a certain cadence, it embeds itself in the nervous system, a motor pattern so practiced it runs on autopilot. Your feet find the beat before your brain registers the pace. Put on music at 180 BPM and your stride syncs without conscious effort. This is the body's metronome, not something imposed but something discovered—the tempo at which the human spring bounces best.
