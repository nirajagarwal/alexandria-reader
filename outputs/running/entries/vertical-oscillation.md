# Vertical Oscillation

Watch a runner from the side and you'll see it: the rise and fall with each stride, the body lifting and dropping in rhythm. This vertical movement—the oscillation—is wasted energy. Every centimeter you bounce upward is a centimeter you're not moving forward.

Elite distance runners minimize this bounce. Studies using motion capture show the best marathoners oscillate around 6-8 centimeters per stride, while recreational runners often exceed 10-12 centimeters. That difference compounds. Over a marathon's roughly 50,000 steps, excessive vertical oscillation means lifting your body weight an extra kilometer or more into the air. Pure waste.

The oscillation comes from the body's response to ground contact. When your foot strikes, the impact force—often 2-3 times your body weight—compresses your muscles and tendons. They act like springs, storing energy and releasing it. But the release isn't perfectly horizontal. Some of that stored energy converts to upward motion, lifting your center of mass higher than necessary.

Running economy—the oxygen cost of maintaining a given pace—correlates directly with vertical oscillation. The more you bounce, the more energy you burn for the same forward speed. Francesco Conconi's research in the 1990s showed that reducing vertical displacement by just 2 centimeters could improve running economy by 3-5%. That's the difference between 2:05 and 2:08 in a marathon.

The fix isn't simply trying to run lower. Overcorrecting creates a shuffling gait that shortens stride length and increases ground contact time—trading one inefficiency for another. Instead, efficient runners land with their foot closer to their center of mass, minimizing the braking force and the subsequent bounce. Their hips stay level, core engaged, controlling the elastic recoil. They don't fight the spring action of tendons and muscles; they channel it forward.

Wearable technology now measures vertical oscillation in real time. Devices like Garmin and COROS track it with accelerometers, giving runners immediate feedback. The data confirms what coaches have long observed: fatigue increases oscillation. As muscles tire in late miles, form degrades. The bounce creeps upward. You start spending energy on height instead of distance.

There's an irony here. Running feels like forward motion, but much of the body's work goes into managing vertical forces. You're constantly catching yourself from falling, controlling the bounce, redirecting impact into propulsion. The best runners make this invisible. They seem to glide, barely rising, all energy funneling forward. Watch Eliud Kipchoge and you see it: minimal oscillation, maximum efficiency. Not running harder. Running smarter. Every centimeter counts.
