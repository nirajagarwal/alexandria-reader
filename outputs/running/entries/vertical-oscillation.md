**Vertical Oscillation**

You're not just moving forward when you run. You're also going up.

Every stride launches you into the air—not much, maybe two to three inches for an efficient runner, but enough. You rise, you fall, you rise again. This vertical motion is called oscillation, and it's one of running's most expensive movements. Every centimeter you lift yourself is energy not spent moving forward. It's the tax you pay for being a bipedal animal who had to figure out how to move fast.

Elite distance runners minimize vertical oscillation ruthlessly. Eliud Kipchoge barely seems to rise at all—his center of mass travels forward like a marble rolling across glass. When researchers analyzed his Berlin marathon world record, they found his vertical oscillation averaged just 6.8 centimeters. He wasn't bouncing; he was gliding. Compare that to recreational runners, who often oscillate 10 to 15 centimeters per stride. Over 26.2 miles, that difference is thousands of feet of unnecessary climbing.

The body trades between vertical and horizontal movement. Push too hard into the ground and you'll go up more than forward—think of a pogo stick. But the ground push is also what creates propulsion. The art is in the angle: driving force backward and down, not straight down. When you see a runner with minimal vertical oscillation, you're seeing someone who's mastered the geometry of that push. Their foot strikes beneath their center of mass, the knee flexes just enough to absorb impact without collapsing, and the leg extends backward with precision.

Cadence plays into this. Faster turnover often means less time in the air, which means less vertical displacement. That's why so many coaches preach 180 steps per minute—it's not magic, but it tends to produce a stride pattern that minimizes bounce. You're spending less time floating and more time in contact with the ground, where you can actually generate forward momentum.

But some vertical oscillation is necessary. Zero would be impossible—you're not a wheel. And the spring-loading of tendons and muscles requires that brief aerial phase to work. When your foot leaves the ground, your Achilles tendon recoils, your calf releases stored elastic energy, and you're propelled into the next stride. That's the stretch-shortening cycle at work, and it needs clearance to function.

Where you feel inefficient oscillation is in the fatigue. Late in a marathon, when form degrades, runners start bouncing more. The core weakens, the hip drive shortens, and suddenly you're spending energy on vertical motion your legs are too tired to control. You can feel it—that sense of working harder but moving slower, of pushing into the ground and getting lift instead of speed.

The fix isn't to consciously push yourself down. It's to run tall, engage the core, and let the foot strike land quietly. Excessive oscillation is loud. If you're slapping the pavement, you're converting force into noise and bounce instead of forward motion. The quieter your footfalls, the more likely your vertical oscillation is under control.

Kipchoge floats forward. The rest of us try to minimize the float upward.
