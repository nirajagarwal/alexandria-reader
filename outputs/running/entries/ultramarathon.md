# The Ultramarathon

At mile 62 of a 100-mile race, your glycogen stores depleted hours ago, your quads feel like they're tearing with each downhill step, and you've been awake for fifteen hours. You're not running anymore—not really. It's more like a controlled stumble, a forward fall barely caught with each footstrike. This is ultramarathon distance, where the body enters territory it wasn't designed for and must improvise survival.

Anything beyond the marathon's 26.2 miles qualifies as ultra, though most races start at 50 kilometers and stretch to 100 miles or beyond. Some take place on roads, but most wind through mountains, deserts, or forests—the Western States 100 through the Sierra Nevada, the Ultra-Trail du Mont-Blanc through the Alps, the Badwater 135 across Death Valley in midsummer. These aren't extensions of marathon running. They're fundamentally different events requiring different physiology, different mental architecture, different fuel.

The marathon depletes glycogen but can be run primarily on stored carbohydrate if you're efficient. Ultras burn through those stores in the first few hours. Your body shifts to fat oxidation—a slower, steadier burn that can sustain effort for days. Elite ultrarunners have trained their bodies to use fat at higher intensities than most runners can manage. They're metabolically flexible, switching between fuel sources as glycogen rises and falls. During the race, they consume thousands of calories—gels, bars, real food from aid stations. But absorption is compromised by reduced blood flow to the gut. Many runners end up calorie-deficient despite eating constantly.

The pain accumulates differently too. In a marathon, you can push through discomfort for an hour, maybe two. In a 24-hour ultra, that strategy fails by mile 30. Ultrarunners learn to exist within pain rather than override it—adjusting pace, changing gait, walking the uphills, finding sustainable rhythms. The body breaks down: blisters open and weep, toenails blacken, quads develop rhabdomyolysis. But if you can keep the damage sub-catastrophic, you keep moving.

Sleep deprivation enters around hour 18. Your brain's prefrontal cortex—responsible for decision-making and motivation—begins to fade. Hallucinations appear: shadows become animals, trees become people waiting on the trail. Some runners microsleep while moving, catching seconds of rest mid-stride. The mind has to stay functional enough to navigate, to eat, to avoid hypothermia or heatstroke, to distinguish actual problems from normal ultra-distance suffering.

Courtney Dauwalter won the 2023 Western States by over 90 minutes, running the final miles with a stress fracture. Yiannis Kouros ran 188 miles in 24 hours, a record that's stood since 1997. These performances aren't about speed. They're about maintaining function when every system is compromised, when the body has moved past its design specifications and into pure duration. At some point in every ultra, you're not running toward the finish. You're just refusing to stop.
