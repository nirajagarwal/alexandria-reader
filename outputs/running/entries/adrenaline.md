# Adrenaline

The gun fires and your heart lurches—not metaphorically, literally. Before your conscious mind registers the sound, before you've decided to start running, your adrenal glands have already dumped epinephrine into your bloodstream. Within two seconds, your heart rate spikes, your pupils dilate, and blood shunts away from your digestive system toward your muscles. You are running before you know you're running.

Adrenaline—technically epinephrine when it's in your blood, though the terms are often used interchangeably—is the body's emergency broadcast system. When you toe the line at a race, when you spot someone ahead you want to catch, when you push into oxygen debt on the final lap, the adrenal medulla releases it in bursts. It binds to beta-receptors in your heart, increasing both rate and contractility. Your cardiac output can jump 50 percent in seconds. Blood vessels in your muscles dilate while those in your skin and gut constrict. Your liver dumps glucose. Your airways relax, pulling in more oxygen per breath.

This is the fight-or-flight response, refined over millions of years for moments of acute danger, now repurposed for a 5K. The system doesn't distinguish between a predator and a personal record. Both trigger the same cascade: hypothalamus signals the sympathetic nervous system, which activates the adrenal glands, which flood the body with the hormone that makes everything faster.

But adrenaline is metabolically expensive and physiologically unsustainable. Race too hard early and you burn through readily available glucose, leaving yourself depleted when you need it most. The spike in heart rate and breathing can exceed what your aerobic system can support, forcing you into oxygen debt you'll spend the rest of the race paying back. Elite runners learn to manage this—they control the surge, let adrenaline sharpen without overwhelming, use it strategically rather than riding it thoughtlessly.

There's also chronic adrenaline: the low-grade elevation that comes from overtraining, insufficient recovery, or treating every workout like a race. When stress becomes constant, when you're always pushing, the system that should activate episodically stays switched on. Heart rate variability drops. Sleep quality degrades. Performance plateaus or declines. Your body remains vigilant when it should be recovering.

This is why easy runs need to stay easy—not just for muscular recovery but for hormonal balance. The body needs periods where adrenaline isn't coursing through it, where the parasympathetic nervous system dominates and repair happens. The hard days work because of the easy days. The adrenaline hits hardest when it's not always there.

Standing on the start line, heart already racing before you've taken a step, you're experiencing chemistry older than running itself. The same molecule that helped your ancestors escape danger now helps you chase time.
