# The 80/20 Rule

Your easy runs probably aren't easy enough. The instinct when you start getting serious about running is to push harder more often—make every workout count, prove your commitment through suffering. But the bodies of elite runners tell a different story. When exercise physiologist Stephen Seiler analyzed the training patterns of Olympic-level endurance athletes across multiple sports, he found something counterintuitive: the faster they were, the more time they spent going slow.

The pattern was consistent across disciplines. World-class runners, cyclists, rowers, and cross-country skiers did roughly 80 percent of their training at low intensity and only 20 percent at moderate to high intensity. Not 70/30. Not 50/50. The ratio was remarkably uniform: four easy days for every hard one.

The physiology explains why. When you run easy—conversational pace, heart rate in Zone 2, effort that feels almost absurdly gentle—you're building the aerobic base that underpins all endurance performance. Mitochondria multiply. Capillary networks expand. The heart's stroke volume increases. Fat oxidation improves. These adaptations happen during low-intensity work, and they require volume. You need hours of easy running to trigger the cellular changes that make you faster.

Hard running builds different systems. Lactate threshold work and VO2 max intervals stress the anaerobic machinery, teach your body to clear lactate more efficiently, push your ceiling higher. These sessions are crucial, but they're also metabolically expensive. They damage muscle fibers, stress the nervous system, demand recovery. Do them too often and you're constantly digging out of a hole, never fully adapting before the next stress arrives.

The mistake recreational runners make is running the middle ground. Their easy runs are too hard—pace creeping up, heart rate drifting into Zone 3, effort that's neither restorative nor sufficiently intense to drive adaptation. Their hard runs aren't hard enough—threshold pace when they should be doing intervals, tempo effort when they should be truly easy. They end up in a gray zone: too tired to go hard, too fast to build the aerobic base.

Seiler's research showed that polarized training—extreme separation between easy and hard—produces better results than threshold-focused or high-volume moderate-intensity training. The body needs contrast. It needs the long, slow runs that feel like you're barely working and the short, intense efforts that leave you gasping. The space between doesn't do much.

So your easy runs should feel easy. You should be able to talk in full sentences. Your breathing should be relaxed. It should feel like you could go forever, because in a sense, you're training your body to do exactly that—building the aerobic machinery that will support you when the race actually matters. And your hard days should be genuinely hard: intervals that hurt, tempos that demand focus, sessions you need to recover from.

The discipline isn't in grinding through every run. It's in holding back four days so you can absolutely empty yourself on the fifth.
