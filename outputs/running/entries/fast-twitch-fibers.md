# Fast-Twitch Fibers

You feel them in the final fifty meters of an 800, when the legs stop responding to what the brain is demanding. That's fast-twitch muscle fibers—the sprinting machinery—running out of fuel. They contract harder and faster than their slow-twitch counterparts, but they fatigue in seconds. They're what makes a cheetah hit seventy miles per hour and what makes it stop after twenty seconds.

Muscle fibers come in types, determined by the version of myosin they express—the molecular motor that makes muscles contract. Type I fibers, slow-twitch, are packed with mitochondria and capillaries. They run on oxygen, contract steadily, and can work for hours. Type II fibers are the sprinters: fewer mitochondria, less oxidative capacity, but they generate force fast. Type IIa are hybrids—relatively fatigue-resistant, trainable toward endurance. Type IIx are pure speed: maximum power, minimal endurance.

The distribution is largely genetic. Elite sprinters have up to 80 percent fast-twitch fibers in their legs. Distance runners tilt the opposite direction—Eliud Kipchoge's muscles are probably 70 to 80 percent slow-twitch. You're born with a baseline ratio, and it shifts only marginally with training. Interval work can convert some IIx to IIa—making them slightly more fatigue-resistant—but you can't transform a sprinter's engine into a marathoner's, or vice versa.

Fast-twitch fibers recruit differently. During an easy run, your body activates slow-twitch first, conserving the fast-twitch for when they're needed. But in a sprint or a finishing kick, motor neurons fire in sequence: slow-twitch, then IIa, then IIx. The harder you push, the deeper into the roster you go. That's why the last hundred meters of a 5K feels so different from the first—you're running on a different fuel system, a different fiber type entirely.

The problem is depletion. Fast-twitch fibers rely on phosphocreatine and anaerobic glycolysis for energy—systems that produce power explosively but deplete in under two minutes. This is why 400-meter runners describe the final straight as suffocating: they've exhausted their phosphocreatine, flooded their muscles with lactate, and the IIx fibers are shutting down. The body is built for one all-out effort, not sustained speed.

In distance running, fast-twitch fibers are the finishing weapon. Slow-twitch carries you through twenty-six miles. Fast-twitch closes the last four hundred meters when someone makes a move. That's why elite marathoners drill speed work—not to change their fiber type, but to teach their fast-twitch fibers to fire when fatigued, to recruit them efficiently when slow-twitch alone won't hold the pace. The best closers aren't necessarily the fastest sprinters. They're the ones who've trained their fast-twitch to engage when everything else is empty.
