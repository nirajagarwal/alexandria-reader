**Fast-Twitch Fibers**

The sprinter in lane four is coiled at the blocks, and inside her gastrocnemius—the diamond-shaped calf muscle visible even at rest—millions of type II muscle fibers wait. Fast-twitch fibers. When the gun fires, they'll contract with such force and speed that she'll accelerate from zero to over twenty miles per hour in less than two seconds. These fibers don't just work faster than their slow-twitch cousins; they work differently, built for power over endurance, explosion over economy.

Fast-twitch fibers come in two varieties: type IIa and type IIx (sometimes called IIb in animal studies). Type IIa fibers are the bridge between worlds—faster than slow-twitch, but with some aerobic capacity. They can sustain moderate power for several minutes. Type IIx fibers are the true sprinters: maximum force, maximum speed, minimum endurance. They fatigue within seconds. A 100-meter sprinter might have seventy percent fast-twitch fibers. A marathon runner might have thirty percent.

The difference is structural. Fast-twitch fibers are thicker, packed with more contractile proteins—actin and myosin—arranged in dense, parallel bundles. They contain fewer mitochondria than slow-twitch fibers because they don't rely primarily on oxygen for fuel. Instead, they burn through stored ATP and phosphocreatine, then shift to anaerobic glycolysis—breaking down glucose without oxygen, generating lactate as a byproduct. This system produces energy quickly but inefficiently. It's a metabolic sprint.

The calcium release that triggers contraction happens faster in fast-twitch fibers. Their sarcoplasmic reticulum—the internal network that stores and releases calcium—is more developed, with faster pumps. The myosin heads themselves move more rapidly, pulling on actin filaments at higher speeds. Everything about these fibers favors velocity. A slow-twitch fiber might complete a contraction cycle in 100 milliseconds. A fast-twitch fiber can do it in 25.

Here's where genetics matter most in running. You're born with a certain distribution of fiber types, determined largely by your DNA. Studies of identical twins show nearly identical fiber-type composition. Elite sprinters and elite marathoners inhabit different ends of this spectrum, and no amount of training will fundamentally change it. You can't convert slow-twitch to fast-twitch, or vice versa.

But training does shift the subtypes. The IIx fibers—the most powerful but least sustainable—can convert to IIa fibers with endurance training. Marathon training transforms some of your explosive power into moderate power with better endurance. This is why sprinters who take extended breaks from training often feel sharper when they return: some IIa fibers have reverted to IIx during rest. The body maintains what it uses.

Carl Lewis, warming up for the 100 meters at the 1988 Olympics, would have felt his fast-twitch fibers priming—phosphocreatine stores full, myosin heads ready. The race lasted 9.92 seconds. His fast-twitch fibers, perfectly trained for exactly that duration of maximum effort, fired at the edge of human capability. By the finish line, they were depleted. But they'd done their job: transform stored chemical energy into explosive motion, faster than the human body does anything else.
