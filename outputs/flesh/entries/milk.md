# Milk

You stop digesting milk. Most humans do, around age five. The gene that codes for lactase—the enzyme that breaks down lactose—switches off after weaning in roughly 65% of the global population. This is normal mammalian development. You're not supposed to drink milk as an adult. The people who can are the mutants.

The mutation emerged around 7,500 years ago in Northern Europe, then independently in East Africa and the Arabian Peninsula. A single nucleotide change kept the lactase gene active into adulthood. This conferred enough survival advantage in dairy-farming populations that it spread rapidly. Today, most Northern Europeans, some Africans, and parts of the Middle East and South Asia maintain lactase production. But most of humanity—including the majority of East Asians, West Africans, and Indigenous peoples—does not. Their guts treat milk as an indigestible foreign substance, which it is.

The symptoms of lactose intolerance are straightforward: undigested lactose reaches the colon, where bacteria ferment it, producing gas, bloating, cramps, and diarrhea. The severity varies by the amount consumed and individual gut microbiome. But the framing matters. This isn't a disorder requiring treatment. It's the species baseline. The pharmaceutical response—lactase pills, lactose-free milk—treats normal development as a deficiency. Meanwhile, the mutation that allows adult milk consumption correlates with specific health risks: higher rates of certain cancers, possibly from the growth factors in milk designed to turn a 65-pound calf into a 600-pound cow.

Milk's composition reveals what it's for. Cow's milk is optimized for rapid skeletal growth in a ruminant herbivore. It's high in calcium, phosphorus, and proteins that promote bone formation and muscle mass. Human milk is different—lower in protein, higher in lactose, with oligosaccharides that feed beneficial gut bacteria. The nutrients are tailored to brain development and immune system priming, not doubling body weight every few months. Drinking the milk of another species means consuming a nutrient profile designed for a different developmental program. It works—humans can metabolize it if they have the enzyme—but it's off-label use.

The calcium question is the most contested. The dairy industry has successfully positioned milk as essential for bone health. The research is less clear. Countries with the highest dairy consumption don't have the lowest fracture rates; often it's the reverse. The Harvard Nurses' Health Study, following 77,000 women for a decade, found no protective effect of milk consumption on fracture risk. Some studies suggest high dairy intake increases fracture risk, possibly through milk's acidifying effect requiring calcium mobilization from bone to buffer pH. Vitamin D and weight-bearing exercise appear more protective than dietary calcium.

But milk is nutritionally dense. An eight-ounce glass provides significant protein, calcium, vitamin D (if fortified), riboflavin, and vitamin B12. For populations with limited access to diverse food sources, dairy provides concentrated nutrition. The issue isn't whether milk is nutritious—it is—but whether it's necessary or optimal for adults, particularly those whose ancestors didn't drink it and whose bodies weren't selected for processing it.

The industrial side changes things. Modern milk comes from cows bred to produce five times the volume of mid-20th century cows. They're fed grain, not grass, altering the fatty acid profile. They're given antibiotics, which pass into milk. They're often treated with recombinant bovine growth hormone (rBGH) to increase production, raising levels of insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) in milk. IGF-1 promotes cell growth, which is useful for calves and concerning for humans, given its association with cancer promotion. The European Union banned rBGH in 1999; the U.S. still allows it.

Homogenization and pasteurization are other interventions. Pasteurization kills pathogens but also beneficial bacteria and denatures some proteins. Raw milk advocates argue for the microbiome benefits; public health officials point to the listeria, salmonella, and E. coli risks. The CDC data shows 150 hospitalizations per year from raw milk in the U.S., but that's from a tiny fraction of the population consuming it. The actual risk per serving is debated. Homogenization breaks down fat globules so cream doesn't separate, but it also allows certain proteins to pass into the bloodstream more easily, potentially increasing immune response.

What you should know: if you digest milk comfortably, it's nutritionally useful. If you don't, stop drinking it—the "recommended daily servings" are written for bodies that kept a mutation yours didn't. Get calcium from leafy greens, sardines, fortified foods. Get vitamin D from sun or supplements. The idea that adults need milk is marketing meeting a genetic accident, not human nutritional requirements.

The practical test is simple: stop consuming dairy for two weeks. If you feel better—less bloating, clearer skin, better digestion—your body was telling you something. If nothing changes, and you're one of the 35% whose ancestors herded cattle through Northern Europe's pastures, drink milk. But understand that you're exercising a mutation, not meeting a biological imperative. You're using your body's ability to do something most humans can't, with a substance designed for a different animal's infant. It works, until it doesn't.
