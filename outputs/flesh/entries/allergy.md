# Allergy

Your immune system is designed to attack foreign invaders—bacteria, viruses, parasites. An allergy is what happens when this system misidentifies harmless substances as threats and launches a full-scale assault. The reaction isn't proportional. It's not even useful. You're not being protected from pollen or peanuts or cat dander—you're being harmed by your own defenses. Understanding why this happens, and why it's becoming more common, reveals something important about what modern environments do to ancient immune systems.

The mechanism is antibody confusion. When you're first exposed to an allergen, your body produces IgE antibodies—the immune system's heavy artillery, normally reserved for parasitic worms. These antibodies attach to mast cells throughout your body, waiting. On subsequent exposures, the allergen binds to these primed antibodies, and the mast cells explode, releasing histamine and other inflammatory chemicals. Your blood vessels dilate, your airways constrict, your skin swells. This is a defense strategy against organisms that burrow through tissue. Against tree pollen, it's friendly fire.

The most telling finding is that allergies are recent and rising. In 1900, hay fever was rare enough to be newsworthy. By 2000, it affected one in four people in developed countries. Food allergies have tripled in children since the 1990s. This isn't genetic drift—populations don't evolve that fast. This is environmental change triggering ancient immune responses in new ways.

The leading explanation is the hygiene hypothesis, first proposed by epidemiologist David Strachan in 1989 after noticing that children with more older siblings had fewer allergies. The idea: early childhood exposure to microbes trains the immune system to distinguish real threats from harmless substances. In sanitized modern environments, that training doesn't happen. The immune system, lacking real parasites to fight, turns its weaponry on pollen, dust mites, and food proteins. Supporting evidence is everywhere. Amish children, who grow up around livestock and bacteria-rich environments, have dramatically lower allergy rates than genetically similar Hutterite children raised with modern hygiene. Children born by C-section, missing the microbial transfer of vaginal birth, have higher allergy rates. Early antibiotic use, which wipes out gut bacteria, increases allergy risk.

The old-friends hypothesis, developed by Graham Rook in the 2000s, refines this. It's not about general dirtiness—it's about specific microbial exposures that co-evolved with humans. We need early contact with particular bacteria, helminths, and other organisms that shaped our immune development over millennia. Modern life has severed these relationships faster than the immune system can adapt.

What's strange is the specificity. Why peanuts and not chickpeas? Why birch pollen and not pine? Some of it is molecular mimicry—proteins in different substances share structural features. People allergic to birch pollen often react to apples and hazelnuts because the proteins are similar enough to confuse antibodies. But there's also timing. The rise of peanut allergies corresponds with public health advice in the 1990s to avoid giving peanuts to infants. The UK and US, which adopted this guidance, saw skyrocketing rates. Israel, where peanut-based Bamba snacks are common infant food, has one-tenth the rate. When researchers finally tested this in the LEAP study, published in 2015 by Gideon Lack and colleagues, they found that early peanut introduction reduced allergies by 81%. The immune system needs to meet potential allergens during a critical window, or it defaults to treating them as threats.

Oral immunotherapy works on this principle. You eat tiny, gradually increasing amounts of what you're allergic to, retraining your immune system to tolerate it. It works but takes months of careful dosing. The fact that it works at all reveals that allergies aren't permanent immune decisions—they're learned responses that can be unlearned.

Anaphylaxis is where this goes catastrophic. Blood pressure drops, airways close, organs fail—all within minutes. This is the IgE response at maximum intensity, mast cells throughout the body degranulating simultaneously. An EpiPen delivers epinephrine, which forces blood vessels to constrict and airways to open, buying time to get to a hospital. The speed matters because anaphylaxis can kill faster than you can get help. The anxiety people with severe allergies live with is proportional to the stakes.

The rise in severe food allergies has changed how public spaces operate. Schools ban peanuts. Restaurants list every ingredient. This isn't coddling—it's responding to a real epidemiological shift. Something about modern life has reprogrammed children's immune systems to treat common foods as deadly threats. The percentage of children with food allergies requiring emergency treatment has more than doubled since 2007.

What you do with this information depends on your circumstances. If you have young children, early diverse food introduction now has strong evidence behind it. If you have allergies, avoidance is still primary—immunotherapy is promising but not universally available. If you have severe allergies, you carry an EpiPen and you check ingredients obsessively, because your immune system has marked certain molecules for destruction and will act on that order faster than conscious thought.

The broader implication is that your immune system is not fixed hardware. It's adaptive tissue shaped by what it encounters, especially early in life. Modern hygiene has reduced infectious disease dramatically but has also deprived developing immune systems of the microbial context they evolved to expect. The result is a generation of bodies treating the world as more dangerous than it is, launching defenses against shadows. You can't reverse modernity, but you can understand that your allergies aren't personal failure or bad luck—they're a mismatch between ancient immune programming and a sanitized world it wasn't designed for.
