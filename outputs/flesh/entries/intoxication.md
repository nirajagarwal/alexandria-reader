# Intoxication

Your liver processes alcohol at roughly one drink per hour. This is not a guideline—it's a metabolic ceiling. Drink faster than this and ethanol accumulates in your bloodstream. The feeling people call "drunk" is your brain operating with a depressant flooding its synapses, slowing neural transmission, disrupting balance centers, and interfering with the formation of new memories. The social ritual of drinking is people deliberately inducing temporary brain damage, then navigating the world with compromised cognitive function.

The mechanism is simpler than most people realize. Ethanol is a small, fat-soluble molecule that crosses the blood-brain barrier within minutes. Once there, it enhances the effect of GABA, the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter, while simultaneously blocking glutamate, the primary excitatory neurotransmitter. The result is a general damping of neural activity. Early in intoxication, this hits the prefrontal cortex first—the region handling judgment, impulse control, and social inhibition. You feel relaxed, confident, talkative. What you're experiencing is executive function shutting down.

As blood alcohol concentration rises, the effects cascade. The cerebellum, which coordinates movement, starts faltering. Balance becomes difficult. Fine motor control deteriorates. Then the hippocampus, responsible for forming new long-term memories, begins to fail. This is where blackouts occur—not from passing out, but from the brain's inability to encode events. The person is awake, moving, talking, but nothing is being written to memory. Researcher Aaron White at Duke demonstrated this in the early 2000s: blackouts can occur at blood alcohol levels as low as 0.16% in people who drink quickly. The drinker experiences no warning. The memory system just stops writing.

The liver's metabolism of alcohol follows zero-order kinetics, which means it processes a fixed amount per hour regardless of how much is in your system. That's unusual. Most drugs follow first-order kinetics—the more you have, the faster you clear it. Not alcohol. The enzyme alcohol dehydrogenase converts ethanol to acetaldehyde at a constant rate, typically about 7 grams per hour—roughly the amount in a standard drink. This is why time is the only thing that sobers you up. Coffee, cold showers, food—none of these accelerate the clearance. They might make you feel more alert, but your blood alcohol concentration drops at the same fixed rate regardless.

Acetaldehyde, the first metabolic product, is more toxic than ethanol itself. It's a probable carcinogen and the primary driver of hangover symptoms. Normally, it's quickly converted to acetate by the enzyme aldehyde dehydrogenase. But in roughly 40% of East Asians, a genetic variant produces a sluggish version of this enzyme. Acetaldehyde accumulates, causing facial flushing, nausea, and rapid heartbeat. This is not an allergy—it's an evolutionary lottery that makes drinking acutely unpleasant. People with this variant who drink regularly have dramatically elevated rates of esophageal cancer. The mutation is protective at the population level; it's lethal if you override it.

The long-term effects are structural. Chronic alcohol use shrinks the brain. MRI studies show reduced volume in the frontal lobes and hippocampus, regions critical for decision-making and memory. Neurologist Adolf Pfefferbaum's work in the 1990s demonstrated this is dose-dependent: more drinking, more atrophy. Some recovery occurs with abstinence, but not complete. The brain adapts to chronic ethanol exposure by downregulating GABA receptors and upregulating glutamate receptors, trying to maintain normal function despite the constant depressant. Stop drinking abruptly, and you're left with an over-excited nervous system—tremors, anxiety, and in severe cases, seizures. Alcohol withdrawal can be fatal. Heroin withdrawal cannot.

The legal threshold for driving impairment is 0.08% blood alcohol concentration in most places, but measurable deficits begin far lower. Psychologist Herbert Moskowitz's meta-analysis in 2000 found significant impairment in divided attention tasks at 0.02%—one drink for many people. Reaction time slows, peripheral vision narrows, risk assessment degrades. The subjective feeling is confidence and competence. The objective reality is diminished capacity. This gap between felt and actual ability is one of the drug's more dangerous features.

Tolerance develops quickly and unevenly. Chronic drinkers can appear relatively functional at blood alcohol levels that would incapacitate occasional drinkers. This is behavioral tolerance—learned compensation. The liver also increases its metabolic capacity somewhat. But tolerance to respiratory depression, the mechanism of alcohol poisoning, develops more slowly. Heavy drinkers can reach lethal blood alcohol concentrations while still appearing conscious. The LD50 for alcohol is around 0.40%, but deaths occur lower. Respiratory arrest happens when brainstem circuits controlling breathing are sufficiently depressed. By the time someone is unconscious from alcohol, they may already be at dangerous levels. The gag reflex shuts down earlier. Aspiration of vomit kills people who might have survived the direct toxic effects.

The culture around drinking obscures this pharmacology. Social drinking is normalized; alcoholism is pathologized. But there's no metabolic difference between ethanol consumed socially and ethanol consumed alone. The dose makes the poison, and the brain doesn't distinguish between contexts. Every drink is a bolus of depressant crossing into your central nervous system, interfering with neurotransmission, and requiring hours of metabolic cleanup. The ritual feels benign because it's ubiquitous. The chemistry is indifferent to the setting.

You are operating a brain that evolved over millions of years with no significant ethanol exposure except from overripe fruit. You've introduced a molecule that disrupts the balance between excitation and inhibition across the entire nervous system. The pleasant early effects—reduced anxiety, increased sociability—are side effects of diminished executive control. The later effects—impaired judgment, memory loss, motor dysfunction—are the same mechanism progressing. There is no separate "good drunk" and "bad drunk." There's only increasing suppression of neural function, and where you stop on that continuum.

Knowing this doesn't require abstinence. But it should make you realize that intoxication is not a social state—it's a neurological one. You're choosing to impair your brain's function, temporarily and reversibly in most cases, but impairment nonetheless. The dose, the frequency, and the trajectory of the evening are all decisions you're making about how much you're willing to compromise your cognitive function and for how long. The only thing metabolizing you back to baseline is time, and every hour drunk is an hour spent with your brain operating in a degraded state. That's what intoxication is. Everything else is narrative.
