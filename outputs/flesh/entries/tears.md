# Tears

You cry three different kinds of tears, and only one of them means anything about emotion. Basal tears are always present, a thin film lubricating the cornea and washing away debris. Reflex tears flood out when you chop onions or get dust in your eye—volume production to flush irritants. Emotional tears are the outlier: the only bodily fluid produced by psychological state, released by feelings that have nothing to do with eye protection.

The composition differs. Basal tears are mostly water, salt, and antimicrobial proteins—functional maintenance fluid. Emotional tears contain higher concentrations of stress hormones: cortisol, prolactin, adrenocorticotropic hormone. This led psychologist William Frey to propose in the 1980s that crying serves as a stress-relief mechanism, literally excreting hormones that build up during emotional arousal. The theory is appealing but not proven. The amount of hormone removed is negligible compared to what's circulating in your bloodstream. If crying helps—and most people report that it does—the mechanism is more likely neural or social than chemical drainage.

Humans are the only animals that produce emotional tears. Other mammals have tear ducts, but they're strictly for corneal maintenance. Elephants don't weep at death. Dogs don't cry when separated from owners. The vocalization of distress is universal in social mammals; the liquid component is uniquely human. This suggests emotional tears evolved as a signal, not just a release valve. The face leaking fluid is harder to fake than words, and it announces vulnerability at a distance. Tears are readable: swollen eyes and wet cheeks communicate distress long after the episode ends.

The social function matters because crying alone feels different from crying with witnesses. Lab studies on emotional crying are difficult—hard to make someone genuinely cry on demand—but survey data consistently show that people feel worse after crying alone and better after crying with others. The relief isn't intrinsic to the tears; it comes from the response the tears elicit. Humans cry to be seen crying. That's not weakness or manipulation; it's how the system is designed. Emotional tears are a social tool that happens to involve the lacrimal glands.

Women cry more than men by every measure—frequency, duration, intensity. This isn't conditioning alone. Testosterone inhibits the lacrimal system; estrogen and prolactin facilitate it. Post-puberty, the gap widens. But culture compounds biology. Gender norms around crying vary sharply across societies, and those norms change behavior even when physiology stays constant. Men in cultures that permit male crying do cry more. The hardware differs slightly; the permission structure matters more.

Crocodile tears—the idiom for fake crying—comes from a medieval belief that crocodiles wept while eating their victims. Not true, but crocodiles do produce tears while feeding, not from remorse but from air pushed through sinuses during jaw work. The phrase stuck because humans intuitively understand that tears can be deployed. Some people can cry on command; most can't, but most can suppress tears when the situation demands it. The system is partially voluntary, which makes emotional tears more complicated as a signal. If tears can be faked or suppressed, their reliability as a distress indicator drops. That's probably why other signals matter: the reddening, the voice cracking, the facial muscles that contort in ways hard to consciously control. Tears are part of a package.

The inability to cry when you want to is its own problem. Dry eye syndrome—when basal tear production drops—causes constant discomfort, grittiness, sometimes sharp pain. It's more common with age, autoimmune conditions, screen use, and low-humidity environments. Worse, the reflex tears that flood out in response to the dryness are the wrong composition—too watery, not enough oil, they evaporate quickly and leave the problem unsolved. Treatment is artificial tears—not the emotional kind but the basal kind, the maintenance fluid your glands should produce but don't.

Emotional crying that won't come when it should might signal depression. Anhedonia—the inability to feel pleasure—often travels with an inability to cry. The lacrimal system still works, but the emotional trigger is numb. This is different from suppression. Suppression is choosing not to cry; anhedonia is the machinery not firing. People describe it as a blankness, a felt absence of feeling. Restoring the ability to cry is sometimes an early sign that antidepressants are working, before mood lifts. The tears return first.

The immediate trigger for emotional tears is still debated. The leading theory involves the autonomic nervous system—specifically the parasympathetic branch, which governs rest and recovery. Crying often follows peak arousal: the moment after the threat passes, the relief after bad news is delivered, the safety after fear. The body shifts from fight-or-flight to rest-and-digest, and the lacrimal glands activate as part of that parasympathetic surge. This explains why you often can't cry during the crisis, only after. The system needs to downshift first.

What you should know: tears are information your body broadcasts about internal states you might not fully perceive yourself. They're diagnostic. If you can't cry when grief or fear or relief should provoke it, that's worth attention—not moral failure, but a signal the system isn't responding. If you cry easily, that's not fragility; it's a functional stress response and a social signal working as designed. And if you feel better after crying, it's probably not the hormones you shed but the vulnerability you showed and the response you received. Tears are a social organ as much as a physical one. They work by being seen.
