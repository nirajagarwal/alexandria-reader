# The Cough

A cough is a spasm you don't control that travels at nearly the speed of sound. The air leaving your lungs during a forceful cough moves at roughly 500 miles per hour—faster than a category five hurricane, comparable to the takeoff speed of a commercial jet. This violence is the point. The cough exists to clear your airways with enough force to dislodge whatever shouldn't be there, and the mechanism that produces this force is older than speech, older than your capacity for voluntary breath control.

The reflex operates in three phases. First, you inhale deeply—an automatic preparation you can't skip even if you try to suppress the cough. Second, your vocal cords slam shut while your chest and abdominal muscles contract, building pressure in your lungs to levels that would burst a party balloon. Third, the vocal cords snap open and the pressurized air explodes outward. The entire sequence takes less than half a second. You can delay it briefly through conscious effort, but you can't stop it. The neural pathway runs through the medulla oblongata, the part of your brainstem that handles breathing and heartbeat—functions too critical to leave entirely under voluntary control.

What triggers this reflex reveals how the body prioritizes threats. Cough receptors cluster in specific locations: the larynx, the carina (where the trachea splits into bronchi), and the branching points of airways deeper in the lungs. These are choke points where foreign material is most likely to lodge and least likely to clear on its own. The receptors respond to mechanical pressure, chemical irritants, and inflammatory signals. Remarkably, the esophagus and stomach have cough receptors too, positioned to catch acid reflux before it reaches the airways. Some people cough chronically not from lung disease but from stomach acid triggering airway reflexes—the system defending against a threat that's real but misdirected.

The chronic cough research shows how a protective reflex can become its own problem. In chronic cough—defined as lasting more than eight weeks—the cough receptors become hypersensitive, firing in response to stimuli that wouldn't bother healthy airways. Perfume, cold air, talking, even laughing can trigger coughing fits. University of Manchester researchers led by Jacky Smith have shown this isn't psychological. The nerve fibers themselves change. They sprout new branches, increase receptor density, and lower their firing threshold. The technical term is "neuronal plasticity," but the practical term is a nervous system that's learned the wrong lesson. It treated a real threat so many times that it now treats everything as a threat.

This matters because the most common causes of chronic cough aren't lung infections but conditions that cause low-grade, persistent irritation: postnasal drip, acid reflux, and asthma. All three can exist without obvious symptoms beyond the cough itself. People cough for months thinking they have lingering bronchitis when they actually have stomach acid touching their vocal cords at night, or airway inflammation responding to allergens they've never noticed. The cough is accurate reporting of a problem, but identifying the problem requires looking beyond the lungs.

The social dimension of coughing is ancient. Darwin noted that coughing is one of the few reflexes that carries social meaning—it signals illness, draws attention, demands space. This is why suppressing a cough in public feels urgent even when it's medically pointless. Cough drops don't stop the reflex; they reduce the irritation that triggers it, mainly through local anesthetic effects and stimulation of saliva production. Menthol creates a cooling sensation that tricks the brain into perceiving clearer airways without actually opening them. You're not treating the cough—you're managing the social discomfort of coughing.

The tuberculosis epidemics of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries made the cough a matter of civic hygiene. Before germ theory was fully accepted, public health campaigns focused on coughing because it was visible, visceral, and obviously transmitted something. Spitting in public became illegal in many cities not because anyone understood bacteria but because sick people coughed up sputum and spat it on sidewalks where it dried and became breathable dust. The laws worked for the wrong reasons. The cough was the vector, but so was the shared air, the close quarters, the poverty that packed people into tenements without ventilation.

What you should understand is that a cough lasting more than three weeks demands investigation. Most coughs resolve when the infection that caused them clears. A cough that persists suggests either ongoing irritation or hypersensitized nerves, and both have causes worth finding. The reflex itself can't be trained away—it's hardwired—but the triggers can be identified and often removed. Chronic cough degrades sleep, strains relationships, fractures ribs in severe cases. It's not trivial, and it's not just something you have to live with.

The velocity matters too. When you cough near someone, you're not just expelling droplets—you're creating a turbulent cloud that travels farther and lingers longer than the droplets alone would. Fluid dynamics research shows that cough clouds can carry particles six feet in still air, farther if there's airflow. Covering your mouth reduces the distance but doesn't eliminate transmission; the particles are small enough to escape through fabric and around hands. The social distance of six feet emerged from cough physics before anyone was thinking about pandemics. Your body evolved this mechanism to protect your airways. It doesn't care what it does to everyone else's.
