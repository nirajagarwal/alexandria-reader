# The Teeth

Your teeth are the only part of your skeleton you're supposed to clean. This tells you something about how badly designed they are for the environment we've put them in. Bones are living tissue, constantly remodeling themselves, covered in regenerative cells. Teeth enamel is crystalline mineral—hardest substance in your body, and completely dead. Once the outer layer chips or dissolves, it's gone. You get two sets in a lifetime and then you're making do with what remains, which for most of human history meant very little by age forty.

The mismatch is dietary. For hundreds of thousands of years, human teeth handled coarse, fibrous foods that required extensive chewing. The mechanical action cleaned surfaces, the fiber scraped plaque, the lack of refined carbohydrates meant oral bacteria had little to ferment. Agriculture changed the game ten thousand years ago—grain-based diets, softer foods, eventually sugar. Tooth decay was rare in hunter-gatherer populations. It became epidemic with farming. The skeleton in your mouth is adapted for a menu you don't eat.

The decay process is chemical warfare. *Streptococcus mutans* and related bacteria metabolize sugars and starches, excreting lactic acid as waste. The acid dissolves hydroxyapatite crystals in the enamel, creating microscopic pits. The bacteria colonize the pits, protected from brushing and saliva. They secrete sticky polymers that build plaque—a biofilm fortress where pH drops low enough to keep dissolving mineral. A cavity is a bacterial infection that eats through rock.

This matters because the standard understanding gets the sequence wrong. People think sugar directly rots teeth. It doesn't. Sugar feeds bacteria that produce acid. The frequency of exposure matters more than the amount. Sipping soda all day keeps the acid bath going. Eating a candy bar in one sitting gives your saliva time to neutralize pH and remineralize enamel between meals. Your mouth is an ecosystem with repair mechanisms, but only when given recovery time.

Fluoride works by changing the crystal structure. When fluoride ions replace some of the hydroxyl groups in hydroxyapatite, you get fluorapatite—more resistant to acid dissolution. This isn't controversial science; it's materials chemistry. The reason fluoride in water supplies reduced cavities by 25 percent, according to CDC data, is that it's present during remineralization, when damaged enamel gets patched. Topical fluoride from toothpaste is more effective than systemic fluoride from water, but both help. The dose makes it useful rather than toxic—one of those public health interventions that worked well enough to become invisible.

But even fluoride can't fix the architecture. Human teeth are too crowded. Our jaws shortened as our brains expanded; there's an inverse relationship between skull size and jaw projection across hominin evolution. The result is thirty-two teeth trying to fit in space for twenty-eight. Wisdom teeth impact because there's no room. Orthodontics is largely about managing a design flaw—applying prolonged pressure to shift teeth into positions they didn't evolve to occupy, so they can function in jaws that didn't evolve to hold them.

The gums are where the skeleton becomes vulnerable. Teeth sit in bone but protrude through soft tissue—the only place bone breaches the body's exterior. The gum seal around each tooth is a weak point. Bacteria that slip below the gumline trigger an immune response. The inflammation is supposed to clear infection, but chronic inflammation destroys the periodontal ligament and resorbs the jaw bone. Advanced periodontitis is an autoimmune problem as much as an infection—your body dismantling the anchors of your teeth because it can't eliminate the invaders.

This connects to systemic health in ways dentistry historically ignored. Periodontal disease correlates with cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and adverse pregnancy outcomes. The mechanism is inflammatory mediators entering the bloodstream, though causation is still debated. But the mouth is not a sealed system. Bacteria from oral infections can be cultured from atherosclerotic plaques. What's happening in your gums is happening in communication with the rest of your body.

The practical implication is that teeth require active maintenance against an environment they weren't designed for. Brushing disrupts biofilm before it mineralizes into calculus—which you can't remove yourself. Flossing cleans surfaces the brush doesn't reach, especially the subgingival spaces where periodontitis starts. The twice-daily routine is countering evolutionary mismatch, using mechanical cleaning to substitute for dietary fiber and low sugar availability.

The deeper problem is that teeth don't hurt until damage is advanced. Enamel has no nerve endings. Dentin underneath does, but by the time you feel sensitivity, the decay is through the enamel and approaching the pulp. Root canals become necessary because bacterial infection has reached living tissue inside the tooth. The pain that finally sends people to the dentist is late-stage; the damage was accumulating silently for months or years.

Tooth loss has cascading effects. Bone resorbs when there's no tooth root to stress it—the jaw literally shrinks. Adjacent teeth drift into gaps, changing bite mechanics. The face shortens vertically, aging appearance. Implants work by providing a titanium root that osseointegrates with jaw bone, maintaining the mechanical stimulus that keeps bone from dissolving. But they cost thousands per tooth, and the underlying issue is that you don't get a third set.

What this means for how you live: your teeth are archaeology happening in real time. They record diet, hygiene, environmental exposure, and systemic health. They're also non-renewable capital. The enamel you have at twenty is the most you'll ever have; it only degrades from there. Frequency of acid exposure matters more than intensity. Mechanical cleaning matters because bacterial biofilms are what drive both decay and gum disease. And what happens in your mouth is not isolated from what happens in your arteries, your blood sugar, your inflammatory state.

You're living in a body adapted for a different world, and your teeth are where the mismatch is most obvious—the only bones you have to scrub.
