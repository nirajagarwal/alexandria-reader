# Nausea

Your brain makes you sick. Not metaphorically—mechanically. Nausea is a protective response gone haywire, a defense system that triggers when the brain detects danger, real or imagined. The stomach itself has no opinion on whether you should vomit. The command comes from a cluster of neurons in the brainstem called the area postrema, which monitors blood chemistry and integrates signals from the gut, inner ear, and higher brain regions. When these signals conflict or suggest poisoning, infection, or some other threat, the area postrema activates the vomiting center. You feel sick because your brain has decided you should.

The system evolved to handle toxins. Eat something poisonous and the gut sends distress signals upward. The area postrema, located outside the blood-brain barrier where it can directly sample blood contents, detects chemical markers of danger. It triggers nausea first—a warning shot. If the threat persists, vomiting follows, forcibly ejecting stomach contents before more poison absorbs. This is useful if you've eaten bad shellfish. It's less useful when the threat is imaginary or when the cure is worse than the problem.

Motion sickness reveals the system's fundamental flaw. Neurophysiologist James Reason showed in the 1970s that nausea occurs when sensory inputs conflict—when your eyes say you're stationary but your inner ear reports movement, or vice versa. Reading in a car creates this mismatch. Your visual field is stable (the book) but your vestibular system detects acceleration. The brain interprets this conflict as evidence of poisoning—because in evolutionary history, sensory confusion often meant neurotoxin exposure. So it makes you vomit, even though you're not poisoned. You're just riding in a vehicle that didn't exist when this system evolved.

The area postrema sits in the dorsal brainstem, exposed to bloodborne signals precisely so it can detect toxins before they cross into protected brain tissue. This is elegant design for poison detection. But it means anything in your bloodstream can trigger nausea—chemotherapy drugs, hormones, metabolic waste products. Morning sickness in pregnancy is likely the area postrema reacting to rising hCG levels, interpreting a normal hormonal signal as potential threat. Margie Profet proposed in 1992 that morning sickness protects the developing fetus by making the mother avoid foods during the period of organogenesis. The data are mixed, but the mechanism is clear: the brain decides the chemical environment is suspicious and makes you too sick to eat.

Anticipatory nausea demonstrates how much of this is neural prediction rather than gastric reality. Cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy often develop nausea before treatment starts—sometimes triggered just by entering the clinic. The area postrema can be conditioned like Pavlov's dogs. Oncologist Gary Morrow documented this in the 1980s: patients learn the association between place and poison, and their brains preemptively activate the vomiting center. The anticipation itself becomes sickening. This is why anti-nausea drugs work better when given before chemotherapy rather than after symptoms start—you're trying to prevent the conditioning, not just block the response.

The neurotransmitter system behind nausea is surprisingly specific. The area postrema is dense with receptors for serotonin and dopamine, which is why drugs blocking these receptors (ondansetron, metoclopramide) are effective anti-emetics. But cannabis works better for some people, acting on different receptors entirely—CB1 receptors in the brainstem. This is not folk medicine. Controlled trials show synthetic THC reduces chemotherapy-induced nausea when other drugs fail. The system has multiple trigger points because it evolved to respond to multiple threats: toxins, infection, motion, fear. Block one pathway and others remain active.

Chronic nausea is where the system becomes actively destructive. Gastroparesis—delayed stomach emptying—creates persistent nausea because the gut sends continuous distress signals to the brainstem. The stomach isn't empty but isn't processing food normally, often due to diabetes damaging the vagus nerve. The area postrema interprets this ambiguous signal as danger and maintains nausea for months. People lose weight, become malnourished, develop anxiety around eating. The protective response has become the primary problem.

Anxiety and nausea form a feedback loop through the brain-gut axis. The gut has its own nervous system—the enteric nervous system, sometimes called the "second brain"—which communicates bidirectionally with the central nervous system via the vagus nerve. Stress activates the sympathetic nervous system, which slows gut motility and increases gastric acid. The gut signals distress upward. The brain interprets this as threat and amplifies the stress response. You feel sick because you're anxious, and anxious because you feel sick. Breaking this loop requires addressing the neural interpretation, not just the gastric symptoms.

What you can do with this: understand that nausea is a brain state, not just a stomach state. Ginger works—compounds like gingerol affect serotonin receptors in the gut and reduce signal transmission to the brainstem. Deep breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system, which calms gut motility and reduces distress signaling. Looking at the horizon during motion sickness provides visual confirmation of movement, resolving the sensory conflict that triggered nausea in the first place.

The larger implication is that your protective systems don't know about cars, chemotherapy, or chronic anxiety. They're running old software in new conditions. The area postrema is trying to save your life, but it's often fighting phantoms—sensory confusion, conditioned associations, stress signals misinterpreted as toxins. You feel sick not because something is wrong with your stomach but because your brain has made a prediction error. Understanding this doesn't eliminate nausea, but it shifts the target. The problem isn't the gut. It's the surveillance system reading normal conditions as threats, and you need to address the interpretation, not just the symptom.
