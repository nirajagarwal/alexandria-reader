# The Heart Attack

The classic heart attack story goes like this: plaque builds up in a coronary artery over decades, narrowing the passage until blood can barely squeeze through, and then one day the narrowing becomes complete blockage and heart muscle dies. Gradual accumulation leading to sudden catastrophe. The cardiologist's job is to find the narrowings and fix them before they close completely.

Except that's not usually what happens. The plaques that kill you are often not the ones causing symptoms. In the 1980s, cardiologist Erling Falk examined hearts from people who'd died of heart attacks and found something strange: most fatal blockages occurred at sites that had less than 70 percent narrowing before rupture. The dangerous plaques weren't the big stable obstructions cardiologists were stenting—they were smaller, softer deposits with thin fibrous caps. These vulnerable plaques could sit quietly for years at 40 or 50 percent obstruction, barely visible on an angiogram, then suddenly rupture, triggering a clot that blocked the artery completely within minutes.

This discovery overturned the plumbing model of heart disease. The problem isn't just the size of the obstruction. It's the stability of the plaque. A large, calcified plaque can be relatively safe. A smaller plaque with a thin cap and a lipid-rich core is a time bomb. You can have a normal stress test one month and a massive heart attack the next because the test measures flow limitation, not plaque vulnerability.

The rupture mechanism works like this: the plaque's fibrous cap thins over time, weakened by inflammatory cells—macrophages that digest the cap's structural proteins. Mechanical stress from blood pressure and heart contractions bends the cap repeatedly. Eventually it tears. When it does, the blood contacts the plaque's interior, which is intensely clotting-prone. A thrombus forms in seconds, and if it doesn't break up quickly, the artery occludes. Blood flow stops. Downstream heart muscle begins dying within twenty minutes.

This is why aspirin works as prevention. It doesn't shrink plaques or improve blood flow. It makes platelets less sticky, reducing the chance that a ruptured plaque will form a fatal clot. The effect is modest—maybe 20 to 30 percent risk reduction—but it's targeting the actual mechanism of sudden coronary death, not the visible narrowings.

The inflammatory component matters more than people realize. Heart disease is not purely a disease of cholesterol accumulation. It's a disease of chronic inflammation in the arterial wall. High LDL cholesterol provides the raw material, but inflammatory processes drive the formation and destabilization of plaques. This is why infections, autoimmune diseases, and chronic stress all increase heart attack risk independently of cholesterol levels. Virologist Sergio Litvak proposed in the 1990s that certain viral infections might trigger the inflammatory cascade that destabilizes plaques—herpes viruses, cytomegalovirus, even the flu. The data remain contested, but seasonal flu increases heart attack rates for weeks afterward, not because of the flu itself but because of what the immune response does to vulnerable plaques.

The timing of heart attacks is not random. They peak on Monday mornings, particularly in the first hour after waking. The circadian surge of cortisol and adrenaline raises blood pressure and heart rate just as you're shifting position, increasing mechanical stress on plaques at the moment when your blood is most prone to clotting. Sleep deprivation amplifies this effect. A study by Suarez in 2008 found that people sleeping less than six hours a night had doubled risk of heart attack, independent of diet, exercise, or cholesterol. The autonomic nervous system's demands on the heart exceed what compromised circulation can support.

Denial kills more efficiently than you'd expect. Half of people having heart attacks delay seeking help for more than two hours, often much longer. The median delay is around four hours. The window for effective intervention is ninety minutes. After that, heart muscle is dying in large quantities. People convince themselves it's indigestion, muscle strain, stress. They don't want to bother anyone. They don't want to look foolish. This cognitive bias—normalcy bias, the assumption that the unusual thing isn't happening—costs tens of thousands of lives annually.

The symptoms themselves are variable enough to fool people. The classic presentation—crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm—occurs in maybe half of heart attacks. Women more often experience nausea, fatigue, back pain, or just a sense of wrongness. Diabetics with nerve damage may feel almost nothing. Silent heart attacks, discovered only later on ECG, account for 20 to 30 percent of all attacks. You can lose a chunk of heart muscle without realizing it, walking around with reduced cardiac function and wondering why stairs got harder.

What you should do: recognize that heart disease is inflammatory as well as mechanical, that the dangerous plaques may not be the ones causing symptoms, and that the window for intervention is narrow. Chest discomfort plus sweating, nausea, or shortness of breath means call emergency services immediately, not wait to see if it passes. The risk of looking foolish is trivial compared to the risk of waiting. If you're wrong, you get a ride home. If you're right and you delay, you get permanent heart damage or death. The only rational choice is to assume the worst-case and confirm otherwise, not the reverse.
