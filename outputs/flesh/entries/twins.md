# Twins

Identical twins are not identical. They share DNA, but DNA is not a blueprint—it's more like a recipe that gets interpreted differently each time. Even in the womb, twins develop asymmetrically. One twin gets a better placental connection, grows larger, may be born minutes or hours ahead. Fingerprints diverge. Birthmarks appear in different places. By adulthood, "identical" twins can differ in height, weight, disease susceptibility, and lifespan. The person who looks most like you in the world is proof that genes alone don't determine who you become.

The science on this comes from twin studies, which have been the workhorse of behavioral genetics for a century. The method: compare identical twins (who share 100% of DNA) with fraternal twins (who share 50%, like any siblings). If identical twins are more similar than fraternal twins for some trait, that suggests genetic influence. Researchers have used this to estimate heritability of everything from height (80-90%) to intelligence (50-80% in adults) to political affiliation (40-50%). These numbers get cited constantly, and they're both revealing and misleading.

The misleading part: heritability is not fixed destiny. It's a population statistic, not an individual prediction. Saying intelligence is 50% heritable doesn't mean your IQ is half genes, half environment. It means that in the specific population studied, genetic differences account for about half the variation in test scores. Change the environment—better nutrition, different schools, less stress—and heritability changes. In environments where everyone gets adequate food and education, genetic differences matter more. In highly unequal environments, environmental differences dominate. Heritability is a snapshot of a specific context, not a fundamental property of the trait.

The revealing part: identical twins raised apart are shockingly similar. Thomas Bouchard's Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart, which ran from 1979 to 1999, found separated identical twins who had never met showing up in the same job, with the same hobbies, sometimes with spouses of the same name. The famous Jim twins, separated at birth and reunited at 39, had both named their sons James Alan (though one spelled it Allan), both drove Chevrolets, both vacationed at the same Florida beach, both bit their nails, both suffered from tension headaches. The temptation is to conclude genes determine personality. The more accurate conclusion: genes create tendencies toward certain environments and behaviors, which then reinforce themselves. You're not programmed to bite your nails, but you might be wired toward anxiety, which finds its own expressions.

The studies also revealed what doesn't seem heritable. Parenting style shows minimal effect on adult personality once you control for genetics. Twins raised by different families end up about as similar as twins raised together. This was a bomb in developmental psychology. It suggested that shared family environment—the thing most parenting books obsess over—accounts for almost none of the variation in how children turn out as adults. What matters is genes and non-shared environment: the unique experiences each child has, even within the same family. Your relationship with your sibling, your specific teacher, your peer group, your birth order effects, the illness you had, the book that changed you—the things that weren't shared.

But identical twins diverge in ways genes can't explain. This is where epigenetics enters. DNA methylation—chemical tags that attach to genes and alter their expression without changing the sequence—differs between identical twins and grows more different with age. A 2005 study by Manel Esteller found that 3-year-old twins had nearly identical methylation patterns, while 50-year-old twins showed dramatic differences, especially if they'd lived apart. The tags accumulate from environmental exposures: diet, stress, toxins, infection. The genome you inherit is less like a fixed text than a heavily annotated manuscript, and the annotations are rewritten throughout life.

This shows up in disease. Identical twins have about a 25% chance of both developing schizophrenia if one does—far higher than the general population, but far from certain. For type 1 diabetes, concordance is about 50%. For breast cancer, 20%. The twin with the condition often developed it after specific environmental triggers: an infection, a period of stress, a dietary change. The genetic vulnerability was shared; the disease was not. You can inherit a loaded gun that may or may not fire.

The strangest findings involve twins who share a placenta versus those who don't. About two-thirds of identical twins are monochorionic—one placenta, two fetuses. The other third are dichorionic—two placentas, despite being genetically identical. Monochorionic twins are more similar in birth weight, IQ, and personality than dichorionic twins. The shared blood supply creates a more similar prenatal environment. This means the moment of twinning—early or late—determines how identical "identical" twins will be. DNA is the same; developmental trajectory diverges from day one.

Twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome is the dark side of this. In 10-15% of monochorionic twins, blood flows unequally through connecting vessels in the shared placenta. One twin becomes the donor, losing blood volume, growth-restricted and anemic. The other becomes the recipient, overloaded with blood, at risk of heart failure. Without intervention, mortality is 80-90%. Laser surgery can seal the connecting vessels, but survivors often face developmental differences. The recipient twin may be larger at birth but more prone to neurological issues later. Shared flesh can be as much risk as resource.

The practical implication for everyone: you are not your genes. Identical twins prove this. They walk around with the same genome expressing itself in different bodies, different lives, different outcomes. What you inherit is a set of probabilities, not a program. The script is loose. Environment rewrites expression. Choices compound. The question isn't how much is genetic versus environmental—it's how genes and environment interact, how early conditions alter later responses, how the system adapts and diverges over time.

Twins also reveal something about identity. If someone with your exact DNA is a different person, then what makes you you? Not the sequence. The arrangement, the expression, the accumulated experiences, the singular path through time. Twins are walking proof that nature and nurture are inseparable, that the body is not a fixed object but a process unfolding, that the flesh you inhabit is yours alone, even if someone else started with the same recipe.
