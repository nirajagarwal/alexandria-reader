# Sweat

You produce a liter of sweat on an average day just sitting around. Two to three liters if you exercise. Up to ten liters if you're running a marathon in heat. Most of it you never notice—it evaporates before forming visible drops. This is by design. Sweat isn't a failure of temperature regulation; it's the primary mechanism. Humans are the only mammals who cool themselves primarily through evaporation rather than panting or seeking shade. We traded fur for sweat glands and became the best endurance runners in the animal kingdom.

The mechanism is elegant. You have two to four million eccrine sweat glands distributed across your skin, densest on your palms, forehead, and feet. Each is a coiled tube reaching down into the dermis, extracting water and electrolytes from blood plasma and pushing them to the surface. The composition is mostly water with sodium, chloride, potassium, and trace minerals—dilute compared to blood, but not pure. When sweat evaporates, it pulls heat away from the skin at a rate of about 580 calories per liter. This is phenomenally efficient. A dog can't do this. A horse can do it somewhat. A human can sustain it for hours.

The evolutionary story is persistence hunting. Before projectile weapons, early humans ran prey animals to exhaustion in midday heat. An antelope is faster but overheats quickly—it has to stop and pant. A human sweats continuously, maintains core temperature, and keeps running. The prey collapses from hyperthermia; the human walks up and kills it with a rock. This only works if you can sweat profusely for extended periods without overheating your brain. The fossil record shows modern human proportions—long limbs, narrow pelvis, reduced body hair—appearing around the same time as the expansion of eccrine sweat glands. We didn't just start sweating more; we redesigned the chassis around the cooling system.

But sweat is also where humans fail in predictable ways. The first is acclimatization—people underestimate how trainable the system is. When you move from a cool climate to a hot one, or start exercising in heat, your sweat rate increases over seven to fourteen days. Not because you're more stressed, but because your body is learning to start sweating earlier and more efficiently. Studies from the 1960s onward—particularly work by Ladell, Wyndham, and later Nielsen—showed that heat-adapted individuals produce more dilute sweat, conserving sodium while maintaining cooling. This matters for anyone moving, traveling, or training: your sweat system needs time to recalibrate. Push too hard before acclimatization and you risk heat exhaustion even if you feel fine initially.

The second failure is the electrolyte mythology. Sports drink marketing has convinced people that sweat depletes sodium to dangerous levels during normal exercise. For most activity, this is wrong. Sweat is hypotonic—you lose proportionally more water than sodium. Drinking plain water during a two-hour run is fine for most people. The danger is hyponatremia from overdrinking plain water during ultra-endurance events. In the 2002 Boston Marathon, thirteen percent of finishers had clinically low sodium—not from sweating it out but from drinking too much water and diluting blood sodium concentration. Dr. Christopher Almond's research showed that weight gain during a marathon (from overdrinking) predicted hyponatremia better than weight loss did. You can drown your electrolytes by overcorrecting for sweat.

Then there's the social dimension. Sweat carries no information about fitness or health—some people have overactive glands, some underactive. The condition of excessive sweating, hyperhidrosis, affects two to three percent of the population and has no correlation with exertion or temperature. People's hands drip during handshakes; their shirts soak through in air conditioning. The mechanism is overactive sympathetic nervous system signaling to the glands. Botox injections, which block acetylcholine release at nerve terminals, can shut down sweat production for months—this is FDA-approved and widely used. The opposite condition, anhidrosis, is more dangerous. People who can't sweat overheat rapidly and don't notice until core temperature is critical. This happens with certain medications, nerve damage, or genetic conditions affecting sweat gland development.

Sweat also explains the thermal limits of human habitation. The theoretical ceiling is a wet-bulb temperature—a combination of heat and humidity—of 35°C (95°F). Above this, even a naked human at rest can't evaporate sweat fast enough to prevent fatal hyperthermia. The body produces heat just from metabolism; if the air is too hot and humid, there's no way to dump that heat. Parts of the Persian Gulf and South Asia are approaching this limit during heat waves. A 2020 study by Raymond, Matthews, and Horton in *Science Advances* documented multiple locations exceeding 35°C wet-bulb for one to two hours—previously thought to be decades away. Sweat stops working as a cooling mechanism when the air can't accept more water vapor. The body's core temperature rises, and there's nothing you can do about it except leave.

For daily life, the practical points are: your sweat system adapts but needs time, you can train it to be more efficient through heat exposure, electrolyte panic is mostly unfounded unless you're doing ultra-endurance events, and the real risk is ignoring early signs that your cooling isn't keeping up—dizziness, confusion, stopping sweating in heat. People assume sweat is gross or a sign of poor fitness. It's actually a major evolutionary innovation that let humans dominate environments other mammals can't tolerate. Respect the system, give it time to adapt, don't override the signals, and recognize that climate change is pushing against the physical limits of what sweating can compensate for. You are, quite literally, a water-cooled animal. When that stops working, everything else stops too.
