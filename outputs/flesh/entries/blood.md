# Blood

You have about five liters of blood in your body, and none of it is sitting still. A single red blood cell completes a full circuit—heart to lungs to tissues and back—in less than a minute. Over its four-month lifespan, one cell makes roughly 170,000 laps. The blood is not a storage tank but a highway system, and what moves through it determines what every other part of your body can do.

The red blood cells themselves are strange devices. They start with a nucleus like other cells, then eject it before entering circulation. What remains is a bag of hemoglobin—no DNA, no ability to repair itself, no capacity to reproduce. The tradeoff makes sense: more room for oxygen-carrying molecules, more flexibility to squeeze through capillaries narrower than the cell's resting diameter. But it also means red blood cells are consumables, not renewable. Your bone marrow produces two million new ones every second to replace the ones wearing out.

Hemoglobin is where the chemistry gets interesting. Each molecule contains four iron atoms that can each bind one oxygen molecule. But they don't bind independently. When the first oxygen attaches, it changes the protein's shape, making it easier for the second to bind, then the third, then the fourth. This cooperative binding is what makes hemoglobin efficient: it grabs oxygen aggressively in the lungs where concentration is high, then releases it readily in tissues where concentration is low. Small changes in oxygen availability produce large changes in delivery. The curve is steep where it matters.

Carbon monoxide kills because it binds to hemoglobin two hundred times more tightly than oxygen does. It doesn't just occupy a seat—it locks the protein in the wrong shape, preventing the remaining sites from releasing oxygen even if they're carrying it. This is why low concentrations are dangerous. You don't need to displace all the oxygen, just enough to break the cooperative mechanism. At 0.1% carbon monoxide in air—one part in a thousand—half your hemoglobin stops working within hours. The blood still looks red. The cells are still moving. But delivery fails.

Blood type was mysterious until Karl Landsteiner figured it out in 1901. He mixed blood samples from different people and noticed that some combinations clumped while others didn't. The cause: sugar molecules on the surface of red blood cells, and antibodies floating in plasma that recognize foreign sugars as threats. Type A blood has A sugars and anti-B antibodies. Type B has B sugars and anti-A antibodies. Type O has neither sugar but both antibodies. Type AB has both sugars and no antibodies. This matters for transfusion because mismatched blood triggers massive immune attack—antibodies bind to foreign cells, marking them for destruction, causing them to burst. Before Landsteiner, transfusions were Russian roulette. After, they became predictable medicine.

The Rh factor is another sugar molecule, named after the rhesus monkeys used in the research. About 85% of people have it (Rh positive); 15% don't (Rh negative). This mostly doesn't matter until pregnancy. If an Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive fetus, fetal blood cells can cross the placenta during birth. The mother's immune system treats them as foreign and builds antibodies. First pregnancy usually goes fine. But in subsequent pregnancies with Rh-positive fetuses, those antibodies cross the placenta and attack fetal blood cells, causing severe anemia or death. The solution is a shot of Rh immunoglobulin—essentially pre-made antibodies that clear fetal cells before the mother's immune system notices them. Developed in the 1960s, it nearly eliminated hemolytic disease of the newborn. The treatment works by hiding the problem from the immune system before it learns to respond.

Plasma—the liquid part—is 90% water, 7% protein, and 3% everything else. That "everything else" includes the entire signaling system of the body: hormones, clotting factors, immune molecules, nutrients, waste products. The blood is the distribution network for chemical communication. When your pancreas releases insulin, it doesn't connect directly to your muscles—it dumps insulin into the bloodstream, and blood delivers it everywhere within seconds. This is why bloodborne diseases are so dangerous: they have access to every organ system simultaneously.

Clotting is a cascade. When blood vessels rupture, exposed collagen activates platelets—cell fragments that stick to damage sites. They release chemical signals that activate clotting factors—proteins floating in plasma, each one activating the next. The cascade amplifies: one signal becomes ten becomes a hundred. The final step converts soluble fibrinogen into fibrin threads that form a mesh, trapping blood cells and platelets into a solid clot. The system has to balance precision with speed. Too slow and you bleed out. Too fast and you clot inappropriately—stroke, heart attack, pulmonary embolism. Hemophilia is a genetic failure to produce clotting factors, leaving the cascade incomplete. Before synthetic factors, severe hemophilia was often fatal by adolescence.

Blood doping works, which is why it's banned. Inject more red blood cells and you can carry more oxygen, which delays fatigue in endurance events. The risk is viscosity. Thicker blood is harder to pump, especially during exertion when you're already dehydrated. Several cyclists died in the late 1980s from EPO use—synthetic erythropoietin that signals the bone marrow to produce more red blood cells. They went to sleep with blood like sludge and their hearts couldn't push it. The performance gain was real. So was the mortality.

Altitude training exploits the same mechanism legally. At 2,500 meters, lower oxygen pressure means less oxygen per breath. The kidneys sense this and release EPO naturally. Red blood cell production increases over weeks. Return to sea level with elevated cell counts and you have a temporary oxygen-carrying advantage. The effect fades as old cells die off, which is why elite endurance athletes time altitude camps carefully before major competitions.

Blood glucose control is the body's most tightly regulated variable after pH. Insulin moves glucose from blood into cells. Glucagon moves it back out of storage when levels drop. Type 1 diabetes is autoimmune destruction of the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. Type 2 is progressive insulin resistance—cells stop responding to the signal, so the pancreas produces more, which eventually exhausts the system. Both end with elevated blood glucose, but the mechanisms and timelines differ. High glucose damages blood vessels—first the small ones in eyes and kidneys, later the large ones in the heart and brain. The damage is cumulative and largely irreversible, which is why glycemic control matters more than treating symptoms after they appear.

You cannot feel your blood moving, but you live inside its delivery schedule. What you eat changes its chemistry within hours. What you breathe changes its oxygen content within seconds. The blood is where the body negotiates between what's available outside and what's needed inside, and the negotiation never stops.
