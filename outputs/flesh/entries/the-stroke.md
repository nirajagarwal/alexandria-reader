# The Stroke

A stroke is a plumbing problem with the brain's blood supply, and what happens after tells you everything about how the brain is organized. The tissue doesn't regenerate well, which means a stroke leaves a map of what got destroyed—and by negative image, what those destroyed cells were doing. The symptoms aren't random. They reveal the architecture.

The mechanism is either blockage or rupture. Ischemic strokes—about 87% of cases—happen when a clot stops blood flow to part of the brain. Hemorrhagic strokes happen when a vessel bursts. Either way, neurons downstream die within minutes. Brain tissue is metabolically expensive; it uses 20% of the body's oxygen while making up 2% of body weight. Cut the supply and cells die faster than anywhere else. The medical window is narrow—tPA, the clot-busting drug, works only if given within three to four and a half hours, and only for ischemic strokes. After that, the damage is done.

The symptoms map directly to brain geography. The middle cerebral artery supplies the motor strip and language areas in most people. Block it and you get weakness on one side of the body and often aphasia—loss of language. The posterior cerebral artery feeds the visual cortex. Block that and half the visual field goes dark in both eyes, but the patient might not notice because they can't see what's missing. A stroke in the right parietal lobe can cause hemispatial neglect, where the patient ignores the entire left side of space—won't eat food on the left side of the plate, won't shave the left side of their face, denies that their paralyzed left arm belongs to them. These aren't vision problems. The patient isn't blind on the left. They've lost the concept of "left."

This reveals something unsettling. The unified world you experience is synthesized from specialized processors. When one processor goes offline, you don't perceive a gap—you lose the category it handled. The neglect patient doesn't see darkness on the left. Left ceases to exist as a direction. Their world is complete; it's just smaller. You are always one clot away from inhabiting a different reality.

The language damage is especially specific. In the 1860s, French physician Pierre Paul Broca treated a patient named Leborgne, who could understand speech but could only say one syllable: "tan." After Leborgne's death, autopsy showed damage to a specific region of the left frontal lobe, now called Broca's area. Stroke that area and you get expressive aphasia—you know what you want to say, but can't produce the words. Stroke Wernicke's area, a few inches back, and you get fluent nonsense—speech that sounds grammatical but means nothing, and you can't understand what others say. Two different kinds of language destruction from strokes in two different places. The brain doesn't have a general language module. It has production hardware and comprehension hardware, physically separate, and both can fail independently.

The recovery patterns are just as revealing. Some function returns in the first weeks as swelling subsides and blood flow improves to damaged-but-not-dead tissue, a region called the penumbra. Real recovery after that is mostly brain rewiring—other areas taking over lost functions. Young brains do this better. Children who lose an entire hemisphere to stroke or surgery can recover nearly normal function if it happens early enough. The other half learns to do the work. Adult brains are less plastic. Stroke at sixty and the remapping is limited. Some therapies help—constraint-induced movement therapy forces use of the paralyzed limb by restraining the good one, and the brain responds to the demand. But the older the brain, the harder the retraining.

The risk factors are boringly vascular. High blood pressure, smoking, diabetes, atrial fibrillation—all increase stroke risk by damaging vessels or promoting clots. Atrial fibrillation matters because the irregular heartbeat lets blood pool and clot in the atria; those clots can break loose and travel to the brain. The effect is measurable: untreated AFib increases stroke risk fivefold. Anticoagulants bring it back down, which is why cardiologists push them hard despite the bleeding risk.

The transient ischemic attack—the TIA, or mini-stroke—is a warning. Symptoms last less than 24 hours, usually minutes, because the clot clears on its own. No permanent damage. But a TIA predicts a major stroke within the next week in about 5% of cases, and within three months in 10-15%. It's a fire alarm going off before the fire spreads. Half of people who have a TIA don't seek immediate medical attention. This is a mistake. Imaging can find the source of the clot—a narrowed carotid artery, a heart arrhythmia, plaque buildup—and intervention can prevent the bigger stroke that's coming.

There's a rough heuristic for recognizing stroke: FAST—Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency services. This works because most strokes hit the middle cerebral artery distribution, which covers motor and language areas. Not every stroke follows this pattern, but most do, and the guideline saves time. Brain tissue dies at a rate of 1.9 million neurons per minute during a stroke. Fast matters.

What you should take from this is that the brain is not a general-purpose processor that can be damaged in general-purpose ways. It's a collection of specialized modules with specific addresses. Damage reveals the map. The deficits are not abstract losses of "brain function" but concrete disappearances of particular capacities—speech production, left-side awareness, face recognition, impulse control. The intact person you are now depends on every part staying intact. Your self is an achievement of continuous blood flow to a three-pound organ that has no backup system and tolerates interruption poorly.
