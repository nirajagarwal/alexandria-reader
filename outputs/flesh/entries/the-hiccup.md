# The Hiccup

Your diaphragm spasms. Your glottis slams shut. You make a sound that has no function, serves no purpose, and that you cannot voluntarily stop. The hiccup is a vestigial reflex, a glitch left over from when our ancestors had gills. It's one of the clearest pieces of evidence that evolution doesn't design—it tinkers, and sometimes it leaves junk code running.

The mechanism is straightforward but strange. The diaphragm—the sheet of muscle separating chest from abdomen—contracts involuntarily. This pulls air into the lungs. But 35 milliseconds later, the glottis (the opening between your vocal cords) snaps closed, cutting off the inflow. The clash produces the "hic" sound. Nothing useful happens. No air exchange, no protection, no communication. Just noise.

Christian Straus at the Pitié-Salpêtrière Hospital in Paris traced the hiccup's origins in 2003 by studying tadpoles. Tadpoles have both gills and primitive lungs. They need to pump water over their gills while preventing it from entering their lungs. The solution: a rapid inhalation with simultaneous glottal closure. It's exactly the hiccup pattern. As tadpoles metamorphose into frogs, the reflex fades. In mammals, which evolved from similar amphibian ancestors, it persists. Your hiccups are a fossil, a motor pattern from 370 million years ago that serves no purpose in air-breathing adults but that development couldn't cleanly excise.

The trigger is the phrenic nerve, which controls the diaphragm, misfiring in coordination with the vagus nerve, which controls the glottis. What causes the misfire varies: stomach distension, sudden temperature changes, carbonated drinks, alcohol, excitement. The list is incoherent because the hiccup isn't solving a problem—it's a system malfunction. Any irritation to the relevant nerves can set it off. Eating too fast is common because it inflates the stomach, which presses on the diaphragm and stimulates the phrenic nerve. Drinking cold liquids triggers temperature-sensitive vagus branches. The hiccup is a hair trigger attached to nothing important.

Most hiccups stop in minutes. The rhythm usually ranges from 4 to 60 per minute. But in rare cases, they persist. Charles Osborne of Iowa hiccupped for 68 years, from 1922 to 1990. He lived a mostly normal life, married twice, fathered eight children. His hiccups stopped a year before he died—for no determined reason. The longest-lasting hiccup bout ever recorded, and medicine never figured out how to stop it or why it ended.

Persistent hiccups—defined as lasting more than 48 hours—can indicate serious pathology. Brainstem tumors, stroke, meningitis, metabolic disorders. The phrenic and vagus nerves run through so much anatomy that irritation anywhere along their paths can cause hiccups. In clinical settings, intractable hiccups prompt MRI scans and metabolic panels. The hiccup is usually benign, but when it won't stop, it's a red flag that something is pressing on, inflaming, or damaging critical neural pathways.

The folk remedies are endless: hold your breath, drink water upside down, get startled, pull your tongue, bite a lemon. Some work sometimes, but there's no consistent mechanism. The most effective interventions target the vagus nerve directly. Drinking cold water stimulates vagal branches. Holding your breath raises carbon dioxide levels, which dampens phrenic nerve activity. Pulling your knees to your chest compresses the diaphragm. These aren't cures—they're resets, attempts to interrupt the reflex arc. They work when they work because they overload the system with competing signals.

For persistent cases, medicine escalates. Chlorpromazine, an antipsychotic, is the only FDA-approved hiccup drug—it dampens dopamine pathways that modulate the reflex. Baclofen, a muscle relaxant, suppresses the hiccup center in the brainstem. In extreme cases, surgeons cut the phrenic nerve. This paralyzes half the diaphragm. It stops the hiccups but leaves you breathing shallower for life. The cure is arguably worse than the ailment unless the ailment is destroying your ability to sleep, eat, or think.

The hiccup reveals how much of your body is automated legacy code. You inherit reflexes from ancestors who lived in water, reflexes that made sense for them but not for you. The grasp reflex in infants—strong enough to support their weight—comes from ancestors who clung to their mothers' fur. The arrector pili muscles that give you goosebumps once fluffed up fur to trap heat or make you look larger to predators. Now they just make you look cold. The hiccup belongs to this category: biological debris, a motor program that development couldn't delete because the nerves involved do other essential jobs.

You are not a designed system. You are an accumulation of modifications to older systems, which were modifications to systems older still. Most of it works well enough. Some of it works brilliantly. And some of it—like the hiccup—is a leftover that no longer serves a function but that you're stuck with because evolution doesn't go back and clean up its code. It just keeps building on what's there, and sometimes what's there is a reflex from when you had gills.
