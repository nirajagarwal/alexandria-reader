# Puberty

The body you inhabited as a child no longer fits. Puberty isn't growth—it's replacement. The hypothalamus, dormant since toddlerhood, begins pulsing gonadotropin-releasing hormone in the middle of the night. These pulses wake the pituitary, which signals the gonads, which flood the system with sex hormones. Within two to four years, you'll gain 20% of your adult height, restructure your skeleton, redistribute your fat, grow new tissue in a dozen places, and rewire significant portions of your brain. This happens to everyone, and almost no one is prepared for how strange it is to live through.

The trigger mechanism is still debated. Body fat percentage matters—leptin, produced by fat cells, appears to signal "enough stored energy to reproduce." This is why athletes with very low body fat often experience delayed puberty, and why the average age of menarche has dropped from seventeen in the 1840s to twelve today as nutrition improved. But fat alone doesn't explain it. Geneticist Douglas Rittenhous found that mutations in the gene for kisspeptin—a neuropeptide that activates the reproductive axis—prevent puberty entirely. Without kisspeptin neurons firing, the hypothalamus never wakes up. The system needs both fuel and ignition.

The sequence is more variable than textbooks suggest. Tanner stages describe an idealized progression, but actual development is chaotic. Breast development might begin at eight or fourteen. Voice changes might lag years behind genital growth. One testicle enlarges months before the other. Pubic hair appears, then pauses, then resumes. The endocrine system coordinates poorly because it's running multiple independent processes simultaneously—growth hormone peaks, sex steroids ramp up, thyroid activity shifts, cortisol rhythms change. Nothing waits for anything else to finish.

The brain changes are more consequential than the body changes. Gray matter volume peaks around age eleven, then begins pruning—eliminating unused neural connections to make the used ones faster. The prefrontal cortex, responsible for impulse control and long-term planning, doesn't finish myelinating until the mid-twenties. Meanwhile, the limbic system, which processes emotion and reward, is fully online and hypersensitive to social feedback. This is not a design defect. The adolescent brain is optimized for learning social hierarchy, taking risks necessary for independence, and forming bonds outside the family. It's supposed to make you do things your childhood self wouldn't. The problem is that modern environments present risks—cars, drugs, social media—that didn't exist when this system evolved.

Sleep architecture shifts. The circadian rhythm delays by two to three hours; teenagers naturally fall asleep later and wake later. This is mediated by changes in melatonin secretion, not laziness. Requiring a 7:30 a.m. school start means requiring chronic sleep deprivation during the period of maximum brain plasticity. Researcher Mary Carskadon showed that pushing high school start times to 8:30 a.m. increased total sleep time and improved academic performance, but most schools ignore this because it complicates bus schedules. The body is trying to do intensive neural remodeling, and we've organized society to interrupt it.

Acne is not a hygiene failure—it's a hormonal feedback loop. Androgens enlarge sebaceous glands and increase oil production. Bacteria metabolize the oil, producing inflammatory compounds. The immune system attacks, causing cysts. Isotretinoin works by shrinking the glands themselves, but it's teratogenic—it causes severe birth defects if taken during pregnancy. This is why it requires monthly blood tests and two forms of contraception for anyone who could become pregnant. The caution is warranted; the drug is a vitamin A derivative that interferes with embryonic development. The same pathway that clears acne disrupts fetal patterning.

Growth is non-uniform. Hands and feet grow first, then limbs, then trunk—which is why adolescents look gangly. They're not clumsy; their proprioception hasn't caught up to their new dimensions. The body map in the motor cortex is still calibrated to smaller limbs. Learning to move in the new body takes months. Athletes who go through growth spurts often see their performance temporarily decline before improving.

The permanent changes are underappreciated. Bone density peaks in late adolescence; what you build then determines fracture risk fifty years later. Hip structure widens in female puberty to accommodate potential childbirth, but the process is variable—pelvic inlet diameter differs by four centimeters between individuals, enough to make some deliveries impossible without surgery. Vocal cords thicken in male puberty, dropping pitch by about an octave. This doesn't reverse. The voice you get is the voice you keep.

Timing matters beyond the immediate. Early puberty correlates with higher rates of depression and risk-taking, likely because the social environment doesn't match the developmental stage. Late puberty correlates with lower bone density and shorter adult height, but also better long-term metabolic health—the endocrine system had more time to mature before the reproductive axis overwhelmed it. There's no optimal timing, just different trade-offs.

The hardest part is that it happens to you, not for you. You don't choose when it starts or how it proceeds. The body you're in is being renovated around you, and you have to live in the construction site. Every human culture marks this transition with ritual because it's genuinely unsettling—one version of you is ending, and another is beginning, and neither has full control of the process. Understanding the mechanism doesn't make it comfortable, but it makes it less isolating. This is what the body does. This is what flesh becomes.
