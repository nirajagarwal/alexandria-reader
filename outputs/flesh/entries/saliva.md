# Saliva

You produce a liter of spit every day. Most people think of saliva as the thing that makes food wet enough to swallow, which is true but dramatically incomplete. Saliva is your mouth's immune system, its pH buffer, its remineralization fluid, its digestive prep team, and its primary defense against the bacteria that would otherwise dissolve your teeth in months. Understanding what saliva actually does explains why dry mouth isn't just uncomfortable—it's a medical problem that cascades into other medical problems.

Start with what it is. Saliva is 99% water, but that remaining 1% does serious work: enzymes, electrolytes, mucus, antibacterial compounds, and growth factors. Three major glands produce most of it—parotid, submandibular, sublingual—plus hundreds of minor glands scattered across your mouth's lining. The parotid makes thin, watery saliva rich in enzymes. The submandibular and sublingual glands make thicker, mucus-heavy saliva. You need both types. The flow rate varies wildly: almost nothing during sleep, up to 7 milliliters per minute when you're eating something sour.

The digestive story is what people know but underestimate. Amylase, the starch-digesting enzyme in saliva, begins breaking down carbohydrates the moment they enter your mouth. This isn't minor prep work—salivary amylase can break down 30-40% of starches before they reach your stomach. The enzyme survives stomach acid for a while, continuing its work in the gastric environment. This front-loaded digestion matters for blood sugar response. Chewing longer, letting saliva do more work, produces slower glucose absorption. People who eat quickly, barely chewing, bypass this system and hit their bloodstream with less-processed starches.

But the immune function is what makes saliva irreplaceable. It contains lysozyme, an enzyme that breaks bacterial cell walls. It contains lactoferrin, which binds iron that bacteria need to grow. It contains immunoglobulin A, antibodies that tag pathogens for destruction. It contains histatins, proteins that kill Candida and other fungi. Your mouth is a bacterial ecosystem containing 700 species of microbes, and saliva keeps the dangerous ones in check. When saliva production drops, the balance collapses. Decay accelerates. Fungal infections appear. Gum disease worsens.

Dry mouth—xerostomia—reveals what you lose. It's a side effect of over 500 medications: antihistamines, antidepressants, blood pressure drugs, opioids. It's a defining symptom of Sjögren's syndrome, an autoimmune disease attacking the salivary glands. It's a lasting consequence of radiation therapy for head and neck cancers, which can permanently damage gland tissue. Patients with chronic dry mouth develop cavities in places teeth normally don't decay—along the gum line, at the roots. They struggle to speak clearly, to taste food, to wear dentures without pain. Food sticks to their mouth. They wake up unable to swallow. The condition degrades quality of life in ways people without saliva problems can't imagine.

The pH buffering matters more than you'd guess. Bacteria in your mouth produce acid as they metabolize sugars. That acid demineralizes tooth enamel. Saliva's bicarbonate and phosphate ions neutralize the acid, keeping your mouth's pH near neutral. This gives enamel time to remineralize—absorbing calcium and phosphate from saliva to repair microscopic damage. The cycle of demineralization and remineralization happens constantly. Saliva tips the balance toward repair. Without sufficient saliva, or if you're constantly snacking (keeping pH low), demineralization wins. The enamel doesn't recover. You get cavities.

The flow rate matters more than most people realize. Stimulated saliva—what you produce when eating—has different composition than resting saliva. It's more alkaline, richer in bicarbonate, better at neutralizing acid. Chewing sugar-free gum works as a decay-prevention tool not because of the gum but because it stimulates saliva flow for 20 minutes. The same mechanism explains why cheese after a meal protects teeth: it's not the calcium—it's that cheese stimulates alkaline saliva production and raises mouth pH quickly.

Taste depends on saliva more than the taste buds. Flavor molecules must dissolve in saliva to reach taste receptors. People with dry mouth report that food tastes like cardboard—not because their taste buds are broken but because the molecules can't reach them. Saliva also contains gustin, a protein necessary for taste bud maturation. Zinc deficiency, which impairs gustin production, causes taste disturbances. The feedback loop between taste and saliva production matters: tasting food stimulates more saliva, which enables more tasting. When one side weakens, the whole system degrades.

The implications for daily life: chronic dehydration reduces saliva production, which means most people are running their mouth's defense systems on reduced capacity without knowing it. Breathing through your mouth instead of your nose dries saliva faster, which is why mouth-breathers have worse dental health on average. Alcohol and tobacco both reduce salivary flow, compounding their other oral health effects. Aging reduces production by 25-40%, which partly explains why older adults have more dental problems—it's not just neglect but diminished biochemical defense.

What most people miss is that saliva production responds to behavior. Eating fibrous foods that require chewing stimulates more saliva than soft, processed foods. Drinking water helps but doesn't replace stimulation—you need mechanical and taste stimulation to trigger full salivary response. The modern diet of soft, quickly-consumed foods under-stimulates the system. The mouth adapted to tough plant foods that required extensive chewing. When you give it smoothies and white bread, you're not just changing nutrition—you're reducing the environment's stimulus for the defensive system your teeth depend on.

You live in an ecology, and saliva is a major part of the regulatory mechanism. It's not inert lubricant. It's an active system keeping bacterial populations in check, repairing mineral damage, preparing food for digestion, enabling taste, and protecting soft tissue. When it fails, everything in the mouth fails—not dramatically, but incrementally, in ways that compound over years. Most people only think about spit when there's too much of it. The real problem is when there's not enough, and by the time you notice, the damage is already accumulating.
