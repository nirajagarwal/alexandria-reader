# The Seizure

A seizure is not a disease. It's a symptom—the brain's electrical system overloading and firing in synchronized waves instead of its usual controlled chaos. Think of it as every neuron in a region deciding to shout at once. The normal brain is a cocktail party of millions of separate conversations. A seizure is everyone screaming the same word in unison. The underlying causes range from genetic mutations to structural damage to metabolic crisis, but the final common pathway is the same: runaway excitation that the brain's inhibitory systems can't suppress.

The most famous type—the tonic-clonic or grand mal seizure—is actually less common than the public assumes. Stiffening, falling, convulsing, foaming at the mouth: that's the Hollywood version, and it happens, but most seizures are subtler and stranger. Absence seizures look like someone has paused mid-sentence, staring at nothing for ten seconds, then resuming as if no time passed. Focal seizures can cause repetitive hand movements, lip smacking, sudden terror, or the uncanny sensation that everything is intensely familiar (déjà vu) or utterly alien. The person may remain conscious but unable to speak or respond. These aren't "minor" seizures—they're the brain misfiring in different territories, producing different symptoms depending on what gets hijacked.

The electrical signature is distinctive. EEG during a seizure shows spike-and-wave patterns—sharp upward deflections followed by slow waves, repeating in rhythmic bursts. Between seizures, many people with epilepsy have normal EEGs. The abnormality is intermittent, which is why diagnosis can take time and why a single normal EEG doesn't rule out a seizure disorder. Neurologist William Lennox spent decades in the mid-20th century correlating seizure types with EEG patterns, work that made epilepsy one of the first brain disorders you could see electrically.

What triggers the electrical storm varies. In people with epilepsy, the threshold for seizure is lower than normal—structural abnormalities, scar tissue from old injuries, genetic mutations affecting ion channels, malformed blood vessels. About half of epilepsy cases have identifiable causes; the other half are idiopathic. But even people without epilepsy will seize under sufficient provocation. Sleep deprivation, alcohol withdrawal, extremely low blood sugar, cocaine, flashing lights at certain frequencies—these can all trigger seizures in a normal brain pushed past its limits. The brain's balance between excitation and inhibition is not robust. It doesn't take much to tip it.

The aftermath is called the postictal period. After a major seizure, the person may be confused, exhausted, unable to speak coherently for minutes to hours. The brain has just fired a massive coordinated volley; it's metabolically and electrically spent. Muscles that contracted violently may ache for days. Some people sleep for hours. Others experience temporary weakness on one side of the body (Todd's paralysis), which can last a day. The postictal state can look like a stroke, and emergency rooms occasionally miss this, treating the aftermath and missing the precipitating seizure entirely.

First aid for witnessed seizures is mostly about not making it worse. Turn the person on their side so saliva drains and they don't choke. Clear space so they don't hit anything. Do not restrain them or put anything in their mouth—the old advice to prevent tongue-biting causes more injuries than it prevents, usually broken teeth or bitten caregivers. Most seizures stop on their own in under two minutes. If it goes longer than five minutes, or if they seize again without regaining consciousness, that's status epilepticus—a medical emergency requiring IV medication to stop it.

The long-term problem is what repeated seizures do to the brain. Each seizure is a metabolic crisis—neurons firing continuously, demanding oxygen and glucose faster than blood can supply it. Prolonged or frequent seizures cause damage, particularly in the hippocampus, which is both prone to seizures and vulnerable to their effects. This creates a vicious cycle: seizures damage the hippocampus, damaged hippocampus becomes a seizure focus, more seizures cause more damage. Controlling seizures early matters not just for safety but to prevent the brain from carving its own epileptic pathways deeper.

The medication landscape is broad but imperfect. Antiepileptic drugs work by different mechanisms—enhancing inhibition, blocking sodium channels, reducing glutamate signaling. About two-thirds of people with epilepsy get adequate control on medication. The other third are drug-resistant, meaning they've tried multiple medications without success. For them, surgery is an option if the seizures originate from a discrete, removable area of the brain. The most famous case is patient H.M., whose hippocampi were removed bilaterally in 1953 to treat intractable seizures. The seizures improved. He also lost the ability to form new long-term memories. Modern epilepsy surgery is more conservative—removing smaller areas, testing function before cutting—but it remains a trade-off between seizure control and collateral damage.

The ketogenic diet works for some drug-resistant cases, particularly in children. The mechanism isn't fully understood, but a diet of 80-90% fat, almost no carbohydrates, shifts brain metabolism to run on ketones instead of glucose, and this appears to stabilize electrical activity. It's medically supervised, difficult to maintain, and not benign—kidney stones, growth problems, nutrient deficiencies. But for children seizing dozens of times a day who haven't responded to drugs, it can be life-changing. Neurologist John Freeman at Johns Hopkins revived interest in the diet in the 1990s after a patient's family pushed for it. The medical establishment had largely abandoned it when anticonvulsants became available. It turned out the old intervention still worked, just not for everyone.

What you should know if you have seizures or live with someone who does: consistency matters more than you'd think. Sleep deprivation is one of the most common triggers—even one late night can provoke a seizure in someone whose medication usually controls them. Alcohol is doubly dangerous: it lowers seizure threshold while present and provokes rebound excitability during withdrawal. Missing doses of medication creates fluctuating drug levels that invite breakthrough seizures. The brain is sensitive to patterns and disruptions of patterns. Keeping a stable schedule—sleep, meals, medication—reduces risk more than people expect.

The social reality of seizures is harder than the medical one for many people. Epilepsy still carries stigma, association with demonic possession and mental illness lingering from centuries when no one understood what was happening. Employment can be precarious; many jobs and all driving privileges are off-limits after a seizure. People hide their diagnosis, skip medication to avoid side effects, refuse to wear medical alert bracelets. The unpredictability is corrosive. You can go months seizure-free and still live with the knowledge that the next one could happen in a meeting, on a date, while holding your child. The brain you live in has a known failure mode, and you can reduce the risk but not eliminate it.

This is what a seizure reveals: the brain is not a solid-state device but an electrical system running near its stability limits. The balance between excitation and inhibition is active, maintained by constant adjustment, and it can fail. When it does, consciousness doesn't just dim—it fractures, producing experiences that defy normal categories. You can be awake but unreachable, moving but not volitional, experiencing intense emotion with no external cause. The seizure is the brain becoming visible to itself in the worst way, its own electrical noise overwhelming the signal. Understanding this doesn't make it less frightening, but it relocates the fear from the supernatural to the biological, where it can at least be managed, if not always controlled.
