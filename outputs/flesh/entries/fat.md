# Fat

Fat has better public relations than it deserves. We've spent forty years demonizing dietary fat while quietly getting fatter, then pivoted to celebrating body fat as morally neutral while the metabolic consequences compound. Neither stance engages with what fat actually is: an endocrine organ that secretes hormones, regulates inflammation, and determines disease risk in ways that have nothing to do with aesthetics or virtue.

Start with the biology. Adipose tissue isn't inert storage. It's metabolically active, producing at least fifty different signaling molecules—adipokines—that affect appetite, insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, and immune function. Leptin, discovered in 1994 by Jeffrey Friedman at Rockefeller University, signals energy reserves to the brain. More fat means more leptin, which should suppress appetite. But in most obese people, the brain becomes leptin-resistant, like a smoke alarm that keeps going off until you stop hearing it. The signal is there; the response is broken.

The location of fat matters more than the total amount. Visceral fat—packed around organs in the abdomen—behaves differently than subcutaneous fat under the skin. Visceral adipose tissue is more metabolically active and more inflammatory. It releases free fatty acids directly into the portal circulation to the liver, driving insulin resistance and fatty liver disease. Subcutaneous fat, especially in the hips and thighs, is relatively benign and possibly protective. Someone with a BMI of 27 and mostly subcutaneous fat faces lower health risks than someone with a BMI of 24 carrying visceral fat. The scale measures total mass; it doesn't distinguish between the dangerous and the harmless.

This is why "skinny fat" is a real phenotype. People at normal weight with low muscle mass and high visceral fat—termed metabolically obese, normal weight or MONW—have diabetes and cardiovascular disease rates similar to the clinically obese. Meanwhile, some obese individuals are metabolically healthy, with normal insulin sensitivity and blood lipids. The correlation between fat mass and metabolic disease is strong but not deterministic. Distribution and metabolic function matter.

Fat cells themselves have a life cycle. They form mostly during childhood and adolescence; adult fat gain is primarily cell expansion, not new cell creation. When you lose weight, fat cells shrink but rarely disappear. This creates a pressure to refill. Shrunken adipocytes signal metabolic distress—they're more inflammatory, insulin-resistant, and hormonally dysregulated than either large fat cells or healthy-sized ones. This is why weight loss triggers compensatory mechanisms: increased hunger, decreased metabolic rate, constant signaling that the body is starving. You're fighting your own adipose tissue's distress response.

The inflammatory component is underappreciated. As fat cells enlarge, they become hypoxic—starved of oxygen because blood supply doesn't keep pace. Hypoxic fat cells release pro-inflammatory cytokines and attract macrophages. This low-grade chronic inflammation—detectable as elevated C-reactive protein—is the likely mechanism linking obesity to cardiovascular disease, dementia, and cancer. The fat isn't just sitting there; it's actively secreting molecules that promote disease throughout the body. Inflammation is the channel through which excess adiposity kills.

Brown fat complicates the picture. Infants have substantial brown adipose tissue, which burns energy to generate heat. Adults were thought to have none until PET scans in the 2000s revealed small deposits, primarily around the neck and collarbone. Brown fat contains dense mitochondria and burns glucose and fatty acids to produce heat rather than ATP. Cold exposure increases brown fat activity; chronic warmth suppresses it. The thermoneutral zone—the temperature range where you're comfortable without shivering or sweating—has risen as indoor heating became ubiquitous. We now live perpetually warm, which may reduce energy expenditure by 100-200 calories daily compared to a century ago. Your fat is partially a function of climate control.

Dietary fat's relationship to body fat is less straightforward than it seems. The low-fat diet movement of the 1980s and 90s failed to prevent obesity. Some populations eat high-fat diets and stay lean; others eat low-fat and gain weight. The Women's Health Initiative, the largest diet trial ever conducted, randomized nearly 50,000 women to low-fat or control diets for eight years. The low-fat group reduced fat intake from 38% to 29% of calories. Average weight loss: one pound. The type of fat matters—trans fats are clearly harmful, omega-3s likely beneficial—but total dietary fat is a weak predictor of body composition.

What degrades fat's function: chronic caloric excess, sugar and refined carbs driving insulin resistance, sedentary behavior reducing insulin sensitivity, sleep deprivation (which increases ghrelin and decreases leptin), chronic stress elevating cortisol and promoting visceral fat deposition. What supports it: maintaining muscle mass (which increases metabolic rate), regular movement (particularly after meals to clear glucose), adequate sleep, managing inflammation through diet and stress reduction, occasional cold exposure.

The practical implication is that fighting fat requires engaging with it as a metabolic system, not a cosmetic problem. Crash diets shrink fat cells and trigger compensatory mechanisms; they don't address the hormonal and inflammatory environment that produced excess fat. Building muscle increases the metabolic sink for glucose and improves insulin sensitivity. Reducing refined carbohydrates lowers insulin levels and permits fat oxidation. Managing sleep and stress addresses the hormonal drivers of visceral fat accumulation. The body defends its fat stores aggressively, but it responds to signals—you just have to send the right ones, consistently, over months and years.

Fat is not a passive reservoir. It's an organ that talks back, regulates energy balance, and either supports or undermines metabolic health depending on how much you have, where it's located, and how inflamed it is. Understanding it as a dynamic system—not a moral failing or a neutral characteristic—changes what you do about it. You're not at war with your fat. You're negotiating with an endocrine organ that has its own agenda.
