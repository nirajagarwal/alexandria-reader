# The Bruise

You can't bruise the dead. Blood pools only when a heart is pumping. The darker the bruise, the more force was involved—but only if it's fresh. Give it time and every bruise turns the same sequence: red to purple to green to yellow to gone. This progression is your body eating itself and rebuilding. The bruise isn't damage. It's the cleanup crew at work.

Start with what a bruise is. Blunt force ruptures capillaries under intact skin. Blood leaks into surrounding tissue. Red blood cells pile up where they don't belong. If the skin breaks, you bleed. If it doesn't, you bruise. The difference is millimeters of epidermis, but it changes everything about how your body responds.

Immediately after impact, the bruise is red—oxygenated blood. Within hours it darkens to purple or blue as oxygen depletes. This is when it looks worst, when people ask if you're okay. But the real work is just starting. Macrophages—specialized white blood cells—migrate to the site and begin consuming the leaked red blood cells. They tear apart hemoglobin, the protein that carries oxygen, and break it into components: iron, which gets recycled to bone marrow, and biliverdin, a green pigment. This is why bruises turn greenish after a few days. You're seeing the chemical signature of hemoglobin digestion.

Biliverdin converts to bilirubin, which is yellow. The bruise yellows as the macrophages finish their work. Then even that fades as the bilirubin disperses into the bloodstream and gets processed by the liver. The entire sequence takes two to four weeks depending on the severity of the initial injury. You can't speed it up. You can only wait for your body to finish eating the evidence.

Forensic pathologists use bruise color to estimate time since injury, but it's an imperfect science. William Eckert, a pioneering forensic pathologist, documented cases in the 1970s showing that identical impacts produced different color progressions in different people. Age matters—older skin bruises more easily and heals slower. Medication matters—aspirin, warfarin, anything that affects clotting or platelet function. Skin tone matters for visibility. Two people can sustain the same injury and show completely different bruising patterns. The bruise is a biological process, not a timestamp.

Women bruise more easily than men. This isn't folklore—it's measurable in clinical studies. Estrogen affects capillary fragility. Subcutaneous fat distribution differs. The threshold force required to produce a visible bruise is lower. This has implications in domestic violence cases, where absence of bruising is sometimes wrongly interpreted as absence of assault. Some bodies mark easily; others hide trauma well. The bruise is not a reliable witness.

Deep bruising can involve more than capillaries. A bone bruise is bleeding within the bone marrow itself, not visible on the surface. These hurt more and last longer—sometimes months. The periosteum, the tissue coating bone, is densely innervated. Trauma there sends sustained pain signals. You can have significant internal bruising with minimal surface discoloration. Pain and visible damage don't always correlate.

The weirdest cases involve bruises that appear days after injury. Gravity pulls the pooled blood downward before it coagulates. An impact to the forehead can produce bruising around the eyes forty-eight hours later. Abdominal trauma can cause bruising at the flanks or groin. The blood follows tissue planes and fascial boundaries, surfacing far from the impact site. Delayed bruising complicates forensic analysis—the visible injury and the actual trauma are separated in both space and time.

People try to treat bruises. Ice reduces swelling immediately after impact by constricting blood vessels—less blood leaks out. But once the bruise has formed, ice does nothing. Heat doesn't speed healing. Arnica is popular and unsupported by rigorous trials. The only thing that works is time and the metabolic capacity of your macrophages. You can't rush the biological cleanup.

What you can do is notice patterns. Frequent bruising without clear cause can signal clotting disorders, platelet dysfunction, or vitamin deficiencies—particularly vitamin C or K. Hemophilia manifests as easy bruising and slow healing. Leukemia disrupts normal blood cell production. The body that bruises too easily is sometimes announcing a systemic problem. The bruise is a symptom, not just an injury.

And sometimes the bruise is forensic evidence. Patterned bruising—finger marks, belt shapes, recognizable objects—can be photographed and documented. But bruises fade, and institutions are slow. Victims of assault are often told to wait until bruises develop fully before photographing, but this delays reporting and risks evidence degrading. The optimal window is narrow. The bruise as proof has an expiration date.

The bruise shows what your body does with damage it can't immediately expel. It sequesters, digests, recycles. The color changes you see are chemical transformations happening in tissue—your immune system performing triage and cleanup. You're watching macrophages work. The bruise is evidence that something hit you, yes, but also evidence that your body is functional enough to respond. Healing isn't the disappearance of the bruise. The bruise is the healing, made visible.
