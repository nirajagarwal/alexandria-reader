# The Heart

The heart is a pump that happens to have symbolic weight. Four chambers, two circuits, seventy beats per minute at rest—roughly three billion contractions over a lifetime, moving five liters of blood through sixty thousand miles of vessels. It starts beating twenty-two days after conception, before the brain exists, and stops when you do. Everything else about the heart—the metaphors, the Valentine's cards, the idea that it "knows" things—is projection onto plumbing.

But the plumbing is stranger than the metaphors suggest. The heart doesn't pump like a mechanical device with an external power source. Each contraction is generated by the muscle cells themselves, which fire electrically in coordinated waves. A cluster of cells in the right atrium called the sinoatrial node sets the rhythm—about once per second, these pacemaker cells depolarize spontaneously, triggering a cascade through the heart muscle. This is intrinsic. You can remove a heart from a body, put it in a nutrient bath, and it will keep beating. The nervous system modulates the rate, but doesn't create it. The heart runs its own show.

This matters for understanding heart attacks. When people say someone's heart "stopped," they usually mean ventricular fibrillation—the chambers quivering uselessly instead of contracting in sequence. The electrical coordination breaks down. Defibrillators don't restart the heart; they stop it completely, briefly, hoping the sinoatrial node will reassert control and restore rhythm. You're forcing a reboot. The heart doesn't need to be shocked into beating. It needs to be shocked out of beating wrong.

The coronary arteries that feed the heart muscle are the system's point of failure. They're narrow, they branch into progressively smaller vessels, and they sit on the surface of an organ that's constantly moving and squeezing them. When plaques form and rupture, clots block these arteries. The muscle downstream dies within minutes—no backup supply, no redundancy. A heart attack is a plumbing failure that causes an electrical failure that stops the pump. The dead muscle becomes scar tissue. The heart keeps beating with whatever's left.

Ancel Keys made the case against dietary fat in the 1950s with his Seven Countries Study, which showed correlations between fat intake and heart disease. The sugar industry paid scientists in the 1960s to downplay sugar's role and emphasize fat. This shaped sixty years of nutritional advice. Recent meta-analyses suggest the picture is more complex—saturated fat's effects depend on what replaces it, inflammation matters as much as cholesterol, and the French paradox (high fat intake, low heart disease) still lacks a clear explanation. The heart disease epidemic of the twentieth century might be less about fat and more about refined carbohydrates, sedentary behavior, and chronic stress. The research is still sorting this out, but the certainty was always premature.

The heart responds to demand. Athletes develop larger left ventricles that pump more blood per beat, which is why their resting heart rate drops—the same output with fewer contractions. This is healthy remodeling. But chronic high blood pressure forces the heart to pump against resistance, and the muscle thickens in ways that make it stiffer, less efficient. Same growth stimulus, opposite outcome. The heart adapts to what you ask of it, but not all adaptations are good.

Heart rate variability is a better indicator of health than resting heart rate alone. The time between beats should vary slightly—more variation means the parasympathetic nervous system is active, keeping the heart responsive rather than locked into a fixed rhythm. Low variability, where the intervals are metronomic, correlates with stress, poor sleep, and cardiovascular risk. Stephen Porges's polyvagal theory traces this to the vagus nerve's regulation of heart rate in response to safety versus threat. A rigid heart rate means the nervous system is stuck in defensive mode. The heart's rhythm reflects your mental state more directly than you'd think.

Exercise is the clearest intervention. Even modest aerobic activity—thirty minutes of walking most days—reduces cardiovascular mortality by 30-40 percent across multiple studies. The mechanism is multi-system: improved endothelial function, better insulin sensitivity, reduced inflammation, favorable changes in cholesterol profiles, lower resting blood pressure. The heart doesn't exist in isolation. It responds to metabolic signals, inflammatory cytokines, autonomic tone. Improving the whole system improves the pump.

The emotional stuff is harder to measure but not imaginary. Takotsubo cardiomyopathy, or broken heart syndrome, is a real diagnosis—acute stress triggering a temporary weakening of the left ventricle that mimics a heart attack. It happens after sudden grief, shock, intense fear. The mechanism involves catecholamine surges that stun the heart muscle. Most people recover fully, but the syndrome proves the heart is not insulated from psychological events. The connection between emotion and cardiac function is not metaphorical—it's mediated by stress hormones that directly affect the muscle.

The heart you have at fifty depends on what you did at twenty. Atherosclerosis starts in adolescence. Plaque builds slowly, silently. The first symptom of coronary artery disease is often a heart attack. By the time you feel something, the damage is decades old. This makes the heart unusual among organs—its failures are deferred, and by the time they manifest, they're often irreversible. Prevention is the only strategy that works at scale, and prevention means understanding that your daily habits now are setting the baseline for an organ that won't complain until it's too late.

You live inside a body with a pump that runs without your conscious input, adapts to what you demand of it, and will keep beating until something stops it—usually a failure of its own supply lines. The metaphorical heart has nothing to do with love. The actual heart has everything to do with how you move, eat, sleep, and manage stress. It's mechanical, but it's not separate from the rest of you. It's counting every choice.
