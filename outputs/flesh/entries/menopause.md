# Menopause

The ovaries shut down. Not gradually, not gently, but in a cascade of hormonal withdrawal that affects nearly every system in the body. Menopause is not a deficiency disease—it's a design feature, though one with consequences modern medicine has spent decades trying to mitigate, misunderstand, and occasionally make worse. What most people know about menopause is a thin layer of cultural clichés over a profound physiological transition that reveals how deeply sex hormones govern more than reproduction.

Start with the timeline. Women are born with all the eggs they'll ever have—about one to two million at birth, declining to around 300,000 by puberty. Each month, hundreds of follicles begin developing; one usually wins. By the mid-forties, the reserve is depleted. Fewer follicles respond to hormonal signals. Estrogen and progesterone production becomes erratic, then drops. The average age of menopause—defined as twelve months without a period—is fifty-one, but the transition starts years earlier. Perimenopause is the unsteady dismount, often more disruptive than the cessation itself.

The symptoms are famous but poorly understood. Hot flashes affect about 75% of women—sudden waves of heat, flushing, sweating, sometimes multiple times per hour. The mechanism involves the hypothalamus, which regulates body temperature. Declining estrogen narrows the thermoneutral zone—the range within which the body doesn't need to heat or cool itself. A tiny increase in core temperature triggers a heat-dissipation response as if you're overheating. You're not. Your thermostat is miscalibrated. Some women have a few hot flashes for a few months. Others have them for a decade. Why the variation? Unknown.

But hot flashes are the visible part. Estrogen receptors exist throughout the body—brain, bones, blood vessels, bladder, skin. The hormone doesn't just govern reproduction; it modulates inflammation, lipid metabolism, bone remodeling, vascular tone, and neurochemistry. When estrogen drops, all those systems shift. Bone density declines—women can lose up to 20% of bone mass in the five to seven years after menopause, which is why osteoporosis and fracture risk spike. LDL cholesterol rises, HDL often drops, and cardiovascular disease risk increases. The protection women had relative to men through their forties often disappears.

The brain effects are less discussed but equally real. Estrogen influences serotonin and dopamine signaling. Many women report memory problems, difficulty concentrating, mood changes during the transition. The research on "brain fog" is mixed—some studies find measurable cognitive changes, others don't. But subjective experience is consistent enough that dismissing it as psychosomatic misses something. The brain is an estrogen-responsive organ. When the hormone environment changes, neural function changes.

Then there's the genitourinary syndrome of menopause, the medical term for what happens to tissues that depended on estrogen. Vaginal walls thin, lubrication decreases, the tissue becomes more fragile. Urinary tract infections become more common because the urethra and bladder lining also thin. These changes affect more than half of postmenopausal women but are underreported because women don't bring them up and doctors don't ask. The cultural silence around this is a minor failure of medical communication.

Hormone replacement therapy has the most contentious history in menopause treatment. In the 1960s and 70s, estrogen therapy was promoted as a fountain of youth, prescribed liberally, and later found to increase uterine cancer risk when given without progesterone. The combination therapy fixed that problem, and by the 1990s, HRT was standard for menopausal women. Then the Women's Health Initiative study in 2002 found increased risks of breast cancer, heart disease, and stroke in women on combined HRT. The findings made headlines; millions stopped therapy overnight.

The problem was the study design. It enrolled women whose average age was sixty-three—over a decade past menopause—and used one specific formulation (conjugated equine estrogens plus synthetic progestin). Reanalysis showed that younger women starting HRT near menopause had better outcomes: reduced heart disease, fracture risk, and possibly dementia risk. But the damage to HRT's reputation lasted. Guidelines now support short-term use for women with severe symptoms, started near menopause, at the lowest effective dose. The pendulum swung too far one way, then overcorrected, and now sits somewhere more evidence-based but cautious.

What matters is that menopause is not one thing. For some women, it's a minor transition—a few irregular periods, mild hot flashes, then done. For others, it's years of disrupted sleep, mood changes, joint pain, and cognitive shifts that affect work and relationships. The variability is enormous, and the medical system often treats it as a uniform experience.

The evolutionary question is why menopause exists at all. Humans are one of the few species where females live decades beyond reproductive capacity. The grandmother hypothesis suggests that postmenopausal women increased their genetic fitness by helping raise grandchildren, improving survival rates more than additional pregnancies would. The data on this is complicated, but menopause as an adaptive feature—rather than simple ovarian failure—changes how you think about it. It's not a breakdown but a transition to a different role in kin networks.

Practically, this means menopause is a physiological event with significant health implications, not a natural process you should just endure. Bone density screening, cardiovascular risk assessment, and management of severe symptoms are all appropriate. The refusal to medicalize menopause has sometimes meant women suffer needlessly. The overmedicalization of it has sometimes meant unnecessary risk. The correct approach is individualized—treat the symptoms and risks that matter to each woman, ignore the ones that don't.

You live in a body that was built for a reproductive span that ended at menopause, in a world where average lifespan now extends decades beyond it. That mismatch creates challenges. Ignoring the transition doesn't work. Neither does treating it as a disease. What works is understanding what's actually happening—a major hormonal shift with system-wide effects—and managing the fallout deliberately, with the best available evidence, and without the cultural baggage that has obscured clear thinking about this for generations.
