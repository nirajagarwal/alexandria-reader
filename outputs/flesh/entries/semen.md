# Semen

Most of what you think you know about semen is wrong or incomplete. Start with volume: the average ejaculation is 2-5 milliliters—less than a teaspoon. It feels like more because of where it ends up and how it's delivered, but the actual quantity is minimal. And sperm makes up only about 5% of that volume. The rest is fluid from the prostate and seminal vesicles—transport medium, fuel, chemical warfare agents.

The sperm themselves are among the most specialized cells in the body. Each one is a stripped-down delivery system: a head containing 23 chromosomes, a midpiece packed with mitochondria for fuel, and a flagellum that whips 10-12 times per second. They're produced continuously after puberty, about 1,500 per second, 100 million per day. But the process takes 74 days from start to finish. The sperm you ejaculate today started developing two and a half months ago, which means exposures from that time—heat, toxins, illness—affect what you're working with now.

The composition of seminal fluid is metabolically expensive and surprisingly complex. Fructose provides energy—sperm burn sugar, not fat. Prostaglandins trigger uterine contractions that help transport sperm upward. Zinc is present in concentrations 100 times higher than in blood; it stabilizes DNA and chromatin. Citric acid and enzymes help liquefy the initially gel-like ejaculate. Spermine gives semen its distinctive smell—the same polyamine is in other rapidly dividing tissues, and it has antimicrobial properties. The fluid is slightly alkaline to buffer against the acidic vaginal environment. Everything about it is designed for survival in hostile territory.

The sperm competition theory explains features that seem excessive. In species where females mate with multiple males, sperm from different males compete inside the reproductive tract. Humans show moderate signs of this: testes size relative to body weight falls between bonobos (high sperm competition) and gorillas (low). The seminal vesicle secretions contain proteins that coagulate immediately after ejaculation, forming a copulatory plug that temporarily blocks subsequent insemination. Prostate secretions contain enzymes that then liquefy this plug after 20-30 minutes, freeing the sperm to swim. It's a timed-release mechanism, a temporary territorial claim.

Then there's the kamikaze sperm hypothesis, proposed by Robin Baker and Mark Bellis in the 1990s. They argued that only a fraction of sperm are designed to fertilize the egg. The rest are blocker sperm—misshapen or slow, existing to physically impede rival sperm—or killer sperm, which attack and destroy competitors. The hypothesis is controversial; later work challenges whether the necessary variation exists or whether the presumed blockers are just defective. But the underlying reality is uncontested: most sperm are abnormal by clinical standards. In a healthy sample, less than 15% have normal morphology. The rest have misshapen heads, bent tails, multiple tails, too many heads. Quality control is loose because volume matters more than individual perfection.

Sperm counts are declining. Meta-analyses covering 1973 to 2011 show a 50-60% drop in sperm concentration among Western men. The trend continues in more recent data. Causes are debated but likely include endocrine-disrupting chemicals (phthalates, BPA), obesity, heat exposure, and stress. Laptop use directly on the lap raises scrotal temperature; tight underwear does the same. The testes hang outside the body because sperm production requires temperatures 2-3 degrees below core body temperature. Anything that keeps them warm—saunas, hot tubs, fevers—temporarily crashes production. That 74-day production cycle means a week-long illness can affect fertility for months.

The post-ejaculatory refractory period is driven by prolactin, which surges after orgasm and suppresses dopamine, the neurotransmitter driving sexual motivation. Duration varies wildly—minutes for some men, hours or days for others, and it lengthens with age. The biological function appears to be preventing continuous mating attempts, conserving resources for other survival activities. But the mechanism is centrally mediated, not peripheral. It's not that the testes need time to refill—sperm are always being produced. The limit is neural, not mechanical.

Semen also contains mood-altering compounds. Gordon Gallup's research in the early 2000s suggested that women who have unprotected sex show fewer depressive symptoms than those who use condoms or abstain, controlling for relationship factors. Hormones in seminal plasma—testosterone, estrogen, prostaglandins—can be absorbed through vaginal tissue. Semen also contains nerve growth factor and mood-modulating compounds. The research is thin and politically fraught, but the biochemical potential is there. Semen is not inert fluid; it's a chemical message, and the recipient's tissues respond.

The practical implications land on heat, timing, and general health. If fertility matters, avoid sustained heat to the groin. The effect is reversible but delayed. Time conception attempts with knowledge that sperm quality reflects conditions from the past 10 weeks, not the past 10 days. Obesity, smoking, and high alcohol intake all degrade sperm parameters. Frequent ejaculation—every 2-3 days—produces better samples than long abstinence; beyond a week, sperm quality drops as dead and damaged cells accumulate.

What you're looking at, finally, is metabolically expensive tissue producing millions of cells per day, most of which are disposable, to deliver a tiny payload in an environment designed to kill it. The whole system is optimized for competition, not cooperation—a legacy of evolutionary pressures that still shape what happens in bedrooms and fertility clinics today. Semen is not just reproductive fluid; it's an arms race in liquid form, carrying half a genome and a chemical arsenal designed to get it where it needs to go.
