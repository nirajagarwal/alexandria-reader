# The Tallest Human

Robert Wadlow stood eight feet eleven inches when he died in 1940 at age twenty-two. No confirmed case of human height has exceeded him. He wasn't tall because of good genes or nutrition. He was tall because his pituitary gland wouldn't stop secreting growth hormone—a tumor the size of a grape that his body never recognized as a problem. The pituitary sits behind the bridge of your nose, weighs half a gram, and if it malfunctions in childhood, it can build a body that cannot support itself.

Normal growth happens in pulses. Growth hormone surges at night, triggering the liver to produce insulin-like growth factor 1, which tells bone cells to divide. At puberty, sex hormones eventually fuse the growth plates—cartilage zones at the ends of long bones where new length is added. Once fused, you're done growing vertically. Wadlow's tumor kept producing growth hormone past puberty. His growth plates never closed. He added three inches in the year before he died.

The engineering problems appear around seven feet. The human spine evolved for a much shorter frame. Wadlow wore leg braces from age twelve because his knees couldn't support his weight reliably. He had little sensation in his feet—nerve compression from the sheer length of the pathway. A blister from a poorly fitted brace became infected. The infection spread. Antibiotics didn't exist yet. He died of sepsis, a death entirely preventable a decade later, caused by a body too large for the medical technology of its time.

The tallest living people today—most around seven-six to seven-eight—have acromegaly or gigantism managed with surgery, radiation, or drugs that block growth hormone. Pegvisomant, developed in the 1990s by endocrinologist John Kopchick, is a growth hormone receptor antagonist. It doesn't stop the pituitary from overproducing; it stops the body from responding. Before this class of drugs, pituitary adenomas often required transsphenoidal surgery—entering through the nasal cavity to reach a tumor sitting at the base of the brain. The pituitary is inaccessible from above without cutting through significant brain tissue. Surgeons go in through the nose because it's the only route that doesn't destroy something critical.

What's revealing is how little margin the body has for scale. Acromegaly doesn't just make you tall; it changes proportions. The jaw grows, the hands thicken, the brow ridge expands. Soft tissues continue enlarging even after bone growth stops. The heart enlarges too, often developing cardiomyopathy—the muscle walls thicken but pump less efficiently. Sleep apnea is nearly universal in acromegaly, because the tongue and soft palate overgrow the airway. Joint pain is constant; cartilage grows abnormally, and bones remodel under stress they weren't designed to handle. The body doesn't scale gracefully. It breaks in predictable places.

Height itself is mostly genetic—around eighty percent heritable—but it's polygenic, determined by hundreds of variants across the genome. Lango Allen's 2010 study in *Nature* identified 180 genetic loci associated with height. Each contributes a few millimeters. Exceptionally tall people without pituitary tumors usually have a favorable stack of common variants, not a single "tall gene." But even genetically tall people—say, six-six—aren't hitting the limits Wadlow did. They stopped growing because their endocrine system followed the normal program: growth plates fused on schedule.

There's an upper bound built into mammalian biology. Larger animals have slower heart rates and lower metabolic rates per unit mass. Kleiber's law shows metabolic rate scales to the three-quarters power of body mass. An elephant's cells run cooler than a mouse's. Wadlow's heart was working at the edge of what a human cardiovascular system can sustain. His resting heart rate was elevated; his blood pressure difficult to manage. He was approaching the size where gravity itself becomes a design constraint—where getting blood to the brain requires pressures that damage smaller vessels, where bones thicken to support the load but become too heavy to move efficiently.

Basketball players in the seven-foot range often have shortened careers. Chronic joint issues, back problems, foot stress fractures. Yao Ming retired at thirty after repeated foot and ankle injuries. The same height that made him dominant made him fragile. The human body is optimized for something closer to five-eight, give or take a few inches. Go much beyond six-six and you're in a zone where the benefits of reach and leverage start getting outweighed by material fatigue.

The Netherlands has the tallest population average—men around six feet, women five-seven—a shift that happened in just a century, driven by nutrition, healthcare, and sexual selection. But even Dutch height is plateauing now, possibly hitting the ceiling of what's sustainable. The trend isn't toward seven-foot averages. It's toward stabilization in the mid-six range, which suggests we're approaching the optimal compromise between the advantages of size and the mechanical costs of being large.

Wadlow's skeleton is still on display at the Alton Museum of History in Illinois. The leg braces are there too. What you see is a body that outgrew its support systems—nerves that couldn't keep up with lengthening limbs, a circulatory system pushed past efficient limits, bones that kept adding length without adding proportional strength. Growth is supposed to be regulated. The body has multiple feedback loops to stop it. When those fail, you get a case study in why the limits exist. Wadlow wasn't just unusually tall. He was a demonstration of what happens when a half-gram gland ignores the constraints evolution spent millions of years calibrating. You can build a body bigger than the design allows, but it won't hold up. Gravity doesn't negotiate.
