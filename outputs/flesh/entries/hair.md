# Hair

Hair is dead. The strand emerging from your scalp has no blood supply, no nerves, no cellular activity. What you brush, cut, and wash is a column of compressed keratin—the same protein that makes up your fingernails and the outer layer of your skin. The only living part is the follicle beneath the surface, which manufactures hair in a cycle that determines far more about your appearance than any product you apply afterward.

The follicle operates on a schedule. It grows hair for two to seven years—the anagen phase—then shuts down for a few weeks, then sheds the strand and starts over. At any moment, about 90 percent of your scalp follicles are in active growth while 10 percent are resting or shedding. You lose roughly a hundred hairs a day as part of this normal cycle. The variation in anagen length explains why some people can grow hair to their waist and others plateau at shoulder length. The follicle doesn't grow forever; it has a timer.

That timer is genetically set, but male pattern baldness overrides it through a different mechanism. The hormone dihydrotestosterone—a metabolite of testosterone—binds to receptors in scalp follicles and progressively shrinks them. The follicles don't die; they miniaturize, producing thinner and shorter hairs until they produce only colorless vellus hair, the near-invisible fuzz that covers most of your body. This is why balding men still have "hair" where the hairline receded—just hair that's barely visible. The follicle is still there, still cycling, just crippled.

The genetics are strange. The primary susceptibility gene sits on the X chromosome, inherited from your mother, but multiple genes contribute and the inheritance pattern is polygenic and poorly predictable. The folk wisdom about checking your maternal grandfather is loosely grounded in this X-linked component but overstates the case. Plenty of men lose hair with no family history; plenty keep it despite one. The only reliable predictor is looking at your own hairline in your twenties. If it's receding, it will continue. The process is androgen-mediated and progressive.

Treatment works by blocking the process. Finasteride inhibits the enzyme that converts testosterone to DHT, reducing scalp DHT by about 70 percent. In clinical trials from the 1990s, led by researchers at Merck, about two-thirds of men maintained or increased hair count after two years. The drug stops progression; it rarely reverses loss completely. Minoxidil, originally a blood pressure medication, prolongs the anagen phase through a mechanism still not entirely understood. Both require continuous use. Stop, and the hair loss resumes where it would have been without treatment.

Hair color is cellular self-destruction. Melanocytes in the follicle produce pigment and inject it into the growing hair shaft. When these cells die or stop producing melanin, the hair grows in colorless—white or gray depending on how light scatters through the keratin structure. This happens on a per-follicle basis, which is why you go gray in patches rather than all at once. The melanocyte death is gradual and irreversible. The average age of onset is mid-thirties for white populations, a decade earlier for some Asian populations, later for those with African ancestry. Stress does not cause gray hair, though a 2020 study from Columbia University found that stress can correlate with hair color in ways that reverse when stress subsides—suggesting that in rare cases, pigment production pauses rather than stops. But this is the exception. Most graying is permanent melanocyte failure.

Hair texture is architectural. Straight hair grows from a round follicle, producing a cylindrical shaft. Curly hair grows from an elliptical follicle, producing a flattened, ribbon-like shaft that spirals. The degree of curl is the degree of ellipse. This is genetically determined and irreversible. Relaxers and perms chemically break and reform the disulfide bonds within keratin to temporarily reshape the structure, but new growth emerges in its original form. The follicle's shape determines the output.

What damages hair damages the dead shaft, not the living follicle. Heat styling, bleach, and physical stress (tight ponytails, extensions) fray the cuticle—the overlapping scale-like outer layer. Once damaged, the cuticle doesn't repair. The shaft is dead tissue. You can't feed it nutrients or heal it; you can only coat it with smoothing agents (conditioners, serums) or cut off the damaged length and wait for new growth. This is why split ends don't mend and why hair "health" is about minimizing new damage, not reversing old.

The metabolic cost of hair is low enough that nutritional deficiency has to be severe before it affects growth. Starvation, iron deficiency anemia, and crash diets can push follicles into early shedding—a condition called telogen effluvium—but this is temporary. When nutrition resumes, growth resumes. The exception is protein deficiency, which can alter hair texture during growth since hair is literally made of protein. But in well-nourished populations, supplements marketed for hair health are mostly addressing deficiencies that don't exist.

What you're optimizing when you care for your hair is not the biology—that's fixed—but the lifespan of the dead shaft. Minimizing heat, avoiding harsh chemicals, reducing friction during sleep (silk pillowcases, loose styles) extends how long each strand remains intact after the follicle extrudes it. That's the margin of control. The follicle determines thickness, color, growth rate, and curl. You determine how much of that length survives before breaking off.

The hair you see is a biological afterthought, a waste product extruded and abandoned. The living part is hidden, working on schedules you can't directly influence, producing a product you can only preserve or destroy. Most of what people do to "improve" their hair addresses the already-dead shaft. The few interventions that matter—blocking DHT, prolonging growth phase—target the hidden machinery. Everything else is damage control on a corpse that happens to still be attached.
