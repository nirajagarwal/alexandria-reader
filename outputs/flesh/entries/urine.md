# Urine

Your kidneys are not removing waste. They're removing *everything*, then reclaiming what's worth keeping. Every drop of blood passes through them roughly forty times per day. Each pass, the kidneys filter out water, salts, glucose, amino acids, urea, creatinine—essentially everything small enough to squeeze through their molecular sieves. Then they run the filtrate through a series of tubules that reabsorb the valuable molecules and leave the garbage. What emerges as urine is what your body declined to save.

This matters because it reveals how filtering actually works at the scale of molecular machinery. The kidney doesn't identify toxins and pluck them out. It dumps everything below a certain size, then selectively rescues useful molecules. It's easier to reclaim the good than to identify and eject the bad. The consequence: anything your kidneys fail to reabsorb leaves your body, whether it should or not. This is why diabetics spill glucose into urine—not because glucose is waste, but because blood sugar exceeds the kidneys' reabsorption capacity. The tubules can only reclaim so much. The rest gets discarded, taking water with it, producing the characteristic thirst and frequent urination of uncontrolled diabetes.

The volume question is where most people's understanding fails. You produce roughly 180 liters of filtrate daily—forty-seven gallons. You urinate one to two liters. The kidneys are reabsorbing 99% of what they filter, reclaiming water and electrolytes in precise ratios to maintain blood chemistry. Dehydration triggers antidiuretic hormone, which makes the collecting ducts more permeable, pulling more water back into circulation. Your urine darkens and concentrates. Overhydration suppresses the hormone; the ducts stay tight, and you excrete dilute, pale urine. The color is a readout of how hard your kidneys are working to conserve water.

The composition is revealing. Urea, the primary solid component, is a nitrogen disposal problem solved. Proteins break down into amino acids; amino acids get stripped of nitrogen for reuse or disposal. Free ammonia is toxic, so the liver converts it to urea—two nitrogens bonded to a carbon and oxygen. Harmless, water-soluble, easily filtered. Humans are ureotelic, meaning we excrete nitrogen as urea. Birds and reptiles are uricotelic—they excrete uric acid, a paste that conserves water. Fish are ammonotelic—they dump ammonia straight into surrounding water. The form your nitrogen waste takes determines how much water you need to survive. Urea is a compromise: less water than ammonia requires, more than uric acid needs.

The pathology teaches the mechanism. Kidney stones form when urine becomes supersaturated with calcium, oxalate, or uric acid. They crystallize in the tubules or collecting system and grow. Most are calcium oxalate. The pain—often described as worse than childbirth—comes when a stone tries to pass through the ureter, a tube the diameter of a pencil lead. But the revealing part is prevention: drink more water. Dilution prevents crystallization. The kidneys can handle high solute loads if there's enough water to keep everything dissolved. Stones form not because you consume too much calcium or oxalate, but because you concentrate your urine too much, too often.

Urinalysis is ancient diagnostics. Physicians tasted urine for sweetness to diagnose diabetes for millennia before blood glucose tests. The practice died out not because it didn't work, but because it was unpleasant and chemical tests became available. Color, clarity, smell—all carry information. Dark urine suggests dehydration or liver disease. Cloudy urine can mean infection or crystals. Sweet-smelling urine might indicate diabetes; foul-smelling, infection; maple syrup-smelling, a rare metabolic disorder where the body can't break down certain amino acids. The kidneys excrete what's in your blood, so urine is a chemical snapshot.

The frequency question is mostly about bladder capacity and habit. The bladder stretches to hold about 400-600 milliliters before signaling the urge to urinate. Some people void at 200 milliliters out of habit or convenience; their bladders learn to send early signals. Others wait until full capacity. The detrusor muscle in the bladder wall contracts to push urine out; the sphincters relax to allow flow. Voluntary control is learned in childhood. Incontinence in adults usually means either the detrusor contracts involuntarily, the sphincters weaken, or the bladder fails to empty completely, leaving residual urine that triggers frequent urges. Pelvic floor exercises strengthen the muscles involved. Habit retraining can reset the bladder's signaling threshold.

What degrades the system: chronic dehydration, high-sodium diets, excessive protein intake (which increases urea production), holding urine too long (which allows bacteria to multiply), and ignoring infections. A urinary tract infection that ascends to the kidneys becomes pyelonephritis, which scars kidney tissue. Repeated infections cause cumulative damage. The kidneys have reserve capacity—you can lose one entirely and the other compensates—but they don't regenerate. Damage accumulates.

What supports it: adequate hydration, low enough sodium that your kidneys aren't working overtime to maintain electrolyte balance, and not ignoring the urge to urinate for hours at a stretch. The bladder is a temporary holding tank, not a long-term storage solution. Bacteria multiply in stagnant urine. Flush them out.

The deeper implication: your kidneys are running a continuous chemical analysis of your blood, making thousands of microscopic decisions every second about what to keep and what to discard. Urine is the output of that analysis—a diagnostic report your body generates for free, if you pay attention to it. Most people treat urination as an inconvenience. It's actually a readout of how well your body is managing the molecular problem of staying alive in a chemical environment that's constantly shifting. The color, volume, and frequency tell you whether your kidneys are coping or struggling. Learn to read it.
