# Bone

Bone is alive. This is the first thing people get wrong. They think of it as inert scaffold, limestone architecture, the leftover frame after everything interesting has decomposed. But bone is metabolically active tissue, constantly remodeling itself, housing marrow that produces two million red blood cells per second, storing minerals that buffer blood chemistry, and responding to mechanical stress by rebuilding stronger where force is applied. The skeleton you have today is not the skeleton you had seven years ago. It's been torn down and reconstructed, cell by cell, in a continuous renovation project that runs from birth until death.

The remodeling process involves two opposed cell types locked in perpetual negotiation. Osteoclasts dissolve bone, releasing acids and enzymes that break down mineralized matrix. Osteoblasts rebuild it, secreting collagen and directing mineral deposition. In a healthy adult, these forces balance. In childhood, building outpaces breakdown—bones lengthen and thicken. After about age thirty, breakdown begins to win. You lose bone density slowly, then faster in the hormonal shift of menopause or andropause. The final decades are usually a managed retreat, trying to slow the dissolution.

This matters because bone strength is not fixed. It adapts. Wolff's law, formulated by German anatomist Julius Wolff in 1892, states that bone remodels along lines of mechanical stress. Load-bearing bones thicken. Unused bones thin. Astronauts lose one to two percent of bone mass per month in microgravity despite exercise protocols. Bedridden patients lose bone density in weeks. Conversely, the striking arm bones of professional tennis players are measurably thicker than their non-dominant side. Bone is not a static frame but a responsive structure that allocates resources based on use.

The mineral composition explains why this matters for whole-body health. Bone is a calcium reservoir. When blood calcium drops, the parathyroid glands secrete hormone that instructs osteoclasts to dissolve bone and release calcium into circulation. This keeps your heart beating regularly and your neurons firing, but it means bone health is downstream of systemic calcium balance. Chronic low calcium intake or poor vitamin D status (vitamin D is required for calcium absorption) forces the body into continuous bone-mining mode. You don't feel this happening. Bone loss is silent until you fracture.

The fracture risk matters more as you age because falls become more common and bones become more brittle. Hip fractures are a mortality event in the elderly. Twenty to thirty percent of hip fracture patients die within a year—not from the break itself but from complications of immobility: pneumonia, blood clots, infections. Another forty percent never regain full independence. A hip fracture at eighty is often the beginning of the end, which makes the decades of prior bone loss a silent countdown.

Peak bone mass is established by your late twenties. After that, you're spending from an account you can't deposit into, only withdraw from more slowly. The standard interventions are mechanical loading—walking, resistance training, anything that stresses the skeleton—and adequate calcium and vitamin D. But the calcium supplement industry is murkier than it appears. Some studies show supplementation reduces fracture risk; others show no effect or even increased cardiovascular risk. The confusion likely comes from calcium source and dosage. High-dose supplements may raise blood calcium in ways that dietary calcium does not, and the body handles these differently. The safer bet is dietary calcium from dairy, leafy greens, or fortified foods, which releases slowly.

Vitamin D is less controversial. Deficiency is common at northern latitudes and in people who avoid sun exposure. Without adequate vitamin D, you can consume all the calcium in the world and absorb little of it. The bone thins regardless. Blood tests can measure 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels; below 20 ng/mL is deficient, 30-50 ng/mL is adequate. Supplementation is cheap, low-risk, and often necessary in winter months above the 35th parallel.

The architecture of bone itself is worth understanding. Compact bone forms the dense outer shell. Trabecular bone fills the interior—a lattice of struts aligned along stress lines, like the internal structure of an aircraft wing. This lattice is where remodeling happens fastest and where osteoporosis first shows up. The trabecular network thins, struts perforate and break, and the bone becomes a foam that collapses under loads it once handled easily. Hip fractures usually occur through trabecular bone. Wrist fractures from catching yourself in a fall—same mechanism. The outer shell holds, but the interior has already failed.

There's also the bone marrow, which is not structural but hematopoietic—blood-forming. Red marrow produces blood cells; yellow marrow stores fat. In infants, nearly all marrow is red. In adults, red marrow retreats to the pelvis, sternum, ribs, vertebrae, and skull. This is why bone marrow biopsies are taken from the hip. If you need to sample the blood-forming tissue, you go where it still lives.

What this means for how you live: bone health is built in youth and defended in age. Weight-bearing exercise throughout life is non-negotiable. Walking is enough to maintain; running or resistance training builds. Calcium and vitamin D matter, but whole foods beat supplements for calcium. Smoking and excessive alcohol both accelerate bone loss. Body weight matters too—very low body weight is a fracture risk because it reduces mechanical loading and often correlates with nutritional deficiency. The skeleton needs to bear weight to justify its own maintenance.

The deeper implication is that bone is not separate from the rest of you. It's metabolically integrated, hormonally responsive, and constantly in conversation with the forces you apply to it. The strength of your skeleton in old age is a record of how you've lived—how much you moved, what you ate, whether your endocrine system stayed balanced. Bone remembers, and eventually it either holds you up or it doesn't.
