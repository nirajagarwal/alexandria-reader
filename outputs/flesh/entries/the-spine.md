# The Spine

Your spine is a structural compromise between two incompatible demands: rigidity and flexibility. It needs to hold your head up while letting you twist, bend, and absorb shock. The solution evolution arrived at—a vertical stack of thirty-three bones separated by cartilage pads—works, but barely. Most back pain isn't injury or disease. It's the ordinary physics of this design hitting its limits.

The basic architecture is counterintuitive. The spine isn't straight. It curves in four places: neck forward, upper back backward, lower back forward, tailbone backward. These curves are structural, not decorative. They distribute load and absorb shock far better than a straight column would. When you walk, the curves flex and spring back, dissipating impact that would otherwise travel straight to your brain. A straight spine would shatter. The curves are what let you run without your skull rattling loose.

But the curves also create stress points. Each vertebra tilts relative to the one below it, creating shear forces at the joints. The lower back—the lumbar curve—takes the worst of it. That's where your upper body's weight concentrates on a forward-tilting platform. Stand up and your L4-L5 disc is compressed by several hundred pounds of force. Sit down and it's worse—sitting increases lumbar disc pressure by up to 40 percent because your hip flexors pull the pelvis forward, exaggerating the curve. Office chairs aren't destroying your back; the geometry of sitting is.

Between each vertebra sits a disc—a gel-filled sac wrapped in tough fibrous rings. The gel is 80 percent water in young adults, which gives it hydraulic properties. Pressure on one side forces the disc to bulge in the opposite direction, letting the spine flex. But discs have no blood supply after adolescence. They absorb nutrients by soaking them up when pressure releases, mostly while you sleep. Dehydration, constant compression, and age all reduce water content. By your sixties, your discs are more like dried sponges, less cushion and more bone-on-bone. You lose an inch of height between morning and evening just from disc compression. You lose another inch or two permanently over a lifetime.

The disc failure pattern is predictable. The outer rings develop small tears from repeated stress, usually in the back where pressure is highest. Gel pushes through the tears—a herniation. If it presses on a nerve root, you get radiating pain: sciatica, neck pain shooting down an arm. But here's the strange part: large herniations often hurt less than small ones. A big herniation breaks through completely and stops pressing on the nerve. A small one wedges itself right where the irritation is maximal. MRI studies show that 30 to 40 percent of people with no back pain have herniated discs. The anatomy doesn't predict the symptoms as neatly as you'd expect.

What does predict back pain is muscle endurance, not strength. Researchers led by Stuart McGill at the University of Waterloo found that people with chronic low back pain don't have weak backs—they have backs that fatigue quickly. The small stabilizer muscles around the spine give out, the large movers take over badly, and the spine starts moving in ways it shouldn't. The classic gym advice to strengthen your core with crunches and sit-ups makes this worse. Those exercises train the rectus abdominis—the six-pack muscle—which pulls the spine into flexion, exactly the motion that damages discs. What actually works is endurance training for the deep stabilizers: planks, bird dogs, dead bugs. Holding position, not moving through range.

The spine's real job is to resist motion, not create it. Movement should come from the hips and shoulders. The spine stabilizes while the limbs move. When people lift by rounding their back, they're forcing the spine to hinge where it shouldn't. The lumbar discs aren't hinges; they're shock absorbers. Hinging at the hip—a skill most adults have lost—loads the legs and glutes while keeping the spine neutral. Every deadlift tutorial hammers this, but the deeper point is that it applies to picking up groceries or tying your shoes. The spine is safest when it's static.

Sitting is the slow killer because it trains this pattern backwards. Hours in a chair teach your body that the spine should stay rounded, the hip flexors should stay short, and the glutes can turn off. Stand up and your nervous system doesn't remember how to stabilize a neutral spine. You hinge at the lower back because your hips are locked. This is why back pain has tracked with office work across every industrialized country. The Swedish researcher Alf Nachemson measured intradiscal pressure in the 1970s and found that sitting unsupported or slouched generated more force on L3 than standing or walking. The modern posture "epidemic" isn't new ergonomic dangers—it's the same geometry being applied for longer durations.

The tailbone—the coccyx—is vestigial but not useless. Four fused vertebrae left over from when your ancestors had tails. It anchors pelvic floor muscles and ligaments. Break it, usually from falling directly on your ass, and sitting becomes excruciating for months. There's no good treatment. The bone heals if you stop sitting on it, which you can't. It's a design flaw with no workaround except time.

The spinal cord itself ends around L1-L2, about waist height. Below that, the spinal canal contains only nerve roots, the cauda equina—"horse's tail." This matters for injury: a lumbar disc herniation can compress nerves but won't sever the spinal cord because the cord isn't there. A cervical injury, neck level, can sever the cord and cause paralysis. Christopher Reeve's injury was at C1-C2, the highest vertebrae, requiring permanent ventilation because the nerves controlling the diaphragm exit lower, at C3-C5. The difference between a neck injury and a back injury isn't severity—it's location and what nerves are in the line of fire.

You inherit some of your spine's durability. Disc degeneration runs in families. Some people have naturally thicker discs, better hydration, more durable collagen. But behavior matters more over decades. Loading patterns, sitting hours, and how you move accumulate. The spine is a vertical structure that evolution adapted from a horizontal one about six million years ago. That's recent, and the adaptation is incomplete. You're living in a prototype that works best when you move frequently, load it correctly, and don't ask it to hold one position for eight hours a day. The pain isn't failure—it's feedback that the design has limits, and you've found them.
