# Infection

You are always infected. Right now, bacteria are replicating in your gut, viruses are integrating into your cells, fungi are colonizing your skin. Most of these you need. Some are neutral passengers. A few could kill you if they crossed the wrong barrier or if your immune system looked away for an afternoon. The body's relationship with microbes is not war—it's détente, with constant skirmishes along contested borders and occasional catastrophic treaty violations.

The numbers are absurd. Your body contains roughly 30 trillion human cells. You also contain roughly 38 trillion bacterial cells, most in your colon. You are, by cell count, slightly more bacteria than human. Add in viruses—an estimated 380 trillion, mostly bacteriophages that prey on your bacteria—and fungi and archaea, and the genetic material in your body is overwhelmingly not yours. The human genome contains about 20,000 genes. Your gut microbiome contains more than 3 million. You are a colony pretending to be an organism.

This matters because the line between infection and symbiosis is movable and heavily policed. The same *E. coli* that manufactures vitamin K in your intestine can cause fatal peritonitis if it gets into your abdomen. Location determines identity. The immune system maintains borders, not through eradication but through constant surveillance and controlled tolerance. When this system miscalibrates—too permissive or too aggressive—you get inflammatory bowel disease, sepsis, autoimmunity. The body doesn't aim to be sterile. It aims to keep the right organisms in the right places.

The germ theory revolution obscured this. In the 1860s, Louis Pasteur demonstrated that microbes caused fermentation and disease. By the 1880s, Robert Koch had established criteria for proving that specific bacteria cause specific diseases. This was clarifying and lifesaving—cholera is *Vibrio cholerae*, tuberculosis is *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*—but it embedded a model where microbes are invaders and health is their absence. A century of antibiotics reinforced this. Penicillin worked by killing bacteria indiscriminately in a localized area. The body benefited from collateral damage because the invader was worse. But the metaphor stuck: good body, bad bug.

The microbiome research has complicated this. Humans raised in sterile environments develop immune dysregulation. Germ-free mice have underdeveloped immune systems, altered brain chemistry, and different behavior. The presence of bacteria—specific strains in specific ratios—is apparently necessary for normal development. This led to the hygiene hypothesis: that rising rates of allergies and autoimmune disease in developed countries result from reduced microbial exposure in childhood. The immune system, deprived of its usual training targets, starts attacking pollen and peanuts and self-tissue. Too little infection creates pathology.

But the details resist simplification. Not all microbes are beneficial. Not all exposure is good. The same unwashed vegetables that deliver diverse bacteria can also deliver *Salmonella*. Measles and polio are not character-building exposures. What matters is timing, dose, and context—variables that make public health messaging nearly impossible. "Some infections during childhood may prevent allergies, but also some infections kill children" does not fit on a pamphlet.

The battlefield is chemical. When bacteria invade tissue, immune cells release cytokines—signaling molecules that recruit more immune cells and raise the local temperature. Fever is not a malfunction but a tactic: many pathogens replicate poorly at 102°F. The aching, fatigue, and loss of appetite that accompany infection are orchestrated responses, not side effects. The body is reallocating resources to immune function, which is metabolically expensive. You feel terrible because your immune system is spending calories and attention that usually go to movement and thought. The pathogen wants you functional and social—spreading. Your immune system wants you in bed, isolated, fasting. The symptoms are the compromise.

Antibiotics broke the evolutionary stalemate. Bacteria replicate every 20 minutes under good conditions. Evolution that takes humans 100,000 years takes bacteria a week. Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin in 1928; by 1945, penicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* was widespread in hospitals. Methicillin was introduced in 1959 to treat resistant staph; methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA) appeared in 1961. The cycle continues. Every antibiotic eventually selects for resistance. The question is how fast.

This creates a tragedy of the commons. Antibiotics work best when used sparingly across a population—preserving their effectiveness by limiting resistance. But they work best for the individual when used immediately at the first sign of infection. Doctors face patients demanding treatment, and prescribing is easier than explaining why a viral sore throat doesn't warrant antibiotics. Agricultural operations face livestock that grow faster on antibiotic-laced feed. About 70% of antibiotics sold in the U.S. go to farm animals, not humans. The benefit accrues to individual actors; the cost is shared.

The body's memory of infection is where vaccines intervene. Immunological memory means that surviving an infection leaves you prepared to fight it again—faster, harder, more specifically. A vaccine is a controlled infection with a defanged pathogen (or a component of one), triggering memory without the disease. This is elegant when it works, but it works only for pathogens that don't mutate rapidly. Measles mutates slowly; one vaccine protects for life. Influenza mutates fast; you need a new vaccine each year, and it's often a guess at which strains will circulate. HIV mutates so rapidly that vaccine development has failed for forty years.

What you should actually do is less obvious than it seems. Soap and water reduce infection transmission more than any other intervention—cheap, effective, massively underused. Hand sanitizer is less effective than washing but better than nothing. Most respiratory infections spread through droplets, not surfaces, so obsessive surface disinfection is mostly theater. Crowded indoor spaces with poor ventilation are high-risk; outdoor transmission is rare. Antibiotics treat bacterial infections and do nothing for viruses; taking them for a cold accelerates resistance and damages your microbiome for no benefit.

The chronic infections are where the picture darkens. *Helicobacter pylori* in the stomach causes ulcers and stomach cancer. Human papillomavirus causes cervical cancer. Hepatitis B and C cause liver cancer. Epstein-Barr virus is linked to several lymphomas. About 15% of cancers worldwide are caused by persistent infections—a fraction that would have seemed impossible before the research connected them. The germ theory of disease is still expanding.

What infections reveal is that you are not a closed system. You are a negotiated territory, inhabited by trillions of organisms with their own evolutionary interests, defended by an immune system that has to distinguish self from non-self billions of times a day and mostly gets it right. The symbiotic bacteria are farming you as much as you're hosting them. The pathogens are trying to replicate, not harm you—harm is incidental. You feel sick because your body is spending everything it has to stay alive. Infection is the ongoing condition. Health is just successful management of it.
