# Mucus

You produce about a liter of mucus every day. Most of it you swallow without noticing. This isn't malfunction—it's the system working. Mucus is your first line of defense against a world trying to colonize your internal surfaces, and the fact that it's disgusting when expelled is precisely because it's done its job: caught and bound the things that would make you sick.

The word itself comes from Latin *mucus*, from the Proto-Indo-European root *meug-*, meaning slippery or slimy. But slipperiness is the point. Mucus is a hydrogel—a network of long, branching proteins called mucins suspended in water, salts, enzymes, and antibodies. The mucins are negatively charged and heavily glycosylated, studded with sugar molecules that attract water. This creates a viscous, sticky layer that coats your respiratory tract, digestive system, and reproductive organs—anywhere your inside meets the outside world.

The respiratory mucus layer moves. Cilia, the microscopic hair-like structures lining your airways, beat in coordinated waves about fifteen times per second, propelling the mucus blanket upward toward your throat at roughly a millimeter per minute. Everything that lands in your nose or lungs—dust, pollen, bacteria, viruses, smoke particles—gets trapped in this moving carpet and transported up and out. You swallow it, and stomach acid finishes what the mucus started. This mucociliary escalator clears your lungs continuously. When it fails, infection follows.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, chronic bronchitis, cystic fibrosis—all involve mucus gone wrong. In cystic fibrosis, a genetic mutation affects chloride channels, altering the salt and water balance that keeps mucus fluid. The result is thick, dehydrated mucus that the cilia can't move. It accumulates, blocks airways, and becomes a breeding ground for bacteria. Pseudomonas aeruginosa colonizes these mucus plugs and forms biofilms that antibiotics can't penetrate. Patients spend hours each day in mechanical percussion therapy, physically shaking the mucus loose. The disease itself is elegant in a terrible way—change one ion channel, and the whole clearance system collapses.

Smoking does something similar through a different mechanism. Cigarette smoke paralyzes cilia. Initially they slow, then stop beating altogether. The mucus layer stagnates. Smokers produce more mucus in response to constant irritation, but they can't clear it. The morning "smoker's cough" is the system attempting to expel what should have been cleared overnight. After years, the cilia die, replaced by scar tissue. The escalator stops permanently.

The composition of mucus changes based on what it's fighting. Neutrophils—white blood cells—pour into infected mucus, releasing DNA nets that trap bacteria. This makes mucus thicker and changes its color. Clear mucus is baseline. White or yellow indicates immune activation. Green means high concentrations of myeloperoxidase, an enzyme from dying neutrophils. The color is a battlefield report. Brown suggests old blood, usually from irritated vessels. Mucus isn't just barrier; it's an active immunological site.

Some pathogens have evolved specifically to defeat mucus. Influenza virus produces neuraminidase, an enzyme that cleaves the sugar molecules on mucins, liquefying the mucus barrier so the virus can reach the cells beneath. Helicobacter pylori, which causes ulcers, produces urease to neutralize stomach acid and mucinase to dissolve the stomach's protective mucus layer. These are not accidents—they're adaptations shaped by millions of years of coevolution with our mucus defenses.

The nose alone produces about a third of your daily mucus output, but most of it drains backward into your throat, not forward out your nostrils. This is why "post-nasal drip" is not an abnormality but normal function you've become aware of—usually because infection or allergies have increased production or changed consistency. The irritation triggers goblet cells in the respiratory epithelium to secrete more mucus, and mast cells release histamine that dilates blood vessels and increases fluid leakage into tissues. Antihistamines work by blocking this cascade, drying mucus production at the source.

Decongestants like pseudoephedrine constrict blood vessels, reducing the fluid component. Expectorants like guaifenesin allegedly thin mucus by increasing water content, though the evidence for efficacy is thin. What definitely works: hydration, humidity, and time. Mucus thickness depends on water availability. Dehydration makes it viscous and difficult to clear.

The digestive tract produces even more mucus than the respiratory system—several liters per day across the stomach, intestines, and colon. This mucus protects the gut lining from its own digestive enzymes and provides a habitat for commensal bacteria. The mucus layer in the colon is dual-structured: a dense inner layer that's nearly sterile, and a loose outer layer where bacteria live and feed. This separation prevents direct bacterial contact with intestinal cells while allowing the microbiome to thrive close by. Inflammatory bowel diseases like ulcerative colitis involve breakdown of this mucus barrier, exposing tissue to bacterial invasion.

Cervical mucus changes throughout the menstrual cycle—thick and acidic most of the time, hostile to sperm, but thin and alkaline during ovulation, forming channels that guide sperm through the cervix. This is biochemical gating, opening and closing access to the uterus based on hormonal signaling.

The disgust response to mucus is interesting because it's context-dependent. You swallow mucus all day without reaction, but seeing it expelled triggers revulsion. This makes evolutionary sense—visible mucus often means infection, something to avoid. But it also means people treat mucus as waste when it's actually a sophisticated, constantly renewed defensive barrier. You're not producing mucus to get rid of it. You're producing it to keep functioning.

What degrades this system: smoking, chronic dehydration, dry air, and anything that damages cilia. What supports it: adequate water intake, humid environments, and not smoking. The mucus system is resilient but not invincible. Treat it as infrastructure, not as something to suppress.
