# Thirst

You are always slightly dehydrated. Not dangerously—just persistently behind on water intake by enough that your body has adapted to it as normal. The thirst signal is a late-stage warning, not an early alert. By the time you feel thirsty, you've already lost one to two percent of your body water, enough to affect mood, attention, and physical performance. The system is designed for scarcity, not abundance, and it shows.

The mechanism is more precise than it feels. Osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect rising sodium concentration in blood plasma—a few percentage points above baseline triggers the signal. But thirst doesn't tell you how much to drink. It tells you to start drinking. The shutoff comes not from rehydration but from throat and stomach sensors detecting water arrival. You stop feeling thirsty ten to fifteen minutes before the water enters your bloodstream. This anticipatory satiation works well for steady sipping but badly for gulping—people routinely overshoot or undershoot because they're responding to the wrong signal.

Neurobiologist Zachary Knight at UCSF mapped this in mice with calcium imaging, watching individual neurons fire when water touched the mouth, then quiet before the water was absorbed. The thirst circuit predicts relief based on drinking behavior, not actual cellular rehydration. Evolution optimized for not dying of thirst on the savanna, which meant drinking quickly at water sources and moving on. It did not optimize for deciding how many ounces of water you need at your desk.

The "eight glasses a day" guideline has no scientific basis. It originated in a 1945 Food and Nutrition Board recommendation that eight cups of fluid would be adequate for most people—including the fluid in food, which contributes roughly 20% of daily water intake. Somewhere in the retelling, the food water got dropped and the prescription became rigid. Actual hydration needs vary with body size, activity, temperature, altitude, and diet. Someone eating watermelon and soup has different requirements than someone eating crackers and cheese. The kidneys adjust output across a wide range. You don't need a target number; you need to respond to thirst and monitor urine color, which should be pale yellow, not clear and not dark.

But certain populations lose the thirst signal. Older adults experience diminished osmoreceptor sensitivity—the concentration change that triggers thirst in a young person doesn't register. This is why dehydration is a leading cause of hospitalization in the elderly, even in developed countries with abundant water. They're not ignoring thirst; they're not feeling it. The mechanism degrades, silently. Caregivers learn to offer water on schedule, not on request.

Athletes present a different problem. Overhydration. Endurance events began pushing aggressive hydration protocols in the 1990s, and cases of exercise-associated hyponatremia—dangerously low blood sodium from drinking too much water—started appearing in marathons and triathlons. Tamara Hew-Butler, a researcher at Oakland University, documented deaths from overhydration in healthy runners. They drank according to a schedule, not thirst, and diluted their blood sodium below functional levels. The kidneys can't excrete water fast enough to compensate for forced overdrinking during exercise. The advice has since reversed: drink to thirst, even in endurance events.

Dehydration affects cognition before you notice physical symptoms. Studies on students by Lawrence Armstrong and colleagues at the University of Connecticut showed that mild dehydration—1.5% body mass loss from restricted fluid intake—impaired mood and increased perception of task difficulty. The subjects weren't thirsty yet, but they were already less focused and more irritable. Hydration status affects headache frequency, anxiety levels, and short-term memory. The brain is 75% water and doesn't function well when that percentage drops.

What dehydrates you: obviously sweat, but also breathing (you exhale water vapor), urination (kidneys need water to clear waste), and digestion (drawing fluid into the gut). Alcohol blocks vasopressin, the hormone that tells kidneys to retain water, which is why you urinate more when drinking and wake up dehydrated. Caffeine has a much smaller diuretic effect than people assume—habitual coffee drinkers develop tolerance, and the water in coffee more than compensates for the mild increase in urination. But high-sodium diets pull water into circulation to dilute blood salt, which you then excrete, requiring replacement.

Cold weather dehydrates as much as heat. You breathe out more water vapor in dry, cold air, and you don't sweat visibly, so the fluid loss goes unnoticed. Winter athletes often underdrink. High altitude compounds this—lower air pressure increases respiration rate and fluid loss through breathing. Mountaineers have to force hydration against blunted thirst signals.

The kidneys are the real marvel here. They filter 180 liters of blood plasma per day and reabsorb 99% of it, adjusting output constantly to maintain blood volume and electrolyte balance. Concentrated urine means they're working hard to conserve water. Dilute urine means you're well-hydrated or overhydrated. But kidney function declines with age and chronic disease, and once it's compromised, fluid balance becomes precarious—too much or too little can spiral into crisis.

What you should do: drink water when you're thirsty, drink more if your urine is dark, and pay attention when circumstances increase fluid loss—heat, altitude, exercise, illness. Don't force hydration to an arbitrary schedule. Don't ignore thirst because you're busy. If you're older or caring for someone older, schedule regular water intake regardless of thirst sensation. If you're exercising hard and long, drink to thirst but not beyond it. The system works if you let it, but it was built for different conditions than you live in, and it doesn't always warn you in time.
