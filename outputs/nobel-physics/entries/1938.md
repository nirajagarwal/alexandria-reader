# 1938 — Slow Neutrons

Enrico Fermi won the 1938 Nobel Prize in Physics for demonstrating the existence of new radioactive elements produced by neutron bombardment, and for his discovery that slow neutrons are extraordinarily effective at inducing nuclear reactions. He was thirty-seven, already one of the century's most formidable physicists, and he would not return to Italy. When he traveled to Stockholm in December to receive the prize, his Jewish wife Laura and their children came with him. From Sweden, the Fermi family continued to America. Mussolini's racial laws had made Rome impossible; the Nobel Prize provided an exit.

The physics that won Fermi the prize had begun four years earlier, in the spring of 1934, on the Via Panisperna in Rome. James Chadwick had discovered the neutron in 1932 — an electrically neutral particle with roughly the mass of a proton. Within months, physicists recognized it as a powerful new probe of the atomic nucleus. Unlike alpha particles or protons, neutrons carried no electric charge. They wouldn't be repelled by the positive charge surrounding the nucleus. They could slip inside and trigger transformations that charged particles couldn't reach.

Fermi, leading a group of brilliant young physicists in Rome — Franco Rasetti, Edoardo Amaldi, Emilio Segrè, Bruno Pontecorvo — decided to bombard every element in the periodic table with neutrons. Systematically. Element by element, starting with hydrogen. They would see what happened.

The experiments were simple in conception, intricate in execution. Fire neutrons at a target element. Wait. Check if the element has become radioactive — if it's emitting radiation it didn't emit before. If so, you've created a new isotope, possibly a new element. Fermi's team worked with a Geiger counter and a chamber containing radon mixed with beryllium powder. When radon decays, it emits alpha particles; these strike beryllium nuclei and knock out neutrons. Not many — the source was weak — but enough.

They worked their way up the periodic table. Some elements absorbed neutrons and became radioactive isotopes of themselves. Others transmuted into the next element up. By May 1934, they'd reached element 90, thorium. When they bombarded it with neutrons, they got activity that decayed in ways thorium never did. New isotopes, Fermi concluded. They moved to uranium — element 92, the heaviest known. What they found was even stranger: multiple new radioactive substances with different decay rates. Fermi suggested they'd created elements 93 and 94, transuranic elements beyond uranium, never before seen.

The claim made headlines. But what Fermi and his team had actually done — though they wouldn't know it for four more years — was split the uranium nucleus in two. They had achieved nuclear fission. They observed it, measured it, and completely misunderstood it. The radioactive substances weren't new heavy elements; they were lighter elements — the fragments of split uranium atoms. It was a reasonable mistake. No one imagined a nucleus could be shattered. That required an entirely new picture of nuclear behavior.

The crucial discovery came in October 1934, by accident. Fermi was testing silver, checking whether the amount of induced radioactivity changed with the position of the neutron source. It did — but not consistently. Then someone suggested placing a piece of lead between the source and the target, assuming it would reduce the neutron flux. Instead, radioactivity increased. Fermi tried paraffin wax. The effect was enormous. He later called it "one of the most exciting moments of my scientific career."

That morning, they tried everything: water, wood, brick. Anything containing hydrogen increased the effect dramatically. By afternoon, Fermi understood: neutrons were bouncing off hydrogen nuclei — roughly the same mass as a neutron — and slowing down. Fast neutrons, fresh from the source, often passed straight through nuclei. Slow neutrons, having lost energy through collisions, lingered near nuclei longer and had a far greater chance of being captured. It was like the difference between catching a bullet and catching a beach ball.

The effect varied by element. For some, slow neutrons increased capture rates by factors of ten or a hundred. For others, the effect was subtler. But the discovery transformed nuclear physics overnight. Slowing neutrons with hydrogenous materials — water, paraffin, graphite — became standard practice. The technique made possible much of what followed: isotope production for medicine and research, nuclear reactors, the atomic bomb.

Fermi's 1938 Nobel lecture was carefully diplomatic. He spoke of neutron physics, of radioactive isotopes, of capture cross-sections. He did not mention that he'd left Italy for good, that his wife was hiding her background, that his colleagues on the Via Panisperna were scattering. Rasetti had already left for Canada. Segrè was in California on a fellowship, and would stay there. Bruno Pontecorvo would eventually flee to the Soviet Union. The Rome school — brief, brilliant, decisive — was finished.

And he did not mention the possibility of fission, because he still didn't know. That revelation came a month after the Nobel ceremony, in December 1938, when Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann in Berlin chemically identified barium among the products of neutron-bombarded uranium. Barium was element 56 — nowhere near uranium. The nucleus had split. Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch provided the theoretical explanation in January 1939, and within weeks physicists worldwide understood: uranium could be split, energy could be released, and if enough neutrons from each split nucleus triggered further splits, a chain reaction was possible.

Fermi had discovered the key to controlling such a reaction — slow neutrons — without realizing what he'd unlocked. In December 1942, beneath the stands of the University of Chicago's football stadium, he built the first nuclear reactor. It used uranium fuel, graphite to slow the neutrons, and cadmium control rods to absorb them. When Fermi pulled out the rods, neutrons from fission events struck other uranium nuclei. The rate of fission rose. Fermi inserted the rods slightly. The rate steadied. He'd achieved a controlled, self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction. The slow neutrons he'd discovered in Rome made it possible.

By then, the Manhattan Project was underway. Fermi became a central figure in the creation of the atomic bomb, a prospect that filled him with neither enthusiasm nor hesitation. He was, colleagues said, the last physicist who could do everything — theory and experiment, calculation and intuition, instrument-building and mathematical elegance. He once estimated the yield of the first atomic bomb test at Trinity by dropping bits of paper and watching how far the blast wave carried them. His answer, calculated in seconds from falling paper, was accurate to within a factor of two.

The 1938 Nobel Prize recognized work that seemed, at the time, to be pure nuclear physics: new isotopes, new elements, a technique for inducing radioactivity. What it actually recognized was the foundation of the atomic age — the technology of reactors and bombs, the medical isotopes that would save lives, the physics that would reshape geopolitics. Fermi understood none of this when he accepted the prize in Stockholm. He simply knew that neutrons, slowed by hydrogen, behaved in ways fast neutrons didn't — and that curiosity, not foresight, had led him there.

He never returned to Italy. He became an American citizen in 1944. When he died in 1954 at fifty-three, from stomach cancer likely caused by radiation exposure, element 100 was named fermium in his honor. The unit of length used in nuclear physics — one quadrillionth of a meter — is a fermi. And every nuclear reactor in the world uses the principle he discovered on that October afternoon in Rome: slow the neutrons down, and the nucleus cannot escape them.
