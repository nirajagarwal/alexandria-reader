# 1970 — Magnetohydrodynamics and Antiferromagnetism

The Nobel Committee had a problem in 1970. They wanted to honor fundamental discoveries about how matter behaves when magnetic forces dominate — but the discoveries had nothing to do with each other. One concerned plasmas hotter than the sun's core, conducting electricity while twisted by magnetic fields. The other concerned crystals at temperatures near absolute zero, where electron spins aligned in perfect opposition. The only connection was magnetism itself, and the fact that both men were Swedish.

The committee split the prize. Hannes Alfvén received half for work in magnetohydrodynamics — the physics of conducting fluids in magnetic fields. Louis Néel received half for discoveries concerning antiferromagnetism and ferrimagnetism. Two careers, two domains of physics, one prize.

## The Plasma Prophet

Hannes Alfvén saw what others couldn't: that space wasn't empty, and neither were the equations that described it. In the 1940s, when most physicists treated interplanetary space as vacuum, Alfvén recognized it was filled with ionized gas — plasma — threaded through with magnetic fields. The plasma could conduct electricity. The magnetic fields could push back on the plasma. They coupled together in a dance that standard fluid dynamics couldn't capture.

He called it magnetohydrodynamics. The field resisted Maxwell's equations and Navier-Stokes in unholy matrimony. When plasma moved through a magnetic field, it dragged the field lines with it. When field lines moved, they pushed the plasma. This feedback created waves unlike anything in ordinary fluids — waves that could carry energy across cosmic distances, that could accelerate particles, that could structure the universe itself.

The signature prediction was the Alfvén wave: oscillations propagating along magnetic field lines like plucked strings, except the strings were lines of force and the vibrations were plasma motions. These waves traveled at what became known as the Alfvén velocity, determined by the magnetic field strength and plasma density. They weren't sound waves or electromagnetic waves — they were a hybrid creature born from the coupling of matter and field.

Most physicists ignored him. Through the 1940s and early 1950s, Alfvén's papers were rejected, his ideas dismissed as speculation. Plasma physics was still a fringe topic. Space physics was barely physics at all. The mathematics was forbidding, the phenomena remote from laboratory experience. Sydney Chapman, the dominant figure in geophysics, opposed Alfvén's ideas about how the sun's magnetic field affected Earth.

Then satellites went up. Explorer 1 in 1958, followed by spacecraft that could measure the solar wind directly. The instruments found exactly what Alfvén had predicted: plasma streaming from the sun at supersonic speeds, permeated by magnetic fields, exhibiting the wave phenomena he'd described. His mathematical fantasies were physical realities. The solar wind, magnetospheres, cosmic ray propagation, the aurora — all required magnetohydrodynamics to understand.

"Most exciting was to find that a purely theoretical concept that I had introduced in order to explain certain solar phenomena could be identified in the laboratory," Alfvén wrote later. He'd worked backward from cosmic observations to fundamental physics, then forward again to find the same physics in controlled experiments. By 1970, magnetohydrodynamics was essential to fusion research, astrophysics, and space science. The waves bore his name.

But Alfvén was no triumphalist. He remained skeptical of mathematical cosmology, arguing that astrophysicists relied too much on pure theory and too little on experimental constraints. He challenged the Big Bang model, proposed plasma-based alternatives for cosmic structure formation, and warned that computer simulations without physical grounding were dangerous toys. Most of those battles he lost, but he fought them anyway — the plasma prophet who'd been ignored once, refusing to defer again to theoretical consensus.

## The Magnetism Nobody Expected

Louis Néel discovered a magnetism that shouldn't exist. In 1932, working on his doctoral thesis in Strasbourg, he was studying magnetic materials at low temperatures when the data refused to make sense. Standard magnetic theory said materials were either ferromagnetic — with all atomic magnetic moments aligned parallel, creating strong magnetism — or paramagnetic, with moments randomly oriented, creating weak magnetism induced by external fields.

Néel's materials did neither. Below a certain temperature, they became more ordered than paramagnets but showed no net magnetization like ferromagnets. Their magnetic susceptibility dropped sharply with cooling, opposite to what paramagnetism predicted. Something else was happening.

The insight came from symmetry. What if neighboring atomic moments aligned not parallel but antiparallel? Spin up next to spin down, alternating through the crystal lattice. The moments would order themselves — satisfying the thermodynamic drive toward lower energy states — but cancel out macroscopically, producing no net magnetization. The material would be magnetically ordered but externally inert.

Néel called it antiferromagnetism. The concept violated no physical laws; it had simply never occurred to anyone. Pierre Weiss's mean field theory of ferromagnetism could be modified to accommodate antiparallel coupling with a negative exchange interaction. The mathematics worked. The question was whether nature used this possibility.

It took years to prove. Direct observation of antiferromagnetic order required techniques that didn't exist in the 1930s. Neutron diffraction — using neutrons' magnetic moments to probe atomic spin arrangements — finally confirmed antiferromagnetism in 1949, seventeen years after Néel's proposal. Manganese oxide showed the alternating spin structure exactly. Néel had been right.

The discovery mattered beyond magnetism. Antiferromagnetism revealed that quantum exchange interactions could create complex ordered states invisible to ordinary observation. It showed that phase transitions could occur without obvious symmetry breaking. It connected to quantum mechanics' deepest features: the Pauli exclusion principle, which made the exchange interaction possible, determined not just atomic structure but bulk material properties.

Néel didn't stop there. He recognized intermediate cases — ferrimagnetism, where antiparallel spins had unequal magnitude, leaving a net moment. He studied domain wall structures in ferromagnets, showing how magnetic domains formed and moved. He worked on rock magnetism, using ferrimagnetic minerals to study Earth's magnetic field history. The superparamagnetism of tiny particles — now crucial for magnetic data storage and medical imaging — emerged from his theoretical framework.

"I have always felt that experimental results, however unexpected, deserve full respect," Néel wrote. His career embodied that principle: following anomalous data to a magnetic order nobody had imagined, then finding it threaded through materials everywhere.

## The Split

The 1970 prize announcement emphasized "fundamental work and discoveries" useful for materials science, plasma physics, and astrophysics. True enough, but it obscured the mismatch. Alfvén's magnetohydrodynamics governed hot, dilute plasmas where individual particle behavior averaged into fluid dynamics. Néel's magnetism governed dense, cold solids where quantum mechanics dictated cooperative behavior. One concerned large-scale fields and flows; the other, lattice-scale spin alignments.

The committee had chosen to honor magnetism broadly construed — the prize covering electromagnetism's reach from interstellar space to crystalline order. But this wasn't a collaboration or complementary halves of a unified picture. It was two excellent physicists working on completely different problems in completely different domains, awarded the same prize because both involved magnetic fields and both were Swedish.

Neither man complained. They'd waited long enough. Alfvén was sixty-two; Néel was sixty-six. Both had seen their radical ideas become textbook physics. Both had built fields from early skepticism to widespread acceptance. The prize recognized not just discoveries but persistence — the stubbornness required to push ideas the physics community initially rejected.

Today, Alfvén waves propagate through fusion plasmas and solar coronae. Engineers designing stellarators and tokamaks solve magnetohydrodynamic equations. Astrophysicists invoke them to explain gamma-ray bursts and pulsar winds. Antiferromagnetic materials store data, enable spintronics, and might power the next generation of memory devices. The Nobel Committee yoked together two unrelated discoveries, but both discoveries shaped physics. Sometimes that's enough.
