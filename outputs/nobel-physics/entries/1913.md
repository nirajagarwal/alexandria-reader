# 1913 — Low-Temperature Physics

Heike Kamerlingh Onnes had been chasing absolute zero for two decades when he finally liquefied helium on July 10, 1908. It took three attempts that day — the first two batches of gas leaked away — but just before midnight, peering through the narrow windows of his cryostat, he watched the last substance in nature surrender to the cold. At 4.2 degrees above absolute zero, helium became a pale blue liquid pooling at the bottom of his apparatus. No one had ever been this cold before. The question was what to do with it.

## The Coldest Place on Earth

Leiden in 1908 was the world capital of low-temperature physics, and Onnes had made it so through methodical obsession. Born to a wealthy Dutch family in 1853, he'd trained under Kirchhoff and Bunsen in Heidelberg, absorbed van der Waals's theories about molecular forces, and returned to the Netherlands determined to test those theories at the extremes. He became professor at Leiden in 1882 and immediately began building what he called a "knowledge factory" — a laboratory organized like an industrial operation, with skilled technicians, specialized equipment, and systematic approaches to manufacturing cold.

Others had liquefied oxygen (1877), nitrogen (1883), hydrogen (1898). Each step down required better insulation, higher pressures, more ingenious cascade refrigeration. Helium was the final frontier, discovered in the Sun's spectrum in 1868, isolated on Earth only in 1895, and stubbornly refusing to condense. The Scottish chemist James Dewar had tried for years at the Royal Institution in London. Onnes succeeded through meticulous engineering: better pumps, cleaner gas, multiple cooling stages, and a liquefaction apparatus so complex it required a team to operate.

But liquefying helium wasn't the goal — it was the tool. With liquid helium, Onnes could reach temperatures below 4 Kelvin and explore physics in a regime no one had seen. Substances might behave completely differently near absolute zero. The question was: how?

## The Disappearing Resistance

Onnes turned immediately to electrical resistance. According to classical theory, as you cooled a metal, its atoms moved less, creating fewer obstacles for electrons. Resistance should drop. But how far? Some physicists predicted it would approach a minimum value — atoms still present, even if not vibrating. Others, following Lord Kelvin's intuition, thought electrons themselves might freeze out, causing resistance to rise again at very low temperatures.

In April 1911, Onnes tested mercury. He chose it for practical reasons: mercury was easy to purify, and its resistance at different temperatures was well-documented at higher ranges. He froze a thread of mercury inside a glass tube, attached electrical leads, lowered it into liquid helium, and measured.

At 4.3 Kelvin, the resistance dropped suddenly to zero. Not "very small." Zero. He checked for experimental error — shorted wires, faulty contacts. He passed larger currents through the mercury. Nothing changed. The resistance had vanished completely. He called the phenomenon "superconductivity," though he didn't yet understand what it meant.

Over the next months, he found the same behavior in tin and lead. Each metal had a critical temperature below which resistance disappeared entirely. Above that temperature, the metal was ordinary; below it, superconducting. The transition was sharp, not gradual. Something fundamental was happening.

## A Phenomenon Without Theory

Onnes grasped immediately that this wasn't an incremental discovery but something that violated intuition. A current started in a superconducting ring should flow forever, requiring no voltage to maintain it. This wasn't just low resistance — it was perfect conductivity, a different state of matter.

He envisioned applications: electromagnets that could generate enormous fields without power consumption, electrical transmission lines with no losses. In 1914, he attempted the first superconducting magnet, winding lead wire into a coil and cooling it below its critical temperature. The magnet promptly failed. What he didn't know — what no one would understand for decades — was that strong magnetic fields destroy superconductivity. The very application that seemed most obvious was impossible with the materials he was using.

But the fundamental mystery remained: why did it happen at all? Classical physics had no explanation. Electrons in metals were supposed to scatter off atoms, creating resistance. In a superconductor, electrons moved as if the atoms weren't there. Or as if something about the electrons themselves had changed.

The answer wouldn't come for 46 years. In 1957, John Bardeen, Leon Cooper, and Robert Schrieffer finally showed that below the critical temperature, electrons form Cooper pairs — bound states held together by vibrations in the atomic lattice — and these pairs move through the metal in a coordinated quantum state that cannot be disrupted by ordinary scattering. Superconductivity, it turned out, was a macroscopic quantum phenomenon, matter behaving collectively in a way that quantum mechanics alone could explain.

## The Machine Behind the Discovery

The 1913 Nobel Prize citation praised Onnes "for his investigations on the properties of matter at low temperatures which led, inter alia, to the production of liquid helium." That phrase — *inter alia*, among other things — captured how the committee saw the achievement. Liquefying helium was technically impressive. Discovering superconductivity was profound. The prize honored both: the infrastructure and the insight it enabled.

Onnes built his laboratory deliberately to make discoveries possible. He hired glassblowers, machinists, cryogenic specialists. He published detailed technical manuals so others could replicate his methods. He understood that pushing into new regimes required not just clever ideas but systematic engineering — better vacuums, purer materials, more precise thermometry. Where other physicists worked alone or with a single assistant, Onnes ran a research institute with a staff of dozens.

This approach was new in physics, borrowed from industrial chemistry. It wasn't romantic — the lone genius puzzling through equations in an attic — but it worked. Between 1908 and 1923, nearly every low-temperature discovery came from Leiden. The rest of the world didn't catch up until liquid helium became commercially available in the 1930s.

## What Came Next

Superconductivity remained an experimental curiosity for decades. No one found a material with a critical temperature above 23 Kelvin — too cold for practical use without liquid helium, which was expensive and difficult to produce. The theoretical explanation, when it finally arrived, was extraordinarily complex, requiring quantum field theory and mathematical tools borrowed from particle physics. Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer won the Nobel Prize in 1972 for a theory that took three years to develop and another decade to be fully accepted.

Then in 1986, Georg Bednorz and Alex Müller discovered superconductivity in a ceramic copper oxide at 35 Kelvin — higher than theory said was possible. Within months, other researchers found materials that superconducted above 77 Kelvin, the boiling point of liquid nitrogen. High-temperature superconductors could be cooled cheaply. They enabled powerful MRI magnets, particle accelerators, quantum computers. Yet 35 years later, no one fully understands how they work. The Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer theory, sufficient for metals, fails for ceramics.

Onnes died in 1926, having seen his discovery remain mysterious but knowing he had opened a door. In his Nobel lecture, he quoted van der Waals, his old mentor: "Through measurement to knowledge." He had measured the coldest temperatures humans had achieved and found nature behaving in ways no theory predicted. The knowledge would take another generation.

But the wonder was immediate — the realization that matter at the extremes obeyed different rules entirely, that classical physics broke down not just in the atom but in the bulk, that a substance could become something fundamentally *other* if you made it cold enough. Onnes built the machine that revealed this. That was enough.
