# 2018 — Optical Tweezers and Chirped Pulse Amplification

Arthur Ashkin spent decades learning to trap things with light. Not metaphorically — literally. Atoms first, then viruses, then bacteria, and finally, in 1987, a living cell held suspended in a beam no thicker than a hair, floating there unharmed while he watched through a microscope. He was sixty-six years old. It would be another thirty-one years before Stockholm called.

The 2018 physics prize split three ways. Half went to Ashkin for optical tweezers — tools that use focused laser light to grab and manipulate microscopic objects. The other half divided between Gérard Mourou and Donna Strickland for chirped pulse amplification, a technique that makes lasers powerful enough to cut through anything yet precise enough to reshape a cornea. Two discoveries, both about light, both about control. One teaches us to hold the smallest things gently. The other lets us strike with the force of a star.

## Catching Smoke

Ashkin's path began with radiation pressure — the physical push that light exerts when it strikes matter. Sunlight pushes on everything it touches, but the force is vanishingly small. A 100-watt bulb exerts less pressure than a grain of sand weighs. Ashkin wondered if you could amplify it.

In the late 1960s at Bell Labs, he aimed a focused laser at tiny glass spheres suspended in water. The spheres moved. Not from heating, not from convection — from the light itself. The beam acted like invisible fingers, pushing particles toward its center, holding them in place. Ashkin had created an optical trap.

But trapping glass beads was just physics. Ashkin wanted to trap life.

The problem was damage. Laser light carries energy. Too much power and you cook whatever you're trying to hold. Ashkin needed a trap strong enough to overcome thermal buffeting — the random molecular jostling that makes microscopic particles dance — yet gentle enough to leave a cell alive.

The solution came from combining two effects. A tightly focused laser beam creates a gradient in light intensity — brightest at the center, dimmer at the edges. This gradient pulls particles toward the bright spot through a process called the gradient force. Meanwhile, radiation pressure from the beam itself pushes particles in the direction the light travels. By carefully tuning the focus and wavelength, Ashkin found he could balance these forces into a three-dimensional trap.

In 1987, at age sixty-six, he trapped his first bacterium. The cell lived, swimming in circles around its invisible prison. Colleagues thought he was wasting his time — Bell Labs wanted telecommunications advances, not biology experiments. Ashkin kept working anyway.

"I thought it would be useful," he said later, with the understatement of someone who'd just handed biologists a new way to study life.

## The Problem of Power

Meanwhile, a different limitation constrained laser physics. Scientists could generate enormous power, but only in brief, intense bursts that destroyed whatever they touched — including the laser itself. The problem was fundamental: amplify a short, intense pulse through a material, and before it can emerge, the pulse's own power obliterates the amplifying medium.

In the mid-1980s, Gérard Mourou was working at the University of Rochester, trying to push laser power higher. Donna Strickland arrived as his graduate student in 1985, twenty-six years old, fresh from her undergraduate degree at McMaster University. Mourou handed her a problem that had stumped the field: how to amplify ultrashort laser pulses to unprecedented intensities without destroying everything in the process.

Strickland saw the solution in a technique borrowed from radar: chirping.

A chirped pulse stretches in time, with different frequencies arriving at different moments — like a bird call that sweeps from high to low. For radar, this helped with detection. For lasers, it was the key to amplification.

Here's the trick: take an ultrashort, powerful pulse and stretch it in time. The same energy now spreads over a longer duration, reducing peak intensity. Now you can safely amplify it — pump energy into the stretched pulse without vaporizing your equipment. Finally, compress the pulse back to its original short duration. All that amplified energy concentrates into the briefest instant. The result: a pulse orders of magnitude more powerful than you could create by direct amplification.

Strickland worked out the physics for her doctoral dissertation. In 1985, she and Mourou published their findings in a paper that took up less than three pages. The technique worked spectacularly. Within years, chirped pulse amplification became the standard method for generating high-intensity laser pulses.

"We were just trying to make a better laser," Strickland said decades later. She never imagined she'd transformed the field.

## From Idea to Industry

Optical tweezers became biology's microscopic hands. Researchers used them to measure the forces that molecular motors exert as they drag cargo through cells — revelations measured in piconewtons, forces a trillion times smaller than the weight of an apple. They stretched DNA molecules to study how genetic material coils and uncoils. They trapped individual viruses, watching them infect cells in real time. They built "optical spanners" that could rotate organelles, mapping the mechanical properties of cellular machinery.

The technique required patience. Ashkin's early traps were finicky, demanding hours of alignment for a few minutes of stable trapping. But refinements followed. Multiple beams could trap multiple objects simultaneously. Holographic optical tweezers created hundreds of independent traps, each holding a different particle. The invisible fingers multiplied into invisible hands, then invisible orchestras conducting entire ensembles of microscopic dancers.

Chirped pulse amplification transformed laser physics and medicine alike. The technique made possible the petawatt laser — a device delivering more power than all the electrical generating capacity of Earth, concentrated into a pulse lasting femtoseconds. Such beams probe matter under extreme conditions, recreating pressures found in planetary cores or triggering fusion reactions in tiny pellets of hydrogen.

But the most widespread application came in ophthalmology. LASIK eye surgery relies on CPA-enabled lasers to precisely ablate corneal tissue, reshaping the eye's surface to correct vision. The laser fires thousands of pulses per second, each removing a layer of cells mere microns thick. Tens of millions of people see clearly because of Strickland's graduate work.

## Thirty-One Years

The Nobel committee doesn't rush. Ashkin published his cell-trapping paper in 1987. The call came in 2018. He was ninety-six — the oldest laureate in physics history. He'd retired from Bell Labs in 1992, but he hadn't stopped experimenting. His basement lab still hummed with laser equipment.

When a reporter asked what he was working on, he said he was trying to collect atmospheric dust with lasers. "I haven't published yet because I haven't succeeded yet," he added. Three months after winning the prize, he died.

Strickland became the third woman to win the physics prize, after Marie Curie in 1903 and Maria Goeppert Mayer in 1963. The gap — fifty-five years between the second and third female laureate — spoke to exclusions that shaped physics for generations. When journalists called her work "revolutionary," Strickland demurred: "We just did what we thought made sense."

The prize paired discoveries in an unexpected symmetry. Optical tweezers use light's gentlest touch to manipulate matter. Chirped pulse amplification delivers light's most violent strike. Together, they demonstrate light's range as a tool — capable of holding a cell unharmed or cutting through steel, depending on how you shape it.

## The Standard of Precision

What unified these discoveries was control. Before optical tweezers, biologists could look at cells but barely touch them. Before chirped pulse amplification, high-power lasers destroyed as indiscriminately as they created. Both laureates found ways to make light do exactly what they wanted — precisely, repeatedly, without unintended consequences.

Ashkin's trap forces measured in piconewtons became the basis for experiments requiring femtonewton resolution — forces so small they can detect the twist of a single DNA base pair. Mourou and Strickland's pulses, compressed to mere femtoseconds, enabled measurements on attosecond timescales — billionths of a billionth of a second, fast enough to watch electrons move within atoms.

The 2018 prize honored tools more than discoveries. Neither optical tweezers nor chirped pulse amplification revealed a new law of nature. Instead, they gave physicists and biologists new powers — the ability to manipulate matter at scales once considered untouchable, the ability to concentrate energy with precision once thought impossible.

Ashkin spent three decades perfecting his trap. Strickland solved her problem in a three-year doctorate. Both gave away their techniques freely, publishing methods other researchers could replicate and refine. The tools proliferated because they were useful, elegant, and surprisingly simple once someone showed how.

In his nineties, still tinkering in his basement, Ashkin embodied a particular kind of scientific devotion — curiosity sustained across decades, long after institutional support ended, long after prizes mattered. He trapped particles because he wanted to see if he could. Strickland and Mourou amplified pulses because the limitation offended them. Both succeeded because they trusted that if you solve the right problem, applications will follow.

The light that holds bacteria aloft and the light that reshapes corneas come from the same source — coherent photons, precisely controlled. What changed was human understanding. We learned to trap, to stretch, to amplify, to compress. We learned to make light gentle or fierce on demand. The universe always permitted these manipulations. The 2018 laureates discovered how.
