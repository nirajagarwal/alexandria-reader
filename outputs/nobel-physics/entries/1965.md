# 1965 — Quantum Electrodynamics

Richard Feynman walked into the hotel ballroom in Stockholm wearing a rented tuxedo that didn't quite fit, stood before the King of Sweden, and accepted the Nobel Prize for work he'd begun two decades earlier in a desperate attempt to save physics from eating itself. The theory that earned him the prize — quantum electrodynamics, QED — had by 1965 become the most precisely tested theory in the history of science. It predicted the magnetic moment of the electron to ten decimal places. But in 1947, when Feynman was scratching diagrams on cocktail napkins at a conference in the Pocono Mountains, most physicists thought the whole enterprise was doomed.

The problem was infinity. Quantum mechanics and special relativity both worked beautifully on their own, but when you tried to marry them — to create a theory of how light and matter interact at the quantum level — the equations spat out infinities. Calculate the energy of an electron's electromagnetic field, and the answer came back infinite. Calculate the probability of a simple process like an electron emitting a photon, and infinities proliferated like weeds. The mathematics was telling physicists something was fundamentally wrong. Perhaps quantum field theory itself was the wrong framework. Perhaps nature required something entirely new.

Then, in 1947, Willis Lamb measured the energy levels of hydrogen with unprecedented precision. He found a tiny shift — a splitting where theory said there should be none. The "Lamb shift" was minuscule, about one part in a million, but it was real. And around the same time, Isidor Rabi's group at Columbia measured the electron's magnetic moment and found it slightly off from what Paul Dirac's beautiful equation predicted. These weren't failures of quantum theory. They were whispers of something deeper — quantum fluctuations of the vacuum itself, virtual particles flickering in and out of existence, the jittering dance that happened when you took quantum mechanics seriously in empty space.

The experimental facts were clear. But the theory was still broken.

Three men found a way forward, working independently and with strikingly different styles: Julian Schwinger at Harvard, Sin-Itiro Tomonaga in war-ravaged Japan, and Feynman at Cornell.

Schwinger was the golden boy of American physics — a prodigy who'd published his first paper at sixteen, equally at home with the most abstract mathematics. He worked at night, slept through the day, and emerged from seclusion with calculations so intricate that even his brightest graduate students struggled to follow. His approach was to face the infinities head-on with mathematical rigor, developing a formalism of exquisite sophistication. He gave a legendary series of lectures at the 1948 Pocono conference that left the audience simultaneously awed and exhausted. "I understood everything until he reached equation three," Niels Bohr reportedly said.

Tomonaga, working in Tokyo through the final years of World War II and its chaotic aftermath, had been cut off from Western physics journals. He knew of the Lamb shift only through a passing mention in a brief report. Yet he'd been thinking about relativistic quantum field theory since before the war, and he developed his own approach to taming the infinities — one that preserved the principle of relativity at every step. His papers, written in Japanese, didn't reach the West until after the war ended. When they finally arrived, translated and circulated, physicists realized he'd reached many of the same conclusions as Schwinger.

Feynman's path was characteristically different. He'd grown up in Far Rockaway, New York, the son of a uniform salesman who encouraged him to question everything. He learned physics by taking radios apart and thinking about problems from first principles. At Los Alamos during the war, he'd been the young wise-ass who picked locks and played bongos. Now, struggling with quantum field theory, he threw out most of the standard formalism and built his own approach from scratch.

He invented a new way of doing calculations — pictures, simple diagrams where wavy lines represented photons, straight lines represented electrons, and every vertex where they met represented an interaction. Feynman diagrams looked almost childishly simple: cartoon physics. But each line and vertex corresponded to a precise mathematical expression, and the pictures made the calculations tractable in a way that pages of equations never had. More importantly, they provided intuition. You could see what was happening: an electron here, a photon emitted there, virtual particles popping into and out of existence.

The infinities didn't vanish. But all three men — Schwinger with his mathematical machinery, Tomonaga with his relativistic consistency, Feynman with his diagrams — found the same essential trick. The infinities could be isolated and swept into the definitions of a few fundamental quantities: the electron's mass and charge. You measured those quantities from experiment, plugged them into the theory, and the infinities canceled out. Everything else — every observable prediction — came out finite and calculable. The procedure was called "renormalization," and it worked with stunning precision.

Freeman Dyson, then a graduate student, proved that Schwinger's method, Tomonaga's method, and Feynman's method were mathematically equivalent — different roads to the same place. "It was rather as though you had three ways of looking at a sculpture," Dyson later wrote. "Schwinger's front view, Tomonaga's side view, and Feynman's top view — and they all described the same object."

The predictions were extraordinary. QED calculated the Lamb shift to six decimal places. It predicted how electrons and photons would scatter off each other in high-energy collisions. It explained the hyperfine structure of atoms, the tiny energy splittings that arose from the interaction between an electron's spin and the quantum fluctuations around it. Every test confirmed the theory. By the 1960s, experiments had pushed the precision further and further, and QED kept pace, predicting the electron's magnetic moment to better than one part in a billion.

Yet for all its success, QED made some physicists uncomfortable. The renormalization procedure felt like a trick — a way of hiding infinities rather than explaining them. "Just because the shoe fits," Dirac grumbled, "doesn't mean it's the right shoe." Feynman himself called renormalization a "dippy process" and suspected that QED, for all its precision, was just an approximation to some deeper theory. The infinities, he thought, were trying to tell us something.

But the shoe fit remarkably well. QED became the template for the Standard Model of particle physics. The same renormalization techniques that worked for photons and electrons worked for the weak and strong nuclear forces. Feynman diagrams became the lingua franca of particle physics — every graduate student learned to draw them, to translate pictures into probabilities.

The three laureates were study in contrasts. Schwinger, formal and mathematical, continued working on quantum field theory with an elegance that few could match, though he became increasingly isolated as physics moved in directions he didn't trust. Tomonaga returned to Japan and became a beloved teacher and administrator, helping rebuild Japanese physics after the war. Feynman became a scientific celebrity — the bongo-playing, safe-cracking physicist who appeared on television and wrote bestselling memoirs. But beneath the showmanship was absolute seriousness about physics. He spent his later years at Caltech thinking about quantum gravity, the problem QED had dodged, and serving on the commission that investigated the Challenger disaster, where he demonstrated the failure of an O-ring by dropping it into ice water during a televised hearing.

In Stockholm, accepting the prize, Feynman gave a characteristically honest speech. He talked about the pleasure of finding things out, the joy of calculation, the beauty of nature's patterns. He didn't pretend that QED was the final answer. "We have always had a great deal of difficulty understanding the world view that quantum mechanics represents," he said. But within its framework, QED worked. It was the first quantum field theory that actually calculated real numbers that matched experiment. It showed that the quantum world, for all its strangeness, could be described with precision.

The magnetic moment of the electron, as measured today: 2.00231930436256. The prediction from QED: 2.00231930436153. Eleven decimal places of agreement. Feynman liked to point out that this was equivalent to measuring the distance from New York to Los Angeles to within the width of a human hair.

He called it "the jewel of physics." He also called it unfinished business. Both were true.
