# 1936 — Cosmic Radiation and the Positron

Victor Hess stood in a wicker basket three miles above Austria, watching his electroscope. The year was 1912, and physics had a problem: radiation detectors discharged faster on mountaintops than at sea level. The obvious explanation — radioactive contamination in the ground — predicted the opposite. So Hess went up. At 5,000 meters, ionization had quadrupled. Something was raining down from space.

Twenty-four years later, Hess shared the Nobel Prize for that balloon flight. His co-laureate, Carl Anderson, won for discovering what came down: particles that traced spirals in the wrong direction, matter with the electron's mass but the opposite charge. Between them, they opened physics to the cosmos and revealed antimatter's first instance. One discovery posed the question; the other changed what matter could be.

## The Sky Problem

Radiation — the invisible something that fogged photographic plates and discharged electroscopes — had been discovered in 1896, but its source remained contentious. Physicist Theodor Wulf climbed the Eiffel Tower in 1910 with an electroscope and found stronger radiation at the top than at the bottom. This was wrong. Radioactive elements decayed in rock and soil; going higher should mean less radiation, not more.

Hess, a young Austrian physicist at the Institute for Radium Research, suspected something radical: radiation from above. In August 1912, he made seven balloon ascents, some at night to rule out the sun, one during a near-total solar eclipse. His electroscope told the same story each time. Ground level: modest ionization. Higher altitude: dramatically more. At 5,300 meters: readings four times background.

"The results of my observations are best explained by the assumption that a radiation of very high penetrating power enters our atmosphere from above," Hess wrote. He called it Höhenstrahlung — high-altitude radiation. The physics community was skeptical. Robert Millikan, who would later grudgingly confirm the effect while dubbing it "cosmic rays," initially dismissed the findings. It took a decade of balloon flights by other physicists before Hess's interpretation gained acceptance.

The cosmic rays themselves presented deeper mysteries. They arrived with extraordinary energy — millions of electron volts, far beyond what terrestrial radioactivity produced. They seemed to come from all directions at once, suggesting an origin outside the solar system. And they did strange things when they hit the atmosphere.

## The Cloud Chamber Detective

Carl Anderson never intended to hunt antimatter. He intended to measure cosmic rays.

In 1930, at Caltech under Robert Millikan, Anderson built a cloud chamber — a device where charged particles leave visible tracks through supersaturated vapor, like contrails in the sky — and placed it in a magnetic field. Cosmic rays entering the chamber would curve; the direction and radius of curvature would reveal their charge and momentum. Straightforward particle physics.

The photographs showed something impossible.

Most cosmic ray particles curved as expected: negative particles (electrons) bending one way, positive particles (protons) bending the other. But some tracks bent like electrons in the wrong direction — gentle curves characteristic of light particles, but with the curvature of positive charge. Anderson checked everything: the magnetic field direction, the track geometry, whether the particles were going up or down. The anomalies persisted.

In August 1932, Anderson placed a lead plate across the middle of his chamber. A particle passing through would slow down; by comparing track curvature above and below the plate, he could determine its direction of travel. One photograph showed a track entering from below, crossing the plate, and continuing upward. The curvature confirmed it: an upward-moving particle with positive charge and electron mass.

"It required considerable courage and imagination to postulate the existence of a new particle," Anderson later recalled. He named it the "positive electron," quickly shortened to positron. The discovery paper, published in *Science* in September 1932, was cautious to the point of understatement: the tracks were "very probably" from a light positive particle.

He was describing antimatter.

Paul Dirac had predicted its existence four years earlier. His relativistic wave equation for the electron — an attempt to marry quantum mechanics with special relativity — produced an embarrassing result: negative energy states. Dirac's interpretation seemed absurd: for every electron, a mirror particle with opposite charge must exist. Most physicists treated this as mathematical pathology, not prophecy.

When Anderson published, Dirac's theory suddenly had physical proof. The positron wasn't just another particle; it was the antiparticle to the electron. When matter met antimatter, both vanished in a burst of gamma rays — pure energy from pure annihilation. The implications cascaded: if electrons had antiparticles, why not protons? Why not every particle in nature?

Anderson took care to note that his discovery was experimental, not theoretical. He had found positrons by looking, not by believing Dirac. "The positron was discovered wholly by accident," he wrote in his Nobel lecture, with evident satisfaction. "Its discovery was not a consequence of a deliberate search."

The distinction mattered professionally — experiment had its own authority — but it also revealed something about Anderson's approach. He trusted what the cloud chamber showed him. When the tracks contradicted expectation, he questioned his assumptions, not the data.

## The Split Decision

The 1936 Nobel Prize in Physics went to both men, but not for the same discovery. Hess won "for his discovery of cosmic radiation." Anderson won "for his discovery of the positron." One prize, divided between the question and one of its answers.

This was characteristic of the Nobel committee's occasional awkwardness. Cosmic rays and positrons were intimately connected — Anderson found positrons in cosmic ray showers — but the discoveries were separated by twenty years and driven by different questions. Hess had revealed that space was not empty and quiet but filled with energetic particles. Anderson had revealed that matter came in mirror pairs. Yoked together in one prize, they represented the beginning and continuation of a story still unfolding.

The committee's choice also reflected a certain conservatism. By 1936, cosmic ray physics was exploding with new discoveries. In the atmosphere, cosmic rays collided with air molecules and produced showers of secondary particles. Some of these particles penetrated deep underground; others decayed in microseconds. The positron was only the most dramatic find. But the Nobel committee understood Hess's discovery as foundational — the opening of a new window — and Anderson's as the first clear view through it.

Missing from the prize: Patrick Blackett and Giuseppe Occhialini, who confirmed Anderson's positron findings independently and published just months later with better evidence linking positrons to cosmic ray showers. Also missing: Dirac, whose theory predicted antimatter but had already won the Nobel in 1933 for the Dirac equation itself. The 1936 prize thus honored experimental discovery over theoretical prediction, observation over imagination.

Both men accepted this. Hess, in his Nobel lecture, spoke of cosmic rays as messengers from the unknown: "We are dealing with phenomena of unexplained origin." He was right. The source of cosmic rays — accelerated by supernovae, by black holes, by colliding galaxies — would remain mysterious for decades. Anderson spoke of the positron as just the beginning: "The existence of the positron definitely extends our knowledge of the elementary particles of nature."

He was right, too. The positron opened the door to antimatter. Within years, physicists would find antiprotons, antineutrons, entire antiatoms. The question would shift from "Does antimatter exist?" to "Why does matter dominate?" — a mystery still unsolved.

## The Human Cost

Hess's story had the sharp edge of history cutting through it. In 1938, two years after receiving his Nobel, Austria was annexed by Nazi Germany. Hess, a Catholic married to a Jewish-American woman, faced immediate danger. He escaped to Switzerland, then to the United States, where he spent the war teaching at Fordham University. His institute in Vienna, his instruments, his data — all remained behind. He never returned to live in Austria.

Anderson's trajectory was gentler but not without cost. The Nobel came when he was thirty-one, before many physicists find their footing. He spent decades afterward at Caltech, productive but never quite matching the positron's impact. Later, he would describe the difficulty of following such a discovery: "I think every scientist would like to make a discovery of comparable importance each year, but this just doesn't happen."

Both men lived to see cosmic rays become astrophysics and particle physics. Hess died in 1964, a year before the cosmic microwave background confirmed the Big Bang. Anderson died in 1991, after the Standard Model had cataloged dozens of particles and their antiparticles. The wicker basket and the cloud chamber had become particle accelerators and space telescopes, but the questions remained theirs: what comes from space, and what is matter made of?
