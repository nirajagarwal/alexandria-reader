# 1956 — The Transistor

John Bardeen worked quietly. When he spoke in meetings at Bell Labs, colleagues leaned in to hear. William Shockley dominated rooms — brilliant, mercurial, ambitious in ways that made people uncomfortable. Walter Brattain was the experimentalist who could coax strange behavior from semiconductors, a skill part craft, part intuition. On December 16, 1947, Bardeen and Brattain built a device smaller than a pencil eraser that amplified electrical current. Within a month, Shockley — who had been shut out of that first success — invented a better version. The transistor would reshape civilization. The Nobel Prize wouldn't come for nine years, and by then the three men barely spoke to each other.

The problem was vacuum tubes. Since 1906, every radio, telephone switch, and early computer depended on them — glass bulbs that glowed with heat, failed frequently, and consumed enormous power. They worked by boiling electrons off a hot cathode and controlling their flow through a vacuum. Bell Telephone needed something better. Telephone networks were expanding; the switching systems filled entire buildings and generated enough heat to require dedicated cooling. The company had nearly unlimited funding for basic research. They pointed their physicists at solid-state devices — amplifiers made from crystals instead of vacuum.

The material was germanium, a semiconductor sitting between conductors like copper and insulators like glass. Add tiny amounts of certain impurities — "doping" in the jargon — and you could control how electricity flowed. With one type of impurity, you got extra electrons (n-type). With another, you got "holes" where electrons should be (p-type). The physics was subtle, quantum mechanical, incompletely understood. Shockley had been pursuing this since before the war. He wanted a "field effect" device where voltage applied to one part of the crystal would control current in another. It should work in theory. In practice, it failed every time.

Bardeen, recruited in 1945, figured out why. The surface of the semiconductor was trapping charge. Electrons weren't flowing the way theory predicted because the crystal face was imperfect, riddled with quantum states that interfered with the field effect. It was a deep insight that explained years of failure — and pointed toward a solution. If you couldn't control current through the bulk of the crystal, maybe you could do it at the surface. By November 1947, Bardeen and Brattain were experimenting with a setup that pressed two gold contacts very close together on a germanium surface. On December 16, with contacts separated by the width of a human hair, they measured amplification: a small signal in, a larger signal out. They'd made a transistor. They called Shockley at home to tell him. He congratulated them. Then he went home and began designing something better.

Shockley's version — the junction transistor — was more elegant and practical. Instead of delicate surface contacts, it used layers of doped germanium: n-type, p-type, n-type, sandwiched together. Current flowing from emitter to collector could be controlled by a small voltage at the base. It was the design that would actually matter. Shockley worked out the theory in a white heat of solitary effort, deliberately excluding Bardeen and Brattain. By January 1948, he had it. When Bell Labs announced the transistor to the world in June 1948, Shockley made sure his name was attached, though his version hadn't been built yet. The other two had made the first device; he'd invented the one that would change everything. The resentment settled in and never left.

The implications came slowly, then all at once. Early transistors were expensive and unreliable. Vacuum tubes still dominated through the early 1950s. But transistors ran cool, used little power, and could theoretically be made very small. Hearing aids came first — devices that could actually fit in the ear. Then portable radios, which became cheap enough for teenagers. By 1954, Texas Instruments had a production line. The Sony pocket radio arrived in 1957. The technology was escaping the lab, becoming consumer product, becoming ubiquitous.

The deeper revolution was invisible. Computers had been possible since the 1940s, but vacuum-tube machines like ENIAC filled rooms, required constant maintenance, and could barely stay running. The SAGE air defense system, using vacuum tubes, needed a building the size of a warehouse and consumed three megawatts. Transistorized computers were smaller, faster, more reliable. Moore's Law — the doubling of transistor density every two years — wouldn't be articulated until 1965, but the pattern was already emerging. The transistor didn't just improve electronics; it made computation cheap enough to embed in everything.

The Nobel committee waited. Partly this was caution — let the impact become clear, let the technology prove itself. Partly it was the messiness of credit. Who invented the transistor? Bardeen and Brattain made the first one. Shockley designed the version that mattered. The theoretical groundwork went back to quantum mechanics in the 1930s. Bell Labs had an entire team working semiconductors. Russell Ohl had discovered the p-n junction in 1940. Dozens contributed. But Nobel Prizes go to at most three people, and the committee tends to favor clear narratives.

By 1956, the answer was obvious: split it three ways, acknowledge the messiness, honor all three. Bardeen was in Stockholm to receive it; he gave a gracious speech about teamwork and thanked Bell Labs. Shockley was there, radiating vindication — he'd gotten his share despite being shut out of the first discovery. Brattain was there, loyal to both and uncomfortable with the tension between them. They stood for photographs, the three men who'd birthed the information age. After Stockholm, their paths diverged completely.

Bardeen returned to the University of Illinois and turned his attention to superconductivity. The physics community thought the problem unsolvable — why certain materials lost all electrical resistance at low temperatures defied explanation. Bardeen worked it out with two younger colleagues, Leon Cooper and Robert Schrieffer. The BCS theory, published in 1957, was a triumph of quantum field theory applied to condensed matter. Bardeen won a second Nobel Prize in 1972, the only person ever to win physics twice. He remained modest, soft-spoken, almost determinedly unglamorous — a man interested in the work, not the glory.

Shockley founded Shockley Semiconductor in Mountain View, California. He recruited brilliant young engineers and physicists, then managed them so erratically that eight of them — the "traitorous eight" — left in 1957 to found Fairchild Semiconductor. That defection seeded Silicon Valley. Shockley's company failed, but he'd planted the industry in California soil. He took a position at Stanford and spent his later years promoting eugenics, arguing that intelligence was racial and hereditary. The Nobel laureate who'd invented the future became a crank spouting theories the scientific community rejected. He died in 1989, estranged from most who'd known him, his late-life obsessions obscuring his early genius.

Brattain went back to Bell Labs and kept working on semiconductor surfaces. He never quite escaped the transistor's shadow. His Nobel Prize was for something he'd done in his forties; the rest of his career felt like epilogue. He retired in 1967, did some consulting, grew bitter about Shockley's self-promotion and Bardeen's second prize. He'd been essential to the invention, the one who could make the experiments work, and history kept trying to reduce him to footnote.

The transistor they made in 1947 sits in a museum now, two gold contacts on a germanium slab, wires soldered with a hand that knew its craft. The descendent of that device is in your pocket, your car, your refrigerator, your hospital. Billions of transistors on a chip smaller than a fingernail, switching a billion times per second, running the world. Three men stood on stage in Stockholm in 1956. One would win the prize again for different physics. One would spiral into disgrace. One would watch others take credit for what his hands had built. The transistor didn't care. It kept amplifying, kept switching, kept multiplying, kept making the future they couldn't have imagined even as they held it in their hands.
