# 2015 — Neutrino Oscillations

For decades, physicists counted the neutrinos streaming from the sun and kept coming up short. The numbers were wrong — consistently, stubbornly wrong — by about two-thirds. Either something fundamental was broken in our understanding of stellar fusion, or these ghost particles were doing something impossible. They were changing identity mid-flight.

Takaaki Kajita and Arthur McDonald proved it was the latter. Working a mile underground in separate hemispheres — Kajita beneath a mountain in Japan, McDonald in a nickel mine in Ontario — they demonstrated that neutrinos oscillate between three types as they travel through space. The missing solar neutrinos hadn't vanished. They'd simply transformed into types the detectors couldn't see.

The discovery did more than solve the solar neutrino problem. It shattered one of the Standard Model's foundational assumptions: that neutrinos are massless. Only particles with mass can oscillate. The lightest things in the universe turned out not to be weightless after all.

## The Ghost Particle Problem

Neutrinos barely exist, by any reasonable measure. A hundred trillion pass through your body every second from the sun alone, almost all without touching a single atom. They're electrically neutral, nearly massless, affected only by the weak nuclear force. When Wolfgang Pauli first proposed them in 1930 to solve an energy-accounting problem in beta decay, he called them "a desperate remedy" and assumed they could never be detected.

He was wrong about detection — barely. The first neutrino was caught in 1956, near a nuclear reactor, after an immense effort. But Pauli's desperation hint proved prescient. Neutrinos come in three "flavors," each associated with a charged particle: electron neutrinos, muon neutrinos, tau neutrinos. The Standard Model treated them as massless and unchanging. An electron neutrino stayed an electron neutrino.

Then came the solar neutrino experiments of the 1960s and '70s. Ray Davis, working in the same Sudbury mine where McDonald would later build his detector, counted neutrinos from the sun's fusion reactions. He should have detected about three times what he measured. Either solar models were catastrophically wrong, or something was happening to the neutrinos themselves.

For thirty years, this solar neutrino deficit haunted physics. Some dismissed it as an experimental error. Others suspected the sun. A few wondered if the neutrinos themselves were more complex than anyone had imagined.

## A Mile Underground

Kajita's Super-Kamiokande detector in Japan was a cathedral to particle physics: a cylinder fifteen stories tall, filled with 50,000 tons of ultra-pure water, its walls lined with 11,000 photomultiplier tubes waiting for the faint flash of Cherenkov radiation when a neutrino finally collided with an atomic nucleus. It sat a kilometer beneath Mount Ikeno, shielded from cosmic ray interference.

Super-K wasn't hunting solar neutrinos. It was watching for atmospheric neutrinos — created when cosmic rays slam into Earth's atmosphere. These neutrinos arrive from all directions: from the sky above and, having passed through the entire planet, from the ground below. By the mid-1990s, Kajita's team noticed something strange. Neutrinos coming from above matched predictions. Neutrinos coming from below — having traveled thousands of kilometers through Earth — were depleted.

The conclusion emerged gradually, confirmed through painstaking analysis: muon neutrinos were oscillating into tau neutrinos during their journey through Earth. The tau neutrinos were invisible to Super-K's detection method. The ones that traveled farthest oscillated most. The pattern was unmistakable.

In 1998, Kajita announced the result. "We conclude that atmospheric neutrinos oscillate," he said, with characteristic understatement. The implications were immediate and profound.

## Heavy Water

McDonald's Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO), two kilometers underground in Ontario, took a different approach to the same problem. Where Super-K used ordinary water, SNO used heavy water — water molecules built with deuterium instead of ordinary hydrogen, loaned by Atomic Energy of Canada at a value of $300 million. The deuterium nuclei allowed SNO to detect all three neutrino flavors, not just electron neutrinos.

This was the key. SNO could count total neutrinos from the sun regardless of flavor, and separately count just electron neutrinos. If neutrinos oscillated, the total count should match solar models while the electron neutrino count fell short.

In 2001, McDonald's team reported exactly that. The total neutrino flux from the sun matched theoretical predictions perfectly. The electron neutrino flux was about one-third the total. The missing solar neutrinos had been there all along, transformed into muon and tau neutrinos during their eight-minute journey from the sun's core.

"The solar neutrino problem is solved," McDonald announced. But the solution opened a deeper mystery. For neutrinos to oscillate, they must have mass. The Standard Model said they didn't.

## Why Oscillation Requires Mass

The physics is quantum mechanical and strange, but the logic is clear. Neutrinos are created in "flavor states" — electron, muon, or tau — matched to their charged-particle partners. But these flavor states, it turns out, are mixtures of "mass states" — three distinct neutrino types with different (tiny) masses.

As a neutrino travels, these mass states evolve at different rates because they have different energies. The mixture shifts. A neutrino born as an electron neutrino might arrive as a muon neutrino, depending on the distance traveled and the energy involved. It's not that the neutrino changes into something else — it's that the quantum superposition of what it always was resolves differently when observed.

For this to work, the mass states must be different. If neutrinos were massless, all states would evolve identically. There would be no oscillation. The very fact of flavor change proves neutrinos have mass, however slight.

## The Weight of Ghosts

Just how massive are neutrinos? Oscillation experiments reveal differences in mass between the three types but not absolute values. The heaviest neutrino weighs at least 0.05 electron volts — about one ten-millionth the mass of an electron, itself the lightest massive particle previously known. The three masses together are no more than a few tenths of an electron volt.

This sounds trivial until you count them. There are more than 300 neutrinos per cubic centimeter throughout the universe, left over from the Big Bang. Their combined mass exceeds that of all visible stars. They affect the universe's expansion rate, the formation of galaxies, the distribution of matter on cosmic scales. Ghost particles, collectively, shape the cosmos.

## Beyond the Standard Model

The Standard Model of particle physics, assembled over decades and confirmed in countless experiments, assumes massless neutrinos. It's not a casual assumption but a structural one, built into the model's mathematical framework. Neutrino mass doesn't fit naturally. It requires new physics.

Most physicists suspect the "seesaw mechanism" — neutrinos might be Majorana particles, their own antiparticles, gaining mass through interaction with a hypothetical right-handed neutrino field far heavier than anything we've observed. This would explain why neutrino masses are so absurdly small compared to other particles. But we don't know.

What we do know: the Standard Model, for all its successes, is incomplete. Neutrino oscillations are the first laboratory-confirmed crack in its foundation. Something lies beyond.

## The Human Element

Kajita and McDonald led teams of hundreds over decades. Super-Kamiokande required extraordinary patience — years of accumulating data, one neutrino at a time. In 2001, an accident during maintenance work caused a catastrophic chain reaction of imploding photomultiplier tubes, destroying more than half the detector. The team rebuilt it.

SNO's heavy water had to be kept ultra-pure; contamination would ruin the detection capability. After the experiment concluded, every drop was returned to Canada. The cavity now serves as SNOLAB, a permanent underground laboratory.

Both men built on earlier work. Davis, who shared the 2002 Nobel for his solar neutrino experiments, laid the groundwork. Bruno Pontecorvo first proposed neutrino oscillations in the 1950s, though he received no Nobel recognition. Lincoln Wolfenstein and Mikheyev and Smirnov developed the theory of matter-enhanced oscillation that explained how neutrinos transform differently traveling through the sun versus through empty space.

The 2015 prize recognized not just intellectual insight but experimental virtuosity — the ability to detect processes so subtle they barely happen, to build instruments so sensitive they can see ghosts changing costume mid-flight.

## What Remains Unknown

We still don't know the absolute masses of neutrinos, their exact ordering (which is heaviest?), or whether they're Majorana particles. We don't know if there are more than three types, though most evidence suggests not. We don't know why neutrino masses are so much smaller than those of other particles — this hierarchy problem is one of physics' deepest puzzles.

And we don't know if neutrinos are connected to the universe's matter-antimatter asymmetry. The Big Bang should have created equal amounts of matter and antimatter, which would have annihilated, leaving a universe of pure energy. Instead, we exist. Something broke the symmetry. Neutrinos might be involved — their oscillation behavior could differ slightly between neutrinos and antineutrinos, a violation of fundamental symmetry that might explain why anything exists at all.

Current and planned experiments — T2K in Japan, NOvA in the United States, the future Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment — probe these questions. Kajita and McDonald's discovery opened the door. We're still learning what lies beyond.

The solar neutrino deficit turned out to be not an error, not a flaw in stellar models, but a message from particles so ethereal they seem almost abstract. They have weight. They change. They matter, in both senses of the word. The ghosts are real, and stranger than anyone imagined.
