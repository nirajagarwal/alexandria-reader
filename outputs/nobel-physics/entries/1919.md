# 1919 — The Stark Effect

Johannes Stark was alone in his laboratory in Aachen when he saw hydrogen's bright red line split into multiple lines under the influence of an electric field. It was 1913, and the discovery would earn him a Nobel Prize — though by the time he accepted it in 1920, he had already begun the transformation from brilliant experimentalist to scientific pariah, a trajectory that would end with him as one of Nazi Germany's most prominent physicists and an architect of its campaign against "Jewish physics."

## The Splitting

Light emitted by atoms appears as discrete spectral lines — hydrogen glows red at 656 nanometers, blue-green at 486, violet at 434. These lines are fingerprints, unique to each element. In 1896, Pieter Zeeman had discovered that magnetic fields split these lines into multiple components, a phenomenon that earned him the 1902 Nobel Prize. The Zeeman effect revealed that atoms had internal structure responsive to magnetism, a critical clue about atomic architecture in the years before Bohr's model.

The question was whether electric fields would do the same.

Theory said no. Classical electromagnetism predicted that electric fields would shift spectral lines slightly, but not split them. The atom should respond to an electric field as a whole, not break apart into separate components. When Stark began his experiments in 1913, most physicists expected to see nothing dramatic.

He used hydrogen gas at low pressure in a specially designed tube, with electrodes creating intense electric fields — up to 100,000 volts per centimeter. The technical challenge was formidable: strong enough fields to affect atoms, but not so strong they caused electrical breakdown and sparking. When he examined the spectrum through his spectrograph, hydrogen's red line had fractured into nine distinct components. The electric field was pulling the atom's emission apart.

This was the Stark effect, and it defied classical expectations.

## What It Revealed

The splitting pattern told a story about atomic structure. Different components appeared polarized in different directions — some perpendicular to the field, some parallel. The number of components and their spacing varied with field strength. Stark had found another window into the atom's interior, a complement to the Zeeman effect's magnetic probe.

The timing was exquisite. Niels Bohr had published his atomic model just months earlier, proposing that electrons orbit the nucleus in quantized states, jumping between levels to emit photons of specific energies. The Stark effect became immediate validation. Bohr and others quickly showed that electric fields should perturb these quantum orbits in precise ways, shifting energy levels and splitting spectral lines exactly as Stark had observed.

The discovery had a deeper elegance: it demonstrated that quantum mechanics wasn't just about energy quantization but about how atoms interact with external forces. The split lines were signatures of quantum states being pulled apart, made visible through light. Every component in that fractured red line corresponded to a different quantum configuration of the hydrogen atom under stress.

"The effect shows that the atom has a definite structure," Stark wrote, "and that this structure can be influenced and investigated by external fields." He had given physicists a new experimental tool for probing quantum reality.

## The Experimentalist

Johannes Stark was forty-five when he won the prize, already a veteran of two decades of difficult, precision work in spectroscopy and electrical discharge. He was known as a skilled experimentalist, meticulous and persistent, though also as a difficult colleague — argumentative, quick to claim priority, suspicious of rivals.

He had grown up in Bavaria, the son of a landowner, and retained throughout his life a provincial suspicion of cosmopolitan physics communities, particularly those centered in Berlin and Göttingen. His scientific style was empirical and concrete; he distrusted abstract mathematical theory, preferring phenomena he could see and measure.

The 1919 prize recognized both the Stark effect and his earlier discovery of the Doppler shift in canal rays — fast-moving ions that show shifted spectral lines depending on their velocity. Both were elegant experimental findings, technically demanding and scientifically significant.

But already by 1913, Stark was becoming isolated. He feuded with other physicists over priority for various discoveries. He rejected Einstein's relativity theory on increasingly ideological grounds. He claimed that modern theoretical physics — with its abstraction, its mathematics, its rejection of mechanical models — was leading science astray.

## The Descent

What happened next is among the darkest chapters in modern physics. As Germany collapsed into economic crisis and political extremism in the 1920s, Stark's scientific isolation merged with his political resentments. He became a vocal Nazi supporter before Hitler's rise to power, seeing in National Socialism a movement that would restore proper German values against cosmopolitan corruption.

After 1933, he emerged as a leader of *Deutsche Physik* — "German Physics" — a campaign to purge physics of Jewish influence and reject relativity and quantum mechanics as "Jewish science." Stark attacked Einstein, Heisenberg, Schrödinger, and others with a viciousness that went beyond scientific disagreement into personal and racial vilification. He used his position to block appointments, close laboratories, and drive colleagues into exile or worse.

The scientific community, even within Germany, largely rejected his ideology. Max Planck, though politically cautious, publicly defended theoretical physics. Werner Heisenberg called Stark's claims "absurd." But Stark had power, and he wielded it viciously.

The man who had revealed the quantum splitting of spectral lines spent the 1930s trying to destroy the quantum mechanics that explained what he had seen.

## Legacy

After the war, Stark was classified as a "major offender" in denazification proceedings. His scientific reputation was destroyed — not by his colleagues, who simply stopped citing him, but by his own choices. He died in 1957, largely forgotten outside historical accounts of physics under Nazism.

The Stark effect remains foundational. It's used to study atomic structure, to measure electric fields in plasmas and semiconductors, to probe molecular bonds. In quantum optics, controlled Stark shifts manipulate atomic states for precision measurements. The splitting that Stark first observed is now a tool in laboratories worldwide.

The discovery was genuine — beautiful physics that opened new paths of understanding. The discoverer's subsequent choices do not erase that contribution, but neither can the contribution redeem those choices. Stark found something true about nature, a glimpse of quantum reality made visible through light. What he did with the authority that discovery granted him is a separate story, darker and more human.

The spectral lines split cleanly in the electric field, separating into distinct components. The line between scientific insight and moral failure is less sharp, but no less real.
