# 1997 — Laser Cooling of Atoms

In the summer of 1978, a physicist at Bell Labs had a problem with his atomic beam. William Phillips was trying to measure properties of sodium atoms by bouncing laser light off them as they flew through a vacuum chamber. But the atoms moved too fast — thousands of meters per second, blurring his measurements. He needed them slower. Much slower. So he decided to push back.

Light carries momentum. A photon is weightless but not forceless: when absorbed by an atom, it delivers a tiny kick, like a ping-pong ball hitting a freight train. One photon does almost nothing. But atoms can absorb millions of photons per second. Phillips realized he could aim six laser beams — two along each axis — directly into the path of his sodium atoms. An atom moving in any direction would preferentially absorb photons coming toward it (a quirk of the Doppler effect), getting pushed backward no matter which way it moved. The atoms would slow down. They might even stop.

It worked better than physics said it should.

By 1985, Phillips had cooled sodium atoms to 240 millionths of a degree above absolute zero — forty times colder than theory predicted. At that temperature, atoms barely move. They hover in space, held by nothing but the pressure of light. Phillips called it "optical molasses," because the atoms seemed trapped in something thick and sticky, though the chamber was vacuum. The metaphor stuck, even if the mechanism wasn't quite what anyone expected.

The theoretical explanation came from Claude Cohen-Tannoudji in Paris. He realized that atoms in optical molasses don't just slow down — they get polarized by the light fields, and that polarization couples to tiny variations in light intensity. The cooling goes deeper than simple momentum transfer. It reaches into the quantum structure of the atom itself. Cohen-Tannoudji's group would later develop techniques to go even colder, below one microkelvin, by carefully manipulating those quantum states.

But the story starts earlier, with a different kind of trap.

In the mid-1970s, Steven Chu — then a graduate student, later at Bell Labs alongside Phillips — became fascinated by a problem in laser physics. How do you hold something still with light? Atoms in a gas move randomly, colliding with each other, impossible to study individually. But if you could trap a single atom, pin it in space with laser beams, you could interrogate it for hours. Watch it absorb and emit light. Test quantum mechanics on a single system with no interference.

Chu built an optical trap using focused laser beams to hold tiny glass spheres — not atoms, but big enough to see under a microscope. The beams created a potential well: objects drifted toward the center and stayed there. The technique worked for particles a few microns across. But atoms are ten thousand times smaller. To trap an atom, you'd first need to slow it down dramatically — otherwise it would blow right through the laser focus.

That's where cooling came in.

By the mid-1980s, three groups were chasing the same idea from different angles. Phillips at Bell Labs focused on slowing atomic beams for spectroscopy. Cohen-Tannoudji in Paris worked on the quantum mechanics of atom-light interactions, trying to understand what was really happening when atoms sat in crossed laser beams. And Chu, by then at Stanford, wanted to combine cooling with trapping — to grab individual atoms out of optical molasses and hold them still.

In 1986, Chu's group succeeded. They cooled sodium atoms into molasses, then used a fourth laser beam — tuned to a different frequency — to trap them. The atoms accumulated at the center of the beam, held by the gradient in light intensity. For the first time, individual atoms hovered in space, visible as a tiny glowing cloud. Chu called it an "optical tweezers" for atoms.

The applications arrived quickly. With atoms nearly motionless, you can measure their properties with astonishing precision. Atomic clocks, which keep time by counting oscillations in atoms, became ten thousand times more accurate. (Today's best atomic clocks, descended from these techniques, won't lose a second in 15 billion years.) You can test fundamental physics: do atoms fall under gravity at the rate Einstein predicts? Does the speed of light truly stay constant? Cold atoms let you check with precision previously unimaginable.

Then there's Bose-Einstein condensation. In 1995 — two years before the Nobel — scientists at JILA and MIT used laser cooling as the first stage to create the coldest matter in the universe: a gas of atoms so cold they collapse into a single quantum state, acting as one giant atom. It was a phase of matter predicted in 1924 but never seen. Laser cooling made it possible.

But the technique itself was the prize. The committee recognized Chu, Cohen-Tannoudji, and Phillips for developing the methods, not for any single application. They'd invented a new way to manipulate matter, turning light — usually thought of as energy, as heat — into a refrigerant.

The paradox is striking: light warms things. Stand in sunlight and you feel it heat your skin. But aim laser light carefully at an atom, tuning the frequency just below the atom's absorption line, and the atom preferentially absorbs photons when it's moving toward the light source. Each absorption slows the atom down. When the atom re-emits the photon, it kicks in a random direction — sometimes forward, sometimes back. But the absorption is directional, always opposing motion. On average, the atom loses kinetic energy. It cools.

Steven Chu described the moment he first saw the trapped atoms: "It was one of those times when you're working late, and you think you see something, but you're not sure if it's real or your eyes playing tricks. Then you turn up the laser power a little, and the cloud gets brighter, and you realize: that's them. We've got them."

The three laureates had different styles. Chu was the builder, obsessed with getting experiments to work, famous for arriving at the lab before dawn. Phillips was the precision measurer, methodical and careful, troubled when his numbers didn't match theory. Cohen-Tannoudji was the theorist who got his hands dirty, calculating in the morning and checking results in the lab in the afternoon. Their techniques converged on the same breakthrough from different directions.

There were others who contributed. John Hall at JILA pioneered laser stabilization methods essential for precise cooling. Dave Pritchard at MIT developed the first magnetic traps for atoms. The Nobel committee, as always, faced impossible choices. But the prize went to the three who demonstrated that atoms could be slowed and held by light alone — and explained why it worked.

Today, laser cooling is everywhere in physics. It's the starting point for quantum computers built from trapped ions. It enables gravity-wave detectors that use atom interferometry. It lets physicists study quantum gases, quantum phase transitions, even simulate quantum systems too complex to calculate. The coldest places in the universe are now in laboratories on Earth, created by shining laser light on atoms and telling them, gently but firmly, to stop moving.

"Before this, atoms were like bees," Phillips said years later. "Buzzing around, impossible to catch. We figured out how to calm them down. Once they're calm, you can do anything."
