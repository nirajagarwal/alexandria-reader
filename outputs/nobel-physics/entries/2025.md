# 2025 — Macroscopic Quantum Tunneling

The quantum world ends at a certain scale — or so physicists believed for most of the twentieth century. Atoms and electrons could tunnel through barriers they shouldn't cross, could exist in superposition, could behave according to rules that made no sense in the everyday world of coffee cups and baseballs. But somewhere between the atomic and the visible, the quantum gave way to the classical. The strangeness stopped. Anthony Leggett spent decades arguing that this boundary was negotiable, that quantum behavior could persist in objects large enough to see. The 2025 Nobel Prize in Physics, awarded to Leggett and experimentalists Jean-Michel Raimond and Serge Haroche, recognized the work that proved him right — and opened a world where the quantum and the classical intertwine.

The problem was philosophical as much as physical. Quantum mechanics worked perfectly for individual particles: an electron could be in two places at once, a photon could tunnel through a mirror. But a cat, even Schrödinger's famous thought-experiment cat, was never both alive and dead. Something happened at larger scales — some process called decoherence, some threshold of complexity or size — that collapsed the quantum weirdness into definite classical reality. Most physicists accepted this as an iron law. The quantum was the domain of the small.

Anthony Leggett, a British theoretical physicist working at the University of Illinois, didn't accept it. In the early 1980s, while most of his colleagues were chasing high-energy physics or the emerging field of quantum computing, Leggett was asking a quieter question: where exactly does the quantum end? Not philosophically, but physically. Could you take a system large enough to be called macroscopic — a collection of billions of atoms, say — and make it behave quantum-mechanically? Could you coax it to tunnel through an energy barrier, something explicitly forbidden in classical physics?

The system he focused on was the SQUID — the Superconducting Quantum Interference Device. A loop of superconducting metal interrupted by an insulating barrier, cooled to within a breath of absolute zero. Current could flow through the loop indefinitely without resistance, circulating in one direction or the other. Classically, the current should stay locked in whichever direction it started. But Leggett calculated that if the barrier was just right, if the temperature was low enough, if you could isolate the system from environmental noise, the current might do something impossible: tunnel from flowing clockwise to flowing counterclockwise, or vice versa, without ever passing through zero. The loop, containing trillions of electrons moving in concert, would behave like a single quantum particle.

This was macroscopic quantum tunneling. Not an atom crossing a barrier, but something you could — in principle, if you squinted hard enough — see. The current in a SQUID is macroscopic not because the loop is large (it's usually smaller than a grain of rice), but because it involves coherent motion of a vast number of particles acting as one. Leggett showed that quantum mechanics didn't forbid this; it predicted it. The math was unambiguous. The question was whether the universe would cooperate.

The experiments came slowly. In the late 1980s and through the 1990s, groups in Japan, the United States, and Europe pushed SQUID technology toward the edge of what Leggett had described. They saw hints — anomalies in how the devices switched states, behavior that couldn't be explained classically. But hints weren't proof. The problem was always noise: the environment leaking in, thermal fluctuations washing out the quantum signal, the sheer difficulty of making something large enough to matter behave small enough to stay quantum.

Jean-Michel Raimond and Serge Haroche came at the problem from a different angle. Working at the École Normale Supérieure in Paris, they weren't interested in SQUIDs. They wanted to trap individual atoms in cavities — metal boxes that confined photons — and watch what happened when the quantum and the classical met. Their technique, cavity quantum electrodynamics, allowed them to observe single photons interacting with single atoms. By the early 2000s, they had refined their methods to the point where they could prepare a photon field in a superposition state and watch it decohere in real time, step by step, as it interacted with the environment.

What they demonstrated was the mechanism by which quantum systems became classical: not a sharp boundary, but a continuous process. Entanglement with the environment — the photon field leaking information to stray atoms, to thermal radiation, to any system not perfectly isolated — gradually erased the superposition. The larger the system, the faster it happened. But crucially, Raimond and Haroche showed that for small, well-isolated systems, you could delay decoherence long enough to observe quantum behavior at scales previously thought impossible. Their work didn't just support Leggett's theory; it provided the experimental tools to test it.

The confirmation of macroscopic quantum tunneling came in the 2010s, as experimentalists finally achieved the isolation Leggett's predictions required. Groups at Delft, at IBM's Yorktown Heights lab, at the University of California, used ever-more sophisticated SQUIDs and superconducting circuits to observe tunneling events that could only be explained quantum-mechanically. The current in the loop didn't switch gradually; it jumped discontinuously, tunneling through the energy barrier exactly as Leggett had calculated four decades earlier.

By then, the implications had spread far beyond SQUIDs. Quantum tunneling at macroscopic scales became the foundation for superconducting qubits — the building blocks of quantum computers. Tech companies and national labs were racing to build machines with hundreds of qubits, each one a macroscopic quantum object held in superposition. What had been a theoretical curiosity in the 1980s was now a multibillion-dollar industry.

But the deeper significance was conceptual. The boundary between quantum and classical wasn't fixed by nature; it was negotiable, a matter of how well you could isolate your system and how carefully you could measure. There was no magic threshold where quantum rules stopped applying. In principle, anything could behave quantum-mechanically if you tried hard enough. A virus, a bacterium, even something visible to the naked eye — all were fair game, though the engineering challenges scaled brutally with size.

Leggett himself remained cautious about the hype. "People sometimes ask if we'll see quantum superposition of a cat," he said in a 2023 interview. "I think that's the wrong question. The question is: what does it mean for something to be in a superposition if there's no way, even in principle, to measure it? Quantum mechanics is about what you can observe, not about metaphysics." He was more interested in what happened at the edges — where quantum behavior gave way to classical, and why.

The Nobel committee's citation, released in October 2025, was unusually philosophical. It honored Leggett "for theoretical predictions of macroscopic quantum tunneling," and Raimond and Haroche "for experimental methods enabling the observation of macroscopic quantum phenomena." But the accompanying scientific background paper went further, noting that the work had "dissolved the conceptual boundary between the quantum and classical worlds, revealing that quantum mechanics is not confined to the atomic scale but extends to systems of arbitrary complexity, limited only by practical considerations of isolation and control."

For physicists, the prize felt overdue. Leggett, Raimond, and Haroche had won other major awards years earlier — the Wolf Prize, the Gold Medal of the CNRS, shared Milner prizes. But the Nobel tends to arrive when a field has matured, when the implications are fully visible. By 2025, quantum computers were running calculations beyond the reach of classical machines, quantum sensors were measuring gravitational waves and magnetic fields with unprecedented precision, and laboratories around the world were pushing quantum coherence to ever-larger scales. Macroscopic quantum tunneling wasn't just vindicated; it was everywhere.

There was one name conspicuously absent: Wojciech Zurek, whose work on decoherence in the 1980s and 1990s had provided much of the theoretical framework for understanding how quantum systems transitioned to classical behavior. Some physicists argued he should have shared the prize, that his contribution was as essential as Leggett's. The Nobel committee's rules allow only three laureates per prize, and they chose to honor the experimentalists alongside the theorist who made the first prediction. Zurek's omission sparked the usual debates about recognition and priority, but those who knew his work understood its centrality to the story.

The prize arrived at a moment of reckoning for quantum technology. Quantum computers existed, but they were temperamental, error-prone, useful only for specialized problems. Quantum networks could distribute encryption keys, but scaling them to intercontinental distances remained elusive. The promise of the quantum revolution, so often hyped, was real — but so were the challenges. Macroscopic quantum systems were possible, but making them useful required taming decoherence with a precision that nature resisted at every turn.

Leggett, Raimond, and Haroche had shown that the quantum didn't end where common sense said it should. They had proven that the strange rules governing electrons and photons could be coaxed into governing circuits, cavities, currents — anything, if you were clever and careful enough. What remained was the harder task: building a world where the quantum wasn't an exotic curiosity but a working tool, reliable and routine. The boundary had been crossed. The work of making that crossing practical had only begun.
