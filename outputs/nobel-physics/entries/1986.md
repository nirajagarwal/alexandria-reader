# 1986 — The Electron Microscope and Scanning Tunneling Microscope

Ernst Ruska received his Nobel Prize at eighty-one, fifty-five years after building the instrument that earned it. The electron microscope he constructed in 1931 had by then transformed biology, materials science, and medicine — yet the Nobel committee had waited through decades of refinement, applications, and two World Wars before honoring the young engineer's breakthrough. His co-laureates, Gerd Binnig and Heinrich Rohrer, waited only four years. Their scanning tunneling microscope, first operated in 1982, was so revolutionary that the committee could not delay. The 1986 physics prize thus became a strange temporal collision: recognition for an elderly pioneer whose life's work had been vindicated, and two researchers still in the first flush of discovery.

## The Barrier That Light Could Not Cross

For three centuries, optical microscopes had pushed deeper into the invisible world. But by the 1920s, they had reached an absolute limit — not of engineering, but of physics itself. Light waves cannot resolve details smaller than their own wavelength. Visible light bottoms out around 200 nanometers, about five hundred times smaller than a human hair. Anything tinier — viruses, large molecules, the fine structure of metals — remained stubbornly out of reach. Better lenses couldn't help. The wavelength barrier was fundamental.

Ernst Ruska, a young electrical engineering student in Berlin, understood that the solution lay in abandoning light altogether. Louis de Broglie had shown in 1924 that electrons, like photons, behave as waves — but with wavelengths far shorter than visible light. An electron accelerated to high voltage has a wavelength measured in picometers, thousands of times smaller than light's limit. If you could focus a beam of electrons the way glass lenses focus light, you could see the unseeable.

The problem was the focusing. Glass is opaque to electrons. Ruska and his supervisor Max Knoll turned to magnetic fields, which bend the paths of charged particles. In 1931, working in the basement of the Technical University of Berlin, Ruska built the first electron microscope — two magnetic coils acting as lenses, an electron gun, and a fluorescent screen. It was crude. The first images were blurry and barely better than optical microscopes. But the principle worked.

By 1933, Ruska had surpassed the resolution of light microscopy. By the end of the decade, electron microscopes could resolve details ten nanometers across. The electron microscope didn't just improve on its optical predecessor — it opened an entirely new realm. Viruses became visible for the first time. The structure of cells, previously guessed at, emerged in startling detail. Materials scientists could examine the grain boundaries in metals, the defects in crystals, the architecture of polymers.

## The Wait

Ruska's invention spread quickly. During World War II, both sides built electron microscopes for military research. By the 1950s, commercial instruments were standard equipment in research labs. Ruska himself spent much of his career at Siemens, refining the technology into a reliable industrial tool. The applications accumulated: the polio virus visualized, the structure of DNA imaged, the inner machinery of cells revealed layer by layer. Every field that depended on seeing small things was transformed.

Yet no Nobel Prize came. The committee watched, waiting for some threshold of certainty. Perhaps the long delay reflected unease about honoring engineering over pure physics. Perhaps they wanted to see just how far the technology would go. Ruska continued his work, patient or resigned. The invention had already changed the world; the prize seemed almost beside the point.

## The Quantum Leap

In the early 1980s, at IBM's research laboratory in Zurich, Gerd Binnig and Heinrich Rohrer were trying to study the surfaces of materials at the atomic scale. Electron microscopes, for all their power, had limitations. They required samples to be sliced thin, often destroyed by the imaging process itself. They couldn't easily probe the surface properties that mattered in semiconductor research. Binnig and Rohrer wanted something different — a way to "feel" the surface of a material, atom by atom, without destruction.

Their insight came from quantum mechanics. When two conductors are brought extremely close together — within a nanometer or so — electrons can tunnel through the gap between them, leaping across a space they classically have no business crossing. This quantum tunneling effect is exquisitely sensitive to distance. Move the tip closer by a single atomic diameter, and the tunneling current increases by an order of magnitude.

Binnig and Rohrer realized they could exploit this sensitivity. Take an atomically sharp metal tip and hold it just above a conducting surface. Apply a voltage and measure the tiny tunneling current flowing between tip and surface. Now scan the tip across the surface in a precise grid, adjusting its height to keep the current constant. The result is a map of the surface topography with atomic resolution — not by looking at atoms, but by feeling the quantum probability of electrons jumping the gap.

The engineering was absurdly difficult. Vibrations from footsteps or passing traffic would destroy the delicate spacing. Thermal expansion would cause the tip to drift. The tip itself had to end in a single atom for the sharpest resolution. They built the instrument on a magnetic levitation stage to isolate it from vibrations, cooled it to reduce thermal noise, and spent months refining the feedback electronics.

In 1982, they succeeded. The scanning tunneling microscope (STM) produced its first images of individual atoms on a silicon surface — not fuzzy probability clouds, but discrete spots marking atomic positions. The resolution was stunning: they could distinguish atoms separated by less than a tenth of a nanometer. Rohrer later recalled the moment with characteristic understatement: "We could hardly believe our eyes."

## Two Instruments, One Prize

The Nobel committee faced an unusual problem in 1986. Ruska's electron microscope and the Binnig-Rohrer STM were fundamentally different instruments, born fifty years apart, addressing different problems with different physics. Yet both represented the same essential breakthrough: finding a way past the limits of optical microscopy to see the architecture of matter at scales previously inaccessible.

The committee split the prize. Half to Ruska, for the electron microscope. Half shared between Binnig and Rohrer, for the scanning tunneling microscope. It was an acknowledgment that seeing the invisible was worth honoring twice, in two different generations.

Ruska, old and frail, traveled to Stockholm for the ceremony. He had outlived most of his collaborators, watched his invention mature from laboratory curiosity to essential tool. In his Nobel lecture, he traced the long path from those first blurry images in 1931 to the exquisite biological structures now routinely visualized. "I am glad that this recognition has come during my lifetime," he said simply.

Binnig and Rohrer, younger and more exuberant, spoke of the STM's future. Already, variants were emerging: atomic force microscopes that could image non-conducting materials, instruments that measured magnetic properties or chemical identities at atomic scale. In 1989, researchers at IBM would use an STM to manipulate individual xenon atoms on a surface, spelling out the company's logo atom by atom. The instrument had become a tool not just for seeing, but for building at the smallest possible scale.

## The Legacy

The electron microscope gave humanity its first look at viruses, at the molecular machines inside cells, at the nanostructure of materials. It remains indispensable — modern cryo-electron microscopes can determine protein structures in near-atomic detail, a capability recognized with its own Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2017.

The scanning tunneling microscope launched the entire field of scanning probe microscopy and made nanotechnology possible in practice rather than speculation. Every smartphone chip, every nanostructured material, every attempt to engineer devices at molecular scale traces back to that first image of atoms on a silicon surface.

Two instruments, separated by half a century. One used the wave nature of electrons to overcome light's limits; the other exploited quantum tunneling's eerie sensitivity. Together they extended human vision down to the scale of atoms — revealing a world that had always existed beneath our perception, waiting to be seen.
