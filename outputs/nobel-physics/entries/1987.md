# 1987 — High-Temperature Superconductivity

On a January morning in 1987, physicists lined up outside a New York City ballroom before dawn. By the time the session started, the room was packed beyond capacity — people sat in aisles, stood against walls, crowded the doorways. The American Physical Society meeting was witnessing something unprecedented: hundreds of scientists eager to hear about materials that conduct electricity without resistance at temperatures once thought impossibly high. The session ran past midnight. Journalists called it the "Woodstock of Physics."

Seven months later, the Nobel Committee awarded the prize for the discovery that had ignited this frenzy. Not to one scientist, but two: J. Georg Bednorz and K. Alex Müller of IBM's Zurich Research Laboratory. The speed was extraordinary — their paper had appeared barely twenty months earlier. But the Committee recognized that Bednorz and Müller had shattered a theoretical ceiling and opened a field that might transform technology.

Superconductivity had been known since 1911, when Heike Kamerlingh Onnes cooled mercury to 4 Kelvin and watched its electrical resistance vanish completely. For seventy years, physicists found more superconductors, but none worked above 23 Kelvin — still far colder than liquid nitrogen's 77 Kelvin. Theory suggested this wasn't just technical limitation but fundamental physics. Superconductivity required electrons to pair up through interactions with vibrating atoms, and at higher temperatures, thermal energy should tear these pairs apart. The consensus: superconductivity above 30 Kelvin was impossible.

Alex Müller didn't accept consensus. Born in Switzerland in 1927, he'd spent his career at IBM Zurich studying materials with strange electronic properties. By the 1980s, he was a senior physicist with the freedom to pursue risky ideas. He'd become fascinated by ceramic oxides — compounds that conventional wisdom dismissed as insulators, the opposite of conductors. But Müller suspected their complex crystal structures might allow unusual electron pairing. In 1983, he recruited Georg Bednorz, a German physicist thirteen years his junior who'd just finished his doctorate and had expertise in preparing delicate ceramic samples.

It was an improbable partnership. Müller was the theorist and senior scientist; Bednorz the experimentalist and technician. They worked almost alone, pursuing an idea most physicists considered quixotic. IBM's management tolerated their project but didn't emphasize it. For two years, they tested ceramic oxides, cooling samples to liquid helium temperatures and measuring resistance. Nothing worked. Bednorz synthesized compound after compound — barium, lanthanum, copper, and oxygen in various arrangements — baking them in furnaces at precise temperatures, grinding and mixing and trying again.

In January 1986, they tested lanthanum barium copper oxide. At 35 Kelvin, the resistance dropped sharply. Not to zero — ceramic samples are never perfect — but the signature was unmistakable. They'd exceeded the theoretical limit. Bednorz prepared better samples. By April, they had clear evidence of superconductivity at 35 Kelvin, possibly higher. They sat on the result for months, checking and rechecking. IBM's patent attorneys advised them to file before publishing. In September, their paper appeared in a German journal, deliberately obscure in title: "Possible High Tc Superconductivity in the Ba-La-Cu-O System."

The physics world took notice slowly, then explosively. By December, groups in Tokyo, Beijing, and Houston were replicating and improving the result. In Houston, Paul Chu replaced lanthanum with yttrium and achieved superconductivity at 92 Kelvin — above liquid nitrogen temperature. That breakthrough made the phenomenon practical. Liquid nitrogen is cheap and abundant; liquid helium is expensive and scarce. Suddenly, superconductors that could work in nitrogen-cooled systems seemed economically feasible.

The March 1987 APS meeting became legendary. The session began at 7:15 PM and was supposed to end at 10:30. So many scientists wanted to present that the organizers extended it to midnight, then kept going. Müller and Bednorz were there, watching their discovery spawn a scientific stampede. Physicists reported new materials, higher temperatures, new theories. Arguments erupted. One speaker's slides were upside down and nobody noticed because the room was too dark and too crowded to fix them. When it ended past midnight, clusters of scientists continued arguing in hotel lobbies until dawn.

The frenzy had reason. High-temperature superconductors promised revolution: lossless power transmission, magnetically levitated trains, powerful compact magnets for medicine and research, faster computers. If superconductivity could work at room temperature — a tantalizing possibility these ceramic materials suggested — the implications for energy and technology were staggering.

Yet the physics proved maddeningly complex. The ceramic copper oxides were layered materials with electrons moving in two-dimensional planes. The mechanism wasn't the conventional electron-phonon coupling. Theorists proposed exotic explanations: quantum spin fluctuations, magnetic interactions, new types of electron pairing. Forty years later, no consensus theory fully explains how these materials work. The complexity that made them surprising also made them resistant to understanding.

And the technological promises proved harder to realize. The ceramic materials were brittle, difficult to form into wires, and their performance degraded under magnetic fields and electric currents. Room-temperature superconductivity remained elusive — claims appeared periodically but failed replication. Applications emerged slowly: MRI magnets improved, particle accelerators used high-temperature superconducting cables, power companies tested transmission lines. But the revolution stayed partial, incremental.

None of this diminished what Bednorz and Müller accomplished. They proved that deeply held theoretical limits were wrong. They demonstrated that materials dismissed as insulators could become superconductors. They opened a field that thousands of physicists still explore. Müller, in his Nobel lecture, noted the discovery's "accidental" quality — they'd been looking for superconductivity, but not necessarily at those temperatures, not necessarily in those materials. "We were lucky," he said. But luck favors the prepared mind, and the stubborn one.

The Nobel came with unusual speed — less than two years from publication to prize. The Committee recognized that verification was solid and impact was immediate. Bednorz was just forty; Müller was sixty. Their partnership, formed in a corner of an IBM lab while pursuing an idea others thought foolish, had cracked open a new physics of condensed matter that remains incompletely understood. They showed that materials could surprise us, that theoretical limits might be wrong, that the path to discovery often begins where consensus says not to look.
