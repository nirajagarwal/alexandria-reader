# 1923 — The Elementary Charge of Electricity

The oil drop hung suspended in the electric field, a microscopic sphere barely visible through the microscope, defying gravity. Robert Millikan watched it rise and fall as he adjusted the voltage, timing its motion with a stopwatch. Each measurement brought him closer to the number that would settle one of physics' most fundamental questions: what is the smallest possible unit of electric charge? After five years, thousands of observations, and disputes that would shadow his reputation for decades, he had his answer.

The electron had been discovered in 1897 by J.J. Thomson, but knowing that something existed wasn't the same as knowing its properties. Thomson could measure the ratio of the electron's charge to its mass, but the absolute value of the charge itself remained elusive. This wasn't mere precision-seeking. The elementary charge — if it existed — was a cornerstone of atomic theory. If electric charge came in discrete packets rather than continuous amounts, it meant matter itself was fundamentally quantized. Everything depended on measuring something impossibly small: 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ coulombs, a number so tiny that 6 billion billion electrons would be needed to light a 60-watt bulb for one second.

Millikan's experimental approach was elegant in conception, painstaking in execution. He sprayed tiny oil droplets into a chamber between two charged metal plates. The droplets picked up electric charge from ionized air molecules — sometimes losing an electron, sometimes gaining one. By adjusting the voltage between the plates, Millikan could balance the electrical force against gravity, holding a single droplet motionless in mid-air. Then, by measuring how the droplet's motion changed when it gained or lost charge, he could calculate the charge itself.

The beauty lay in what happened next. Droplet after droplet, measurement after measurement, the charges always came in multiples of the same base number. A droplet might carry one unit, or three, or five — but never 2.3 or 4.7. The elementary charge revealed itself not through a single perfect measurement but through the pattern: thousands of data points, all integer multiples of the same fundamental quantity. It was like weighing coins to discover that money comes in pennies, or measuring water to find that everything divides into molecules. Electric charge was quantized. The electron's charge was real, discrete, irreducible.

The work consumed Millikan from 1909 to 1913 at the University of Chicago. He and his graduate student Harvey Fletcher spent years perfecting the apparatus, controlling temperature, eliminating air currents, accounting for every source of error. The oil drops — each about a thousandth of a millimeter across — would evaporate or drift out of view. They had to work in a darkened room, watching pinpoints of light for hours. Fletcher later recalled: "We would come out of that dark room after five or six hours and we could hardly see."

But there was another person in that dark room whose name wouldn't appear on the papers: Harvey Fletcher himself. The young graduate student had been instrumental in developing the technique and gathering data. Years later, Fletcher revealed that Millikan had offered him a choice: publish the oil drop work under his own name and delay his PhD, or let Millikan publish it solo and graduate immediately with work on a different topic. Fletcher, eager to finish and support his young family, chose the latter. "I agreed," Fletcher wrote decades later, "since I was married, and wanted a job, and the oil drop [paper] would not have helped me get one." It was standard academic practice at the time, but it meant the Nobel committee never knew Fletcher's contribution.

The controversy deepened when another physicist, Felix Ehrenhaft in Vienna, claimed to have found charges smaller than Millikan's elementary unit — suggesting either that Millikan was wrong or that something even more fundamental than the electron existed. The dispute grew bitter. Millikan dismissed Ehrenhaft's results as experimental error. Ehrenhaft accused Millikan of theoretical bias. The physics community had to choose whom to believe.

In 1978, historian Gerald Holton examined Millikan's original notebooks and discovered something troubling. Millikan had discarded a significant fraction of his data — 49 of 140 observations — from the published results. Next to rejected drops, Millikan had written notes like "best one yet" or "beauty" for those he kept, and "something wrong" or "will not work" for those excluded. His published papers claimed he had reported all observations. The omitted data points didn't change his final answer significantly, but they violated his explicit claims of complete transparency. Millikan had found the right answer through selective reporting — scientifically valid but methodologically dishonest.

Yet the result itself stood firm. Millikan's value for the elementary charge — 1.592 × 10⁻¹⁹ coulombs — was remarkably close to today's accepted value of 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ coulombs, accurate to within 1%. The quantization of charge became a cornerstone of quantum mechanics. It confirmed that atoms really did contain discrete particles with specific properties. It made possible the understanding of chemical bonds, the behavior of semiconductors, the structure of matter itself. Every electron in the universe carries exactly that charge, no more, no less. Millikan had revealed a fundamental constant of nature.

The Nobel Prize came in 1923, not just for the oil drop experiment but also for his work on the photoelectric effect, which had helped confirm Einstein's quantum theory of light. The citation praised his determination of "the elementary charge of electricity and on the photoelectric effect." It mentioned no collaborators. Fletcher's name appeared nowhere. The controversy over the dropped data wouldn't surface for another half-century.

Robert Millikan accepted his prize in Stockholm at age 55, by then a towering figure in American physics. He would go on to lead Caltech and help establish American physics as a world power. Harvey Fletcher became a distinguished physicist in his own right, pioneering work in acoustics and inventing the hearing aid, but never received credit for the work that helped define the electron. The oil drop experiment remains a staple of undergraduate physics labs, where students recreate Millikan's measurements — though usually with less patience and more frustration than the original required.

The elementary charge turned out to be more than just a number. It was proof that the quantum world was real, that nature operated in discrete units, that the smooth continuous physics of Newton and Maxwell broke down at the smallest scales. A single number, measured by watching nearly invisible oil drops hover in Chicago, opened the door to everything that followed: quantum mechanics, particle physics, the Standard Model. Sometimes the most fundamental truths hide in the smallest places, waiting for someone stubborn enough to measure them precisely, whatever the cost.
