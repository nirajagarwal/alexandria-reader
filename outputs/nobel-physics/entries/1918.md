# 1918 — Energy Quanta

Max Planck did not want to be revolutionary. At sixty, in a Germany collapsing under war and blockade, he had spent two decades trying to undo what he'd done in 1900. The equation that bore his name — *E = hν*, energy equals a constant times frequency — had solved the blackbody radiation problem. It had also introduced something deeply wrong into physics: the quantum.

Planck had hoped it was mathematical sleight-of-hand, a calculational trick that would eventually dissolve into proper continuous physics. Instead, the quantum had metastasized. Einstein used it to explain the photoelectric effect. Bohr built an atom from it. By 1918, the discontinuous, the discrete, the granular had infected all of physics. And the man who'd opened this door kept trying to close it.

The Nobel committee gave Planck the 1918 Physics Prize in November 1919 — delayed a year by the war's aftermath and the committee's reluctance to honor German scientists while the armistice was still raw. The citation read "in recognition of the services he rendered to the advancement of Physics by his discovery of energy quanta." Services. As if he'd cleaned up a mess rather than making one.

## The Problem That Wouldn't Behave

The problem was simple to state, impossible to solve. Heat a cavity — an oven, essentially — until it glows. Measure the spectrum of light it emits at different temperatures. Classical physics, the beautiful edifice of thermodynamics and electromagnetism, predicted catastrophe: infinite energy at high frequencies, the "ultraviolet catastrophe." Objects should radiate away all their energy instantly. They didn't. Why not?

Planck attacked it in 1900 with the tools he trusted: thermodynamics, statistical mechanics, rigorous calculation. He derived a formula that matched the experimental data perfectly. Then he worked backward to understand what physics the formula implied. What he found horrified him.

Energy couldn't be continuous. Radiation wasn't emitted in smooth waves but in discrete packets — quanta — whose size was determined by *h*, a new constant of nature. An oscillator of frequency *ν* could only emit or absorb energy in chunks of *hν*. Not 1.5 times *hν*, not 0.7 times *hν*. Integer multiples only. Energy came in denominations, like money.

It worked. That was the problem. It worked so well that Planck couldn't dismiss it as error, but it violated everything he believed about how nature operated. "A theoretical interpretation had to be found at any cost, no matter how high," he wrote later. The cost turned out to be classical physics itself.

## The Conservative Revolutionary

Max Karl Ernst Ludwig Planck came from Prussian academic aristocracy. His great-grandfather and grandfather were theologians; his father a law professor. He absorbed their values: duty, rigor, reverence for established order. He considered becoming a philologist before choosing physics, and he brought a philologist's precision to the subject. His doctoral thesis, completed at twenty-one, applied thermodynamics with mathematical elegance. He worshipped Clausius, revered the second law, believed in the absolute nature of physical principles.

This was not a man built for revolution. When his own quantum hypothesis proved resilient, he spent years trying to interpret it away. Perhaps the quantization was in matter's interaction with radiation, not in radiation itself? Perhaps it was a statistical approximation, not a fundamental truth? He explored every possible escape route. None worked.

The young Einstein, thirteen years Planck's junior, had no such hesitations. In 1905, he took Planck's reluctant quantum and ran with it. Light itself was quantized, Einstein argued — not just its emission and absorption. Radiation traveled in discrete particles, later called photons. Planck thought this went too far. He spent the next fifteen years trying to preserve wave theory while keeping the quantum hypothesis. "The hypothesis of light quanta," he wrote in 1913, "is not reconcilable with experimentally verified facts of interference." He would rather abandon his own discovery than accept Einstein's extension of it.

Yet he recognized Einstein's genius. In 1913, Planck recruited Einstein to the Prussian Academy and helped him settle in Berlin. Their relationship mixed intellectual respect with fundamental disagreement. Planck the conservative, Einstein the radical — but Planck had lit the fuse.

## The Number That Changed Everything

The Planck constant, *h*, measured approximately 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ joule-seconds. Absurdly small. At everyday scales, it vanished into irrelevance, which was why classical physics had worked so beautifully for centuries. But at atomic scales, *h* dominated everything. It set the size of atoms, determined spectral lines, governed chemical bonds, limited the precision of measurements. It was the toll booth between the classical and quantum worlds.

Planck calculated *h* from blackbody radiation data with extraordinary precision. From *h* and other measurable quantities, he extracted Avogadro's number — how many atoms in a given mass — more accurately than any chemist had managed. He estimated the charge on an electron five years before Millikan measured it directly. These were not abstractions. Planck's constant touched the tangible world at every point. You couldn't build a laser without it, couldn't explain why metals conduct, couldn't understand why the sun shines.

The equation *E = hν* looked simple. Its implications were vertigo-inducing. If energy came in packets, so did everything built from energy. Angular momentum was quantized. Electron orbits were quantized. Vibrations, spins, fields — all quantized. The smooth, continuous universe Newton and Maxwell had described was actually grainy at the bottom, pixelated like a photograph examined too closely. Nature didn't flow; it jumped.

## War and Physics

The Nobel Prize found Planck in ruins. His first wife, Marie, had died in 1909. His son Karl fell at Verdun in 1916. His daughter Grete died in childbirth in 1917. His other daughter Emma died in childbirth in 1919. He had remarried — Marga von Hösslin, his late wife's niece — and tried to maintain normalcy while Germany starved under blockade and revolution convulsed Berlin's streets.

Through it all, he kept working. He served as permanent secretary of the Prussian Academy, a position of considerable administrative burden. He mediated between scientists and government. He defended academic freedom, though carefully, knowing how little freedom survived in a militarized state. When colleagues drifted toward extreme nationalism, Planck urged restraint. When others called for total boycotts of German science after the war, Planck worked to preserve international communication.

The Nobel Prize ceremony in 1920 was complicated. Planck couldn't travel to Stockholm — the wounds were too fresh, the politics too fraught. He sent a written lecture instead. The Royal Swedish Academy praised his "epoch-making discovery" while European science remained fractured by national hatreds. French physicists still refused to cite German papers. British scientists debated whether to readmit Germans to international conferences. Planck's quantum had no nationality, but science itself was tangled in bitterness.

## What They Gave Him the Prize For

The 1918 Nobel wasn't for quantum mechanics — that didn't exist yet. It wasn't for the photoelectric effect — Einstein got that prize in 1921, also delayed. It was specifically for the quantum hypothesis as applied to blackbody radiation, the original 1900 work. The committee wanted to be precise. They gave Planck credit for introducing *h* into physics, for solving a problem that had defied solution, for creating a tool that others would use. But they didn't claim to understand where it was leading.

By 1920, nobody did. Bohr's atom worked but violated classical physics at every step. Sommerfeld's refinements added epicycles on epicycles. Experiments kept finding phenomena — the Compton effect, atomic spectra, the Stark effect — that demanded quantum explanations. But there was no consistent theory, just a collection of quantum rules bolted onto classical frameworks. Planck had opened the door in 1900. Twenty years later, physics was still trying to figure out what room it had entered.

The full quantum mechanics wouldn't arrive until 1925-1926, when Heisenberg, Schrödinger, and Dirac finally constructed a complete mathematical framework. Planck would live to see it, though he never fully embraced its stranger implications — uncertainty, wave-particle duality, the collapse of determinism. He accepted that the quantum was real. He never stopped hoping it might still be tamed.

## The Elegant Horror

Here was the terrible beauty of Planck's discovery: he'd found the smallest possible change. Not small, smallest. There was no such thing as half a quantum. You couldn't split *h*. It was nature's Planck length in action — the fundamental tick of the universe's clock, the minimum bid in the energy auction.

Classical physics assumed infinite divisibility, that you could always zoom in further, divide finer, smooth out discontinuities. Planck proved otherwise. At the bottom, nature was discrete. This discreteness had elegance — it prevented the ultraviolet catastrophe, stabilized atoms, explained spectral lines. But it also meant that determinism, the clockwork universe Laplace had envisioned, was dead. You couldn't predict exactly when an atom would emit a photon, only the probability. God, as Einstein would later complain, was playing dice.

Planck never liked that either. "I regard consciousness as fundamental," he said in a 1931 interview. "I regard matter as derivative from consciousness. We cannot get behind consciousness." This from the man who'd first quantized energy. The revolutionary remained a conservative at heart, searching for order and meaning in a universe that turned out to be built from discontinuity and chance.

## The Aftermath

The quantum hypothesis outlived its creator's doubts. Every subsequent discovery vindicated it. The Standard Model of particle physics, quantum field theory, quantum computing — all built on the foundation Planck laid in 1900. The Planck constant appears in Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, in Schrödinger's equation, in every calculation about the microscopic world. Modern technology — semiconductors, lasers, MRI machines, atomic clocks — couldn't exist without *h*.

Planck lived through worse than his own conceptual discomfort. The Nazis came to power when he was seventy-five. He tried to intercede personally with Hitler to protect Jewish scientists. It accomplished nothing. He watched colleagues dismissed, institutions corrupted, science weaponized. His son Erwin, implicated in the 1944 plot to assassinate Hitler, was tortured and executed. Planck survived the war, barely, dying in 1947 at eighty-nine.

The quantum survived too. It turned out that energy quanta were the least strange part. Once you accepted that energy came in packets, you had to accept that particles could be waves, that observation affected outcomes, that reality at the quantum level was probabilistic, not deterministic. Planck had tried to introduce a minimal modification to classical physics. Instead, he'd demolished it.

"A new scientific truth does not triumph by convincing its opponents," Planck wrote, "but rather because its opponents eventually die, and a new generation grows up that is familiar with it." He was describing his own discovery, and his own reluctance. The man who introduced the quantum into physics never fully accepted what he had done. But he'd done it anyway, with precision and rigor, because the data demanded it. That was enough.
