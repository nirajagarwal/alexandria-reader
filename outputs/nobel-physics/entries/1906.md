# 1906 — The Electron

The electron had no name yet when J.J. Thomson found it. He called them "corpuscles" — particles thousands of times lighter than the lightest atom, torn from matter itself, carrying a charge that could be bent by magnets and accelerated by voltage. Other physicists were skeptical. Atoms were supposed to be indivisible. The word meant "cannot be cut." Thomson had cut them anyway, and in 1906 the Nobel Prize recognized what he'd done: discovered the first subatomic particle, the first piece of matter smaller than an atom.

He hadn't expected to find it. Nobody had.

## The Glow in the Tube

In the 1890s, physicists were obsessed with cathode rays — the mysterious glow that appeared when you pumped most of the air out of a glass tube and ran high voltage between metal plates at each end. The glow streamed from the negative electrode (the cathode) toward the positive one. But what *was* it? German physicists thought it was waves, like light. British physicists thought it was particles. The arguments were fierce and national, tinged with the academic rivalries of the era.

Thomson, running the Cavendish Laboratory at Cambridge, sided with the particle camp but couldn't prove it. The problem was that cathode rays traveled straight and seemed unaffected by electric fields — which particles carrying charge absolutely should not do. When he placed charged plates around the beam, nothing happened.

In 1897, he tried again with better vacuum pumps. The residual gas in the tube had been masking the effect. With the pressure low enough, the cathode rays bent. They curved toward the positive plate, which meant they were negatively charged particles, not waves. Thomson could measure how much they bent under both electric and magnetic fields. From that, he could calculate the ratio of their charge to their mass.

The number shocked him. The ratio was enormous — about 1,800 times larger than for a hydrogen ion, the lightest charged particle known. Either the charge was impossibly large, or the mass was impossibly small. Thomson concluded the mass was small. These "corpuscles" were nearly two thousand times lighter than hydrogen, the lightest atom. He had found something smaller than atoms, something that came *from* atoms.

He tested corpuscles from different cathodes — platinum, aluminum, iron — and got the same result every time. He extracted them from gases using ultraviolet light. Same corpuscles. Whatever these particles were, they weren't unique to one material. They were universal, a constituent of all matter.

"Could anything at first sight seem more impractical than a body which is so small that its mass is an insignificant fraction of the mass of an atom of hydrogen?" he later wrote. "Yet it is the electron that has made television possible, and the electron microscope, the valve radio, the talking film, and many other revolutionary technical developments."

That was decades later. In 1897, he was just trying to understand a glow in a tube.

## The Man Who Built the Cavendish

Joseph John Thomson — always J.J., never Joseph — was 41 when he identified the electron, and he'd been running the Cavendish since he was 28. The appointment had been controversial. He was a theorist in a position meant for an experimentalist, young and unproven, replacing Lord Rayleigh. But Thomson had a talent for making things work and a gift for asking the right questions. He transformed the Cavendish into the most productive physics laboratory in the world.

He wasn't personally dexterous. His students learned to keep him away from delicate apparatus. But he had an intuition for what mattered and a willingness to let others handle the fine work. His lab was collaborative, almost familial. Seven of his research assistants eventually won Nobel Prizes — more than any other mentor in history.

Thomson himself was modest about the discovery. He knew the corpuscle was important, but he understood it as one piece of a larger puzzle. Atoms weren't indivisible after all. They had internal structure, and the corpuscle was part of that structure. But what was the rest? He proposed a model: the atom as a sphere of positive charge with corpuscles embedded in it, like plums in a pudding. 

It was wrong. His student Ernest Rutherford would prove that in 1911, scattering alpha particles off gold foil and discovering the nucleus — a tiny, dense core of positive charge with electrons orbiting around it. But Thomson's plum pudding model was a necessary step. You can't build the right theory until you've learned from the wrong one.

## A Name for the Thing

The word "electron" existed before Thomson's discovery, coined in 1891 by the Irish physicist George Johnstone Stoney to describe a unit of electrical charge. After Thomson's work, the name stuck to the particle itself. "Corpuscle" faded from use, though Thomson kept calling them that for years.

Not everyone accepted Thomson's interpretation immediately. Some physicists thought the corpuscles might be artifacts of the experimental setup, or clusters of smaller things, or a form of ionized gas. But the evidence accumulated. In 1909, Robert Millikan measured the charge of an electron directly with his oil-drop experiment, confirming that the charge was quantized — always the same value or simple multiples of it. The electron was real, fundamental, and everywhere.

Thomson's discovery opened a door. Within a decade, physicists were probing atomic structure, discovering isotopes, unraveling radioactivity. Within two decades, quantum mechanics was born. The electron was at the center of it all — the particle that absorbed and emitted light, that determined chemical bonds, that carried electricity through wires and across vacuum tubes.

The 20th century was the electron's century. Electronics, telecommunications, computing — all built on the manipulation of particles Thomson pulled from a glow in a glass tube. He couldn't have known. In 1897, he was just trying to settle an argument about cathode rays.

## The Nobel, Given Late

The Nobel committee waited nine years to award Thomson the prize, unusually long for such a fundamental discovery. Part of the delay was caution — they wanted to be sure the corpuscle was real and universal. Part was the nature of the discovery itself: there was no single dramatic experiment, no moment when everything changed. Thomson built the case piece by piece, measuring deflections and calculating ratios, ruling out alternative explanations. It was painstaking, meticulous work, the kind that doesn't always get celebrated until its implications become undeniable.

By 1906, those implications were clear. Thomson received the prize "in recognition of the great merits of his theoretical and experimental investigations on the conduction of electricity by gases." That phrasing undersells it. What he'd done was reveal the electron, the first hint that matter had layers, that atoms were not the end but the beginning of a deeper structure.

His Nobel lecture was characteristically understated. He talked about cathode rays and discharge tubes, about measurements and ratios, about the universality of the corpuscle. He mentioned that the mass was small and the implications were large. He didn't claim to have revolutionized physics. He let the work speak.

But he had. The electron was the first crack in the foundation of classical physics, the first sign that the world was stranger than anyone imagined. Thomson found it by asking a simple question about a puzzling phenomenon and following the answer wherever it led, even when it led somewhere impossible.

The atom could be cut after all. Inside, waiting to be discovered, were particles so small they made atoms look like mountains. And once you saw them, you couldn't unsee them. The world was made of electrons. It still is.
