# 1972 — Superconductivity

In 1957, John Bardeen walked past his own office at the University of Illinois. Inside, two younger physicists were still working—Leon Cooper and Robert Schrieffer. They'd been at it for months, trying to explain something that had baffled science for forty-six years: why certain metals, when cooled to nearly absolute zero, lost all electrical resistance. Bardeen had already won one Nobel Prize, in 1956, for inventing the transistor. He was fifty years old and could have rested. Instead, he was chasing what he called "the most important unsolved problem in solid state physics." That night in 1957, the three men finally cracked it. Fifteen years later, they would share the Nobel Prize, and Bardeen would become the only person ever to win the physics prize twice.

The puzzle began in 1911, when Dutch physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes discovered that mercury's electrical resistance didn't just decrease as it cooled—below 4.2 Kelvin, it vanished completely. Current flowing through a superconducting ring would circle forever, without losing energy. It violated intuition. Resistance came from electrons colliding with atoms; cooling reduced atomic vibrations, so resistance should decrease—but why would it disappear entirely? And then there was the Meissner effect, discovered in 1933: superconductors expelled magnetic fields, making magnets hover above them as if repelled by an invisible force. Whatever was happening wasn't just extremely low resistance. It was a fundamentally different state of matter.

For decades, the problem resisted solution. Einstein tried and failed. The greatest minds in quantum mechanics couldn't explain it. The breakthrough required a conceptual leap that seemed, at first, absurd.

The key insight came from Cooper in 1956. Electrons repel each other—their identical negative charges push them apart. But Cooper showed that in a metal lattice at low temperatures, electrons could form pairs. An electron moving through the lattice attracted nearby positive ions, creating a tiny distortion in the crystal structure. That distortion could attract a second electron. The effect was vanishingly small, but at temperatures near absolute zero, it was enough. Electrons paired up.

These "Cooper pairs" behaved nothing like individual electrons. A single electron is a fermion, obeying Pauli's exclusion principle—no two can occupy the same quantum state. But a pair of electrons acts like a boson, and bosons can all crowd into the same quantum state. In a superconductor, all the Cooper pairs condense into a single quantum state, moving through the lattice in perfect synchrony. They flow as one coherent wave, and nothing can scatter them. Resistance requires knocking individual electrons out of their paths, but you can't scatter part of a quantum state that spans the entire material. Either you disrupt the whole thing—which requires more energy than thermal vibrations provide—or it flows unimpeded.

Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer—the "BCS theory" that now bears their initials—published their complete theory in 1957. The mathematics was formidable, drawing on quantum field theory techniques rarely used in condensed matter physics. But the physical picture was elegant: electrons dancing in pairs through a lattice, protected by quantum mechanics from the friction that plagues ordinary conductors.

The theory explained why only some materials superconduct, why each has a specific critical temperature, why that temperature is so low. It predicted how superconductors should respond to magnetic fields, how energy gaps should appear in their electronic spectra. Every prediction checked out. Within a year, experimentalists were confirming details of the theory. Philip Anderson later called it "the most beautiful paper I ever read in my life."

Bardeen was an unlikely revolutionary. Soft-spoken, methodical, he worked in a quiet office and went home to dinner with his family. His students remember him as patient, never claiming more credit than due, always focused on the physics rather than the glory. The Nobel Committee had honored him in 1956 alongside William Shockley and Walter Brattain for the transistor—the invention that launched the digital age. Now, six years later, he was developing the theory that would explain superconductivity. The double Nobel seemed inconceivable. No one had done it, not Einstein, not Bohr, not Fermi.

Cooper had arrived at Illinois as a postdoc in 1955, fresh from working with Heisenberg. The Cooper pair insight was his first major contribution. Schrieffer was a graduate student, twenty-five years old when the theory came together. He'd been ready to give up several times, convinced the problem was beyond them. Bardeen kept him going, convinced the pieces were nearly in place.

The night they finally solved it, Schrieffer had been working through the mathematics for hours. Around midnight, everything clicked. He ran to Bardeen's house—Bardeen was already asleep, but came downstairs in his bathrobe. They worked through the details at the kitchen table until 3 a.m. By morning, they knew they had it.

The 1972 Nobel Prize recognized work published fifteen years earlier—an unusual delay. The committee wanted to be certain. Superconductivity theories had been proposed before; BCS theory was so complete, so precise in its predictions, that it took years for the experimental verification to become overwhelming. By 1972, superconductivity wasn't just understood—it was being engineered.

The applications multiplied. Superconducting magnets produced the strongest magnetic fields achievable, essential for particle accelerators and eventually for MRI machines. Superconducting wires could carry enormous currents without loss, though the cooling requirements kept them impractical for power grids. SQUIDs—superconducting quantum interference devices—became the most sensitive magnetometers ever built, measuring magnetic fields billions of times weaker than Earth's. They mapped brain activity, detected ore deposits, measured ancient magnetism frozen in rocks.

But the cooling problem remained. The highest-temperature superconductor known in 1972 required cooling to 23 Kelvin—still far too cold for practical large-scale use. BCS theory suggested a fundamental limit: materials should stop superconducting above 30-40 Kelvin. The electron-phonon interaction that formed Cooper pairs grew too weak at higher temperatures.

Then, in 1986, everything changed. Georg Bednorz and Karl Alex Müller discovered ceramic materials that superconduct above 90 Kelvin—higher than the temperature of liquid nitrogen, which is cheap and abundant. Within months, other groups found materials superconducting above 130 Kelvin. These "high-temperature superconductors" shouldn't exist according to conventional BCS theory. Thirty-five years later, physicists still don't fully understand how they work. The Cooper pairing mechanism seems to be different, possibly involving magnetic interactions or quantum fluctuations that BCS theory doesn't account for. The discovery won Bednorz and Müller the 1987 Nobel Prize—one of the fastest awards in Nobel history—but the underlying theory remains incomplete.

Bardeen died in 1991, having seen superconductivity transform from an inexplicable curiosity to an explained phenomenon to a new frontier of mystery. He'd won his second Nobel at age sixty-three, defying the notion that theoretical physics belongs to the young. When reporters asked him about winning twice, he was characteristically modest: "It's nice to get recognition, but what really matters is the work."

The two Nobel medals sat in his office at Illinois, not in any special display case. Just on the shelf, among the papers and books. His family said he was prouder of the science than the prizes. He'd helped invent the device that made the information age possible, then explained a quantum phenomenon that seemed to break the rules of ordinary matter. Both achievements came from the same approach: careful observation, patient thought, willingness to pursue an idea wherever it led.

Cooper pairs still flow through superconducting wires, their quantum coherence maintained by the same physics Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer illuminated in 1957. The theory stands as one of quantum mechanics' great triumphs—a complete explanation of an emergent phenomenon, where the collective behavior of countless particles creates something impossible for individuals alone. And somewhere in a laboratory, physicists are still cooling new materials, still hoping to find room-temperature superconductivity, still chasing the dream that began in 1911 when resistance first vanished into quantum mystery.
