# 1977 — Electronic Structure of Magnetic and Disordered Systems

Philip Anderson stood at a blackboard in 1958, drawing what looked like a random scattering of points. He was explaining why electrons could stop moving entirely — not because they were trapped by some obvious barrier, but because disorder itself could freeze them in place. The idea seemed absurd. Everyone knew that electrons in metals moved freely, bouncing off occasional impurities but generally flowing like water through a pipe. Anderson was saying that if you added enough randomness, the pipe would somehow become a wall. He was right.

Nearly twenty years later, the Nobel committee honored Anderson alongside Nevill Mott and John van Vleck "for their fundamental theoretical investigations of the electronic structure of magnetic and disordered systems." The citation tied together three strands of work that had reshaped how physicists understood the behavior of electrons in materials that weren't perfect crystals — which is to say, most materials in the real world.

## The Problem of Imperfection

Solid state physics in the mid-twentieth century had achieved extraordinary success with perfect crystals. The mathematical machinery worked beautifully when atoms sat in orderly arrays. But real materials aren't perfect. They have impurities, defects, irregularities. Glass isn't crystalline at all. Magnetic materials have spins pointing in complicated patterns. For decades, physicists treated these complications as inconveniences — minor perturbations to clean theory.

The three 1977 laureates showed that disorder and magnetism weren't complications to be explained away. They were central to understanding how materials actually behave. Their work opened up the physics of everything from semiconductors to glass, from magnetic storage to the transition between metals and insulators.

## Van Vleck's Magnetic Foundation

John van Vleck was the elder statesman, seventy-eight at the time of the prize. His contribution dated back to the 1930s, when quantum mechanics was new and the magnetic properties of materials were deeply mysterious. Why did some materials become magnetic while others didn't? Why did magnetism appear and disappear at specific temperatures?

Van Vleck applied quantum mechanics to the problem with mathematical rigor and physical insight. He showed how the magnetic moment of atoms in crystals arose from the quantum mechanical properties of electron orbitals — how electrons paired up, how they interacted with neighbors, why certain configurations led to magnetism and others didn't. His 1932 book *The Theory of Electric and Magnetic Susceptibilities* became the foundation for modern magnetic theory.

The work required formidable mathematics, but van Vleck never lost sight of the physics. He once said he tried to understand "what was going on, not merely to get an answer." What was going on was that magnetism emerged from the quantum dance of electrons, and the details of atomic structure determined whether that dance produced a magnet or not.

## Mott's Metal-Insulator Transition

Nevill Mott attacked a different puzzle: why do some materials conduct electricity while others don't? The simple answer — metals have free electrons, insulators don't — masked deeper mysteries. Some materials that should be metals according to simple theory turned out to be insulators. Others switched between conducting and insulating depending on temperature or pressure.

In the 1930s and 1940s, Mott studied transition metal oxides that refused to conduct despite having partially filled electron shells. Classical physics said they should be metals. Quantum mechanics said they should be metals. Reality said otherwise. Mott realized that electron-electron repulsion could lock electrons in place when atoms were far enough apart. If bringing two electrons together cost too much energy, they'd stay put rather than hop between atoms to conduct current.

This became known as the Mott transition. It explained why materials like nickel oxide were insulators and why heating them could sometimes trigger a sudden switch to metallic behavior. The transition wasn't gradual — it was a phase change as sharp as water freezing. Mott's work connected to everything from superconductors to the emerging field of electronics. Understanding when electrons would flow and when they'd freeze was central to making devices.

## Anderson Localization

Philip Anderson's contribution was the most paradoxical. In 1958, he published a paper showing that electrons in a disordered material could become completely localized — unable to move at all — even without any obvious trap. The mechanism was pure quantum interference.

Imagine an electron trying to move through a material with randomly placed impurities. Classical physics says it would bounce around but eventually get somewhere, like a drunk wandering home. Quantum mechanics says the electron is described by a wave that can take many paths simultaneously. In a perfectly ordered crystal, the waves traveling different paths interfere constructively, and the electron propagates. But add enough disorder, and the waves interfere destructively more often than constructively. At a critical level of disorder, the destructive interference wins everywhere. The electron's wave function decays exponentially away from a single location. The electron is localized — not because it's trapped, but because every path away cancels itself out.

Anderson localization, as it became known, was counterintuitive enough that many physicists initially dismissed it. It took years of work by others to confirm the effect experimentally and to understand its full implications. By the 1970s, it was recognized as fundamental to understanding everything from metal-insulator transitions to quantum transport in disordered materials.

Anderson himself was characteristically blunt about the initial reception: "It was ignored for a long time because people didn't believe it." He had a gift for finding simple models that captured deep physics, and a temperament that didn't require immediate approval. The localization paper was pure theory — no experiments, no phenomenology, just a careful analysis of what quantum mechanics implied about disorder.

## Convergence

The committee linked these three bodies of work because they addressed the same fundamental question from different angles: what happens to electrons in materials that aren't perfect? Van Vleck showed how quantum mechanics explained magnetic ordering. Mott explained when and why electron interactions stopped conduction. Anderson showed how disorder alone could localize electrons.

Together, they created the theoretical framework for understanding materials as they actually exist — messy, disordered, magnetic, complicated. The perfect crystal of textbook solid state physics was revealed as a special case, and the real world became theoretically tractable.

The impact was immediate and continues. Anderson localization shows up in ultracold atoms, photonic systems, and quantum computing. Mott insulators are central to high-temperature superconductivity research. Van Vleck's magnetic theory underpins every hard drive and magnetic sensor. The 1977 prize honored work that made modern electronics possible — not by improving materials, but by explaining them.

Anderson later won a second major recognition for entirely different work in superconductivity and symmetry breaking. Mott continued researching metal-insulator transitions into his eighties. Van Vleck, asked late in life about his greatest contribution, pointed to his students and colleagues rather than his papers. The tradition of theoretical physics that attacks real materials with fundamental principles — physics that explains what you can hold in your hand — traces back through these three.

Disorder, it turned out, wasn't a complication. It was the key.
