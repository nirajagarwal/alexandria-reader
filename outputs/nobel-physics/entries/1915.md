# 1915 — X-Ray Analysis of Crystal Structure

In the summer of 1912, a young physicist named Lawrence Bragg sat in his father's Cambridge laboratory, staring at photographic plates marked with strange, symmetrical spots. X-rays had passed through a copper sulfate crystal and emerged bearing a pattern — not random scatter, but an ordered geometry that seemed to encode the crystal's internal architecture. Bragg was twenty-two years old. Within weeks, he would derive the equation that unlocked those patterns. Within three years, he would share a Nobel Prize with his father. He remains, to this day, the youngest person ever awarded the physics prize.

The story begins not with the Braggs but with Max von Laue, working in Munich. In 1912, von Laue conceived an audacious experiment: if crystals were truly regular arrangements of atoms, and if X-rays were waves with wavelengths comparable to atomic spacing, then passing X-rays through a crystal should produce diffraction — the same interference pattern that light makes passing through a grating. His colleagues were skeptical. X-rays were poorly understood; their wave nature was contested. But when physicist Walter Friedrich and Paul Knipping performed the experiment, the photographic plate revealed exactly what von Laue had imagined: a pattern of spots, symmetric and precise, proof that both crystals had atomic order and X-rays were waves.

The discovery electrified physics. But von Laue's mathematical analysis of the patterns was complex, rooted in the full three-dimensional geometry of wave interference. It was elegant but difficult to apply. The spots on the photographic plate held information about the crystal's structure, but extracting that information required navigating a thicket of equations.

Enter William Henry Bragg and his son William Lawrence Bragg — known to distinguish them as W.H. and W.L. The elder Bragg, fifty years old in 1912, was a professor at Leeds, respected but not renowned. He had come to X-rays late, initially skeptical of their wave nature, favoring instead a particle interpretation. His son, fresh from Cambridge with a mathematics degree, was supposed to be starting graduate work. The von Laue photographs changed both their paths.

Lawrence Bragg saw something von Laue's formalism had obscured. He imagined the crystal not as a three-dimensional lattice diffracting waves in all directions, but as a stack of parallel planes of atoms, each plane acting like a mirror reflecting X-rays. When X-rays struck these atomic planes at a particular angle, they would reflect. But the reflected waves from successive planes would travel slightly different distances. If that difference was exactly one wavelength — or two wavelengths, or any whole number — the waves would reinforce. If not, they would cancel.

The mathematics was suddenly simple. The condition for reinforcement became an equation so straightforward it could be written on a single line:

*nλ = 2d sin θ*

Wavelength times an integer equals twice the spacing between atomic planes, times the sine of the angle. This was Bragg's Law, and it transformed X-ray crystallography from an exotic phenomenon into a practical tool. Given the wavelength and the angle where you saw a bright spot, you could calculate the spacing between atomic planes. Given enough measurements from different orientations, you could map the entire structure.

Father and son worked in parallel, then together. W.H. Bragg built an X-ray spectrometer, replacing photographic plates with an ionization chamber that could measure X-ray intensity precisely as the crystal rotated. W.L. Bragg developed the theory and began applying it. By 1913, still just twenty-three, Lawrence had determined the structures of several simple crystals: sodium chloride, potassium chloride, potassium bromide. He showed that in common salt, there was no "molecule" of NaCl — just a lattice where sodium and chlorine atoms alternated in three dimensions, each sodium surrounded by six chlorines, each chlorine by six sodiums. The chemical bond, it turned out, could be a property of an extended structure, not just paired atoms.

It was revolutionary. For the first time, science could see atomic arrangements directly. Not infer them from bulk properties, not model them from theory, but measure them. The interior of matter became visible.

The Nobel Committee moved with unusual speed. In 1914, they awarded the prize to Max von Laue for the discovery of X-ray diffraction by crystals. A year later, in 1915, they gave the prize to W.H. and W.L. Bragg "for their services in the analysis of crystal structure by means of X-rays." Physics had recognized that discovery and method were distinct contributions, both essential.

The choice of a joint award to father and son was deliberate but created quiet tensions. The fundamental insight — Bragg's Law — was Lawrence's. The experimental apparatus was William's. The applications were collaborative, but Lawrence was the driving theoretical force. Some felt the prize should have been Lawrence's alone. Others noted that without William's instruments, the theory would have remained elegant but unproven. The Nobel Committee split the difference, and the Braggs accepted together.

Lawrence was twenty-five years old when he traveled to Stockholm. He would live another fifty-six years, becoming one of the twentieth century's most influential physicists — not for additional breakthroughs of his own, but for building the field of X-ray crystallography into a discipline that would reshape chemistry, biology, and materials science. The double helix structure of DNA, determined by Rosalind Franklin and refined by Watson and Crick, was found using X-ray crystallography. So was the structure of proteins, viruses, and eventually the molecular machinery of life itself. The youngest Nobel laureate became the elder statesman of a method that revealed the atomic architecture of the world.

His father lived to see some of this flowering. W.H. Bragg moved to University College London, then the Royal Institution, where he continued experimental work and became a gifted popularizer of science. He died in 1942, as X-ray crystallography was beginning its conquest of biology. Lawrence would live to see that conquest complete, to see his method determine the structure of hemoglobin, ribosomes, and the molecular mechanisms of heredity.

The 1915 prize marked a shift. Before Bragg's Law, matter was studied through its effects — density, conductivity, chemical reactivity. After, it could be studied through its geometry. Structure became a fundamental category of explanation. Why does diamond conduct heat but not electricity while graphite does the opposite? Structure. Why does one protein catalyze reactions while another stores oxygen? Structure. The question "what is it made of?" evolved into "how is it arranged?"

In his Nobel lecture, young Lawrence Bragg spoke with precision and clarity about planes of atoms and angles of reflection, but he closed with something else — a note of wonder. The spots on a photographic plate were not just data. They were shadows cast by the architecture of matter itself, a geometry too small to see but precise enough to measure. For the first time, science had learned to read that geometry. The invisible had become legible.

Three decades later, when Bragg's method revealed the double helix, Francis Crick would describe the X-ray diffraction pattern as "the most beautiful X-ray photograph of any substance ever taken." It was beautiful because it was information — because somewhere in that pattern of spots was the structure of heredity itself, waiting to be decoded. That beauty, that legibility, began with a twenty-two-year-old staring at spots on a photographic plate and seeing not mystery but mirror planes, not chaos but structure. The Nobel Prize of 1915 recognized the moment when matter learned to tell us how it was built.
