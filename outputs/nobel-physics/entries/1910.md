# 1910 — Equations of State for Gases and Liquids

The equation looked simple enough: *pV = RT*. For a century, physicists had used it to describe gases — pressure times volume equals a constant times temperature. It worked beautifully for air at room temperature, for steam in engines, for any gas behaving "ideally." The problem was that no real gas actually behaved ideally. Cool them down, squeeze them hard enough, and they all started breaking the rules. Some liquefied. Some became impossible to compress further. The simple equation that powered the Industrial Revolution was, strictly speaking, a lie. Johannes Diderik van der Waals had spent his career making it tell the truth.

Van der Waals was forty when his doctoral thesis arrived in 1873, already a schoolteacher with a family, cramming physics between lessons. The work he submitted to the University of Leiden was not incremental refinement. It was a wholesale reimagining of molecular reality, and it would earn him the 1910 Nobel Prize in Physics for his work on the equation of state for gases and liquids — the mathematical relationship that describes how substances behave under different conditions of pressure, volume, and temperature.

The ideal gas law assumed molecules were mathematical points with no size, no attraction, no reality beyond their motion. Van der Waals knew better. Real molecules take up space. They also pull on each other — not strongly enough to lock them in place like a solid, but enough to matter when they get close. He added two correction terms to the ideal equation. One accounted for the volume the molecules themselves occupied, reducing the free space available. The other captured the attractive forces between molecules, which effectively reduced the pressure they exerted on container walls.

The result was the van der Waals equation: $(p + \frac{a}{V^2})(V - b) = RT$, where *a* measured molecular attraction and *b* measured molecular size. Those two constants, different for every substance, transformed an idealization into a description of physical reality. The equation was still approximate, but it worked across an astonishing range — from high-temperature gases to the threshold of liquefaction.

What made the work revolutionary wasn't just improved accuracy. Van der Waals's equation predicted something no one had expected: it described the transition from gas to liquid as a continuous phenomenon. At high temperatures, a gas remained a gas no matter how much you compressed it. But below a certain critical temperature, compression would cause the gas to condense into liquid. The equation revealed that gas and liquid weren't fundamentally different states — they were regions in a continuum of molecular behavior.

He could calculate that critical temperature, that critical pressure, that critical volume where the distinction between gas and liquid dissolved. For carbon dioxide, he predicted a critical temperature of 31°C. When experimentalists tested it, they found 30.98°C. The precision was startling. Van der Waals had taken a theoretical model of invisible molecules and extracted numbers that matched the real world with almost eerie accuracy.

The work gave physicists something profound: a bridge between the microscopic and the macroscopic. From assumptions about molecular forces and sizes — quantities far too small to measure directly in the 1870s — van der Waals derived the behavior of matter at scales humans could observe and manipulate. It was one of the early triumphs of what would become statistical mechanics, showing that bulk properties emerged inevitably from molecular reality.

James Clerk Maxwell saw the thesis and praised it immediately. "There can be no doubt that the name of Van der Waals will soon be among the foremost in molecular science," he wrote. Maxwell died in 1879, but his prediction proved accurate. Van der Waals became professor at Amsterdam, where he spent decades refining his theory and watching it spread through physics and chemistry.

The equation became essential for industrial chemistry. Engineers designing refrigeration systems needed to know when gases would liquefy. Chemical plants needed to predict behavior at high pressures. The van der Waals equation gave them a tool that worked. It wasn't perfect — real substances at extreme conditions still defied its predictions — but it was vastly better than the ideal gas law, and computationally far simpler than trying to track every molecule.

The Nobel Committee recognized something deeper than engineering utility. Van der Waals had demonstrated that a relatively simple mathematical model, grounded in physical reality, could capture the essence of complex phenomena. His molecules had size and attraction — just two properties beyond motion — yet from those emerged the entire landscape of phase transitions, critical points, and the continuity between different states of matter.

The work opened doors. By 1910, physics had moved far beyond van der Waals's original equation. Einstein had published his papers on relativity. Planck had introduced the quantum. Atomic physics was being born. But van der Waals's insight remained foundational: that macroscopic behavior emerged from microscopic properties, that theoretical physics could make quantitative predictions about the real world, that an equation didn't need to be exact to be profoundly true.

Other equations of state followed — more accurate, more complex, better suited to specific conditions. The Redlich-Kwong equation, the Peng-Robinson equation, elaborate polynomials with dozens of terms. All descendants of van der Waals's insight. All trying to do what he had done first: describe the reality of matter in the space between the ideal and the impossibly complicated.

Van der Waals was seventy-three when he received the Nobel Prize, long since established as one of physics's elder statesmen. He had watched his equation become part of the standard toolkit, taught to every chemistry student, used in industries he'd never imagined. The prize citation noted his work had "greatly widened the sphere of research in this field" — understated praise for having shown that molecules, though invisible, obeyed laws that could be written down, tested, and used.

He died in 1923, having lived long enough to see quantum mechanics begin to explain *why* molecules attracted each other, *why* they had the sizes they did. The quantum world would reveal the van der Waals forces as arising from fleeting electrical fluctuations, but it only confirmed what he had assumed: molecules were real, they interacted, and their interactions determined everything we could see and measure. His equation was an approximation, but it approximated something true — which was, in the end, what physics was for.
