# 1998 — The Fractional Quantum Hall Effect

Robert Laughlin sits in his office at Bell Labs in 1981, staring at an impossible experimental result. At temperatures near absolute zero and under magnetic fields a million times stronger than Earth's, electrons in a two-dimensional sheet are doing something that shouldn't be possible: they're behaving as if each one carries a fraction of an electron's charge. One-third of an electron. The mathematics of quantum mechanics says this cannot be. An electron is fundamental, indivisible. Yet the data is unambiguous.

The fractional quantum hall effect would earn the 1998 Nobel Prize in Physics for Laughlin, along with experimentalists Horst Störmer and Daniel Tsui, who first observed it. But between the observation and the prize lay one of the most elegant theoretical explanations in condensed matter physics — and a profound revelation about emergence, the idea that collective behavior can create phenomena utterly unlike the components that produce it.

The story begins in 1980, when Störmer and Tsui were hunting for interesting physics in gallium arsenide heterostructures at Bell Labs. These semiconductor sandwiches trap electrons in a layer just a few atoms thick — essentially a two-dimensional world. Cool them to a fraction of a degree above absolute zero and apply a powerful magnetic field perpendicular to the plane, and strange things happen.

Strange, but by 1980, partly understood. The integer quantum Hall effect, discovered by Klaus von Klitzing (Nobel Prize, 1985), had shown that electrical resistance in such systems becomes quantized — it jumps in discrete steps as you vary the magnetic field. The explanation involved quantum mechanics: electrons in a magnetic field can only occupy certain energy levels, called Landau levels, like rungs on a ladder. As you fill these levels with electrons, the Hall resistance — measured perpendicular to the current flow — takes on values that are exact multiples of a fundamental constant: the ratio of Planck's constant to the square of the electron charge.

Störmer and Tsui pushed further. Working at the National Magnet Laboratory in Florida, they accessed stronger magnetic fields and cleaner samples than von Klitzing had. On February 11, 1982, they saw something new: additional plateaus in the Hall resistance, but at fractional values. The clearest appeared at exactly one-third the value of the first integer plateau.

The observation was clean, reproducible, astonishing. And utterly unexplained.

Electrons don't break into pieces. Quarks carry fractional charge, but electrons are fundamental. The Landau level picture that explained the integer effect couldn't account for fractions. If individual electron behavior produced the integer effect, what collective phenomenon could produce something that looked like fractional charges?

Laughlin, a theoretical physicist also at Bell Labs, heard about the result in a seminar. He was 32, trained in particle theory but increasingly fascinated by condensed matter physics — the study of materials and their emergent properties. The fractional quantum Hall effect struck him as deeply weird, the kind of weird that signals something fundamental.

He spent months thinking about it. The key insight came from considering not individual electrons, but the quantum mechanical wave function of all the electrons together. In normal circumstances, electrons repel each other and this interaction makes the mathematics nightmarishly complex. But in the extreme conditions of the experiment — strong magnetic field, low temperature, two dimensions — the electrons are forced into a collective state.

Laughlin realized he could write down a wave function describing this collective state. It was mathematically elegant, a product of terms that ensured electrons stayed apart (respecting their mutual repulsion) while occupying partially filled Landau levels. When he calculated the properties of this state, something remarkable emerged: the system behaved as if it contained quasiparticles — excitations above the ground state — that carried exactly one-third of an electron's charge.

These weren't real fractional electrons. They were collective excitations, like ripples in a pond that behave as if they're objects in their own right. The Laughlin wave function described an incompressible quantum liquid, a new phase of matter where the interactions between electrons created emergent particles obeying emergent rules.

The paper, published in Physical Review Letters in 1983, was revelatory. Laughlin showed that at certain special filling fractions — when the number of electrons divided by the number of available quantum states equals 1/3, 1/5, 1/7, and so on — the system forms particularly stable states. These fractions correspond exactly to the plateaus Störmer and Tsui had observed.

"The electron is not alone," Laughlin later wrote. "It lives in a marriage with other electrons, and the marriage is so complete that the idea of an individual electron is no longer meaningful."

The fractional quantum Hall effect became a cornerstone of modern condensed matter physics. It demonstrated that interactions between particles could create entirely new physics, not predictable from the properties of individual particles. It was emergence made quantitative and precise.

The discovery also opened conceptual doors. In 1988, Laughlin's graduate student Frank Wilczek (who would win his own Nobel Prize in 2004) coined the term "anyon" for quasiparticles in two dimensions that could have quantum statistics different from the fermions and bosons found in three-dimensional space. Some fractional quantum Hall states contain anyons. This realization connected condensed matter physics to ideas in quantum field theory and would eventually inspire proposals for topological quantum computing, where information is stored in the collective state of anyons, protected from local errors.

The 1998 Nobel Prize recognized both the experimental discovery and the theoretical explanation. Störmer and Tsui shared half the prize for the observation; Laughlin received the other half for the theory. It was physics at its best: a startling experimental finding and an elegant explanation that revealed something profound about nature.

Störmer and Tsui's work exemplified experimental art — the ability to push instruments and materials to extremes where new phenomena emerge from the noise. They created conditions that didn't exist anywhere else in the universe and looked carefully enough to see what those conditions revealed. Their semiconductor samples were so pure that electrons could travel vast distances (by atomic standards) without scattering, allowing the delicate quantum correlations to form.

Laughlin's contribution was different but equally essential. He provided not just an explanation but a new way of thinking about collective phenomena. His wave function couldn't be derived from first principles in any simple way; it was an inspired guess, a piece of mathematical intuition about how nature might behave. That it worked so precisely, predicting exactly the fractions observed experimentally, validated both the guess and the intuition.

By 1998, the fractional quantum Hall effect had spawned an entire subfield. Physicists had discovered dozens of different fractional states, each with its own quasiparticles and properties. Some couldn't be explained by Laughlin's original wave function and required further theoretical development. The field became a laboratory for studying emergence, topology, and exotic phases of matter.

The prize also illustrated a subtle point about physics honors. Von Klitzing had won for the integer quantum Hall effect in 1985, just five years after discovery. Störmer, Tsui, and Laughlin waited sixteen years from observation, fifteen from explanation. The delay wasn't skepticism about the result — the fractional effect was well-confirmed and theoretically solid by the late 1980s. Rather, the Nobel committee seemed to wait until the full significance became clear, until the discovery grew into a field.

Today, the fractional quantum Hall effect remains a frontier. Proposals for topological quantum computers rely on creating and manipulating anyons in certain fractional Hall states. Experimental groups worldwide hunt for the most exotic states, including the so-called 5/2 state, whose quasiparticles might be non-abelian anyons — objects whose quantum states depend on the order in which you exchange them, a property that could revolutionize quantum computing.

The discovery also carries a philosophical lesson. Three men shared the prize, but the fractional quantum Hall effect emerged from the work of dozens: the engineers who built the magnets, the materials scientists who grew the samples, the theorists who developed the mathematical tools, the experimentalists who refined measurement techniques. Physics is portrayed as a solo endeavor — Einstein in the patent office, Newton under the apple tree — but modern physics is collaborative, built by communities.

Still, there are moments when one person sees what others miss, when one wave function captures what pages of calculation cannot. Laughlin had that moment in 1981, staring at impossible data and realizing that impossibility was emergence — that electrons together could create something no electron alone could be.

"It is wrong to think of emergent things as not real," he said later, defending the idea that quasiparticles deserve to be called particles. "The waves in the ocean are not real in the sense that water is real, but they are real enough to surf on."

The fractional quantum Hall effect taught physics that reality has layers. At the bottom are electrons, fundamental and indivisible. But in the right conditions, those electrons form collectives with their own reality, their own particles, their own rules. The world is richer than its building blocks. Sometimes, one-third of an electron is exactly as real as it needs to be.
