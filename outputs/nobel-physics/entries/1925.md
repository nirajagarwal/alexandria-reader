# 1925 — The Franck-Hertz Experiment

On a spring day in 1914, James Franck and Gustav Hertz watched mercury vapor glow with a faint ultraviolet light inside a glass tube. They had been accelerating electrons through the vapor, measuring how much energy the electrons lost as they collided with mercury atoms. The results made no sense according to classical physics — but perfect sense if Niels Bohr's radical new atomic theory was correct. They had accidentally proven that atoms exist in discrete energy states, that electrons can only absorb specific amounts of energy, that the quantum world was real. They just didn't know it yet.

Franck and Hertz hadn't set out to prove anything about quantum mechanics. In 1914, when they performed their experiment at the University of Berlin, they were investigating ionization — trying to understand how much energy it took to knock electrons out of atoms. Bohr had published his quantum model of the atom the year before, proposing that electrons orbit the nucleus only at certain allowed distances, like planets that can only occupy specific orbits. Between experiments, energy exists in discrete chunks — quanta. Most physicists regarded this as mathematical convenience at best, physical absurdity at worst.

The apparatus was elegant in its simplicity. A heated filament emitted electrons. An electric field accelerated them through a tube filled with low-pressure mercury vapor. A collecting plate at the far end measured how many electrons made it through. By varying the accelerating voltage and measuring the current that reached the collector, they could map exactly what happened as electrons gained energy.

The results were striking. As they increased the voltage, the current rose smoothly — until it reached 4.9 volts. Then it dropped sharply. Increase the voltage further, and the current rose again, only to drop at 9.8 volts. Then 14.7 volts. A regular pattern of peaks and valleys, each separated by exactly 4.9 electron volts.

Franck and Hertz interpreted this as ionization. They thought electrons were knocking other electrons out of mercury atoms, losing 4.9 eV in the process. But something else was happening in their tube: at each voltage drop, the mercury vapor glowed with ultraviolet light at a wavelength of 254 nanometers. They noted this but didn't connect it to their main result.

Others made the connection. The energy of a photon at 254 nm is 4.9 electron volts — exactly the energy difference in Bohr's model between mercury's ground state and its first excited state. The electrons weren't ionizing atoms; they were exciting them. An electron would accelerate through the vapor until it had exactly 4.9 eV of kinetic energy. At that moment, it could transfer all that energy to a mercury atom in a single quantum jump, boosting one of the atom's electrons to a higher orbit. The electron that caused this would lose its energy and slow down, no longer reaching the collecting plate — hence the current drop. The excited mercury atom would then drop back to its ground state, emitting that energy as a 254 nm photon — the ultraviolet glow they'd observed.

It was direct, unambiguous evidence that atoms absorb energy in discrete amounts, that Bohr's quantized orbits were physically real, not mathematical fiction. Electrons couldn't gradually transfer energy to atoms; they either had enough to cause a quantum jump or they didn't.

By the time the Nobel Committee recognized this work in 1925, both men understood what they'd proven. But the path to that understanding had been complicated by war and isolation. When World War I began months after their experiment, normal scientific communication collapsed. Franck served in the German army, conducting research on gas warfare — work he would later call the greatest mistake of his life. Both men were cut off from developments in quantum theory happening elsewhere.

The human story of these two physicists reflects the convulsions of early twentieth-century Europe. Franck, Jewish and politically conscious, would leave Germany in 1933 the day after Hitler came to power, forgoing his university position on principle before being forced out. He eventually settled at the University of Chicago, where during World War II he would lead the committee of scientists urging the U.S. government not to use the atomic bomb on Japanese cities without warning — the "Franck Report," ignored by decision-makers.

Hertz, nephew of the Heinrich Hertz who discovered radio waves, navigated different currents. Not Jewish, he remained in Germany through the Nazi years, managing the ethical compromises that entailed. The two men's friendship survived war and exile, but their paths diverged in ways that captured the century's moral fault lines.

Their experimental technique became a standard demonstration in physics courses — a rare example of a Nobel-winning experiment simple enough to recreate in an undergraduate lab. The peaks and valleys in the current curve are sometimes called "Franck-Hertz resonances," and generations of students have watched mercury vapor glow and understood viscerally what took years to comprehend in 1914: energy in atoms comes in packages, not continuums.

The 1925 Nobel Prize came at a pivotal moment. Quantum mechanics was about to explode into full mathematical form. Within two years, Heisenberg would develop matrix mechanics, Schrödinger would write his wave equation, Born would give quantum mechanics its probabilistic interpretation. The Franck-Hertz experiment had provided crucial evidence that this strange new physics was necessary. Classical physics couldn't explain those voltage drops, couldn't explain that ultraviolet glow. Atoms really did jump between discrete states.

But perhaps the most remarkable thing about the Franck-Hertz experiment is what it reveals about scientific discovery itself. They had proven Bohr's model while thinking they were studying something else entirely. They had the key piece of evidence in their hands and didn't recognize it. The ultraviolet glow — the smoking gun — was a footnote in their paper. Science advances not just through brilliant insight but through careful measurement, through anomalies noted even when their significance isn't understood, through other scientists seeing what the original researchers missed. The truth was in the data; it just took time for everyone, including Franck and Hertz themselves, to see it.
