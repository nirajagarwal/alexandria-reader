# 1973 — Tunneling in Semiconductors and Superconductors

In 1957, Leo Esaki was making diodes worse on purpose. At Sony's new semiconductor laboratory in Tokyo, the young physicist was deliberately introducing massive amounts of impurities into germanium crystals—concentrations a thousand times higher than anyone thought useful. The conventional wisdom said this would ruin the material. Esaki wanted to see what happened when you pushed past conventional limits. What he discovered, almost by accident in a batch of failed experiments, was a diode that broke the fundamental rules of how electricity should flow through matter.

The 1973 Nobel Prize recognized three physicists who revealed that quantum mechanics doesn't stop at the atomic scale—it shapes how electricity moves through the materials of modern electronics. Leo Esaki found a way to make electrons tunnel through barriers they shouldn't be able to cross. Ivar Giaever proved that the same quantum tunneling could reveal the fundamental nature of superconductivity. Brian Josephson, while still a graduate student, predicted an effect so strange that experienced physicists initially dismissed it as impossible. Together, they opened a world where particles slip through walls and supercurrents flow without voltage.

## The Impossible Diode

A diode is supposed to be simple: current flows one way easily, the other way barely at all. Plot current against voltage and you get a smooth curve rising in one direction. But Esaki's heavily-doped diode did something bizarre. As he increased the voltage, the current rose as expected—then suddenly dropped, creating a valley in the curve, before rising again. There was a region where adding more voltage gave you less current.

This made no sense in classical physics. Esaki realized he was seeing quantum tunneling on a macroscopic scale. In his ultra-heavily-doped semiconductor, the impurity atoms were packed so densely that the barrier between conducting and non-conducting regions became incredibly thin—thin enough that electrons could quantum-mechanically tunnel through it even when they didn't have enough energy to go over it.

The effect was pure quantum mechanics: electrons behaving as probability waves that could leak through barriers forbidden to classical particles. But this wasn't happening to individual atoms in isolation. This was measurable current in a real device, flowing because of quantum effects that persisted even in the messy, hot, impure world of practical semiconductors.

Esaki published his results in 1958. The "Esaki diode" or tunnel diode could switch between high and low current states faster than any conventional device. It found immediate use in high-frequency circuits and microwave applications. Sony built Japan's first transistor radio around Esaki's work. But the deeper significance was conceptual: quantum tunneling wasn't just a curiosity of atomic physics—it was an engineering principle.

## Tunneling Into Superconductors

Ivar Giaever, a Norwegian immigrant working at General Electric, read Esaki's paper and wondered: what if you could use tunneling to probe inside superconductors?

Superconductors—materials that conduct electricity with zero resistance below a critical temperature—had been discovered in 1911, but their fundamental nature remained mysterious. In 1957, Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer (who would share the 1972 Nobel Prize) had proposed that superconductivity arose because electrons pair up in a special quantum state, separated from normal electron states by an energy gap.

Giaever saw that tunneling could measure this gap directly. If you sandwich a thin insulating barrier between a normal metal and a superconductor, electrons can tunnel through the barrier. But they can only tunnel into available energy states. By measuring how the tunneling current varies with voltage, you could map out the energy states in the superconductor—including the gap.

It worked. In 1960, using aluminum strips separated by their own oxide layer, Giaever observed exactly the tunneling signature that BCS theory predicted. The superconducting energy gap was real and measurable. His experiments provided crucial confirmation of the BCS theory and created a new spectroscopic tool—tunneling spectroscopy—that could probe the quantum states of matter with unprecedented precision.

Giaever hadn't been trained as a superconductivity expert. He had a bachelor's degree in mechanical engineering from Norway and had been working on thin-film metallurgy at GE. But he understood Esaki's work, had a good idea, and had the experimental skill to make it happen. Sometimes outsiders see connections that specialists miss.

## The Graduate Student Who Saw Further

In 1962, Brian Josephson was a twenty-two-year-old graduate student at Cambridge, attending a seminar about Giaever's tunneling experiments. During the talk, Josephson had an unsettling thought: Giaever's analysis assumed that the superconductors on each side of the barrier were independent. But what if they weren't? What if the quantum wave functions of the paired electrons in each superconductor could extend through the barrier and talk to each other?

He worked through the mathematics. If the wave functions overlapped through the barrier, two extraordinary effects should occur. First, a supercurrent should flow between the superconductors even with zero voltage across the junction—a "DC Josephson effect." Second, if you applied a constant voltage, the supercurrent should oscillate at a frequency directly proportional to the voltage—an "AC Josephson effect" where the frequency would be in the hundreds of gigahertz for voltages of just millivolts.

Josephson wrote up his prediction and showed it to his supervisor, Brian Pippard. Pippard was skeptical—this seemed too strange—but he let Josephson submit it for publication. The paper appeared in 1962, when Josephson was still two years from his PhD.

Experimentalists were skeptical too. A DC supercurrent through a barrier with no voltage? That violated intuition about how tunneling worked. Anderson and Rowell at Bell Labs, among the best experimentalists in superconductivity, initially looked for the effect in their data and concluded it wasn't there. But they looked again, more carefully, and in 1963 found exactly what Josephson had predicted: pairs of electrons tunneling coherently through the barrier, maintaining their quantum-mechanical phase relationship, producing a supercurrent without resistance and without voltage.

The AC effect was even stranger. When they applied a small DC voltage across a Josephson junction and shone microwaves at it, they observed steps in the current-voltage curve at voltages where the microwave frequency matched the natural Josephson oscillation frequency. The junction was converting a DC voltage into an AC supercurrent oscillating at hundreds of billions of times per second.

## Precision and Paradox

The Josephson effects turned out to be exquisitely precise. The relationship between voltage and frequency—the Josephson constant—depends only on fundamental constants: the charge of the electron and Planck's constant. This made Josephson junctions the most accurate voltage standards in existence, eventually redefining how the volt itself was measured.

But there was something philosophically troubling about the DC Josephson effect. A supercurrent flows with no voltage drop. That means no energy dissipation, no loss. Yet the current carries energy across the junction. How can you have energy flow without energy dissipation? The answer lies in the quantum-mechanical phase of the superconducting wave function: the phase difference between the two superconductors encodes the energy, and current flows to maintain phase coherence. It's a purely quantum phenomenon with no classical analog.

The three laureates had each revealed a different facet of quantum tunneling in condensed matter. Esaki showed that tunneling could occur on device scales and be engineered for practical use. Giaever demonstrated that tunneling could probe quantum states that were otherwise hidden from measurement. Josephson predicted that tunneling could preserve quantum coherence across macroscopic distances, creating effects that seemed to violate classical causality.

## The Human Stories

Esaki's path to the prize was unconventional for a Japanese physicist of his generation. He'd joined Sony in 1956, when the company was still a small, scrappy radio manufacturer trying to compete with American electronics giants. His tunnel diode discovery came from a willingness to try things that conventional wisdom said wouldn't work—a trait that fit Sony's engineering culture but was rare in academic physics. He moved to IBM in 1960, where he spent the rest of his career exploring quantum effects in semiconductors.

Giaever's story was even more unlikely. He'd emigrated from Norway to Canada to work in the booming post-war engineering economy, then moved to GE. He took undergraduate night courses at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute while working full-time. His PhD dissertation, completed at age forty, was essentially his tunneling work—experiments he'd done on company time, motivated by curiosity about something he'd read in a journal. He later joked that he'd never have gotten the Nobel Prize if he'd followed a conventional academic path; he wouldn't have had the practical skill or the freedom to follow odd ideas.

Josephson was the opposite: a brilliant theorist working at one of physics's great institutions. But even Cambridge struggled with his result. When Josephson's prediction was confirmed experimentally, some senior physicists remained puzzled. How could a graduate student have seen something that experts had missed? The answer was partly that Josephson had a gift for mathematical physics and partly that he wasn't burdened by the accumulated intuitions that told experts what "should" be possible.

After his PhD, Josephson remained at Cambridge, working on superconductivity and later branching into more speculative areas, including parapsychology and consciousness studies. His later interests became controversial among physicists, but they didn't diminish the early work that bore his name.

## The Technologies That Emerged

Tunnel diodes found niche uses in microwave circuits and fast-switching applications, though they were eventually superseded by other semiconductor technologies. But the principle—that quantum tunneling could be a useful device mechanism rather than a nuisance—became fundamental to modern electronics.

Josephson junctions became the basis for SQUIDs (Superconducting Quantum Interference Devices), the most sensitive magnetic field detectors ever created. They can measure fields billions of times weaker than Earth's magnetic field, making them essential for brain imaging, geological surveys, and the detection of magnetic anomalies in materials.

The voltage standard based on the Josephson effect is now used by every national metrology laboratory. The AC Josephson effect converts a frequency (which can be measured with extraordinary precision using atomic clocks) into a voltage, tying voltage measurements to the fundamental constants of quantum mechanics.

More recently, Josephson junctions have become the basis for superconducting qubits—quantum bits for quantum computers. The junction's ability to maintain quantum coherence while being large enough to fabricate and manipulate makes it one of the leading platforms for building quantum processors. The strange oscillations that Josephson predicted in 1962 now encode quantum information in machines pursuing the next revolution in computing.

## The Elegance of the Barrier

There's something almost perverse about the way these effects work: you learn about matter not by removing the barriers but by carefully controlling them. Make a barrier thin enough, and quantum mechanics takes over. Make it thick enough, and you can tune the tunneling probability. Sandwich it between the right materials, and you can probe quantum states or generate effects that don't exist in classical physics.

The 1973 prize celebrated this counterintuitive insight. Barriers aren't just obstacles—they're instruments. Tunneling isn't a curiosity—it's a fundamental process that shapes how matter behaves at the quantum scale and how we can engineer that behavior for use in the macroscopic world.

When Josephson predicted his effect, he was describing something that seemed to violate causality: a current flowing with no driving voltage, or oscillating at a frequency determined by a DC voltage that shouldn't contain any time dependence. But quantum mechanics doesn't care about classical intuition. The superconducting wave function has a phase, that phase can wind up as current flows, and pairs of electrons can tunnel while maintaining phase coherence. The universe permits this because the mathematics says it must.

The tunnel through the barrier isn't a shortcut. It's the way quantum particles actually move when the barrier is thin enough and the wave function strong enough. These three physicists saw that this weird quantum process wasn't confined to atoms or abstract thought experiments. It was happening in semiconductors and superconductors, in devices you could hold in your hand, creating effects you could measure with ordinary instruments. They took the strangeness of quantum mechanics and showed it was present in the everyday materials of electrical engineering—not as a correction or perturbation, but as the dominant physics.
