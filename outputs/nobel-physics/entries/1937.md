# 1937 — Electron Diffraction

Clinton Davisson was methodical to the point of obsession. For years at Bell Laboratories, he'd been firing electron beams at nickel targets, measuring scattering angles, cataloging results that made sense within classical physics. Then, in April 1925, a glass tube exploded. Air rushed in, oxidizing his nickel crystal. To clean it, Davisson heated the sample for hours. When he rebuilt the apparatus and resumed firing electrons, the numbers were wrong. Completely, inexplicably wrong. The scattered electrons clustered at specific angles, like light diffracting through a grating. Particles weren't supposed to do that.

In Aberdeen, Scotland, George Paget Thomson — son of J.J. Thomson, who had proven the electron was a particle — was passing electron beams through thin metal films. He saw rings. Diffraction rings. The kind that waves make.

The 1937 Nobel Prize honored both men for demonstrating experimentally what Louis de Broglie had proposed in 1924: matter has wave properties. Electrons could diffract, interfere, behave like light. The discovery confirmed the strangest prediction of the new quantum mechanics and opened a tool that would let scientists probe the atomic structure of materials with unprecedented precision. It also captured the central paradox of quantum physics — the electron is both particle and wave, and which you see depends on how you look.

**The Heresy**

De Broglie's 1924 doctoral thesis was audacious. If light could behave as particles (Einstein's photons), perhaps matter could behave as waves. He proposed that every particle had an associated wavelength, inversely proportional to its momentum: λ = h/p, where h was Planck's constant. The idea was so radical that his examiner, Paul Langevin, sent the thesis to Einstein for assessment. Einstein found it "quite interesting" — faint praise that legitimized publication.

But theory without experiment is speculation. The wavelength de Broglie predicted for electrons was tiny — on the order of atomic spacing in crystals. To see diffraction, you'd need a grating with atomic-scale features. Crystals were natural candidates. X-ray diffraction, discovered in 1912, had proven that crystals could act as three-dimensional gratings. If de Broglie was right, electrons should produce similar patterns.

**The Accident**

Davisson hadn't been looking for wave behavior. At Bell Labs, he was investigating electron scattering for practical reasons — understanding how electrons moved through materials. He worked with Lester Germer, firing slow-moving electrons at nickel targets and measuring the scattered intensity at various angles. The data fit classical expectations: electrons bouncing off like billiard balls.

Then came the accident. The liquid-air trap on their vacuum system failed, the glass tube burst, and their carefully prepared nickel crystal oxidized. Davisson and Germer heated the crystal to 600°C to remove the oxide layer, not realizing the heat was annealing the nickel — fusing its tiny crystalline grains into larger, more regular structures.

When they repeated their measurements, the pattern had changed. Scattered electrons peaked sharply at certain angles. Davisson, puzzled but systematic, kept taking data. In 1926, he traveled to Oxford for a meeting of the British Association for the Advancement of Science. There, he heard about de Broglie's hypothesis. Max Born explained the implications. Suddenly, Davisson's strange data made sense. The electrons were diffracting through the nickel crystal's atomic lattice. The peaks corresponded to constructive interference — wave behavior.

He rushed back to New York and, with Germer, redesigned the experiment to test the wave hypothesis explicitly. They rotated the crystal, varied electron energies, and measured diffraction angles. In 1927, they published results showing that electrons with specific energies diffracted at angles precisely matching de Broglie's equation. The wavelength they measured was λ = h/p, exactly as predicted.

Davisson later wrote: "The real credit... belongs to the glass-blowing industry for its admirable lack of reliability in the matter of liquid-air containers."

**The Deliberate Search**

Thomson took a different path. He knew the prediction — he'd been following quantum theory closely, aware that wave-particle duality needed experimental proof. His father had discovered the electron as a particle in 1897. Now he would show it was also a wave.

Thomson's method was more direct. He fired high-energy electrons through thin films of celluloid and metals — gold, aluminum, celluloid — and photographed what emerged on the other side. If electrons were waves, they should produce diffraction patterns like those seen with X-rays passing through polycrystalline materials: concentric rings corresponding to different atomic planes in the randomly oriented crystallites.

That's exactly what he saw. Beautiful, clear rings. He published in 1927, independently confirming electron diffraction. His images were striking — visual proof that matter could behave as waves. While Davisson and Germer had provided precise quantitative measurements from single crystals, Thomson's films offered immediate, undeniable evidence that even physicists outside the quantum vanguard could appreciate.

The Nobel committee noted the symmetry: "J.J. Thomson was rewarded for his work on the conduction of electricity through gases and for his discovery of the electron; G.P. Thomson has been rewarded for his discovery that the electron also exhibits the properties of a wave."

**The Tool**

The discovery was more than confirmation of quantum theory — it was the birth of a technique. Electron diffraction became a way to study matter at atomic scales. Because electrons interact more strongly with matter than X-rays, they revealed surface structures, thin films, molecular arrangements. Crystallographers used it to map atomic positions. Materials scientists probed defects and interfaces. The method was essential to understanding everything from alloys to biological membranes.

Low-energy electron diffraction (LEED), descended directly from Davisson and Germer's work, became standard for studying crystal surfaces. Transmission electron diffraction, following Thomson's approach, let researchers identify crystalline phases in materials too small or complex for X-ray analysis. By the 1930s, electron diffraction was routine in industrial labs and universities.

And there was a deeper impact. The wave-particle duality of matter, once philosophical speculation, was now experimental fact. It forced physicists to accept that particles didn't have fixed trajectories, that observation shaped reality, that the classical picture of nature was wrong at fundamental scales. Davisson and Thomson's electrons weren't traveling along definite paths and then hitting the crystal. They were probability waves, interfering with themselves, manifesting as particles only when detected.

**The Irony**

The Nobel Prize highlighted an uncomfortable truth about discovery: credit often depends on timing, interpretation, and institutional position. Davisson and Germer published first, but their 1927 paper took months to recognize wave behavior in their earlier data. Thomson published independently but later, with clearer intent to confirm de Broglie. Both deserved recognition — but so did others.

Lester Germer, Davisson's collaborator for the entire experimental process, was left off the prize. The Nobel committee rarely honored more than three people per prize, and in 1937, experimental collaborators were often treated as junior partners, not co-discoverers. Germer continued working at Bell Labs, publishing significant work on electron diffraction, but never received the recognition Davisson did.

There was also the matter of priority. Japanese physicist Kikuchi Seishi had observed electron diffraction patterns in 1928, though his work focused on different aspects of the phenomenon. Soviet physicist Pyotr Tartakovsky published electron diffraction studies in 1927, working independently. Neither was considered by the Nobel committee, which focused on Davisson and Thomson's definitive demonstrations.

And de Broglie, who predicted the effect, had already won the 1929 Nobel Prize in Physics for his wave mechanics hypothesis. His was a triumph of theory; Davisson and Thomson provided the experimental proof that made it undeniable.

**The Legacy**

Davisson spent the rest of his career at Bell Labs, refining electron diffraction techniques and studying crystal surfaces. He was shy, uncomfortable with fame, and deeply skeptical of quantum mechanics' philosophical implications even as he accepted its mathematical accuracy. Thomson returned to Cambridge, eventually becoming Master of Corpus Christi College. During World War II, he worked on aerodynamics and nuclear physics, contributing to Britain's understanding of fission.

The electron microscope, developed in the 1930s by Ernst Ruska and Max Knoll, depended on electron wave properties. So did the scanning tunneling microscope, invented in the 1980s, which let scientists image individual atoms. Every device that exploits quantum tunneling, every semiconductor that relies on electron behavior in crystalline lattices, descends from the insight that particles are also waves.

Davisson once remarked: "The observations were made... without any suspicion that they might be related to the problem of the nature of the electron." Science often works that way — the breakthrough comes from looking carefully at something unexpected, following the data even when it contradicts everything you thought you knew. A broken glass tube. Electrons scattering wrong. And the world, once again, revealed to be stranger than anyone imagined.
