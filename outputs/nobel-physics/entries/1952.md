# 1952 — Nuclear Magnetic Resonance in Solids

Felix Bloch was measuring nothing. Or trying to. In 1946, at Stanford, he'd set out to detect nuclear magnetic resonance — a phenomenon predicted but never observed, the faint radio whisper of atomic nuclei responding to magnetic fields. His apparatus was sensitive enough to pick up almost anything. The problem was proving it wasn't picking up noise. Edward Purcell, working independently at Harvard, faced the same challenge: how do you know you've heard something real when the signal is buried in static?

Both men solved it within weeks of each other that winter. Bloch detected protons in water. Purcell found resonance in paraffin wax. Neither knew the other was close. When they discovered they'd succeeded simultaneously, using different methods on different materials, it was the best kind of scientific confirmation — independent proof that they'd both heard something genuine.

The Nobel Committee waited six years to honor them, long enough to see what nuclear magnetic resonance would become. By 1952, it had already transformed how physicists studied matter. Today, as MRI, it's transformed medicine. But in 1946, it was just two groups trying to measure the unmeasurable.

## The Puzzle in the Nucleus

The atomic nucleus spins. Not metaphorically — it has angular momentum, like a top. And because protons carry charge, that spinning nucleus acts as a tiny magnet. In theory, you could flip these nuclear magnets with radio waves, and they'd emit a signal as they flipped back. The frequency would depend on the magnetic field and the nucleus itself — a fingerprint of the atom.

Isidor Rabi had demonstrated this with molecular beams in 1937, work that won him the 1944 Nobel. But molecular beams required elaborate vacuum apparatus and gave signals from isolated atoms. What Bloch and Purcell wanted was harder: resonance from nuclei in ordinary matter — liquids and solids, where nuclei are jostled by neighbors, their magnetic behavior complicated by the surrounding environment. The signal would be weak, the noise overwhelming. Previous attempts had failed.

The elegance was in the subtlety. You couldn't just blast matter with radio waves and hope. You needed to tune everything precisely — the magnetic field strength, the radio frequency, the sample itself — until the nuclei and the radio waves fell into resonance. Then, if you'd built your apparatus sensitive enough and eliminated false signals, you might detect the faint echo of nuclei responding.

## Two Paths to the Same Signal

Bloch's method was continuous wave. He held the radio frequency steady and slowly swept the magnetic field strength. When the field hit the right value, nuclei in his water sample absorbed energy — a dip in the transmitted radio signal told him he'd found resonance. The Stanford group's first clear signal came in January 1946. Bloch saw the dip and knew immediately it was real: too sharp, too repeatable, too precisely where theory predicted.

Purcell's approach was different. At Harvard, working with graduate students Edward Torrey and Robert Pound, he used a bridge circuit to detect changes in the radio-frequency absorption. When they swept the magnetic field through the resonance point in paraffin, the nuclei absorbed energy and the bridge went out of balance. Their signal appeared in late 1945, but they spent weeks confirming it wasn't an artifact of their electronics.

The two groups published in the same issue of *Physical Review* in January 1946 — not as competitors, but as mutual confirmation. Different techniques, different materials, same phenomenon. The physics community took notice. This wasn't a fluke.

## The Human Element

Bloch, Swiss-born, had fled Nazi Europe in 1933. He'd worked on the Manhattan Project but hated it — the secrecy, the weapons purpose, the military hierarchy. After the war, he returned to fundamental physics with something like relief. NMR was pure science: no classified applications, no immediate military use. He could publish freely, share methods, collaborate openly. For a refugee who'd watched physics twisted into service of destruction, there was moral clarity in measuring the spin of protons.

Purcell was American-born, Harvard-trained, a radar physicist during the war. He brought that microwave expertise to NMR — the techniques for generating and detecting radio frequencies, the obsessive attention to electronics and shielding. Where Bloch was a theorist who'd learned experimental skill, Purcell was an experimenter who understood theory deeply. His graduate students did much of the apparatus-building, but Purcell drove the interpretation.

Neither was particularly competitive. When they learned of each other's success, the response was excitement, not jealousy. Bloch and Purcell became friends. They shared techniques, discussed puzzles, and watched together as other labs took up NMR and pushed it further.

## What NMR Revealed

Within a year, it became clear that nuclear magnetic resonance was more than a curiosity. The resonance frequency shifted slightly depending on a nucleus's chemical environment — what molecules it was part of, what neighbors surrounded it. This "chemical shift" meant NMR could distinguish identical atoms in different settings. You could map molecular structure without breaking molecules apart.

In solids, the signals were broader, complicated by interactions between neighboring nuclei. This wasn't noise — it was information. By analyzing those line shapes, physicists could learn about crystal structures, molecular motion, and quantum mechanical interactions that would be invisible any other way. Bloch and Purcell had handed condensed matter physics a new probe, one that could see inside matter without disturbing it.

The medical revolution came later. In the 1970s, Paul Lauterbur and Peter Mansfield (who shared the 2003 Nobel for Physiology or Medicine) realized that by varying magnetic fields spatially, you could create images from NMR signals. The hydrogen in water and fat gives different signals; map those signals across a human body, and you're seeing tissue density, blood flow, tumors. Today, MRI — magnetic resonance imaging, stripped of the word "nuclear" to avoid frightening patients — is standard diagnostic technology. Every scan traces back to Bloch and Purcell measuring protons in 1946.

## The Nobel Question

The Nobel Committee's six-year wait was unusual. Some prizes are awarded for predictions later confirmed; this was confirmation of theory, with immediate experimental success. Why delay?

The answer seems to be impact. By 1952, NMR spectroscopy was an established field. Multiple labs had reproduced and extended the work. Applications were emerging in chemistry and physics. The method's power was no longer potential — it was proven.

There was no controversy about the prize itself, a rarity for shared awards. Bloch and Purcell had clearly done equivalent work independently. Neither had priority; neither deserved sole credit. The only question was whether others — Torrey and Pound at Harvard, Hansen and Packard at Stanford — should have shared. They didn't. Graduate students rarely do, even when their hands built the apparatus.

In his Nobel lecture, Purcell was characteristically modest: "The discovery of nuclear magnetic resonance in condensed matter was, I think, a fairly obvious next step... The main thing was to try it." But obvious steps often go untaken for years. Rabi's work was published in 1937. Nine years passed before anyone successfully extended it to solids and liquids. Sometimes the obvious is visible only in hindsight.

## The Aftermath

Bloch spent much of the 1950s as the first director-general of CERN, helping build the European physics community back from the war. He returned to Stanford, continued work on fundamental physics, became known as much for his teaching as his research. His relationship with experimental physics remained pragmatic: he was good at it, but his heart lived in theory.

Purcell stayed at Harvard, trained generations of physicists, and maintained his dual passion for elegant experiments and deep understanding. His textbook on electricity and magnetism became a classic. In the 1960s, he and his student Harold Ewen detected the 21-centimeter line of neutral hydrogen in space, opening the field of radio astronomy. It was NMR in reverse: using radio waves to map the magnetic resonance of hydrogen atoms across the galaxy.

Both men were uncomfortable with celebrity. The Nobel brought attention, lecture invitations, administrative requests. They mostly said no. The work was what mattered: the next measurement, the next student, the next puzzle. Fame was an interruption.

## The Wonder

There's something almost absurd about NMR. You put matter in a magnetic field, tickle it with radio waves, and listen for an echo from individual atomic nuclei — objects ten-trillionths of a meter across, drowned in the thermal noise of room-temperature matter. That it works at all feels like magic. That it works reliably enough to image a human brain, to map protein folding, to identify molecules in forensic samples — that stretches belief.

The elegance is in the gentleness. You're not smashing atoms or heating samples or stripping electrons. You're just listening, at exactly the right frequency, to the quietest possible whisper: the precession of nuclear spin in a magnetic field. Matter is mostly empty space, but NMR hears what fills that space — the quantum mechanical hum of nuclei, each one singing its own note.

Bloch and Purcell gave physics new ears. They proved that if you listened carefully enough, matter would tell you its secrets. All you needed was patience, precision, and the courage to measure nothing until it became something.
