# 1932 — Quantum Mechanics

Werner Heisenberg was twenty-three when he climbed Heligoland, a treeless island in the North Sea, to escape his hay fever and finish what had been tormenting him for months. It was June 1925. Classical physics could not explain the atom. Every model broke down. In his spartan room overlooking the cliffs, working through the night, Heisenberg abandoned the attempt to visualize what electrons were doing and focused instead on what could be measured: the frequencies and intensities of spectral lines. When he finished the calculation at three in the morning, he was too excited to sleep. "I had the feeling that, through the surface of atomic phenomena, I was looking at a strangely beautiful interior, and felt almost giddy at the thought that I now had to investigate this wealth of mathematical structures nature had so generously spread out before me."

The Nobel Prize in Physics 1932 went to Heisenberg alone "for the creation of quantum mechanics, the application of which has, inter alia, led to the discovery of the allotropic forms of hydrogen." By the time he received it, quantum mechanics had already rewritten physics. But the prize announcement landed on a field still raw with priority disputes, philosophical quarrels, and the question of who deserved credit for a revolution that many had helped make.

Heisenberg's breakthrough was matrix mechanics — a formalism where physical quantities like position and momentum were represented by arrays of numbers that didn't commute. Multiply them in one order and you got one answer; reverse the order and you got another. The difference was proportional to Planck's constant, a tiny number that marked the boundary between the quantum and classical worlds. It was deeply strange. Heisenberg himself didn't fully understand matrices; his mentor Max Born and colleague Pascual Jordan had to develop the mathematics. But the method worked. It predicted the spectrum of hydrogen with perfect accuracy.

Except Heisenberg's approach was forbidding, abstract, and nearly impossible to visualize. Nine months later, in early 1926, Erwin Schrödinger published a completely different quantum theory — wave mechanics — where electrons were described by smooth, continuous wave functions governed by a differential equation. Physicists could picture waves. Schrödinger's approach felt familiar, rooted in classical physics. It predicted the same results as Heisenberg's matrices but seemed infinitely more intuitive.

The two men loathed each other's theories. Heisenberg thought wave mechanics was a retreat into classical thinking, a refusal to accept the radical discontinuity of the quantum world. Schrödinger found matrix mechanics ugly and sterile, "a veritable witches' multiplication table." When Schrödinger visited Copenhagen to debate Niels Bohr, Heisenberg's mentor, the exchanges grew so heated that Schrödinger fell ill and took to bed — where Bohr continued to argue with him.

Within months it was proven that the two formalisms were mathematically equivalent: different languages describing the same physics. Paul Dirac, a twenty-three-year-old Englishman of startling mathematical power, showed how both approaches could be unified in a more general framework. But equivalence didn't settle who had done it first, or who deserved more credit. Heisenberg had published in July 1925. Schrödinger's papers appeared in 1926. Chronology favored Heisenberg. Accessibility favored Schrödinger.

The Nobel Committee chose Heisenberg alone for the 1932 prize, awarded in 1933. Schrödinger and Dirac shared the 1933 prize, awarded in 1934. The timing was awkward, the message unclear. Was Heisenberg being honored for precedence? For the matrix formalism specifically? The citation's reference to allotropic hydrogen — ortho and para hydrogen, discovered using quantum mechanics — seemed almost apologetic, as though the committee needed a concrete application to justify recognizing something so fundamental.

What Heisenberg had actually done went far beyond matrices. He had articulated the uncertainty principle in 1927: you cannot simultaneously know a particle's position and momentum with arbitrary precision. This wasn't a limitation of measurement technique — it was a statement about reality itself. The electron didn't have a definite position and momentum; those quantities were fundamentally indeterminate until measured. Heisenberg had helped Bohr develop the Copenhagen interpretation, the philosophical framework that described quantum mechanics as complete and probabilistic, not just incomplete and waiting for a better theory. And he had shown that you couldn't think about atoms classically at all. You had to let go of visualization, of trajectories, of the comforting idea that particles were little billiard balls following definite paths. The math would tell you what could be observed; the rest was metaphysics.

This was what made quantum mechanics revolutionary. It wasn't just a better set of equations. It was a new epistemology. Classical physics had been deterministic: know the initial conditions, apply the laws, and you could predict the future with certainty. Quantum mechanics said that nature was probabilistic at its foundation. You could predict the odds that an electron would be found in a certain location, but not which individual electron would appear where. God, famously, was playing dice — and Heisenberg, more than anyone, had written the rules.

The human cost of this achievement was entanglement with history's darkest currents. Heisenberg remained in Germany after the Nazis took power. He was not a Nazi; he was apolitical to the point of moral blindness. He stayed because Germany was home, because he believed in separating science from politics, because he thought he could preserve German physics through the catastrophe. He spent the war leading the German atomic bomb project — which, to the Allies' relief and historians' enduring debate, never came close to success. Was it intentional sabotage, as Heisenberg later suggested? Bureaucratic incompetence? Lack of resources? Heisenberg's own memoirs are unreliable, self-serving, haunted by the need to be remembered as something other than a Nazi scientist.

Meanwhile, many of those who built quantum mechanics alongside him had fled. Schrödinger left Austria after the Anschluss. Dirac remained in England. Born, who had taught Heisenberg matrices, was Jewish and was forced to emigrate. Einstein, who had laid groundwork for quantum theory but never accepted its probabilistic nature, was in America. The Copenhagen school scattered. The generation that had rebuilt physics in the 1920s spent the 1930s and 40s in exile, or silence, or collaboration with regimes that despised the cosmopolitan, intellectual culture that had made their work possible.

What Heisenberg created in 1925 endures. Quantum mechanics is the foundation of atomic physics, solid-state physics, chemistry, and quantum field theory. It explains why atoms are stable, why the periodic table has the structure it does, why metals conduct and insulators don't. It gave us semiconductors, lasers, MRI scanners, and the transistor — which means it gave us the digital age. Quantum computing is its latest frontier: machines that exploit superposition and entanglement to perform calculations impossible for classical computers.

And it remains strange. Ninety years later, the measurement problem has not been solved. We don't know why observation collapses the wave function, or what "observation" means in a universe with no observers, or whether the wave function is real or just a calculational tool. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle is still the bedrock. Particles are still indeterminate until measured. The double-slit experiment still makes no classical sense.

Heisenberg's Nobel lecture never mentioned matrices. Instead, he spoke about the philosophical implications — how quantum mechanics forced a new conception of reality, how the act of measurement creates what we observe, how nature at the smallest scale refuses to be visualized. "We can no longer speak of the behavior of the particle independently of the process of observation," he said. "The discontinuous change in our knowledge in the instant of observation is the most characteristic feature of the quantum theoretical description of nature." In a room full of physicists, some of whom had built the theory alongside him, some of whom would never forgive his wartime choices, he described what he'd glimpsed that night on Heligoland: a world that worked by rules no one had imagined, beautiful and inexplicable and true.
