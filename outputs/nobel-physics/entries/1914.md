# 1914 — X-Ray Crystallography

Max von Laue stood before the Munich Academy in June 1912 with photographs that should not have existed. Dark cards speckled with symmetric patterns of dots — shadows cast by atoms. For two centuries, scientists had argued whether crystals were truly ordered at the atomic scale or merely appeared so. Von Laue's images settled it. He had sent X-rays through a crystal of copper sulfate and captured, on photographic film, the diffraction pattern of matter's hidden architecture. The dots were echoes of atomic arrangement, proof that crystals were lattices, not accidents. Within months, every physicist who saw the images understood: a door had opened.

The Nobel Committee moved with unusual speed. Two years later, in 1914, they awarded von Laue the prize in physics "for his discovery of the diffraction of X-rays by crystals." It was recognition not just of a discovery but of a method — a way to see what had been invisible since matter first condensed into solid form.

---

The problem had haunted crystallography from its birth. Crystals fascinated because they were regular — salt formed cubes, quartz formed hexagons, snowflakes branched in sixfold symmetry. In 1784, René Haüy had proposed that crystals were stacks of identical units, like bricks in a wall. The idea was elegant but impossible to verify. No microscope could resolve atomic scale. By 1900, chemists worked with molecular formulas, physicists calculated atomic weights, but no one could *see* how atoms arranged themselves. The lattice theory was plausible inference, not observed fact.

X-rays complicated matters. Wilhelm Röntgen had discovered them in 1895 — mysterious radiation that penetrated flesh and exposed bone on film. But were X-rays particles or waves? If waves, what was their wavelength? Estimates suggested something near the spacing between atoms in a crystal, but that raised an odd possibility. If X-rays were waves with atomic-scale wavelengths, and if crystals were periodic lattices of atoms, then a crystal should act as a three-dimensional diffraction grating. Send X-rays through, and they should scatter in predictable patterns. If it worked, it would prove two things at once: X-rays were waves, and crystals were lattices.

Von Laue saw it first. He was a theoretical physicist, not an experimentalist, but he understood optics and symmetry. In early 1912, at age thirty-three, he discussed the idea with colleagues in Munich. Walter Friedrich and Paul Knipping, both experimentalists, agreed to try. They set up a simple apparatus: an X-ray tube, a crystal mounted between lead screens with a pinhole, and photographic film behind. They chose copper sulfate because it formed large, clear crystals. On April 21, they exposed the first plate.

When Friedrich developed the film, he saw spots. Not a single spot or a blur, but a pattern — clusters of dots arranged around the central beam. Von Laue was summoned. He stared at the image and knew immediately what he was seeing: interference. The X-rays had passed through the crystal lattice, scattering off rows of atoms. Where scattered rays reinforced, they left bright spots. Where they canceled, darkness. The pattern was fingerprint of the crystal's internal order.

Von Laue worked through the mathematics in weeks. The geometry was intricate — three-dimensional diffraction required accounting for scattering from all lattice planes simultaneously — but the core principle was simple. Constructive interference occurred when the path difference between scattered rays equaled an integer multiple of the wavelength. The spots on the film mapped directly to the crystal's symmetry. You could work backward from the pattern to the atomic arrangement.

The paper appeared in June 1912. It sent a shockwave. Physicists immediately grasped that von Laue had done more than confirm lattice theory. He had invented a tool. If you could interpret the diffraction patterns, you could determine the exact positions of atoms in a crystal. The structure of matter was suddenly knowable.

In England, William Henry Bragg read von Laue's paper with interest and irritation. Bragg was fifty years old, a physicist at Leeds, and he had spent years arguing that X-rays were particles, not waves. Von Laue's diffraction patterns seemed to prove him wrong. But Bragg was not a man to dismiss evidence. He studied the mathematics and saw something von Laue had not emphasized: a simpler way to think about it.

Instead of treating diffraction as scattering from individual atoms, Bragg imagined X-rays reflecting off planes of atoms within the crystal — like light bouncing off mirrors stacked at regular intervals. Reflection would be strong when the path difference between rays reflecting from adjacent planes matched the wavelength. The condition was elegant: *nλ = 2d sin θ*, where *λ* was the wavelength, *d* the spacing between planes, *θ* the angle of incidence, and *n* an integer. Later generations would call it Bragg's Law.

Bragg realized this formulation made interpretation vastly easier. Instead of solving von Laue's complex equations, you measured angles and intensities and calculated spacing directly. He built an instrument — the X-ray spectrometer — to do exactly that. A crystal mounted on a rotating table, X-rays at variable angles, a detector to measure intensity. Rotate the crystal, plot the intensity peaks, and read off the structure.

But Bragg had help. His son, William Lawrence Bragg, was twenty-two, a recent Cambridge graduate working in the Cavendish Laboratory. The younger Bragg saw his father's work and pushed it further. In November 1912, just months after von Laue's discovery, Lawrence Bragg worked out the crystal structures of sodium chloride and potassium chloride — common salt and its cousin. He showed that sodium and chlorine atoms alternated in a cubic lattice, exactly as chemists had guessed but never proven. It was the first-ever determination of atomic positions by X-ray diffraction.

Father and son formed an extraordinary partnership. William built instruments; Lawrence interpreted results. They were competitive, sometimes prickly with each other, but unstoppable together. By 1913, they had solved the structures of diamond, fluorite, calcite, and pyrite. Each solution revealed not just atomic positions but something deeper — how atoms bonded, why crystals cleaved along certain planes, what gave minerals their properties. Crystallography transformed from geometry to chemistry.

The Nobel Committee faced a dilemma. Von Laue had made the initial discovery; the Braggs had turned it into a science. In 1914, they awarded the physics prize to von Laue. Then in 1915, they gave it to William Henry Bragg and William Lawrence Bragg jointly "for their services in the analysis of crystal structure by means of X-rays." It was the first time a father and son had shared the prize. Lawrence, at twenty-five, remains the youngest physics laureate ever.

Von Laue accepted his prize in Stockholm on a continent sliding toward war. By the time the Braggs received theirs the following year, Europe was convulsed. The ceremony was canceled; they collected their medals later. William Henry Bragg spent the war years working on submarine detection. Von Laue, back in Germany, worked on electron optics and avoided politics where he could.

But X-ray crystallography outlasted the war and the men who founded it. By the 1920s, it was routine. By the 1950s, it had revealed the structure of penicillin, vitamin B12, and DNA — the double helix itself confirmed by X-ray diffraction patterns. Rosalind Franklin's Photo 51, the image that convinced Watson and Crick of DNA's helical structure, was a direct descendant of von Laue's first spotted films. Dorothy Hodgkin won the chemistry Nobel in 1964 for solving insulin's structure through X-ray crystallography. Max Perutz and John Kendrew won in 1962 for hemoglobin and myoglobin.

The method kept evolving. Synchrotron light sources in the late twentieth century generated X-rays brilliant enough to solve structures of viruses, ribosomes, and membrane proteins. By 2020, the Protein Data Bank held structures of over 170,000 biological molecules, nearly all determined by X-ray crystallography. Drug design, materials science, structural biology — entire fields rest on the foundation von Laue glimpsed in those first diffraction spots.

Von Laue lived to see it. He survived the Nazi years, refused to give the Hitler salute, and helped Jewish colleagues when he could. After the war, he worked to rebuild German physics. He died in 1960, struck by a motorcyclist while driving near Göttingen. He was eighty-one.

Lawrence Bragg lived longer, dying in 1971 after a career directing the Cavendish Laboratory, where he mentored the generation that solved DNA. William Henry Bragg, his father, had died in 1942, still working in his eighties. Both had spent their lives refining the tool they had helped create.

Von Laue's insight was elegant in its simplicity: if you want to see atoms, use light with a wavelength to match. But the true achievement was recognizing what the patterns meant — that the spots were not random scatter but shadows of order, proof that matter at its smallest scale followed rules as precise as the stars. The Braggs turned that recognition into a language, a way to read structure from symmetry.

When Max von Laue exposed that first copper sulfate crystal in 1912, he revealed more than atoms. He revealed that hidden order was knowable, that the invisible could be mapped, that matter itself was a text we could learn to read. The spots on his photographic plates were the first words of a new literature — the structure of everything.
