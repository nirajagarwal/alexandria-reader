# 2007 — Giant Magnetoresistance

Albert Fert kept notebooks. Dense, methodical, filled with measurements that made no sense until suddenly they did. In 1988, he was studying thin films of iron and chromium at the University of Paris-Sud — layers so thin that ten of them stacked together would barely match the width of a virus. He watched the electrical resistance plunge when he applied a magnetic field. Not a small drop. Not the modest effect physicists had seen before. The resistance fell by half. By *half*. He stared at the numbers, recalculated, measured again. The effect held.

Three thousand miles away, Peter Grünberg at the Research Center Jülich in Germany was seeing the same thing. Different lab, different films, same stunning result. They had independently discovered what would come to be called giant magnetoresistance — GMR — and neither quite believed it at first.

---

The physics was strange enough to be beautiful. Take two magnetic layers separated by a non-magnetic spacer. When the magnetizations of the two layers point the same direction, electrons flow easily through the sandwich. Their spins match the magnetic landscape; resistance is low. Flip one layer's magnetization, and suddenly electrons scatter wildly at the interfaces. Some with spin-up encounter a layer that wants spin-down. They bounce, deflect, impede the current. Resistance spikes.

This much was expected. Physicists had known since the 1930s that magnetism affects resistance — that's ordinary magnetoresistance, a few percent at most. But Fert and Grünberg had built something new. By making the layers atomically thin and choosing materials carefully, they'd amplified a quantum mechanical effect that exists at interfaces into something macroscopic and dramatic. The electrons weren't just encountering one or two interfaces. They were scattering back and forth through a quantum-scale maze, each interface multiplying the effect. Hence "giant" — not hyperbole, but precision. This was magnetoresistance on a scale that changed what was possible.

Fert published in November 1988. Grünberg's paper appeared two months earlier, though he'd seen a smaller effect — about 1.5 percent compared to Fert's 50 percent. Both men were in their fifties, established condensed matter physicists who'd spent careers studying the quantum behavior of electrons in materials. Neither was chasing applications. They were chasing understanding.

The applications came anyway.

---

By the mid-1990s, engineers at IBM realized what GMR meant for data storage. A hard drive reads information by detecting tiny magnetic fields from bits written on a spinning disk. The smaller the bit, the weaker the field, the harder to detect. GMR sensors were exquisitely sensitive to magnetic field changes — exactly what you needed to read ever-tinier bits. Stuart Parkin at IBM Almaden led the charge to shrink GMR sensors down to functional size, to integrate them into read heads, to make them work at industrial scale.

The first GMR-based hard drive shipped in 1997. Within five years, they'd taken over the industry. Storage density exploded. The laptop you could buy in 2007 held a hundred times more data than one from 1997, in the same physical space. GMR hadn't just improved hard drives incrementally — it had enabled an entirely new scaling regime. Every smartphone, every laptop, every data center relied on sensors derived from Fert and Grünberg's atomically thin films.

The technology arrived just as the world needed it. The internet was swelling with data. Google was indexing billions of pages. Digital photography was replacing film. Music was moving from CDs to MP3 players. GMR didn't cause this transformation, but it made it physically possible. Without denser storage, the digital explosion would have hit a wall of physics.

---

Fert was a theorist turned experimentalist, a Frenchman who'd studied at the École Normale Supérieure and gravitated toward solid-state physics. He worked with a kind of quiet precision, building his understanding layer by layer, literally. His lab specialized in molecular beam epitaxy — growing crystalline films atom by atom in ultra-high vacuum. It was painstaking work. A single sample took days to prepare. But it gave him control at the scale where quantum effects dominated.

Grünberg came from a different tradition. Trained in Germany, he'd spent his career at Jülich, one of Europe's major research centers, studying magnetic coupling between layers. He was interested in how magnetism propagated through materials, how layers "talked" to each other across non-magnetic spacers. His discovery of GMR emerged from this fundamental question: if you stack magnetic layers with precise spacing, how do their magnetizations interact?

Both men were working in the thick of condensed matter physics, a field often dismissed as less glamorous than particle physics or cosmology. No grand unified theories, no origin of the universe — just electrons moving through materials. But condensed matter is where quantum mechanics becomes tangible. It's where strange quantum rules manifest in substances you can hold, effects you can measure, devices you can build. Fert and Grünberg had revealed a quantum phenomenon and watched it become a pillar of the information age.

---

When the Nobel committee announced the 2007 prize, the citation read: "for the discovery of giant magnetoresistance." Clean, direct, no ambiguity about priority. Both men shared equally — a rare instance of the committee acknowledging simultaneous independent discovery without dispute. They'd published within months of each other, worked on the same problem from different angles, reached the same conclusion. Neither had scooped the other; both deserved recognition.

The prize carried a different weight than some. This wasn't a discovery about the fundamental nature of reality. It didn't reveal new particles or forces. It was about engineering the quantum behavior of electrons in carefully constructed materials. But that distinction mattered less than the fact that GMR had done what physics is supposed to do: reveal something true about nature and make it useful.

Fert, in his Nobel lecture, emphasized the surprise of it. "The discovery of GMR was not the result of a research program aimed at applications," he said. "It emerged from basic research on the electronic properties of magnetic multilayers." This was a theme threading through much of 20th-century physics — chase understanding for its own sake, and occasionally you stumble into something the world desperately needs.

---

By 2007, GMR was already being supplanted by an even more sensitive effect: tunnel magnetoresistance, or TMR. But GMR had opened the door. It had shown that quantum spin — that intrinsic angular momentum of electrons that seems so abstract in textbooks — could be manipulated and harnessed at room temperature in solid-state devices. This launched the field of spintronics, which treats electron spin as a degree of freedom for information processing, separate from charge.

Some physicists saw GMR as a glimpse of what's possible when you control quantum phenomena in materials with atomic precision. We've been manipulating charge for a century — that's all conventional electronics is. But spin is a newer frontier, less explored, potentially richer. GMR was proof that this frontier wasn't just theoretical. You could build devices that exploited spin, that worked reliably, that scaled to billions of units.

Others saw it as a reminder that the most transformative physics doesn't always come from the most celebrated corners of the field. Fert and Grünberg weren't at CERN or probing the cosmic microwave background. They were in materials labs, grinding through sample preparation and measurements. The work was incremental until it wasn't. The discovery was obscure until every computer on Earth depended on it.

Fert died in 2024, Grünberg in 2018. Both lived to see their discovery become invisible infrastructure — so woven into technology that no one thinks about it. That's the fate of the most successful physics: it disappears into the background, humming quietly inside machines, making the digital age possible one electron spin at a time.
