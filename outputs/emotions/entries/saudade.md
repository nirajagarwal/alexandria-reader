# Saudade

The Portuguese insist it's untranslatable, which is both true and a bit of a flex. Every language with saudade—Portuguese, Galician—makes the same claim: this feeling belongs to us, shaped by our history, our *fado* music, our relationship to the sea and to empire's ghosts. The Spanish have *añoranza*, the Germans *Sehnsucht*, the Welsh *hiraeth*, the Romanians *dor*. All describe longing tinged with loss. But the Portuguese argument is that saudade isn't just longing—it's a particular pleasure taken in that longing, a way of dwelling in absence that transforms grief into something almost sacred.

The word appears in Portuguese literature as early as the 13th century, but it crystallized into something culturally central during the Age of Discoveries. When Portuguese sailors left for years-long voyages to Africa, India, Brazil—many never to return—the people left behind developed an idiom for the specific ache of loving someone who is gone but not dead, distant but not entirely lost. The anthropologist Gustavo Sorá traced how saudade became nationalist: by the 19th century, Portuguese writers were claiming it as evidence of a uniquely deep Portuguese soul, a people who felt more profoundly than others. Romantic nationalism does this—turns local emotional habits into proof of national superiority.

But here's what's interesting: when researchers actually study saudade, they find it's not one feeling but several, bundled together in a way that's culturally specific but emotionally familiar. In 2014, a team led by Portuguese psychologist David Pais analyzed hundreds of Portuguese descriptions of saudade and found it combines elements of grief, nostalgia, hope, pleasure, and social connection—sometimes all at once. The distinguishing feature isn't any single component but the simultaneous activation of positive and negative affect. You feel bad about the absence, good about the memory, hopeful about reunion, and somehow satisfied with the ache itself. Most emotions are either positive or negative. Saudade is both, stably.

The neuroscientist Antonio Damásio, himself Portuguese, has argued that saudade involves what he calls "homeostatic emotions"—feelings tied to the body's monitoring of its social bonds. When someone important is absent, the brain registers it as a kind of deficit, a missing resource. But unlike fear or sadness, which push toward resolution, saudade settles into the deficit, even cultivates it. In neuroimaging studies of nostalgia (the closest English proxy), researchers find activation in both the reward system (remembering something valued) and the default mode network (self-referential thought, often melancholic). Saudade likely shows a similar pattern but with less urgency to resolve the dissonance.

The philosopher Aubrey de Grey, in an odd 2008 essay on emotional semantics, suggested that what makes saudade distinct isn't the feeling but the meta-feeling—the Portuguese cultural practice of noticing, naming, and dwelling in this particular emotional state. English-speakers experience the same neural pattern but move through it quickly, code it as "missing someone" or "nostalgia," and try to fix it or forget it. The Portuguese have built a cultural infrastructure around saudade: songs, poems, whole evenings devoted to feeling it together. *Fado* music institutionalizes saudade, makes it performable, shareable, almost virtuous.

This matters for the Lisa Feldman Barrett question: are emotions discovered (universal states we find words for) or constructed (cultural inventions that shape experience)? Saudade is a test case. The mixed-valence pattern—sad-but-sweet longing for the absent—probably occurs in all humans. But the Portuguese language and culture have made that pattern more stable, more accessible, more legitimate. They've built a concept that then shapes how the emotion is experienced. You don't just feel saudade; you recognize that you're feeling it, which intensifies and sustains it. The word creates a feedback loop.

The Brazilian version complicates things. Brazilian Portuguese kept *saudade* but shifted its valence. In Portugal, saudade carries melancholy, a connection to loss and the ocean and empire's decline. In Brazil, it's lighter, more tied to memory of good times and anticipation of reunion. Same word, different emotional ecosystem. When Portuguese and Brazilian psychologists compared notes in 2013, they found that Portuguese respondents rated saudade as more painful and less hopeful than Brazilians did. The language crossed the Atlantic, but the feeling bent to fit new conditions—separation from home rather than separation at home.

Does English need this word? We've borrowed it, somewhat—*saudade* shows up in English poetry and essays, usually italicized, marked as foreign. But we haven't naturalized it, and that's revealing. American culture in particular has an impatience with unresolved longing. We have "closure," "moving on," "staying positive"—a whole vocabulary for not dwelling in absence. Saudade refuses closure. It makes a virtue of the refusal. That's not a feeling English-speaking cultures have particularly wanted to cultivate.

Yet people do experience it. The research shows that immigrants, expatriates, and refugees report something very close to saudade—especially when return is possible but indefinitely delayed. It's not homesickness (too urgent) or nostalgia (too backward-looking). It's the sustainable ache of being split between two homes, two times, two versions of yourself. English doesn't have a word for it, but that doesn't mean English-speakers don't feel it. They just don't have a way to mark it, share it, or honor it as something valuable in itself.

The paradox at the heart of saudade: it feels best when you don't try to resolve it. The moment you book the flight home, start the reunion plans, distract yourself into forgetting—the quality of the feeling changes. Pure saudade requires distance to remain distant. The Portuguese figured this out centuries ago, built a culture around it, and told themselves it was untranslatable. Mostly, they were right.
