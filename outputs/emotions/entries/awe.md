# Awe

Edmund Burke thought it was fear. In his 1757 *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Sublime and Beautiful*, he argued that awe arises from anything that threatens pain or death—storms, vast heights, powerful animals, God. The sublime, which he considered synonymous with awe, was "delightful horror," terror at a safe distance. The experience required both the perception of danger and the knowledge that you weren't actually in it. This became the standard view for over a century: awe was essentially fear plus safety, the emotion of encountering something that could destroy you but won't.

Turns out Burke got the mechanism backwards. The modern research shows awe isn't built on fear at all—it's built on the violation of your cognitive schema.

Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt published the framework in 2003. They'd surveyed hundreds of awe experiences across cultures and found two consistent features: *vastness* (perceiving something larger than yourself) and *accommodation* (needing to adjust your mental models to fit what you're experiencing). The fear response is optional, sometimes present but not definitional. People report awe at birth, at mathematical proofs, at acts of moral beauty, at microscopic complexity—none of which threaten harm. What they share is that they break your existing frameworks for understanding the world.

The neurological signature confirms this. When people experience awe in fMRI studies, the default mode network—the brain's self-referential processing system—shows decreased activation. The sense of self literally quiets down. At the same time, areas associated with attention and sensory processing light up. You're taking in more information while thinking less about yourself. Michelle Shiota at Arizona State found that awe produces a distinct facial expression: raised inner eyebrows, widened eyes, a slightly open mouth—the face of absorption, not fear. It's the expression of someone whose model just broke.

This matters clinically because awe has effects that other positive emotions don't. Paul Piff's studies at UC Irvine showed that brief awe experiences—watching awe-inducing nature videos, standing among tall trees—made people more generous, more ethical, less entitled. They became more willing to help strangers and less focused on personal gain. The effect size was comparable to many therapeutic interventions, and it lasted. In one experiment, people who spent sixty seconds looking at tall eucalyptus trees were more likely to help someone who "accidentally" dropped pens. The awe group picked up more pens than the happiness group or the control group.

The mechanism seems to be the quieting of self-interest. When the default mode network decreases activity, self-concern decreases with it. You become less focused on your own importance, your own goals, your own status. Keltner calls this the "small self" effect—awe makes you feel smaller, but not in a diminishing way. In a diminished way. You're still here, but you're not the center. For people prone to narcissism or depression (both conditions of excessive self-focus), this recalibration can be therapeutic.

Here's where it gets strange. Awe doesn't require the sublime or the beautiful. In a 2021 study, researchers induced awe using threatening stimuli—videos of tornadoes, volcanic eruptions, dangerous animals. Participants still reported the characteristic features: vastness, need for accommodation, decreased self-focus. But they also reported fear, and the prosocial effects disappeared. This suggests two types of awe: threat-based (closer to Burke's model) and non-threat-based (closer to Keltner's). Both involve cognitive accommodation to vastness, but only one makes you generous.

The Chinese term *jìng wèi* (敬畏) captures this split. *Jìng* is reverence, respect; *wèi* is fear, trepidation. The word contains both the prosocial and the threat-based versions. Ancient Chinese philosophy recognized awe as dual—you could feel it toward a virtuous teacher or a powerful tyrant. English collapsed these into one term and lost the distinction.

The evolutionary story is contested. Keltner argues awe evolved to bind groups to charismatic leaders and to encourage learning from those with superior knowledge—it's a social emotion. Shiota proposes it evolved for exploration and accommodation of new information—it's a cognitive emotion. Both could be true. Awe at a tribal elder's wisdom would serve group cohesion; awe at a massive waterfall would serve environmental learning. The research hasn't settled this, partly because inducing genuine awe in a lab is difficult and the ecological validity of nature videos is questionable.

One finding stands out from the longitudinal data. Regular awe experiences—defined as weekly or more—predict life satisfaction better than regular happiness, excitement, or contentment. Jennifer Stellar's work shows this holds even controlling for personality and socioeconomic status. People who seek out awe (visiting natural wonders, engaging with great art, contemplating scientific discoveries) report more meaning in their lives. The effect is stronger than for other positive emotions, and it compounds over time.

But awe is increasingly rare in modern environments. Urban design minimizes vastness; digital life fragments attention; information overload makes accommodation exhausting rather than exhilarating. In a 2018 survey, Americans reported experiencing awe an average of 2.5 times per week—down from estimates of 5-6 times per week in pre-industrial societies. The number varies by access to nature, cultural activities, and leisure time. Wealthy people report more awe, not because wealth causes awe but because it purchases access to awe-inducing experiences: travel, art, education, time.

The paradox researchers keep hitting: you can't manufacture awe. The moment you try to produce it, the accommodation requirement fails—you're expecting it, so your schema doesn't need to adjust. Theme parks try; mostly they produce excitement. IMAX nature documentaries try; they produce interest. Real awe requires genuine surprise at genuine vastness. This makes it resistant to commodification, which may explain why it's declining. You can't buy it efficiently. You have to go where it is and wait for it to happen.

The implications are architectural, educational, cultural. If awe has the effects the research claims—prosocial behavior, decreased self-focus, meaning, satisfaction—then environments that enable it matter. Not for hedonic reasons but for functional ones. Societies that preserve access to vastness (natural or cultural) may be building their moral infrastructure, not just their aesthetic one.
