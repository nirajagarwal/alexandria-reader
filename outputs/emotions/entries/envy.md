# Envy

The English language collapses two distinct emotions into one word. The Greeks knew better. They had *phthonos*—malicious envy, the kind that wants to tear down—and *zelos*, which could be translated as envy but meant something closer to emulative rivalry, the kind that spurs you to rise up. Aristotle thought *phthonos* was always vicious, but *zelos* could be virtuous, depending on the object. The Romans kept the distinction: *invidia* versus *aemulatio*. Then English came along and dropped the second term, leaving us with one word that has to do too much work.

This creates confusion in the research. When studies measure envy, they're often measuring a blend—some subjects are feeling malicious envy (wanting the other person to lose what they have), others are feeling benign envy (wanting to gain what they have). Niels van de Ven at Tilburg University spent years trying to tease these apart. His 2009 study found they have different action tendencies: malicious envy predicts schadenfreude and sabotage; benign envy predicts effort and emulation. They're not variations on a theme. They're different emotions that English accidentally merged.

The Dutch still maintain the distinction—*benijden* versus *afgunst*—and this turns out to matter. When van de Ven asked Dutch speakers to recall episodes of envy, they easily separated them into two categories without prompting. English speakers mixed them together. The language shapes what you can distinguish in your own experience.

Here's what both types have in common: they require upward social comparison, a sense of deserving or wanting what the other person has, and an element of pain. But malicious envy adds hostility and a zero-sum frame—your gain is my loss. Benign envy adds admiration and a sense of possibility—if you got it, maybe I can too. The difference is whether you see the other person's success as a threat or a proof of concept.

The evolutionary story is contested. Some researchers argue envy evolved as a mechanism for tracking relative status in hierarchical groups—constant monitoring of who has what keeps you from falling too far behind. But this doesn't explain the malicious variant. Why would natural selection favor an emotion that wastes energy on tearing others down rather than building yourself up?

One hypothesis: malicious envy is a deterrent. Richard Smith at the University of Kentucky argues that the threat of envy—and the social punishment it motivates—kept successful individuals from flaunting too much in ancestral environments. If you knew that displaying your good fortune would provoke hostility, you'd be more careful. Envy, in this model, is the enforcement mechanism for egalitarian norms. The emotion is costly for the envier but keeps inequality in check.

This maps onto anthropological data. Hunter-gatherer societies have elaborate rituals for dampening envy—successful hunters downplay their kills, distribute meat according to strict protocols, avoid boasting. The !Kung have a practice called "insulting the meat" where the successful hunter is mocked to prevent pride. These aren't primitive superstitions; they're social technologies for managing an emotion everyone knows is dangerous.

Modern capitalism eliminates these constraints. Conspicuous consumption isn't taboo; it's advertising. Social media amplifies the problem. A 2017 study by Hanna Krasnova found that passive Facebook use—scrolling through others' highlight reels—reliably induces malicious envy, which then predicts depression. The upward comparisons are constant and the egalitarian norms are gone. You're supposed to celebrate everyone's success, but the older parts of your brain are registering threat.

The neuroscience adds a wrinkle. Hidehiko Takahashi's fMRI studies found that envy activates the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex—the region associated with physical pain. The metaphor "envy hurts" is literal. Schadenfreude, in turn, activates the ventral striatum, the reward center. The pain of envy and the pleasure of schadenfreude are neurologically linked. When the envied person suffers, the envier's pain centers quiet down. The emotion has its own internal logic: reduce your pain by increasing theirs.

But benign envy shows a different pattern. It activates the ventral striatum earlier—not when the other person fails, but when you imagine yourself succeeding. The reward comes from the goal, not from someone else's loss. This is why van de Ven thinks they're separate emotions, not poles on a spectrum. They have different neurological signatures, different action tendencies, different outcomes.

The question is whether you can cultivate one and suppress the other. Carol Dweck's work on growth mindset suggests you can—people who believe ability is malleable experience more benign envy and less malicious envy when confronted with others' success. If success is a fixed pie, someone else's slice threatens yours. If success is a learnable skill, someone else's achievement is data about what's possible. The frame shifts the emotion.

Helmut Schoeck, writing in 1966, argued that all egalitarian politics is sublimated envy—that the drive to redistribute wealth is just malicious envy dressed up as justice. This is too simple, but the book raises an uncomfortable question: how much of our moral outrage at inequality is actually envy? The left suspects the right of disguising greed as merit; the right suspects the left of disguising envy as compassion. Both might be right, which means neither can dismiss the other's critique.

What we know for certain: envy is more common than people admit. Jan Crusius at the University of Cologne found that people reliably underreport their own envy while accurately detecting it in others. The emotion is socially unacceptable, which drives it underground but doesn't eliminate it. We've decided envy is shameful without building the social structures that would make it less common. The !Kung had it backward—they accepted the emotion and constrained the conditions that provoke it. We deny the emotion and maximize the conditions.

The Christians used to put envy in the seven deadlies, but they also prohibited usury and pride. You couldn't flaunt, and you couldn't profit from others' desperation. Envy was a sin in a world structured to minimize it. Modernity kept the moral condemnation but dropped the structural constraints. Now we're stuck with an emotion we can't admit to feeling, triggered constantly by a system designed to provoke it, with no socially acceptable way to respond except to feel vaguely bad about ourselves for feeling it at all.
