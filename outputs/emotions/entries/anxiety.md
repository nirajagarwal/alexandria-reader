# Anxiety

The word comes from the Latin *angere*: to choke, to strangle. The Romans meant it physically—a tightness in the throat, a constriction in the chest. This turns out to be more accurate than the modern usage, which treats anxiety as primarily mental. What happens in the body comes first; what happens in the mind is commentary.

The clinical distinction between fear and anxiety is supposedly clean: fear has an object, anxiety doesn't. You're afraid of the bear; you're anxious about nothing in particular, or everything in general. But Joseph LeDoux, who mapped the fear circuits in the brain, argues the distinction is messier than that. The amygdala responds to threats whether they're present or imagined, proximate or distant, specific or vague. What we call anxiety is often just fear with a longer time horizon.

The Stoics understood this. Seneca wrote that "we suffer more in imagination than in reality," which sounds like self-help wisdom until you read the mechanism he's describing. The mind projects itself forward, generates scenarios, and the body responds to those projections as if they were current threats. The suffering is real even if the threat isn't. Seneca's prescription was *premeditatio malorum*—deliberately imagining worst outcomes to drain them of their power. Paradoxically, thinking about the thing you're anxious about, systematically and completely, often reduces anxiety more than trying not to think about it.

Modern exposure therapy operates on the same principle, but the research shows a complication. Peter Lang at the University of Florida found that anxiety doesn't extinguish through exposure alone—it requires prediction error. The anxious person expects catastrophe; exposure works when catastrophe doesn't arrive. But if the person engages in what clinicians call "safety behaviors"—subtle avoidances that prevent them from fully testing their predictions—the anxiety persists. You have to find out you were wrong, not just endure the discomfort.

The biology is surprisingly specific. When Jerome Kagan at Harvard studied temperamentally anxious infants—about 20% of babies show high reactivity to novelty—he found they had lower thresholds in the amygdala and more activity in the sympathetic nervous system. These kids grew up to be more anxious adults, but not inevitably. The ones who were gently pushed to face novel situations developed normally; the ones who were protected from discomfort stayed anxious. The nervous system needs training, and overprotection interferes with that training.

This created a public health problem nobody quite knows how to solve. Rates of anxiety disorders in American adolescents have roughly doubled since 2010. Jonathan Haidt and Greg Lukianoff argue this tracks with the rise of overprotective parenting and social media, both of which prevent the kind of exposure that would train the system to regulate itself. But the data is complicated—rates are rising across many countries with different parenting cultures, and the causal arrows could point multiple directions.

Here's what's less controversial: the modern environment is catastrophically mismatched to the human anxiety system. The system evolved to handle acute threats—predators, rival groups, storms. It mobilizes energy, narrows attention, primes action. But contemporary anxiety is chronic and diffuse: economic insecurity, climate change, algorithmic judgment, ambient awareness of distant catastrophes. The system stays activated without resolution, which is exactly what it's not designed for.

Robert Sapolsky's work with baboons showed what chronic activation does. Baboons low in the social hierarchy—constantly anxious about aggression from above—had elevated cortisol, suppressed immune function, and increased cardiovascular disease. The human equivalent is obvious. Chronic anxiety isn't just uncomfortable; it's pathogenic. The body isn't meant to sustain that level of mobilization.

The pharmaceutical response has been inconsistent. Benzodiazepines work immediately but create dependence and rebound anxiety. SSRIs work eventually for some people but not others, and nobody is quite sure why—the serotonin hypothesis of anxiety turns out to be dramatically oversimplified. The most effective interventions remain behavioral: exposure, cognitive restructuring, aerobic exercise. The body learns by doing, not by chemical adjustment.

One finding complicates the picture. Not all anxiety is pathological. Martin Seif, who treats anxiety disorders, distinguishes between "danger anxiety" (the system working correctly) and "anxiety about anxiety" (the system treating its own activation as a threat). The second-order anxiety—the fear of the physical sensations themselves—often causes more impairment than the original trigger. People develop phobias of their own panic attacks. The treatment is counterintuitive: learn to tolerate the sensations without escalating them into emergencies.

The existentialists took anxiety seriously as a fundamental feature of human consciousness, not a disorder. Kierkegaard called it the "dizziness of freedom"—the vertigo that comes from recognizing you have to choose without guarantees. Heidegger argued anxiety reveals our "thrownness" into existence, the fact that we're here without having chosen to be, facing a future we can't control. This sounds abstract until you notice that existential anxiety—about meaning, mortality, freedom—doesn't respond to the same interventions as phobic anxiety. You can't exposure-therapy your way out of the fact that you're going to die.

The cross-cultural data suggests anxiety is partly constructed. The Japanese have *taijin kyofusho*, anxiety about offending others through one's own inadequacy—a social anxiety that assumes the problem is the self, not others' judgment. Americans tend toward the opposite: anxiety about being judged, which assumes the problem is others' perception. Same nervous system, different cultural interpretation, different phenomenology.

What remains consistent across cultures: anxiety is anticipatory. It's the organism bracing for something. The something can be real or imagined, imminent or distant, specific or vague. The bracing is the same. And here's the evolutionary puzzle: why does the system err so heavily toward false alarms? Why are we so much more anxious than we need to be?

The answer is probably that in ancestral environments, the cost of a false negative (missing a real threat) was death, while the cost of a false positive (responding to an imaginary threat) was just wasted energy. Better to jump at shadows. The problem is that modern environments generate infinite shadows—notifications, news cycles, social comparisons, economic precarity. The system hasn't updated its priors.

One more thing the research consistently shows: anxiety is contagious. Parents' anxiety predicts children's anxiety independent of genetics. Workplaces with anxious cultures create anxious employees. Social networks amplify it. The nervous system is social, constantly calibrating to others' states. You catch anxiety the way you catch a cold, through proximity. This means interventions can't be purely individual—anxious environments create anxious people, and no amount of personal resilience fully compensates.

The Stoics were wrong about one thing: you can't think your way out of anxiety entirely. The body has to learn safety, not just hear about it. But they were right that the mind's projections magnify what the body feels. The gap between actual threat and perceived threat is where most anxiety lives. Closing that gap requires both cognitive and somatic work—updating your predictions and training your nervous system. Neither alone is sufficient.
