# Restlessness

The Vikings had a word for it: *ófriður*, the opposite of peace. Not war exactly, but the inability to be still. When the sagas describe men seized by restlessness, they pack ships and sail into the unknown, often to their deaths. The Old Norse didn't pathologize this—it was understood as a force that moved through people, demanding outlet. You could resist it, but the sagas suggested you probably shouldn't.

Modern psychiatry sees it differently. Restlessness appears in the DSM-5 as a symptom—of ADHD, generalized anxiety disorder, major depression, mania, substance withdrawal, akathisia from antipsychotics. It's never the thing itself, always evidence of something else wrong. The underlying assumption: healthy people can be still.

This assumption turns out to be recent and probably wrong.

Anthropological evidence suggests that prolonged stillness is the evolutionary novelty, not restlessness. Hunter-gatherer populations studied in the 20th century showed constant low-level movement throughout the day—gathering, tracking, processing, visiting. The !Kung San walked an average of six miles daily well into old age. Stillness for hours at a time, the thing we now call "sitting," appeared only with agriculture and intensified with industrialization. We've been still for maybe 10,000 years, restless for 300,000.

The physiology bears this out. Marc Hamilton's research at the University of Houston found that even people who exercise regularly show metabolic dysfunction from prolonged sitting—increased insulin resistance, decreased HDL, higher triglycerides. The body isn't designed to be still. Below the level of conscious awareness, restlessness might be the organism's attempt to move back toward metabolic baseline. It feels psychological but may be chemically driven, the body's protest against a posture it wasn't built for.

Kay Redfield Jamison, studying manic episodes, found that restlessness precedes the obvious symptoms by days or weeks. Patients report an increasing inability to sit through meals, movies, conversations—a sensation she describes as "the blood being carbonated." But the restlessness in mania is chemically distinct from the restlessness in anxiety or ADHD. Same behavior, different mechanism. In mania, it's driven by dopamine excess; in anxiety, by cortisol and adrenaline; in ADHD, by dopamine deficiency. The body's solution to too much stimulation and too little stimulation looks identical from the outside.

The research on akathisia—medication-induced restlessness—reveals something uncomfortable. When psychiatrists prescribe antipsychotics, 20-40% of patients develop akathisia: an overwhelming compulsion to move, often described as worse than the symptoms the drugs were meant to treat. Patients pace, rock, march in place. Some become suicidal. The mechanism is dopamine blockade in motor pathways, but the phenomenology is interesting: patients don't describe wanting to move but *needing* to, as if restlessness were something that happened *to* them rather than something they felt. This distinction—restlessness as compulsion versus restlessness as emotion—gets blurred in clinical practice, often with grim results.

Søren Kierkegaard thought restlessness was existential, not pathological. In *Either/Or*, he describes it as the natural response to a life without commitment, without chosen limits. The aesthetic life—keeping all options open, refusing to be bound—generates infinite possibility and infinite anxiety. Restlessness was the price of refusing to choose. His prescription was commitment: to a person, a vocation, a God. The restlessness resolves not through finding the right choice but through *having chosen*.

The data on this is surprisingly supportive. Longitudinal studies find that people who commit to long-term relationships, careers, or communities report less restlessness over time, even controlling for satisfaction. It's not that commitment makes people happier—sometimes it doesn't—but it does seem to make them stiller. Barry Schwartz's work on choice overload confirms the mechanism: more options don't lead to better outcomes, but they do lead to more second-guessing, more scanning, more inability to rest in a decision. The paradox is that freedom can be a cage.

But there's another kind of restlessness the clinical literature mostly ignores. Artists, writers, scientists at the beginning of breakthrough often report acute restlessness—Rilke's "*Unruhe*," Einstein's "happy thought" preceded by months of agitation. This isn't pathology or existential crisis; it's the mind working on something it hasn't consciously formulated yet. Cognitive scientist Mark Jung-Beeman's research on insight finds that restlessness often precedes breakthrough by 48-72 hours—the brain reorganizing in ways not yet accessible to conscious thought. The restlessness signals a pattern forming below awareness.

The Greeks had a related concept: *aporía*, the state of being at an impasse, unable to proceed. Socrates deliberately induced it in his interlocutors—taking away their certainties until they stood intellectually restless, forced to think. The restlessness wasn't failure but method. Plato argued it was the prerequisite to real knowledge, the discomfort that precedes understanding.

Blaise Pascal wrote that "all of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone." The contemporary research tends to agree. Timothy Wilson's studies at Virginia found that people would rather self-administer electric shocks than sit alone with their thoughts for 15 minutes. When asked why, subjects described the experience as intolerable—not boring but actively uncomfortable, a rising restlessness they couldn't explain and had to escape.

Yet meditation traditions from Buddhism to Hesychasm are built on doing exactly this: sitting with the restlessness until something shifts. The shift isn't to stillness but through restlessness to something on the other side. Advanced meditators report that restlessness doesn't disappear but becomes transparent—you see it arising and passing without identifying with it. The restlessness continues; the relationship to it changes.

The question the research keeps circling without answering: Is restlessness malfunction or signal? The psychiatric model treats it as noise. The evolutionary model suggests it might be an alarm—the body saying "this situation is wrong." The existential model frames it as a call. The creative model sees it as pre-knowledge. These aren't compatible. We don't have a unified theory of restlessness because we haven't decided whether it's bug or feature.

What's clear is that suppressing it often doesn't work. Benzodiazepines reduce the feeling but increase depression. Beta-blockers reduce the motor component but leave the psychological restlessness intact. Forcing stillness through will depletes willpower. The sagas might have had it right: sometimes restlessness requires outlet, not management. The modern mistake may be assuming we can be still when the organism is asking us to move.
