# Hiraeth

The Welsh didn't just name homesickness. They split it. *Hiraeth* is what happens when home isn't a place you can return to—because it never existed, or because it existed once and doesn't anymore, or because you're standing in it right now and it's not the same. The word carries longing, grief, and nostalgia in the same package, but it's not quite any of those. English doesn't have an equivalent because English-speaking cultures haven't needed to theorize this particular configuration of loss.

This matters for the research. When psychologists study homesickness, they're usually studying undergraduates away from home for the first time—separation distress with a clear remedy. Go home, feel better. But hiraeth patients (if you can call them that) don't get better when they go home. They're homesick for something that can't be recovered. The clinical literature mostly misses this because it's looking in the wrong place.

The closest English gets is nostalgia, but that's not quite right either. Constantine Sedikides at Southampton has spent decades researching nostalgia and finds it's generally positive—bittersweet at worst, psychologically protective at best. People use nostalgia to buffer against loneliness and meaninglessness. It works. But hiraeth isn't buffering against anything. It's the thing you need buffering from.

The Welsh poet Dafydd ap Gwilym described it in the 14th century as longing for "that which is past, and may not return." Not the object of longing (which could be anything), but the structure—longing for something that time has removed from possibility. The Irish have *an bhliain dar gcionn* ("the year that's gone"), which gets at the same irreversibility, but it's about specific losses. Hiraeth can attach to places that never existed. You can have hiraeth for the childhood you didn't have, the country your parents left before you were born, the person you might have become.

This confused researchers for a long time. How do you have separation distress for something you've never had? The attachment literature assumes you're attached to something actual—a person, a place, a state of being you've experienced. But hiraeth suggests you can be attached to an idea of something, and the loss of that idea can hurt as much as the loss of the thing.

The neuroscience is speculative but interesting. Default mode network activity—the brain regions active during mind-wandering and self-referential thought—shows characteristic patterns during nostalgic reminiscence. But preliminary fMRI work on Welsh speakers experiencing hiraeth (yes, someone actually studied this) shows additional activation in regions associated with semantic memory and future imagination. The brain isn't just remembering; it's constructing and grieving a counterfactual. You're homesick for a place that exists primarily as a contrast to where you actually are.

The Welsh revivalist movement of the 19th century weaponized this. As English displaced Welsh in schools and public life, cultural leaders invoked hiraeth to mobilize resistance—not "save our language" but "recover what we've lost." Except the thing they'd lost was often idealized, a pure Wales that never existed. Hiraeth became a political emotion, a way to generate collective longing for a past that could motivate a future. It worked. The language survived, barely, but the feeling was real even when its object wasn't.

Émile Durkheim would have called this a social fact—a collective emotion that shapes individual experience. The Welsh had (have) a shared object of hiraeth: Wales itself, as it was imagined to have been before conquest, before industrialization, before English. You can catch hiraeth from your culture the way you catch an accent. The feeling comes first; the personal history justifies it later.

This raises an uncomfortable question for emotion research. Are emotions like hiraeth cultural constructions, or are they universal feelings that cultures happen to name? Lisa Feldman Barrett argues emotions are constructed from concepts—if your culture doesn't have a word for hiraeth, you can't feel it, only something adjacent. Paul Ekman and his followers insist basic emotions are universal, hard-wired, cross-cultural. But hiraeth doesn't look like a basic emotion. It requires too much cognitive scaffolding, too much narrative, too much time-sense.

The compromise position, articulated by researchers like Batja Mesquita, is that emotions exist on a spectrum from simple to complex. Fear and disgust are probably universal. Schadenfreude and hiraeth are culturally elaborated—built from universal components (longing, loss, nostalgia) but assembled in culture-specific ways. The Welsh didn't discover a pre-existing emotion; they *made* one by treating a particular cluster of feelings as coherent and naming it.

But tell that to someone experiencing hiraeth and they'll tell you you're wrong. It doesn't feel constructed. It feels like the most real thing in the world—more real than the place you're standing, more real than the present moment. Which is maybe the point. Hiraeth is the feeling of reality being in the wrong time, of the present being a pale copy of something more vivid that you've never actually seen.

The Portuguese have *saudade*, which is close—longing for something absent, probably forever. The Romanians have *dor*, which combines longing with a physical ache. But these are generally about specific losses. Hiraeth has a speculative quality, a relationship with the imaginary that makes it particularly hard to shake. You can't mourn your way through it because you don't know exactly what you've lost. You just know something's missing.

Modern diaspora communities—refugees, immigrants, displaced populations—report experiences that map onto hiraeth even when they don't have the word. Somali refugees in Minneapolis, Syrian families in Berlin, Indigenous people on reserves—they describe homesickness for countries that no longer exist, or never existed the way they remember, or exist now but aren't accessible. The therapeutic interventions that work for regular homesickness (maintain connection, process grief, build new attachments) don't reliably work here. You can't process grief for something you can't clearly identify.

One thing does seem to help: community. When hiraeth is shared—when everyone around you is homesick for the same impossible thing—it becomes bearable, even generative. Welsh culture didn't die from hiraeth; it survived through it. The emotion became a kind of connective tissue, a way of being together in loss. This suggests hiraeth isn't just individual pathology but a social phenomenon, something that emerges between people as much as within them.

Which means maybe the question isn't how to cure it but what it's for. Not all difficult emotions are dysfunctions. Some are adaptations to difficult realities. If your home genuinely is lost, hiraeth might be the appropriate response—not moving on, but carrying the past forward as a kind of counterweight to an unsatisfying present. Not healthy, exactly, but real. And sometimes reality isn't healthy.
