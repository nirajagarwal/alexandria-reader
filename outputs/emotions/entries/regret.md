# Regret

The philosopher Bernard Williams distinguished two kinds of regret that most people collapse into one. There's the regret you feel about something you did—the DUI, the affair, the email sent in anger. And there's the regret you feel about something that happened—the car accident you didn't cause, the genetic condition you didn't choose, the timing you couldn't control. Williams called the first "agent-regret" and noted that only humans seem to experience the second kind. We regret things we had no hand in, which is either a bug in our emotional system or a feature no one asked for.

The research keeps confirming that this second type is more common and more destructive. In a 2018 study by Shai Davidai and Tom Gilovich, people reported that their biggest regrets weren't about things they did but things they didn't do—the career not pursued, the relationship not started, the year abroad not taken. The regret of inaction outweighs the regret of action by a consistent margin across cultures and age groups. By the end of life, according to Bronnie Ware's palliative care research, the dominant regret is "I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself"—a regret about a cumulative failure to act, not any particular action taken.

This makes evolutionary sense only if regret is a planning mechanism, not a punishment mechanism. The neuroscientist Nao Tsuchiya found that regret activates the medial orbitofrontal cortex and the anterior cingulate—regions involved in decision-making and error detection, not emotion per se. When rats make suboptimal choices in maze experiments, they show behavioral signs of regret and make better choices afterward. The rat that takes the wrong turn and misses the food pellet sits at the junction longer next time, deliberating. Regret is counterfactual learning: constructing the alternative timeline to extract information about future choices.

But human regret doesn't stay in the planning department. It metastasizes. Marcel Zeelenberg's research on regret and decision-making shows that people will pay money to avoid situations that might produce regret, even when the decision is objectively neutral. In one experiment, participants chose between two gambles with identical expected values but different structures. Most picked the gamble less likely to produce regret if it failed—the one where bad luck, not their choice, would be to blame. We engineer our lives to minimize regret exposure, which often means minimizing choice exposure, which often means not doing much at all.

The temporal dimension matters more than researchers initially recognized. In the immediate aftermath of a decision, people regret action more than inaction—the buyer's remorse, the next-morning clarity. But this reverses over time. Thomas Gilovich found that after several months, regrets shift from "why did I do that?" to "why didn't I do that?" The psychological immune system kicks in: we rationalize our actions, find silver linings, tell ourselves it wasn't so bad. But we can't rationalize what we didn't do. The alternate timeline stays pristine, idealized, unrebutted by reality. The life not lived doesn't accumulate flaws.

This creates a cruel asymmetry. The regrets that feel most painful in the moment are the ones that fade. The regrets that feel manageable in the moment are the ones that accumulate weight. By the time you realize the real source of regret, the window for correction has usually closed. The study abroad program doesn't accept 50-year-olds. The relationship you didn't pursue married someone else. The career path not taken required credentials you no longer have time to earn.

Janet Landman's foundational work on regret identified what she called "regret intensity factors": the closer you came to the alternative, the worse the regret. Missing a flight by two minutes generates more regret than missing it by an hour. The NBA draft pick chosen one spot before your team picked generates more regret than the one chosen ten spots before. This is the counterfactual proximity effect: the easier it is to mentally undo the outcome, the more it hurts. Daniel Kahneman won a Nobel Prize partly for demonstrating that people have stronger emotional reactions to near-misses than to outright failures.

Different cultures construct regret differently, which suggests it's not entirely hardwired. Chinese and Japanese participants in cross-cultural studies report less regret than Americans about individual choices but more regret about failing to fulfill social roles. The locus of regret shifts from the self to the relationship. In collectivist cultures, the question isn't "did I make the wrong choice?" but "did I fail my obligations?" This reframes regret from decision error to relational failure, which may be why some East Asian languages don't have a word that maps cleanly onto English "regret"—they have words for shame, remorse, and disappointment, but the individualistic regret-about-your-own-choices doesn't cleave out the same way.

The clinical literature on regret is surprisingly thin. Unlike guilt, shame, or anxiety, regret doesn't have its own diagnostic category, despite being a major predictor of depression and suicidality in older adults. Amy Summerville's work at Miami University found that chronic regret—the kind that doesn't resolve, that keeps replaying the counterfactual—correlates with rumination, which is a better predictor of depression than negative life events. The problem isn't the regrettable event; it's the inability to stop simulating the alternative.

Here's what the research doesn't show: that regret teaches us what to do next. The counterfactual learning model predicts that regret should make us better decision-makers over time, more calibrated to our actual preferences. But longitudinal studies don't bear this out. People who report high levels of regret don't show improved decision-making in subsequent choices; they show increased decision avoidance. The learning doesn't transfer. Regret tells you what you should have done, which is useless information about the past, not actionable information about the future.

The philosopher Søren Kierkegaard argued that regret is what happens when possibility collapses into actuality—when the open future becomes the closed past. "What is done cannot be undone, but one can prevent the future becoming the past." This sounds like wisdom until you try to operationalize it. How do you prevent the future from becoming the past? By acting, presumably, which requires choosing, which exposes you to regret. The regret-averse strategy is paralysis, which guarantees the regret of inaction. There's no exit.

The most recent findings suggest regret might have a saturation point. In a 2022 study of adults over 70, reported regret levels didn't correlate with number of regrettable choices but with the presence of *any* major regret. One suffices. Once you have a big enough counterfactual haunting you, additional ones don't make it worse. The brain can only simulate so many unlived lives before it gives up and picks one to replay. This is either a cognitive mercy or evidence that regret isn't really about the alternative timeline at all—it's just the narrative form disappointment takes when we need someone to blame, and the only available person is our past self.
