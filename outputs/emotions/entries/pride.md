# Pride

The Abrahamic traditions called it the deadliest sin. Thomas Aquinas ranked it first among the seven—*superbia*, the root of all other moral failures. The logic was theological: pride meant claiming credit that belonged to God, elevating the self above its station. But the Greeks, who got there first, had a more specific diagnosis. *Hubris* wasn't just excessive pride—it was pride that forgot the gods were watching, pride that invited disaster. The pattern was so consistent they built their tragedies around it: Oedipus, Agamemnon, Creon. Overreach, nemesis, fall.

Modern psychology inherited this suspicion, then discovered it might be wrong.

In 2007, Jessica Tracy and Richard Robins at UC Davis ran a series of studies that split pride into two distinct emotions with different expressions, different antecedents, and different outcomes. They called them *authentic pride* and *hubristic pride*. Authentic pride follows achievement—you accomplished something difficult, you feel proud, you express it with a modest posture, expanded chest, slight smile. Hubristic pride follows no particular achievement—you feel superior, you express it with head tilted back, arms akimbo, smirk. The facial expressions are different enough that undergraduates can distinguish them from photographs at rates significantly above chance.

The two prides have opposite effects. Authentic pride predicts achievement orientation, prosocial behavior, and genuine self-esteem—the kind that holds up under challenge. Hubristic pride predicts narcissism, aggression, and shame-proneness (not less shame, more—the defensive kind that erupts when superiority is questioned). In longitudinal studies, people high in authentic pride maintain relationships; people high in hubristic pride cycle through them. The Tracy-Robins model has held up across cultures, including in populations that don't have distinct words for the two prides.

This matters because it suggests Aquinas was tracking the wrong thing. The deadly sin isn't pride-after-achievement; it's pride-instead-of-achievement. The feeling you get from actually doing something difficult is protective—it motivates you to do more difficult things, and it's tied to reality closely enough that failure recalibrates it. The feeling you get from imagining you're superior is volatile—it needs constant defense because it isn't anchored to anything.

The neuroscience backs this up in unexpected ways. Pride activates reward circuits (ventral striatum, ventromedial prefrontal cortex), but it also activates theory-of-mind networks (temporoparietal junction, medial prefrontal cortex)—the regions that model what others are thinking. Pride is *for* an audience, even an imagined one. Embarrassment and shame activate overlapping networks. This makes sense: all three emotions are regulating your place in the social hierarchy. Pride says "I'm moving up," shame says "I've fallen," embarrassment says "I might fall." They're monitoring your rank.

But here's where it gets strange. Cultures disagree radically about whether pride should exist at all. The English word "proud" is broadly positive—proud parent, proud tradition, proud achievement. In Mandarin, *jiāo'ào* (骄傲) is almost always negative, closer to arrogance. Japanese culture has *hokori* (誇り), which is acceptable when applied to group achievement (national pride, family honor) but questionable when individual. And the Christian tradition, which shaped Western psychology for centuries, treated all pride as pathological—Augustine wrote that even pride in virtue was sinful because it displaced God as the source.

This cultural variation might not be random. In a 2009 cross-cultural study, Yi-Chia Wang and colleagues found that members of collectivist cultures (China, Japan, Korea) showed lower self-reported pride after personal achievement but similar or higher levels of what they called "familial pride"—pride in the achievements of children, siblings, parents. The emotion didn't disappear; it relocated. The question isn't whether pride exists but whose achievement counts as yours.

Developmental psychology adds another wrinkle. Children start showing authentic pride around age three, as soon as they can represent their own agency—they did the thing, they feel proud. But hubristic pride emerges later, around age five or six, when they develop stable trait attributions: not just "I did well" but "I am smart." Michael Lewis, who studies self-conscious emotions, argues that hubristic pride requires believing in a fixed self that has inherent worth apart from actions. Authentic pride requires only believing you caused a good outcome.

This maps onto Carol Dweck's growth mindset research in uncomfortable ways. People with fixed mindsets (I am smart) experience threats to performance as threats to identity—hence the defensiveness, the need to protect superiority. People with growth mindsets (I got smarter) experience the same threats as information—adjust and continue. The deadliest sin might be thinking you *are* something rather than thinking you *did* something.

The clinical picture is messier. Bipolar mania often presents with grandiose pride—patients feel supremely capable, special, chosen. But this isn't straightforward hubristic pride; it's disconnected from the social-comparison networks entirely. The pride system has detached from reality-testing. And in depression, pride capacity goes offline completely—achievements don't register, anhedonia extends to self-assessment. The system stops caring about rank because it stops caring about anything.

One finding cuts through the complexity. Across cultures, visible expressions of pride reliably trigger envy, resentment, and coalition-building against the proud. The evolutionary logic seems clear: pride signals rising status, which threatens others' status in a zero-sum hierarchy. The emotion invites challenges. This explains why cultural norms so consistently regulate pride's expression—the rules aren't protecting the proud person's soul (Aquinas), they're protecting social stability. Unchecked pride starts dominance contests.

But there's an exception that researchers keep encountering: pride shared becomes celebration, which builds coalitions rather than threatening them. Team pride after victory, collective pride after group achievement—these activate the same reward systems but don't trigger the same defensive responses from others. The emotion is chemically identical but socially transformed. You can feel proud together; you can't feel superior together. Superiority requires someone beneath.

The paradox at the center: you need pride to function (it motivates, rewards, sustains effort), but pride makes you vulnerable (it signals threat, invites challenge, distorts perception). Cultures and individuals who navigate this balance stay in contact with achievement while managing the social risk. Those who don't either suppress pride entirely (and lose motivation) or let it detach from achievement (and lose stability). The Aristotelian mean turns out to be neurologically grounded, not just philosophical. Pride calibrated to actual achievement, expressed with awareness of the audience, regulated by reality-testing. Not humility, not arrogance. Accurate self-assessment performed for others who matter.

The Greeks were tracking something real. It's not that pride causes falls. It's that the kind of pride that doesn't remember it can fall is the kind that will.
