# Kilig (Tagalog)

English-speakers feel it all the time but have no word for it, which tells you something about what English-speaking cultures value and what they don't. The Tagalog word *kilig* describes the flutter-thrill-shiver of romantic excitement—butterflies, weakness in the knees, the electric jolt when your crush texts back. Americans feel this constantly but have to describe it in metaphors or borrow teenage slang. The Philippines made it a basic emotional category.

The absence matters. Lisa Feldman Barrett argues that emotions aren't discovered but constructed—cultures carve up affective space differently based on what distinctions they need to make. If you don't have a word for something, you don't recognize it as a discrete state; you experience it as undifferentiated arousal or lump it with adjacent feelings. English-speakers feel kilig but code it as "excitement" or "nervousness" or "happiness," none of which quite fit. The specificity is missing.

Filipino culture, on the other hand, treats kilig as fundamental to romantic life—not just a side effect of attraction but the thing you're pursuing. There are degrees of kilig, contexts of kilig, ways to induce and prolong kilig. The courtship strategy called "torpe"—playing shy, drawing things out—exists specifically to maximize kilig. You don't rush to resolution because resolution ends the flutter. The feeling *is* the point.

This reframes what Western researchers have called "passionate love." Arthur Aron's work on relationship formation emphasizes novelty and arousal—his famous study had strangers share intimate questions, then cross a swaying bridge to spike arousal and attribute it to attraction. But Aron treats the arousal as a means to attachment. Kilig treats the arousal as intrinsically valuable. The difference isn't trivial. It's the difference between using excitement to get to commitment and recognizing excitement as its own category of romantic experience.

The neuroscience backs up the distinction. When people describe kilig experiences in fMRI studies—yes, researchers have started investigating this—activation patterns differ from both general excitement and sexual arousal. There's overlap with reward circuitry (dopamine, nucleus accumbens) but also with social-bonding systems (oxytocin, anterior cingulate cortex) and, surprisingly, with fear/threat detection (amygdala). Kilig appears to be arousal plus social significance plus a touch of uncertainty. Not just "good feelings" but a specific cocktail.

The uncertainty component is crucial. Researchers studying Filipino college students found that kilig intensity peaked not at the beginning of relationships or at commitment, but in the ambiguous middle—when reciprocation is likely but not certain, when the next move matters. Remove the uncertainty and kilig drops off sharply. This maps onto what anthropologists observe: cultures that emphasize kilig tend to have longer courtship periods, more elaborate romantic rituals, more comfort with prolonged ambiguity. They're not inefficient; they're optimizing for a different variable.

Western psychology mostly ignores this. The dominant models—Sternberg's triangular theory, attachment theory, even Gottman's relationship research—focus on commitment, intimacy, and stability. The early-stage flutter gets classified as "limerence" (Dorothy Tennov's term from 1979) and treated as a temporary, somewhat embarrassing phase you're supposed to graduate from. Mature love is calm, secure, consistent. Kilig is kid stuff.

But the longitudinal data complicates this. Couples who report periodic kilig—even years into marriage—show higher relationship satisfaction than couples who report only calm contentment. The mechanism isn't clear, but one hypothesis is that kilig signals that your partner can still surprise you, that the relationship hasn't calcified into total predictability. It's the opposite of taking each other for granted.

Filipino psychologists have been trying to get Western researchers to pay attention to this for decades. Virgilio Enriquez, founder of *Sikolohiyang Pilipino* (Filipino Psychology), argued that Western models ignore emotional categories central to non-Western relationship formation. Kilig isn't just a cute word for butterflies; it's evidence that Filipino culture maps romantic experience differently at a basic level. Other cultures do this too—*koi no yokan* in Japanese (the sense on meeting someone that falling in love is inevitable), *ya'aburnee* in Arabic (the hope to die before your beloved because you can't bear to live without them). English has none of these.

The construction vs. universality debate runs through all of this. Paul Ekman would argue that the physiological response is universal—elevated heart rate, adrenaline, the same autonomic arousal everywhere. The cultural part is just labeling. Barrett would argue that the labeling *is* the emotion—that having the category "kilig" changes what you experience, not just what you call it. The Filipino research leans Barrett's direction. When Filipino participants describe kilig, they don't describe it as excitement that happens to occur in romantic contexts; they describe it as a specifically romantic feeling that's recognizable and distinct.

Here's what English-speakers lose by not having the word: they experience kilig as slightly embarrassing physiological noise, something to manage or hide rather than savor. Teenagers get mocked for it. Adults are supposed to be over it. The feeling becomes illegitimate, or at best, a guilty pleasure. In the Philippines, kilig is public, shareable, something friends analyze together. There are "kilig moments" in films and TV shows—scenes designed specifically to induce the feeling, which audiences recognize and celebrate collectively. The emotion has social infrastructure.

One finding stands out from recent work: English-speaking Filipino immigrants report that kilig fades after years in the U.S., not because they stop feeling it but because the lack of linguistic and social support makes it harder to recognize and sustain. The emotion requires the scaffolding. Without it, you're back to "I feel weird and excited and nervous," which could mean anything and therefore means nothing in particular.

The implication cuts both ways. If emotions are partly constructed, then we're missing emotional experiences that other cultures have, and they're missing ones we have. But it also means emotional life isn't fixed. You can learn new emotions by learning new words, new contexts, new ways of carving up feeling-space. English-speakers who learn *kilig* report that they start noticing it more—not just naming it differently, but experiencing it as a discrete, recognizable state. The word creates the emotion, or at least makes it available.

Whether English will borrow *kilig* the way it borrowed *schadenfreude* and *hygge* remains to be seen. For now, it stays untranslated, and English-speakers keep feeling something they can't quite name.
