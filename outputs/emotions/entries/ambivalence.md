# Ambivalence

The word came from psychiatry. Eugen Bleuler coined it in 1910 to describe something he kept seeing in schizophrenic patients: the simultaneous experience of contradictory feelings toward the same object. Love and hate for the same person. Desire and repulsion toward the same act. Not oscillation—*simultaneity*. Bleuler thought this was pathological, a symptom of splitting that healthy minds didn't experience.

He was wrong about that last part. Within a decade, Freud had appropriated the term and universalized it. Ambivalence wasn't a symptom; it was the human condition. Every intense relationship contains its opposite. Every desire carries resistance. The normal mind doesn't resolve these contradictions—it lives with them, manages them, usually without noticing. What Bleuler had identified in schizophrenia was just an extreme, visible version of what's happening in everyone.

This reframing created a problem that psychology still hasn't solved: if ambivalence is universal, is it even an emotion? Or is it a meta-state, something about emotions rather than itself a feeling?

The research has gone both ways. Some investigators treat ambivalence as a stable trait—people score consistently on measures of tolerance for contradictory feelings. Others treat it as a temporary state that arises in specific situations. The confusion runs deep enough that there isn't agreement on how to measure it. Do you ask people if they experience mixed feelings? Do you measure positive and negative responses separately and look for where they co-occur? Do you track physiological markers of approach-avoidance conflict?

The physiological approach has produced the clearest findings. When people experience ambivalence, researchers see simultaneous activation in brain regions associated with reward (ventral striatum) and threat (amygdala). Iris Mauss at Berkeley found that ambivalent emotional states correlate with increased heart rate variability—the nervous system is literally doing two things at once, accelerating and braking. The body knows it's in conflict even when consciousness hasn't named it yet.

But here's what's strange: most people don't report feeling ambivalent even when physiological measures show they are. Naomi Rothman at Lehigh studied this gap and found that Americans in particular resist acknowledging ambivalence. When experiencing contradictory feelings, they report one or the other, or they average them out ("I feel neutral"). European and Asian subjects are more willing to report simultaneity. Americans seem to have an ideological commitment to emotional clarity.

This turns out to be cultural. East Asian philosophical traditions—Buddhism, Taoism, Confucianism—all make room for contradiction. The yin-yang isn't a problem to solve; it's reality's structure. Studies comparing Chinese and American subjects find that Chinese participants report more mixed emotional experiences, hold contradictory attitudes more comfortably, and show less physiological distress when experiencing ambivalence. The American attachment to consistency may be a local preference, not a psychological universal.

The clinical picture complicates further. People high in ambivalence tolerance handle uncertainty and change better. They make more flexible decisions. They're less vulnerable to cognitive dissonance effects—the psychological discomfort that drives people to resolve contradictions by distorting their beliefs. In leadership research, effective executives score higher on ambivalence tolerance than ineffective ones. The ability to hold contradictory information without premature resolution correlates with better judgment.

But too much tolerance creates paralysis. The classic research on approach-avoidance conflict, going back to Neal Miller in the 1940s, showed that equal opposing forces produce oscillation, not movement. Rats placed equal distance from food and shock froze. Humans do the same thing. When positive and negative evaluations balance exactly, people defer decisions indefinitely. Ambivalence feels like freedom from bias but functions like quicksand.

This is where Bleuler might have been onto something after all. In extreme cases, ambivalence does look pathological. Borderline personality disorder features what clinicians call "splitting"—rapid oscillation between idealization and devaluation, love and hate, with no stable middle ground. Obsessive-compulsive disorder can involve paralyzing ambivalence over simple choices—the "yes and no" that blocks action. These aren't just high tolerance for contradiction; they're collapse of the mechanism that allows people to act despite contradiction.

The evolutionary puzzle is why we experience ambivalence at all. Clear feelings produce clear action. Mixed feelings produce hesitation. From a survival standpoint, hesitation in the presence of threat or opportunity is costly. But Randolph Nesse, who studies the evolution of emotion, argues that ambivalence is adaptive precisely in situations where immediate action would be a mistake—complex social situations, long-term tradeoffs, decisions with irreversible consequences. Ambivalence is the brake that stops you from acting on incomplete information.

Relationships are where this shows up most. Attachment researchers find that ambivalence is common in close relationships, but its effects depend on how it's handled. Willfully suppressing one side of the ambivalence (pretending you're not angry when you are, pretending you're not attracted when you are) predicts relationship instability. Acknowledging the ambivalence without requiring resolution—"I love you and sometimes you drive me crazy"—predicts stability. The feeling isn't the problem; the demand that it shouldn't exist is the problem.

There's recent work suggesting that the capacity to experience and tolerate ambivalence develops. Children under seven generally can't report mixed feelings. They're happy or sad, not both. The ability to hold contradiction emerges around age eight, and tolerance for it increases through adolescence. Some people arrive at adulthood without developing it fully. Whether this is temperamental, cultural, or experiential remains unclear, but therapists report that teaching ambivalence tolerance—especially in clients who think in extremes—is possible and often transformative.

One last finding that doesn't fit the obvious interpretation: meditation increases ambivalence. Multiple studies show that mindfulness practice makes people more aware of mixed feelings, not less. You'd expect the opposite—that attention to present experience would clarify emotion. Instead, it reveals the contradictions that inattention smooths over. The mind that isn't trying to resolve finds more to resolve. Whether this is progress or just better reporting depends on what you think emotions are for.
