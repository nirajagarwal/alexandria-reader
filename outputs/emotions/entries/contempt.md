# Contempt

Darwin thought it was universal. In *The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals* (1872), he described the characteristic contempt face—lip curled on one side, nostril slightly raised—and reported seeing it across cultures. He was half right. The expression shows up everywhere, but what triggers it doesn't. The Utku Inuit of northern Canada reportedly don't experience contempt at all. The emotion requires a particular kind of social hierarchy to exist, and not all societies build that hierarchy.

Paul Ekman confirmed Darwin's observation in the 1960s, identifying contempt as one of the basic emotions recognizable across cultures. But his research assistant, Wallace Friesen, noticed something Ekman initially missed: contempt was the only "basic" emotion that involved asymmetry. Fear, anger, disgust—these engage both sides of the face. Contempt pulls one corner of the mouth. The asymmetry matters. It signals something different from disgust, though the two are often confused.

The distinction is positional. Disgust responds to contamination—something impure that threatens to pollute. You feel disgust toward rotting food, bodily waste, violations of cleanliness. Contempt responds to inferiority—someone beneath you in a social hierarchy. Lisa Feldman Barrett argues that disgust is older, more primal, while contempt is more cognitively sophisticated. You have to first establish a hierarchy, then locate someone below you in it, then assess them as failing to meet the standards of their position. That's a lot of computation for a feeling.

John Gottman can predict divorce from it. In thirty years of research studying couples, he found that contempt—more than anger, more than criticism—was the strongest predictor of relationship dissolution. Not contempt expressed occasionally but contempt as a stable stance: eye-rolling, mockery, sneering, the suggestion that one partner is fundamentally inferior to the other. Gottman calls it "the sulfuric acid for love." The metaphor is precise. Contempt doesn't erode affection; it dissolves the possibility of respect, and without respect, intimacy can't exist.

The mechanism runs through status. Contempt communicates "I am above you" but also "you deserve to be below me." It's not just hierarchical; it's moral. You feel contempt toward someone who has violated the norms of their position—the incompetent doctor, the cowardly soldier, the lazy worker. The philosopher Martha Nussbaum traces this to ancient Greece, where contempt (*kataphronēsis*) was reserved for those who failed to live up to their social role. The Greeks didn't feel contempt for slaves, because slaves had no role to fail at. They felt it for citizens who behaved shamefully.

This creates a paradox. Contempt requires seeing someone as beneath you, but it also requires caring about hierarchy enough to enforce it. The truly powerful often report feeling no contempt—there's no one positioned to threaten their status. Contempt concentrates in the middle ranks, among people anxious about their position. Studies of workplace dynamics find that managers who feel insecure about their authority express more contempt toward subordinates than confident managers do. The emotion signals status but also reveals status anxiety.

Psychologist Jennifer Lerner found that contempt, unlike anger, leads to inaction. Anger motivates confrontation—you want to eliminate the source of frustration. Contempt motivates withdrawal—you want distance from the inferior person. In her experiments, people induced to feel contempt were less likely to engage in problem-solving and more likely to give up on collaborative tasks. The emotion doesn't mobilize; it dismisses.

Political scientists now track contempt as a predictor of democratic decline. When voters feel contempt for political opponents—not disagreement, not anger, but contempt—they become more willing to violate democratic norms to defeat them. Why follow rules designed to protect the rights of people who don't deserve rights? The emotion short-circuits the mechanisms that make pluralism possible. Studies of polarization find that Republicans and Democrats increasingly report feeling contempt (not just dislike) toward each other, and this contempt correlates with support for undemocratic tactics.

Here's what researchers keep noticing: contempt is sticky. You can change your mind about whether someone is threatening (fear) or frustrating (anger), but once you've categorized someone as inferior, the judgment tends to persist. Psychologist Roy Baumeister calls this "the myth of pure evil"—once you see someone as contemptible, you see their actions through that lens. Everything confirms the initial judgment. The contempt becomes self-sealing.

The Stoics tried to solve this. Marcus Aurelius argued that contempt was incompatible with cosmopolitanism—if all humans share the same rational nature, no one is truly inferior. The move is to separate the person from their actions: you can judge behavior without judging essence. But this turns out to be cognitively difficult. The research on person perception finds that we're much better at forming global impressions ("this person is contemptible") than at maintaining distinctions between character and behavior.

Buddhism takes a different approach. Mudita, often translated as sympathetic joy, is explicitly framed as the opposite of contempt. Where contempt sees inferiority, mudita sees shared humanity. Where contempt withdraws, mudita connects. But the tradition is realistic about how hard this is. Contempt is one of the "near enemies" of equanimity—it feels like detachment but isn't. Real equanimity doesn't rank; contempt can't stop ranking.

The developmental research adds a wrinkle. Children don't show contempt until around age five or six, right when they start understanding social hierarchies and internalize norms about who deserves respect. Younger children show disgust (they recoil from gross things) but not contempt (they don't sneer at inferior people). The emotion requires social learning. Some researchers argue this means contempt is culturally constructed, not universal. Others argue it's universal but requires cognitive maturation to activate. The debate isn't resolved.

What's clear is that contempt scales. You can feel it toward an individual who failed you, or toward an entire group you've decided is beneath you. The leap from personal contempt to categorical contempt—racism, classism, ethnic hatred—doesn't require new emotions, just wider application of the same judgment. Hannah Arendt noticed this. She argued that contempt for "inferior" groups made atrocities possible not by creating new feelings but by extending familiar ones. Once you've established that some people don't count, the usual constraints on cruelty stop applying.

The question left open is whether contempt ever serves a legitimate function. Some philosophers argue yes—contempt for cruelty, for hypocrisy, for betrayal might be justified. But the research suggests that even "righteous" contempt creates the same cognitive distortions and same relationship damage as ordinary contempt. The emotion doesn't distinguish between justified and unjustified targets. Once activated, it operates the same way.
