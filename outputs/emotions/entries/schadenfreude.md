# Schadenfreude

The English language has no word for pleasure at someone else's misfortune, which tells you something about English-speaking culture's relationship to admitting this happens. German does: *Schadenfreude*, from *Schaden* (harm) and *Freude* (joy). The compound is precise. Not just noticing someone's misfortune, not just failing to feel bad about it, but actively feeling good. The thing we're not supposed to feel but do.

Researchers struggled to study it for years because subjects wouldn't cop to it. Early surveys asking "do you ever feel pleasure when others fail?" returned suspiciously low rates. Then Wilco van Dijk at the University of Amsterdam shifted the methodology. Instead of asking people if they felt schadenfreude, he showed them scenarios and measured their responses physiologically—facial muscle activation, skin conductance. The numbers changed dramatically. Nearly everyone showed signs of pleasure at certain kinds of misfortune. We feel it; we just lie about feeling it.

The pattern of who triggers schadenfreude turns out to be remarkably consistent across cultures. Van Dijk's studies, replicated in the US, Japan, and the Netherlands, find the same formula: schadenfreude increases with the perceived deservingness of the misfortune and the envy you felt toward the person beforehand. A bragging coworker loses his job—high schadenfreude. A struggling single mother loses hers—none. The emotion isn't indiscriminate sadism. It's a specific response to perceived violations of fairness or to envy finding resolution.

This maps onto the neuroscience. In a 2009 study, Hidehiko Takahashi at the National Institute of Radiological Sciences in Japan used fMRI to watch what happens when subjects see envied rivals succeed or fail. When the rival succeeded, it activated the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex—the region associated with social pain. When the rival failed, the ventral striatum lit up—the reward center, the same area that activates for money, food, and drugs. Schadenfreude isn't a cognitive judgment about fairness; it's a reward response. The brain is treating another person's downfall like dessert.

The evolutionary logic is straightforward. Primates live in status hierarchies. Someone else's loss can be your gain, either materially (more resources) or socially (higher relative position). Richard Smith at the University of Kentucky argues that schadenfreude evolved as a motivator for competition and a regulator of social hierarchies. When high-status individuals fail, it creates opportunities for advancement. The pleasure isn't aberrant; it's functional. Natural selection built a reward system for noticing when rivals stumble.

But here's where it gets complicated. The same neural pattern—pain at their success, pleasure at their failure—shows up regardless of whether you actually benefit from their misfortune. Your coworker's firing doesn't get you his job, but you still feel the ventral striatum activation. The emotion is tracking something more abstract than direct advantage. It seems to be responding to fairness violations or status incongruities, even when correcting them doesn't help you.

This is where the cultural variations matter. Shimul Melwani at the University of North Carolina found that Americans report more guilt about schadenfreude than Germans, Japanese, or Dutch respondents, even though they feel it at similar rates. The emotion is cross-cultural; the shame about the emotion is not. Cultures that emphasize individual achievement and meritocracy seem to generate more discomfort about schadenfreude, possibly because it contradicts the narrative that you want others to succeed on their merits. Cultures more comfortable with hierarchy and competition are more comfortable admitting they enjoy seeing rivals fall.

The deservingness factor does interesting things. In studies where subjects watched scenarios of deserved versus undeserved misfortune—someone who cheated failing versus someone who worked hard failing—schadenfreude only showed up for the deserved cases. This suggests the emotion is doing moral work, functioning as an enforcement mechanism for fairness norms. But "deserved" is slippery. In political contexts, people feel schadenfreude when bad things happen to members of opposing parties, and they're remarkably creative about constructing narratives of deservingness. Brain imaging shows the same ventral striatum activation whether the person "deserved" the misfortune objectively or you just decided they did because they're on the other team.

Social media has industrialized schadenfreude. Before the internet, you mostly encountered the misfortunes of people you knew—limited sample, high signal. Now you can watch strangers fail all day. The subreddit r/JusticeServed has 3.2 million members. Entire genres of content are structured around schadenfreude: cringe compilations, public freakouts, cancel threads. Van Dijk has argued that the constant availability of others' misfortunes may be changing the emotion's character, shifting it from a rare response to status rivals toward a routine form of entertainment. The reward pathway is the same, but the context is different—you're not competing with these people, you're consuming them.

There's a dark boundary here that researchers keep finding. Schadenfreude correlates with aggression and antisocial behavior when it becomes detached from deservingness. People who report high schadenfreude at undeserved misfortunes—someone randomly getting hurt, an innocent person suffering—also score higher on measures of psychopathy and Machiavellianism. The emotion has a functional range, and outside that range it starts to look like cruelty. The line between "pleasure at a bully getting his comeuppance" and "pleasure at suffering generally" is real and matters.

The philosophers haven't resolved whether schadenfreude is moral or immoral. Aristotle thought pleasure at deserved misfortune was appropriate—it's just desserts, not malice. Schopenhauer called it "diabolic." Nietzsche, predictably, thought the discomfort with schadenfreude was itself suspect, a sign of ressentiment—the weak pretending they don't enjoy the suffering of the strong because acknowledging it would reveal their own impotence. 

What's clear from the data: schadenfreude is universal, neurologically grounded, and responsive to fairness cues. What's unclear: whether its universality excuses it, whether its responsiveness to fairness makes it moral, and whether the industrial production of opportunities for it is changing something about how we relate to other people's pain. The Germans have the word. They don't have the answer.
